The copper hands of the astronomical clock tower groaned as they shifted, marking the hour when the last grain elevator in town fell silent. [20]

Liora Voss adjusted her spectacles, her fingers still warm from the choir’s final rehearsal, their voices lingering in her ribs like an afterglow. [38]

She wasn’t just the librarian—she was the one who coaxed the town’s fractured harmonies into something whole, who slipped sheet music between the pages of seed catalogs and left them on the elevator’s dusty ledges. [65]

Tonight, beneath the hush of borrowed starlight, she climbed the tower’s spiral stairs, the cuckoo clock weight—brass, chipped, inherited from her grandfather—nestled in her coat pocket like a secret. [92]

The weight had been missing for weeks, ever since the elevator foreman, Elias, had stormed into the library demanding to know why his men couldn’t understand each other anymore. [119]

*"They talk past one another,"* he’d growled, his calloused hands flexing. *"Polish curses, Ukrainian prayers, English orders—it’s all noise."* [138]

Liora had known then: the weight wasn’t just a counterbalance for time. [147]

It was the thing that had once kept the elevator’s rhythms steady, its mechanical *tick-tock* a neutral pulse beneath the workers’ clashing tongues. [163]

Without it, the silos had become echo chambers. [168]

She reached the clock’s heart, where the gears whispered in languages older than the town. [180]

There, Elias sat on the floor, his back against the pendulum’s arc, a half-empty bottle of *śliwowica* cradled in his lap. [196]

*"You stole it,"* he accused, but his voice was tired, not angry. [204]

Liora knelt beside him, the weight cool against her palm. [211]

*"I rehabilitated it,"* she corrected, turning it over to reveal the engraving she’d added: *"Mówimy—Ми говоримо—We speak."* [229]

Elias exhaled, sharp as a blade unsheathing. [235]

She pressed the weight into his hand. *"The clock doesn’t care what language you feed it. Neither should the elevator."* [252]

For a breath, the only sound was the celestial mechanism above them, planets aligning in silent judgment. [263]

Then Elias laughed, rough and sudden. *"You’re telling me a hunk of brass fixes thirty years of grudges?"* [277]

*"No,"* Liora said, empathetically decisive. *"It reminds us to listen for the spaces between words."* [291]

She reached into her bag and pulled out a stack of index cards, each inscribed with a phrase in Polish, Ukrainian, English—*pass the wrench*, *mind the gap*, *thank you*—the kind of words that greased the gears of daily labor. [318]

*"Interspersing languages,"* she explained, tapping the cards. *"Not erasing them. Not forcing one to swallow the others."* [334]

Elias traced the weight’s engraving with his thumb. [339]

*"My father used to say the elevator was the town’s spine,"* he murmured. *"But spines bend. Or they break."* [354]

Liora didn’t flinch. *"Then we teach it to move."* [360]

Outside, the wind carried the scent of crushed grain and distant rain, the kind of air that promised change if you were brave enough to breathe it in. [378]

Elias stood, the weight clutched like a rosary. *"Tomorrow,"* he said. *"We try."* [387]

Liora watched him descend, then turned back to the clock’s face, where the moon-phase dial glowed like a held breath. [401]

She thought of the choir, how a single off-key voice could unravel a chord—unless the others leaned in, adjusted, *listened*. [418]

The town was no different. [421]

As she stepped into the starlight, the elevator’s whistle pierced the night, one long note—then another, answering. [435]

Not harmony yet. [437]

But the beginning of a conversation. [440]

The next morning, Liora found the cuckoo clock weight hanging from a chain in the elevator’s control room, polished to a mirror shine. [456]

Elias met her gaze over the noise of the grains rushing upward, his nod almost imperceptible. [485]

She didn’t need words to understand. [489]
