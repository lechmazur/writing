The winterglass conservatory had been sealed since the Great Hush, its panes frosted into opaque mirrors that reflected nothing but silence. [24]
Mara pressed her palm against the nearest wall, feeling the sub-zero chill bite through her mittens, and wondered if the building remembered the concerts it once held. [39]
She was the last capsize survivor of the research vessel *Auralight*, whose hull had cracked beneath a soundless aurora three months ago, spilling its acoustic scientists into the dark water. [58]
While the others had clung to debris, Mara had clung to the watertight tuning fork case she used for calibrating underwater microphones, the only object that floated with purpose. [78]
Since that night, every frequency above a whisper had vanished from the world; engines refused to roar, birds opened their beaks to release only motion, and the ocean lay flat as polished steel. [98]
Scientists called it an impossible vacuum, priests called it judgment, but Mara called it unfinished, because she still felt rhythms inside her chest demanding translation. [117]

There she opened the tuning fork case, revealing twelve forks ranging from whale-low to bat-high, each labeled in her neat handwriting with the emotion it once measured. [203]
She struck the lowest fork against her knee; the tines vibrated, but no sound emerged, yet the heat-map flared crimson, sketching the outline of a double bass that had occupied this spot years ago. [227]
Encouraged, she struck another, then another, moving through the scale, watching the conservatory bloom into a silent aurora of instruments: brass rivers, percussion constellations, string nebulae. [251]
The unshaken marvels of music, stripped of their noise, revealed themselves as pure thermal emotion, and Mara realized the building was not dead—only translated. [271]
