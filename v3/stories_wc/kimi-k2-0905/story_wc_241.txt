The rainwashed library at midsummer smelled of wet stone and old paper, a scent that clung to Mira’s clothes as she stood before the restricted archives. [24]  
She had come after the heart attack—her father’s, not hers—though the tremor in her hands suggested otherwise. [35]  
He was a compulsive gambler who had taught probability at the university, a man who knew the odds and defied them daily. [50]  
Mira, his theoretically rebellious daughter, had inherited his mind but not his disease, or so she told herself. [65]  
Still, she carried his candle lantern with the cracked mica pane, the one he used during blackouts when he’d mutter equations like prayers. [82]  
The lantern now held no candle, only a folded slip of paper she’d found in his coat pocket—cryptic scrawls that matched the ones she’d been finding on subway seats for weeks. [101]  
Each message was a contradiction: “The only way out is further in,” or “Bet on the thing you refuse to see.” [117]  
She had begun to traverse the city not by destination, but by interpreting these scrawls as instructions. [131]  
They led her here, to the library’s basement, where the air was thick with clover melancholy—the scent of dried herbs pressed between pages of books no one checked out anymore. [150]  
Mira pulled out a volume titled *Lucid Contradictions: A Field Guide to Impossible Logics*. [164]  
Inside was a pressed four-leaf clover, browning at the edges, and a note in her father’s handwriting: “For when you think you’ve out-thunk yourself.” [181]  
She sat on the floor, lantern in her lap, and opened the book. [191]  
It was not a treatise, but a diary—her father’s, begun the day she was born. [203]  
Each entry was a wager: “Today I bet she’ll hate me for leaving,” or “I stake my last breath on her finding the lantern.” [220]  
The final entry was dated the day before his heart attack. [230]  
It read: “If she reads this, she’s already traversing the city I mapped in riddles. [242]  
Tell her the odds were never in my favor, but she is the exception that proves the chaos.” [256]

She had come to widen perspectives responsibly, to understand without forgiving. [275]

“Bet on the thing you refuse to see.” [319]  
She had refused to see him as anything but a failure. [328]  
Now she saw the lattice of love beneath the ruin. [336]  
Mira stood, lantern in hand, and walked to the subway. [345]  
This time, she didn’t sit. [350]  
She stood, eyes open, and waited for the next scrawl. [359]  
When it came—”The only way out is further in”—she didn’t resist. [370]  
She rode to the end of the line, then back again. [378]
