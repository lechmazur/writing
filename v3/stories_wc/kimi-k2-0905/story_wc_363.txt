The loom spinner’s fingers remembered the orchard before her mind did, coaxing thread from moon-white bamboo fiber while the shuttle clacked like a second heart. [29]
She worked in the forgotten grove where fruit trees leaned into one another, their branches stitched by decades of moss, the air thick with cider-scented rot and the hush of time’s unraveling tapestry. [47]
Every spindle-full she wound carried a wistful note, a vibration she could almost read, as though the yarn itself wanted to speak but had forgotten the shape of words. [68]
Tonight the silence felt different—taut, expectant—like a raveling silence being pulled backward through the eye of a needle. [85]
She set the loom aside and walked between lichened trunks until her foot struck something half-buried: a cardboard box warped by monsoons, its flaps sealed with brittle tape the color of dried blood. [109]
Inside lay a jigsaw puzzle packed in a velvet pouch, the pieces cool and heavy as night soil; each edge was saw-toothed with tiny runes that pricked her skin like nettles. [133]
She carried the box back to her wheel, spread the pieces on the packed-earth floor, and watched them catch starlight, stoically sparkling despite mildew and age. [155]

Piece by piece, the picture grew: not an image but a hollow—an absence shaped like a swallow in flight—negative space rimmed by golden seams. [199]
At the final gap she realized something was meant to nest inside: a single bead she had worn since childhood, a droplet of tree sap amber that once held a fossil ant and now held only the memory of wings. [227]
She pulled the bead from her hair, set it into the puzzle’s last cavity, and the orchard exhaled a chord of distant bees. [248]
The assembled panel lifted like a kite, tugging her forward; threads from her loom rose likewise, anchoring to the floating puzzle until a loom-sized portal quivered between stake and sky. [276]
Through it she saw not another place but another silence—an impossible distance measured not in miles but in forgotten lullabies, in letters never mailed, in names erased from family scrolls. [302]
She understood then the bargain: to carry a message across that gap she must offer the only voice still hers. [323]
She cut the amber bead free from the puzzle, crushed it between molars, and swallowed the grit of ancient resin and ant-wing dust; the taste was cider and copper and goodbye. [354]
Words pooled on her tongue like cooling iron; she wound them onto the bobbin, fiber drinking voice until the yarn glowed gold. [375]
