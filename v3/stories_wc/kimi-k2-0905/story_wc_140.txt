The tidal hush at dawn found Mara folded between rusted walls, her lungs counting the cadence of brine and machinery. [23]
She was officially dead—paper said so—yet her heart hammered against the death certificate crammed in her pocket, the clerical error that had erased her like a smudge of ink. [42]
In this port city's towering labyrinth of shipping containers, every stacked corridor filtered reality differently: some aisles glowed with sodium light, others drowned in salt shadow, and a few repeated yesterday's echo as if time itself had been mislabeled. [65]
Mara moved through the metal canyons, collecting fragments of overheard stories the way dockworkers pocket dropped washers—an argument about a vanished crew, a lullaby in a language no flag claimed, a joke whose punch line had been redacted by censorship. [90]
Each scrap she layered inside her mind, smoothing them like sheets of tin, certain that if she pressed enough voices together the texture of memories would emerge and prove she had once been real. [115]
She had no name that authorities recognized; even the barcode tattooed on her wrist now beeped invalid, so she answered only to the wet slap of waves against hulls, a nameless identity shaped by rhythm. [140]
At the turn of the tide, when containers groaned like whales, she discovered a label from a poison bottle peeled off a crate and fluttering like a dying moth. [165]
The label warned of a slow, forgetful death, and its adhesive still carried the sour scent of the chemical, a scent that blurred edges the way her erased histories blurred years. [190]
She pocketed the label, feeling it burn cold against her thigh, a relic from the same shipment that had reportedly killed the dock union's archivist that week—an archivist who, rumor claimed, kept forbidden ledgers of every worker ever unloaded here. [218]
Mara wondered if the poison was meant to silence stories, to keep the port's memory sterile, and whether her own erasure was simply one more container in that cargo. [242]
Night slid in with fog; sodium lamps became moons that bled yellow halos, and she followed them deeper, climbing external stairs welded to a container tower, each level a day she could not reclaim. [270]
On the sixth tier she found a hatch ajar, inside it an improvised archive: newspaper shards, torn uniforms, and a child’s toy boat, all arranged like sedimentary layers of lives that had slipped through official nets. [300]
A dying man lay there, lips the color of wet ash, whispering that he once captained the freighter which carried the poison, a shipment ordered by the city to dissolve recalcitrant histories. [330]
