In the shadow of the colossal wind turbines that hummed endlessly over the old farmhouse, Elias lived as the blank-faced performer, his expressions forever sealed in neutrality after the happiness mandate reshaped the world. [28]
The mandate had come a decade ago, outlawing sorrow and enforcing joy through surveillance and subtle enforcements, leaving society in an emotional void where true feelings festered unspoken. [53]
Elias, once a grieving widower, had turned morally ambiguous in his survival, sometimes reporting neighbors for hidden tears to secure his rations, yet secretly aiding others to navigate their pain without detection. [83]
His motivation was simple: to cultivate lightness in grief, transforming heavy hearts into something bearable, like whispers against the turbines' relentless whoosh. [103]
Tonight, as the sun dipped behind the spinning blades, he prepared for a visitor, his blank face betraying nothing of the risk. [122]
The farmhouse creaked under the turbines' shadows, their low drone a constant reminder of the remade hush—the artificial silence imposed on emotions, rebuilt from the chaos of unchecked sadness that the mandate had "fixed." [154]
Elias sat at the worn kitchen table, a dried flower bouquet from his late wife's garden resting before him, its petals brittle and faded, a relic he dared not discard lest it invite scrutiny. [185]
He picked up his whittling knife, the blade glinting faintly in the dim light, and began to whittle a small piece of oak, shaping it slowly into a delicate stem to match the bouquet. [215]
As he worked, he employed his method: dramatically narrating mundane tasks to cloak deeper rituals in performative absurdity, making the forbidden act of mourning feel light, almost comical. [242]
"Behold," he intoned in a theatrical baritone, his voice echoing slightly off the wooden walls, "the noble knife descends upon the stubborn wood, carving destiny from mere timber, as if the gods themselves applaud this epic shave!" [275]
His blank face remained impassive, eyes dull, but the words danced with exaggerated flair, turning the simple whittling into a grand saga. [295]

She slipped inside, glancing warily at the turbines outside, their blades slicing the air like indifferent sentinels. [339]
Elias nodded subtly, continuing his narration without pause: "And lo, the wood yields to the blade's persuasion, shedding curls like autumn leaves in a whirlwind ballet, each sliver a testament to transformation!" [368]
