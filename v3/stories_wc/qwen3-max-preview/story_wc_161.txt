The attic smelled of foxed paper and cedar, its shelves leaning like tired sentinels guarding unread epics and unsent letters. [93]
Elara’s fingers, calloused from tightening harnesses and untangling wing-straps, moved with ritual grace—measuring grain, checking flight paths, whispering coordinates into feathers. [120]
She taught the birds to carry more than messages; they bore the weight of unsung melodies, fragments of songs lost to time, tucked inside hollow quills. [150]
Her motivation was not fame or fortune, but preservation: to cradle the echo of lost songs before they dissolved into the static of forgetting. [182]
