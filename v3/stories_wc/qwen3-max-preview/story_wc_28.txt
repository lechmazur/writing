Ash clung to every surface like powdered grief, and the wind whispered through fractured arches as if mourning aloud. [32]

She was retrocognitively trapped, not by magic but by conscience: every step forward pulled her gaze backward, forcing her to relive not just her own losses but those of everyone who had wept on this ground. [88]
Her hands, calloused from rebuilding and rebellion, trembled as she pried loose a single minaret mosaic tessera—cobalt blue, fractured at one edge, its glaze still shimmering with stubborn light. [115]
This tessera, she believed, was the key—not to restoration, but to renaming. [126]
Not the plaza, not the ruins, but the very concept of mercy that had failed them all. [141]
The old mercy had been passive, a sigh in the face of slaughter; she sought an aberrant mercy—one that wielded justice like a blade but cradled empathy in arms of steel. [170]

Mercy, she decided, must no longer ask permission to act. [212]

It must rename the rules. [219]

These were the tesserae of a new mosaic, one she would assemble not on walls, but in law, in gesture, in breath. [296]
She pressed the blue shard into the hollow of her palm, letting its edges bite just enough to remind her: mercy without consequence was theater; aberrant mercy bled, and in bleeding, bound. [330]
