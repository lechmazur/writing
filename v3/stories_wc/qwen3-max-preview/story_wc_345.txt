On the orchard planet of Virelune, where gravity bent gently around floating rock arches and fruit trees grew upside-down from crater ceilings, the altruistic ghost of Elira wandered barefoot through dew-slick moss. [19] She had no body, but carried purpose — a strangely trustworthy presence that made even skeptics lower their weapons and listen. [38] Her whispers didn’t echo; they rooted. [42] And now, after strangers had become family beneath the twin moons, she knew the time had come to reexamine their survival. [59] The orchard’s bounty was thinning, its roots fraying at the edges of reality, and whispers spoke of a nebula of respite drifting just beyond the seventh arch — a sanctuary woven from stardust and silence, where wounds of the soul could mend. [92] But reaching it required more than courage; it demanded orchestration — collective effort, synchronized like the wings of migrating sky-eels. [115] 

Elira chose her method with care: messages chalked onto deserted sidewalks at dawn. [126] Not digital, not shouted — but handwritten, fragile, intimate. [133] Sidewalks here weren’t paved with concrete, but with petrified root-veins that glowed faintly violet under morning mist. [150] Each message appeared overnight — looping script in silver chalk, always just ahead of the reader’s next step. [167] “The bell remembers the rain,” one read near the hollow where children played with gravity-marbles. [186] “Ring it when the third moon blushes.” [194] They didn’t know what bell — not yet. That bell was the butterfly garden watering bell — a relic from Before, when gardens were tended by hands and not ghosts. [261] Butterflies, genetically ancient and spiritually attuned, would flutter to its sound, wings brushing petals into bloom. [283] Elira had found it buried beneath a collapsed gazebo, half-swallowed by singing vines. [300] She’d cleaned its brass with spectral tears — the only substance that didn’t corrode its magic.
