[239] He fashioned the crystal from a shard of quartz he had kept for years, polishing it until its facets caught the light in a thousand tiny mirrors. [266] When he inserted the crystal into the gear, the timer shivered, and a low hum rose from its core, drawing the reflected beams into a single, steady pulse. [294] The pulse acted as a conduit, pulling the scattered echoes from the cavern walls into a hollow jar that Corin had placed on a pedestal, a jar he called the cellar of reason. [327] He felt a quiet triumph as the jar filled with a soft, luminous sound that seemed to contain the wonder of the wounded season itself, now safely stored for later contemplation. [358] The locksmith’s mind, always reflective, turned inward to consider the paradox of capturing something as fleeting as an echo without imprisoning its source. [381] He realized that the cellar of reason was not a prison but a library, a place where wonder could be revisited like a cherished volume when the world outside once again grew bright. [414] As the wounded season slipped toward spring, the cavern’s echo‑harvest grew richer, and Corin, peripherally aware of the light’s slow return, added new jars to his cellar, each a silent testament to the patience of reverse‑engineering. [450] When travelers later entered the marble cavern and heard the soft hum emanating from the jars, they would learn that the locksmith had turned broken time into a chorus of stored wonder, a lesson in listening to the periphery of existence. [491] Thus the reflective locksmith, ever patient, continued his work, reverse‑engineering the secrets of the cavern one reflected ray at a time, confident that the cellar of reason would keep his wonder alive long after the last echo faded. [529] Night fell, and the marble walls glowed faintly with the reflected light of distant stars, each photon dancing like a memory across the surface of the crystal timer, reminding Corin that even in darkness, study of angles could reveal hidden pathways.
