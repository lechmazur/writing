<story>After a wind chime falls silent, the vigilant levee warden steps onto the midnight ridgeway at twilight and listens to the reservoir breathing in a mirrored hush. [27]
He works nights not only to keep the banks intact, but to spin stories into shared meaning before the drowned town forgets itself again. [51]
The drought has drawn the lake down so far that foundations print the mud like patient bones, and street names glint on rusted corners. [75]
Among the weeds he finds a sapphire-hued liqueur label, lacquered by time, its letters curling into a familiar flourish that stirs an ache he cannot place. [101]
He pockets it, then notices a photograph half buried nearby, a boy on a bicycle laughing beside a brick storefront with the same blue label in the window. [129]
The shoreline becomes a trail of photographs leading to a forgotten memory, each image pinned by muck and reed, beckoning him down the scoured blocks. [154]

He remembers a mentor who taught him river gauges, a bar called The Saffron Pike, and a wind chime that rattled above the door whenever ice met glass. [213]
But the label in his pocket does not say saffron; it flashes that colder blue, and the flourish is the script his mentor used to mark borrowed books. [241]
The warden is precisely humane in his patrols, never pocketing keepsakes without cause, never stepping on a foundation stone without pausing to name who might have laid it. [269]
Now the photographs draw him toward the intersection where the old levee once met the main street, a place he has avoided because memory there behaves like undertow. [297]
He hears frogs slow their percussion as the sky deepens, and the drowned bell tower reveals its shoulders, and the mirrored hush of the water steadies his breath. [325]

He had said the duty of a levee warden was not only to fortify earth and concrete, but to keep what should not be washed away from being washed away. [413]
