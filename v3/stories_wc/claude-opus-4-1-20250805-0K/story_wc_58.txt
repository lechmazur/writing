The palace carpet had begun unraveling three autumns ago, when the last merchant ship slipped beyond the harbor's embrace, taking with it everyone who still believed in tomorrow. [27] Now Elara sat in the shadowed garden, pulling a single crimson weft thread between her fingers, watching it catch what little light filtered through the dying maple canopy. [55] 

She wrote obituaries for the living—those who remained in the abandoned capital, their souls already halfway to wherever souls go when hope departs. [78] Each morning, she would project herself into their empty houses, her presence filling rooms like incense smoke, learning the geography of their particular wounds. [103] The baker who still kneaded phantom loaves at dawn. [112] The seamstress whose needles moved through absent fabric. [120] The harbor master who lit lanterns for ships that would never return. [132]

The thread in her hand had once held together the throne room's magnificent tapestry, its fibers soaked in centuries of coronations, treaties, and betrayals. [156] Now it served as her divining rod, leading her through the labyrinth of explained absences that haunted every corner of the dying city. [180] Each pulled thread revealed another story, another life that persisted despite having already ended. [195]

Tonight's obituary belonged to Marcus, the court astronomer who still climbed the tower each evening to chart stars that no longer seemed to care about human affairs. [223] Elara had projected herself into his observatory yesterday, her consciousness settling like dust on his instruments, feeling the tremor in his hands as he adjusted the telescope's brass fittings. [254] His absence was different from the others—not a departure but an erosion, as if he were becoming transparent, layer by layer. [276]

The garden around her exhaled the peculiar perfume of autumn's last roses, their petals edged with frost that would never quite commit to killing them. [302] Shadows pooled in the fountain's dry basin where koi had once traced golden circles, their absence now a kind of presence itself. [326] This was the lonesome splendor of endings: everything more beautiful for being almost gone. [341]

She began writing in the air with the thread, her words visible only to her, each letter a small act of intimacy with the departed-but-present. [367] "Marcus, who loved the constellation Lyra above all others, continues his nightly vigil despite the sky's indifference." [384] The thread grew warm in her fingers, pulsing with the heat of connection. [470] The palace carpet's unraveling had taught her this: sometimes things must come apart thread by thread to reveal the pattern beneath. [492]

[519] He paused at the sundial, tracing its shadow with fingers that barely disturbed the fallen leaves.
