[30] Inside, dust motes swirled in the slanting light of a dying sun, illuminating the myth buster—Lira Vey, a woman who’d spent years dismantling legends with logic, only to find herself chasing one she couldn’t debunk. [65] She clutched a painted seashell in her palm, its swirls of cobalt and gold a map of constellations only she claimed to see. [89] “An ephemeral compass,” she muttered, tapping the shell against the leg of a broken tripod. [104] “It points where the stars *were*, not where they are.” [114]

The cello leaned against a wall, its body half-finished, strings frayed. [125] Lira had found it in the observatory’s attic, alongside journals filled with a astronomer’s frantic scribbles: *“The answer hums in the in-between!”* [150] She’d spent weeks reimagining past conversations between the astronomer and his late wife, a musician, piecing together their shared obsession with a “universal riddle” that tied music to the cosmos. [190] “They thought the shell’s hum could decode it,” Lira said, plucking a string. The note echoed, brittle and sad, like a memory trying to reform. [220]

The observatory’s control panel still buzzed faintly, its dials frozen at 11:59 PM—the exact moment the astronomer had vanished. [245] Lira often worked inside the whisper of closing pages, her notes piling up like unsent letters. [268] Today, she’d bring the cello to life, using its resonant hums to retell the astronomer’s story, to prove his riddle wasn’t madness. [299] “Gloomy cheerfulness,” she chuckled, a term she’d coined for the bittersweet thrill of chasing ghosts. [319]

[495] She played faster, the hum growing louder, until the glass in the windows rattled. [510]

Then, as suddenly as it began, the sound cut out. The shell fell silent, the cello’s strings still. [525] Lira opened her eyes. [590] She tucked the shell into her coat and packed the cello, its half-finished body now a relic of hope.
