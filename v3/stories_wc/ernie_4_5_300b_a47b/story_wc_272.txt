Elias adjusted his smudged glasses, the lamplight in the forgotten library flickering like a tired heartbeat. [21] He’d come here to escape the city’s indifference, to lose himself in the dust-choked silence of a lighthouse that hadn’t guided ships in decades. [45] But tonight, the walls felt too close, the air thick with the scent of old paper and salt-rotted wood. [65] He traced his fingers over the half-finished portrait of a woman whose eyes he couldn’t quite capture—a client who’d vanished mid-session, leaving only a broken tarot card box on the table. [97] The box lay open, its velvet lining torn, cards scattered like shattered mirrors. [112] Elias had always hated tarot; the pretense of fate in fractured symbols. [127] Yet here he was, staring at the VII of Swords, its image of a thief fading into the grain of the card. [144] “Why do we cling to fragments?” he muttered, a rhetorical question that hung in the air like a moth. [161]

[193] Now, he wondered if the books themselves were lies, their stories as incomplete as his portraits. [212] He reached for the tarot box, its hinges creaking as he lifted it. [224] A single card slipped free—the Hanged Man, upside-down, suspended in a void. [239] “What if reality’s just a collage of half-truths?” he asked, another question swallowed by the dark. [258]

[279] It was the city, the people, the way his art had stopped mattering. [294] He’d become a crestfallen portrait artist, painting ghosts for strangers who never looked twice. [313] The tarot box, though, felt like a challenge. [322] He tipped it over, letting the cards spill onto the floor. [333] Among them lay a thread, thin and frayed, caught in the box’s crack. [346] He plucked it free, its texture rough against his fingertips. [357] “Is this what’s left of us?” he whispered. [366]

[474] The woman from the unfinished portrait reappeared in his mind, her eyes no longer vacant but fierce, almost alive. [493] He dipped his brush into a jar of ink made from crushed tarot cards and began to paint. [508] The thread he’d found became a bridge between her face and the background, a shimmering line that defied perspective. [527]

[555] “What if darkness isn’t the absence of light, but the canvas for it?” [568] The portrait evolved, the woman’s expression shifting from sorrow to resolve.
