[35] Every morning, he awoke to the whisper of frost-kissed leaves and the faint shimmer of twilight frost, which clung to the skeletal branches like a second skin. [54] The district had once been a thriving hub of industry, but now it stood as a testament to time’s indifference, its cobblestone streets swallowed by mist and its buildings reduced to crumbling husks. [83] Elias, however, was firmly grounded in the present, his hands steady as he etched delicate patterns into sheets of glass, each mirror a portal to some unseen world. [110]

One evening, as the frost began to shimmer with an otherworldly glow, a stranger stumbled into his workshop. [129] The man carried an astronomer’s broken sextant, its brass arms twisted and its lenses shattered. [146] “I need this fixed,” the stranger said, his voice hoarse with urgency. “It’s the only way to track what’s been lost.” [173] Elias examined the sextant, noting the intricate gears and the faint scent of starlight that clung to its fragments. [192] “What’s been lost?” he asked, though he already suspected the answer. [204]

The stranger hesitated, then whispered, “A dream. A runaway dream that slipped through the cracks of reality.” [223] Elias’s breath caught. He had heard tales of such things—dreams that fled the waking world, leaving behind only echoes. [242] “And you think this sextant can guide you to it?” he asked, gesturing to the broken instrument. [259] The stranger nodded. “It once mapped the heavens. Now it must map the in-between.” [278]

[316] “I’ll help you,” he said, “but we’ll need to recreate the sextant’s purpose. Not to measure the stars, but to measure the space between.” [343]
