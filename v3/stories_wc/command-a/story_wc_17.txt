[22] Corridor Echo Cartographer, a name she’d adopted as both title and identity, stepped cautiously, her boots brushing against the moss-covered stones. [45] Her mission was peculiar: to discover what walls dream about. [59] It wasn’t mere curiosity that drove her; it was the legacy’s burden, a weight passed down through generations of her family, each cartographer more obsessed than the last with mapping the unseen. [103] Her ancestors had charted oceans, forests, and skies, but she sought the intangible—the echoes trapped in stone, the memories buried in mortar. [150]

[220] The air was thick with humidity, and the ivy seemed to watch her, its tendrils curling like fingers. [247] She paused, her hand brushing a jagged crack in the wall. [261] For a moment, she thought she glimpsed something—a flicker of light, a shadow of movement. [283] But when she blinked, it was gone. [290]

[358] She’d been unable to develop it, lacking the proper chemicals, but she carried it anyway, a talisman of sorts. [391] As she walked deeper into the passage, the walls seemed to close in, their dreams growing louder, more insistent. [420]
