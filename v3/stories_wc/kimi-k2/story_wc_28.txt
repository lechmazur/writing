The ancient plaza still smelled of ash, though the great fire had burned weeks ago. [14]
I stepped gently through the frost-rimmed colonnades, my coat stitched from soldier's canvas but lined with salvaged velvet, proof that I was the gentle radical the embers had half-forgotten. [28]
Above us, a sky once porcelain lay cracked like old glaze; stars dripped through the seams, pooling in cracks between the flagstones, making the night both cold and glowing. [44]
Children’s soot fingerprints had been washed away by winter rain, leaving only the faint odor of mercy twisted askew—the aberrant mercy of those too exhausted to hate. [62]
In the center, a fallen minaret had shattered into footpath tesserae, its colored glass chipped to glassy bones. [74]
At that edge waited a single square mosaic stepping stone still intact in the ugly geometry of survival, its many tesserae forming a dull green leaf, a pearl sky, and one blood-red dot shaped like a teardrop. [97]
I knelt, and history folded like wet parchment around my lungs. [107]
Images burst backward through me: lamb stew cooling, rulers renaming streets overnight, a blacksmith painting forgiveness onto armor, then further to the day the minaret was laid, when mosaics were pressed wet and hopeful under calloused thumbs. [139]
I understood then, retrocognitively trapped upon the stone’s glassy script, that the red tessera was once the pupil of a tile gazelle meant to watch over a people who feared their own hearts. [167]
Footsteps scraped up behind me. [172]
“Azim,” the voice said, and I knew without looking it was Leylah, the fire-cartographer who mapped loss in charcoal stains. [182]

“You shouldn’t be here,” she whispered, the warm coldness of her breath mixing with the freezing vapor of mine. [227]
“The plaza belongs to no one now,” I replied, tracing the chipped leaf. [236]
Because we both sought to cradle empathy in arms of steel, she stayed. [246]
Together we sifted through broken panes of celestial blue, silver moon chip, and what had been the prophet’s tunic gold. [263]
While we worked, I told her how mercy had strayed during the fire: surgeons amputating without anesthetic, their kind eyes promising the hurt was love; mothers forgiving soldiers who still carried unconsumed bullets. [295]
Leylah produced the single blood dot my memory had named, the lost tessera now warm from her palm, and placed it softly between us. [314]
“Empathy burns more than rage,” she murmured, “but rage leaves less to rebuild.” [326]
I nodded, understanding that this plaza needed to rename itself before any rebuilding began. [336]
So I bent and pressed the red teardrop tessera into the minaret mosaic where the pupil belonged. [350]
Its edges, jagged from the great fire, became a pupil that clenched the whole broken world, seeing back as well as forward. [369]
Under my thumb, the tessera clicked, and a shard of mirrored sky reflected Leylah’s face warped by centuries of flame and pity. [386]
The plaza felt the shift, a tremor like a child finally exhaling after a nightmare. [396]

With the chipped patterns beneath our boots as guide, we stepped in tandem along the borders of the stepping stone, letting our footfalls finish the interrupted images. [426]
Where the leaf ended, we placed jade chips; where sky fractured, we laid cerulean; where the gazelle had lost its feet, we knelt and inserted powdered gold. [456]
