The borrowed-time timekeeper stood at the edge of the emerald silo, its copper panels oxidized into verdigris scales that caught the last light of a day whose hours no longer marched in order. [34]  
After numbers changed meaning, she had learned to feel duration as a texture—sometimes velvet, sometimes broken glass—rather than a count. [47]  
In her pocket she carried a cremation urn fragment, no larger than a guitar pick, chipped from the vessel that once held her grandmother’s ashes and, according to family rumor, a damaged time capsule buried inside the ashes themselves. [70]  
The fragment was suddenly timeless; it neither warmed nor cooled, and when she pressed it to her wrist the pulse beneath it paused, waiting for permission to resume. [86]  
She had come to the silo to rediscover the capsule’s message, convinced that whoever had sealed it understood the texture of time passing better than any clockmaker. [104]  
The silo’s interior smelled of wet straw and old diesel; shafts of greenish light fell through bullet holes left during the Resource Riots, when days were still numbered in neat rows. [124]
