### 1. Executive Insight

For this Q6 cohort—a robust set of 600-800 word stories—**the dominant pattern is a remarkable commitment to a singular, character-driven point of view (POV), maintaining a controlled psychic distance and a distinct perceptual signature even under escalating narrative pressure.** Across nearly every story, whether via Track A (single-POV default), permissible lyric compression, or—with some exceptions—Track B/C micro-segmentation, the prose reliably filters all perception, metaphor, and lexical drift through the consciousness and biases of the focalizer. This discipline in both POV and voice is crucial for reader immersion and credibility, especially given the stories’ brevity and the heightened conceptual or emotional demands. When closure lands seamlessly—with rhythmic line control, payoff for any patterned elision/compression, and no resort to head-hopping or expository fallback—the result is deeply immersive. Across the board, dialogic moments, if present, are functional, natural, and subtext-rich; interior monologue is modulated tightly to match situational stress. The batch is overwhelmingly Track A; only rarely does a story attempt Track C multi-POV, and usually with section or line-break clarity. Closure most often lands with clear emotional and/or narrative cost visible on the page.

### 2. Strength Profile

- **Consistent, Singular POV and Perceptual Signature:** Stories maintain a stable, character-specific lens—“a cosmos,” “grammar of ascent,” “calloused from weaving,” “tactile and visual hierarchies”—so every detail is filtered, biased, and made selective by who is seeing and why.
- **Purposeful Rhythmic Modulation:** Syntax and sentence length modulate in pressure—short clipped fragments at climaxes, longer lyrical cadences in reflection—e.g., “syntax modulates with pressure: longer in calm, clipped fragments under strain.”
- **Registered Dialogue/Voice Contrast:** When present, dialogue is differentiated and goal-driven (“This garden is a drain on resources”; “Why do you weaponize that noise?”); subtext and power shifts are rendered through what is left unsaid.
- **Patterned Compression and Elision:** When scenes withhold, compress, or lyricize (“the hush before the bells was a tangible thing”; “kelp-drift peace”), the pattern is taught early and always paid off—readers know what costs are felt in closure.
- **Sensory Bias and Diction Control:** Each story’s lexicon is tailored—be it a scientist, artist, or laborer, “synesthetic whispers,” “slab of dark glass,” and “frayed remnants” keep word choice and worldbuilding grounded in the focalizer’s plausible knowledge.

**Representative Micro-Quotes:**
- "strip needless drama from the loss, to reduce this personal catastrophe to a set of quantifiable variables"
- "she moved through the world with a gracefully alert precision"
- "dust motes, thick as felt"
- "slow dance of celestial bodies"
- "finding patterns in static"

### 3. Weakness Profile

- **Occasional Register Elevation under Pressure:** In peak moments, several stories drift into diction or abstraction beyond the POV’s natural lexicon ("cathedral of silence," "fractally precise," "a chaos变量 outside his control"), briefly diluting immersion or character consistency.
- **Filter Verb Clustering/Flat Interiority:** In a number of entries, immediacy and pressure are dulled by filter-heavy narration: e.g., “she saw,” “she felt,” “he looked,” stacking around pivotal beats.
- **Voice Lapses into Abstraction or Exposition:** At peaks, a handful of stories turn from embodied sensory prose to generality (“profound, unquenchable need”), therapy-speak, or explanatory lines that flatten drama and skirt BRR (“cartographer of emotional terrain with her primary map stolen”).
- **Missed Specificity in Dialogue or Character Contrast:** Some entries featuring >1 character allow dialogue tones to converge in emotional peaks (“both voices hushed/whispered”), missing opportunities for subtext or status contrast.
- **Experimental Disruption or Inconsistencies:** A minority introduce code-switches, untranslated foreign terms, or lyric patches (“潮汐-like,” “赋予 weight”) not taught by pattern or paid off, causing minor breaks in voice or reader orientation.

**Track-Test Failures (Rare):**
- Untaught multi-POV or code-switching without section breaks.
- Abstract, committee-like prose at climax, momentarily averaging out character-specific voice.

### 4. Performance Boundaries

This writing excels when it **anchors every moment in a single, highly particular character consciousness, and modulates prose rhythm, lexicon, and detail-selection to that interiority—even as stakes escalate.** Compression, lyric flourish, or purposeful elision should be both legible and taught by early pattern, repaid where the scene's cost or revelation lands. Voice risks breaking when stress induces register drift into generic lyricism, academic abstraction, or when filter verbs flatten immediacy—especially if not motivated by character or taught by the story’s prior moves. Success is more likely when the story’s method (lyric compression, patterned noticing, accumulative closure) is visible from the first third and paid off explicitly in the last quadrant or stanza.

### 5. Signature Moves and Blind Spots

**Signature Moves:**
- Artful patterned lyric compression: teaching a motif or diction in opening lines, then crystallizing it for closure (“gradually shift of epochs,” “lantern-moth hush”).
- Sensory hierarchy as worldbuilding: prioritizing the character’s dominant sense (sound, touch, taste) as the lens for external reality.
- Goal-driven, subtext-loaded dialogue: minimal but efficient use that advances plot and status.
- Modulating psychic distance for emotional beats: pulling close for realization, gently receding for aftermath.

**Blind Spots:**
- Over-ambition in register at crisis: straining for lyric/academic elevation rather than sticking to characters’ core noticing habits.
- Filter-verb or narrative summary at the expense of immediate sensory scene.
- Occasional unexplained stylistic/linguistic experiment disrupting flow for the reader.
- Emotional temperature similarity: secondary voices mirror the primary, especially under emotional pressure.

### 6. High-Band Signals (~8.8+/9+ Tells)

- **Coherent, Character-Specific Closure Lens:** The story’s final lines land through a filter of the POV’s core bias—not summary, but crystallized, textural insight inspired by the protagonist’s method of noticing.
- **Cost Legibility Paid On-Page:** Purposeful elision, omission, or compression is taught by pattern (not random), and closure shows what’s lost, changed, or discovered with emotional or narrative cost on the page.
- **Clean, Pressurized Rhythm to the End:** Line-level control (especially in the final third) shifts rhythm to support emotional acceleration or resolution; sentence structure and diction tighten or open in sync with the character’s state, without lapsing into syntactic monotony or summary.

### 7. Revision Levers & Keepers

**Keep:**
- 1. The highly specific perceptual signatures that filter every sensory detail through the focalizer’s bias.
- 2. The rhythmic modulation in prose—the ability to shift from long, meditative lines to clipped, urgent sentences as stress rises.
- 3. The pattern of teaching any experimental move (compression, lyric elision, motif) early, so closure feels inevitable and earned.

**Change:**
- 1. Audit for register elevation at pivots: trim/adapt any moments where metaphor, abstraction, or jargon (“fractally precise,” “cosmological schema,” unexplained code-switches) breaks the established voice.
- 2. Eliminate or paraphrase filter-verb stacks during key discoveries (“she saw,” “she felt,” “he noticed”), especially in action or epiphany sequences; replace with direct sensory or kinesthetic detail.
- 3. Sharpen dialogue distinction: ensure every speaker, if present, has a clearly differentiated speech rhythm, idiom, and subtext—particularly in scenes of confrontation or revelation.
