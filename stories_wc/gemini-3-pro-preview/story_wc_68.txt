The plague had finally burned itself out, leaving the sunken city ruins a hollowed, weeping shell of crumbling stone and silence. [22] Down here in the dripping bowels of the municipal sewers, Silas maintained an existence that was less about survival and more about an irritating, abrasive calm. [48] He was a self-proclaimed student of the truth, a wannabe philosopher who lectured the rats on the metaphysics of decay while searching for salvation in the muck. [75] The surface world, now claiming to be purified by the scourge, had constructed a convenient mythology to explain the apocalypse, but Silas found their mysticism logically offensive. [102] Their dogma was a soft, fluid lie, whereas he sought the hard, cold veracity of ancient methods. [119] To prove them wrong, he needed to restore the Great Clock of the port authority, a machine that operated on physics rather than prayer. [143] His rebellion was not violent, but rationally rebellious; he simply refused to let the objective reality of time be replaced by the subjective whims of a priesthood. [170] For weeks, he had forced his way through sedimentary layers of silt and trash that had settled over the machinist’s quarter when the sea walls breached. [196] It was a geological excavation of the recent past, slicing through strata of plague-ridden bone, rotted velvet, and industrial rust. [216] Silas did not weep for the dead, for emotion was a variable he could not calculate; instead, he chose to agonize over the precise dimensions of a missing brass fragment. [247] The specific component he required was a seafarer’s chronometer cog, a toothed wheel of exacting specifications that once allowed navigators to map the longitude of the world. [274] Without it, the Great Clock was merely a statue; with it, the mechanism would prove that the celestial spheres moved independently of the High Priest’s chanting. [300] He sifted through the heavy clay of the workshop floor, his fingers numb and raw, reciting axioms about the immutable nature of mechanics to steady his breathing. [327] The mud pushed back with the weight of the ocean, a physical manifestation of the competing narratives battling for the soul of the survivors. [351] If the priests were right, the gears were irrelevant; if Silas was right, the gears were the only things that mattered. [372] His hand brushed against something hard, circular, and serrated buried deep beneath a petrified ledger. [387] He pulled it free, rinsing the object in a pool of brackish water until the dull glint of brass cut through the gloom. [410] It was the cog, miraculously uncorroded by the salt and the sickness. [422] Silas retreated to his dry alcove, a nest of stolen books and schematics, and set the artifact onto his workbench. [442] He picked up his tools with a steady, unhurried demeanor that belied the urgency of his insurrection. [459] This was where the true struggle began, as he attempted to integrate the recovered piece into the salvaged escapement. [478] He began to agonize over the friction, filing the microscopic burrs of the cog with obsessive, painful slowness. [496] A single grain of dust could disrupt the frequency, rendering his counter-narrative invalid. [509] He worked with the abrasive calm of a man who knows that he is the smartest person in the room, even if that room is a sewer. [536] The priests upstairs preached that the plague had stopped time itself, creating a new era of eternal mercy. [572] He wound the mainspring, his heart hammering against his ribs in a rare display of biological rhythm. [606] It was a heartbeat of pure logic, a mechanical assertion that the past had happened, the present was measurable, and the future was a calculation, not a gift. [634] The ticking echoed up the ventilation shafts, rising through the sedimentary layers to the surface, a subtle, rhythmic heresy against the new gods.
