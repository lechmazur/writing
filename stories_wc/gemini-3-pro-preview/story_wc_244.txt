Arthur Pumble, the world's most elusive disaster-relief logistician, preferred the company of invoices to people, largely because invoices never asked him why he was crying. [25] He had set up his headquarters inside a disused railway station overgrown with ivy, where the creeping vines held up the crumbling Victorian brickwork better than the original mortar ever had. [56] Here, amidst the polite anarchy of the post-crisis world, looters would tip their hats as they stepped over sleeping families to pilfer copper wiring from the walls. [83] Arthur found the breakdown of civilization hilarious, often quipping to his silent radio that at least the trains weren't running late anymore, mostly because they weren't running at all. [112] His humor was desperately jocular, a frantic shield of puns and witticisms designed to deflect the crushing silence that settled whenever the work stopped. [136]

Arthur was a master of moving things—grain to the starving, blankets to the freezing—yet he remained paralyzed by a singular, internal deficit. [159] He was consumed by the motivation to find the cure for loneliness, a malady he considered more lethal than the floods or famines he managed daily. [185] In a reality bound by reciprocity, where a tin of beans bought a pair of boots, he reasoned that the ultimate connection required an offering of celestial value. [213] He reached into his breast pocket and withdrew the object: a jagged, shimmering fragment of moon rock, stolen from a museum before the roof caved in. [239]

To market this impossible item, Arthur did not use a broadcast frequency, for he was a man who understood the power of whispers. [262] He initiated his method through improbable rumor chains, whispering a secret to an indiscreet scavenger who told a nervous barber, who then informed a deaf composer. [288] The message was distorted and embellished at every node, transforming from a sales pitch into a mythic quest, yet the core truth remained intact. [312] The rumors spread like a benign virus through the refugee camps and shantytowns, carrying the promise that the man in the ivy station held the stars in his hand. [341]

Arthur waited on the platform, organizing crates of medical gauze into a pyramid, checking his watch though time had long since ceased to matter. [365] He needed the rock to catalyze a reaction, to force the universe to balance his ledger of isolation with an equal weight of intimacy. [389] Eventually, the ivy at the station's entrance rustled, disturbed by a visitor who moved with the hesitant grace of someone who had heard the call. [413] It was a woman wrapped in a patchwork coat, carrying nothing but a battered violin case and an expression of weary hope. [435]

"I heard a story about a man who wanted to trade the moon for a friend," she said, her voice cutting through the ambient drip of rainwater. [462]

"A rumor is just a truth that's had a few drinks," Arthur replied, his grin stretching too wide, masking the terror in his chest. [486] "I assume you have the payment?" [492]

"I have a song," she answered, opening the case to reveal an instrument that had survived as many disasters as Arthur had managed. [515] "It isn't much, but in this economy, a shared moment is the only currency that doesn't depreciate." [532]

Arthur looked at the cold, dead stone in his palm, then at the warm, living eyes of the stranger. [551] He realized that the transaction could not be a simple swap of goods, for reciprocity in human terms demanded vulnerability, not barter. [574] He tossed the moon rock onto the tracks, where it clattered indistinguishably among the earthly gravel, effectively destroying its value to preserve its meaning. [598]

"Play," Arthur said, sitting on the edge of the platform, no longer hiding behind a joke. [614]
