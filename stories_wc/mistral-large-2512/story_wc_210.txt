Elias Veyne, the last alchemist of Hollow’s End, had found it half-buried in the silt of the old docks, its glass warm to the touch despite the autumn chill. [53]

He had tried everything to open it: fire, acid, even the edge of his own knife, but the bottle remained sealed, mocking him with its silence. [116]

Hollow’s End had once been a town of dreamers, where prospectors and poets alike had come to chase the legend of the Wishwell—a spring said to grant a single, perfect desire to whoever drank from it. [152]
But the last wish had been granted decades ago, and now the town was a skeleton of itself, its streets overgrown, its buildings sagging under the weight of time and neglect. [187]
Elias had stayed when everyone else left, not out of sentiment, but because he believed the town’s ruin was a kind of alchemy in reverse: a civilization distilled into its purest, most potent form—potential. [230]

That was when he began to experiment with his own mind. [296]

Working memory, he decided, was the key. [360]
If he could hold the bottle’s essence in his thoughts long enough, perhaps he could will it into being. [382]
