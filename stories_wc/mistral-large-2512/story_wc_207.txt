The first time Elias heard the petrichor, it wasn’t rain he smelled—it was stone remembering water. [12]
He had been a petrichor collector for seven years, ever since the factory shut down and left the valley hollow, its smokestacks like broken teeth against the bruised twilight. [41]

Elias carried a glass vial in his coat pocket, stoppered with cork, and whenever the scent thickened, he’d uncork it, let the vapor curl inside, then seal it away. [109]
People called him sensibly eccentric, which was kinder than saying he talked to rocks. [129]

He decided to delve. [260]
The entrance to the subterranean lagoon was a fissure in the cliffside, half-hidden by ferns that glistened with condensation. [288]
