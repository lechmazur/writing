Liora, the poetic wharf weaver, traced her fingers along the ancient grooves, her breath steady despite the weight of the centuries pressing in. [42]
She had come here after the tide pools emptied, when the salt-stiffened air still hummed with the memory of water. [68]
Her motivation was simple: to carve a path from ruin to reverence, though she wasn’t yet sure how the labyrinth would answer. [97]

It depicted a woman sewing the sky back together with threads of starlight, a hauntingly optimistic vision that had lodged in Liora’s chest like a splinter. [159]
She had found it years ago in the wreckage of a shipwrecked library, its pages waterlogged but its spirit intact. [183]
Now, it was her compass, though she didn’t know it yet. [196]

The labyrinth’s paths twisted in on themselves, a puzzle without a key, but Liora walked them as if she’d known them all her life. [225]
She had begun to notice something strange: the same footprints, faint but unmistakable, overlapping hers in the dust. [249]
They weren’t hers—too narrow, too deliberate—and yet they mirrored her steps with eerie precision. [271]
It was as if someone walked beside her, unseen, via overlapping sleepwalking paths. [290]

One evening, as the last light bled from the canyon, she knelt to examine the prints more closely. [314]
That’s when she saw the needle. [320]
A slender bone needle, half-buried in the dirt, its eye threaded with a single silver filament. [341]
Liora picked it up, and the moment her fingers closed around it, the air shimmered like heat off a forge. [364]
A voice, soft as wind through reeds, whispered: *"You’ve been sewing the wrong things."* [382]

She turned, but there was no one there. [391]
Only the labyrinth, its shadows deepening, its secrets thickening like syrup. [409]
Liora’s hands trembled as she tucked the needle into her belt. [426]
Broken precedents, she realized. That’s what this place was—a monument to them. [443]

She walked faster, her pulse a drumbeat in her throat. [498]
The overlapping footprints led her to a chamber where the walls were not stone, but woven—countless threads crisscrossing in a tapestry of time. [530]
At its center stood a loom, its frame warped with age, its threads tangled but not broken. [553]
Liora approached, her breath shallow. [564]
This was where the unseen walker had been leading her. [578]

She sat at the loom and began to sew. [590]
Not with thread, but with memory: the scent of salt and old paper, the weight of the illustration plate in her palm, the echo of the voice in the dark. [625]

When she finished, the labyrinth sighed around her, its walls pulsing with the rhythm of her heartbeat. [718]
The overlapping footprints had vanished, but Liora no longer needed them. [736]
