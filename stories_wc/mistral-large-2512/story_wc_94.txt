Elias Voss, the censored poet, stood at the edge of the circular platform, his fingers tracing the cold brass railing as if it were the spine of a book he could not yet read. [56]

He had come here not to observe the stars, but to wait, to hold a vigil in pause, though for what, he wasn’t entirely sure. [120]

A darkroom timer sat on the ledge beside him, its red digits blinking in silent accusation. [136]
Elias had stolen it from the university’s photography lab, where he’d once developed images of protests—faces half-obscured by gas masks, fists raised against the floodlights of riot police. [168]
The timer was meant to measure exposure, but now it measured something else: the space between what was said and what was felt, the seconds stretching like scars across skin. [197]
