[27] She was furiously tranquil, her hands steady despite the rage burning in her chest at what had been stolen from her lineage. [51] The frost-covered tables that once held her grandmother's collection now stood empty, every jar and packet taken except for one small oak acorn cap wedged between floorboards. [78]

She pried it loose with numb fingers. [85] Inside the tiny vessel, a single seed remained—smaller than a grain of rice, darker than the void between constellations. [105] This was what the thieves had missed, the key to everything her family had guarded for seven generations. [124]

The vortex inheritance had passed to her three days ago, after the egg hatched. [139] Her grandmother had kept it hidden in the atrium's central fountain, frozen now into a pillar of ice that caught starlight and bent it into impossible colors. [167] The egg had been ancient when the first seed saver found it, and what emerged wasn't a creature but a doorway—a spiraling tunnel through time itself, narrow as a needle's eye and just as sharp. [252] Now, holding the acorn cap, she understood. [260] Time wasn't smooth—it had grain and knots like wood, rough patches and smooth. [275] The vortex inheritance allowed one to feel these textures, to sand against them or with them. [292]

The atrium filled with a sound like wind through ancient forests, though no breeze stirred the frigid air. [393] She pressed her palms against the ice and felt time's texture—coarse here where the thieves had disrupted decades of careful preservation, smooth there where her grandmother's hands had worked for sixty years. [592] She would age years in moments, or the atrium would crumble, or the seeds, if restored, would carry a debt that would come due in failed harvests and barren soil. [622]
