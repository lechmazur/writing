[22] Maya had worked there for seven years, a serene hospice music therapist who moved through the white corridors like a prayer made flesh. [100] He was seventy-eight, a former jazz musician who hadn't spoken to his daughter in fifteen years. [117] The silence between them had calcified into something that felt permanent, immovable. [129]

"I need to say goodbye properly," he told Maya, his voice threaded with urgency. [143] "To execute a flawless goodbye, the kind she deserves." [153] His eyes were wet. [157] "Can you help me?" [161]

"Then we'll make it whole again," Maya said. [284] "Not the record—the memory." [289]

[594] In his other hand: that broken piece of vinyl, no longer a symbol of fracture but of things carefully, lovingly mended.
