[21] She'd been chasing the ghost shift story for three weeks now, ever since the first caller mentioned it on WKRP's overnight paranormal show. [43] Not that she believed in ghosts—her religiously skeptical worldview had survived two decades of exploring forgotten industrial sites without supernatural intervention. [64] But something about the callers' frantic consistency had hooked her, the way they all described the same impossible thing: night workers filing through this very tunnel, lunch pails swinging, mouths moving in silent conversation. [95]

The tunnel walls wept with condensation. [101] Mara's exhaustion from thirty hours awake merged with her adrenaline into that familiar drowsy alertness, where every sound registered with preternatural clarity but her thoughts moved through honey. [129] She'd learned to trust this state. [136] It made her reckless in useful ways. [143]

[169] She crouched, turning it in her hands, and recognized it from the grainy photo a caller named Doug had texted to the radio station two nights ago. [195] "Right there in the tunnel," he'd said, voice shaking. [205] "Right where Eddie dropped it." [211]

Eddie Torres. [213] The name had surfaced in four different calls now, always whispered, always followed by uncomfortable silence from the callers. [231] Mara had started mapping the conversations like an archaeologist maps strata, appraising each frantic testimony for the truth compressed between words. [251] The callers weren't describing ghosts, she'd realized. [259] They were describing guilt. [263]
