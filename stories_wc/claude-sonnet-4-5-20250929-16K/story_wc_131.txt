The power grid failed at 4:47 AM, plunging the city into darkness just as the first gray light touched the library's eastern windows. [26] Maren had been living in the crawl spaces beneath the old building for eleven months, moving through the narrow passages between floors with the practiced silence of someone who had learned to disappear. [60] But tonight—this morning—everything felt different. [66]

She emerged through the ventilation grate into the main reading room, where dawn filtered through tall windows in shades of deepest blue. [88] The sapphire hush that filled the space felt sacred, untouched by the city's usual hum of electricity and traffic. [108] In the flickering uncertainty outside, car horns bleated and distant voices rose in confusion, but here the silence held like a promise. [131]

Maren moved between the shelves with kindly methodical care, running her fingers along spines she had memorized in darkness. [150] She knew every aisle, every corner, every hiding place. [159] For months she had cached supplies in the pipe chases—bottles of water behind the heating ducts on the third floor, protein bars tucked where the plumbing ran through the staff bathroom walls. [191] She had become invisible by necessity, after losing her apartment, her job, her place in the visible world. [209]

But something had shifted last week when she found the boy. [220]

He couldn't have been more than twelve, thin and frightened, hiding in the young adult section after closing. [238] Maren had watched him from the shadows, recognizing the desperate calculation in his eyes—where to sleep, how to stay unseen. [259] She had left a granola bar on the shelf beside him, withdrawn before he could see her. [277] The next night, she'd left two. [283]

[365] She brought it close in the blue-gray light. [374] The oxide coating released a smell that triggered something deep and unexpected: her grandmother's voice, reading aloud.
