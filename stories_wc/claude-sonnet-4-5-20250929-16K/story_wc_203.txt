The abandoned circus ticket office smelled like rust and old popcorn, a ghost of happier times that clung to the peeling paint and warped floorboards. [27] Marcus had been called to assess the ancient freight elevator that connected this forgotten space to the underground storage tunnels below, though he suspected no one had ridden it in decades. [57] As an elevator repair technician, he'd learned to read buildings the way others read books—analyzing dust accumulation patterns on cables and pulleys told him everything about a machine's recent history. [88] But his true skill, the one he never mentioned on job applications, was seeing the other passengers who rode between floors. [109]

The ghost appeared when the overhead fluorescent buzz died off, leaving only the pale afternoon light filtering through grimy windows. [129] She wore a sequined costume from another era, her form flickering like a damaged film reel, and she clutched something small against her chest with desperate fingers. [156]

"You can see me," she said, not quite a question. [166]

Marcus nodded, crouching beside the elevator shaft to examine the ancient mechanism. [178] Among the cables and counterweights, something glinted—a horologist's mainspring barrel, impossibly intricate, wedged into the pulley system like a mechanical heart. [200] It didn't belong there, yet it seemed to have fused with the elevator's workings, as if the building itself had grown around it. [224]

"I hid it up there," the ghost whispered, pointing toward the ceiling. [237] "Before they came for me." [243]

Marcus followed her gesture, his skillfully observant nature catching what others would miss—hairline fractures spreading across the pressed tin ceiling tiles, patterns too deliberate to be random deterioration. [271] These were cracks in the façade, intentional breaks that formed a map. [284] His motivation had always been to discover what ceilings hide, ever since childhood when he'd found his grandmother's lost letters tucked above false panels in their old house. [312] Buildings kept secrets in their upper reaches, and the dead often remembered where they were buried. [329]

"What did you hide?" [334] Marcus asked, already pulling out his tools. [342]

"The truth about the fire. [347] About why they burned the big top. [355] The mainspring barrel belonged to the ringmaster—it was from his grandfather's watch, the only thing he valued."
