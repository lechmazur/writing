The pigeons knew Elena's lies better than anyone. [8] She'd been a courier pigeon trainer for three years in the bookshop attic, but she told newcomers she'd done it since childhood, that her grandmother had taught her, that trust came naturally. [43] The truth was smaller and sharper: she'd reinvented her personal history after the fire, after Marcus, after the choir dissolved into ash and silence. [68]

In the slanted light beneath the skylight, Elena balanced on the crossbeam, one foot placed carefully before the other. [88] Below, centuries of forgotten books gathered dust. [95] Above, the pigeons cooed in their careful architecture of wire and wood. [107] She'd learned this balancing act to calm her racing thoughts, and lately she combined it with another ritual—mapping the swirl of dust motes under the single skylight, tracking their golden paths through air, naming their patterns. [142] Spiral. Cascade. Dissolution. [145]

The paper trails accumulated in shoeboxes: tissue-thin messages the pigeons carried between the city's remaining analog romantics, the old-fashioned and the deliberately disconnected. [167] Elena read none of them. [171] She'd promised herself that much integrity in her invented life. [180] But she kept the Mars meteorite chip Marcus had given her pressed between the pages of a ruined hymnal, a fragment of elsewhere wedged into the remains of song. [207]

"You're hiding," said Yuki from the ladder's top rung. [216]
