<story>
The diminutive titan had tended the ancient temple library for longer than kingdoms remembered their own names. [17] She stood barely three feet tall, yet her shadow stretched across centuries, and scholars who stumbled into this place lost in time often mistook her for a child until they met her ageless eyes. [51] The library itself was dying—gracefully, inevitably—its stone columns threading with luminous cracks, its scrolls becoming gossamer, its very existence slipping between the folds of the world. [78]

Kael, as she had been called in an empire now dust, moved through the collapsing archives with regally unpretentious purpose. [98] She wore simple robes the color of faded parchment and touched each crumbling book with reverence, though she herself was more ancient than most of the texts she guarded. [127] Her work had always been preservation, but now she labored toward a different end: to sunder the lies holding a false king upon a throne that still existed somewhere in the waking world beyond these temporal walls. [165]

The pretender called himself Orian the Restored, claiming lineage from the Starborn Dynasty. [178] Kael knew better. [181] She had catalogued the true heir's birth in these very records, across the pause of shifting constellations, when Polaris bent toward its neighboring stars and the great wheel of heaven seemed to hold its breath. [215] The true heir had been lost, yes, but not dead—merely drifting through the same temporal currents that kept this library suspended between moments. [237]

To unmask Orian, she could not simply emerge from this dissolving sanctuary and proclaim the truth. [253] The library's magic would crumble entirely if she left, and with it, all proof of the pretender's deception. [271] Instead, Kael worked through cryptic messages left in library books, the only objects that could still traverse the boundary between this frozen place and the flowing world. [298]

She ground iron gall ink pellets between her small palms, mixing them with water drawn from a spring that ran backward through time. [321] The ink shimmered with purple-black iridescence. [327] With a reed pen, she inscribed the rune of drift into the margins of texts she knew would find their way to scholars, priests, and truthseekers. [352] The rune was not a symbol but a kind of temporal mathematics, a proof written in curves and angles that would plant doubt in even the most loyal minds. [382]

Each message was different, tailored to its reader. [390] In a treatise on royal bloodlines, she noted discrepancies in Orian's genealogy. [402] In a philosophical text about the nature of identity, she posed questions that had no answer—unless one knew about the true heir drifting through unmapped time. [428] In a book of star charts, she marked the configuration of constellations on the night of the true heir's birth, a pattern that would not recur for another thousand years and matched no date in Orian's claimed history. [467]

The library sighed around her, its walls becoming translucent. [476] She could see through them now to the void beyond, beautiful and terrible, where lost things went to dream. [496] The graceful collapse was accelerating. [501] She had perhaps three more cycles before the temple library dissolved entirely, but she needed only one. [518]

The final message she wrote in the only book that mattered: the Chronicle of Succession, which rested on the pretender's own desk. [540] She sent it through with the rune of drift burning bright upon its frontispiece. [555] The message was simple, written in her careful hand: "The stars remember what mortals forget. [569] The heir drifts still, and will return when the false king's shadow sunders from his flesh." [586]

Kael set down her pen as the first wall became pure light. [598] She had been a guardian, then a witness, and finally an unmaker of lies. [613] The library exhaled its last breath, and she with it, content in her dissolution. [628] In the waking world, Orian read the Chronicle and saw the rune, and in seeing it, understood that he had already lost. [651] The truth, once released, could not be recalled. [659] The diminutive titan had moved the world without ever leaving her collapsing sanctuary, and that, she thought as she faded into memory, was nobility enough. [686]
