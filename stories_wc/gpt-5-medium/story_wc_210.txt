<story>The deserted alchemist returned to the paper-lantern cove with salt in her coat and ash under her fingernails. [18]
It was the first evening after the last wish is granted, and the bay carried only the sigh of spent miracles. [39]
She had survived the era of easy bargains by refusing them, and still she rejects false comfort swirling like smoke through the ruined streets. [63]
Her name was Iria, and her last apprentice had fled when the fountains ran backward, leaving her tools, her notebooks, and an amber bottle with no opening. [90]
The bottle held the city as a problem and as a promise, nothing inside and yet heavy, a vessel for design instead of escape. [114]
On the quay, abandoned lanterns bobbed like faded thoughts, and Iria began to conceive a way to coax life back without asking the exhausted sky for mercy. [141]
The townsfolk had taken their wishes and drifted, thinking the work of living was concluded, but their absence made the world feel poorly punctuated. [165]
Iria unrolled her ruined maps on a beached skiff and sorted the problems out loud, step by step through working memory until the sequence held. [190]
She balanced power, faith, food, and story, those four kept tugging one another loose like quarrelsome siblings in the dark. [210]
Revival, she reminded herself, is not a performance but a rehearsal that never ends, a ghost town revival only if the living learn their lines again. [236]
She lit three lanterns and placed them where the fish market used to crowd, then listened for footsteps that would not come. [258]
In the echo, she heard future children arguing about prices, which meant the prices must first exist. [275]
She ground kelp into ink, pried dates from warped timbers, and wrote a charter that began as a recipe and ended as a song. [299]
No gods were named, only ovens, wells, benches, and the obligations of neighbors who might not yet have faces. [318]
The amber bottle with no opening she set in the center as if it were the heart of a clock waiting for hands. [341]
If she could not pour from it, perhaps people could pour into it, each life a beat, each gift a tick, each refusal of ease a tock. [368]
She wrote invitations onto shells and slipped them into the tide, knowing that those who read waves can also read courage. [389]
At moonrise a boat arrived, not with settlers, but with the once-silent bell that had hung in the schoolhouse, now polished by storms. [412]
The sailor bowed to Iria and said he knew her work, that she rejects false comfort and might welcome something that asks instead of answers. [437]

She nodded and set the bell beside the bottle, and together they traced how water could be coaxed inland with staggered cisterns and laughing children. [488]

By noon the cove no longer resembled a memory, and the maps she had redrawn looked like receipts, debts paid in broomstrokes, handshakes, and the first market arguments. [757]
