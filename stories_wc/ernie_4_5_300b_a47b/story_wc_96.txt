[38] His trench coat, patched with mismatched fabric, flapped like a moth’s wings, and his eyes—one green, one gray—scanned the shelves for books too worn to be borrowed. [108] Cedar’s fingers trembled as he lifted a tattered copy of *Leaves of Grass*; inside, a coffee tin with a secret false bottom nestled in the spine, its surface dented but unyielding. [146]

He’d found the tin months ago in a dumpster behind a bakery, its false bottom hiding a stack of dog-eared poems and a vial of ink made from crushed berries. [187] The tin was his talisman, a relic of a life he’d molted like a snake shedding skin—each month, his personality fractured, leaving him to rebuild himself from fragments. [228] Tonight, though, the tin felt heavier, its weight a challenge. *Blooming resistance*, he thought, the phrase echoing in his mind like a mantra. [366] She’d become his unlikely ally, their friendship sparked by her quiet request to help sort books. [479] Each month, when his personality molted, he’d return to the tin, sifting through its contents to reclaim pieces of himself. [518] The poems were fragments of past selves—a lover of jazz, a failed accountant, a man who believed in ghosts.
