Lira pressed her forehead against the damp bark of the oak, her fingers tracing the grooves where the Scholar’s Guild had carved their library into its hollowed trunk centuries ago. [35] The air smelled of wet soil and old parchment, a heightened stillness settling as the demolition crew’s shadows lengthened outside. [52] She’d been commissioned to paint the tree’s interior before the wrecking ball arrived at dawn—a “final portrait of knowledge,” the guild’s last surviving member had called it. [75] But Lira, a face-blind portrait artist who memorized souls through textures and hues, saw more than wood and shelves. [90] She saw fractally precise patterns in the grain, spirals that mirrored the constellations she’d never seen but could sketch from memory. [110]

Her brush hovered over the canvas as she studied the walls. The guild had lined the hollow with shelves holding thousands of volumes, their spines faded to muted earth tones. [130] Yet one section remained empty, save for a single oak panel embedded in the trunk. [145] Lira’s fingers brushed the wood, and she froze. Faint indentations dotted the surface—a pattern too deliberate to be random. [165] She dug into her satchel, withdrawing a magnifying glass and a centuries-old coin, its edges worn but etched with a matching design. [185] The guild member had given her the coin weeks prior, muttering about “hidden doors” and “the sky’s reflection.” [200] Lira had dismissed it as the ramblings of a dying mind—until now. [210]

She pressed the coin’s edge into the indentations, rotating it until the grooves aligned. [225] A click echoed, and the panel swung inward, revealing a narrow compartment. [240] Inside lay a leather-bound book safe, its lock mechanism shaped like a crescent moon. [255] Lira’s breath caught. The guild had safeguarded secrets here, and she, a stranger with no face to recognize, had stumbled upon them. [275] But curiosity outweighed caution. She lifted the safe, its weight surprising, and carried it to her worktable. [295]

The safe’s interior held a single volume: a journal filled with sketches of the sky, each page marked with dates and times. [315] Lira flipped through it, her pulse quickening. The drawings weren’t just observations—they were blueprints. [330] The guild had mapped the stars’ movements to align with the tree’s hollow, creating a living calendar. [345] But why? She turned to the final entry, dated the day the guild disbanded. [360] *“To mirror the sky inside walls,”* it read. *“When the tree falls, let the stars remain.”* [375]

A rumble outside interrupted her. The demolition crew was testing the wrecking ball. [390] Lira glanced at the canvas, still blank. She’d intended to paint the library’s decay, but now she saw a deeper truth: the guild’s legacy wasn’t in the books, but in the act of preservation. [415] They’d crafted a way to immortalize the sky, even as their sanctuary crumbled. [430]

She returned the journal to the safe and closed the panel, then set to work. [445] Using the coin’s indentations as a guide, she mixed pigments to match the oak’s grain, blending ultramarine and gold to mimic the night sky. [465] Her brushstrokes grew bolder, fractally precise spirals emerging as if the tree itself had breathed them onto the canvas. [485] By midnight, the portrait shimmered with constellations, their light seeming to pulse from within the wood. [500]

The first blow of the wrecking ball shook the trunk. Dust rained down as Lira stepped back, her eyes stinging. [520] The safe, still hidden, would survive. So would the journal’s message. [535] She’d discovered the guild’s limit: they couldn’t save their home, but they could ensure their knowledge outlived it. [555] And Lira? Her limit had always been faces, but tonight, she’d crafted something beyond sight—a legacy etched in light and wood. [580]

As the tree groaned, she grabbed her satchel and the safe, slipping outside into the chaos. [600] The crew shouted, but Lira didn’t look back. The portrait remained, a testament to fractal precision and the quiet defiance of those who dare to mirror the sky. [630]

Later, under a starless city sky, she’d open the safe and read the journal again. [645] But for now, she walked on, the coin warm in her pocket, its indentations a map to secrets yet undiscovered.
