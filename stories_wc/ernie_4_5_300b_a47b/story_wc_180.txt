[46] Her daughter’s hair ribbon—faded rose silk, frayed at the edges—was knotted around her wrist, a reminder of the deciduous vows she’d sworn years ago, when the world still felt whole. [104] After the breach of contract, when the cathedral’s patrons had fled and the orchard’s roots had clawed through stone to claim the sky, Lira had vowed to persist, to mend not just quartz eyes but the fractures between past and present. The orchard was a tangle of apple and pear trees, their leaves whispering secrets in a language only the wind understood. [215] Lira’s boots crunched on fallen fruit as she reached the highest branch, where a cracked quartz eye lay nestled in a nest of moss. [258] She’d found it days ago, abandoned by a client who’d forgotten their promise to return. She’d promised Mara she’d find a way to keep the orchard alive, even after the contract’s collapse. [460]

The breach had come without warning. [546] Lira had stayed, using forgotten bag contents—a rusted key, a spool of copper wire, a vial of liquid starlight—to mend what others had abandoned. [595]

Now, she unwrapped the quartz eye from its mossy cradle. The crack ran deep, a jagged line of imperfection. The deciduous vows—vows that changed with the seasons, that adapted and endured—had worked. Mara’s hair ribbon slipped from her wrist, caught by the wind. Lira caught it midair, tucking it into her pocket.
