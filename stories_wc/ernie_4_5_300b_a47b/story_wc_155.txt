In the ruined orchard district, lost in centuries of thick fog, there lived a vivid mural artist named Lira. [25] She moved through the moss-choked paths with the grace of someone who knew each stone by heart, though the stones themselves had forgotten their purpose. [50] Her studio was a crumbling greenhouse, its glass panes long shattered, where she painted with light—using prisms, mirrors, and the occasional stolen sunbeam to cast color onto the damp walls. [85] The orchard’s fog clung to her like a second skin, softening edges, blurring time, until even her memories felt like half-remembered dreams. [110]

One morning, while scavenging for materials in the skeletal remains of a peach grove, Lira found a fresco fragment buried beneath ivy and decay. [135] The shard depicted a figure laughing, hands raised as if to catch falling stars, its edges chipped but the colors still vibrant after who knew how many years. [160] She held it to the weak sunlight, and for a moment, the fog seemed to part, revealing a glimpse of something brighter beneath the gloom. [185] “You’re a message,” she whispered to the fragment, “aren’t you?” [195]

Her motivation had always been to rediscover lost joy, though she’d nearly forgotten what that felt like. [220] The orchard’s silence had seeped into her bones, turning her art into quiet elegies for things no longer alive. [245] But the fresco fragment hummed with a different energy—a reckless, giddy hope that made her fingers itch. [270] She decided to display it in the heart of the district, a challenge to the fog’s monopoly on memory. [295]

To prepare, Lira painted with light for hours, weaving strands of color into a temporary mural around the fragment.
