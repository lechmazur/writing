[40] Her boots sank into soft, peat-rich earth, and the air hummed with the low, keening songs of spirits who’d long since forgotten their names. [60] She carried a gargoyle’s rainspout shard—a jagged, alabaster fragment from a statue that once guarded a crumbling cathedral—in her satchel, its edges sharp enough to draw blood if she wasn’t careful. [105] The shard, she believed, was a key to the unsent letters trapped in the space between worlds, letters that lingered like ghosts in the mangrove’s twisting corridors. [150]

Lirael had come here because the labyrinth was said to heal in moonlight, its waters purging sorrow and its roots mending broken things. [180] She needed that healing, though she’d never admit it aloud. [195] Her motivation was simple: to find the moment before deciding, the split second where choices hung suspended, and apologies waited unspoken. [240] She’d lost too much to silence, too many words left unsaid to the people she’d loved and lost. [270] Now, she sought to evoke those words, to coax them into the light through messages in condensation on windows—or, in this case, the glistening leaves of mangrove trees. [320]

The spirits’ songs grew louder as she pressed deeper into the labyrinth, their voices weaving through the air like smoke. [350] Lirael paused, closing her eyes, and let the music wash over her. [365] It was fierce tenderness, a paradox that made her throat tighten. [385] The spirits weren’t mourning; they were singing of resilience, of survival, of the way love could endure even when the world tried to drown it. [430] She opened her eyes and reached into her satchel, withdrawing the gargoyle’s shard. [450] Its surface was cool, but she knew it would warm in the moonlight, pulsing with a faint, silver glow. [480]

[525] She pressed her palm to the earth, feeling the vibrations of the spirits’ songs through her bones. [555] “Show me,” she whispered, her voice barely audible over the hum of the labyrinth. [870] The shard’s glow faded as the moon set, but Lirael didn’t need its light anymore. [895] She’d found what she’d come for: not answers, but the courage to face the unspoken. [920] The mangrove labyrinth had healed her, not by erasing her pain, but by showing her that even broken things could hold beauty. [960]

[1000] Lirael tucked the gargoyle’s shard back into her satchel, its edges no longer sharp, but smooth, worn down by time and moonlight. [1040] She walked on, her steps lighter, the unsent letters still echoing in her mind, but no longer heavy.
