[19] Lira, a claustrophobic spelunker with a heart that raced at the mere thought of tight spaces, pressed her palm against the moss-slicked concrete wall. [37] The facility, half-swallowed by the sea and time, had been her obsession for months—ever since she’d found a single, waterlogged page in a drowned library, its margins filled with frantic scribbles about undersea cables and something called “illuminant gold.” [80]

[104] Lira’s breath came in shallow gasps as she descended a spiral staircase, her headlamp cutting through the gloom. [119] The core concept driving her wasn’t glory or treasure—it was the codex margin’s fleck of gold, embedded in the paper like a star trapped in amber. [147] It was said to “track invisible things,” a phrase that had haunted her since childhood, when her brother vanished during a dive, leaving only a single bubble rising to the surface. [180]

[208] She remembered the codex’s mention of undersea cables, how they’d been designed to transmit not just data, but *presence*—a way to feel the pulse of the ocean itself. [241] The gold fleck, she believed, was a key to unlocking that technology, to proving that the invisible could be made visible. [265]

[310] In the center stood a pedestal, its surface etched with a spiral pattern. [321] There, resting in a depression, was the codex—or what remained of it.
