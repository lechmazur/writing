[35] Carved from obsidian and infused with ancient magic, Lumen’s body pulsed with a faint inner light, and its telepathic voice echoed in the minds of those who drew near. [58] For centuries, it had tended the garden’s relics—crumbling fountains, moss-choked statues, and beds of coral that once bloomed with color. [81] But the garden’s true secret lay hidden beneath the silt: a foxglove-stained glass ampoule, sealed with wax and forgotten by time. [104]

Lumen had always been incisively kind, its thoughts as sharp as the edges of its stone form. [123] It communicated not in words but in impressions—a flicker of warmth, a tingle of curiosity, a pang of sorrow. [146] When a diver named Mara stumbled into the garden, her suit glowing faintly in the dim light, Lumen sensed her fear. [169] She clutched a spool of thread, its silver strands trailing behind her like a lifeline. [188] “Who are you?” she thought, her mind brushing against Lumen’s. [201] The statue projected an image: itself, centuries ago, placing the ampoule beneath a sunken pedestal. [224]

[250] The ampoule, she realized, might hold a cure for the blight ravaging the oceans—a blight her grandmother had tried to stop. [273] But the pedestal was sealed, its surface unyielding. [286] Lumen’s thoughts shifted, urgent now. [299] *Through structural weakness*, it insisted, projecting a vision of the pedestal’s base, where cracks spiderwebbed beneath the silt. [322]

Mara knelt, her gloves slick with algae, and began to thread her fingers into the fissures. [345] The stone resisted, then groaned, fragments breaking away to reveal a narrow opening. [368] Inside lay the ampoule, its glass streaked with purple, the foxglove’s poison transformed by time into something else—a serum, perhaps, or a key. [536] *To welcome nuance into a certainty*, the statue projected. [549] Mara had believed her grandmother’s death a tragedy. [716] Mara realized the ampoule wasn’t a cure but a catalyst, its true power lying in how it changed those who wielded it. [739] She turned to Lumen, whose light now burned steady and bright.
