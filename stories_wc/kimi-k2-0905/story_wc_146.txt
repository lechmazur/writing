He listened to the ghost town’s silence, a hush so complete it felt alive, coiling through sagging roofs and shattered bell towers like smoke that had forgotten how to rise. [55]  
Before the collapse, Viremont had rung with nine hundred bells; now wind rattled iron fragments against stone, producing only bruised clanks that made the air feel older. [83]  
Elian was infinitely patient, the last practitioner of Tempris, the lost art of healing places by tuning their bones back into shared balance. [106]  
Legend claimed a single true note could rebuild what grief had unmade, but the note had to be born from the town itself, coaxed from memory trapped in every footstep ever taken on its cobblestones. [140]

The fork hummed, not with sound, but with absence, a vacuum begging to be filled; Elian felt it tug at the hollow inside his chest where his own bell once hung before he broke it to save a child from drowning in its echo. [213]

Windows without glass trembled in their frames; shutters swung inward as though curious; somewhere a cat too starved to meow exhaled a rasp that almost sounded like anticipation. [285]  
Elian repeated the circuit seven times, once for each quarter-tone missing from the town’s original chord, and on the seventh pass the fork began to sing—not audibly, but as pressure behind the eyes, the way moonlight tastes on the tongue of a sleepwalker. [325]  
He stopped beneath the archway again, raised the fork overhead, and struck it against the keystone carved with a mouth forever open in mid-chorus; the stone sang back, a low pulse rolling outward like distant thunder apologizing for arriving late. [365]
