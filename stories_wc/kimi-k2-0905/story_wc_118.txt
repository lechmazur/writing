The historian pressed her palm to the reclaimed concrete of the silo library and felt nothing, the way a tuning fork feels after the last note has gone. [24]
In the bronze quiet that followed the final human dream, she had become the only creature still driven to collect the stories walls have heard. [44]
She was odd even among the remaining archivists: sensitively perceptive to the tremor of old narratives trapped in stone, brick, and riveted steel. [65]
Tonight the air tasted of cooled ember and graphite, as if every erased reverie had settled into dust on the shelves. [84]
She carried no lantern; instead she let the faint copper glow of the horizon leak through the high windows and gild the tiers of salvaged books. [106]

Pages peeled away at her touch, confessing in soot-flecked handwriting how the owner had watched dragons stitched into a tapestry breathe while no one else was looking. [150]
The historian’s pulse answered: she owned a fragment of that very textile, smuggled out of a collapsing cathedral years earlier. [171]

“Axis displaced,” one line read, the ink bubbling where heat had fractured the paper’s grain. [212]

In the hush, she sensed other silos—grain, missile, textile—swaying imperceptibly across the empty continent, all of them realigning to a new, uncharted center. [257]
The tapestry fragment, tucked inside her coat, grew warm against her ribs, threads tightening like muscles remembering an old flight. [278]

She pictured dragons on the tapestry circling that invisible pivot, tails tethered to the hinge where the world had slipped. [407]

The writer, a child, described hiding inside a hearth while adults argued about whether to burn the tapestry to keep warm. [452]
“I heard the dragons speak,” the child wrote. “They said the fire was just another door, and doors remember feet.” [472]
The historian closed the diary, understanding at last why the fragment had sought her: it needed a wall that still listened. [492]
