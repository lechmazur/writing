Dr. Mara Ellison stepped through the warped aluminum doorway of the storm-damaged greenhouse, her boots crunching on shattered glass that glittered like frost beneath the bruised morning light. [29]
The flood had retreated three days ago, leaving behind a humid hush and the sour smell of drowned orchids. [39]
She had come not to salvage the botanical collection but to collect the fine dust that now filmed every leaf and pane—dust that, under her spectrometer, might reveal patterns older than the greenhouse itself. [60]
As a neutron star researcher, Mara was used to listening to the slow heartbeats of collapsed suns, but here she sought quieter fossils: microscopic scales shed from butterfly wings that had once fluttered among the hibiscus. [84]
The butterflies were gone, of course, swept away by the rising river, yet their discarded color still drifted, weightless, waiting for someone who knew how to preprocess reality into data. [107]
She knelt beside a toppled trellis, unscrewed the lid of a sterile vial, and gently swept the silver-gray film into it, watching motes swirl like galaxies in miniature. [130]
Each scale was a diffraction grating, splitting light into spectral memories; accumulated across hundreds of wings, the dust formed a map of gradual change—temperature, humidity, pesticide traces—encoded in the spacing of ridges no wider than a wavelength. [160]
Mara’s motivation was neither fame nor rescue; she wanted only to strip needless drama from the story people would tell about the flood. [183]
No cosmic retribution, no wrathful planet—just slow variables converging: a dam upstream overdue for maintenance, a jet stream stalled by warming poles, larvae hatching a week early because the greenhouse glass had thickened the air. [220]
She labeled the vial “Post-Flood Lepidopteran Layer #1,” then pressed it into the foam slots of her field case next to a lead-lined capsule containing a teaspoon of neutron-star matter. [250]
That exotic sample decayed at a rate fixed by the weak force, shedding energetic electrons that her team on the orbital station would record; the butterfly dust, by contrast, decayed through accumulated dust patterns—erosion, oxidation, forgetting—yet both processes obeyed the same patient arithmetic.
