Kael, a destitute philanthropist who had once funded orphanages and now slept beneath their abandoned eaves, crouched barefoot at the shoreline, turning a corked bottle in his scarred hands. [45]
Inside, a dozen lightning bugs pulsed like bottled constellations, the last salvageable piece of his former life; he had caught them the night the treasury burned, believing their glow might guide him back to purpose. [67]
Above him, the slow dance of celestial bodies—two moons eclipsing in a violet hush—cast twin shadows that braided across the obsidian grains, and Kael felt the universe lean closer to listen. [90]
He uncorked the bottle, releasing the bugs; instead of dispersing, they spiraled upward in a slow helix, orchestrating coincidences with every blink: a gust lifted a scrap of sailcloth at the exact angle to snag on driftwood, forming a lean-to that blocked the acrid wind for a shivering child Kael had not yet noticed. The child, Lira, stepped from behind a basalt boulder, her eyes reflecting the insects’ glow, and asked whether transformation hurt as much as the silence that followed it. [155]

Inside, walls glazed with volcanic glass mirrored their silhouettes, stretching them into giants who merged and separated in vibrant hush, and Kael saw his own face fractured into a mosaic of every person he had ever helped, each shard whispering a single word: texture. [380]
