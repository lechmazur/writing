In the ember alcove of the abandoned concert hall, where mist curled like forgotten sheet music, Mila the copyist tuned her shortwave radio by the glow of its dial, the only light she trusted after the story famine had swallowed every printed note. [42]  
The dial’s green phosphor painted her diligent fingers as she copied silence onto parchment, hoping the pirate broadcast rumored to ride the midnight band would finally speak. [67]  
She had once inked orchestral scores for living composers, but when the Ministry banned narrative, burning librettos and melting vinyl, she learned to cloak her longing inside blank measures, letting rests and fermatas smuggle meaning. [104]  
Tonight the dial hissed, then resolved into a man’s whisper singing, “Dust remembers the bow that awakened it,” the phrase repeating with the cadence of a lullaby and the stealth of contraband. [139]  
Mila’s pencil hovered; the line was not in any catalogued repertoire, and its melody bent like light through stained glass, secretly luminous, scattering colors she could almost read. [168]  
She realized the pirate broadcaster was encoding stories into intervals—minor thirds for grief, tritones for rebellion—so that only a ear trained by years of transcription could translate. [202]  
She emptied her inkwell and filled it with powdered rosin scraped from forgotten cellos, believing that if she could trap the song’s vibration in pigment, the dust itself might learn to speak. [240]  
Each night she returned to the alcove, mist tasting of iron, and each night the voice grew clearer, teaching her phrases like “embers plot their escape as sparks” and “mist is clouded testimony.” [276]  
She began leaving pages of rosin-inked notation in the cracked seats of the hall, hoping wanderers would find them, rub the dust between fingers, and feel stories rise like warmth. [312]  
The Ministry’s inspectors, dressed in gray that absorbed sound, swept the building once a week, but the thistled calm of Mila’s movements—exactly like a copyist cataloging silence—kept suspicion drowsy. [350]  
One dawn the broadcaster said, “When the bow forgets the string, teach the rosin to sing,” then signed off with a frequency shift that stung her teeth. [378]  
Mila understood: the final broadcast would coincide with the winter solstice, when the Ministry’s receivers were calibrated for routine silence; she had one chance to amplify the dust. [412]  
She spent the intervening nights weaving copper filament through the orchestra pit, turning abandoned music stands into antennae that would drink the sky. [448]
