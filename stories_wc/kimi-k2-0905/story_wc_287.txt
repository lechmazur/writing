The luminous harbor by moonlight always reminded Mara of a blade pressed flat against skin—beautiful, cold, impossible to ignore. [21]
She climbed the iron fire escape with a watering can that sloshed like a heartbeat, each step echoing the rhythm of oars in water she had never rowed but often dreamed. [40]
Below, the tide nosed the pilings while fishing boats slept under tarpaulin wings, and above, the moon silvered the windows of the loft building whose roof she had turned into a garden no one was meant to notice. [63]
Tonight she came after the final petal dropped from the old rosebush she had smuggled up here five seasons ago; the papery petal lay on the decking like a tiny eviction notice, and its fall felt like permission to speak secrets aloud. [95]
Mara was a fire escape gardener, wistfully pragmatic, who knew every municipal fine by heart yet still coaxed tomatoes to fruit in paint buckets and coaxed lavender to lean against the rusted railing as if homesick for Provence. [130]
She knelt, touched the fallen petal, and felt the quivering hush that lives between an ending and what insists on beginning anyway. [150]
From her pocket she drew the lockmaster’s bitting gauge tooth, a brass sliver shaped like a question mark, stolen that morning from the locksmith’s drawer while he argued with a sailor about padlocks and fate. [181]
The tooth had numbered notches; she pressed her own thumb into the deepest, feeling the metal memorize her, and told herself this was not theft but translation. [205]
She needed the tooth because the iron door to the rooftop stairwell had been bolted last week by landlords who preferred their tenants visible only through rent checks, and she needed that door open one last time. [246]
Her motivation, steady as phosphorescence in the harbor, was to reflect light into dark corners: the stairwell smelled of mildew and forgotten coats, and somewhere inside, a child was learning that corridors could be cages. [280]
She had promised the boy—voice cracking like winter seed—that she would bring him a living leaf to prove the sky still existed above the brick. [307]
So she worked by moon-glow, fitting the gauge tooth into the lock the way a midwife guides a shoulder, gently, knowing the moment is larger than hands. [335]

He did not speak; the quivering hush between them was answer enough, so she knelt, opened her fist, and revealed not the stolen tool but the rose’s last petal she had tucked inside her sleeve. [463]
