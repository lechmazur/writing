Eira had never asked to become the caretaker of the cursed orchard that clung to the cliffside labyrinth patrolled by mechanical birds, yet the dead leaves had whispered her name one dusk. [32]
She tended the gnarled apple trees with a reluctant heart, knowing that every broken branch released a whisper of the orchard's original curse, a curse that turned wandering travelers into stone statues. [64]
Above her, the mechanical birds-brass kestrels with glass wings-swept the narrow stone paths, their gears grinding a mournful rhythm that warned of danger, but also hinted at a forgotten blueprint for a device that could guide the lost. [102]
The blueprint had been sketched on a crumbling scroll, its ink faded and its purpose hidden in an offhandedly exact notation that only a mind as scattered as Eira's could decipher, yet she had never dared to read it before. [142]
A small nut, dark as a moonless night, had fallen from the oldest tree, and when she picked it up, she felt a pulse that matched the rhythm of the mechanical birds, a pulse that demanded she assemble the pieces of the hidden device. [186]
She laid the nut upon a weathered stone, its surface cold, and began to assemble the delicate brass gears, the brittle copper plates, and the brittle silver wires, each part fitting with an offhandedly exact precision that made the whole mechanism feel like a living heartbeat. [232]
Her method was to leave impressions without touching, tracing ghostly lines in the dust with a feather she’d taken from a fallen mechanical bird, the feather's tip humming as it outlined where the device would rest within the labyrinth's maze. [272]
The night frost threatened to freeze the cliffs before the device could be completed, and the frost would silence the birds, so she hurried, sealing each joint with a thin film of melted pine resin while whispering ancient lullabies learned from her grandmother. [315]
As the last gear clicked into place, the small nut glowed, and the device emitted a soft, amber light that seeped through the labyrinth's stone walls, forming a pattern of luminous footprints that only the lost could see. [353]
Eira watched as a group of shivering travelers stumbled into the cliffside maze, their eyes wide with fear, and she felt a surge of purpose: to guide them safely through the labyrinth before the night frost locked the paths in ice. [394]
She activated the device, and the amber footprints pulsed in rhythm, leading the travelers along a winding route that avoided the sharp cliffs and the patrolling mechanical birds, each step guided by the faint glow of the small nut's light. [434]
The travelers, unaware of the orchard's curse, followed the luminous trail, their breath misting in the cold, and as they reached the exit, they turned back, seeing only a shadowed figure standing among the gnarled trees, her hands still steady despite the graceless beauty of the night. [481]
Eira felt a strange relief, not because the travelers were safe, but because the cursed orchard now had a purpose beyond its own decay, a purpose that turned the mechanical birds into silent guardians rather than threats. [518]
She returned to the weathered stone, the small nut still humming faintly in the device, and she allowed herself a moment to smile, an offhandedly exact smile that betrayed no arrogance, only the quiet acknowledgment that she had finally assembled what the forgotten blueprint had promised. [564]
The night frost began to form, coating the cliffside labyrinth in a thin veil of ice, but the amber footprints glowed brighter, a beacon that warned the mechanical birds to stay away, and the orchard's curse seemed to relax, its grip loosening as the device sang a low, steady hum. [614]
When dawn broke, the frost melted, and the orchard swayed in the gentle wind, its branches heavy with fruit that no longer turned travelers to stone, and Eira knew that the next time a lost soul wandered in, the small nut would again light the way, a reminder that even the most reluctant caretaker can become a guide.
