[23] She stood now on the granite spine of Carrick Isle, watching the retreating tide expose the causeway that appeared only twice daily, a gray ribbon connecting her exile to the drowned mainland. [42] The water wars had re-sculpted every coastline, but this tidal island remained, stubborn as the insomnia that kept her witness to every darkness. [61]

[84] She had bartered three months of dried kelp for it at the floating market, drawn to its preserved stasis, its refusal to decay. [106] Tonight, she would exchange it. [111]

She moved wryly earnest through the morning, speaking aloud to the gulls in a tone that acknowledged her own absurdity while meaning every word. [135] "We're all just cataloging erosion," she told them, "trying to thresh meaning from experience before the tide returns to blur the edges." [158] The island demanded this posture—sincerity armored in dry humor against the vast indifference of the sea. [179]

[208] The water wars had taught survivors to read landscape as grammar, to follow the syntax of erosion toward resources or refuge. [231] These particular trails led to the sinkhole where the recent storms had unearthed something the rising seas had buried decades ago. [255]

The unearthed convergence waited there: not merely the meeting she had arranged, but the exposed cavity itself, a chamber opened by landslide, revealing pre-war artifacts fused into the mud like strange tubers. [287] Elara descended carefully, her boots squelching in the clay that smelled of rust and ancient cellars. [306]

Maren was already waiting, wrapped in oilcloth that shed moisture in steady drips. [323] "You look like hell," Maren said, her voice carrying the haggard awe that had become the common dialect of survivors—exhausted reverence for anything that persisted. [349] "The water gives and the water takes," Elara replied, removing the brooch from her pocket.
