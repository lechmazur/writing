Lyra set her brushes in the amber pavilion as the last oak leaves fell, their burnt sienna color matching the honeyed light that filtered through the glass walls. [28]
She was a vivid mural artist, known for colors that shouted across city blocks, yet she spoke in whispers, quietly driven by questions she never voiced aloud to the empty air. [31]
Spread across the wooden table lay her collection of old postcards, their corners soft from years of handling, each depicting the same cathedral window in different qualities of light, sent by her mother before the illness took her voice, then her breath. [42]
The postcards showed night-blooming cereus and moonflowers captured in stained glass, but Lyra had come to this autumn sanctuary to understand the living flowers that opened only after dark behind the pavilion's garden wall. [34]
Her mother had tended them with devotional precision, claiming they held secrets about endurance, and now Lyra needed to know why any flower would choose to bloom when the world slept and stars were its only witness. [37]

Winter arrived early, dusting the amber pavilion with frost that blurred the glass into something milky and remote, isolating her in a bell of suspended amber. [26]
Lyra worked on her mural through the short days, painting layers of shadow and silver onto the curved walls, but each dawn she walked to the cathedral carrying the postcards, comparing them to the actual etched glass that rose three stories above the nave in pale blue stone. [48]
The sexton, an old man with resin-dark eyes and hands that smelled of beeswax, allowed her entrance during the blue hour, when the world held its breath between night and day and the building seemed to inhale. [37]
Across the melting of winter's last snow, she returned daily, watching the ice retreat from the garden paths as the etched glass slowly yielded its secrets to her patient gaze. [30]

"You look for the hidden layers," he said one morning as the first thaw began, water dripping from the eaves in a rhythm like the counting of rosary beads. [29]
He showed her how the cathedral window was not one pane but three distinct sheets, etched glass laminated with pockets of air between, creating depths that shifted and breathed as the sun rose through the eastern hills. [37]
Through these hidden layers of etched glass, the morning light fractured into spectral roots and invisible stems, revealing that the night-blooming flowers in the design were not facing upward toward the safety of darkness, but eastward, already yearning toward a dawn they could not yet see but trusted completely. Lyra stood in the dawning hush as the truth settled over her like sediment clearing from deep water, leaving the air pristine and resonant. [24]
She began to reckon with the years of misunderstanding, realizing her mother had not been a creature of darkness retreating from the world's brightness, but someone preparing for a light she trusted would come, even if she wouldn't live to stand in its warmth. [44]
The postcards had been maps of faith, not melancholy, each night-blooming flower a compass needle pointing toward morning with absolute conviction. [21]

Back at the amber pavilion, the snow began to melt in earnest across the garden beds, revealing earth that steamed with the scent of waking soil and returning life. [29]
Lyra changed her mural, painting over the somber purples and blacks with golds that pulsed beneath the surface like a second heartbeat, depicting flowers that opened in darkness not to hide from the sun, but to be ready for its first ray. [42]
