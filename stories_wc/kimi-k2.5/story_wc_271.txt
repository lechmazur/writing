[15] He had arrived three years after the final keeper retired, inheriting both the arboretum and the silence that settled over the cliff like salt spray. [42] His fingers, quietly talented at grafting stubborn rootstock to salt-resistant scions, moved through the needles with the precision of a surgeon who had learned to work without anesthesia. [68] The photograph in his pocket crinkled as he bent to check the soil moisture, a somatic marker that tightened his throat before his mind could register the memory. [92] He did not need to unfold it to see the image: his sister laughing beneath paper wedding lanterns, the moment captured fifteen minutes before the sky delivered its mechanical judgment. [117] His hands would freeze then, suspended above the earth, while his heartbeat accelerated into the hollow space between soil and sky. [136] Through these bodily warnings—trembling fingers, shallow breath, the taste of copper—he navigated the topography of his survival. [154] The arboretum itself seemed to understand this language of trauma written in flesh. [167] Each tree had been planted by keepers long dead, their roots clutching the cliff face with the same desperate grip that kept Elias from following the others into the dark water below. [193] He worked here not to forget but to cultivate a pearl of stillness within the irritant of his grief. [253] Elias sat back on his heels among the shore pines, letting the somatic marker run its course through his shoulders and spine. [333] The crumpled photograph pressed against his hip, no longer a wound but a weight that anchored him. [348] He understood now that optimism untethered from reality was merely denial wearing a bright mask. [400] The lighthouse below had no keeper because technology had made human vigilance obsolete, yet the trees required human hands.
