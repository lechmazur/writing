The shrewd seaside cargo broker, Marisol Vega, locked the harbor ledger at dusk and followed the last violin note drifting from the open-air symphony. [28]
She had spent the day haggling over spice crates and sailcloth, but her mind kept circling the locket photo she wore beneath her blouse: her mother smiling beside a younger Marisol, both framed by the same restless tide. [50]

He handed her an envelope addressed to “M. Vega, Wharf 9,” but the ink was smudged, and the letter inside began, “Dear Marta.” [135]
Marisol’s pulse quickened; the mistaken address felt like a tide pulling her toward someone else’s story. [152]
She read the letter aloud to Theo: Marta’s grandson thanked her for years of anonymous tuition grants, enclosed a pressed edelweiss, and promised to “initiate kindness forward.” [178]
Theo’s eyes shone; he confessed he had once been such a silent contributor, shelving books by day and coding shipping schedules by night so dockworkers’ children could study. [205]
Together they traced the envelope’s faint postmark to a side street where an elderly laundress named Marta lived above the machines, her window open to the same star-drunk breeze. [232]
Marta, stooped but bright, recognized the edelweiss and wept; she had mailed dozens of similar letters, never knowing if they arrived. [253]
Marisol felt the locket photo warm against her skin, the image of her mother—who had scrubbed decks to pay for Marisol’s first accounting lessons—seeming to nod in approval. [278]
