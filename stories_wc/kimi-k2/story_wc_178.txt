In the hush between regimes, when the last king had fled and the next had not yet arrived, the ancient Babylonian palace reimagined itself as a labyrinth of dusk and silence. [34]  
Columns that once sang with lapis now hummed with the low bronze of twilight, and every corridor curved like a question mark toward the sky. [47]

Inside the box lay a single magic square tile, fired from riverbed clay and etched with cuneiform numerals that rearranged themselves whenever moonlight touched them. [94]  
The tile had been his mentor’s dying gift, whispered to be “a lock that listens for the right devotion.” [113]  
Naram-Sin mistrusted gifts; he trusted only measurements, yet the tile’s shifting rows and columns aligned nightly with the stars he studied through a cracked quartz lens. [139]  
Between regimes, borders dissolved like salt in water, and rumor said the palace would soon belong to whoever could seal a covenant with the unknown powers that slept beneath its ziggurat heart. [172]  
Naram-Sin desired neither throne nor treasure; he wanted certainty that the heavens would still turn after the next ruler’s name was carved on every brick. [199]  
To gain that certainty, he needed to uncloak the palace’s buried engine, the thing that kept sky and earth in their ancient balance. [222]  
He spread the tile on the cracked malachite floor of the throne room, now stripped of its gold, and began to draw schematic representations of its permutations with charcoal ground from date pits. [256]  
Each square became a map of walls, each diagonal a corridor, each sum a hidden pressure point in the architecture. [278]  
The palace answered with a dusky harmony: wind through empty archways tuned itself to the minor key of his breathing, and torchless sconces glowed ember-soft. [304]  
Night after night he worked, fasting to save oil, sleeping rolled in his coat, waking to realign the tile when the moon slipped a degree. [330]  
On the seventh dusk, the tile stilled; its numbers froze into a pattern that matched the constellation called the Crooked Staff, which no scribe had recorded for three centuries. [361]  
Empirically ironic, Naram-Sin thought, that a sky he had catalogued for thrift should now reveal a vault he had never suspected. [385]
