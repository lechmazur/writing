Every evening after last orders, Lin pushed open the lantern-room hatch of the old lighthouse on Black Skerries and breathed salt air that tasted of wet stone and carbon paper. [36]  Between the third and fourth lens panel he hid a brass biscuit tin inside which twenty-three microdot slivers—each no larger than a gnat’s wing—glowed with the faint silver of undeveloped spycraft specs. [65]  What seemed a hobby was secretly orchestration: every dot carried a line she had typed onto fortune-cookie paper, lines distilled from overheard whispers in the harbour bar and the dreams dropped by strangers who asked only for tea. [98]  Tonight, after wiping the last pint rings from mahogany, she carried the tin down the spiral, her palms remembering the rhythm sailors gave her when they thought her blind to their coded laughter. [140]  
The lighthouse groaned like an elderly monk; wind fingered its ribs while Lin set a single yellow bulb over a developing tray the size of a matchbox. [172]  One dot at a time unfurled its miniature, revealing not troop movements but ordinary lines: “You will find change where the tide does not look.” [198]  She studied them with the frown of a translator convinced the riddle had already happened to her. [220]  Because she baked the slips inside almond crescents sold at the island kiosk, the men who bit into the cookies believed the wisdom was ancient; yet to Lin each phrase was a deliberately accidental prophecy, the only kind people trusted. [255]  
Between exposures she replayed conversations, directing flow—her phrase for steering drinkers toward subjects she needed, steering tides of gossip back toward light. [288]  All winter she had built a lattice of suggestion across town: a hint dropped beside the fuel pump, a postcard left on the pub piano, until the very air carried her refrains. [329]  The system worked because open secrets need witnesses: if everybody already half-knows the rumour, nobody asks who started it. [358]  They merely pass it, the way gulls pass a gust. [373]  
The last microdot image caught her breath: a slip not yet typed that read, “When the last keeper turns the key inland, the sea begins to hum.” [413]  She had not written it; the handwriting trembled like her own, but older. [440]  Cold licked her wrist. [447]  She recalled yesterday’s fortune—her own cookie, torn open at dawn: “Leave before you are asked to stay.” [469]  Prophecy had folded once more, accidentally prophetic, eating its tail. [486]  
Below in the watch room waited an envelope addressed to a mainland postbox she had never used. [511]  Inside rested another spycraft lens, smaller than the rest, meant to record the final transmission. [539]  She realised the flow had reversed; the island itself had begun directing her. [559]  Lin thought of transposing life into a new key, the secret key that opened not outward but inward, where light refracted into colour no chart mapped. [590]  
The beam, usually shuttered at ten, stayed lit. [602]  She slipped the envelope into the pneumatic tube used in wartime to signal ships, then turned the crank until it hissed away. [634]  Wind pushed the door at her back, carrying the smell of iodine and wet paper, familiar enigma smelling like coming home. [663]  At the last stair she set her hand on the bronze knob worn thin by keepers long dead, and felt their pulse mix with hers. [698]  Somewhere beyond reef break she pictured the envelope riding moonlight, carrying the open secret whose question even she had not yet phrased. [730]  
Lin switched off the lamp, closed the hatch, and left the stairs unlit so future footsteps would trust the dark. [752]  Outside, sea level shifted an octave; gulls wheeled, uncertain. [766]  She hummed the new note she had not known she needed, hearing the island join in. [785]  Tomorrow the pub would reopen; tomorrow she might still write fortunes. [801]  But tonight, after last orders, the music belonged not to words she had typed but to tides now teaching her tongue. [834]  She stepped into the corridor between rock and horizon, carried by orchestration greater than any story she believed she began. [869]  And below, the tin—empty, shining—gently cooled like a metronome wound down after a final measure. [900]  Lin smiled, feeling her own fortune unfurl though she had eaten nothing but the silence, the last bite always sweetest when shared in open secret with tide-smoothed dark. [950]  She would be back for dawn, maybe, but the tune had slipped beyond return; the light had changed, and she let it carry her out of count, and out of need.
