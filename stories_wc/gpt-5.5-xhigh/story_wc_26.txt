<story>After dreams become shareable, the blooming library above clouds opened each evening like a patient greenhouse of glass, pollen, and remembered light. [22]
Its balconies rooted in vapor, and its books grew as blossoms whose petals replayed the sleep of anyone brave enough to lend it. [45]
Mara, the sharp-eyed dusk noticer, tended the west windows because only at sunset did the archived dreams cast honest shadows. [65]
She had been hired for her talent of seeing what changed when everyone else was looking at what glowed. [84]
That talent had once saved her brother Nerin, in a shared nightmare that spread through three villages like fever. [103]
It had not saved him later, when hope itself became tradable, edited, and sold in bright little packets. [121]
In his last message, he sent Mara a river stone from their drowned valley and asked her to keep it dark. [142]
For twenty years she carried the stone in her apron, unable to decide whether darkness was a burial or a promise. [163]
Then, on the longest solstice of the library, one dream flower bloomed black among the gold chrysanthemums of childhood. [182]
Its shadow rose from the floor without permission, no longer attached to stem, shelf, or sun. [198]
The catalog called such a thing a shadow unbound, but the old catalog had never sounded afraid before. [216]
Mara watched it slip between aisles, tasting dreams and leaving behind flat scenes where sleepers had once reached for dawn. [236]
The council wanted to burn the black flower and every shelf it had touched. [250]
Their fear was practical; if shareable dreams lost their shadows, people would remember joy but not why they had needed it. [271]
Mara argued in a softly deliberate hush that the shadow was not evil but orphaned. [286]
To understand its grief, she climbed to the attic observatory where obsolete screens slept under quilts of star moss. [305]
There she chose her method, by comparing silent video footage from decades apart, footage gathered before and after the valley drowned. [326]
The first reel showed Nerin at twelve, standing ankle-deep in the original river, holding up a stone veined like blue smoke. [347]
The second showed the same place forty years later, water replaced by white roofs and cloud anchors for the library. [367]
Though both reels were silent, Mara saw at dusk what no microphone could have caught. [382]
Nerin's boy-shadow leaned toward the river, while the modern library-shadow leaned away from the sky. [397]
Between those angles, the unbound shadow had been born, stretched thin by every dream sold without its sorrow. [415]
Mara understood then that Nerin had asked her to keep the river stone dark so one small night would remain unpriced. [436]
She placed it beneath the black flower, and the library dimmed as if all its blossoms were closing their eyes. [456]
The shadow rushed back, wrapping the stone, not attacking but remembering the weight of current, silt, and a brother's palm. [476]
Mara opened her own shareable dream, the one she had never archived, and let the room enter it. [494]
In it, Nerin was alive enough to laugh, and dead enough that the laugh passed through him like sunrise through rain. [515]
The unbound shadow bent over that impossible mercy and began to metamorphose. [527]
It became neither captive nor free, but a hinge of shade linking every bright dream to the wound that made brightness meaningful. [549]
The council watched hope regain its edges, not as cheer, not as commerce, but as a shape that could hold loss. [570]
Mara sealed the new hinge into the catalog, naming her purpose plainly: to preserve the shape of hope. [588]

The books bloomed less brightly, yet their colors deepened, and the air smelled of rain over stone. [626]
Mara kept her post at the west windows, where every evening lowered a ladder of amber through the clouds. [645]
Sometimes she saw Nerin's outline in that ladder, and sometimes only her own, thinned by age and duty. [663]
