<story>During the reconstruction, when scaffold bells rang over the blackened terraces, Grandmother Sava left the ration line and walked uphill to the volcanic hot springs where the old council had once baptized newborns in steam. [35] Everyone knew her as an insurgent grandmother, not because she had carried bombs in the occupation, though she had, but because she still refused any peace that rebuilt obedience under a cleaner roof. [68] At the springs, crews were restoring the Judgment House, a round basalt chamber whose cracked door guarded the village relic, the embryo in amber. [92] For twelve generations, midwives had held infants before that honey colored stone, and the child received an implanted destiny: healer, mason, singer, guard, or exile. [117] Sava did not despise the rite; she despised how habit had replaced choice, and she wanted to evolve tradition with intent before the first reconstructed nursery opened. [144] During the war, a seed flare from a shattered greenhouse shell had entered her ear, and since then she had been accidentally telepathic with plants. [169] Cabbages murmured hunger in the ration gardens, pine roots repeated old troop routes, and the fern mats around the springs whispered whenever hidden metal warmed beneath them. [196] That morning the ferns were chanting a direction, tail, tail, tail, toward a cedar snag above the vents. [214] There Sava found an abandoned kite snagged by its ribs, its paper weathered to scales and its long cloth tail painted with a cryptic script. [239] The script was not the invaders' cipher or the council's prayer marks; it was vine logic, each knot a verb, each stain a season. [263] Sava touched the tail, and the cedar above her thought, Read backward from drought, as all roots do. [281] By decoding a cryptic script on the tail of an abandoned kite, she understood that the relic had never chosen destinies; it had stored them. [306] The old founders, fleeing ash and famine, had sealed possibilities inside the amber embryo and let fear narrow them into assignments. [327] Below, the foreman shouted that the door was ready, and midwives in clean aprons gathered with the first babies of the reconstruction. [349] Sava hid the kite tail beneath her shawl and stepped into the chamber before the council speaker could bless the hinge. [370] Inside, steam pearled on basalt, and the embryo in amber glowed on a tripod of glass like a sleeping yellow moon. [391] The speaker said, "Grandmother, we honor your sacrifices, but reconstruction needs order." [403] Sava looked at the mothers, at the wrapped infants, and at the masons who had lost sons to both armies. [423] "Order without consent is only rubble stacked higher," she said. [433] She laid the kite tail across the door seam, pressed its knots in the backward order the cedar had taught, and sang the seasons named by stains. [460] With each word, the ferns outside tightened their thoughts around the chamber, drawing mineral heat through roots until the ancient resin softened without cracking. [484] The seal gave a sound like ice remembering water, and Sava whispered, "Unseal," as the amber case opened. [502] The embryo did not wake as a monster or a saint; it unfolded a pulse of images into every mind nearby. [523] They saw founders arguing, not commanding, placing dreams in amber so descendants could borrow courage when choices became too many. [543] They saw the first script written on kite tails because children sent questions higher than priests did. [560] They saw no list of healer, mason, singer, guard, or exile, only a circle: learn, serve, refuse, return, begin. [579] The speaker fell to his knees, but Sava remained standing because insurgency had trained her not to mistake revelation for repair. [600] "Then what do we do with the babies?" a midwife asked. [611] "We give them the rite later," Sava said, "when they can answer, and until then we teach them many beginnings." [631] The mothers murmured, frightened by freedom, but one mason lifted his daughter and kissed the soot on her brow. [650] Outside, the ration cabbages suddenly thought of sunlight instead of hunger, and Sava almost laughed at the simplicity of their politics. [671] By dusk, the council wrote a new law on the clean wall, not abolishing the amber, but naming it a witness instead of a master. [696] Sava hung the abandoned kite above the nursery door, its decoded tail fluttering whenever the springs breathed. [713] Years later, children would choose before the embryo in amber, and tradition, at last, would choose to change with them. [733]</story>
