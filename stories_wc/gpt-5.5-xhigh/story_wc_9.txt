<story>After the birth, Lenna could not sleep, because every clock in the house breathed once for her daughter and then fell silent. [22] Her mother called the silence a toll, not a blessing, and tightened the swaddling. [36] Lenna understood that a gift had been taken for a gift, and the missing hours had gone somewhere. [54] To find where lost time accumulates, she sent for Aram Vell, the onyx-ink cartomancer who read maps by darkening their silences. [75] He arrived at sundown in a coat smelling of unearthed rain, carrying cards painted black on both sides. [93] Each card showed its image only when he asked a question he did not know how to mean. [111] "I work best willingly perplexed," Aram said, smiling with the embarrassment of a man before a hidden altar. [129] Lenna nearly sent him away, but the cradle hiccuped, as if the child were aging inward. [145] Aram asked for the oldest birthday tablecloth. [152] In Lenna's family, every birthday ended with a family custom of tracing hands on birthday tablecloths, so the linen held palms of infants, brides, widowers, and ghosts. [179] Her mother brought it from a cedar chest, and Aram asked for the dolls' tea set saucer crack, a dark line splitting porcelain since Lenna was seven. [206] Lenna remembered dropping that saucer at a party, certain she had broken the afternoon itself. [221] Aram laid the cloth on the bed, set the cracked saucer at its center, and bled onyx ink into the fissure. [242] The black line drank, traveled from porcelain to linen, and found Lenna's childhood handprint. [256] Then every traced hand lifted its fingers in the fabric, pointing upward. [268] "Above," Aram said. [271] Her mother crossed herself from habit and whispered that the stairs to the sky had rotted. [287] Aram answered that remorse builds better ladders than wood. [296] Lenna wrapped the baby against her breast, took the tablecloth, and followed him through the orchard into mist. [314] Under a moon thin as a fingernail, Aram found a silver ribbon tied around nothing and asked Lenna to unfasten it. [335] When she did, the orchard fell away, and they rose through vapor so beautiful it seemed to choke the stars. [355] At last they entered the midnight glade above clouds, a meadow of blue grass rooted in thunderheads. [372] The place had a choking beauty, not gentle but pressing, as if all unsaid apologies had learned to bloom. [391] In the glade stood figures made of cloud, each kneeling before a clock with no hands. [407] Some wore crowns, some aprons, some soldiers' coats, and all murmured apologies that condensed into silver pools. [424] "This is cloudbound repentance," Aram said, and his dark cards showed pictures without being touched. [439] On one card Lenna saw herself during labor, begging time to hurry and make the pain end. [456] On another she saw the infant's first cry stretching across the room, a thread cut by the silent clocks. [475] "You did not steal hours," Aram said. [482] "You promised them away without understanding." [488] Lenna wanted to deny it, but the tablecloth stirred, and her old handprints curled like accused children. [505] All her life, at every birthday, she had pressed her hand beside her family's and wished the next year would arrive faster. [527] Those wishes had climbed here, patient and obedient, becoming a hoard of unused minutes guarded by penitent clouds. [545] The baby woke then and rooted for milk with the authority of dawn. [558] Lenna laughed and wept, and the sound loosened something in the kneeling clouds. [571] "How do I take it back?" she asked. [579] Aram turned the cracked saucer over and showed that its fissure matched the crease of her infant daughter's palm. [598] "Not back," he said, dipping Lenna's finger in onyx ink. [608] "Forward, but honestly." [611] He guided her hand to the birthday cloth and traced not Lenna's palm but the baby's, tiny, furious, complete. [630] The cloud figures inhaled, and the silver pools rose as rain inside the glade. [644] Each drop struck the cloth, filling old outlines with moments Lenna had wished away: a grandfather's joke, first snow, her mother's steady hands. [667] The clocks below began to speak through the soles of her feet. [679] When morning found them in the orchard, Lenna's mother stood at the window, younger by one forgiven hour. [697] Lenna kept the cracked saucer on the table, not mended, because the crack taught her that time is not saved by sealing it. [720]</story>
