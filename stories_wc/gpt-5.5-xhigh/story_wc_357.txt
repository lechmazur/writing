On Viridia, the orchard planet riddled with floating rock arches, every tree had learned to cling harder than stone. [19]
Its roots braided cliffs, its branches caught mist, and its fruit ripened even after hail, drought, and the nightly showers of ash. [41]
Mara Venn, an intrepid orchard pilot with sap scars on her knuckles, had spent five seasons hauling grafts between valleys in a canvas wing skiff. [66]
She loved the work, but she loved one question more: why did Viridia rise green again whenever other worlds would die? [87]
She wanted to discover the truth, to find the source of resilience before the Consortium arrived with drills, patents, and a hunger that never pollinated anything. [113]
Her only map was a sliver in a cedar box, a broken arrow tip dug from the oldest root hollow in the northern groves. [137]
The metal was not metal, not exactly, and it carried what botanists call a field that accelerates plant growth, waking seeds before dawn. [160]
The tip also hummed only during inversion, when gravity inverts for an hour and rivers lift from their beds like clear serpents searching the sky. [185]
Most pilots moored their craft then, fearing dropped mountains, upward rain, and ladders suddenly becoming traps. [201]
Mara waited for that whirling silence, the hour when engines sounded far away inside their own housings and every leaf twisted without rustling. [224]
As the sun ringed the arches in copper, she unlashed her skiff and let herself fall upward. [241]
Instead of compass needles, she had jars of blue minnows, ribbons of current weed, and her grandmother's rule for navigating by water flow. [264]
During inversion, water remembered where it wanted to go, pouring skyward along invisible slopes toward springs hidden in rock. [283]
Mara watched the weeds lean, watched the minnows nose against glass, and steered through arch after arch as if listening to a river beneath silence. [308]
The floating stone arches spun slowly around her, each carrying orchards on its back: pear terraces, plum moss, almond towers, and red vines thick as ropes. [334]
Between them rose all the planet's loose water, braiding into shining roads that crossed and split, while loose fruit drifted like lanterns. [356]
The arrow tip pulled against its cedar box, pointing not north but toward a tight knot of streams vanishing through a cracked basalt arch. [380]
Mara followed and entered a hollow where no wind moved, yet every sound she had ever made circled her in pieces. [401]
Her first engine cough, her mother's laugh, the crack of a branch under boot, even the question that had burned her life, all returned as a gathering of echoes. [430]
At the center floated a spring shaped like a clenched fist, its water running nowhere, its rim furred with newborn leaves. [451]
A skeleton crouched beside it, not human, not alien enough to be dismissed, with one hand grown into the roots and one hand empty. [475]
Mara opened the box, and the broken arrow tip flew to that empty palm, completing a small black arrow aimed downward. [496]
The spring unfolded. [499]
Images spilled without noise: ancient orchard keepers fleeing a war in the stars, shooting seeds into this barren planet, and one wounded archer burying a promise in bedrock. [527]
The arrow had not been a weapon here but a root key, and its missing tip had weakened the old pact. [548]

The accelerated growth from the tip was only an emergency shout, a command meant to wake life when catastrophe returned. [595]

Mara could take the key and become rich, or leave it and let the source remain everyone's inheritance. [632]

Soon every valley shared channels, songs, cuttings, and scars, and Viridia grew stronger because no grove was asked to stand alone. [756]
