By dusk, the abandoned carnival ship drifting in a foggy bay looked less like a wreck than a thought no one could finish. [23]
Mara Venn rowed toward it with her brother's oarlocks muffled in wool and an emerald swatch card tied against her wrist. [44]
The card had once sampled fabrics for the ship's masquerade, but now each green square darkened when a stolen name lay near. [66]
She followed it because she was known in every asylum registry as the chimera twin discoverer, which was mostly a polite curse. [88]
Chimera twins were children born with two bodies and one remembered childhood, or one body haunted by a second pulse. [108]
Mara could hear that extra pulse, and years ago she had heard it inside herself, answering to the name Ivo. [128]
Then the carnival ship came for the bay festival, and Ivo vanished from every family story except Mara's. [146]
Freedom followed, cruelly, for doctors stopped calling her divided, while loneliness took the empty chair beside her bed. [164]
She called that feeling unkindled solace, comfort without flame, and swore to find the garden of forgotten names. [182]
The ship accepted her boat with a sigh, its gangplank lowering though no hand touched the ropes. [199]
Paint peeled from carousels bolted to the deck, and fog sat in the ticket booths like patient customers. [217]
A sign above the midway read ADMIT ONE MEMORY, then changed, as memories alter, becoming ADMIT ONE MOTHER. [235]
At the ringmaster's tent, a boy with antlers and a girl's shadow bowed in one body. [251]
He was blissfully aberrant, smiling as if wrongness were a warm coat, and Mara heard two pulses under his ribs. [271]
"You seek the garden," he said in two voices, "but the garden seeks gardeners." [285]
Mara showed the emerald swatch card, and its deepest square glowed like a leaf under moonlit water. [302]
The boy opened his palm, revealing threads knotted from ticket stubs, hair ribbons, and whispered last words. [319]
"Names are held through narrative threads," he said, "so you must tell what no one else can bear to remember." [339]
He tied one thread to Mara's wrist, beside the card, and the midway lurched into motion. [355]
Calliopes wheezed without music, swings lifted empty seats, and each attraction offered a version of Ivo she could recapture. [374]
In the maze he was her brother with cherry pits; in wax, her sister sewing cuffs; in the booth, a fox behind the laundry. [398]
Each version begged to be chosen, because choice would make it true and drown the others. [414]
Mara nearly chose the brother, for grief had worn that shape longest, and trapped freedom whispered that certainty was escape. [434]
But the thread tightened, cutting skin, and she understood the ship's bargain. [446]
It did not steal names by hatred; it gave passengers a single easy story and charged every possible self as fare. [467]
Mara knelt in the sawdust and began telling Ivo whole. [477]
She told of the crib with two breaths, the mirror games, the doctors' cold pencils, the fox, the sister, the brother. [498]
She told how she had enjoyed the silence after losing him, and hated herself for that small borrowed peace. [517]
The carnival listened, and the thread in her wrist split into many bright strands instead of one leash. [535]
They pulled her beneath the deck to a greenhouse made from broken aquarium glass. [549]
There, in wet soil, grew labels on stems, some blank, some bearing names in handwriting that trembled. [566]
The antlered child stood at the gate and offered pruning shears shaped like a question mark. [582]
"Cut one name free, and the ship will sink; cut one story short, and it will sail forever," he said. [602]
Mara found Ivo's stem at the center, grown from three labels braided together. [615]
She did not cut it. [620]
Instead she threaded the emerald card through its leaves, matching every shade of green to one remembered life. [638]
The garden shivered, releasing not Ivo alone but the lost chorus around him, thousands of names rising like gnats. [657]
Above, the ship groaned, no longer carnival or prison, only a vessel remembering its harbor. [672]
When dawn thinned the fog, Mara lay in her rowboat with another pulse beside her own. [688]
She was not cured, not captured, not alone. [696]
Behind her, the empty ship drifted seaward, free at last and still carrying everyone she had saved. [713]
