<story>After the energy beings arrive, the roads around Cinderwell glow faintly, as if every mile has swallowed a blue star. [20] Along one of those roads comes Narek, a drifting roadside poet with dust in his beard, mercy in his jokes, and a frayed coil of rope slung across his shoulder. [50] The rope is famous to soldiers and fugitives alike, because it carried him over the north wall of Red Glass Prison on the night the governors tried to hang every singer. [81] Narek keeps it not as proof of cleverness, but because each snapped fiber scratches his palm like a question he has not earned the right to answer. [108] He wants to discover the texture of time, to learn whether a lost hour feels like ash, skin, rain, or braided hemp. [130] The energy beings, called the Shiners by market children and the Unburning by priests, have asked the city for judgment in the terra-cotta grotto under starlight. [156] Their bodies are tall flames without heat, and their voices make clay jars hum with the memories of water. [175] Since their arrival, wells refill at midnight and lamps burn for free, but every gift comes yoked to an impossible choice. [196] For Cinderwell, the choice is cruelly exact: the Shiners will smooth the rebellion from history, saving the prison guards who died, or leave freedom intact and let the dead remain dead. [227] The council summons Narek because the rebellion began with his escape poem, whispered through drainpipes before anyone saw his face. [247] He goes willingly, though he smiles at the guards and offers to rhyme their names with vegetables, a habit that makes fear stumble. [270] Narek is ethically playful, never mocking pain, only prying at the locks that solemn men call destiny. [287] Inside the grotto, red clay rises in folded shelves, and stars peer through a broken ceiling like patient witnesses. [306] The Shiners hover above a basin where water shows not reflections but near choices: hands unclasping, doors unopened, blades lowered too late. [328] Narek sees Red Glass again, and the rope burns suddenly heavy on his shoulder. [342] That night, seven years earlier, he had climbed first because he carried the list of cells where children were hidden. [362] Below him, Sela the locksmith and Orren the youngest guard fought over the same swinging rope, one desperate to flee, one desperate to stop her falling. [388] Narek cut a strand to free the coil from an iron spike, and the movement sent Orren into darkness while Sela lived to open the city gates. [415] No verdict had ever fitted the shape of that moment. [425] The eldest Shiner lowers a hand of light and says Narek may choose for all, because his act made the fork in time. [448] He asks instead for a game, and the council groans, but the Shiners love rules because rules are bones made visible. [469] Give me one hour, he says, and I will pattern my answer where none of us can pretend it is only mine. [491] He kneels by the basin and begins uncovering layers, scraping the soft terra-cotta floor with a spoon stolen politely from the mayor's supper table. [515] Everyone knows he stole it, because he announced the theft in couplets and promised to return it cleaner. [533] Beneath the top crust lies a paler clay stamped with old boot marks; beneath that, a seam of charcoal from the prison kiln; beneath that, a scatter of seed shells prisoners once chewed during forced labor. [569] At every layer, Narek lays the frayed coil differently: a wall, a ladder, a noose, a question mark, a road. [589] People begin to speak, not because he commands them, but because the patterns make silence feel dishonest. [606] Orren's mother admits her son smuggled bread to prisoners; Sela confesses she pushed him during the struggle; the captain reveals orders to burn the children's cells if no riot came. [636] By uncovering layers, Narek uncovers not innocence, but thickness. [645] Time, he understands, is not a clean ribbon waiting for one noble cut; it is rope, fiber crossing fiber, guilt braided with rescue, cowardice with mercy, hunger with song. [674] He stands and gives the Shiners his answer with a quiet so fierce that even the free lamps dim. [693] Do not erase us, he says; teach us to feel the pull of every strand. [708] Let Orren remain dead, and let his bread remain part of our freedom. [721] Let Sela live, and let her hands remember both gate and shove. [733] Let me keep walking until my poems wear holes through my excuses. [745] The Shiners tremble, tasting the pattern, and agree to take no history, only the city's promise to uncover its buried choices each year in the grotto. [771] When dawn comes, Narek shoulders the lighter rope and finds, at last, that time feels frayed, warm, and held by many hands. [793]</story>
