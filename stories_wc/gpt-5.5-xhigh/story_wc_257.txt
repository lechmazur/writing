After hitting rock bottom, Mara Vale climbed the observatory road with a jeweler's lamp, a tray of black enamel lockets, and the last pinch of her husband's ash sealed in a thimble. [32]
She had been the city's finest mourning jewelry maker, braiding hair and grief into rings, until the fever took Elias and customers began to look like mirrors she could not survive. [63]
The road ended at the timeless observatory, a marble dome older than calendars, where telescopes watched extinct stars and dust never settled unless invited. [87]
Mara came because Elias had once guarded its locked archive, and because his final letter said the dome kept curtains of intention behind the northern lens. [113]
He had described them not as prophecy, but as veils woven from every promise a person nearly kept, visible only to those who still wanted something enough. [140]
Wanting had frightened her for months, yet it returned now as subdued ardor, small and warm as coal under funeral silk. [161]

She followed the numbers through the empty hall to a cabinet where a tattoo stencil on vellum lay between plates of meteor glass. [237]
The stencil showed a thorned vine curling around a human wrist, but its leaves were star charts, each bruise-dark point matching a cemetery in the city below. [264]
Mara remembered drawing that vine for Elias on a rainy birthday, laughing when he said mourning should not end in a clasp, but travel under skin. [290]
Under the vellum waited his unfinished invention: a genuinely artificial sun, no wider than a cabochon, made of gears, phosphorus, and captive dawn. [313]
When she fitted her jeweler's lamp into its cradle, the little sun woke without heat, pouring amber light through the stencil and onto the northern lens. [339]
The observatory did not brighten; it began to illuminate the spaces between things, revealing strings from locket to grave, from promise to fear, from ash to seed. [366]
Then the curtains of intention opened, and Mara saw the people who had bought her mourning pieces: widowers polishing rings, mothers fingering hair brooches, children hiding urn charms beneath shirts. [396]

She understood at last that Elias had not summoned her to resurrect him; he had left a lesson for her to carry down the hill. [485]
