<story>Before they find the bodies, at the hour when the tide turns copper and the gulls fold their wings, Edda Foyle rows Marek across the black chop to the lighthouse prison off a jagged reef. [35] The place has been empty since the crown quit hanging men there, yet its lamp has begun to burn without oil, and three lobster skiffs have vanished from its lee. [65] Everyone onshore wants a priest or a soldier, not Marek, because Marek is the cry wolf boy's son. [83] His father had once cried wolf at harmless mist, then bled saving sheep when the real pack came, and disbelief had outlived him. [106] Marek carries that inheritance like a bell with no clapper, all weight and no sound. [121] In his satchel lies the lattice from the confessional of the prison chapel, a square of dark oak slotted by patient hands. [143] The chapel roof fell when Marek was small, and his father bought the lattice from a wrecker for a penny and a promise. [166] He said a good confession did not forgive a lie; it made a path around it. [182] Marek did not understand until the night his mother whispered through it and her dead husband's answer shivered back. [201] The lattice sustains impossibilities, not loudly, but with the steady courtesy of a chair offered to a ghost. [219] Edda will not dock, so Marek climbs the iron ladder alone while the reef gnashes under him. [236] Above, copperlight repose fills the lantern room and settles on the broken cells as if evening has pardoned them. [255] Then the hush moves. [259] It is not silence, because silence belongs somewhere. [267] This hush slips from stair to corridor, swallowing drip, gull, breath, and even the sea until Marek hears only his heart inventing wolves. [290] He has come for chasing hush, the old island craft of following absence to what fear has hidden. [308] His father taught him before shame soured him. [316] Call danger too soon, the lesson went, and you feed it your name; call it too late, and it feeds on everyone. [338] Marek opens the satchel, holds the lattice to his face, and speaks through the narrow squares. [354] I am Marek Vale, he says, and I will not borrow my father's terror or your mercy. [371] The oak warms in his hands. [377] Lines of dust lift from the floor and hover, each grain pausing where a prisoner's foot once turned. [395] They form a map, then refuse to be a map, curling into loops no architect could have drawn. [413] This prison was built as punishment, but grief has rebuilt it as a labyrinth. [427] Marek remembers his reason for coming, sharper than proof and kinder than revenge: to thread a labyrinth with your own story. [448] A maze tells you where you are lost; a thread tells you that you can return. [464] He begins at the chapel. [469] The door there has stood cracked since the last chaplain fled, and Marek enters through the scuffs on a narrow door left ajar for decades. [494] The scuffs are not scratches but sentences worn by knees, boots, and shackles, all begging to be read sideways. [513] Marek presses the lattice against them and tells his first true thing. [525] When I was ten, I wished my father had died a liar, because then I could hate him cleanly. [544] The door sighs wider. [548] Behind it waits a corridor that should fit inside the chapel wall but stretches downward, damp and green, toward the soundless center. [570] He follows, paying out words. [575] He tells how the village laughed when he warned of cracked moorings, and how the next storm took two boats. [595] He tells how he learned to weigh fear before releasing it, as fishermen weigh anchors before trusting rope. [613] At each confession, the impossible corridor accepts another step. [622] At last he reaches the cistern under the lighthouse, where the hush crouches around a locked grate. [639] Beyond the bars, pale hands float beneath the water, many hands, too many for three skiffs. [655] Marek's throat closes, because this is the moment his father always lost: the instant proof appears but language fails. [674] The hush rises to cover him. [680] He could run back crying bodies, bodies, and become another joke before sunrise. [693] Instead he sets the lattice against the grate and speaks softly enough that only truth can pass. [710] I am afraid, he says, but fear is not a witness unless I give it work. [726] The dust thread, drawn from every corridor, slides through the lattice and stitches itself to the lock. [743] Iron blooms open. [746] Air rushes in, carrying rot, salt, and a small human sound from a ledge above the cistern. [763] One missing skiff boy hangs there alive, lips blue, kept beyond sense by the lighthouse's impossible mercy. [780] Marek lifts him, and together they climb toward Edda's lantern, leaving the thread for dawn. [795] This time, silence follows. [799]</story>
