<story>After the mirror cracks, Mira Venn stopped painting the harbor as if it were already gone. [16]
She had been known for forty years as the nostalgic quay painter, the woman who could make rusted bollards, gull stained ropes, and vanished ships look newly beloved. [44]
Tourists leaned over her shoulder, and widows brought her tea, yet she remained intimately aloof, remembering every birthday and accepting no chair beside any fire. [69]
The cracked mirror above her worktable had belonged to her mother, and its silvered back hid a narrow packet of oilcloth. [90]
She found it only because the storm slammed the shutters at dawn and split the glass from corner to corner. [110]
Inside lay a painted seashell, thumb sized, lacquered with a blue door, a white moon, and two black dots for children. [131]
Mira touched the dots, and salt seemed to move under her fingertips. [143]
She had not opened the packet since she was twelve, when she and Niko Caro wandered beneath the western cliff to escape a market fight. [168]
They entered the coastal cave network at low tide, carrying bread, matches, and the shell Niko had stolen from a shrine boat. [190]
When they tried to return, fear made them choose the slick passage that looked nearest to daylight. [207]
It was the wrong turn, and the wrong turn leads to a hidden oasis, though neither child had words for such mercy then. [230]
Beyond a crawl of black stone, the cave opened around a freshwater pool where ferns drank from a roof hole and the sea breathed far below. [256]
Sunlight lasted there only seven minutes at noon, but during those minutes every droplet became a star. [273]
Niko called it temporary eternity and laughed so hard the pool answered him twice. [287]
Before they left, he painted the shell with pigments scraped from her lunch tin and made her swear not to let greedy men map the place. [313]
Later, after fever took him and the harbor grew hungry for stone, Mira changed the oath into something lonelier: to keep a hidden promise to oneself. [339]
She would protect the oasis until protection became another kind of theft. [351]
The cracked mirror told her that hour had come. [360]
Through the broken reflection she saw not age, but the quay outside, flooded to the warehouse steps after three weeks without clean wells. [383]
People were boiling rain from tarpaulins while masons argued about blasting the cliffs for springs. [398]
If they blasted blindly, the oasis would die, and the town might still thirst. [412]
Mira wrapped the painted seashell in her scarf and went to the council house, where maps hung like lies. [431]
She revealed nothing at first except that she could guide one careful party before the afternoon tide sealed the mouths. [451]
The mayor sent his daughter Lira, a surveyor with a lamp, a rope, and the patience to distrust simple routes. [471]
Mira disliked her immediately, then liked her because Lira did not ask why the old painter's hands shook. [489]
They entered under cormorants and passed chambers ribbed like whale bones, walls wet with echoes of larger water. [507]
Twice the shell's blue door matched a smear of mineral on stone. [519]
At the third fork, a fresh collapse had erased the remembered path, and Lira pointed left, where air smelled safer. [539]
Mira nearly agreed, then noticed a white chip on the shell moon, a flaw she had always mistaken for damage. [559]
It marked the rightward crawl. [564]
They went that way on their bellies, cursing softly, until the rock scraped paint from Mira's shoulder and opened into green light. [586]
The oasis waited, smaller than memory but alive, its pool clear above sand, its ferns silvered by the noon beam. [606]
For seven minutes, Lira forgot her instruments, and Mira forgot to be alone. [619]
When the light passed, Mira knelt by the pool and revealed the truth: there were no spirits guarding it, no family curse, only a frightened girl guarding a dead boy's laughter. [650]
Lira sat beside her without touching, an intimacy Mira could bear. [661]
Together they marked a route that would bring buckets, not dynamite, and gates, not crowds. [676]
Back on the quay, Mira painted the first public map on a warehouse door, placing the shell at its center. [696]
By dusk, neighbors who had never been invited into her room carried water past her easel and called her by her name. [718]
Mira did not answer at once. [724]
She was looking west, where the tide covered the cave mouths, and understanding that some promises remain hidden only until they can save more than memory. [750]</story>
