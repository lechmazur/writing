Mara Venn was the last counter clockwise clock maker on Skerry Black, a rocky island whose cliffs rang like cracked bells. [21]
The trade had begun as defiance: her clocks unwound grief, measured departures backward, and annoyed the priests of the lighthouse. [41]

Every newborn name was scratched into its copper register, beside a day the sea would claim that life. [80]
Mara's own date was tonight, under the slow exhale of low tide. [92]
The cause, according to the register, was simple drowning, which offended her more than death itself. [108]
She was neatly inventive, a woman who labeled screws by moon phase and kept salvaged springs in oyster tins. [127]
If doom wanted spectacle, she thought, it should at least build a better machine. [141]
For ten years she had studied the tower's forbidden mechanisms by repairing the clocks its keepers broke in secret. [160]
Each repair taught her that the lighthouse predicted deaths because it arranged them, signaling reefs to shift and currents to tighten. [181]

Her proof arrived in a sailor's crate: an ancient yoyo of whale ivory, brass pins, and a string that refused knots. [266]
The toy was rumored to have belonged to the first keeper, who had vanished laughing during a moonless ebb. [285]
Through reverse-engineering the puzzle of an ancient yoyo, Mara learned the tower had been copied from a child's lesson in return. [306]
A yoyo descends because it is wound, but rises because falling teaches the string how to climb. [323]
The lighthouse had been doing the opposite, winding every islander downward and calling the result prophecy. [339]
To break it, Mara would initiate a return cycle inside the hunger stone before the tide turned. [356]

Ahead, the mysterious lighthouse breathed fog from every window, although there was no fire within. [409]
