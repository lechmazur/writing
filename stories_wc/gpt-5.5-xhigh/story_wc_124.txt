<story>At dusk, when the city lamps began to blink like tired insects, Oran Vale unlocked his cart and counted the lanterns by touch. [23]
He had been a wistful lantern broker for twenty years, selling flame, oil, and measured darkness to museums that could no longer trust the sun. [48]
Oran was aromatically cautious, keeping his wicks unscented and his gloves sealed, because one careless odor could pull a buried morning from the mind. [72]
He wanted, more than profit, to understand why some smells trigger tears. [84]

Inside, crystal shadows crossed the gravel paths, thrown by moonlight through panes cut from failed telescopes and repaired with lead. [125]

Their living colors were gone, but seed ghosts slept in refrigerated soil, waiting for exactly calibrated lamps. [164]

Oran had grieved her by refusing all fragrances, as if grief could be starved by clean air. [259]
