<story>Nira Vell was called the ascending servant because she belonged to the elevator more than to any kitchen, nursery, or grave little broom closet in the icebound spire above the clouds. [31] She had polished its brass gate since childhood, rising with soup, letters, ashes, and secret errands while storms rolled below like slow white cattle. [55] This morning she carried a velvet box of the late Captain Rovan's effects to Lady Sella on the tenth floor, a duty everyone treated as funereal and Nira treated with affectionate detachment. [87] Grief, in her experience, was a guest who deserved tea but not the best chair. [102] She was warmly inquisitive, the sort of girl who asked why frost grew feathers on the pane and why sorrow made proud people whisper. [126] Her mother had once told her to seek out the joy beneath anguish, not to deny the anguish, but to keep it from becoming landlord of the soul. [154] So, as the elevator climbs ten floors, Nira did what she always did: noticed. [168] On the first floor, the velvet box clicked, though she had tied its ribbon twice. [183] On the second, a thin book slid from a false bottom and landed beside her sensible boot. [200] Nira should have ignored it, as servants survive by seeing dust and not scandals, but the cover bore the airship captain's goggles stamped in tarnished silver. [226] The real goggles lay in the box among medals, a cracked compass, and one glove stiff with sky salt. [245] Nira lifted the book only to return it, which is how every encrypted diary discovery begins when the discoverer is decent and doomed to curiosity. [270] Its pages showed columns of birds, moons, and teacups, nonsense arranged with the discipline of a naval prayer. [288] By the third floor she had fitted the captain's goggles over her cap, because the lenses were not glass but sliced prisms from aurora ice. [313] The elevator's walls were ribbed with windows, and dawn entered through scattered light, broken by rime, brass lattice, and the shudder of ascent. [336] When that light crossed the goggles, the diary's teacups began to unfold into letters, the birds into gaps, the moons into pauses. [358] Nira, who admired practical miracles, read while the fourth floor passed with its locked aviary and sleeping ravens. [376] Captain Rovan had not written of treasure or betrayal, as the spire gossips hoped, but of Lady Sella's laughter before the fever bent it into silence. [402] He had recorded her ridiculous songs to the boiler, the way she forgave broken cups, and the date she first smiled after their child was stillborn. [428] At the fifth floor, Nira's own face in the mirrored gate looked too young to trespass on such tenderness, but the diary had chosen its thief with care. [456] The next cipher appeared only when the lift swayed, scattering sunlight across the left lens like crumbs for a clever sparrow. [477] On the sixth and seventh floors, servants loading coal and linen saw her wearing the dead man's goggles and politely decided not to see. [501] That was loyalty, or fear, or merely the house breathing around its secrets; Nira loved them for all three possibilities. [521] On the eighth floor the diary turned severe, describing the captain's last voyage into a blue blizzard to fetch medicine from the cloud monasteries. [545] He knew the engine would fail, yet he went because Sella had whispered, in delirium, that she wanted one more morning without pain. [568] The ninth floor brought a wash of sun so broad the whole diary opened, and the final page shone gold under the goggles. [591] Rovan had survived long enough to write that the medicine was hidden in his compass, but also that Sella must be told the uglier mercy. [616] He had died not grieving, he claimed, but laughing at a memory of her throwing snowballs at ambassadors and calling diplomacy improved weather. [639] Nira stood between floors, hand on the stop lever, with the compass warm from the box and the captain's absurd courage crowding the cage. [663] If she delivered only relics, Lady Sella would keep mourning a shadow; if she delivered truth, mourning might learn its old ridiculous song. [686] At the tenth floor, the gate folded back, and Sella waited in furs, gray, narrow, and angry at hope for being late. [708] Nira knelt, not like a servant lowering herself, but like someone setting a lamp where a path might start. [727] She placed the goggles, diary, and compass in Sella's hands, then explained the cipher before the medicine, because joy needed evidence as much as cure. [752] Sella did not smile then; people are not bells to be rung on command. [766] But her fingers tightened around the goggles, and Nira, descending at last, heard one cracked note of boiler song follow her down. [788]</story>
