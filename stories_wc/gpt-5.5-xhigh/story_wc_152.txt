In the cobalt observatory in autumn, where rain glazed the blue tiles and leaves clung like rust to the stair, Mara stitched galaxies onto black silk. [25]
The courtiers called her the galaxy-chart embroiderer, as if the title were a toy, and she helped them keep thinking so by wearing mismatched slippers and asking whether comets slept standing up. [57]
Foolish Mara, they laughed, while she knotted the routes of fleets, pilgrim ships, and royal messengers into constellations that only a patient eye could read. [82]
Mara had learned early that wisdom survived longer when it wore bells. [94]
The observatory suited that disguise because every dome held an echoed silence, the kind that returned a whisper thinner and older than it had left. [119]
Scholars came to hear the planets through brass funnels, but Mara listened for unheard stories: the pause in a clerk's report, the missing name in a prayer, the threadbare lie carried by a senator's sleeve. [154]
This was the last week before the coup, though no herald had dared name it. [169]
The queen still slept beneath her amber canopy, the Council of Nine still bowed at supper, and the captain of the western gate still sent Mara sweet pears with notes folded into birds. [202]
Yet the notes had changed. [207]
They no longer spoke of weather or his bad knee, but of supply wagons arriving empty, lantern codes on the river, and officers promoted after midnight. [233]
Mara stitched each warning into the hem of a star field, not as evidence but as possibility. [250]
Her master, old Ilyr, had taught her that maps lied when they chose one path and truth began through adjacent possibilities, through the roads next to roads, the doors beside the locked door, the almosts. [285]
So she made her charts with loose threads tucked close to every star, letting one route become three if the viewer pulled gently enough. [309]
On the night the first frost silvered the orchard, the royal astrologer brought her the flame from the eternal candle. [329]
It burned inside a blue glass cup, untroubled by wind, a small gold tongue stolen from the chapel where kings swore mercy and later forgot. [354]
The astrologer was pale and powdered, with ink on his cuffs and terror under his perfume. [370]
They plan to extinguish the queen's line tomorrow, he said, and lit the edge of Mara's newest chart. [388]
Where the flame touched silk, hidden threads brightened, and the observatory ceiling answered with hundreds of reflected stars. [406]
Mara saw at once why he had risked sacrilege: the candle did not reveal what was secret; it revealed what had been sworn over. [430]
Every conspirator had touched chapel fire while taking office, and now their vows glowed along the silk as red veins. [450]
Nine veins gathered at the palace, but a tenth curved away toward the western gate. [465]
Mara set down her needle. [470]
The captain's pears had not been warnings, then, but bait, unless the red thread meant a forced oath or a borrowed seal. [492]
Adjacent possibilities, she reminded herself, and smiled so widely that the astrologer flinched. [505]
Fetch me a goose, three blind boys, and the worst singer in the kitchen, she told him. [522]
By dawn, the observatory seemed to have surrendered to madness: a goose honked under the transit lens, blind choirboys beat spoons against copper bowls, and a scullion sang murderously off key. [553]
Council spies, hearing the racket, laughed and reported that Mara had cracked. [565]
But the bowls were clocked to the river lanterns, the song matched the captain's folded notes, and the goose tugged a cord whenever the western gate signal flashed in its polished lens. [597]
Mara used the din to synchronize every adjacent possibility on her chart: coup at dawn, coup at noon, false coup to expose loyalists, true queen hidden elsewhere. [624]
When the threads aligned, the tenth vein changed from the gate to the old aviary, where dead ravens were kept for omen study. [647]
There, in a cabinet of black wings, she found the captain alive, gagged, and sealed beneath the astrologer's ring. [666]
The man who had brought the sacred flame had also forged the captain's hand, hoping Mara would accuse the gate and leave the palace tunnel unguarded. [692]
Mara did not shout. [696]
She unwound the red threads from her chart, one by one, and as each oath loosened, a bell rang in the chapel without a hand. [721]
The conspirators, bound by vows they had trusted no one could hear, froze in corridors, bedrooms, and saddle rooms while their own promises named them. [746]
Before sunrise, the queen walked into the observatory wrapped in a hunting cloak, and the captain knelt beside her with blood on his collar. [770]
The coup ended before it began, not with trumpets, but with echoed silence returning every unheard story to the mouth that had buried it. [794]
