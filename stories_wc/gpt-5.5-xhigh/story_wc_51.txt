After the first snowmelt, Maren the fractal weaver descended alone into the bronze grotto under starlight. [16]
The thaw had opened the old mouth in the hillside, and cold water slid over the metal ribs with a sound like distant whales breathing through bone. [43]
Maren carried a loom no wider than her palm, a pouch of black salt, and the brass kerosene lamp burner she had stolen from her father's ruin. [70]
She had come to collect the weights of different burdens, because only weight, measured honestly, could buy a voice back from the grotto. [93]
In summer she mended nets with repeating knots that divided like ferns, but after snowmelt her fractal patterns could catch sorrows before they dissolved. [117]
The elders called the cavern's silence the Shingled hush, for each drip laid another thin scale of quiet over the last. [138]
Speak inside it carelessly, and a grief might calcify around you, clear as amber, until you became boundary-lessly trapped between one step and the next. [163]
Her brother Sol had learned that rule when he entered with the village's flood debt roped to his shoulders and did not return. [186]

She set the lamp burner in the loom's center, poured salt where a wick should be, and waited for starlight to find the brass. [230]
By orienting properly, north point of the burner toward the cut of sky, west edge of the loom toward the sufferer's name, she made pain take form. [257]

Maren understood then that burdens did not obey size, and that a child's secret could outweigh a drowned bell. [377]
When the villagers withdrew, the Shingled hush settled lower, scale upon scale, sealing every sound except the lamp burner's soft tick in the cooling dark. [402]

Sol's voice rose there, not calling her name, but repeating the promise she had forced on him before the flood. [448]
Take what they owe us, she had said, meaning timber, flour, apologies, anything that might repair their father's wasted house. [468]
