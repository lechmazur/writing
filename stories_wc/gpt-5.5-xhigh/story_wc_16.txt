In the lowest chamber of the extinct language archive, Mara Vey kept the last glass ink well beside her pulse, because its ink thickened whenever anyone lied. [27]
She was an arcane linguist, trained to wake dead alphabets by tasting dust, tracing scarred parchment, and humming until stone answered. [48]
Most scholars called her gift dangerous, but her brother Kael had always called it carefully reckless, which felt kinder and more accurate. [70]
For seven months he had not spoken to her, not since she translated their mother's farewell tablet and hid the line naming him heir to a forbidden debt. [98]
Mara had chosen silence to protect him, and silence had taught him only that she thought him weak. [116]
By dawn Kael would ride north with the wardens, Mara would be sealed inside as keeper, and, before paths diverge, she had to restore trust after silence. [143]
The archive had offered a chance when a quake split open Shelf Null, where languages slept after their last dreamer died. [164]
From the crack spilled blue vellum strips tied in sailor knots, each bearing a sentence from Iskarin, the tongue their mother had studied before vanishing. [189]
Mara recognized the script as soon as the ink well brightened like a caught star. [204]
Iskarin was not a language of names, but of vows: every noun leaned on an oath, every verb carried a witness. [225]
Read badly, it bound the reader into whatever bargain the dead had left unfinished. [239]
Read truly, it released symphonies of truth, harmonies that could make even a guarded heart hear what had been meant beneath what was said. [263]
Kael stood at the doorway with travel cloak fastened, one hand on the knife she had given him when they were children. [285]
"You asked for ten minutes," he said, and the ink in the well stayed clear. [300]
"I need twelve," Mara said. [305]
It clouded black. [308]
Despite himself, Kael laughed once, and the sound shook dust from a hundred mute dictionaries. [323]
Cautious exuberance rose in Mara so swiftly that she had to steady both hands on the reading table. [341]
Truth had made him laugh; perhaps truth could do more. [351]
Mara laid the strips between them and explained that their mother's debt was a promise web made to keep a plague word asleep, not money, land, or blood. [379]
Kael's anger returned, sharp and deserved. [385]
"You hid a debt made of language from the only other person who speaks our family's cipher." [402]
"Because the tablet said the first child to answer would be swallowed by the archive," Mara said. [419]
"Then you answered for me," he said. [426]
"Yes," she said, and watched the ink remain bright. [435]

She promised first that she had feared losing him more than she had feared death. [491]
Kael promised, after a softer silence, that he had wanted trust more than rescue. [505]
A strand loosened. [508]
Mara promised she would not ask forgiveness as payment for confession. [519]
Kael promised he would not make pride sound like justice. [529]
Another strand slipped free, and the archive answered with a chord from nowhere, low as whales under ice. [547]
The glass ink well overflowed, not down the table but upward, beads of luminous black arranging themselves into notes above the vellum. [569]
Mara read them with the bright terror of someone stepping onto a bridge she was still building. [586]
Each Iskarin phrase became a tone, each tone drew a memory, and soon the chamber filled with their mother's hidden symphony. [607]
It told how she had trapped the plague word by promising her children would one day choose truth together, not because destiny required them, but because love might. [635]
Kael's face changed as the music entered him. [643]
The anger did not vanish; truth was not erasure. [652]
But it found its place among fear, grief, and the old habit of reaching for Mara when the dark became articulate. [673]
Together they spoke the final vow: no silence would be used again as a mask for care. [690]
