<story>Mara Vale reached Orison after the last ship sails, as the harbor clock called the hour when gulls owned the piers. [21] Behind her, the sea lay black and polished, with lantern wakes closing like stitches pulled through velvet. [38] She carried one trunk, six bottles of ink, and the profession that made innkeepers cross themselves. [54] Mara was a traveler who wrote obituaries for the living. [64] Families hired her when a son entered prison, a wife forgot her own name, or a friend sailed beyond return. [84] Her pages did not declare death; they made explained absences, giving grief a door instead of a wall. [102] She had begun the work after burying nobody, only losing her brother to a monastery whose gate never opened twice. [122] Since then she crossed kingdoms to understand the geography of healing, believing sorrow had climates, rivers, and dangerous passes. [141] Orison's palace rose above the empty docks in lonesome splendor, all blue tiles, green roofs, and windows bright as unshed tears. [162] A footman led her not to a hall but to a shadowed garden in autumn. [177] Maples burned low over marble paths, chrysanthemums rusted in urns, and fountains whispered under coins of yellow leaf. [195] There Empress Seline waited beside a drained pool, holding a single palace carpet weft thread. [210] It was crimson silk, frayed at one end, smelling faintly of cedar, smoke, and crushed thyme. [226] "My daughter Liora is alive," the empress said, "but the court must learn how to miss her." [243] Liora had vanished from a treaty feast, cutting one red strand from the carpet where ten provinces were woven under her chair. [265] The council called it treason because she had been promised to a northern prince, and promises fed armies. [283] Seline asked for an obituary so vivid, so tender, that citizens would stop searching and accept the marriage's failure as mourning. [304] Mara set her ink on a stone bench and asked permission to work by projecting presence, her most intimate method. [324] The empress frowned, then nodded as if granting entry to a room she had locked inside her chest. [342] Mara placed an empty cup on the bench, angled it toward the path, and said, "Let Liora arrive." [360] At first Seline spoke like an inscription, reciting titles, tutors, needlework prizes, and the names of acceptable gods. [378] Mara waited until the fountain filled the silence, then asked what mud looked like on the princess's hem. [396] The question broke the palace polish. [402] Seline remembered Liora kneeling among fever tents after the summer plague, describing each patient with vividly descriptive patience: walnut hands, apricot breath, moon pale scars. [427] She remembered Liora stealing kitchen salt for orphan soup, laughing with sailors, and refusing to step over beggars at the gate. [448] The obituary began to breathe. [453] Mara wrote, "Liora leaves the court the way rain leaves a roof, not absent, but gathered elsewhere for roots." [472] The red thread warmed beneath her palm. [479] Its smoke and thyme scent did not belong to banquet carpets; it belonged to the physic beds bordering the garden wall. [500] Mara followed that scent past sleeping peacocks and black statues to a locked postern tied with the same crimson silk. [520] Seline whispered that the gate opened onto the old plague quarter, sealed by order of the council before the feast. [540] Beyond it, someone had repaired the latch with palace thread, as if stitching city to garden. [556] Mara did not accuse the empress of blindness; grief had always explained absences better than guilt did. [573] She only handed her the unfinished page and said, "If she is dead to you, read this aloud; if not, knock." [594] Seline knocked once, twice, then with the helpless rhythm of a mother at a nursery door. [610] The gate opened to lanterns, cots, and Liora, thinner than rumor, her sleeves rolled above arms mottled by healing bruises. [630] She had hidden there after rescuing plague orphans from ships refused harbor, using the carpet strand as a signal her mother would know. [653] But Seline had seen only scandal, not a map. [662] Mother and daughter stood among camphor smoke and fallen leaves, too proud to embrace until one sick child coughed. [681] Then Liora reached for Seline, and the empress folded around her with a sob that shook jewels loose from her hair. [702] Mara finished the obituary by lantern light, changing only the ending. [713] Liora of Orison, it said, has departed the palace of obedience and survives in the province of mercy. [731] Let every household mourn the woman she was commanded to be, and send blankets to the woman who remains. [750] At dawn, before any ship could return, Mara left by the inland road, carrying a geography newly marked. [768] Healing, she had learned, was not the closing of distance, but the courage to project love across it. [786]</story>
