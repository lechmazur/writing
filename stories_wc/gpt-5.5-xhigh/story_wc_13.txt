<story>Mira Quill, natural history illustrator to the Museum of Small Beasts, waited with her sketch satchel where the rooftop canal system weaving between towers widened into a silver basin. [29]
Below the city, a mountain sized giant slept faceup in the valley, and every afternoon his breath lifted water through the towers for ferries and gardens. [55]
His naps lasted six hours, never more, so all errands above the streets had to be finished across a giant's nap. [76]
Mira's errand was older than commerce: to find the place where echoes are born. [90]
Her brother Niko had vanished searching for it, leaving only his cracked flute and an echo that answered one note late. [111]
Around Mira's neck lay his last gift, an infinity symbol pendant of black glass that the family said sculpts time itself. [132]
She had never believed that, not until Niko's echo whispered from the pendant whenever a canal bell struck an interrupted cadence. [153]
The music never resolved; it leaned toward home, swerved away, and pointed east. [166]
At the third bell, Mira pushed her narrow boat from the basin and let the breath tide carry her between roofs. [187]
She passed cistern orchards, laundry bridges, and schoolchildren who tossed paper fish that flickered like species she had drawn from marshes. [208]
Habit made her sketch them, though the page blurred when Niko's delayed flute note rose from the pendant. [226]
Borrow time, he seemed to say, as if lateness were a key. [238]

She touched the pendant, and the black glass warmed, bending the fall of one drop upward before letting it fall again. [301]

Their beaks snapped into the borrowed gap, missed her boat, and left the grille loose enough for her to squeeze through. [345]
Beyond it, the canal narrowed into a throat of stone where every splash returned as a smaller splash, then a smaller one. [367]

An interrupted cadence had caged them, teaching every echo to expect a home it could not reach. [506]
