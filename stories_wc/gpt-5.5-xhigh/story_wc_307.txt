<story>On the night the youngest moon dissolved, Liora climbed the cedar stair to the floating rock meditation platform, where Master Erem waited in a sycamore hush. [26] He had been a backwards aging researcher once, famous for proving that time could misremember a body, and infamous for letting it misremember his. [50] Each week his beard lost another inch of winter, his hands rounded toward boyhood, and his notebooks grew more urgent. [70] Liora had come for those notebooks, and for the art he promised her, to teach vanishing to appear before the tax riders found her hidden kin. [96] The platform drifted above a black pond, tethered by invisible force to the magnetic grotto under starlight far below. [115] No wind moved, yet the sycamores around the cliff leaned inward, keeping the night quiet as a held breath. [134] "People say the old master weaves truth carefully, but you mostly withhold it," Liora said, closing her fingers over the river stone. [156] Erem smiled with teeth that had grown new and small since harvest. [168] "Then trust the cloth until the pattern arrives," he answered. [178] He had named tonight a delayed revelation, but would not say what had been delayed or who had hidden it. [198] Liora's mother had vanished during the raid on Pale Ford, leaving only a sandal, a smear of ash, and a lullaby cut short. [221] If Erem knew whether Nadi was dead, taken, or saved, he locked the answer behind his young old eyes. [240] He knelt at the pond's edge, thin knees cracking like twigs, and motioned for her to sit facing him. [259] "Breathe through the trance, not into it," he murmured. [268] "Let the water do the remembering." [274] He threw his stone. [278] The lesson came through the patterns in the rings of a pond after a stone is thrown, each circle catching a shard of starlight and bending it toward the grotto. [308] Liora watched ripples cross and recross until they became maps, then faces, then doorways. [322] Through the trance, Erem's voice thinned behind her, and the pond opened onto the night of Pale Ford. [340] She saw tax riders drag torches along the reed roofs while villagers ran for the ferry. [356] She saw Nadi standing not at the ferry, but in the millpond, throwing stone after stone. [372] Every ring hid a fleeing child beneath reflected flame, and every overlap showed a path nobody looking straight could see. [392] Nadi had not disappeared because she was helpless; she had made others vanish first, then stepped last into the unseen road. [413] The ripples deepened, and Liora saw the road descend through cold blue stone into the magnetic grotto under starlight. [432] There, in chambers veined with lodestone, her kin lived by lamps covered at dawn, silent as roots, safe because the world believed them gone. [456] Her mother sat among them, hair silvered, singing the broken lullaby to calm a child Liora recognized as her own brother. [477] Liora lunged toward the water, but Erem caught her wrist. [487] "Why withhold this?" she cried, and the ripples shattered into ordinary moon. [499] Erem's face had grown younger through the trance; his cheeks were smooth, his eyes too large for their grief. [518] "Because if I told you, you would run before you could read the door," he said. [534] "Because the riders followed every mourner, but never a girl practicing patience on a stone above a pond." [552] "And because I am nearly young enough to forget the way." [563] Below, hoofbeats climbed the cedar stair. [569] Fear struck Liora clean awake, yet the last pattern remained under her eyelids, a braid of circles crossing circles. [588] She took Erem's second stone, chose the dark between stars reflected on the pond, and threw. [604] Rings ran outward, met the platform's shadow, and folded it like a cloak. [617] When the riders burst from the trees, they saw only sycamore hush, empty rock, and a pond too still to accuse. [638] Liora did not vanish from herself; she appeared inside the hidden road, holding Erem, now light as a child, by the hand. [660] The grotto drew them down with a gentle pull, and Nadi rose from the lamplit crowd before Liora could speak. [680] Mother and daughter touched foreheads, the delayed revelation complete not as an answer delivered, but as a way learned. [699] Above them, the riders cursed the empty night, while Liora listened to Erem humming her mother's lullaby and understood the mercy of a truth made visible only when someone was ready to see.
