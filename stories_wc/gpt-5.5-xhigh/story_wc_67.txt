In the Vanishing Point Gallery, every corridor narrowed toward a painted horizon, and mourners walked it as if the floor itself were remembering them. [24]
Mara worked there as the city's last charcoal portrait ash scatterer, making likenesses from willow charcoal, cremation dust, and the soot of burned letters. [48]
Her clients came after floods, fevers, and disappearances, offering urns with both hands while asking for one face to remain exact. [69]
By necessity, she weaves truth carefully, trimming hope without cutting it away, because mourners often arrive carrying questions no drawing can answer. [91]
Across her mouth and cheeks she wore a mask made from shattered porcelain shards, each shard from a teacup broken during the first evacuation. [115]
The mask clicked softly when she breathed, a small china storm that made strangers lower their voices. [132]
On the evening the seawall sirens failed, the gallery filled with people carrying bread, oil lamps, damp blankets, and urns. [152]
Director Vale stood beneath the converging ceiling beams and promised the building had survived worse, his palms open like clean pages. [173]
He maintained order through incomplete information, naming the upper rooms dry while not mentioning the cracked cisterns beneath them. [192]
Mara knew only half the danger herself, because Vale had shown her a council notice torn where the tide estimates should have been. [215]
That ignorance was the mirage of safety, shimmering inside every candle flame and every practiced reassurance. [231]
In the west alcove, Mara's mother rocked beside a crate of rescued canvases, one slipper pushing, one hand curled around an empty cup. [254]
She had not spoken since the morning, but her eyes followed Mara with a lucid sorrow that would vanish if named too soon. [277]
Mara wanted to crystallize a fleeting emotion, that last mingling of dread, love, apology, and wonder, before grief melted it flat. [298]
She set a black bowl on her bench, tipped in ash from memorial packets, and used a pestle to powder the brittle charcoal. [321]
The sound drew the room together, grain against stone, a patient thunder fit for people who had nowhere else to go. [342]
Then a boy named Ivo pushed forward with a blue jar and asked whether his sister could be drawn smiling. [362]
He did not ask if the gallery would hold; children trust walls until adults teach them arithmetic. [379]
Mara looked at Vale, who looked toward the locked service door, where water had begun breathing under the sill. [398]
For one second her mask caught Ivo's reflection in twenty broken pieces, and every piece showed the same waiting fear. [418]
She understood then that careful truth could become another porcelain wall, beautiful, bright, and useless against rising water. [436]

Vale whispered that panic would kill them, but Mara answered that a calm lie could drown just as neatly. [474]
