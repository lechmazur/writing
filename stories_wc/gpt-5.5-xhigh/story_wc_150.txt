After twenty years across the western sea, Leora returned to Vey with a passport full of stamps and no prayers left. [21]
The elders called her a diaspora returnee, kindly enough, but she heard the hinge in the phrase, half welcome and half warning. [43]
She answered with warmly curious questions, because curiosity was safer than grief and warmer than the silence she had learned abroad. [64]
Vey itself seemed to be holding its breath around the monastery hill, where workmen were excavating a tomb beneath the ruined chapel. [86]
Under that chapel lay the catacomb orchard, an underground grove where pale figs feed on darkness and tree roots snake between bones. [108]
Children had once carried lanterns there on feast nights, but the passages closed after the war, interrupting every pilgrimage home. [128]
Leora had grown up hearing that Vey was a town of interrupted journeys, its saints buried mid road and its families scattered mid sentence. [152]
Her own father's voyage had ended in a refugee barracks, where he stopped blessing bread and began counting exits. [171]
When he died, she inherited only his disbelief and a sketch of a broken sundial base. [187]
The base, the sketch claimed, had sealed the saint's tomb and could mark the hour when roots opened the oldest gate. [208]
Leora came not for relics or property, but to restore lost faith, if faith could be rebuilt from stone, shade, and witness. [230]
The prior's niece, Sima, led the dig and distrusted miracles, yet she welcomed Leora's foreign tools and patient hands. [249]
Together they descended while the tomb was being excavated, crossing each day's new threshold as carefully as a sentence in another language. [271]
The orchard waited below, neither dead nor alive, with pearly mushrooms on skulls and leaves whispering without wind. [289]
Leora felt enchanted, but cautiously, the way a thirsty traveler distrusts the first green line on the horizon. [307]
At the tomb mouth, workmen found the sundial base in three pieces, its numbers worn, its central socket packed with grave dust. [329]
Sima wanted to lift it out by pulley, catalogue it, and send Leora upstairs before the roof answered with collapse. [349]
But Leora noticed that each cracked numeral was joined to a tiny carved road ending at a different root. [368]
Her father had drawn only the stone, not the roots, because exile teaches children to copy monuments and ignore what feeds them. [390]
Leora asked permission to wander the orchard before anyone moved the fragments, and Sima, after frowning, gave her one lamp. [410]
For three evenings she practiced purposeful wandering, choosing paths not by speed but by questions: whose bones faced east, which roots drank deepest, where cool air moved. [437]
She found pilgrim shells, rusted keys, a child's shoe, and on every object the same unfinished spiral cut with a careful blade. [459]
These were not offerings, she realized, but messages from travelers whose passages had been blocked by soldiers, illness, storms, or shame. [480]
Each had entered the catacomb orchard seeking a way to continue, and each had left a mark on the sundial's hidden map. [502]
On the fourth morning, while chisels loosened the tomb's last clay seal, Leora fitted the base pieces together around a living root. [524]
She did not restore it smooth; she rebuilt it with visible seams, filling gaps with lime, fig ash, and powdered blue glass from Sima's stores. [549]
When the seal broke, a breath rose from the tomb, smelling of rain on old roads. [565]
The root through the base tightened like a wrist taking a pulse, and dust slid from the carved roads. [584]
Sunlight, though no sun reached there, gathered on the rebuilt dial and cast a gnomon's shadow from the empty socket. [604]
It pointed not to an hour but to a narrow passage behind the saint's coffin, where root tips glowed like cautious candles. [626]
Inside lay no treasure, only a stair climbing toward the abandoned north road and a wall scratched with names from every scattering of Vey. [650]
Leora read her grandfather's name, then her father's, carved shakily beside the words, I began, I failed, I ask you to go on. [673]
Her disbelief did not vanish; it loosened, becoming something humbler and more useful, like soil around a stubborn seed. [692]
She understood faith as a journey often interrupted, never guaranteed, but mended by those who return to map the break. [712]
By dusk, townspeople followed her through the orchard, touching bones gently aside and singing the bread blessings her father had abandoned. [733]
Leora carried the lamp, not as proof that all roads opened, but as invitation to rebuild the next closed gate together. [754]
