After the shadow detaches, everyone on Mirrorgreen Isle learns not to look down, because down looks back with the face of whatever was lost. [24]
Nara Vell, a crafty lantern maker, kept looking anyway. [33]
Her own shadow had peeled from her heels the night her husband Arun died, and now it drifted above her like a dark kite, tugging whenever she tried to sleep. [63]
Sleep was dangerous after a detachment, for the loose shadow stole dreams and played them backward until love became accusation. [83]
Nara needed her dreams whole, not for comfort, but to fold grief into origami cranes for Arun's sky burial. [102]
Each crane had to carry one true memory, and a false crease would send his spirit circling forever beneath the roots of the floating islands. [127]
Only the glass basilica on a floating isle above the tea garden could help her, because its bells were made for dream recording. [150]
It rose from the center of the floating island tea garden, all panes and spires, shining like ice that remembered fire. [171]
Pilgrims drank silver leaf tea below it and watched recorded dreams flutter across the basilica walls like fish. [189]
At night, loose shadows gathered in the spilled light. [198]
Nara arrived at dawn with her lantern kit, seven unpolished gems, and a packet of crane paper cut from Arun's funeral robe. [220]
Master Iseo, keeper of the tea garden, blocked the arched bridge with his broom of woven moonreed. [237]
"No recorder will open for you," he said, "while your shadow is loose." [250]
"Then I will make a key," Nara answered, and knelt beside the steaming tea pools. [265]
Lantern making had taught her that light obeyed patient hands, but grief required a crueler patience. [281]
She chose the ruby first and began gem polishing with ash, tea oil, and the corner of her sleeve, turning the rough stone until it caught sunrise. [308]
As she polished, the ruby transformed the light into the color of Arun's laugh, warm and almost unbearable. [326]
Her shadow swooped, trying to smear the shine, and whispered from above her, "He left because you were always shaping fire for strangers." [349]
Nara's hands trembled, yet she polished sapphire, pearl, topaz, emerald, moonstone, and black opal until all seven held dawn. [368]
She set them into a lantern frame shaped like a crane's hollow body, and the frame seemed to transform into an emptiness that could hold a bird. [395]
Iseo stepped aside, warning, "A key is not a cure." [405]
"No," Nara said, "but a crane is not an escape; it is grief given wings." [420]
Inside the basilica, a glass stair bore one warning, "descends while ascending," and every upward step sank Nara through layers of other people's dreams. [444]
A fisherwoman's childhood spilled around her ankles, and a dead prince's blue birthday candles burned along the rail. [462]
At the center hung the recorder: a bell of clear crystal with no clapper, waiting for a dreamer to strike herself against it. [485]
Nara lifted her crane lantern, and the polished gems cast seven doors of colored light across the bell. [503]
Her shadow darted through the black door and became Arun, younger and furious, with rain in his hair from their last quarrel. [525]
"You chose the lantern commission," the false Arun said, "while I climbed fever alone." [539]
The accusation was partly true, and its truth cut brighter than the lie. [552]
Nara nearly let the lantern fall, but then a recorded dream opened in the ruby door: Arun on their wedding morning, folding a napkin into a crooked crane to stop her crying. [584]
"Bad wings," dream Arun had said, laughing, "still know the sky." [595]
Nara set the packet of robe paper on the floor and began to fold. [609]

The shadow shrank as the cranes multiplied, not defeated, but transformed into ink lines along their paper spines. [650]
When the hundredth crane rose from Nara's palm, the basilica flooded with luminous torment, every pane blazing with a sorrow that no longer wanted to bite. [676]
The cranes flew out over the tea garden, carrying Arun's memories into the morning wind. [691]
Nara came down the upward stair lighter than she had entered, though a small crane shaped from shadow followed at her heel. [713]
It was not Arun, and not forgiveness, but when she lifted it inside the lantern, it shone. [730]
