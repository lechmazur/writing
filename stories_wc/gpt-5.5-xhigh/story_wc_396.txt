The lighthouse orchard perched above shifting ice floes had been planted by people who trusted roots more than harbors. [19]
Its apple trees leaned away from the cliff wind, their trunks whitewashed with crushed shell, while the tower behind them kept watch over lanes of blue ice grinding like teeth. [49]
On the morning the floes changed direction, Mara stood beneath the dead foghorn, and the horn gave no tone. [68]
She was the shadow-laced peacekeeper of Last Lantern, dark ribbons moving under her skin whenever anger gathered nearby. [86]
Children thought the shadows were punishments, but elders knew they were agreements she had carried instead of letting them become graves. [107]
Mara had survived because she was calmly intuitive, not because she was quick with a blade. [123]
When a stranger climbed from the ice with blood on his mittens, the orchard pickers reached for pruning hooks, and the lighthouse crew barred the gate. [149]
The stranger was hardly older than sixteen, all elbows and frost, yet he crossed the last ladder unarmed. [167]
Around his neck hung a broken crystal ball fragment, clear at the center and blackened along one jagged edge. [186]
Mara recognized the old oracle glass from stories of the first treaty, when ice navigators and orchard keepers shared weather, lantern oil, and fruit. [210]
The boy held it up and asked, "Will you open the cliffs, or shall my people drown below your light?" [230]
The council captain looked to Mara, waiting for the single word that would become law. [245]
Time tightened then, as prophecy glass sometimes did, until the whole orchard hung during the weighted breath before reply. [264]
Snow paused above the thorn hedges, the captain's mouth stayed half open, and the boy's eyelashes glittered without blinking. [283]
Mara could have answered from habit: defend the orchard, preserve the stores, keep peace by keeping outsiders out. [301]
But peace built from closed gates was only fear wearing a civic sash. [314]
Her duty, as she understood it, was to map empathy onto action before dread chose action for everyone. [332]
The fragment warmed her palm, and within its crack she saw the signboard of Odds and Ends, the vintage store sealed under the lighthouse stairs. [357]
In the still breath, Mara walked there while no one else could move. [370]
The shop smelled of salt wool, brass polish, and apples stored too long, and every wall carried mirrors rescued from wrecked salons and captains' cabins. [395]

A green-framed mirror showed orchard children counting the last barrels of cider, ashamed of hunger and terrified of being called selfish. [439]
A cracked shaving mirror showed floe mothers lashing babies to sleds as seawater climbed through their sleeping tents. [457]
A gilded pier mirror showed the first peacekeeper smashing the oracle ball after a false harvest report cost both peoples their dead. [479]
Then a child's hand mirror, small as a saucer, reflected the boy not as invader or beggar but as a courageous anomaly. [501]
Every older path had taught his clan to raid or flee, yet he had climbed to ask. [518]
Mara understood that courage was not the absence of calculation; it was the refusal to let another person's terror remain abstract. [539]
She tilted the mirrors until their reflected shards circled the fragment, making a sphere of borrowed light above the counter. [559]
Within it lay no perfect future, only a practical sequence: lower the cargo ladders, share lantern heat, ration cider, and let floe pilots chart the ice lanes in return. [588]
It was not mercy alone, and not surrender; it was a bridge with work on both ends. [605]
The breath released. [608]
Mara stood again before the gate, snow falling, crowd muttering, boy shivering with the fragment now dull in his fist. [628]
"Yes," Mara said, "but our yes will have handles." [637]

"Sira, open the cider house and weigh every cup in public." [661]
"Boy, bring your strongest pilots up first, because nobody here knows tonight's ice better than the people it tried to kill." [682]
The captain's face hardened, but Mara's shadows spread across the snow, touching each person's boots like cool hands. [700]
They did not threaten; they remembered. [706]
The pickers lowered their hooks. [711]
The lighthouse crew opened the gate. [717]

Mara carried the reconstituted sphere of reflections to the lantern room, where it would never again belong to one side. [758]
When the dead foghorn stayed silent, no one missed its tone, because below it voices answered one another through the snow. [779]
