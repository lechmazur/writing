After the breach of contract, Nera Voss climbed through the west window of Saint Orison's with her mending bag banging her hip. [22]
Above her, where the roof had burned away fifty winters before, a lush orchard spanning the rafters of an abandoned cathedral swayed in moonlit draft. [47]
Apple ladders grew from choir beams, plum roots drank from gutters, and pear boughs leaned over broken saints as if blessing their own trespass. [71]
Nera was a quartz-eye mender, one eye brown and one polished clear, and cracks in wood, glass, or people flashed for her like lightning under ice. [97]
She had repaired bells, bread ovens, and two marriages by accident, which made villagers trust her tools and mistrust her advice. [118]
That suited her, because her accidental wisdom usually escaped while she was looking for nails. [133]
The Candle Guild had sworn to maintain the cathedral orchard if its fruit fed the parish children, but after three lean harvests they broke the promise, named the rafters rotten, and sold the timber to roof a merchant bathhouse. [172]
At dawn their sawyers would come with writs, axes, and the smugness of paid men. [187]
Under the nave, old keepers argued for dignity in loss, while orchard apprentices, endearingly feisty and dirt-streaked, sharpened pruning knives for war. [209]
Nera thumped her bag on the altar slab and told both sides to stop auditioning for tragedy. [226]
She had come to bridge generational wisdom, though she would have called it borrowing old knees and young elbows before anyone could embroider it. [250]
Her daughter Mira had once tied a scarlet hair ribbon to the eldest pear tree and declared it captain of the sky. [272]
Now her daughter's hair ribbon lay folded in Nera's forgotten side pocket, faded to rust but still stubborn. [290]
Mira herself had gone to clerk for the guild, believing ledgers safer than her mother's patched miracles. [307]
Nera missed her fiercely and blamed no one aloud, which fooled nobody. [319]
The eldest keeper, Palli, said the old pump below the crypt had died, and without lifted water the young trees would fail, proving the guild's accusation by sunrise. [347]
The apprentices said pumps were for museums, not rafters, and suggested ropes, buckets, and dramatic cursing. [363]
Nera peered upward with her quartz eye, saw one silver line of dampness behind a saint's cracked cheek, and grinned. [383]
"The roof remembers rain," she said, then looked embarrassed because it sounded useful. [396]
She emptied the bag and found what honest order had hidden for years: a candle stub, fishhooks, chalk, buttons, a thimble, sealing wax, a cracked comb, and Mira's ribbon. [425]
Using forgotten bag contents, Nera made the children laugh by calling the heap an emergency parliament, then put it to work. [446]
Palli remembered where the crypt channel bent, and a boy named Tem, who could climb like a rumor, carried chalk marks along the beams. [470]
Nera waxed a button into a missing valve cup, bent fishhooks into cotter pins, and used the thimble as a sleeve for a split copper rod. [496]
When the rod still shuddered, she wrapped Mira's ribbon around it, not as sentiment but because love had been woven stronger than current cloth. [520]
The pump coughed once, spat mice, and began to breathe. [530]
Water climbed the hidden pipes in pulses, and every pulse made the rafters creak, not with collapse but with thirst ending. [551]
Palli taught the apprentices to listen for safe wood by knuckle taps; Tem taught Palli his quick harness knot; each looked surprised to need the other. [577]
Nera called these lessons deciduous vows, promises that must shed old leaves before roots can feed new ones, and nobody argued because sap was already dripping on their noses. [606]
By sunrise the sawyers entered beneath a shower of wet leaves shaken loose by apprentices crossing from rafter to rafter. [626]
Mira came with them, ink case in hand, face pale around the mouth when she saw her ribbon binding the pump and her mother binding the crowd. [653]
The guild factor read the charge of neglect, but his voice thinned as baskets descended, heavy with late pears kept alive in the high shade. [678]
Nera handed Mira the damp ribbon and said, "A contract can break, chick, but a vow should compost before it quits." [699]
Mira laughed through tears, the way children do when parents become right by tripping over a better sentence than they deserved. [720]
Then she opened the guild ledger, wrote that breach had voided the sale, and asked Palli and Tem to witness a new maintenance bond. [744]
The factor protested until a pear dropped into his hat, which settled the law more persuasively than Nera could. [763]
So the sawyers ate breakfast instead of rafters, the orchard would persist, and Nera went home lighter by one ribbon but fuller by one daughter. [788]
