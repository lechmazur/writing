In the season when the canyon wind learned to repeat only names of the dead, Mira came home to the towers of bone in a canyon of endless echoes. [29]
She was a copper-veined storyteller, born with greenish lines under her skin that brightened whenever she heard a lie, and she had spent ten years collecting the last scraps of Varu. [60]
Across the death of a language, every village had asked her for the same impossible service: make the vanished speak without pretending they were alive. [85]
Mira was quietly articulate, never wasting a syllable, yet children followed her because her pauses seemed packed with secret weather. [105]
Her return was not for mourning alone; she wanted to translate texture into sound, the old Varu art that made rough stone growl, silk hum, and bread crust crackle like winter grass. [137]
The elders said the art had depended on a flawed diamond rumored to be cursed, a cloudy stone kept beneath the lowest bone tower. [161]
Anyone who held it heard not one echo but every echo, including the future answer to the question that would ruin them. [183]
Mira found the tower guarded by Senn, the last speaker born before the empire outlawed Varu songs and replaced their textured consonants with flat trade vowels. [209]
He had been her teacher, and now his mouth trembled around ordinary greetings as if his own tongue were a loose tooth. [231]
You came too late, he signed on her palm, using the finger code reserved for storms and funerals. [249]
Mira answered by pressing his knuckles to a wall of rib bones, letting him feel how the tower shivered with the canyon's memory. [272]
Below them lay a tunnel of echoes, a natural throat carved by water before the river sank underground. [290]
If the diamond were set at the tunnel's center, Senn believed, it could catch the canyon's repetitions and sort them into the lost grammar. [314]
But the old mechanism had broken, and no hand could adjust the mirrors without being deafened by its verdict. [333]
Mira watched the swifts circling the bone spires at dusk and understood how to harness the echoes through the patterns in a flock of birds in flight. [360]
Varu had always trusted surfaces; a bird's wing, flashing dark or pale, could be a moving letter if the right listener stood still. [383]
At dawn she tied red thread around the diamond's iron setting, climbed the highest tower, and scattered millet across the ledges. [404]
Hundreds of swifts rose and dipped for the seed, their bodies stitching curves above the canyon while Mira spoke the first Varu word for pumice. [429]
The tower answered with a dry rasp, and Senn laughed, a startled sound, because the stone under his hand had just spoken roughness. [452]
Then the curse arrived as prophecy: from the diamond came Mira's own voice asking, What will you sacrifice when no one remains to understand you? [477]
The swifts scattered, and the echoes multiplied the question until even the bones seemed to beg for an answer. [496]
Senn clutched her sleeve, his eyes bright with terror, for he knew the legend: the stone spared only those who gave it a living language. [521]
Mira could have fed it trade speech, empire speech, any sturdy tongue, but the canyon would have turned that theft into endless accusation. [544]
Instead she opened the satchel of field notebooks that held her decade of collected Varu: lullabies, insults, recipes, birth cries, names for rain on sheep wool. [570]
She tore out pages one by one, not as fuel but as seed, reading each line into the diamond while rubbing bark, ash, salt, and cloth between her fingers. [599]
Texture became sound; sound became flock; flock became grammar, and the swifts wheeled tighter, writing in air what paper could never keep. [621]
Senn, hearing the words lifted from captivity, began to speak with her, not fluently, but honestly, his broken Varu making room for hers. [644]
By noon the diamond split along its flaw, releasing a clear note that ran down the tunnel of echoes and returned as many voices. [668]
It did not restore the dead or save Varu from ending; no story worth trusting promised that. [685]
Yet when the last speaker's breath failed that winter, Mira struck bone, silk, bread, and stone, and the canyon answered in textures anyone could learn to hear. [712]
