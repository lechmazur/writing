Mara Venn had spent twenty years as an orchestra pit musician, hidden below bright singers, learning to read meaning in breaths, raised bows, and the in between spaces where silence told the truth. [33]
When the river broke the west embankment and poured through Saint Orfeo's Opera House, she returned not for wages, but because the ghost of Maestro Bell had finally left her a code. [65]
She found it pinned beneath a chair in the flooded opera house lit by flickering lanterns, an antique opera program whose velvet cover had swollen like a dark heart. [94]
Outside, officials shouted from the arcade that pumps were coming at dawn, and that any stray musician still inside would be arrested before the building was condemned. [121]
Mara lifted one hand in reply, but no words crossed the flooded aisle between them. [136]
Her apprenticeship to Bell's music had always been a wordless conversation. [147]
She had grown lately inspired by touches on her sheet music, by ripples that kept time when no shoe moved, and by the conductor's shadow bowing from Box Five. [176]
Tonight, amid the hush of unstruck chords, his pale baton pointed at the program. [190]
The paper should have been ruined, yet she saw water damage creating images too precise for accident: stains made a map of the stage, boxes, trapdoors, and pit. [218]
Between the printed arias, tiny brown islands formed notes, and between the notes lay blank channels that she knew were rests. [239]
Bell had drilled every player to honor rests, saying music entered a room through its absences. [255]
She set the program on her cello case and placed lanterns around it as if arranging a small orchestra. [274]
As each flame flickered, shadows slid through the blank channels, joining stain to stain until a stair appeared under the page. [295]
Mara understood then that the code was not in the ink, but in between spaces, in what the flood had spared. [316]
She had come to unravel the code left by a ghost, and the ghost wanted her to go below the pit. [337]
That was impossible, because the pit was already a black pond, swallowing the old wooden risers where her boots had tapped through thousands of overtures. [362]
Then Bell's baton struck the nearest lantern, not with sound, but with a shiver of light. [378]
The reflection on the water showed a doorway that the real wall did not show. [393]
Mara waded in, her cello slung above her shoulder, and felt for the seam beneath the conductor's stand. [411]
The city men outside began hammering boards across the doors, trying to force her out by fear and echo. [430]

Lantern light followed her only as trembling coins on the ceiling. [525]
