<story>After the procedure, Mara Vale woke with a name printed on her wrist and no sorrow in her chest. [19] The surgeons called it mercy, because mercy was cheaper than prison and quieter than revenge. [34] Mara called it a clean slate, though the slate had been scrubbed with acid and still smelled of burned rooms, forged ledgers, and children turned into statistics. [61] She had been a clean slate seeker with a dirty past, paying to forget the stain but not the habit of choosing. [83] Three days later, hollow as a saint in a glass case, she found the baseball in her coat pocket. [102] It was scuffed, red-stitched, and signed in blue ink by Tomas Venn, Time Traveler, with tomorrow's date under his name. [122] Mara had killed Venn eight years earlier, before he could invent anything deadlier than a better batting glove. [140] The dead were poor correspondents, but excellent creditors. [148] Around the signature, the leather carried tiny reversed newsprint scars, as if the ball had slept inside a press. [167] Mara bought every edition on the corner, spread them across a cafe table, and began the old smuggler's method of reading between newspaper lines. [191] The gutters between columns joined into arrows, the spaces after obituaries became numbers, and the sports fold pointed beneath the city. [212] She disliked miracles that used subscription fonts, yet followed them because disgust was the closest feeling she had left. [231] Her journey began at a sealed subway spur where advertisements for painless conscience removal peeled like old skin. [249] She slipped past the locks by muscle memory, which the procedure had failed to cauterize. [264] The tunnel descended through heat, mineral rain, and a dark so thick her flashlight looked embarrassed. [280] Then the roof opened, and jaded wonder, that exhausted cousin of awe, took her by the throat. [297] Below lay an underground bioluminescent lake, wide as a stadium, glowing turquoise where blind fish stirred like lanterns. [315] Across it floated islands of white fungus, delicately robust, flexing under falling drops but never breaking. [331] From above, they formed a labyrinth of choices: left bridges bright with spores, right bridges dull as ash, loops shaped like questions. [353] On the first island stood a brass pitching machine, green with age, aimed at a black doorway in the far cavern wall. [375] A placard read, "Choose for another, or circle yourself forever." [385] Mara laughed, because after the procedure another person was an abstract, like weather on a planet she would never visit. [405] The baseball warmed in her palm and showed a second inscription, invisible in daylight: Kindle empathy where none remains. [424] Venn's voice crackled from the machine, younger than death and amused by it. [437] "You shot me for a payroll code, Mara," it said, "but I traveled far enough to see who you might become after they emptied you." [462] The doorway opened onto two paths, one leading to stolen fortunes, the other to a ward where victims of the procedure lay numb and smiling. [487] Money could buy Mara an island above, a new face, a gospel of absence. [501] The ward could buy her trouble, gratitude she could not feel, and perhaps a spark she did not deserve. [520] She chose the ward, not nobly, but because Venn had signed tomorrow, and tomorrow was the only witness she trusted. [540] The labyrinth punished the choice by showing every person her crimes had thinned: a teacher coughing in smoke, a bookkeeper blamed for theft, Venn dropping the baseball he had brought to a charity game. [574] None of it hurt at first. [580] That was worse. [583] At the ward's center, children and old smugglers floated in blue-lit pods, their faces peaceful with manufactured vacancy. [601] The machine asked for a heart vector, and Mara understood, with professional annoyance, that a choice could be thrown like a ball. [623] She placed Venn's signed baseball in the pitching cradle, aimed at the lake instead of the exit, and pulled the lever. [644] The ball flew, struck the water, and the bioluminescence rose in rings, not pretty exactly, but stubborn enough to pass for grace. [666] Each ring entered the pods, then the tunnel, then the city pipes, carrying recorded pain like medicine nobody had consented to need. [688] Mara collapsed as the first borrowed sorrow arrived, small and ferocious, a match in a ruined house. [705] When she woke, the pods were opening and people were weeping with the clumsy shame of the newly human. [724] Mara did not become clean. [729] She kept the stain, and for once it pointed forward. [739]</story>
