Upon the shimmer of twilight frost, Mara the mirror maker returned to the ruined orchard district lost in centuries of thick fog. [22]
The apple trees stood as black ladders in the mist, their branches glazed until the whole valley had the tone of glass mountains. [45]
Mara had not come to mourn them; she had come because her dead son's dream had escaped its sleeping jar and was running loose beyond the ditches. [72]
Every night the dream showed people a sunlit harvest, then tore away at waking, leaving their hands full of fog and grief. [94]
If it reached the old canal, it would dissolve into the country, and no one would remember orchards had ever grown there. [116]
Mara, who was firmly grounded even when sorrow pulled at her like weather, unloaded twelve sheet mirrors from her handcart. [136]
Last came the astronomer's broken sextant, wrapped in oilcloth and tied with red thread. [150]
It had belonged to Pell, who once measured stars above the district before the fog erased the sky and blurred every road. [172]
Pell had said a sextant found angles by honoring boundaries, but a mirror found truth by erasing boundaries. [190]
Now both instruments were needed, because the runaway dream had no shape until someone gave it a horizon. [208]
Mara set the mirrors among stumps in a circle, each pane catching another pane, each reflection tunneling deeper into silver cold. [229]
She planted the sextant at the center, though one sight was cracked and the arc was bent like a bitten moon. [250]
To make it useful, she did not repair it exactly; she meant to recreate the lost angle it had once known. [271]
For that she needed witnesses who remembered the old stories of clear air before the fog. [287]
They came reluctantly: kiln girls, ditch diggers, a priest with moss in his beard, and old women carrying lanterns under their coats. [309]
To capture a runaway dream, she said, they must become one waking body through synchronized breathing. [325]
The people laughed at first, because breath was the poorest tool left to them, but Mara raised her hammer and struck a mirror frame. [349]
At once, somewhere among the dead rows, a child's voice answered with Mara's son's laugh. [364]
Her knees weakened, yet she pressed her boots into the frozen dirt and kept her chin level. [381]
She lifted the sextant, breathed in for four beats, and asked them to follow. [395]
When all breathed out, fog flowed from the mirror circle and flattened, no longer wall or road or sky. [414]
Boundaries softened first at the edges of things: bark became cloud, frost became star, the broken sextant became a silver birdbone. [435]
Then the dream appeared, small and bright, darting between reflections with an orchard sun cupped in its hands. [453]
It wore Mara's son's face, but younger, unburied by fever. [463]
Mara almost called his name, which would have given the dream a door through her mouth. [479]
Instead she matched the villagers' inhale, steady and communal, and turned the sextant toward the mirrors. [495]
The cracked sight split the dream into twelve images, not to trap it cruelly, but to show it every road home. [516]
Each villager exhaled, and each mirror answered with a memory: blossoms, ladders, cider steam, bees, boys sleeping beneath carts. [535]
The dream faltered, dazzled by the district it had been carrying alone. [547]
Mara understood then that it had not run from her, but from the jar where she had locked all joy after the funeral. [570]
To catch it, she would have to open, not close. [580]
She whispered that she remembered him, and also the world. [590]
The synchronized breathing deepened, and the mirror circle erased the boundary between loss and offering. [605]
The dream stepped into the sextant's broken sight, not captured as a prisoner, but held as light is held in morning glass. [627]
Fog shuddered outward from the orchard rows. [634]
Where it thinned, trees reappeared in pale ranks, ruined still, but visible, and that was enough for roots. [652]
Mara housed the dream inside her largest mirror, whose surface now held a tiny sun moving behind black branches. [671]
The people did not cheer; in a place shaped by silence, they simply breathed together one last time. [689]
Mara loaded the mirrors at dawn, but left the repaired angle of the broken sextant pointing upward from the soil. [709]
She was still firmly grounded, yet above her the fog had lifted into peaks of glass mountains, and beyond them waited sky. [731]
