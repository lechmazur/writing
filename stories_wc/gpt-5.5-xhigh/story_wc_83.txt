<story>At dawn the floating observatory rode the high storm like a black seedpod, its balloons bruised by lightning and its keel whispering over the cloud sea. [26] Inside, Samir al Dassin listened to the circuit breakers tick and cool, each metal click reminding him of the drones that had found his wedding. [51] He had survived beneath a catering table, one hand locked around his bride's ankle until the smoke took her name away, and afterward the investigators called him a drone strike wedding survivor as if that were an occupation. [89] The title brought him here, because grief had made him patient, mathematics had made him useful, and patience plus mathematics could become a hunt. [113] The observatory's arrays searched not for enemy heat or missile signatures but for where unused potential pools, the slack places in the world where choices gathered before anyone spent them. [143] Governments wanted those pools to predict unrest, markets, fertile ore, and the next war; Samir wanted one pool deep enough to drown the moment before the first bomb fell. [172] On his console lay the geode crystal he had meant to give Lina, plain stone outside, a violet cathedral within, its broken mouth catching emergency light. [198] She had said hidden splendor was the only honest kind, and he had laughed because the wedding band was richer than the rock. [221] Now the band was fused into his palm scars, and the cheap geode was the most precise instrument aboard. [240] The shutdown screen gave him six minutes, a maintenance interval labeled WHILE CIRCUIT BREAKERS COOL, before command regained access and sealed his unauthorized search. [264] Samir opened the crystal clamp, seated the geode against the quantum interferometer, and began the hunt through perturbation theory, applying it to causality itself. [288] Every measured future was too vast to solve directly, so he nudged the equations with tiny imagined disasters and watched where reality rang loudest. [312] A cup knocked over, a missed train, a vendor's shout at a checkpoint: each perturbation sent ripples through the model, and the geode translated them into colors that hurt his teeth. [343] The first maps glowed above battlefields and stock exchanges, predictable reservoirs of fear, but Samir rejected them with a flick that split his thumbnail. [367] He did not need more war, and he did not need profit wearing the perfume of probability. [384] He needed the place where nobody had yet decided whether to forgive, build, flee, confess, revolt, love, or live, because there unused potential pools like rainwater in bomb craters. [413] The hunt narrowed as the observatory drifted east, and through the storm gaps he saw his homeland burn in small orange grammars along the rivers. [438] Then the geode flared with something intangibly tangible, a pressure without weight, as if a hand made of memory had pressed its thumb inside his chest. [464] The pool was not under a capital or a silo but in the abandoned wedding grove, where refugees now slept among chairs melted into white roots. [490] In the model, one child there was about to kick open a charred pantry and find either sacks of grain or the unexploded guidance core of a drone, depending on a thousand nearly equal causes. [525] Command would sell that knowledge as leverage, yet Samir could send a warning if he stripped the observatory's filters and let the raw equation pass through him. [552] The engineers called such exposure unfiltered awakening, a poetic name for seizures, blindness, and permanent empathy with every branching probability nearby. [573] Samir touched the wedding band buried in his scar, remembered Lina choosing a geode because beauty needed a wound to be seen, and disabled the last safeguard. [600] Light burst upward, not bright but intimate, showing him all futures at once: the child laughing over grain, the child vaporized, the refugees saved, the refugees armed, Lina turning toward him beneath falling dust in every impossible version of yes. [640] Pain erased the ceiling, and exhilaration rose bleak and clean inside it, because for the first time since the strike he was not trapped in one aftermath. [667] He sent the warning on every civilian band, then added the perturbation that mattered: a false drone signature above the ridge, enough to draw the child's pursuers away from the pantry. [698] The cooled breakers slammed back, alarms returned, and armed command voices filled the room, but Samir was already elsewhere, bleeding from the nose and smiling at the geode's dark mouth. [728] Far below, a child found grain, and in that small spared hunger Samir felt unused potential spill into the world, cold as ash, sweet as rain, finally tangible enough to hold. [759]</story>
