In the quiet bay in spring, Mira Bell kept watch in the hour she named "while the streetlights daydream": that pearly interval after sunset when lamps glow before the dark needs them. [32] She walked then because windows had not chosen between mirror and mouth, and houses revealed, in small blinks, whether they were alive. [54] Mira called herself, without pride, a creeping normality noticer, the woman who saw a fence lean one finger width each week until a lane forgot its shape. [81] Neighbors mocked her until she marked the schoolhouse crack for forty mornings and the floor gave way on the forty first. [102] After that, people brought her misgivings the way they brought jars to the spring. [116] That April, her misgiving was the Chandle house, a blue saltbox above the tide road that had once hummed around its family. [138] Now it merely stood. [142] Its shutters opened, its chimney smoked, children crossed the kitchen, yet the place felt as empty as a held breath no one dared release. [166] Mira had begun her walks to understand why some houses feel alive, and why others, with the same beams and bread smell, went corpse still. [191] In her coat pocket lay her mother's last tool, a perfumer's orris root shard polished by years of thumbs. [210] It smelled of violets, dust, and locked drawers, and her mother had called it a shard of hush, though Mira had thought that perfume talk. [235] A true perfumer used orris to fix a fragrance, to teach fleeing sweetness where to stay. [251] When she touched the shard to a doorstep, misplaced things shivered in her mind: a thimble under a piano, a wedding spoon in a child's boot, a photograph behind flour tins. [282] Her mother had taught her that a home was not arranged by tidiness but by return, sanctified through displaced objects restored to the stories that needed them. [309] The lesson had sounded inadvertently profound when Mira was nine, because her mother said it while fishing a pepper mill from a chamber pot. [333] At the Chandle gate, the orris root warmed until it nearly burned. [345] Inside, Mistress Chandle whispered that nothing was wrong, which was how wrongness dressed when it wanted supper. [362] Mira asked permission to look for lost things, and the family, relieved to have a practical madness, agreed. [380] She began in the parlor, where a brass compass sat on the mantel though no Chandle had gone farther than the oyster beds. [403] The shard tugged her toward the coal scuttle, and there, beneath black dust, she found a porcelain swan. [421] Mistress Chandle pressed both hands to her mouth. [429] Her dead sister had bought it at the fair and hidden letters in its hollow belly during the winter when taxes closed the harbor. [453] They placed the swan on the sill in spring light, and the parlor creaked like a throat remembering song. [472] Next the orris led Mira upstairs to a nursery trunk full of winter blankets. [486] Under them lay a sea chart, folded around a baby's first curl. [498] Old Captain Chandle, who slept by the stove now, began to cry before she opened it. [514] He had hidden the chart when his son drowned, ashamed that the boy had followed his routes, and the house had swallowed the shame like a nail. [541] Mira carried the chart to the kitchen table, not to accuse him, but to let the tide lines lie among bread crumbs again. [564] As the family touched the curl, the ceiling beams crackled with a hushed blaze, and Mira understood that warmth could return without forgiving everything. [588] The last tug came from the yard, where hyacinths grew around a rain barrel. [602] Mira knelt in mud and lifted out a house key tied to a red ribbon. [617] No one claimed it until the youngest girl, Nell, said she had thrown it there after hearing her parents discuss selling the house. [640] She thought a house without a key could not be given away. [652] Her logic pierced them all, and because it was childish it became law. [665] Mistress Chandle set the key on a nail beside the door, where every departure would see it and every return would answer. [687] Mira touched the orris shard to the threshold and asked, softly, to sanctify what had been merely standing. [705] The Chandle house inhaled. [709] Not loudly, not like magic in a theater, but as laundry inhales wind, as soup takes salt, as a sleeping dog knows its name. [733] By then the bay had gone dark, and the streetlights had stopped daydreaming and begun their work. [750]
