On the echoing terrace in spring, after rain had washed the hill city clean, Liora waited with the other children, their whispers floating over the hush before bells ring. [29]
Beyond them, the prism parapet after rain broke the westering light into strips of amber, violet, and green that trembled across the council's seven mirrors. [54]
In one of those mirrors, her little brother Jory had no reflection; if it remained empty at first bell, the healers would end the spell that kept him breathing. [83]
He lived by a borrowed heartbeat, half of Liora's own pulse carried to the infirmary through a silver thread knotted around her wrist. [106]
If the thread was cut, she would be whole again, but Jory would become a name whispered into soup and pillows. [127]
The council called this mercy, and its mirrors agreed, because they had been trained for generations to show only the safest ending. [149]
Then a man in a rain-faded coat stepped from behind the wet orange trees, carrying a flat case painted with cloudless skies. [171]
Every child knew the tales of Sel Varren, the elusive mirage painter, who could paint a door on fog and walk through it by noon. [196]
He was smaller than the tales, gray at the temples, and perceptively calm, as if listening to thoughts before people dared make them. [219]
When Liora hid her wrist, he looked not at the thread but at her face. [234]
"You sing the second verse too quickly," he said, though she had not opened her mouth. [250]
The children drew nearer, for everyone had been practicing the same harmless tune about sparrows, cradles, and the moon. [269]
Only Liora's grandmother had told her the truth: old painters once taught mirrors through coded lullabies in children's songs, hiding angles inside rhymes so tyrants would hear nonsense. [297]
Sel opened his case and revealed brushes made of horsehair, glass, and one feather from a swallow that had survived winter. [318]
No paint lay there, only shallow bowls to catch the colors falling from the parapet. [333]
"I cannot change a mirror," he told Liora, "but I travel to persuade mirrors to show possibilities, if someone lends me a beat that has loved more than one future." [363]
Liora thought of Jory, pale under quilts, and of the selfish relief that sometimes rose in her when the shared pulse hurt. [385]
Sel saw that too, and his calm did not accuse her; it made room for the ugly thought to pass. [405]
"Then grace the glass," he said. [411]
"Do not beg it for Jory's life; offer it every life he might choose." [425]
Liora nodded, and when Sel laid two fingers on the silver thread, her chest stumbled as if a small bird had flown out. [448]
Across the terrace, the children began the sparrow song, soft at first, their voices joining the gutters' drip and the far cooing of doves. [472]
To the waiting councilors it sounded like bedtime, but Liora heard numbers in the rocking syllables, blue to green, third mirror, turn. [494]
Sel brushed the wet air above the bowls with impossible care, gathering color without touching it. [510]
Light slid from his brush to the nearest mirror, and the mirror shivered like a pond remembering wind. [528]
For one breath Liora saw Jory dying, which was what the council expected, and the loaned half of her heartbeat snapped painfully in her wrist. [553]
Then the song turned, and another Jory appeared, older, leaning over engine plans; another sold apples; another taught deaf children to read lips. [576]
There were failures among them, and griefs, but none were empty. [587]
The seven mirrors took up the visions, reflecting not verdicts but branching roads, until the terrace filled with a dusky harmony of colored rain and humming children. [614]
A councilor shouted for silence, but the first bell still hung unstruck in the tower, fat with its own bronze breath. [635]
Sel's hand trembled at last. [640]
"Choose," he whispered, "not for the mirror, but for yourself." [650]
Liora expected to choose sacrifice or rescue, but grace had a quieter shape. [663]
She loosened the knot, not severing the thread, only giving it slack, and her heartbeat found Jory's as a companion rather than a debt. [687]
The mirror brightened, and in the infirmary below, someone cried out that the boy was breathing on his own. [706]
When the bells finally rang, Sel Varren was gone, leaving only a wet brush and fifty children singing possibilities into the spring dusk. [729]
