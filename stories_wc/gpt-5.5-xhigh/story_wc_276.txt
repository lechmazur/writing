<story>Neris was a cool-headed fisher, which meant she could watch a storm eat three boats and still tie the fourth man's wound before she cried. [25] In Glasshook village, that steadiness was treated like a lantern, borrowed whenever fear made hands useless. [41] Yet Neris had come alone to the sound wave garden at midnight, carrying a rusted rail spike wrapped in kelp and linen. [63] The garden filled an old amphitheater below the cliffs, where stone beds grew reeds of glass and every breeze crossed them as visible music. [87] Notes traveled over the soil in silver ripples, broke against shell walls, and returned as colors that climbed the air. [107] This was the week the almanacs named when patterns break themselves, when knots slipped open, tides forgot their covenants, and sleepwalking constellations wandered from their mapped roads. [134] During such nights, the echo of Luma, the drowned goddess of hearing, rose like a fish no hook could hold. [154] Neris meant to catch the echo of a goddess before dawn, not for glory, but because Glasshook's children had been born hearing only half the world. [180] They saw lips move, gulls wheel, mothers clap, and nets splash, but the softer half, the warning half, never reached them. [201] Her brother Tavin had been one of them, and the sea had taken him because he could not hear the cliff bell. [223] Since then Neris had trained her gift until it hurt, for she transliterates emotions the way scribes transliterate sacred names. [243] Fear became hooked marks in her ledger, anger a row of black teeth, tenderness a small blue vowel. [261] When frightened parents brought children to her, she read the shapes around their throats and taught them which wave to trust. [282] But Tavin's last silence had never become a mark she understood. [293] Old Mother Orris, keeper of the garden, had told her the goddess could be taken only by voicing silence. [312] Do not sing, she had warned; do not pray; let the unsaid stand up inside your mouth. [329] Neris had nodded, cool as tide glass, though she had no practice letting anything inside her stand. [346] She planted the rail spike at the garden's center, where seven paths of humming gravel met. [362] The spike had come from the drowned railway, a line once meant to bind coast to capital, and Tavin had used it as a pretend sword. [388] Iron remembered routes, Orris said, so Neris tied her net to it and let the mesh drink the old direction. [408] Above her, the sleepwalking constellations leaned down, huge and pale, dragging star feet through cloud like animals searching for beds. [428] Their broken wandering stirred the reeds, and the sound wave garden woke into a feral lullaby. [444] It crooned, snarled, hushed itself, then crooned again, rocking the shells until moonlit water crawled from basin to basin. [463] Neris followed the flowing notes in a zigzag, because a straight chase would teach the echo how to flee. [482] At each turn she opened her ledger, not to command the garden, but to understand what it felt. [500] Its joy wrote itself as green spirals, its hunger as red hooks, and beneath both lay a blank so bright her eyes watered. [523] The blank was not absence. [528] It was Tavin's face at thirteen, turned toward the bell he could not hear, trusting her to tell him when to run. [550] She had seen the storm sign and chosen to save a basket of cod first, because cod fed families and brothers always waited. [573] The garden's lullaby sharpened, and every glass reed bent toward her shame. [585] Neris could have translated that shame into neat script, folded it away, and remained the cool-headed fisher everyone needed. [604] Instead she dropped the ledger into the wet gravel and gripped the rail spike until rust opened her palm. [623] She opened her mouth. [627] No apology came, no prayer, no song, only the shape of Tavin's name held without sound. [643] By voicing silence, she gave the garden the one language her gift had failed to master. [659] The rail spike rang once, deep under hearing, and the net leaped from the ground as if full of silver birds. [680] In its knots trembled Luma's echo, not a voice but the memory of all voices listening for one another. [699] It was too large to keep. [705] Neris laughed once, wild and small, and cut the net where it bit deepest. [719] The echo poured through the zigzag paths, entered the glass reeds, and broke into a thousand soft warnings. [737] Below the cliffs, sleeping children stirred as if someone had set warm shells against their ears. [753] At dawn Glasshook heard gulls, bells, mothers, nets, grief, and the careful breathing between wave and wave. [770] Neris walked home bleeding, empty handed, and finally less empty. [780]</story>
