After tomorrow disappears, the city learns to wake without promises. [10]
The clocks still strike, the bakers still flour their sleeves, and the harbor gulls still quarrel over clean silver fish. [30]
Only the calendars have changed, each page turning from today directly into yesterday, leaving a blank hinge where hope used to rest. [52]
In that hinge stands the tidal atrium, a glass-roofed library whose marble floor accepts the sea twice daily and returns it sparkling. [74]
Its guardian is Iro, a titan-gear librarian, small inside an iron exoskeleton built for lifting drowned shelves and moon-heavy archives. [94]
Iro's work has always been ordinary: oil the gears, dry the folios, stamp returns, and shelve the stories people borrow to survive. [116]
He loves that order fiercely, because his secret vocation is to preserve wonder in routine. [131]
Since tomorrow vanished, however, wonder has become dangerous. [139]
Citizens arrive at the atrium in procession, carrying letters addressed to names they wore last week and cannot recognize today. [159]
Their migrating identities pass from body to body like winter light crossing windows, and the exchange fluctuates with moon phases. [179]
Under the new moon, a cobbler may remember being queen; under the full, a child may speak with a widower's grief. [200]
At first Iro files these changes as curiosities, noting them on blue cards and tying each card with tide-safe cord. [220]
Then Old Venn, the last harbor fisherman, brings him a fisherman's bamboo creel, lacquered black by salt and age. [239]
Inside are not fish but little brass name-scales, each warm and trembling, bitten with symbols no catalog can hold. [258]
"I caught them where tomorrow sank," Venn says, "and they are too many for my hands." [274]
That evening, the moon is half, and so is everyone. [284]
A judge enters laughing with a thief's wrists; a bride asks for the grave of a grandfather who has not been born. [306]
The atrium tide rises through the grates, lifting paper boats children had folded from overdue notices. [322]
Iro understands that if the names keep migrating unchecked, the city will become a crowd of borrowed fragments, unable even to mourn. [344]
He searches the forbidden metallurgy shelves, because before the library held books, it held bells. [359]
The oldest manual says lost futures can be smelt into present anchors, but only by accepting contradictions. [376]
Iro lowers the creel into his gear-carried arms and descends to the furnace chamber beneath the tidal atrium. [394]
There the sea breathes through basalt vents, and wintersun serenity fills the cold room with pale gold instead of warmth. [414]
One by one, he pours the brass name-scales into a crucible shaped like an open book. [430]
They resist melting, each insisting it is singular, each singing a different birthday, crime, lullaby, and death. [447]
Iro nearly fails, for he has spent his life sorting things apart: fiction from ledger, wet from dry, silence from speech. [468]
Then he thinks of his own contradiction, a frail man trusted with titan strength, a clerk who tends tides, an adult who still gasps when dust becomes sunlight. [496]
He opens the furnace wider and answers every clashing song, "Yes." [507]
Yes, the bride remembers an unborn grandfather; yes, the judge has stolen bread; yes, tomorrow is gone and breakfast matters. [527]
The brass softens. [530]
It does not become one name, but many names braided into a metal clear as honey. [546]
By dawn, Iro has smelt seven thin library cards, each punched with a moon-shaped hole. [561]
When citizens touch them, their migrating identities do not stop, but begin to travel with manners. [577]
A grief knocks before entering; a childhood leaves its shoes by the door; a king's pride must share tea with a washerwoman's patience. [600]
The city is not cured, and tomorrow does not return. [610]
Yet routine resumes with altered grace. [616]
Bakers knead dough while remembering storms they never sailed; schoolchildren recite multiplication in voices old and new; Venn lends his empty creel to collect rain. [641]
Iro records every change, not as damage but as circulation, like tide through the atrium. [656]
On full moons he shelves memoirs beside prophecies, and on dark moons he files apologies under maps. [673]
No one knows who they will partly be by supper, but they learn to ask one another gently. [691]
Each morning, after the clocks strike and the gulls quarrel, Iro polishes his titan gears until they reflect a small, impossible sunrise. [713]
Then he opens the doors to the sea-bright library, and the people come in quietly, ready to borrow themselves again. [733]
