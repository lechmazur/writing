After the reef dies, the lagoon becomes a plain of white ribs, and the armies leave their rusted guns standing like scarecrows in the salt. [25]
Only one road remains above the tides, a handwide causeway ending at the blooming jetty at twilight, where purple bindweed opens between the planks as if the day has bled gently instead of ending. [59]
Along it comes Orven Pell, the gloom-hill coachman, with two gray ponies and a hearse carriage emptied of coffins. [78]
He has driven the dead up Gloom Hill for thirty years, learning patience from slow wheels and grief from every house. [99]
In the war, both flags hired him, because a grave cart was the only vehicle no sniper wanted to shame. [119]
Now his black coat smells not of funerals but of the collection of dried seed pods tied beneath the seat in linen sacks. [142]
They are poor treasures: sea bean, widow pea, marsh fennel, bitter lupin, and the red trumpet vine whose flowers once covered the barracks before fire covered them. [169]
Orven brought them to let a garden reclaim a battlefield, not because flowers forgive, but because roots can hold what men keep losing. [192]
Under his collar hangs a copper disk stamped with the words of the submerged concord, an old pact made when the first islanders promised the reef that no victory would outlive its nursery. [225]
The pact is written in Pelagic Keth, Luro, and Vey, three dead languages that Orven speaks better than the living speech of officers. [248]
He learned them from grave stones rescued from drowned chapels, sounding each mark aloud until widows laughed and then, hearing their ancestors answer in the cadence, stopped laughing. [276]
According to the concord, if the reef died through human quarrel, the quarrelers owed the shallows a season of feeding and planting. [298]
The generals had signed a truce, but neither would step onto the old killing ground first, afraid the other side would call it surrender. [322]
Orven, who had carried sons from both camps in the same carriage, considered that fear smaller than a seed. [341]
At the jetty, Captain Sella of the north and Sergeant Maro of the south wait with rifles held low, each guarding half the twilight. [365]
Between them stretches the narrow vastness: a single wet track leading across miles of exposed reef, shell holes, shattered keelbones, and pools where the sky trembles like trapped silver. [394]
Orven lifts his empty hands, then points to the sacks. [404]
"I have come to fertilize," he says, and the word sounds more dangerous than attack. [419]
Sella says the ground is poisoned, Maro says it is sacred, and both mean that no boot should disturb the dead. [440]
Orven answers in Keth, the funeral tongue shared by their grandmothers, and the vowels roll softly as oars in fog. [460]
He tells them the dead are already disturbed, baked white under gulls, and that memory without green shade becomes another weapon. [481]
Then the birds begin to argue. [487]
Terns stitch the air above the causeway, sandpipers click from the pools, and a lone curlew gives three falling notes from the muzzle of a cannon. [513]
Years of dawn burials taught Orven that gulls cry differently over rot, oil, eggs, and fresh water, and he has listened hard enough to understand bird calls as a second scripture. [544]
He can fertilize the reef only by understanding bird calls, because the birds know where guano has sweetened cracks, where mines leak bitter metal, and where rain has gathered under the coral crust. [577]
Following the curlew, Orven drives onto the track, and to everyone's shock the ponies place their hooves only on stones ringed by tiny green algae. [602]
Sella and Maro follow, not together at first, but with the identical caution of men who have buried too many mistakes. [623]
At each pool, Orven translates the cries: here, kelp and ash; here, pony dung; here, crushed pod; here, nothing but silence, for a shell sleeps below. [649]
The soldiers, ashamed by the old man kneeling where they had once charged, begin passing sacks, breaking clods, and scattering meal made from fish bones. [674]
Twilight deepens until the jetty flowers glow like embers behind them and the battlefield ahead seems both endless and no wider than Orven's palm. [698]
When the last seed pod splits, the curlew lands between the two officers and picks calmly at the damp earth, accepting their tribute without salute. [723]
Orven speaks the closing line of the submerged concord in all three dead tongues, and even those who understand none of them lower their rifles. [748]
By morning, the first roots will enter the bones of the reef, not to hide the war, but to hold it still long enough for peace to grow through it. [778]
