<story>After the hydraulics fail, the sea gates of Orison City remain half open, and the whole delta learns to leave in one night. [23] Families climb the west road with mattresses on their backs, while roofs, orchards, and graves sink behind them like dark bread in broth. [46] Mara Vale stays at the starlit signal station above the drowned rail yard, because leaving would make her father's last map useless. [68] People call her the patchwork navigator, first for the coat she sews from sailcloth, uniforms, and school banners, then for the routes she pieces from rumor. [94] She accepts the name because every important country she knows is made of scraps: safe roofs, bad bridges, kitchens where no one cooks anymore. [118] In the evacuation camps, a fever for reverse migration begins as soon as the first winter passes. [135] Mothers want wedding rings from mud filled houses, sons want bones from chapel crypts, and mayors want proof that taxes still have addresses. [158] Mara wants something poorer and truer: to navigate the geography of loss without pretending loss is a road with an end. [179] The problem is that the delta itself lies. [187] Its channels shift under silt, its drowned street signs turn in currents, and its remaining dry hours fluctuate with moon phases so sharply that a full moon can grant a boulevard and a new moon can take a church. [226] One black midnight, while patching the dead telegraph line, Mara finds the old station phone ringing from a wire that should have ended in saltwater. [250] The voice on it is her father's, thin with distance, though he drowned opening Gate Seven on the night of flight. [271] He does not say hello; he says, Bring me the lion that carried smoke, and hang it where the lantern has no heel. [294] Then the line dies, leaving only the raw solace of having heard him and the cruelty of having heard too little. [315] After that, Mara learns to build through cryptic phone calls at midnight, never twice in the same voice, and never with mercy enough to explain. [340] A woman who sounds like Mara's aunt says, Count the steps under Saint Ivo when the moon is a bitten coin. [361] A child whispers, Do not trust north if the gulls are sleeping inland. [374] An old captain coughs coordinates that lead Mara to the flooded vestry of Saint Ivo, where incense once sweetened funerals during the cough plague. [398] There, beneath a fallen shelf of hymnals, she finds a patina bronze censer foot shaped like a crouching lion, green as bruised mint. [421] Her father had repaired censers before he repaired gates, and his initials are cut into the lion's belly with the same impatient knife. [444] Mara carries it back against her ribs, angry that a thing so small can weigh more than any survivor's bundle. [464] The next midnight, her father tells her, Not a relic, a foot; let grief stand on something. [481] So she studies the silent lantern tower, where the original lift pistons hang useless and dry, and understands at last what the voices have been making her build. [509] Not a lighthouse, but a moon clock: a hand turned signal whose counterweight is the censer foot, whose dial maps exposed hours across the drowned avenues. [535] For three months she works under stars, filing teeth into scrap gears, sewing maps into canvas panels, and testing each phase against the lion's slow swing. [561] When camp elders demand a route for the spring reverse migration, Mara shows them the station wall, crowded with black thread and white pins. [585] Some weep because their homes are marked as water forever, and some because, for the first time, forever has edges. [605] On the appointed night, the people descend with empty sacks, prayer tins, and shovels polished bright from being held too hard. [626] Above them, Mara turns the crank until the bronze lion rises and falls, and the starlit signal station answers the moon in pulses. [649] Its beam does not promise safety; it admits danger honestly, drawing a trembling path over roofs, canal rails, and the square where Gate Seven failed. [674] At the gate, the field phone in Mara's coat rings, though no wire follows her there. [690] Her father's voice says, Leave me under the wheelhouse, little patch, and lead them past. [705] She wants to dive, to claw, to spend every remaining breath proving love by disobedience. [720] Instead she plants a white pin on the map, turns her back on the churned water, and guides the living homeward only as far as grief allows. [747] By dawn, people return with jars of soil, a violin neck, a child's blue cup, and silence that no longer wanders loose. [769] Mara hangs the wet map in the signal station and sleeps beneath it, not healed, but found. [787]</story>
