<story>Beyond the murmur of lost languages, when names survived mostly as gestures and scars, Ila came up from the salt marsh carrying reeds under one arm. [26]
She was a marsh-reed sculptor, known in the drowned villages for braiding hollow stems into birds that whistled when grief breathed through them. [49]
The road ended at a crystalline orchard perched at the edge of a shimmering crater, where glass-fruited trees chimed in the night wind. [72]
Ila had come there not for trade, though her pack held slender eels of woven reed, but to graft oneself onto community, as the old marsh saying put it. [101]
In Orchard-Crater, kinship was literal: families joined their dead to rootstock, and every child learned which crystal bough hummed with which ancestor. [123]
Ila had no ancestor tree here, no aunt's laugh caught in quartz, no cousin's handprint warming a path after rain. [143]
Because longing made her brave, and because loneliness had not made her small, she greeted strangers with a boldly sympathetic face. [164]
That was why Mira, the orchard's teen bellkeeper, trusted Ila with the rumor before the council did. [181]
"A tag appeared on the inner crater wall," Mira said, "the one signed by Nox, the street artist nobody ever catches." [202]
For years, Nox's marks had surfaced where people were most divided: on ration gates, clinic doors, even the mayor's empty fountain. [223]
Each tag, a knot of black lines around a bright gap, became a meeting point, and arguments softened beside it. [243]
The new mark, Mira whispered, had been painted near the abandoned pilgrim stair that descended toward the crater's silver vapor. [263]
The council wanted it copied for archives, but Mira wanted a shared search for an elusive street artist's tag, before wind-glitter erased it. [286]
If everyone climbed together, she said, perhaps Nox would reveal what the bright gap meant. [301]
Ila recognized the hunger under Mira's plan: not curiosity, but the need to weave the orchard's quarrelsome households into webs of kinship. [323]
So Ila offered her reeds as trail flags, and, more daringly, offered to shape whatever they found into a public shrine. [344]
At dawn, forty people gathered beneath boughs of ice-green fruit: growers, glasscutters, crater miners, two widows who had not spoken since a boundary trial. [368]
Ila tied reed whistles to their belts, so no one could vanish quietly into mist. [383]
As they descended the pilgrim stair, the crater below shimmered like an eye remembering light, and the orchard above rang farewell. [404]
The search became slower than any of them expected, because Nox had hidden small black knots on broken steps, each pointing to another. [427]
People who disliked each other had to kneel shoulder to shoulder, comparing shadows, sharing water, arguing, apologizing, and laughing when Ila's reed birds squeaked insults in the draft. [455]
At the seventh turn, one widow slipped, and the other caught her by the sleeve without thinking. [472]
Their hands stayed clasped after the danger passed. [480]
Near dusk they found the true tag, not on the wall but on a soot-dark slab standing upright in a cleft. [501]
Black lines interlaced around a leaf-shaped absence, and within that absence the crater's glow poured through, making emptiness seem tender. [521]
Beneath it, in fresh chalk, Nox had written in the common glyph code: Build where loss can enter. [539]
The slab was obsidian, volcanic glass from the crater's first wound, and its surface glimmered with reflected orchard stars. [558]
Ila touched it and understood that Nox had not given them an answer, but a scaffold. [574]
All night they hauled the slab upward with belts, ladders, jokes, curses, and the stubborn rhythm of reed whistles. [593]
In the orchard plaza, Ila began the work, setting the obsidian upright and bending marsh reeds around it in living arcs. [614]
Growers brought cuttings from ancestor trees, miners brought crater wire, glasscutters brought splinters that caught dawn and broke it into soft rainbows. [636]
Even the widows brought one root between them, cradled in both their palms. [649]
For three days, Ila wove, not pretending her marsh blood had always belonged there, but leaving spaces for difference to breathe. [670]
The structure rose as a glimmering obsidian arbor, dark at the heart, bright along every joint where reed, crystal, and root crossed. [692]
On its lintel, Mira carved Nox's gap-sign, and below it Ila carved no name, only open hands. [709]
When the orchard gathered, the council asked whom the arbor honored: the unknown artist, the dead in the roots, or the newcomer who made it. [734]
Ila answered with a luminescent ache in her voice: "All of us who need a place for absence, and all of us willing to hold it together." [761]
Then she asked to dedicate it not as a monument, but as a threshold: whoever entered alone would be answered by bells, roots, and neighbors. [786]
For the first time, Ila heard the orchard answer through her own reeds. [799]</story>
