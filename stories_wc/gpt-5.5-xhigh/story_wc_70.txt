<story>After the city-wide blackout, the lightning collection tower stood above Harrow like a glass reed, drinking stormlight that never reached the streets. [22] At its base, in the hour the old painters called nectar dusk, Mara weighed toll coins beside a lantern with no flame. [44] She was the tower's candid toll keeper, not by virtue but by accident, because a strike through the booth had made her involuntarily truthful. [68] Ask her the weather, and she gave the pressure, the danger, and the ache in her bones. [85] Ask if the council would save them, and her mouth said, Not unless saving us serves their dinner, before mercy could catch it. [108] People came anyway, because Mara made the gate feel less like a tax and more like a small rite. [127] She asked each traveler to touch the cold rail and listen for the tower's buried hum. [143] To preserve wonder in routine was her private rebellion against dark windows and rationed bread. [158] That evening, the queue was thinner than fear should have allowed, and the western sky gleamed thick as warmed honey. [178] No electric bees circled the tower flowers, no copper wings flashed from the municipal hives on the roofs. [196] The official notice blamed clouds, but the silence sounded to Mara like drone beehive failure. [211] The bees had always carried pollen, weather readings, and pinches of charge from blossom to cable, joining gardens to lamps. [231] Without them, the tower gathered lightning like a miser clasping gold, unable to spend it. [246] Near sundown, a barefoot boy in a bee veil reached Mara's booth and set down no coin. [263] I have payment, he said, and offered a matchbox, its cardboard softened by rain. [277] Mara meant to refuse, but truth shoved past habit: That is worth more than silver if it came from your grandfather. [298] The boy's eyes widened, for he had not told her old Lio, first keeper of the drone bees, was dead. [318] Inside the matchbox lay one black match and a strip of waxed paper printed with a cryptic riddle. [336] Burn me not; feed me the toll of wings; open the box that remembers rain; let the queen taste thunder. [356] Mara read it aloud because mysteries were the only lies her mouth permitted, and the line of citizens leaned closer. [376] The lacquer box beneath her counter had held toll coins for decades, black sides painted with tiny gold raindrops. [395] She had polished it every dawn, loving how an ordinary fee vanished into something beautiful. [410] Now she tipped it over, and the coins spilled like dull seeds while a hidden panel slid free. [428] A brass comb key fell out, followed by Lio's ledger, damp at the edges and blunt with accusation. [446] Mara wanted to spare the crowd, but when Widow Pell asked what it said, she could only reveal the truth. [466] The blackout was no act of weather; the council had ordered cheaper nectar cells, starving the queen engine until every drone hive locked. [489] The tower still brimmed with charge, but no bee remained brave or powered enough to carry it downward. [507] The riddle meant the tolls were not money at all, not originally. [519] Each coin's touch on the rail had once calibrated a wing, a citizen's pulse teaching the swarm the city's need. [539] Mara looked at the dark lanes below, at children who had forgotten the sound of refrigerators and fountains. [557] Then she set the black match against the rail, not to strike flame, but to bridge the comb key's teeth. [577] The tower answered with a blue note so sweet the crowd smelled clover through the ozone. [593] Far above, one drone bee twitched, then another, until the hives rustled like dry leaves remembering spring. [610] Mara opened the gate without asking payment, and for the first time no painful truth corrected her. [627] This is still the toll, she told them, and it was true, only changed. [641] Bring your lamps, your dead batteries, your wedding rings, anything that has touched your daily life. [657] One by one they climbed, offering ordinary metal to the rail while the waking bees drank their measures and flew. [677] At the tower crown, the queen engine opened a glass eye and tasted thunder from the storm. [694] Light moved below them house by house, not sudden, but golden and patient, like honey finding its level. [712] Mara could not promise the council would confess, so she promised to make them, and the city laughed because everyone believed her. [734] When dusk deepened from nectar to night, routine returned as wonder: a toll, a touch, a swarm carrying truth into light. [755]</story>
