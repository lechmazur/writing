After the plague ended, the city remained under twenty feet of patient green water, and the living moved uphill to argue about it. [23] They named their camp New Mercy, which was a lie, because mercy had not survived the coughing any better than the docks. [45] I stayed below, in the old storm sewer where brick arches still held, with eels for neighbors and dry ledgers for company. [67] The children called me a wannabe philosopher living in a sewer, and I accepted half the charge. [84] Philosopher was generous; sewer was exact. [90] My name was Rusk, though names mattered less than methods after the plague, when every survivor discovered a talent for prophecy. [111] The bell-ringers said the sea had judged us for vanity; the surgeons said miasma had risen from gutters; the new council said sewers bred treason. [136] Competing narratives, all tidy as coffins, were being nailed over the sunken city ruins before anyone had measured a single depth. [157] I disliked nonsense with an abrasive calm that made officials itch. [168] A rationally rebellious man does not throw stones at power; he weighs them, records their grain, and asks who quarried them. [189] That was why I climbed nightly through drowned avenues with a sounding line, wax tablets, and a hook hammered from a bed frame. [212] I meant to preserve ancient methods: counting breaths, reading tide scars, boiling instruments, balancing accounts by hand, distrusting any miracle too convenient. [234] The ancient methods had kept ferries crossing, wells clean, and bodies counted until panic taught people to prefer hymns. [253] One spring tide, my hook struck brass in the silt below the drowned Admiralty steps. [268] I scraped downward through sedimentary layers: plague ash, roof tile, fish bones, market straw, and the fine black mud of the first flood. [291] From that black mud came a seafarer's chronometer cog, no larger than my thumbnail, crusted with gray salt and stamped with the western fleet's mark. [316] A cog is not much unless you know old clocks, and I knew them badly but stubbornly. [333] For three days I let myself agonize over it beside my sewer lamp, turning it with tweezers made for pulling plague splinters from skin. [357] At last its tooth count matched a repair sketch in a dry Admiralty ledger. [371] The sketch and gray salts placed a western relief chronometer beyond the quarantine chain at low tide, before the council's proclaimed storm surge. [394] The official plague story said we were abandoned because we were already damned and contagious. [409] The cog said ships came, waited, and left while the high gates stayed shut. [423] Only people in the hill citadel could have ordered that, and their grandchildren now sold morality by the ladle in New Mercy. [445] I considered dropping the cog into a drain. [453] Facts are corrosive, and our poor camp was already more rust than iron. [466] Yet preserving methods without preserving what they reveal is embalming a knife and refusing to cut bread. [483] So I carried the cog uphill in my cheek, because council guards searched pockets but not insults. [500] At the assembly, three factions shouted under a sailcloth roof while rain tapped time on their tin bowls. [518] The bell-ringers wanted a shrine over the drowned cathedral, the surgeons wanted a furnace, and the council wanted my sewer sealed before memory leaked out. [543] I spat the cog into my palm and explained, calmly enough to be rude, how brass, tide tables, and layered mud contradicted them all. [567] I did not accuse the citadel descendants of murder; I demonstrated murder, which they found less polite. [584] They called me sewer rat, blasphemer, clock-licker, and, once again, wannabe philosopher. [596] I thanked them for improving their accuracy by one word. [606] Then old Mara, who had lost two sons behind those gates, asked whether my methods could also tell when the next fever water would foul the wells. [633] They could, I said, if people stopped worshiping explanations and started cleaning sluices. [646] Silence passed through the tent like a careful boat. [655] By dusk, even the bell-ringers were arguing over measurements, which is the first honest prayer. [670] We returned to the sunken avenues with ropes, baskets, and grudges. [681] I taught them to read the city through sedimentary layers, not as guilt or innocence, but as sequence, cause, and repair. [702] The chronometer cog became our school bell, struck against a pipe before each lesson. [716] If that sounds symbolic, forgive the weakness; a man living in a sewer must allow himself one ornament. [734]
