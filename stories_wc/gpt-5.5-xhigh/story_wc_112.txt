<story>At Auriga Nine, the xenobiology research station clung to a cliff of blue ice above an atmosphere full of sleeping wings. [21] The migratory creatures below were huge and transparent, more sail than animal, and when they folded themselves at dusk the whole sky seemed to hold its breath. [48] Mara Venn, the station's obliging sky mechanic, listened for that breathless pause better than any biologist. [64] She tuned sampling kites, thawed jammed airlocks, carried tea to midnight dissections, and never refused a repair if something important might learn to live. [88] Yet her kindness had a secret spine: she meant to prove that time moves sideways. [103] Her brother Jalen had vanished from a storm skimmer three years earlier, though his distress beacon still arrived each evening, clear and frightened, from different directions at once. [131] The directors called it echo decay, a common grief among machines left in clouds. [145] Mara called it a door. [150] Her chance came at twilight, when the station's lowest aviary cooled into glassy silence and the winged colonies entered their nightly torpor. [172] Within the hush of beating wings at rest, a magnetic nexus at twilight formed between the creatures, the ice, and the old skimmer mast bolted under the cliff. [200] The instruments showed a knot of force no textbook predicted, cold as starlight but mechanically warm in its steady pulse. [220] Mara had rebuilt the mast around a prism engine whose instructions were not written in code but through holographic principles. [240] Every bolt held several possible positions, and every reflected image suggested a different angle of now. [256] If the nexus accepted the engine, Jalen's signal might not be an echo behind her, but a neighbor beside her. [276] Director Sovan found her in the mast chamber with frost silvering her lashes and the prism cases open like captured moons. [297] He was a careful man, made harder by responsibility, and he carried a tranquilizer rifle meant for panicked specimens. [316] The colonies were due to wake in twenty minutes, and a false pulse could drop hundreds of fragile bodies into the freezing updraft. [339] Mara told him the creatures were already teaching them the safest way. [351] They did not wake all at once, as station alarms assumed; they practiced subdued awakenings, one membrane shivering, then another, until the flock became a thought before it became motion. [381] Time, she said, might do the same. [388] Sovan wanted proof that did not risk the station. [397] Mara lifted a small cryo case from her tool sling. [407] Inside rested three translucent larvae rescued from failed incubators, each declared dead because its heart had stopped in sequence, never together. [428] Under the prism engine, their still bodies glimmered with delayed colors. [439] If he killed the experiment, they would remain tidy specimens in a freezer, and Jalen would remain a superstition. [458] Sovan lowered the rifle, not because he believed her, but because an obliging mechanic had spent years saving everyone else's work before asking one impossible favor. [484] Mara took the lever with both hands. [491] It was not a switch so much as a question, brass rimed in frost, connected to the prism engine and the mast beyond the hull. [516] When she drew it down, the station lights dimmed to an icy warmth, blue edges around amber hearts. [534] The nexus answered. [537] No thunder came; only a sideways pressure, like remembering a room she had never entered. [552] Holograms unfolded from every ice crystal, layering the mast chamber with duplicate Maras: one younger, one gray haired, one kneeling beside a recovered skimmer. [576] Sovan cursed, but quietly, as if loud fear might tear the images. [588] In the cryo case, the larvae breathed in turn. [597] First one heart flickered, then the second, then the third, not returning from death but arriving from beside it. [616] Their subdued awakenings sent a low vibration through the chamber, and outside the whole sleeping colony shivered without rising. [635] Then the distress beacon spoke from a hologram that stood waist deep in the floor. [650] Jalen's voice was older by an hour and younger by grief, asking Mara to trim the skimmer's left fin before the storm swallowed its tail. [675] Beside the voice, a diagram of tomorrow's maintenance log appeared, marked with her own handwriting. [690] Proof did not flare; it settled, precise and tender. [699] Mara understood she could not pull Jalen out by force without ripping the sideways seam wider. [715] She could only serve the path, as she had always served fragile machines and stranger lives. [731] She recorded the fin repair, sealed the larvae into a live incubator, and eased the lever back before the colony woke. [752] At dawn, a battered skimmer dragged itself onto the cliff pad, left fin bright with a repair nobody had made yet. [773] Mara met it in the cold light, holding her brother carefully, while behind them thousands of wings opened like quiet clocks. [794]</story>
