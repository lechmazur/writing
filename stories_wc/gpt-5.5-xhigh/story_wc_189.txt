<story>Mara, the tidal-pool chronicler of Nares, had a habit of greeting each stranded crab as if it were a neighbor with news. [22]
That warmly observant habit made children trail her at low tide, but tonight only her brother Senn stood with her in the ancient grotto under stars. [48]
The roof had fallen centuries ago, so constellations now held court over its black pools. [63]
Between Mara and Senn, on a basalt ledge slick with spray, rested their mother's jade cicada, its folded wings carved thin enough to tremble when currents changed. [90]
Their difficult conversation had reached the worst place and stopped. [100]
Senn had said, "If your pools truly speak ahead, why did they not warn you about Father's boat?" [118]
The words hung like a gull refusing to land. [127]
Mara did not answer at once, because the pause itself was one of the transition periods she had spent her life studying. [149]
She studied such edges: ebb to flood, egg to larva, grief to blame, blame to whatever could follow if two people survived it. [172]
Since Father's drowning, she had wanted to discover the texture of time: did it tear like kelp, click like shell, or soften like sand? [196]
Her teacher had written a maxim inside her first ledger: Foresee through tangential approach; never face the wave, watch what the wave startles. [219]
So across the pause in that difficult conversation, Mara turned slightly aside to foresee. [233]
She did not look at Senn's anger, or at the main channel where moonlit water breathed. [249]
She looked at the smaller signs that did not know they were signs. [262]
In the pool by her left foot, the sand fleas had climbed onto a dry rib of stone although the tide was still falling. [286]
A red anemone, usually bold as a festival mouth, had shut itself around nothing. [300]
The two reflected stars nearest its rim stretched eastward, though the sea wind slid from the west. [317]
Most tellingly, a thread of freshwater coiled along the salt surface without mixing, cold from underground rain that had fallen three days inland. [340]
Mara picked up the jade cicada. [346]
Their mother had let it skate across washbasins, but after the funeral Mara found measuring grooves under its belly. [365]
It was not magic, Mother had said once; it was politeness toward forces too shy to announce themselves directly. [384]
Mara set it on the side pool, because futures rarely entered by the front door if they could seep under a sill. [406]
The carved insect drifted, hesitated, then turned against the apparent current. [417]
Its wings tapped three times, like a living cicada buried in summer bark. [430]
Senn's eyes flicked to it despite himself. [437]
Mara felt the pause deepen rather than break. [445]
Three taps meant the buried spring below the village had found a new throat. [459]
The inward flood at dawn would press seawater into that throat, lift the clay beneath the lower houses, and unbutton the cliff from below. [483]
She saw it then, not as prophets saw burning doors, but as a chronicler reading margins: water in lower rooms, the west stair cracking, Senn carrying Mistress Ra, ledgers floating like fish. [515]
She also saw a reachable version in which bells rang early, children climbed to goat terraces, nets were cut loose, and the village emptied with curses instead of screams. [544]
The difference between the two futures lay inside this pause, inside whether she defended herself or made a bridge for Senn to cross. [567]
Mara closed her hand around the jade cicada until its edges bit her palm. [581]
"I missed Father's danger because I was looking where I wanted mercy to be," she would say when speech returned. [601]
"Tonight I am looking beside my fear." [608]
But before words, she offered him the evidence their mother would have trusted. [621]
She tilted her bleeding palm over the pool. [629]
A drop struck the freshwater thread and fled along it, not out to sea, but inward, toward the foundations of Nares. [650]
Senn swallowed, and the harshness in his face shifted into something worse and better: recognition. [665]
He had been a fisherman since twelve; he knew no tide should drink blood uphill. [680]
The pause thinned. [683]
Mara understood then that time was grit when resisted, silk when followed, shell when counted, and living skin when touched with another's wound. [706]
Senn lifted the jade cicada with surprising gentleness. [714]
"Tell me who to wake first," he said. [722]
Mara named the bedridden, the stubborn, the mothers with twins, and the bell keeper. [736]
