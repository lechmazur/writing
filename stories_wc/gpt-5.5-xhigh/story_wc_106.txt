<story>At Emerald Station, the glass roof burned green under a fleeting golden sunset, then cooled to jade as moonlight flooded the rails. [22] That was when I worked, not as a pickpocket of purses, but as the golden-hour portrait thief. [39] My name was Mara Venn, and I stole the shining instant in which a face remembered who it meant to become. [60] Collectors called them illuminated moments, but I knew them as small, warm thefts that left sitters practical and gray by morning. [81] I had learned the trade from Lord Cerith, the quietly savvy master of timetables, bribes, and soft ruin. [99] He paid me to sketch travelers at sunset, then locked their bright possible selves inside lacquered frames beneath his private platform. [120] He claimed the station prospered because he preserved hope; I saw how the hopeful stopped changing trains. [137] The first crack in my obedience arrived in a bouquet left beside my easel: three white lilies, one bent fern, and a thornless rose. [161] Only the old resistance used codes in flower arrangements, and this one said, Child arriving, future stolen, help. [179] I should have burned it, yet I carried the rose all evening, feeling its impossible smooth stem like a question. [199] At dusk a girl stepped from the salt train with a violin case and eyes full of unfinished weather. [218] Her ticket bore Cerith's silver mark, meaning he had already chosen her moment before she knew she possessed it. [237] When my charcoal touched the paper, the station lamps flared blue, and the sketch showed not a musician but a queen of signals. [260] I stared with luminous disbelief, because no stolen portrait had ever resisted me by drawing itself forward. [277] The girl noticed, smiled as if we shared a joke with time, and whispered, You hear it too. [295] Before I could answer, Cerith's porters moved through the crowd, their gloves smelling of varnish and sleep. [312] The second bouquet appeared in my paint box: marigold for danger, violet for secrecy, moonflower for the jar. [330] I knew the jar of captured moonbeams, a relic sealed in the clock tower to read reflections that daylight lied about. [351] Cerith used it each midnight to reinterpret stolen portraits, selecting the one future he preferred and sanding away the rest. [371] If I reached it first, perhaps I could reinterpret what I had done. [384] That hope was foolish enough to feel holy, my reason to press your ear against destiny's door and mistake thunder for invitation. [470] The resistance had not sent me a victim; they had sent me someone who could turn the whole station. [532] Faces filled the tower: farmers, smugglers, brides, a boy who should have become an inventor, my own brother Tomas. [551] I had believed Tomas dead by fever, yet there he was, young and blazing, trapped among Cerith's favorite predictions. [570] Luminous disbelief became rage, then grief, then a steadier flame. [580] Cerith entered with his pistol low and said I was confusing theft with mercy, as amateurs always did. [676] The paper drank the light, and the queen of signals stepped from it, not as fate, but as possibility. [695] For one breath, everyone at Emerald Station by moonlight saw a different self waiting on a different train. [713] Some laughed, some wept, and Tomas's pane cracked open with a smell of rain and coal smoke. [730] Cerith's pistol became only a dull object in his hand, for no one was willing to stay his prophecy anymore. [750] By dawn, I returned every illuminated moment I could, though they did not resume their old shapes.
