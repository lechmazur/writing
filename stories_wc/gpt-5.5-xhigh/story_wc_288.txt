After the reincarnation memory returned, Mara found herself lying among salt damp ladders in the tidal library above clouds. [19]
Dawn had drawn the cloud ocean low, and coral shelves creaked like boats moored in the sky. [36]
Her new life, twenty years of apprentice quiet, sat inside her like thin glass around a fiercer, older life. [55]

Their calls rushed back so sharply that she bit her palm to keep from answering. [96]
The librarians called such returns brittle awakenings, because one careless question could crack a borrowed self into panic or prophecy. [116]
Mara suffered no prophecy, only a loneliness with hooves, wings, and accusing yellow eyes. [130]
Before the memory, she had wanted promotion and permission to chart the ink tides that rose through the stacks each noon. [151]
After it, she wanted to translate isolation into independence, not by forgetting the dead but by refusing to be their only door. [173]

Mara understood why Od Sen had hidden it. [296]
The library did not merely preserve species; it missed them, and grief made poor law. [311]

Mara took the sundial across swinging bridges to the oldest balcony, where seven black stones stood in a ring above the vapor. [354]
