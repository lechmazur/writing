<story>After the carousel halted, the kinetic lagoon above the clouds lost its music, but not its motion. [17] In the fractured stillness, water kept climbing invisible tracks, glass fish circled dry air, and every anchored skiff rocked toward a different yesterday. [40] Mara, a resolute scavenger with callused palms and a weather hood patched from awnings, waited until the last painted horse froze with one hoof raised. [65] Only then did she cross the milky shallows to collect what the stopping had shaken loose. [81] She was chronologically confused, as everyone was near the lagoon now: breakfast bells rang after dusk, widows mourned before funerals, and Mara sometimes met her own footprints coming uphill through rain. [112] To keep herself from scattering, she carried a disposable camera wheel on a chain and turned it one click after every decision. [134] The tiny gray cog had once advanced pictures of tourists laughing beneath storm lanterns; now it promised Mara that one frame, at least, could follow another. [160] Her ambitions had been larger once. [166] She had wanted to repair the altitude engines, teach the lagoon to rise above all weather, and sell maps to sunlit cities no scavenger had seen. [192] Years of salt, salvage, and delayed tomorrows had eroded those ambitions into a single purpose: to negotiate with her own ghost. [213] The ghost appeared only after the carousel halted, standing in the ticket booth where the mirrors still remembered moving faces. [233] It looked like Mara at seventeen and seventy, wearing the same split lip she had earned when the main axle failed. [254] It never threatened her. [258] It simply held out a translucent hand and asked for the story Mara refused to finish. [274] Mara had no whole story, because the halt had chopped memory into bright, useless pieces. [289] So she did what scavengers did, collecting fragments of overheard stories. [300] From the shell of a snack kiosk she heard a sleeping vendor murmur, "She warned us before the horses broke." [320] From a gondola snagged in midair she heard two children repeat a rhyme about the girl who stole thunder for the poor. [342] Beneath the brass ribs of the carousel roof, an old mechanic dreamed aloud that Mara had pulled the brake with both hands, not fled. [366] Each fragment went into her satchel beside screws, valve pearls, and ribboned wire, and after each one the camera wheel clicked. [387] The lagoon moved around her without going anywhere. [395] Its turbines churned clouds into slow spirals below; its wave gates opened onto blue emptiness; its stranded pleasure boats knocked softly like teeth. [418] Mara crossed them all, resolute because stopping would let time choose for her. [431] At the west spillway, where minnows flashed in puddles of sky, she found her youngest memory waiting ahead of her. [451] She was kneeling beside the failed axle, younger hands blistered from the emergency brake, while crowds screamed for the ride to start again. [474] The memory did not speak, but under it lay the last missing fragment: a strip of wet film caught around a bolt. [496] Mara unwound it and saw twenty faded squares, blurred except one. [507] In that square, her own laughing mouth promised the council she could lift the lagoon higher if nobody slowed her. [527] Regret came then, not as a wave but as a careful hand setting weight after weight upon her chest. [546] She regretted the promise, the applause she had swallowed, and the way she had named warning cowardice. [563] She also regretted hating the brake, because the brake had saved every passenger except the version of Mara that needed applause to breathe. [586] When she returned to the ticket booth, the ghost was sorting moonlight into coins. [600] Mara laid out the overheard fragments, the film strip, and the disposable camera wheel. [614] "I have come to bargain," she said, "but not to buy back the carousel." [628] The ghost watched the little wheel as if it were a heart removed cleanly from a bird. [645] "What, then?" it asked. [649] "Take my old height," Mara said. [655] "Take the maps I never drew and the cities I imagined cheering my name." [669] "Leave me mornings in their order, and leave me hands steady enough to mend what my ambition broke." [687] The ghost touched the fragments, and they rose around them, making one uneven story from many stolen voices. [705] In it, Mara was neither savior nor villain, only a woman who had climbed too fast and then stopped in time. [726] The ghost took the wheel, turned it once, and placed it back in her palm. [741] "No bargain without payment," it said, and exhaled into the cog. [752] The teeth filled with pale dust, the ghost's dust, and Mara felt tomorrow settle behind today like a skiff finding its mooring. [774] Outside, the carousel did not restart. [780] That was the mercy. [784] Mara clicked the wheel once, accepted regret, and walked into the first plain morning. [798]</story>
