After the alien signal reached Earth, everything changed, and nothing did. [12]

Maren stood at the observation window of Pelagia, the benthic city that had grown like coral around the sunken research platforms of the mid-Atlantic ridge. [30] Below, bioluminescent lines traced streets through the abyssal dark, and above, the ocean pressed its immense, indifferent weight against three inches of reinforced glass. [50] She was a navigation instructor—one of the last people teaching celestial mechanics to students who might never see a star. [66]

The discovery had unfolded horizons no one had imagined. [74] The aliens—Vela, the broadcast called them, though no one knew if that was their name or their frequency—had transmitted maps. [91] Not of their world, but of the spaces between worlds, the gravitational folds and tidal corridors that made interstellar travel not possible but *plausible*, if you understood the geometry of edges. [115]

Maren held the object in her palm: a boat anchor chain link, corroded copper-green, pulled from the wreck of a pre-submersion freighter that had settled into the ridge centuries ago. [140] One of her students, a boy named Iker, had brought it to her during the morning lesson. [153] "I found it in the artifact corridor," he'd said, balancedly curious, the way all her best students were—eager but controlled, unwilling to let wonder collapse into carelessness. [176]

She had turned the link over, studying its edge geometry: the way the metal had worn into a subtle asymmetry, one side thinner than the other from centuries of chain-on-chain abrasion. [202] This was how she taught. [208] In the benthic city, you could not point to Polaris. [218] You could not trace the seasonal drift of Orion across an open sky. [229] But you could show a student how a worn edge reveals direction—the way differential erosion records the pull of current, the drag of anchor, the history of force. [254]

"The Vela maps work the same way," she told her afternoon class, projecting the alien transmission onto the classroom wall. [272] The image was not a star chart but a topological surface, edges folded over edges, each crease marking a gravitational boundary. [291] "They don't tell you where to go. [297] They tell you where the geometry *bends*. [304] You read the edges." [308]

She set the anchor chain link on the table and let the students gather around it. [321] "This is three hundred years old. [327] Look at how the wear has expanded the opening on one side. [336] That expansion tells you which direction the chain pulled, every time a ship set anchor. [350] Thousands of pulls, all recorded in the metal." [359]

Iker leaned forward. "So the Vela are telling us the same thing. [371] The folds are wear marks. [377] Something pulled through spacetime, over and over, and left a record." [389]

"Exactly." [391] Maren felt the somber marvel that never left her—the knowledge that somewhere above the four kilometers of water, stars still burned, and that beings she would never meet had found a way to read the wear patterns of the cosmos. [430] She would never navigate by starlight herself. [439] Pelagia would not rise. [445] But the Vela had shown that the universe carried instructions in its edges, if you knew how to look. [464]

After class, she returned to the observation window. [475] The bioluminescent grid pulsed faintly, mimicking the city's circadian rhythm—day and night manufactured for organisms that had forgotten the sun. [500] Maren pressed her fingers to the glass and thought about the anchor chain link, about how its erosion was a kind of language. [522]

The Vela signal had not brought hope, exactly. [532] It had brought *evidence*: that somewhere, the dark had been crossed, that the folded geometries between stars were navigable, that the horizons could be unfolded if you understood the mathematics of edges. [564] Maren would teach this. [569] She would show her students the chain link and the alien maps and say: *this is how you read what the universe has worn away.* [594]

She did not know if humanity would ever leave the ocean. [607] She did not know if the Vela still traveled the corridors they had mapped. [620] But she knew that the edge geometry of a corroded copper link and the edge geometry of a gravitational fold obeyed the same principle: history leaves its signature in the margins, and navigation begins where wear meets wonder. [659]

Outside the glass, the abyssal current shifted, carrying sediment in slow spirals toward the ridge. [679] Somewhere above, impossibly far, stars were setting. [690] Maren closed her eyes and tried, as she did every night, to imagine their light reaching her—diminished, filtered through four thousand meters of salt and pressure, but *arriving*. [722] Starlight, she told herself, was just another edge. [733] You had to learn to read it. [741]
