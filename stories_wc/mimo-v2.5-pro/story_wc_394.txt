<story>The workshop floated on the acid sea like a prayer no one had bothered to finish. [16]

Inside, perched on a rail that shouldn't have been able to hold anything, sat the cart glider—the first of its kind, the pioneer, the idiot who had strapped wings to a mining cart and called it transportation. [86]
Its name was Fer, and it was steadily curious about everything, which was both its greatest virtue and the reason it had ended up on an acid sea in a workshop full of cogs instead of somewhere reasonable, like a meadow. [127]
Fer had found the hunter's arrow tip three tides ago, lodged in the floorboards near the kiln where the workshop's strange gears were forged. [151]
The tip was black obsidian, chipped to a wicked point, and it hummed when Fer touched it—not with sound, but with a yearning so specific it felt like math. [181]
Fer held the arrow tip to the light and studied it the way it studied everything: with the tireless attention of something that had decided, somewhere throughout the evolution of its own making, that ignorance was unacceptable. [218]
"Right," Fer muttered to no one, because the workshop had no one in it besides the gears and the acid sea and whatever ghosts haunted the space between mechanical teeth. [248]
"There's a pattern here." [252]
There was always a pattern. [257]

Fer lifted the hunter's arrow tip and held it against the kaleidoscope that hung from the ceiling on a chain of interlocking rings. [307]
The kaleidoscope had been here since before the workshop, or so the workshop's logs suggested, and Fer had learned to transliterate its meanings by reading the images it produced—fragments of light, refracted into sacred geometry, each rotation revealing something the eye alone could never parse. But the bell was real, and someone had to ring it, because the yearning algorithm demanded that all things which could exist eventually must. [481]
