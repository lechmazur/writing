Maren pressed the brass lever and the elevator shuddered upward, beginning its climb through the first of ten floors of the icebound spire. [23]
The Spire of Althenar pierced the cloud sea like a needle of frozen glass, its upper chambers forever sealed in rime and silence. [46]
As a servant here for eleven years, Maren had learned every groan of its iron bones, every whisper of wind that threaded through its frost-rimed corridors. [72]
She knew which floors held ballrooms abandoned to ice crystals and which ones sheltered the last warmth of the generators below. [93]
This evening, however, she carried something she had never carried before: a leather-bound diary found behind a heating grate in the servants' quarters, and a pair of airship captain's goggles discovered beside it, their tinted lenses catching what little light the spire offered. [136]
The diary was encrypted—she could tell from the neat rows of symbols marching across every page, none of them belonging to any alphabet she recognized. [162]
But its leather cover was warm to the touch, impossibly warm for a place like this, as though it remembered hands that had once loved it. [188]
She could not read it, but she meant to try. [198]
As the elevator passed through the third floor, a slant of dying sunlight pierced the frost-clouded window and struck the goggles in her hands. [222]
The light scattered through the tinted lenses in unexpected ways, fracturing into pale bands of color that fell across the open diary page on her lap. [248]
And there, where the scattered light touched the encrypted script, the symbols shifted. [261]
Maren leaned forward, warmly inquisitive, watching as the scattered light methodically revealed each hidden word beneath the cipher. [279]
The goggles, she realized, were a key, designed to work with a particular quality of light that existed only at this altitude, where the sun fractured through ice-choked atmosphere. [308]
The fourth floor. The fifth. [313]
A name began to form beneath the light: Captain Lira Voss. [324]
Maren had heard the name spoken only once, in a corridor she was not supposed to occupy, in a tone that carried more grief than reverence. [350]
Captain Voss had commanded the last airship to dock at the spire before the cloud-sea sealed them off from the lower world. [372]
The crew had spoken of her with a peculiar mixture of anguish and wonder. [386]
The sixth floor. The diary's decrypted passages began to unfold like petals. [398]
Captain Voss had loved this place—truly loved it—not for its isolation but for what the isolation revealed. [417]
Above the clouds, she had written, you discover that silence has a texture, and loneliness has a warmth. [435]
The anguish of separation is real, but beneath it, always beneath it, is the strange joy of having had something worth losing. [457]
Maren felt the words settle into her chest like embers. [467]
She had served in this spire for over a decade, carrying her own quiet anguish—the distance from the world below, the faces she could no longer recall clearly, the feeling that her life was spent maintaining someone else's frozen monument. [508]
But Voss's diary offered something she had not expected: permission to feel affection for the very thing that isolated her. [528]
The seventh floor. The eighth. [533]
More passages unfolded under scattered light. [539]
Voss wrote of watching sunrises from the observation deck, of how the ice caught colors that the lower world never saw, of laughter echoing through empty halls when the wind played them like instruments. [573]

The ninth floor. [597]

It was no longer the whole shape of her life. [635]

Maren closed the diary gently and removed the goggles, blinking at the dim corridor ahead. [702]
She would return tomorrow, she decided, and climb again. [711]
There were more pages, more light, more words waiting to emerge from their careful hiding places. [727]
Captain Voss had left this gift knowing someone would find it—someone warm enough to look, and patient enough to let the light do its work. [753]
