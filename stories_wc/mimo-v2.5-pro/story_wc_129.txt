<story>The marsh-reed sculptor named Liora had lived at the crater's edge for three seasons before anyone learned her name. [19]
She had arrived carrying bundles of salt-cured reeds and a longing she could not name, settling into the shadows of the crystalline orchard perched at the edge of the shimmering crater, where mineral branches caught light like frozen music. [58]
The orchard's trees grew in geometries that defied ordinary botany, their translucent trunks refracting the crater's glow into prismatic haze. [78]
Beyond the murmur of lost languages that once echoed through this place—tongues worn smooth by centuries of wind—Liora worked in silence, weaving reeds into shapes that held no practical use but carried the ache of something sacred. [117]

Her motivation was simple and impossible: to graft herself onto the community that orbited the crater like moths drawn to its luminous wound. [140]
The settlement's people were descended from crater-miners and orchard-keepers, bound by webs of kinship so intricate that even strangers turned out to share blood, or at least memory. [168]
Liora, who came from the salt marshes far to the south, had neither. [181]
She was boldly sympathetic by nature—her sculptures, gifted freely to neighbors, became bridges she hoped others might cross toward her. [202]

Then the tags began appearing. [207]
Scratched into obsidian outcroppings, painted in phosphorescent pigment on the walls of root cellars, the mysterious symbols appeared overnight: spirals nested within spirals, letters from alphabets no one recognized. [236]
Someone was marking the settlement with an elusive street artist's tag, and no one could identify the hand behind it. [256]
The community buzzed with speculation. [261]
Was it a miner's child? [266]
A traveler passing through on the crater road? [274]
The tags carried the same luminescent ache that Liora felt in her own work, and this recognition moved her deeply. [294]

She decided to dedicate herself to the search, not to unmask the artist for punishment, but to understand what bound them. [315]
She joined Kael, a young orchard-keeper with skin like oiled birch, who had found the first tag on a glimmering obsidian arbor at the orchard's northern edge—a massive natural formation where black glass branches arched overhead like the ribcage of some buried leviathan. [359]
Together they studied each new symbol, comparing stroke patterns, tracing pigment trails through the orchard's crystalline corridors. [376]

"The artist knows these trees," Kael murmured one evening, pressing his palm against a trunk that sang faintly under his touch. [397]
"Every tag appears near something that refracts light." [405]

They followed this thread for days, mapping the tags against the orchard's luminous landmarks. [419]
Liora found herself telling Kael about the marshes, about her mother who braided reeds into funerary masks, about the loneliness of arriving in a place where kinship was already complete. [449]
Kael listened with a stillness that made her words feel witnessed rather than merely heard. [464]
In return, he shared stories of the crater—how its shimmer was not reflection but slow geological fire, how the orchard had grown from seeds that fell into the vent centuries ago. [496]

The webs of kinship began to form around their shared search, threads neither had spun alone. [512]
Other settlers joined them: Maren the cistern-builder, old Thess who remembered the lost languages as faint melodies hummed under her breath. [533]
The search became a communal ritual, each new discovery deepening the bonds between people who had been neighbors but not yet family. [555]

They found the artist on the seventh night. [563]
She was a girl of perhaps twelve, standing inside the glimmering obsidian arbor itself, painting a final spiral onto the black glass with fingers dipped in crushed mineral. [591]
She had been orphaned during a landslide, taken in by no one, surviving on crater fruit and rainwater. [609]
Her tags were not art—they were prayers, each symbol a word from a language her grandmother had spoken, a language now dissolved into wind. [634]

Liora knelt before her, boldly sympathetic, and offered her hands. [644]
The girl hesitated, then placed her painted fingers into Liora's palms, leaving spirals of light on the sculptor's skin. [663]

"I want to belong," the girl whispered. [670]

"So do I," Liora answered. [675]

They stood together beneath the obsidian arbor while the crystalline orchard hummed around them, and Liora felt the graft take—painful, luminous, necessary. [698]
The lost language lived now in pigment on their joined hands, and the crater's shimmer seemed, for the first time, like something that welcomed rather than merely shone. [726]
