Queen Lysenne had not slept without dreaming in eleven years. [9]
Each night the same vision returned: a ridge above the clouds, a wound of light leaking from stone, and her own hand reaching toward something she could never grasp. [33]
She was disenchanted in every sense of the word—once beloved by her people, now merely tolerated, her soft commands met with the stiff politeness of advisors who had already chosen her successor. [65]
She spoke softly still, but the softness had calcified into something harder, a quiet authority that frightened courtiers more than shouting ever could. [90]
The final warning came from her oldest counselor, Merith, who set a sealed letter on the throne's armrest and said, "The Council gives you until the solstice to abdicate. This is their final warning." [122]
Lysenne read the letter, folded it into her sleeve, and said nothing. [133]
That evening, she left the palace alone, carrying nothing but a lantern and the eleven years of dreams folded behind her eyes. [153]
She climbed the northern trail toward the ridge her dreams had drawn in obsessive detail, every switchback and scree slope familiar despite her never having visited. [176]
The path wound through twilight forests where lichen draped the branches like lace, beautiful and parasitic, feeding silently on what was still alive. [197]
As she climbed higher, the clouds gathered below her, a churning sea of violet and pewter, until the trail deposited her above them entirely. [218]
There, embedded in the ridge's spine, she found the ember grotto—its mouth glowing with a faint, pulsing warmth, like a stone breathing in its sleep. [242]
She entered, and the grotto opened into a vast cobalt chamber, its walls veined with mineral deposits that caught the last light and held it, reluctant to let the day end. [271]
Water dripped from the ceiling in arrhythmic drops, each one a small bell tolling nothing. [284]
This was the place from her dreams, yet it was larger, colder, and more silent than any sleeping vision had prepared her for. [302]
She sat on a shelf of stone and waited, though she could not have said for what. [317]
Then the starlight began to bleed. [323]
It seeped through a fissure in the grotto's ceiling—not the clean silver of ordinary starlight, but something wounded and heavy, pooling on the floor like spilled wine made of constellations. [353]
The light moved, swirling around her boots, climbing her ankles with a tenderness that felt almost predatory. [369]
Lysenne remained still, softly commanding her own pulse to slow, and exhaled. [382]
The exhale left her lips and met the bleeding light, and something extraordinary happened: the starfire paused, contracted, and then scattered outward in every direction. [407]
In that scattering, the first fireflies of summer appeared—impossible this high above the trees, yet there they were, tiny lanterns blinking into existence from the dispersing starlight itself. [438]
They filled the grotto like living sparks, each one a punctuation mark in a sentence the universe was trying to finish. [456]
Lysenne understood then that she was not alone in the exhale. [468]
Something else had breathed with her, a presence woven into the grotto's stone, ancient and patient and tired. [484]
The fireflies swirled together above her head and formed a shape—not a face, not a word, but a gesture, the way an old woman might reach toward a granddaughter she had only seen in dreams. [516]
The recurring dreams had never been warnings. They had been invitations. [529]
The grotto had been calling her, not to destroy her reign but to ask for something she had not realized she possessed: the willingness to climb, to ascend above the world's indifference and sit in a bleeding wound of light without flinching. [571]
She whispered to the fireflies, her voice softly commanding even now, and asked what was required of her. [589]
The answer came not in words but in a shared exhale—the grotto breathing out as she breathed in—and in that exchange, Lysenne felt the weight of eleven sleepless years lift, replaced by a purpose that was neither throne nor crown but something older and stranger. [637]
She would not abdicate. She would return to the palace carrying the ember grotto's secret in her chest, a queen who had climbed above the clouds and returned with starlight still bleeding gently from her fingertips. [672]
The fireflies followed her down the ridge, a luminous retinue threading through the lacy darkness of the forest. [691]
Behind her, the grotto dimmed, its breathing slowed, and the stones settled back into silence—but not before exhaling one final time, a sound like relief, like recognition, like a door closing only because someone had finally arrived. [733]
