Silas had driven the black carriage down from Gloom Hill three nights running, following a road that narrowed until it was barely more than a suggestion between the marsh grasses. [30]
The jetty appeared at twilight, as it always did—not quite solid, not quite real, its wooden pilings furred with pale blossoms that had no business blooming in salt air. [60]
He reined the horse to a stop and sat listening. [70]
Below the jetty, the reef was dead. [77]
He had watched it die over the course of a single autumn: first the color bled from the coral, then the fish vanished, then the water itself seemed to thicken and dull, as though grief had a viscosity. [115]
The war had done this—not the fighting on the bluffs above, but the chemicals they'd poured into the harbor to deny the enemy passage, and the munitions that had cracked the seabed and released something older and worse into the current. [157]
The battlefield stretched behind the jetty in both directions, a scab of churned earth and rusted metal. [174]
Silas climbed down from his perch and opened the compartment beneath his seat, where he kept his collection of dried seed pods wrapped in oilcloth. [199]
They rattled softly, like the bones of small animals—hawthorn, sea lavender, samphire, and others he couldn't name but whose calls he recognized. [222]
That was the strange gift his father had given him, or the curse: the ability to understand bird calls not as songs but as language. [247]
The curlews spoke in a tongue he knew from the old records—a dead language, a concord dialect once used to negotiate between coastal peoples before the empire swallowed their treaties whole. [279]
Here was the concord, submerged: not a document but a living agreement between species, between soil and sea, that the war had drowned. [302]
He walked to the edge of the jetty and listened to the birds. [315]
A knot of sanderlings was chattering in the shallows, and he caught the words plainly: fertilize the western ridge first. [331]
The salt has burned deep. [336]
The clay needs loosening before the seeds will hold. [349]
He nodded to no one. [354]
That was his method—had always been his method. [363]
He could not read soil the way farmers could, could not test pH or gauge moisture with his hands. [382]
But the birds watched what he could not, nested in what he would never see, and they told him what the land required. [405]
From his coat he drew a flask of fish emulsion he'd mixed on the road down, and he knelt at the western edge of the battlefield where the jetty met the bluffs. [437]
The earth there was white with salt deposits, cracked like old porcelain. [449]
He poured the emulsion into the furrows and worked it with his fingers, feeling the soil resist and then, grudgingly, accept. [470]
From his pocket he drew three pods of sea lavender and pressed them into the loosened clay, covering them gently, the way one might close the eyes of the dead. [500]
A heron on the far piling spoke in a language even older than the curlew tongue—a language that predated human settlement entirely. [523]
Silas listened. [525]
The reef beneath remembers. [529]
It will feed what grows above. [535]
Plant where the concord was signed, and the garden will know. [546]
He understood. [548]
The garden was not decoration. [553]
It was not memorial, not even healing, exactly. [561]
It was a letting—allowing the living world to write over the record of violence, to claim the ground where men had died so that no one could ever again pretend the land had always been war. [598]
The twilight narrowed to a blade of amber on the horizon, and in that thin light the jetty seemed to stretch into a vastness he could feel but not measure—the vastness of what the reef had been, of what the concord had promised, of what might yet root in poisoned ground if someone carried the seeds and listened. [657]
He pressed the last pod into the earth, stood, and wiped his hands on his coat. [673]
The birds had gone quiet. [678]
In the silence, he climbed back onto the carriage and turned the horse toward Gloom Hill, where the road would narrow again and the dark would take him, and tomorrow at twilight he would return with more seeds and more emulsion and more patience than the war had ever learned to hold. [730]
