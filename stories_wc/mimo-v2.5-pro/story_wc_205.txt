<story>The asteroid turned slowly in the void, its surface carved with the scrolls and volutes of a baroque age that had ended long before anyone could remember it beginning. [29]
Oren Vasik stood at the lip of a collapsed colonnade and watched the temporal storm gather beyond the asteroid's thin atmosphere—ribbons of visible time curling like smoke against the stars. [60]
Since the Cascade, time had stopped being something you endured and become something you saw: amber currents, gray eddies, the occasional bright fracture where a moment refused to pass. [89]
He was a defunct revolutionary, a title he wore the way other people wore scars. [104]
Twenty years ago, he had led the Unbinding, an uprising against the Archivists who controlled the baroque asteroid's relic vaults and, with them, the official memory of its vanished civilization. [134]
The revolution had failed spectacularly. [139]
The Archivists still held the vaults; Oren held only his exile and a gift for verse that felt, in this burned-out life, like the cruelest joke of all. [167]
Now he had returned under a ceasefire to participate in something almost worse than revolution: an archaeological dispute. [185]
A team from the Outer Confluence had unearthed a fragment deep in the asteroid's mantle—a shard of crystal vase, pale blue and veined with gold, that they claimed predated the baroque period by millennia. [220]
If authenticated, it would rewrite the civilization's timeline entirely, suggesting that the asteroid's artists had inherited, not invented, their ornamental tradition. [241]
The Archivists called it fraud. [246]
Oren called it hope. [250]
He had spent every night since his return devising a counter-argument, a poetically inclined manifesto that married evidence with elegy. [270]
The Archivists wielded data; Oren wielded metaphor. [277]
But metaphor, he had learned, could move things that data could not. [289]
On the third evening, the temporal storm reached the asteroid's surface. [300]
Oren moved through the half-collapsed apartments of the old quarter, pulling heavy cloth over every mirror he could find. [319]
Covering mirrors during storms was an ancient practice of the vanished civilization—one the Archivists had catalogued but never understood. [339]
Oren understood it because he had read the poets. [348]
When visible time struck glass, it didn't just reflect; it refracted, scattering the past across walls and skin, making the present unbearable. [370]
The old civilization had known that a people could not build beauty while being haunted by every prior version of themselves. [391]
The shard was brought before the arbitration panel on a velvet tray. [403]
Chief Archivist Duren presented spectrographic analyses, isotopic charts, a dozen reasons the fragment was a forgery. [419]
Oren listened, turning the shard over in his hands when his turn came, feeling its impossible thinness, the way light moved through it like memory through grief. [446]
He did not present charts. [451]
Instead, he recited a poem he had written that week, one that described the shard not as an object but as a question the old civilization had asked itself: What do we owe the people who taught us to dream? [491]
The poem traced the vase's possible journey from ancient hands to baroque ones, imagining each fracture as a deliberate act of transmission— [513]
a civilization breaking its own masterwork so that every fragment would carry the whole design, scattering itself into the future like seeds. [535]
When he finished, the chamber was quiet in the way that storms are quiet before they reshape the landscape. [554]
Duren objected, of course. [558]
Poetry was not evidence. [562]
But Oren had spent twenty years learning that revolutions fail when they fight on the enemy's terms. [579]
He had not come to win the dispute; he had come to reimagine the entire civilization that both sides were fighting over. [601]
The panel ruled the shard inconclusive, which meant neither side had won. [613]
But something had shifted. [617]
In the weeks that followed, young historians from both factions began quoting Oren's poem in their papers, asking not whether the shard was authentic but what it meant that someone had once made something so beautiful it was worth breaking. [657]
That was how reimagining began—not with an answer, but with a better question. [671]
Oren left the asteroid on a cargo vessel, carrying nothing but the poem and a small piece of the shard that had somehow found its way into his coat pocket. [701]
Through the viewport, the baroque asteroid receded, its carved surfaces catching light like a burned-down cathedral. [717]
He pressed his hand against the glass and watched time itself drift past the ship in golden veils, visible and relentless and, he thought, almost beautiful enough to forgive. [746]</story>
