<story>
Mei had always been a wistful loom spinner, her fingers drawing threads through warp and weft while her mind wandered beyond the bamboo walls of her workshop. [27] She spun not just cloth but silence itself—a raveling silence that pooled in the empty spaces between shuttle and reed, between breath and heartbeat. [46] It was in those quiet intervals that she felt closest to her mother, who had vanished into the bamboo forest years ago and never returned. [67]

One autumn morning, Mei visited a rummage sale at the village edge, where elders traded forgotten things for coin or sympathy. [83] Buried beneath tin cups and moth-eaten scarves, she found a wooden box of jigsaw puzzle pieces—only the edges, the border fragments, the parts that defined a shape's limit. [105] She bought them for almost nothing and carried them home, not knowing why. [115]

That evening, by lamplight, she spread the edge pieces across her loom's wooden frame and began to connect them. [130] The border formed not a rectangle but a map—jagged coastlines, a winding path, a cluster of dots she recognized as bamboo stalks rendered in faded ink. [150] At the map's center, someone had drawn a small circle labeled with a single character: *orchard*. [163]

Mei's hands trembled. [167] Her mother had spoken once of a forgotten orchard deep in a bamboo forest, a place where fruit trees still bloomed out of season, where the air tasted of old honey. [188] She had dismissed it as a bedtime fancy. [195] Now, studying the puzzle edges she had found through sheer accident—or perhaps through the patient gravity of unfinished things—she understood it was real. [213]

Around her neck hung the only inheritance her mother had left: a tree sap amber bead on a cord of braided silk. [231] It stoically sparkling in the lamplight, its golden interior trapping not an insect but something stranger—a tiny folded leaf, visible through the translucent resin, bearing her mother's handwriting. [255] Mei had never been able to read it through the amber, but she knew, the way daughters know things, that it was a message meant for someone she could not reach alone. [279]

She would recommence her mother's journey. [285]

Through the hush of time's unraveling tapestry, the bamboo forest had changed. [296] Paths that once existed had been swallowed by root and undergrowth; landmarks had fallen and rotted into soil. [311] But the puzzle-edge map held true. [317] Mei followed its winding path for three days, sleeping in hollow trunks, eating the rice balls she had wrapped in cloth before dawn on the first morning. [339] The silence here was different from her workshop's—it was vast and alive, a raveling silence that seemed to pull at the edges of her thoughts, loosening everything she had knotted shut inside herself. [368]

On the third evening, she stepped through a curtain of hanging bamboo and found it: the forgotten orchard. [385] Pear trees stood in crooked rows, their branches heavy with fruit that glowed pale gold in the failing light. [401] The ground beneath was carpeted with fallen blossoms that had never fully decomposed, as though time itself had forgotten to carry them away. [423]

At the orchard's center stood a stone pedestal, and on it, a shallow depression shaped exactly like an amber bead. [441]

Mei understood then what the message was, and what it would cost. [452] The amber bead was not meant to be read—it was meant to be given, surrendered to this place where the boundary between the living and the gone grew thin as thread. [476] Her mother had carried it here once but could not part with it. [487] That failure had pulled her back, again and again, until she had finally stayed. [499]

The sacrifice was latent in every step Mei had taken: to deliver the message across this impossible distance, she had to release the last physical proof of her mother's existence. [524] She would lose the bead, the handwriting, the warm weight against her sternum that she touched each night before sleeping. [541] She would return to her loom with empty hands and a silence that could never be unraveled back into speech. [559]

Mei lifted the cord over her head. [566] The amber bead sparkled stoically one final time, catching the orchard's strange light as though it understood and approved. [584] She set it into the stone depression. [591]

For a moment, nothing happened. [597] Then the pear blossoms stirred without wind, and the silence shifted—not breaking, but deepening, as though a door had opened inside it. [618] Mei felt her mother's hand on her shoulder, light as a thread pulled taut, and then release. [634]

She walked back through the bamboo forest carrying no map, no bead, no proof. [649] But when she sat again at her loom and recommenced spinning, the cloth that emerged was different—warmer, luminous, threaded with a silence that did not feel like absence but like presence, held and gentle, carrying across every impossible distance.
