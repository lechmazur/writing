The Tower at Arabel Point had no walls, only pillars. [10]
Maren climbed the last stone steps and stepped into open air, the wind pressing her coat flat against her ribs. [30]
Below, the sea churned silver under a moon that had not yet decided whether to stay. [46]
She told herself she had come for the whales—the annual migration was peaking, and the tower offered the best vantage on the coast. [70]
That was easier to say than the truth. [78]

Her brother Silas had died in autumn. [85]
He had loved this place. [90]
He believed, with the unshakable faith of someone who listened too carefully, that the tower's stones held frequencies—patterns of vibration that collected memory the way salt collects moisture from air. [121]
Maren, who had studied acoustics and published three papers debunking resonance myths, had always smiled at this. [138]
Now she climbed to his favorite spot and sat on the cold floor, knees drawn up, feeling foolish for hoping to hear something. [161]

The telescope at the tower's center was a relic—brass, enormous, aimed at nothing in particular tonight. [178]
At its base, half-buried under dust and dead moths, lay a single tile. [191]
Square, ceramic, no larger than her palm. [198]
Its surface was etched with a grid of numbers—rows and columns summing to the same value in every direction. [218]
A magic square. [221]
Silas had made dozens of them during his last months—obsessively, feverishly, as though each arrangement of integers might unlock some door she could not see. [247]
Maren had assumed it was the morphine, some numerical hallucination his mind had clung to when words stopped coming. [266]

She picked it up. [270]
It was warm, which meant nothing—ceramic held heat, and the tower trapped the day's last warmth in its stone bones. [291]
She turned it over. [295]
On the back, in Silas's cramped handwriting: Transcribe what you cannot hear. [307]

Something in her chest tightened. [312]
She almost put it down. [317]
She was not the kind of woman who followed instructions from the dead. [330]
But the wind shifted, and with it came a sound—or not a sound, exactly, but a pressure against her eardrums, low and sustained, like a chord held by invisible hands. [361]

The whales. [363]
They were close tonight, their songs carrying through water and stone and air in ways physics should not allow but always did. [385]
She had written papers about how whale song traveled. [394]
She had never considered that it might arrive somewhere and mean something more than itself. [409]

It would have been easy to leave—easier to drive back to her apartment, file the tile away with Silas's other belongings, and call this grief managed. [436]
But staying was a kindness—to him, to the years he had spent believing in something she had refused to hear. [457]
She pulled a notebook from her coat and a pencil from its spine. [470]
She began to transcribe—not the whale song, which she could now hear threading through the tower's open frame like a living thing, but the numbers on the tile. [499]
Row by row she copied them, and the pattern beneath her pencil began to shift. [514]
The forgotten patterns Silas had encoded were not arithmetic—they were maps. [526]
Each diagonal, each column, read through his cipher, resolved into coordinates, dates, fragments of voice. [541]

Remember the day we saw the breach off Point Reyes? [551]
Remember how you laughed when I said the ocean had a memory? [563]
You were always so afraid of things you could not measure. [574]

She was crying, but she did not stop writing. [583]
The tile's numbers unfolded into an architecture of moments she and Silas had shared—entangled, she realized, not just with each other but with the place, the migration, the frequencies he had spent his life chasing. [619]
He had built this—this strange, patient thing—for her. [629]
Not a code or a puzzle but a space sculpted for reflection, open as the tower itself, waiting for her to finally arrive. [652]
The whales sang on below, their voices merging into something vast and slow and unresolvable by any instrument she owned. [672]
And in the open tower, with the migration passing beneath her and the tile warm in her hand, Maren transcribed the unheard symphony her brother had left for her—note by careful note, until her pencil wore down and the moon committed to staying and the sea carried every forgotten thing she had written back to where it belonged.
