After the shadow detached from her body, Maren knew she had little time left to work. [16] She stood at the edge of the floating island tea garden, where lanterns she had crafted hung from ancient wisteria branches, each one containing a polished gemstone that pulsed with captured dreams. [48] The floating island drifted through violet clouds, carrying the scent of jasmine and something older—something like memory turning slowly to dust. [70]

Below them—or above, depending on how gravity chose to behave—the glass basilica caught the dying light. [88] Its walls were transparent as breath, its spires thin as needles, and it descended while the island continued ascending through the atmosphere. [110] Maren had watched this paradox every evening for three years, the falling and rising simultaneously, and she had come to understand it as a metaphor for grief itself. [138]

She returned to her workshop, where the polishing wheel waited. [148] The gems she worked were no ordinary stones; they were dream-recording crystals, harvested from volcanic glass beneath the island. [167] When polished with precise care, each facet trapped a single dream—her mother's laugh, her father's hands shaping clay, the warmth of a kitchen that no longer existed. [195] The craft was her inheritance, passed down through generations of lantern makers who understood that light without memory was merely fire. [216]

Tonight, she worked with particular urgency. [222] Her shadow had separated at noon, peeling away from her heels like a living thing and drifting toward the basilica. [242] The elders said this meant the dreamer was nearly empty, that soon the recording would stop and the gems would go dark forever. [265] Maren could feel it—the hollowness spreading through her, luminous and unbearable. [277]

She held a sapphire to the wheel and began to polish. [288] The stone transformed under her fingers, its rough exterior giving way to impossible clarity. [302] Within that clarity she saw her mother standing in this very garden, planting tea seedlings, her shadow still attached, her smile still whole. [325] Tears fell onto the wheel, and where they touched the gem, new facets formed—facets that held not just the image but the texture of that morning, the specific humidity, the exact pitch of her mother's humming. [362]

The glass basilica outside rang like a bell. [370] Its walls vibrated, responding to the dream being recorded, and the sound traveled through the garden, shaking loose petals from the tea bushes. [393] Maren understood the basilica now: it was not a building but a receiver, a great crystalline ear designed to collect the dreams of those whose shadows had detached. [421] It descended while ascending because grief moved in both directions—pulling you into the earth while lifting you toward something you could not name. [445]

She polished three more gems before midnight. [452] Each captured a different memory: her father folding paper cranes at the kitchen table, her sister singing in the rain, the sound of water on a roof that had since collapsed. [483] And as each dream entered its stone, Maren took sheets of translucent paper and began to fold. [500] This was the tradition—grief must be given form, or it would consume the dreamer entirely. [516] Each origami crane held a single polished gem in its breast, a heart of captured light. [532]

By dawn, she had created forty-seven cranes. [539] They sat on every surface of her workshop, luminous and trembling, each one a perfect vessel of sorrow transformed into something almost beautiful. [562] The shadow had nearly reached the basilica now, and Maren could feel the last of her dreams gathering at the edges of her mind, desperate to be recorded before the emptying was complete. [595]

She lifted one final gem—clear quartz, the most receptive stone—and held it against her heart. [612] The dream that entered it was not a memory but a wish: her mother, alive, folding cranes with her in a garden that floated forever, ascending without ever descending, shadow intact, laughter whole. [645] The gem polished itself in a sudden flare of inner light. [656] Maren folded the last crane, placed the stone within its paper breast, and released it through the window. [674] It joined the others rising toward the glass basilica, where they would be received, catalogued, and preserved for as long as the island continued its endless climb through clouds that tasted of jasmine and loss. [709]

Her shadow detached completely. [713] Maren watched it go, feeling both emptied and illuminated—descending into herself while ascending into the light she had spent a lifetime crafting. [736]

The cranes continued upward, carrying their luminous torment toward the sky. [747]
