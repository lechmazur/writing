<story>The hedge maze designer had not always been lost in the living bridge garden. [14]
Her name was Ellara, and she had once drawn paths with the certainty of someone who understood that every turn was a choice, every dead end a lesson. [42]
But that was before she found the halo fragment half-buried beneath a moss-covered archway, glowing faintly like a memory refusing to forget itself. [65]
She had come to the garden seeking the texture of time—a thing no mathematician could prove and no philosopher could name. [87]
The living bridge garden was itself a paradox: pathways woven from roots and vines that shifted when observed, arching over streams that flowed in directions that changed with the hour. [117]
Ellara had designed hundreds of mazes in her career, labyrinthine constructions of yew and privet that invited visitors to lose themselves in deliberate confusion. [141]
But this garden designed her. [146]

The halo fragment fit in her palm like a shard of frozen light, and when she touched it, the first photograph appeared. [168]
It hovered in the air—a fading photograph of a woman standing at the center of a maze that Ellara recognized as her own. [192]
The image pulsed, then dissolved at its edges, consuming itself in silence. [204]
That was when she understood: the fragment showed moments that had already ended, places that had already shifted, and she was retrocognitively trapped in their dissolving wake. [231]
She tried to set it down, but her fingers would not release it. [244]
The next photograph bloomed: herself as a child, drawing spirals in wet sand at the shore, the waves erasing her work before she could finish. [269]
Then another: her first commission, a maze built for a lord whose name she had forgotten, the hedges already browning at their tips. [292]
Each image faded faster than the last, as though time itself was accelerating its decay through hyperbolic geometry—the spaces between moments stretching, curving, folding into shapes Euclid never imagined. [322]

The garden responded to her distress. [328]
Vines curled toward her ankles, and the living bridges groaned as they reshaped themselves into new configurations. [345]
She was walking now, stumbling through corridors of green that should not exist, the halo fragment casting prismatic light against leaves that wept dew like tiny mourning eyes. [373]
In the quaking reverie that gripped her, she could feel time not as a line but as a surface—creased, torn, pasted together from moments that did not know their own order. [405]
Another photograph materialized before her: this one showed the garden as it would appear after her death, overgrown and abandoned, the bridges collapsed into the water. [431]
She gasped, and the image shivered but did not fade. [441]
Instead, it began to disintegrate, not vanishing but crumbling outward in particles of silver dust, spreading through the air like pollen from a flower that had never bloomed. [469]

Ellara fell to her knees on a bridge that pulsed with the heartbeat of the root system beneath it. [488]
She understood now why she had been trapped. [496]
The halo fragment was not a relic—it was a question. [507]
It asked whether the past existed only in its own erasure, whether memory was simply the shape of things leaving. [527]
As a designer of mazes, she had always believed that the point was the journey, the navigation, the deliberate bewilderment. [547]
But here, at the junction of every path the garden offered, there was only one direction: toward the dissolution of what had been. [570]
The photographs kept coming, each more luminous and more fleeting. [580]
She saw her mother braiding her hair in a kitchen she could no longer recall. [595]
She saw the first man she had loved walking away through a corridor of boxwood. [610]
She saw the halo fragment itself, intact, held by a hand that was not hers, in a garden she had never visited. [632]
Everything was disintegrating through the same warped geometry, folding outward into impossible dimensions where cause preceded feeling and feeling preceded thought. [653]

She pressed the fragment against her chest and closed her eyes. [664]
The garden fell silent. [668]
When she opened them, the living bridge beneath her had reshaped into a spiral, and at its center stood a single hedgerow door, carved from wood so old it had turned to stone. [701]
She walked toward it, the fragment dimming in her hand. [711]
On the other side waited nothing she could name—only the texture she had sought, the grain and weight of seconds passing through her fingers like sand, like light, like the fading edge of every photograph she had ever loved. [751]
The door closed behind her, and the garden exhaled. [760]</story>
