Elen had apprenticed to the Cartographer's Guild three years ago, but aboard the antique airship Meridian Drift, she felt less like a student than a castaway. [26]
Her master, old Varenth, had departed for the Summit of Chartists six weeks prior, leaving her to tend the vessel and its trove of half-finished maps. [52]
She didn't mind. [55]
The isolation was sociable in its own way -- she talked aloud to the brass altimeters, hummed to the wind-battered canvas, and kept a running commentary in her logbook as though narrating her discoveries for an invisible audience. [92]
What drove her was singular and bright: she wanted to chart the next great discovery, something that would redraw the known world and earn her name a place on the Guild's etched ledger. [125]
She was diligently curious, filling every margin with questions, tracing phantom coastlines in the condensation of her cabin window, noting anomalies that other apprentices would dismiss. [151]
One evening, amid starling murmurations that swirled like living ink against the amber dusk, she found the phrasebook. [169]
It had been wedged behind a storage locker in the chart room -- a leather-bound volume, soft with age, its cover embossed with scripts in seven languages. [195]
A polyglot's companion, clearly: someone who had sailed far and spoken to many peoples. [209]
One page was dog-eared, its corner folded into a precise triangle, and on it a single passage had been underlined in faded violet ink. [233]
"The luminance does not wander," it read, in a trade-tongue Elen barely recognized. [246]
"It waits where children counted light." [252]
She read the line twice, then a third time, and felt the familiar tingle of a mystery unfurling. [270]
The next morning she searched the ship from gondola to gasbag, looking for anything that might answer the phrasebook's riddle. [290]
It was on the lower observation deck that she found it: a hopscotch board etched into the brass plating, half-obscured by decades of dust. [314]
Children had once crewed airships like these -- orphan cabin-boys and apprentices no older than herself -- and they had played here between shifts. [336]
She knelt and brushed the dust away with her sleeve. [346]
The squares were numbered, but not sequentially; some bore strange glyphs she did not recognize, and the spacing between them was irregular in a way that felt deliberate. [374]
Elen did what she always did: she noted the patterns. [384]
Working by lantern light as the murmurations outside thickened -- thousands of starlings wheeling in great pulsing shapes against the violet sky -- she copied every symbol, every measurement of distance between the hopscotch squares, onto fresh parchment. [420]
Then she overlaid the phrasebook's dog-eared page and held both up to the lantern. [434]
The underlined words matched the glyphs. [440]
Each symbol on the board corresponded to a word in the polyglot's passage, and the spacing between squares mirrored the angular measurements on her nautical charts. [466]
Her hands trembled as she realized what she was reading. [476]
The hopscotch board was a map. [482]
Not a game -- a coordinate system, hidden in plain sight by whoever had owned the phrasebook, encoding a location where the atmosphere trapped light itself. [507]
Stranded luminance: a phenomenon she had read about in Guild archives, theorized but never charted, a region where solar refraction and magnetic interference created a permanent, hovering glow -- an island of radiance in open sky. [542]
She worked through the night, cross-referencing the decoded coordinates with her existing charts, noting each adjustment with the careful precision her master had drilled into her. [568]
By dawn, the starlings had dispersed, and the sky was pale and ordinary. [581]
But on her drafting table lay something extraordinary: a course plotted toward coordinates that no Guild cartographer had ever recorded. [601]
Elen set her compass, adjusted the Meridian Drift's heading, and felt the old airship shudder awake beneath her feet. [620]
She was alone, yes -- but her logbook was full, her charts were alive with new ink, and somewhere ahead, light was waiting to be found. [645]
The phrasebook lay open on her desk, its dog-eared page fluttering in the breeze from the open hatch, as though the polyglot who had left it was cheering her onward. [675]
She smiled, picked up her pen, and began to note everything. [686]
