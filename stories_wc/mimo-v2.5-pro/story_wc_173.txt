Lian was the last chaste courtesan in the province of Mèng, and everyone knew it. [15]
Not chaste by accident or by failure, but by deliberate, radiant choice—the way one chooses silence in a room full of noise. [38]
She served the magistrate's court, poured wine for generals and poets alike, and never once let a hand linger on hers longer than a breath between sigh and smile. [67]
That silence was her art, and art, she believed, required a body as well as a voice. [84]
She was bodily poetic—the curve of her wrist when she lifted a cup, the geometry of her spine as she knelt—each gesture composed like calligraphy written in air. [114]
The courtiers called her a statue. [120]
She called herself a prism. [125]

At night, when the court slept, Lian walked to the silver courtyard under snow. [139]

Under the winter sky, the sanctuary hummed with an alarming serenity—peace so sharp it made the edges of things tremble. [199]
No one else visited it. [204]

Every visitor who passed through the courtyard left something behind—a laugh, a confession, a phrase whispered in grief—and the sanctuary kept them all. [239]

Lian came each night because she was looking for something no one had thought to search for: her own voice, unperformed. [267]
