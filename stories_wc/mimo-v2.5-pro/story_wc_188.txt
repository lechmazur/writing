The fortress had been built by people who believed walls could bloom, and so they had—stone by stone, each one fitted with soil-pouches and drip-lines, until the battlements cascaded with night-blooming jasmine and the towers wore crowns of wisteria. [50]

At dusk, when the light turned copper and the fortress seemed to exhale, Sorin arrived at its eastern gate with his cart of tubes, powders, and delicate glass shells. [80]

He was a wandering fireworks designer, which meant he belonged nowhere and to every sky simultaneously. [95]

He chanted while walking, always—a low melody carried from a childhood he could barely remember, syllables that didn't belong to any language he'd ever been taught. [124]

The guards didn't stop him. [131]

The fortress had been expecting someone like him, though it had stopped expecting anyone at all roughly seven winters ago, when the wars ended and no one remembered why they'd started. [163]

Inside, the courtyards were alive with flowering vines that strangled the old war-engines, turning catapults into trellises and siege towers into vertical gardens. [190]

It was beautiful in the way that old grief sometimes transforms into something resembling peace. [206]

Sorin set up in the central plaza, beneath a canopy of honeysuckle, and began to filter his powders through ambient awareness—the way his master had taught him, feeling the weight and texture and chemical memory of each grain between his fingers, letting his skin decide what the eyes could not. [253]

Some mixtures needed brightness, others needed patience, and a few needed something close to sorrow. [271]

He was preparing the night's display for a fortress that had forgotten celebration, and he knew the stakes. [290]

His motivation had always been simple: to defy the gravity of hopelessness. [305]

Not with grand speeches or revolutions, but with fire arranged into temporary miracles. [320]

As he worked, he noticed a fire spinner practicing near the fountain—a young woman named Veda, whose staff left trails of orange light against the purple sky. [350]

She was rehearsing for the evening's gathering, and when she stopped to rest, she set her tools down with a care that struck Sorin as reverent. [377]

Among her equipment was a fire spinner's kerosene rag knot, wound tight and soaked, the kind that burned slow and blue and gave spinners their ghostly afterimages. [408]

The knot was old, its fabric softened by years of use, and Sorin recognized it as the sort of object that accumulates stories the way stones accumulate moss. [438]

"You filter your powders by feel," Veda said, watching him work. [452]

"Through ambient awareness," he replied, not looking up. [464]

"You can hear the quality of a chemical by listening to the silence it makes when you move it." [484]

She laughed—a serious playfulness that matched the fortress itself, grave and whimsical in equal measure. [502]

"My grandmother left me this," she said, holding up the knot. [516]

"She said that inside the weave, there was a note in mosaic—tiny tiles of colored glass pressed between the fabric layers, spelling something she never read to me." [548]

Sorin stopped filtering. [554]

He looked at the knot and understood something the way he understood his powders—without seeing it directly, through the weight of the object and the silence it carried. [582]

"May I?" he asked. [589]

She handed it over, and he held it up to the last of the dusk, turning it slowly, letting the fading light pass through the layers. [614]

There, caught between folds of kerosene-stained cloth, fragments of colored glass arranged themselves into letters: *We bloom because the walls told us to.* [646]

He read it aloud, chanting the words the way he chanted his walking melodies, and Veda's eyes widened. [666]

"That was her message," she whispered. [676]

"The fortress bloomed because someone believed it should." [689]

Sorin returned the knot and resumed his work, moving faster now, his fingers finding the right combinations with renewed certainty. [712]

During the hush of a night with no stars, when the clouds pressed low and the world felt sealed, he launched his fireworks from the blooming fortress walls. [742]

They climbed like questions, then answered themselves in cascades of gold and silver and deep, impossible violet. [760]

Veda spun her staff beneath them, trailing fire, and the people of the fortress—those who had nearly forgotten wonder—stood in their vine-strangled courtyards and looked up. [791]
