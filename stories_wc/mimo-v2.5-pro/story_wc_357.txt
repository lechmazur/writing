<story>Merin was an intrepid orchard pilot, one of the few licensed to fly solo across Verath's labyrinth of floating rock arches and fruit-bearing groves. [24] For three seasons, she had mapped the planet's upper canopy, following water channels that threaded between root systems in deliberate, curving lines. [46] She navigated by water flow the way ancient sailors navigated by stars—where the currents converged, the orchards grew thickest; where they scattered, the trees thinned toward barren stone. [75] Tonight, the sky changed. [79] Every seventy-one days, gravity on Verath inverted for exactly one hour. [90] Rivers reversed. Fruit tumbled upward. The floating rock arches sank slowly toward the ground while the surface bucked beneath them like a breathing thing. [114] Merin had timed her flight to catch this window. [123] She believed the answer to her question—the source of the orchards' impossible resilience—could only be reached when the world turned inside out. [147] She killed her engines and let the inversion carry her skimmer upward, drifting through a canopy that now hung below her like an inverted cathedral. [172] The silence was immediate and strange—a whirling silence, as if the air itself had begun to rotate without sound, pressing against her eardrums like held breath. [199] Then the echoes came. [203] Not one echo, but hundreds—a gathering of echoes, layered and shimmering, rising from every surface as the inverted gravity pulled sound along new paths. [228] Voices of the groves: sap splitting bark, roots grinding stone, pollen striking water—all compressed into a single, reverberant chord that seemed to emanate from one point ahead. [256] Merin floated toward it, steering with small bursts of compressed air, following the acoustic convergence the way she had always followed the water. [279] The sound led her to the innermost arch, the one pilots called the Keystone—a massive ring of basalt suspended above a pool so still it looked frozen. [307] During normal gravity, the pool drained into underground caves, inaccessible and dark. [319] But now, with the inversion pulling water skyward, the caves were exposed: a hollow space beneath the arch, dry and glittering. [340] Merin touched down on the cave floor and stepped out into whirling silence broken only by the gathering echoes that surrounded her like a living choir. [366] The walls were covered in root structures she had never seen—fibrous, crystalline, pulsing with faint light. [383] They grew from a single point in the center of the chamber, radiating outward like the spokes of a wheel. [403] And at that center point, embedded in the rock itself, was a broken arrow tip. [418] It was small—no longer than her finger—but the roots clung to it the way rivers cling to their source. [439] Merin knelt and touched the metal. [445] It was warm. [448] The root structures responded immediately, surging outward in a visible wave, new tendrils sprouting from the walls and ceiling. [467] She watched, breathless, as a thin vine erupted from the bare stone beneath her palm, unfurling a leaf in seconds. [487] The broken arrow tip accelerated plant growth—every seed, every spore, every dormant cell within reach responded to its proximity. [507] This was the source of resilience. [513] Not some atmospheric condition or mineral deposit, but an artifact—ancient, fractured, half-buried—feeding the entire planet's orchards from a single, forgotten wound. [536] Merin sat back on her heels as the echoes swirled around her, their gathering chorus now almost musical. [554] She thought of the agricultural councils, the failed investigations, the years of satellite scans that had missed what lay beneath the water. [576] She thought of what would happen when she reported this—the expeditions, the extraction attempts, the inevitable arguments about ownership and use. [598] The vine beneath her hand had already reached the cave entrance, climbing toward the light. [613] The inversion hour was ending. [618] She could feel gravity beginning to reassert itself, the echoes thinning as water began its slow return to the caves below. [639] Merin made her decision. [643] She photographed the arrow tip, recorded the coordinates, and sealed the chamber entrance with loose stone. [659] She would report the location but not the cause—let them find it themselves, the way she had, by following the water and listening to what the silence carried. [688] When gravity restored itself and the rivers roared back into their channels, Merin flew low over the orchards, watching the trees sway in the moonlight as if nothing had changed. [718] But something had. [721] She had discovered the planet's secret and chosen, for now, to let it remain one. [736]</story>
