<story>The package arrived on a Tuesday, which Marlow already distrusted as a day for beginnings. [15] It sat on the undertaker's workbench among the usual shipments—embalming fluid, pine caskets, sympathy cards printed in fonts that pretended grief was simple. [39] But this box bore no return address, only a string of numbers he recognized as coordinates, and when he pried the lid, there lay a lighthouse lens, fractured into seven clean pieces like a compass rose that had been asked too many questions. [82]

Marlow had been an undertaker for thirty-one years. [90] He had grown so weary of endings that his hands moved through each burial with the detached grace of sleepwalking. [110] He was not unkind. [114] He was simply finished with the ceremony of it—the eulogies that said the same thing in different voices, the way mourners touched the casket lid as though the wood could transmit forgiveness. [147] What Marlow wanted, and could never quite articulate, was to give rest a permanent address. [162] Not a grave. [165] Not a plot. [168] A place where the tired dead could finally stop being summoned back by grief. [182]

He held one of the lens fragments to the light and heard it. [195] A signal—a low, keening frequency that settled behind his eyes like a headache that remembered music. [212] He turned the piece slowly, and the signal sharpened into coordinates. [223] Not the kind on any chart. [229] The kind that exist in the margins of things, in the space between sleeping and waking, in the impossible geometry of loss. [251]

Marlow packed a bag. [255] He did not question why. [260] This was his inadvertent stubbornness—a trait he never recognized in himself because it wore the disguise of routine. [279] Others saw him as flexible, even passive. [286] But underneath, Marlow moved toward things the way water moves toward a drain: not with urgency, but with an inevitability no wall could redirect. [310]

The coordinates led him to a stretch of coastline he had driven past a hundred times and never seen. [329] A stone staircase descended into the sea, and at the bottom, impossibly, air. [342] He walked down into an underwater city—its streets thick with kelp, its buildings lit by bioluminescent algae that pulsed like slow heartbeats. [365] And at the center: a library. [371]

The library of Drenthis had been forgotten for centuries, swallowed when the ocean decided it wanted to read. [389] Its shelves stretched in every direction, and every book was a record of someone who had died without being properly mourned—whose rest had been interrupted, revoked, or never granted. [419] The distress signals Marlow had heard were coming from here, from thousands of unshelved souls vibrating in the water like plucked strings. [441]

He began at the first shelf. [447] The task was impossible—there were more books than any lifetime could catalog, and the library itself occupied impossible spaces, rooms that existed inside other rooms, corridors that doubled back through dimensions geometry had not yet named. [484] Marlow would step through a doorway and find himself in a reading room the size of a cathedral, then turn and squeeze through a gap between two shelves so narrow it could not have held a body, yet there he was, occupying it, breathing its ancient air. [531]

Each soul he placed received a card. [538] Name. [539] Date. [540] Duration of unrest. [543] And one line Marlow wrote himself: Here is your address—you may stop traveling now. [558]

The library began to change. [563] The bioluminescence brightened from its sickly pulse to something warm, almost golden. [575] Shelves that had been scattered and chaotic rearranged themselves into rows. [586] The water grew stiller. [590] Marlow felt the chasing stop—the constant pull of unfinished deaths, the pressure of work he had carried like stones in his coat. [613] Chased stillness, he realized, was not the absence of motion. [623] It was motion finally arriving where it was meant to be. [634]

He did not know how long he worked. [642] Time in Drenthis moved the way the dead move—without hurry, without resistance. [655] When he surfaced, the lens had reassembled itself on his workbench, whole and shining, and the package was gone. [674]

Marlow returned to his funeral home. [680] The bodies still arrived. [684] The mourners still touched the caskets. [690] But now, when he performed the last rites, he did not speak into emptiness. [704] He spoke into an address—a permanent, underwater place where rest was not a metaphor but a location, cataloged and shelved and waiting. [727]

He was still weary. [731] But the weariness had somewhere to live now, and that made all the difference. [745]</story>
