She died in April, but the desk seal still sits on my windowsill, heavy as a conscience I never earned. [20]

It started with sugar. [24]
Elara knocked on my door one September evening, holding an empty ceramic bowl, and asked to borrow enough for a single cup of tea. [46] I gave it to her. [51] She returned the next week with scones. [58] The week after that, she brought loose-leaf chamomile in a tin. [68] By October, we had migrated to the rooftop garden above our building, where someone had arranged wooden planters and wrought-iron chairs into a kind of accidental sanctuary. [95] Every Thursday at dusk, we sat among the rosemary and spoke until the city lights swallowed the stars. [113]

Elara had been a dead language tutor — Sumerian, Akkadian, fragments of Linear A that no living mouth had shaped in millennia. [132] But what she truly wanted was to learn the language of old buildings. [143] She pressed her palms against the limestone facade of our apartment and listened, as though the mortar between the stones might still whisper the intentions of the men who laid them. [175] "Every building has a grammar," she told me once, her fingers tracing the cornice above our doorway. [193] "Load-bearing walls are sentences. Archways are subordinate clauses. You just have to know where the punctuation falls." [213]

I never asked what she meant by that, not really. [223] That was one of our agreed-upon lies — the pretense that I understood her more than I did, balanced against her pretense that my confusion didn't matter. [251] We maintained these fictions the way the garden maintained its roses: with patience, with routine, with a nimble willingness to prune whatever threatened to overgrow. [280]

She was patiently nimble in everything. [287] When she taught me three words of Sumerian — kalam, é, and nam-lú-u₁₈-lu, meaning land, house, and humanity — she moved her hands through the air as if sculpting the syllables from clay. [320] When we walked through the old quarter downtown, she would pause at certain doorways, interrogate the hinges with her fingertips, and declare whether the building remembered kindness or cruelty. [353] I believed her. [357] I needed to believe her. [362]

The desk seal appeared on my doorstep the morning after her funeral, left in a box with no note. [380] It was bronze, tarnished green at the edges, with a cuneiform inscription pressed into its flat face. [398] I recognized two of the symbols from her lessons — the wedge marks for é and kalam — but the third was unfamiliar, a cluster of impressions she had never shown me. [430] House, land, and something else. [436]

Tonight the meteor shower draws the rooftop crowd upward, but I have come alone. [451] I set the seal on the iron table where she once balanced her teacup, and the meteors begin their silent argument with the atmosphere — bright, temporary, indifferent. [485] I hold the seal in both hands and try to interrogate it the way she interrogated buildings: with patience, with the willingness to wait for an answer that may never arrive. [520]

The rooftop garden has changed since April. [528] The rosemary has overtaken the planter nearest the east wall, and someone has strung paper lanterns between the vents. [551] But the iron chairs remain, and the particular angle of sky between the water tower and the neighboring chimney is unchanged. [576] This is what old buildings do, I realize. [584] They agree to hold their shape while everything around them renegotiates its meaning. [600]

I press the seal into the soft soil of the nearest planter and lift it away. [616] The impression it leaves is clean, three symbols holding their position in the earth. [634] House. Land. And the third mark, which I now decide means "witness." [650]

The meteors continue their falling. [657]
The rosemary smells exactly the way it did on Thursdays. [668]
I do not wipe the soil from the seal. [677]

This is how the language of old buildings works, I think. [690] It is not spoken. [695] It is pressed into things and left there, patient and nimble, waiting for someone willing to read what was never meant to be translated. [727]

The garden keeps its agreement. [734]
The seal keeps its silence. [740]
I keep my Thursdays. [746]
