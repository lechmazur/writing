Marguerite had worked the odd hours at the Selvedge Scientific Poetry Library for eleven years—the late closings and early openings, the liminal stretches when the building's old heating system ticked like a metronome counting beats no one else could hear. [41]
She was, by any reasonable measure, sanely eccentric. [49]
She believed the library was alive—not metaphorically but structurally—and that its architecture contained a hidden geometry that, if followed correctly, could lead to the place where stories went after being read, the place where they lived between tellings. [89]
Her colleagues humored her. [93]
The director called her "our resident mystic" at staff meetings, always with a fond, diminishing smile. [109]
But Marguerite had studied topology before choosing librarianship, and she knew that surfaces could hold more than their apparent area suggested—that a crumpled sheet of paper contained, in its folds, vastly more surface than a flat one. [147]
On the highest shelf in the east wing, among volumes of ekphrastic verse about protein folding and chromatic spectra, she kept a tattered silk scarf wrapped around a first edition of Ramanujan's notebooks. [180]
The scarf had belonged to her grandmother, a mathematician at Yunnan University before emigrating, and it still carried the faint analog memory of her skin and lavender oil—proof, Marguerite felt, that objects held what digital storage could never capture: the weight of contact, the residue of being held. [229]
Tonight, while patrons browsed the lower floors—two grad students arguing over whether Mandelbrot sets constituted landscape poetry, a retired physicist reading aloud to herself from "The Quantum Rose"—Marguerite climbed the spiral staircase to the restricted mezzanine. [267]
She unrolled the scarf across a reading table. [275]
Its silk was so worn that light passed through it in scattered constellations, and when she held it up to the reading lamp, she noticed something she had overlooked for years: the weave followed a hyperbolic pattern, each thread curving away from its neighbors at angles that defied Euclidean flatness. [325]
Her grandmother had always said that the most important truths lived in the folds. [339]
Marguerite had assumed this was poetry. [345]
Now she understood it was geometry. [351]
She pressed the scarf flat against the table and traced the curves with her fingertip, following each negative curvature as it spiraled inward, cataloging the way the fabric resisted being pressed smooth, the way it insisted on its own dimensionality. [391]
The library's floor plan, she realized, mirrored the pattern exactly—the shelves radiating outward in exponential curves, the reading alcoves nested like lobes of a Riemann surface. [418]
She had walked these paths a thousand times without seeing them. [429]
The building had been waiting for her to read it. [439]
As her finger completed the final curve, something in the architecture shifted. [451]
Not physically—the walls held, the shelves stood—but dimensionally. [461]
The space between the books deepened, reawakened into a fullness she had only theorized about. [476]
She could see, or rather sense, a vastness opening behind the spines, as though each volume had been a door she'd never thought to open. [501]
This was where stories lived: not on the pages, but in the hyperbolic space between them, in the infinite surface area that curved away from every point, always expanding, never bounded. [532]
Every story ever shelved here had seeped through its cover and continued, metabolized into something that breathed in the geometry of the room. [555]
Marguerite sat very still. [559]
She did not move to touch the space or call out to it. [572]
She had spent eleven years in observed solitude—watched by no one and watching everything—and now the solitude was complete: she could see the thing she'd been looking for, and it was not a place a person could enter. [612]
It was a place that entered you, slowly, the way lavender oil entered silk. [626]
Below, the retired physicist turned a page. [633]
The grad students fell quiet. [638]
The heating system ticked its steady count. [645]
Marguerite folded the scarf along its old creases and returned it to the shelf beside Ramanujan's dreams of infinity. [664]
She descended the stairs. [668]
She did not tell anyone what she had found—not because it was a secret, but because the place where stories live does not survive the telling. [695]
It survives the looking. [699]
She straightened a display of Bekenstein's entropy verses near the front desk and smiled at the physicist, who smiled back without knowing why. [722]
The library breathed. [725]
