The poet has been walking since before the energy beings arrived, carrying a frayed coil of rope across three provinces and a border no one patrols anymore. [27]
The rope came from a prison wall—a real prison, concrete and guards, where someone the poet loved had spent eleven years studying the weakness in a window frame. [56]
That person cut through the frame one winter night, looped the rope over the sill, and dropped into darkness, calling back once: "Follow me." [80]
The poet didn't. [83]
That was the first impossible choice, though not the last, and the poet has carried the rope ever since the way some people carry guilt—openly, almost cheerfully, with the ethical playfulness of someone who knows that pretending not to hurt is its own kind of dishonesty. [130]
Now the poet sits in a terra-cotta grotto, the walls glowing faintly amber under starlight that pours through a crack in the low ceiling. [154]
Above, the energy beings drift like auroras brought low, their luminous bodies threading between trees and stone, reshaping shadows into geometries that didn't exist six months ago. [181]
They don't speak, don't land, don't acknowledge the people who shout questions at them from rooftops. [197]
They simply are, and their presence has changed the texture of everything—light bends differently, sound carries farther, and time, the poet suspects, has begun to pool in certain places like water finding low ground. [232]
The poet came here to feel that pooling. [240]
To discover the texture of time itself. [247]
This is the poet's obsession: not fame, not love, not even beauty, but the granular substance of moments, how they press against one another, how they thin when you're waiting and thicken when you're afraid. [282]
The rope rests in the poet's lap, coarse and warm. [292]
It smells of salt and sweat and something faintly metallic, like a key left in rain. [308]
The poet runs fingers along its frayed end, uncovering layers—first the brittle outer fibers, then the twisted core dark with old strain, then something small and hard buried inside the weave. [340]
A note, folded tight as a seed. [347]
The poet opens it carefully. [352]
The handwriting belongs to the prisoner—the one who escaped, the one the poet failed to follow into the dark. [372]
Three lines, written in the steady hand of someone who had eleven years to choose every word: [389]
"I didn't cut the rope to free myself. I cut it to free you. You were always the one in the cell." [411]
The poet sits with this a long time, turning the note over as though its back might hold a rebuttal. [431]
Outside, an energy being drifts close to the grotto mouth, its light casting strange patterns on the terra-cotta walls—circles within circles, spirals that seem to breathe. [458]
The poet watches the patterns and begins to see: they're not random. [470]
They're a language. [473]
Not of words but of tempo, of acceleration and pause, of time expressing its own nature in shapes the poet can almost read. [496]
The poet takes charcoal from the fire ring and writes on the grotto wall, tracing the energy being's spirals, translating them into lines of verse. [521]
Each poem is a layer uncovered. [527]
First, the surface poem—the one about beauty, about light. [537]
Then, beneath it, the harder poem about grief, about the night the poet stood at the prison wall and chose to stay. [559]
Then, beneath that, the poem the poet didn't want to write: that staying was not cowardice but a different kind of courage, that some escapes require you to remain exactly where you are. [592]
The prisoner knew this and left the rope behind not as a tool but as a mirror. [609]
This is the impossible choice the poet keeps remaking: to follow or to stay, to cut the rope or to let it hold, to chase time or to sit still until it reveals itself. [643]
The energy being enters the grotto. [649]
Its light is warm and without shadow. [656]
It doesn't speak, but the poet feels something—pressure, invitation, a question asked in the language of spirals. [674]
The poet understands, at last, what the texture of time feels like: it feels like rope. [690]
Twisted, frayed, strong enough to bear weight and weak enough to snap. [702]
It feels like a choice you keep making, not once but forever, each repetition a new strand woven into the cord. [723]
The poet writes one final line on the wall and sets the charcoal down. [738]
The energy being's light pulses once—acknowledgment, perhaps, or farewell—and drifts back into the starlit sky. [755]
The poem reads: "The rope was never to escape. The rope was the texture. Hold it." [771]
The poet stays in the grotto, holding the frayed coil, feeling the night breathe around the walls of amber clay. [791]
