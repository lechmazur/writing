The umbral grotto hung above the clouds like a dark pearl nestled in the throat of the mountain. [18] Eira had tended it for eleven years, sweeping basalt floors, trimming moss that crept over shrine stones, and keeping the sacred pool clear of needles from the ancient cedars. [47] She was a committed caretaker--every soul who climbed the cloud stairs to pray or grieve left feeling attended to, though few could name what she had done to ease them. [78]

Three days ago, she had forgiven her sister Morwen. [87] It had not been easy. [92] Morwen had stolen the temple's offerings, sold the bronze bells, and left the grotto exposed to wind and silence for an entire season while Eira lay ill far below the clouds. [123] When Eira finally returned, half the cedars had died from neglect, their bark splitting like old skin. [140] The shrine stones were bare. [145] The pool had turned a color she did not recognize, greenish and opaque, as though the water itself had grown resentful. [166]

But Eira was kindly decisive. [171] She understood that carrying the weight of Morwen's betrayal would change nothing--not the dead trees, not the tarnished stones, not the silence where bells should have rung. [199] So she wrote a letter, folded it once, and sent it down the cloud stairs with a runner. [217] It contained only three words: I forgive you. [225]

Now she needed to find something she had lost long before Morwen's betrayal--the source of contentment that had once made her step lighter than air across this precipice. [254]

She climbed a cedar. [258] This was her method, and it had never failed her. [268] The first branch was wide as a hearth, moss-soft and patient, and she pulled herself onto it the way she had since childhood--forearms, belly, then legs swinging over. [297] The second branch rose at an angle she trusted. [306] The third put her above the canopy, and there, breaking through the crown, she could see the clouds rolling below like a pale, endless field. [331] The grotto was a dark mouth in the cliff beneath her. [342] Eagles circled at a distance, slow as prayers. [350] The world was quiet. [354]

From her coat pocket she took the antique hairpin. [363] It had belonged to the grotto's first caretaker, a woman named Alis, who had planted the cedars three hundred years before Eira was born. [387] The hairpin was carved from bone, shaped like a spray of clematis, its tip worn smooth by centuries of hands turning it in contemplation. [411] Eira held it against the light and watched how the bone glowed faintly, the way old wood does when it remembers being alive. [434]

She had been told, when she first took her post, that the cycle of rebirth was written into every object in the grotto--that wood becomes soil, soil becomes stone, stone becomes water, and water feeds the root. [472] The hairpin had been carved from the rib of an ox that had fed on grass growing from the grave of the woman who shaped it. [498] Everything returned. [500]

Eira turned the pin slowly, and in the turning, she began to scallop the edge of a piece of bark she had stripped from a lower branch. [527] It was a small, curved motion--thumb pressing, blade pulling--creating the wavy pattern that Alis had used on the original shrine shutters. [550] She had learned it from the old texts. [558] The scalloping was not practical. [563] It was a gesture, a way of saying: I am here, I am still tending, I accept what cannot be fixed. [584]

Below her, the clouds shifted, and for a moment the vineyards of the lower terraces appeared--rows of vines so distant they looked like stitching on cloth. [611] The vineyard hush reached her even here, that particular silence of growing things, dense and warm and unhurried. [629] She breathed it in. [633]

Contentment, she realized, had never been a thing she needed to find. [645] It was the climb itself, the hush, the returning to what was broken and choosing to tend it anyway. [664] It was the hairpin in her palm, the scalloped bark, the forgiving of the unforgivable. [679] It was the knowledge that the cycle would continue--cedars dying, cedars growing, stone softening, water pooling, a new caretaker appearing one day to sit where Eira now sat. [708]

She descended the tree slowly, the bark in her pocket, the hairpin fixed in her hair, the silence of the high place settling around her like a cloak. [736] The grotto waited below, dark and patient, ready to be tended again. [748]
