Elara moved through the corridors of the Thesaurum—the bookstore that consumed the whole of Mount Seren, its shelves carved from living rock, its passages branching like the vasculature of an ancient body. [33]
She had come here after remembering became painful, after every thought of the village where she'd been born arrived like a bruise pressed too hard against bone. [60]
By profession, she was a mercury-glass seer: someone who read futures and forgotten pasts in the pooled quicksilver of an orb her grandmother had left her, watching shapes form and dissolve like smoke trapped in water. [96]
But lately the orb had gone dark, and she carried it wrapped in oilcloth at the bottom of her satchel, because abandoning it felt like abandoning the last person who had known her before she'd become someone else. [134]
Every night she substituted reality. [139]
She'd find an alcove between shelves of forgotten philosophy and untranslatable poetry, close her eyes, and rebuild the world as she wished it had been—the village river running clear, her mother's face unhurt, the door of her childhood home open and welcoming instead of locked against her forever. [188]
She'd wake with the substitution still clinging to her, and the morning light would burn it away, and she'd feel the loss all over again. [213]
It was on the forty-seventh day that she found the stone. [224]
It sat on a low shelf in a passage so narrow she had to turn sideways, nestled between two volumes bound in cracked leather. [248]
A smooth dark stone no larger than her palm, with symbols etched into its surface in a script she'd never seen and yet, when she traced the grooves with her fingertip, felt as familiar as her own pulse. [286]
The lost language stone. [290]
She'd read about such artifacts—stones carved in tongues that had been spoken and then forgotten, carrying meaning that existed outside grammar, outside syntax, in the raw tissue beneath language itself. [321]
She needed a method to unlock it. [328]
She sat cross-legged on the stone floor and pulled from her satchel the feather—a single quill from a bird she could no longer name, its barbs so fine they caught light like spun glass. [363]
She drew the feather across the surface of the stone. [373]
The texture of the feather against the etched symbols made a sound too small for ears, a vibration that traveled through the stone into her fingertips and up her arms and into the marrow of her. [409]
And then she understood. [413]
Not words. Not sentences. But shapes—the shapes of things she'd buried so deep she'd mistaken them for absence. [432]
She had buried her talent. [437]
Not just the mercury-glass reading, which had been her grandmother's gift, but her own—the ability to hear what stones remembered, to feel the pressure of forgotten speech against her skin, to carry entire languages in the silence of her body. [478]
She had buried it because the village had told her such things were dangerous. [492]
Because her mother had said, "Hide what you are, or they will come for you," and so she had hidden it, layered it beneath obedience and silence until she'd forgotten it was there. [525]
The stone pulsed against her hand. [531]
She understood now the shape of home—not the village, not the locked door, not the river running brown, but the language inside her that had never been given a voice. [562]
Home was not a place she'd lost. [569]
It was a talent she'd never allowed herself to use. [579]
She pressed the feather into the grooves one final time and closed her eyes, and this time she did not substitute reality. [601]
She let reality arrive. [605]
It came like hidden thunder—not the kind that splits the sky, but the kind that rolls through the ground beneath you, the kind you feel in your teeth and your ribs, the kind that has been sounding all along while you pretended not to hear. [651]
She opened her eyes. [655]
The labyrinthine corridors of the Thesaurum stretched in every direction, and she saw them now not as passages but as sentences, each shelf a clause, each alcove a pause between words, the whole mountain a single vast utterance that had been waiting for someone to read it aloud. [703]
She stood and placed the mercury-glass orb on the shelf where the stone had been, bright and reflective, already catching the shape of the next reader who would come down this narrow passage and find it waiting. [740]
She had no more need of borrowed sight. [748]
She had the stone, and the feather, and the language that moved through her like weather, and she walked deeper into the mountain's pages, seeding each one with a voice that had always, always known how to speak. [786]
