Lior had always been the worst mage in his cohort, the one whose charms fizzled and whose wards collapsed like wet paper, but nobody cared about grades anymore because the Engines had arrived and automated everything that once required a human hand. [47]
Creativity itself had been outsourced, algorithmically distilled into matrices of probability that spat out perfectly calibrated spells faster than any living mage could blink, and so Lior found himself redundant before he'd even begun. [45]
His final commission, issued with bureaucratic indifference, was to ascend the icebound spire above the clouds and retrieve a garden folly keystone that the Automated Bureau deemed "of negligible magical value." [38]
The spire rose from a glacier shelf like a frozen scream, its walls slick with ancient rime, and when Lior stepped from the cloud-lift onto its narrow balcony, the wind nearly threw him back into the sky. [43]
He steadied himself and began the long spiral upward through corridors of blue ice, his breath crystallizing in lantern-light, each step a negotiation with vertigo and self-doubt. [32]
Halfway up, the mist thickened until it swallowed the walls entirely, and Lior remembered the old technique they'd taught in the first year before the Engines made such things quaint: mist walking, the art of finding solidity in vapor, stepping where no step should hold. [47]
He closed his eyes, let his weight shift forward, and the mist accepted him—each footfall landing on something that was neither air nor ground but a compromise between belief and physics. [37]
It was the first spell he'd ever cast properly, and its quiet success unsettled him more than any failure had. [20]
At the spire's summit, in a chamber open to the stars, he found the keystone resting on a pedestal of frozen moss—a small, rough-cut block of gray stone with a carved notch along its base, the kind of thing that once capped a decorative arch in a nobleman's garden, a folly built for beauty's sake alone. [56]
When Lior lifted it, the stone hummed with a warmth that defied the chamber's killing cold, and he understood what the Bureau had overlooked: the keystone accommodates impossibilities. [34]
It could hold the weight of what should not exist—grief and gratitude in the same breath, failure and fulfillment carved into one face—and it had done so for centuries, quietly, without complaint. [36]
A faint glow pulsed from the notch, not bright but persistent, a lingering luminance that seemed to have no source, as if the stone remembered every hand that had ever placed it and every garden it had ever crowned. [42]
Lior sat on the ice floor and turned the keystone over, studying the tool-ridges along its edges, the marks of someone who had shaped it without Engines, without algorithms, with nothing but intention and a chisel and the willingness to get it imperfectly right. [46]
He thought about the spire itself, how someone had built it stone by stone into the clouds, an act of magnificent uselessness, a folly on a grander scale than any garden arch, and yet here it stood, accommodating the impossible altitude, the impossible cold, the impossible stillness above the world. [54]
Carefully, he unstrap the leather case from his back—the one his mentor had given him years ago, filled with hand-tools and chalks and pigments ground from minerals he could no longer name—and spread its contents across the ice. [44]
The tools glinted with that same lingering luminance, as though the keystone had recognized them, or perhaps they had always carried it and he had simply never been still enough to notice. [37]
He took the smallest chisel and pressed its tip to a scrap of ice, not to carve anything useful, not to impress the Bureau or prove his worth, but simply to feel the resistance of a material under his own hand, to make a mark that mattered only because he had made it. [54]
The shaving curled away, thin as breath, and in that small act Lior found what he had crossed the clouds to discover: the sacred in the simple, the dignity of effort that produces nothing marketable, nothing scalable, nothing an Engine would bother replicating. [47]
He did not hurry. [5]
The stars wheeled overhead, indifferent and beautiful, and the spire held him in its brilliant dimness—a light that was not bright enough to dazzle but too steady to ignore, the kind of illumination that asks you to lean closer rather than shield your eyes. [47]
When Lior finally descended, keystone wrapped in cloth against his chest, he carried something the Bureau would never inventory: the understanding that his underachievement had never been a lack of magic but a surplus of it, ungovernable and human, impossible and therefore exactly the kind of thing the stone was built to hold. [56]
The mist parted for him on the way down, and he walked it without closing his eyes. [17]</story>
