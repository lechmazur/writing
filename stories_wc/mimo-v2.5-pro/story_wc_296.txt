The druid Maren had not spoken to another soul in eleven days, which suited her fine, because every word she spent was a wall she lost. [29] She was a guarded woman by nature—arms folded, eyes averted, voice kept low and spare—and the villages she passed through learned quickly that she would find their lost rings and missing children but would not stay for supper. [61] Finding things was her gift, though she called it a burden: she could sense the absence of objects the way other druids sensed the presence of roots, could feel the negative space where something used to be and follow it like a scent trail through the world. [107] It was this sense that drew her now to the shores of Lake Vessara, where the water churned in slow, dark spirals and the air smelled of rotting sweetness. [135]

At the heart of the cursed lake, an orchard grew—impossibly, grotesquely—from the water itself. [153] Trees spiraled upward from the vortex, their trunks twisted, their branches heavy with fruit that was almost beautiful and almost wrong, and the whole formation turned in expanding circles like a living whirlpool made of wood and leaf and hunger. [198] Maren stood at the reed-choked edge and felt the absence so strongly it made her teeth ache: something had been lost here, something central, and the lake had been curdling around the wound for decades. [238]

The village elder, a hollow-cheeked man named Drest, had told her the story before she left his doorstep without eating. [262] Forty years ago, a traveling illusionist had come to perform magic for the harvest festival. [279] His final trick was to make the lake bloom with a floating orchard, a spectacle of conjured blossoms that would delight the crowd. [303] But the trick failed—catastrophically, grotesquely—and instead of illusions, the magic struck root and the orchard became real, a permanent vortex of carnivorous growth anchored to the lake's heart. [341] The trees bore flesh-eating plant seeds in their fruit, and each season those seeds drifted closer to shore. [363]

What Drest had not told her, but what Maren found pressed into the bark of the outermost tree like a fossil, was a confession. [387] It was frozen in place—literally crystallized, the words etched in amber-like resin as though the speaker had been mid-sentence when time stopped around them: *I did not fail. I planted it on purpose. You must not let it* [424] The confession ended there, truncated by whatever force had halted it, and Maren traced the letters with her fingertip and felt the immense, warlike serenity of understanding settle over her. [458] The illusionist had not made a mistake. He had seeded the curse deliberately, and someone—or something—had frozen his admission before he could finish it. [490]

Maren circled the vortex in expanding circles, spiraling outward from the nearest tree, then wider, then wider still, letting her finding-sense pull her through the pattern like thread through a loom. [529] Each loop revealed something: a second frozen phrase caught in a root, a third pressed into a leaf. [552] Together they assembled themselves—*on purpose. You must not let it reach the mirror orchard in the valley below. What grows there cannot be reflected. What cannot be reflected cannot be seen.* [590]

Here was the answer to what she had always sought. [602] Mirrors did not show everything; her whole life had taught her that surfaces lied, that what gleamed back at you was only the flattering skin of things. [631] But the flesh-eating orchard and the frozen confession revealed what lay beneath: a world of things that existed precisely because they could not be seen, that grew strongest in the blind spots of every reflective surface. [672] The illusionist had tried to warn them, and someone had stopped his tongue. [688]

Maren reached into her coat and withdrew the one flesh-eating plant seed she had pried from a fallen fruit on the outer ring. [713] It was warm and pulsed faintly, as though dreaming. [726] She could unbend the curse, she realized—not by destroying the orchard, but by planting this seed where the confession could finally complete itself, in soil where no enchantment could freeze speech again. [758] She was guarded, yes. She had always been guarded. [770] But some lost things demanded that you open your hands. [783]

She stepped into the vortex. [789]</story>
