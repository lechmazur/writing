Maren had not slept in the way others understood sleep, not since the carousel halted and the lagoon lost its rhythm. [19]
She knelt at the edge of the kinetic lagoon, where water moved in slow spirals above the clouds, catching light that had no source and casting it in fractured patterns across her weathered hands. [42]
The lagoon breathed, expanding and contracting like a sleeping lung, and each pulse sent ripples toward the horizon where clouds stacked in bruised towers. [68]
She was a scavenger—resolute, deliberate, unhurried by the cold—and she had been gathering things from the lagoon's surface for longer than she could accurately measure. [95]
Time, she had learned, was unreliable here. [103]
Mornings arrived twice, or not at all. [111]
She would remember yesterday's conversation as something that happened next week, or she would recognize a stranger's face and know, with sudden certainty, that she had not yet met them. [142]
This chronological confusion did not distress her anymore; it was simply the weather of this place, the way the lagoon twisted experience into knots she could not untie. [173]
Today she found the disposable camera wheel half-buried in silt near the shallows, its plastic housing cracked and fogged but its spool still intact, a strip of exposed film curling from it like a tongue. [211]
She lifted it carefully. [216]
On the film, barely visible, were images: a woman laughing, a doorway, a child's hand reaching for something out of frame. [236]
None of these were hers. [241]
That was the point. [245]
The scavenger's method had always been to collect fragments of overheard stories, to gather the narratives that drifted through the lagoon like pollen—spoken by travelers passing on makeshift rafts, whispered by the old woman who sold fog in glass jars, murmured by the sleepers who floated facedown and dreaming on the warmest currents. [302]
Maren would listen, absorb, and preserve. [310]
She kept these stories in boxes she had built from carousel wood, stacked in the ruins of the platform where the great machine had once turned. [336]
The carousel had been beautiful, she was told—brightly painted horses rising and falling, music threaded through its rotation like a heartbeat. [360]
But the gears had seized long before she arrived, and now it stood motionless, its painted horses staring at nothing, their expressions frozen mid-gallop. [388]
She sat among them now, the camera wheel in her lap, and tried to remember her own ambition. [409]
It had been something once. [414]
She was sure of that. [419]
There had been a reason she climbed above the clouds, a reason she chose this lagoon of all places, a purpose that once burned steady as a pilot light. [447]
But ambition erodes—it is water over stone, wind over sand—and hers had worn so thin she could no longer read its shape. [473]
She pressed the camera wheel to her ear and heard, or imagined she heard, the click of a shutter. [495]
The sound rearranged something inside her. [503]
She thought of the woman in the photograph, laughing, and wondered if that woman had also once climbed above the clouds chasing something luminous and necessary. [533]
She wondered if that woman, too, had watched her purpose dissolve into fog. [550]
Regret arrived not as a wave but as a settling—fine dust collecting on every surface of her chest, her throat, the backs of her eyes. [578]
She regretted the stories she had kept and never returned. [590]
She regretted the years she could not place, the conversations she had archived instead of joined, the way she had stood apart from every narrative she touched, preserving them but never entering them. [631]
A ghost sat down beside her. [638]
It looked exactly as she had looked the day she arrived—younger, hungrier, bright-eyed with that eroded ambition still blazing. [661]
"I came to negotiate," Maren said. [669]
The ghost tilted its head. [676]
"I want to stop collecting," Maren said. [684]
"Then what?" the ghost asked, and its voice was the sound of the lagoon at rest—water held perfectly still between two pulses, the world holding its breath. [715]
"I want to enter the next story I hear," she said. [727]
The ghost considered this, then offered its hand. [738]
The lagoon breathed in, and the clouds below shifted, and the carousel's horses seemed, for one moment, almost to move. [763]
Maren took the ghost's hand and waited, listening. [775]
