The clock tower rose from the tidal obsidian ridge like a bone jutting from the earth, its base fused with volcanic stone that each dawn emerged bare and gleaming from the retreating sea. [33]
Elias Maren had been its winder for thirty-one years, arriving before first light with his oil can and his ladder and the broken rake handle he could never bring himself to discard. [65]
He was ethically diligent in the way of men who have failed once and refused to fail again—every gear tooth inspected, every chain link tested, the pendulum bob polished until it threw back his own distorted face. [103]
The townspeople below trusted him with the clock and, by extension, with their tides, their harvests, their departures and returns. [123]
That trust was the wound he tended daily. [131]
Twelve years ago he had been late. [138]
A storm, a bottle, a morning lost to unconsciousness—and the great clock had stopped. [153]
Old Matteo Voss had been descending the ridge path at the wrong hour, trusting the tower's strike to signal the tide's retreat. [175]
The sea took him where the obsidian turned slick. [184]
Elias found the body when the water pulled back, and the rake Matteo had been carrying lay snapped in two nearby, handle severed clean. [208]
Elias kept the handle. [212]
He did not know why at first. [219]

It was a doorway. [282]

The next week he added the date. [339]

I was lost. [353]
