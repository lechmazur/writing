[38] She was a portrait thief—one of the rarest kind—who worked only in this fleeting margin between day and night, when paintings hung in galleries seemed to glow from within, loosening their hold on the walls. [108] Within the arrangement, the angle of each stem, the number of blooms per color, the deliberate absence of greenery—these formed a language older than Morse, a floral cipher the station's elderly groundskeeper, Mrs. Yoon, had taught her years ago. [228] She wanted to understand how time could be made still. [237]

[284] The Vermeer study hung opposite a mahogany desk, no larger than a dinner plate, and in the residual golden light the painted girl's pearl seemed to pulse like a living thing. [628] She had spent years stealing illuminated moments from walls, never suspecting that destiny's door could swing open not with a grand key but with a jar of borrowed moonlight and a woman sweeping the platform at midnight.
