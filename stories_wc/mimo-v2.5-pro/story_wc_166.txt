After the Nostalgia Ban, the coastal provinces fell silent. [9]
No more lullabies carried saltwater memories. [16]
No more elders whispering of shipwrecks that never were. [25]
The Bureau of Contemporary Accord had declared all backward-looking emotion a civic poison, punishable by severance of one's anchor permit. [45]

Maren was the last skilled tide dancer in the western basin. [56]
Each dusk, she had waded into the foam and read the currents with her body—hips turning like compass needles, arms parting brine the way a reader parts pages. [85]
The tides still spoke to her, but speaking back had become dangerous. [96]
Every gesture she made was now scrutinized for its pull toward yesterday. [108]

So she chose elective exile. [113]
Not the exile of punishment, but the exile of purpose—the deliberate walking-away from a world that could no longer hold what she carried. [137]
In the pocket of her weathered coat, wrapped in a scrap of sailcloth, lay a moonstone cabochon dust speck no larger than a freckle. [161]
She had found it years ago in a tide pool, luminous and cool, and the old dancers told her it was a seed: given the right conditions, it could anchor verse directly into living skin. [196]

Her motivation was singular—she needed to pen a cosmic verse on mortal skin before the speck lost its charge. [216]
The verse was not hers alone. [222]
The tides had composed it over centuries, passing it from dancer to dancer in the language of undertow and moonpull. [242]
If she failed to inscribe it, the poem would dissolve into the ocean floor, unreadable forever. [258]

She found the twilight garden under stars on the third night of walking inland. [272]

The garden was spontaneously anchored to the earth—no gardener had planted it, no architect had planned it. [339]

Maren knelt on the moss and unwrapped the moonstone speck. [366]
It caught starlight and fractured it into threads that danced across her forearms. [379]

Gaps between stars. [405]
Gaps between breath. [408]
The gaps the Bureau had tried to seal when they banned the backward glance, not realizing that nostalgia lived precisely in what was missing. [432]

She pressed the speck to her left wrist. [440]
It dissolved instantly, cool as a wave's first touch, and began to write. [453]
Not with ink but with light—silver-blue script that coiled from her wrist to her elbow, each character a constellation, each line a current she had danced in the old waters. [484]
The verse told of how the moon once wept salt into the sea, and how the sea remembered, and how memory itself was a tide that no law could ban. [514]

Her skin prickled. [517]

This was what obsidian grace meant—beauty forged in darkness, carried without apology. [569]

Somewhere far off, a wave reached a shore no one watched. [613]

She had fledged. [617]
She was no longer a dancer of tides but a vessel of them—the verse now legible on her body, waiting for someone who could still read the pull of the moon. [649]
The Bureau could ban what it wished. [656]
Some poems survive by hiding in skin, in gaps, in the obsidian space between stars. [671]

Maren walked back through the garden gate, unobserved, her forearms glowing faintly under her sleeves. [686]
Ahead of her, the road split into many directions. [695]
She chose none of them yet. [701]
