Lin Mei had written fourteen thousand fortunes and not once had anyone understood them. [15]
She lived in a glass spire that rose from the shore of Lake Baikal like a thorn of light, its walls so clear that in winter the frozen water seemed to press right up against her desk. [42]
The spire had no architect's name; it had simply appeared one spring, a lattice of transparency where the reeds had been, as though someone had asked the ice itself to dream upward. [72]
Lin Mei did not question its origin. [80]
She had other mysteries to tend. [88]

Each night she sat cross-legged before her workstation, rolling dough into thin crescents, folding slips of paper into the warm folds, pressing each cookie shut with the pad of her thumb. [120]
The sentences she wrote were not predictions. [128]
They were bars of music, notation hidden in grammar, measures and rests disguised as wisdom. [144]
"The river bends before it remembers" was a cello phrase in D minor. [157]
"You will find what you stopped seeking" was a harp arpeggio resolving into silence. [172]
No one heard the music. [178]
They cracked the cookies, read the words, smiled or frowned, and moved on with their evenings, pockets full of symphonies they could not perceive. [201]

Lin Mei was chronologically homesick, which was not the same as being homesick for a place. [218]
She missed a version of time itself. [225]
She missed the hours when she was seven, sitting beneath her grandmother's kitchen table in Harbin, listening to the old woman hum songs that had no words, songs that rose and fell like breath, songs that made the nautilus shell on the windowsill seem to glow. [274]
Her grandmother had kept the nautilus shell segment—just a quarter of the spiral, broken and beautiful—on a saucer beside her tea. [298]
"It remembers every room it ever grew," her grandmother had said, turning the fragment so light ran through its chambers. [320]
Lin Mei had taken it when the old woman died, carried it through cities and years, and now it sat on her desk inside the glass spire, the last opaque thing in her world. [352]

Tonight was different. [357]
Tonight she had decided to stop writing fortunes one at a time and instead map the whole symphony at once, the complete unheard composition that had been building inside her for decades. [389]
She would do it by mapping her childhood memories—tracing each recollection back to its chamber in the nautilus, following the spiral inward to the earliest note. [420]
She had to finish before exhaustion hit, before her body surrendered and the music dissolved into the ordinary silence of sleep. [446]

Dawn began without announcement. [452]
The horizon east of the lake shifted from black to charcoal to a pale, mineral gray, and the frozen surface below became a mirrored horizon—sky doubled, clouds trapped in ice, the boundary between up and down dissolving into reflection. [496]
Lin Mei pressed her palm against the nautilus shell segment and closed her eyes. [512]

She was seven. [515]
Her grandmother hummed. [518]
The kitchen smelled of sesame oil and winter. [526]
She was four, sitting in her father's lap while he read aloud from a book she could not yet understand, and his voice was a low instrument, a bass note that vibrated against her ribs. [561]
She was nine, standing at the edge of a pond in summer, watching dragonflies stitch patterns over the water, and the stitching was music, she realized now, it had always been music. [598]

The dawn deepened. [603]
Light climbed the glass spire in bands of rose and amber, and the frozen lake caught each hue and held it, and Lin Mei began to write—not on fortune slips but on a vast sheet of paper she had unrolled across the floor, her pen moving in spirals that mirrored the nautilus, each line a measure, each curve a phrase. [661]
She could hear it. [666]
For the first time, she could actually hear what she had been writing all along—the cellos, the harps, the low hum of her father's voice, the dragonfly percussion, her grandmother's wordless melody threading through every bar like silk through a loom. [709]

Her hand trembled. [714]
Exhaustion pressed at the edges of her consciousness, a tide that would not wait. [731]
She wrote faster, the spiral tightening toward its center, the final chamber, the oldest memory, the first sound she had ever heard. [756]

It was her mother's heartbeat, before birth, before language, before time split into past and present. [776]
The nautilus knew. [780]
The glass spire held its breath. [787]
And on the mirrored lake, dawn completed itself in perfect, upturned silence. [800]
