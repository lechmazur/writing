The fever broke at dawn, and Maren woke to the sound of instruments clicking in their housings like the joints of old men rising from chairs. [24]
She lay on the cot in the mountain weather station's observation room, the wool blanket damp with three days of sweat, and watched pale light creep across the ceiling where the aurora had painted its sorrow the night before. [49]
She remembered the colors—or tried to—but the memory had already begun its migration toward something softer, less certain. [67]

Maren was the station's oracle, though she would never have used that word herself. [80]
The Bureau called her a "fault-tolerant predictor," which meant she sat in this glass-and-concrete outpost at eleven thousand feet and read the sky for signs that the models could not capture. [109]
Fault-tolerant because she had been wrong before—publicly, catastrophically—and they had rebuilt her reputation the way engineers rebuild a bridge after collapse, adding redundancies no one trusted completely. [138]
But the station still needed someone who could feel the mountain's breath shift half a day before the sensors caught it. [159]

She sat up, and the room swayed. [167]
Everything swayed now. [173]
During the fever, she had dreamed of certainties tipping like metronomes that no one had set, pendulums swinging across a dark stage without rhythm or origin. [196]
The dream had felt like prophecy, which was the most dangerous thing a prophet could admit. [213]

On the desk beside her sat a botanical illustration she had been pressing before the fever took her: a specimen of alpine avens she had found growing impossibly at the station's doorstep, far above its known elevation range. [247]
The illustration was half-finished, the roots rendered in meticulous ink, the petals still bare outlines waiting for pigment. [268]
She remembered kneeling in the scree to harvest it, her hands shaking not from the cold but from the strangeness of finding a living thing where nothing should live. [294]

She picked up the illustration and carried it to the window, where the first auroral light of evening—a premature and mournful violet—was beginning to stain the horizon. [322]
Her peripheral vision caught something: a dark shape moving along the ridge below the station, too large for a mountain goat, too steady for a person who knew where they were. [351]
She turned to look directly, and it vanished. [360]
This had been happening since the fever—the world offering glimpses only at the edges, as though the truth preferred her oblique attention. [386]

Maren held the botanical illustration against the glass and studied how the violet light passed through the paper, illuminating the veins of the avens like a map of underground rivers. [417]
She thought of the truth she had swallowed two years ago, the night the Bureau had asked her to alter a forecast that would have evacuated a valley she knew would flood. [450]
She had complied. [455]
The valley flooded. [460]
Thirty-one people climbed to their rooftops and watched the water rise, and every one of them survived, and the Bureau called it validation. [484]
She had carried the weight of that calculation inside her ever since, lodged somewhere below her ribs, denser than bone. [506]

Now she wanted to find its exact weight—not to confess, not to forgive herself, but to understand how much of her was still her own. [532]

She pressed the botanical illustration to her chest and felt its edges against her collarbone, the paper absorbing the heat of her skin where the fever had recently burned. [564]
The roots of the avens, rendered so carefully in ink, seemed to pulse. [581]
She smudged the illustration deliberately with her thumb, dragging the ink of the root system into the blank margin where the petals should have been. [611]
The roots spread into the empty space like a bruise blooming, and in the ruined illustration she saw something she had not intended: the shape of a valley, the curve of a riverbank, the precise contour of the land she had chosen not to save. [654]

She had found the weight. [661]
It was the weight of a line of ink pushed into territory it was never meant to occupy—by occupying margins, by bleeding into the space where something else should have grown. [693]

Outside, the aurora deepened its sorrow across the sky. [708]
The instruments clicked and recorded. [714]
The mountain weathered. [719]
