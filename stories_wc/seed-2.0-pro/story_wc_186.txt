Lira, the last beacon-tower custodian, ducked through the warped entry arch of the glass humming pavilion, her scuffed leather boots crunching over shards of old window panes and dried thistle heads scattered across the stone floor. [32]
Seventy years had passed since the collapse of civilization, and most survivors in the nearby scrubland camps had written off all pre-fall technology as useless scrap, fit only for prying apart to make crude tools. [59]
Lira was retrocognitively blessed, a rare gift that let her see the full history of any object she held, and she had never been able to shake the belief that small, unplanned moments held weight, not just random chaos. [92]
Her core motivation, the thing that drove her to hike ten miles across wind-scoured dunes every week, was to prove that coincidences have meaning, that the universe did not stop leaving signposts when the old power grids died. [129]
Three moons prior, she had tripped over a half-buried thistle root and found a broken crystal prism tucked in the dirt, its facets still glinting even after decades buried under sand and debris. [162]
When she first closed her fingers around it, her retrocognitive gift flared to life, showing her the prism’s old purpose: it sat at the heart of the pavilion’s beacon, amplifying signal across 200 miles of flat plains to warn settlements of incoming dust storms and radiation surges. [204]
The prism was missing one tiny, jagged shard, the piece the old technicians had called the shard of hush, designed to filter out false signal spikes so the beacon never sent communities fleeing for no reason. [238]
If she could recover that shard and fit it back into the prism, she could rekindle the dormant beacon, a tool that would cut the camp’s annual storm death rate by nearly 60 percent, if the visions she’d seen were accurate. [275]
The old star charts she’d found tucked in the beacon tower’s storage locker told her the shard had been flung hundreds of yards from the pavilion during the first cataclysmic explosion of the collapse, its landing site perfectly aligned with the path of a dying red star that crossed the horizon only once every three moons. [322]
Her method to find the lost shard was by tracking star paths, marking its position each night against the faded grid lines of the pre-fall charts, adjusting her search radius by a dozen feet every time the star shifted its position in the sky. [361]
Last night, the star had aligned exactly with a small hollow half a mile west of the pavilion, and when she’d dug through the sand there an hour earlier, she’d found the tiny, milky shard tucked between two thistle roots, exactly where the charts said it would be. [407]
She stepped to the center of the pavilion, where the old beacon cradle sat perched on a stone plinth, its glass sides still humming softly with the faint residual solar power trapped in its panels even after all these years. [444]
She slotted the broken crystal prism into the cradle first, then fit the shard of hush into its jagged empty slot, pressing it gently until she heard a soft, satisfying click that echoed through the quiet space. [480]
For a long moment, nothing happened, and Lira’s chest tightened, worried she had misread the charts, that the coincidence of finding the prism had just been random luck after all, nothing more. [516]
Then a soft, golden light bloomed from the center of the prism, spreading out across the pavilion’s glass walls until the whole structure glowed like a tiny sun, its low hum rising to a clear, steady tone that carried far over the dunes. [557]
Lira laughed, brushing a thistle seed that had blown into her hair off her shoulder, feeling that thistle-blown hope she had carried for so long finally rooting into something solid, something real. [592]
Back at the camp, someone would look up and see the glow, they would know the beacon was working again, and they would start to believe too, that the small, unexpected moments that lead you exactly where you need to be are never just accidents. [637]
She leaned against the plinth, watching the light pulse in time with the distant beat of the star she had tracked, knowing she had proven what she set out to: even after the world falls apart, the universe still leaves clues for anyone willing to look for them. [680]
The thistle seeds swirling through the pavilion’s open arch caught the golden light as they blew out across the dunes, tiny, floating points of potential that would root wherever they landed, just like the quiet, unshakable truth she had finally brought back to life. [724]
