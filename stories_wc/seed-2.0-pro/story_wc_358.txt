His chosen method, honed over 17 years of high-stakes crash probes, was documenting wind patterns between the stacked container towers, as shifting gusts in this manmade canyon had been flagged as a possible contributing factor to the crash. [108]
He was rapidly adapting to the eerie, unchanging haze that hung over the port, adjusting his anemometer’s calibration to cut through the salt-rich air that distorted standard wind readings. [137]

As he picked it up, the data he had collected over the past 72 hours clicked into place, a singular convergence of variables he had been chasing since he first reviewed the plane’s black box recordings. [246]

His wind measurements confirmed that a rare, 90-degree cross gust had ripped through the container canyon exactly when the plane was on final approach, yanking its left wing down just as the compromised hydraulic system failed to compensate for the sudden shift in force. [370]
This is exactly what he had spent his career chasing: the ability to spot the small, easy-to-miss details before they converged into disaster, to cultivate foresight in action rather than just assigning blame after lives were lost. [407]
His rapidly adapting workflow had let him combine on-site measurements, black box data, and recovered personal effects far faster than most investigative teams, cutting through bureaucratic red tape that usually delayed probe conclusions for months. [442]

This singular convergence was not just a quirk of physics or human error, but a reminder that every safety protocol was written in the blood of people who had been failed by a dozen small, preventable missteps. [524]
He would finish his survey by the end of the day, compiling his wind data, the patch’s location, and the black box recordings into a report that would mandate new wind monitoring stations along the port’s approach path, and new policies that let engineers ground flights for minor mechanical issues without facing disciplinary action.
