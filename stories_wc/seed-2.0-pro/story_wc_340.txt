Elara, the ascending servant tasked with restocking the spire’s upper observation decks, stepped into the frosted glass elevator as the first streaks of dawn painted the cloud sea pink. [29]
She knew the ride would last exactly seven minutes, long enough to watch the ice-frosted archways of the icebound spire above the clouds blur past as the elevator climbs ten floors. [60]
She had taken the low-ranking servant position three months prior, her quiet motivation to seek out the joy beneath anguish of her mother’s unexplained disappearance ten years earlier driving every small, unobtrusive search of the spire’s forgotten nooks. [98]
Half-buried under a frayed cleaning cloth tucked by the doorframe, she spotted a pair of scuffed, fur-lined airship captain’s goggles, the strap embossed with the raven emblem her mother had stitched to every piece of her flight gear. [136]
Her warmly inquisitive nature, which had kept her from growing bitter through years of unanswered questions, pushed her to lean down and brush the frost off the goggles, revealing a small, leather-bound book tucked in the goggles’ hard side case. [176]
It was the encrypted diary discovery she had almost given up hoping for, its cover etched with the same tiny raven mark as the goggle strap, the pages stiff with decades of accumulated frost. [210]
She held the book up to the elevator’s frosted glass, where the dawn filtered through in soft streaks, knowing the cipher her mother had taught her as a child would only reveal itself through scattered light hitting the page at a precise angle. [253]
She carefully pried the cover open, watching as the events of her mother’s final weeks begin to unfold across the yellowed pages, the faint swirling ink shifting into legible script as the light caught it just right. [290]
She read with the same affectionate detachment she had cultivated for all memories of her mother, not letting grief cloud the words that revealed her mother had intentionally crashed her damaged airship to avoid colliding with the spire’s crowded lower residential floors. [332]
Page after page detailed the small, bright moments she had clung to in her final hours: the taste of wild blueberry jam on toast that morning, the sound of Elara laughing as she chased a stray cat around their cottage yard, the sight of the cloud sea glowing gold at sunrise. [383]
The spire’s ruling council had covered up the crash to avoid admitting their navigation beacons had failed, leaving Elara to grow up thinking her mother had abandoned her without a second thought. [415]
The elevator dinged as it passed the third floor, ice crystals skittering across the glass as a gust of wind buffeted the spire, and Elara flipped to the final page, where her mother had scrawled a note addressed directly to her, should the diary ever be found. [462]
It told her she was loved more than all the stars above the cloud sea, and that no amount of sadness or distance could ever erase the joy they had shared in their years together. [497]
Elara traced the faint indent of her mother’s fingernail on the edge of the page, a small, soft smile tugging at the corner of her mouth as the elevator passed the seventh floor, the frost on the glass glittering like scattered diamonds in the dawn light. [543]
She tucked the diary and goggles into the inner pocket of her uniform, feeling the weight of ten years of unanswered questions lift from her shoulders, no longer angry or lost, just grateful for the small, perfect piece of her mother she had found. [587]
The elevator dinged one final time as it reached the tenth floor, the glass door sliding open to reveal the crisp, cold air of the observation deck, the cloud sea stretching out endless and gold below her. [624]
She stepped out, hand resting lightly over the pocket holding the diary and goggles, knowing she had finally found the joy she had been searching for, soft and bright beneath all the old pain. [658]
