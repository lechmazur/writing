The wisely adventurous lore keeper adjusted her bio-luminescent satchel, counting the seconds until she would sally past the kaleidoscopic waterfall that hid the last remaining marker of the colony’s forgotten founding. [31]
She knew she had until sunrise to locate the stairway of ascension, the only path to the unclaimed throne that had sat vacant for 300 years, ever since the first colonists vanished mid-coronation. [64]
The waterfall’s shifting, holographic hues had long hidden the hidden orchard behind it, a grove of genetically engineered fruit trees that bore bioluminescent pears that glowed the same pink as the colonists’ old flag. [98]
She had spent three years decoding fragments of old data chips, cross-referencing oral histories that warned only the pure of motive could find the throne, and she carried the only clue she had ever found: a tiny ruby flake she’d pulled from the ruins of the old colonist meeting hall, etched with a tiny symbol of a staircase wrapped in vines. [159]
When she stepped through the waterfall’s cool, ionized curtain, the hum of old technology faded, replaced by the soft rustle of orchard leaves and the sweet, sharp scent of overripe pears that glowed like scattered starlight across the grass. [198]
At the center of the grove stood a weathered stone structure, its outer walls covered in chipped, faded paint that looked like it had been layered and re-layered dozens of times over the centuries. [232]
She remembered the old texts that said the colonists had hidden their most sacred knowledge in plain sight, so she pulled a soft scraping tool from her satchel and began to work through the peel of paint layers revealing hidden murals underneath, each one depicting a step of the colonists’ journey from their dying home planet to this new world. [292]
As she scraped, she held the ruby flake up to the bioluminescent light of the nearest pear, and the tiny etchings on its surface cast a faint red shadow that landed on the lowest portion of the mural, marking a small stone door half-hidden behind a painted vine of red flowers. [343]
She pushed gently on the spot marked by the shadow, and the stone swung inward with a low, rumbling creak, revealing a narrow set of stone steps leading upward, their edges worn smooth by centuries of disuse, exactly the stairway of ascension she had spent years searching for. [391]
She checked her wrist display, noting that she had just two hours left until sunrise, when the waterfall’s holographic shield would re-calibrate and lock the orchard away from the rest of the colony for another hundred years. [428]
She climbed slowly, careful not to slip on the loose moss that dotted the steps, and as she climbed, she thought of the stories her grandmother had told her, of a ruler who would sit on the unclaimed throne not to command, but to heal the rifts that had split the colony into warring factions after the founders vanished. [487]
When she reached the top of the stairs, she stepped into a small, circular chamber lit by the same bioluminescent pink light as the orchard below, and at its center sat the unclaimed throne, carved from a single block of pink quartz, its arms etched with the same vine symbols as her ruby flake. [541]
She did not rush to sit, instead taking a moment to trace the carvings, thinking of the thousands of colonists who had waited for someone to prove they cared more about the community than power, and she knew her wisely adventurous spirit had led her exactly where she was meant to be. [593]
As the first faint streaks of gold began to bleed over the horizon, she tucked the ruby flake into a small niche in the throne’s arm, a promise to the founders that she would honor their vision of a world where no one was left behind, and sat down, feeling the hum of old technology wake up around her, connecting her to every colonist in the valley below. [661]
The first rays of sunrise spilled through the chamber’s small skylight, gilding the edges of the throne and sending a soft pink signal out across the colony, letting every faction know that the long wait for a leader who understood their past and would build their future was finally over. [711]
