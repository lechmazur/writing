The scorched wasteland stretched for three hundred miles in every direction, cracked red earth dotted with bleached animal bones and the rusted husks of pre-Gap supply trucks, but at its heart lay the abandoned beekeeping oasis, its weathered wooden hives half-buried in wind-blown sand and its wild clover still somehow clinging to life two years after ghosts became visible. [59]
Elias, the astonished recluse who had retreated to the oasis three years prior after losing his wife and daughter to the wildfires that turned the surrounding land to ash, was tranquilly perceptive, able to read stories from scuff marks on wood, faded chalk scribbles on stone, and the faint, shimmering trails ghosts left behind when they passed through a space. [119]
Each morning, he would perch on the edge of the oasis’s only standing stone wall and gaze out across the wasteland, not for visitors, but for the thin, wispy shapes of ghosts drawn to the small patch of green in the endless expanse of burnt nothing. [165]
He rarely spoke to the ghosts, instead piecing together their unfinished business through contextual clues: a locket hanging loose around a shimmering neck, the way a child ghost kept patting its pockets for a lost toy, the faint smudge of ash on the cuff of a farmer’s ghosted flannel shirt. [215]
His core motivation, the thing that got him out of his threadbare cot each cold desert night, was to preserve the weight of unspoken words, the apologies, I love yous, and goodbyes that ghosts carried but could no longer speak aloud, trapped between the world of the living and the world of the dead. [269]
He kept these words inside a stained glass box he had found tucked in the back of the oasis’s old beekeeper’s cottage, its panels swirled with honey gold and sky blue, crisscrossed with thin lead lines that looked like the veins of a leaf, its surface dusted with faint honey residue that never seemed to wipe away no matter how often he touched it. [333]
He did not know until the day his wife’s ghost appeared at the edge of the clover patch that the box was bound to a tangled covenant, an old promise the previous beekeeper had made to the land: every unspoken word stored inside the box would feed the oasis, keeping the clover alive, the groundwater sweet, and the ghosts able to visit the last piece of home they had left. [403]
His wife said nothing, just held out a shimmering hand that held the shape of the half-finished birthday card she had been writing for their daughter the day the fire hit, and the mute eloquence of her small, sad smile told him everything he needed to know: she had not come to move on, she had come to add her own unspoken words to the box, to help keep the oasis alive for all the other ghosts who had nowhere else to go. [486]
Elias was astonished, his breath catching in his throat, because he had spent two years assuming he was alone in his work, that he was just a broken man hiding from the world, but he realized now the covenant had called him here, that his own unspoken grief was part of what kept the clover growing, the hives still humming with the faint, ghostly buzz of bees that had died in the fire. [559]
He walked slowly to her, lifting the lid of the stained glass box, and watched as the faint, glowing shape of her unspoken words drifted from her hand into the box, swirling with the thousands of other words he had collected over the years, turning the glass a warm, soft gold that lit up the entire oasis for a full minute. [620]
When the light faded, his wife’s ghost gave him a small, gentle nod before she drifted away, not gone, but free to visit whenever she wanted, along with the dozens of other ghosts who found their way to the oasis each week. [662]
Elias sat back against the stone wall, holding the stained glass box to his chest, and realized that he was no longer a recluse hiding from his grief, but a caretaker, bound to the tangled covenant that gave both the living and the dead a small, safe place to lay down the weight they could no longer carry alone. [721]
As the sun dipped low over the red wasteland, he turned his gaze to the clover patch, where a small child’s ghost was chasing a ghostly bee, and smiled, knowing every unspoken word he preserved was a small act of love that kept the last bit of green in the desert alive. [773]
