The late-shift pier lamplighter, Elias, had spent three months wandering purposefully along the fog-curled Atlantic boardwalk, chasing the rumor that sound carried a distinct hue if you listened close enough to the spaces between waves. [35]
Tonight, the coastal town’s annual sky lantern release painted the low clouds in streaks of tangerine and ivory, and the usual crash of surf softened to a murmur that matched the meteor hush of the night, no wind loud enough to snuff out the floating flames before they drifted past the horizon. [87]
Halfway through his shift, he spotted a glint of brass half-buried in the sand at the pier’s furthest piling, a dented hotel skeleton key tag etched with the name of the Grand Oriana, a luxury liner that sunk half a mile off the coast in 1927. [133]
He’d heard old timers talk of the vessel’s forgotten library, its oak shelves still packed with leather-bound poetry collections and love letters sealed with wax, preserved by the cold, still saltwater that never stirred far below the pier. [171]
The tag’s engraving held a tiny scrawled inscription on its back: “Luminous vows hide behind truth, not in the words we say out loud”, a line Elias recognized as the opening of a sonnet the Oriana’s poet-in-residence had written for his fiancée, who’d drowned in the sinking before they could marry. [222]
Local legend said the poet had released hundreds of paper lanterns on the night of their scheduled wedding, each lit to signify the promise he’d kept to love her even when they were separated by the whole width of the sea and the veil of death. [268]
Elias had never believed in ghosts or old legends, but when he closed his fingers around the warm brass tag, he heard a soft, thrumming hum rise from the water below, low and sweet as a lullaby, and he knew he had to dive down to see what was calling him. [319]
The water was clearer than he’d ever seen it, the light from the floating sky lanterns filtering all the way down to the Oriana’s hull, illuminating the cracked double doors of the forgotten library just past the ship’s grand ballroom. [359]
He pressed the hotel skeleton key tag to the lock on the library doors, and they swung open without a sound, revealing shelves lined with glowing books, their pages fluttering softly in a current he couldn’t feel. [396]
At the center of the room, on an oak reading desk, sat a leather-bound notebook, its pages glowing with pale gold light, the words of the poet’s luminous vows scrawled across every page, each line thrumming with the same soft hum he’d heard from the pier. [442]
He realized then that the poet’s line about vows that hide behind truth meant that the promise wasn’t in the public wedding ceremony they’d never gotten to have, but in the quiet, unspoken devotion he’d carried every day after she was gone. [484]
When he pressed his palm to the glowing page, the hum rose to a warm, ringing tone, and he saw it at last: the color of sound, a soft, shifting shade of rose-gold that wrapped around him like sunlight through honey, bright enough to cut through the dimness of the sunken ship. [536]
Up above, the sky lanterns continued to drift across the horizon, each tiny flame there to signify the hundreds of quiet, unspoken promises people carried for loved ones they’d lost, too gentle to ever be said out loud. [574]
When Elias swam back to the surface an hour later, the last of the sky lanterns had just faded from view, and the meteor hush of the night still wrapped around the pier, no sounds of traffic or chatter from the town to break the quiet magic of what he’d seen. [625]
He kept the dented hotel skeleton key tag on the lanyard of his lamplighter’s vest after that, a quiet reminder that the most beautiful things in the world are not the ones we shout from rooftops, but the ones we carry close, in the spaces between sound and light, between what we say and what we know to be true. [685]
