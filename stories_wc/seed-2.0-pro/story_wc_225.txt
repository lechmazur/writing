Kael, the exiled chess tutor, hunkered on the stone step of the village’s abandoned tapestry shop as distant thunder rumbled then faded, leaving the world suspended in its lull. [29]
Every cottage window held a half-poured mug of cider, every market stall held peaches soft enough to burst if touched, proof that the entire time-frozen village had been caught mid-afternoon when the curse settled three years prior. [66]
He fidgeted productively as he waited, twisting scraps of dyed wool into tiny chess pawns that he tucked into the lining of his frayed wool coat, his fingers never still even when his mind was fixed on the task ahead. [106]
The air hummed with the woven hush the curse had laid over the village, no bird calls, no laughter, no wind stirring the oak leaves, a silence so intentional it felt stitched into every surface around him. [143]
He had come here to forgive himself for past mistakes, most of all the reckless argument that had made him storm out of the village the day before the curse hit, leaving his 12-year-old chess student, Lila, behind to be trapped in the frozen time. [188]
The elders who had lifted his exile for this quest told him he could winnow through the layers of the curse by decoding frayed threads in a tapestry’s border, the same tapestry Lila had been weaving as a gift for him when the curse struck. [233]
That tapestry’s central image was the platinum labyrinth he had taught Lila to draw as a chess strategy exercise, each twisting path representing a possible gambit, each dead end a lesson in when to yield instead of fight. [271]
He stepped into the shop, his boots making no sound on the dustless floor, and settled cross-legged on the floor in front of the tapestry, his posture holding the fierce repose of a chess player waiting for their opponent to make their first move. [315]
He ran one finger along the tapestry’s frayed border, noting the tiny shifts in thread color that matched the code he and Lila had invented to pass notes during lessons, each shade corresponding to a chess piece, each knot a letter of the alphabet. [359]
As he decoded the threads, he began to winnow out the curse’s false layers, peeling back the fear that had wrapped the village in stasis, each knot he untangled sending a tiny ripple through the woven hush around him. [398]
The message in the border slowly revealed itself: Lila had woven a note saying she had never been angry at him for leaving, that she knew he had been hurting, that she had always believed he would come back to fix what was broken. [442]
Tears stung his eyes as he traced the final knot, which was shaped like the tiny queen piece Lila had carved for him out of apple wood, the only possession he had kept with him during his three years of exile. [483]
He realized then that forgiving himself did not require lifting the curse, though he would keep working to do that, it only required accepting that he was allowed to let go of the guilt he had carried for so long. [523]
He sat back, still holding that fierce repose, as the distant thunder rumbled again, the lull ending, and watched as the platinum labyrinth on the tapestry glowed soft silver, the paths shifting to open a new route he had never noticed before. [565]
He fidgeted productively again, twisting a loose gold thread from the tapestry’s border into a tiny replica of Lila’s pawn, tucking it next to the wooden queen in his coat pocket, a promise that he would stay until the village thawed and he could apologize in person. [612]
For the first time in three years, the woven hush felt like a comfort instead of a weight, a quiet space where he could finally rest, unburdened by the regret that had chased him across every mile of his exile. [652]
