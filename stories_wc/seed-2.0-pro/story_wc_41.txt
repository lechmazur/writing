The junkyard prophet, whose pockets were stuffed with bent nails and half-broken radio tubes, ducked under the rusted chain marking the entrance to the fog sculpture garden, the hush of old videotapes left unwatched wrapping around him like a well-loved frayed blanket. [42]
He was precognitively forgetful, meaning he could never hold onto the shape of moments that hadn’t yet come, leaving him always clinging to echoes of half-remembered futures he’d already lost before they arrived. [75]
For three years, his driving motivation had been to capture the sound of growing older, the quiet, gritty shift of bones and memory that he could almost hear hum under the surface of every still, unhurried space he stumbled into. [115]
He’d come here today to locate the source of that hum, a faint thrumming he’d picked up on a busted transistor radio he’d fished out of a car crushed in the junkyard two weeks prior. [150]
His chosen method for navigating the fog, which twisted and shifted so fast it could erase paths in ten seconds flat, was arranging driftwood as directional markers, each piece dragged from the shore a mile west and carved with a tiny X on its base so he could tell which way he’d come. [203]
Tucked in the back pocket of his oil-stained overalls was a piece of plain chalk, worn down to a nub from months of marking numbers on scrap metal and scrawling fragmented verses on the sides of discarded refrigerators back at his yard. [245]
The fog sculptures shifted around him as he walked, soft shapes of deer and lighthouses and childhood bedrooms dissolving and reforming just out of reach, stoking the tidal longing that lived in the hollow of his chest, the constant pull of what he could almost touch but never hold. [294]
Every few feet he dropped a piece of driftwood, its rough, salt-crusted surface catching the faint golden glow of the sun trying to cut through the mist, and he tried not to think about how easily the fog could swallow those markers before he found his way back. [342]
After twenty minutes of walking, the hum grew louder, warm and thrumming like the purr of a cat curled on a windowsill, and he spotted a small stone bench at the center of a ring of fog sculptures that looked just like the people he’d loved and lost over his seventy-two years of life. [396]
He pulled out the piece of plain chalk and knelt to mark a circle around the bench, knowing the fog would erase it soon but wanting to leave a marker of the moment, even if he forgot he’d been there by the next morning. [440]
He sat on the bench and closed his eyes, letting the hum wrap around him, and for the first time in years, he didn’t fight the precognitively forgetful fog in his own head, letting the echoes of future grief and future joy wash over him unimpeded. [486]
The sound of growing older wasn’t a single note, he realized, it was a chorus, all the small, unspoken moments that stacked up like old videotapes on a shelf, the ones you never got around to watching but still kept, just in case. [529]
He pulled a beat-up cassette recorder from his other pocket and hit record, holding it out to the air, and for three perfect minutes, the hum flowed into the tape, thick and warm and alive with all the echoes he’d spent years chasing. [572]
When he opened his eyes, the fog sculptures of his loved ones were gone, dissolved back into the mist, and he knew without checking that his driftwood markers had vanished too, swallowed by the shifting fog as if they’d never existed. [613]
He tucked the cassette tape into his shirt pocket, right over his heart, and traced the edge of the chalk nub in his overalls, feeling the tidal longing in his chest soften from a sharp pull to a gentle, steady sway, like the tide coming in to kiss the shore. [663]
He didn’t know how he’d find his way out of the garden, but he didn’t care, not really, because the sound he’d chased for so long was safe in his pocket, a little piece of time he could hold onto even when his memory forgot the day he’d found it. [713]
Somewhere behind him, a fog sculpture of his seven-year-old self flickered into being for half a second, holding a piece of chalk in one small hand, before it dissolved back into the static hush, another echo waiting to be held another day. [755]
