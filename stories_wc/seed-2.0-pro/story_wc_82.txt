Three days after the shadow detaches from her soul, leaving only gossamer streaks of pearl gray where sorrow used to fester, Lira, a crafty lantern maker, slips through the wispy cloud barriers surrounding the floating island tea garden she has tended since her wife’s passing. [45]
For six months, she has woken each dawn with half-formed memories of her wife’s laugh tangled in her linen sheets, and her deepest, quietest motivation has been to fold grief into origami cranes, each holding a shard of the dreams she can no longer hold onto on her own. [94]
She carries a leather pouch of raw moonstone, the same material she uses for the lens of her dream recording lanterns, and her hands move automatically through the familiar motions of gem polishing, each circular rub smoothing the rough stone into a clear surface that can trap the echoes of sleeping minds. [146]
Across the thin stretch of cloud-dusted sky, the glass basilica on a floating isle glows with the warm gold of trapped sunlight, its spires curving upward so gently they seem to melt into the pale blue horizon. [183]
Lira has watched it for weeks, noting its strange, unhurried movement that follows the only rule she has ever observed for the realm beyond the mortal veil: it descends while ascending, its base drifting lower toward the ocean far below even as its highest spire climbs closer to the edge of the stratosphere. [236]
She spreads a linen cloth across the low bamboo tea table, sets the polished moonstones in a circle around a stack of uncreased rice paper, and lights a stick of jasmine incense that smells exactly like the perfume her wife wore on their wedding day. [281]
When the first wisp of incense curls up to touch the nearest moonstone, the air around her begins to transform, the soft rustle of tea garden cherry blossoms mixing with the distant chime of the basilica’s bells until the line between waking and dreaming blurs almost entirely. [328]
The polished moonstones glow with soft, pearlescent light, projecting the half-forgotten dreams Lira has carried with her for months: her wife laughing as they planted cherry saplings in the tea garden, sharing a pot of oolong under the stars, pressing a kiss to Lira’s cheek before she left for her fateful voyage across the sea. [383]
Tears slide down Lira’s cheeks, warm and sweet and sharp all at once, the luminous torment of seeing her wife’s face again cutting through the numbness that has wrapped around her heart since the funeral. [418]
She picks up the first sheet of rice paper, her fingers moving with the same careful precision she uses to cut lantern glass, and folds the first crane, tucking a tiny shard of polished moonstone holding a fragment of the dream of their first date into the curve of its wing. [469]
Each crane she folds lifts from the table as soon as she tucks the last crease into place, drifting slowly toward the glass basilica, which grows larger as its spires climb higher and its base drifts lower, drawing the small paper birds into its glowing interior. [515]
She folds one hundred cranes by the time the sun dips low enough to paint the clouds pink and orange, each holding a different memory, a different shard of grief she no longer needs to carry alone, each safe in the basilica’s glass walls where the dreams will be preserved for as long as the isle floats. [572]
When the last crane drifts out of sight, Lira sits back on her woven cushion, pours herself a cup of cold oolong, and watches the basilica continue its strange dance, its light now mixing with the first faint pinpricks of starlight in the darkening sky. [617]
The numbness is gone now, replaced by a soft, steady ache that feels less like a wound and more like a reminder, proof that the love she shared was real, that the memories are worth keeping even when they hurt. [657]
She runs a finger over the remaining shard of moonstone in her pocket, the one holding the dream of her wife’s last goodbye, and smiles, knowing she can return whenever she wants, to add more cranes, to visit the dreams she has stored, to feel close to the person she lost without letting grief consume her. [713]
As the first crickets begin to chirp in the tea garden’s cherry trees, she blows out the incense, packs up her supplies, and heads down the cloud path back to her workshop, her heart lighter than it has been in half a year. [756]
