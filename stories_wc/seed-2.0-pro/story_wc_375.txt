After the last echo of laughter fades, Elara brushes the smudged charcoal dust from her frayed canvas apron, her black-stained fingertips catching the faint, iridescent glow of the subterranean crystal cave’s walls. [32]
A mnemonically gifted portrait sketch artist who’d run from her small town commission work three months prior, she’d thought hiding in this remote cave system would let her escape the endless, soulless requests for wedding portraits and corporate headshots that had drained her creativity dry for years. [79]
The laughter had come from a group of student hikers who’d stumbled into the cave an hour earlier, teasing each other as they took photos of the crystal formations before leaving her alone again with the weight of her own choices. [120]
Tucked in the inner pocket of her satchel rests the dented, broken combination lock she’d pried off her late mother’s locked art supply cabinet two weeks before she left town, its tumblers jammed halfway between the numbers she’d memorized as a kid: her mom’s birthday, the year she sold her first painting, the address of their old cottage. [173]
She’d come to this cave, whose shifting refracted light made every surface look like a half-finished watercolor, with a single, urgent motivation: to uncork the suppressed creativity she’d locked away along with her mom’s unused paints and half-finished canvases when she was 17. [215]
For three months, she’d tried to paint anything that wasn’t a face, but every sketch she made drifted back to portraits, to the lines of a person’s jaw that held unspoken grief, the crinkle at the corner of an eye that told of hidden joy, a harsh reminder of the inescapable callings she’d been running from. [271]
She realized now that running hadn’t fixed anything, that she needed to rectify the mistake she’d made when she’d turned her back on the kind of portrait work that mattered, the kind that captured a person’s whole story instead of just their best side for a social media profile. [320]
She’d brought her mom’s final, unfinished self-portrait with her, and she’d decided her method of making amends would be to finish that painting, tucking the broken combination lock behind the canvas when she mounted it on the cave wall as a tribute to both of their lost artistic voices. [369]
She set up her easel in the pool of pale pink and gold light that spilled through a narrow crack in the cave ceiling, the faint hum of distant water mixing with the quiet scratch of her charcoal as she sketched in the lines of her mom’s smile, the one she remembered so clearly even after 12 years of trying to forget. [431]
The auroral sorrow that had clung to her chest since she’d pried the lock off the cabinet softened, not going away entirely, but shifting into something warmer, something that felt like permission to stop running and start making the art she’d been meant to make all along. [478]
She didn’t have to give up portrait work entirely, she realized; she just had to stop taking commissions that erased the parts of people that made them human, that reduced their lives to a single, polished frame. [515]
When she finished the portrait three hours later, her charcoal-fingered tips blacker than they’d ever been, she slid the broken combination lock into the gap between the canvas and its wooden stretcher, pressing it flat against the back so it would stay there, hidden, for as long as the painting hung in the cave. [568]
She stepped back to look at her work, the crystal light catching the flecks of mica in the paint she’d mixed from cave dust and the water she’d collected from the cave’s underground stream, and for the first time in years, she didn’t feel the urge to crumple up her sketch or paint over the face she’d drawn. [626]
The inescapable calling she’d resented for so long didn’t feel like a cage anymore; it felt like a gift, one her mom had given her before she died, one she was finally ready to stop hiding from. [663]
She packed up her satchel as the light through the cave ceiling shifted from pink to bright gold, knowing she’d go back to town the next day, not to take the same empty commissions, but to offer free portraits to people who couldn’t afford them, to capture the stories of single parents, elderly veterans, teen runaways who felt like no one saw them. [726]
She took one last look at the painting hanging on the cave wall, the lock safe behind it, and smiled through the thin, glowing tears that traced paths through the charcoal dust on her cheeks, the auroral sorrow still present but now tangled up with hope, with the quiet certainty that she was finally where she was meant to be. [786]
