Elara, a hands-on folklorist chronically curious about the way coastal communities bury grief in folk tales, arrived at the sunken orchard greenhouse ringed by shipwrecks on the morning her 40-year habit of making her grandmother’s honey oat porridge for breakfast finally broke, the core of that familiar routine having eroded since she realized large chunks of her childhood visits to this very cove were missing. [65]
Her drive to recover stolen memories of the summer she turned seven, when her grandmother was dying of lung cancer, had led her to this spot decades later, the first time she’d dared cross the tidal flats to the half-submerged structure since she was a small, fumbling kid chasing fireflies between cracked glass panes. [119]
She’d spent months cross-referencing local folk records, learning that the cove’s fairy ring mushrooms were said to hold the echoes of moments lost to trauma, if one knew how to read their alignment against the sky. [155]
Today, the tide was low enough that she could pick her way past rusted ship hulls draped in sea grass, their splintered masts poking through the mist like the broken ribs of some great sea beast, and slip through the greenhouse’s collapsed west wall. [199]
Inside, the overgrown apple trees still bore small, sour fruit, and a perfect circle of pale, spotted fairy ring mushrooms grew at the base of the gnarled old Winesap tree her grandmother had planted the year Elara was born. [238]
Elara knelt in the soft, mossy dirt, pulling out her star chart, because she knew the way to detect the hidden memories locked in the fungi was by studying broken constellations, the gaps between the stars that aligned with the gaps in her own mind. [283]
She’d mapped the gaps in the Ursa Major constellation the previous night, noting where three faint stars had faded from view due to light pollution over the past 40 years, matching their positions exactly to the spacing of the mushrooms at her feet. [326]
When she pressed her palm to the largest mushroom, its cool, damp flesh pressed back, and a flood of warm, crisp images washed over her, sharp and bright as the salt wind blowing through the broken panes. [363]
She was seven again, sitting on a wool blanket next to her grandmother, who was wrapped in a frayed cream shawl, handing her a small jar of wild blackberry jam she’d made just for Elara, her voice soft as she explained that the memories we lose aren’t ever gone, just hidden in the things we love. [419]
She remembered then that she’d buried that jar of jam at the base of the Winesap tree that same day, right after her grandmother told her she wouldn’t live to see Elara go to first grade, and she’d buried the memory too, too heavy for a seven-year-old to carry. [468]
She dug with her bare hands into the soft dirt beside the mushroom ring, her nails caked with moss and loam, until her fingers brushed the cool, dented glass of the small jar, its paper label faded but still marked with her seven-year-old scrawl, ELA + GMA, in sparkly purple crayon. [519]
She twisted the lid off, and the sweet, tangy scent of blackberries hit her immediately, still preserved after all those years, sealed tight by the cool, constant temperature of the sunken greenhouse and the quiet magic of the fairy ring that had grown around it. [564]
She took a small bite, and the taste flooded her with more memories: running through the greenhouse chasing butterflies, her grandmother teaching her to name the stars, the way the shipwrecks looked at sunset, glowing orange against the dark blue water, all the moments she’d locked away out of grief finally coming home. [617]
As she sat there, wiping jam from her chin and watching the tide start to creep back in around the shipwreck hulls, she realized the break in her morning habit hadn’t been a mistake, it had been a nudge, the part of her that still remembered guiding her back to the place where she’d left the best part of herself decades before. [679]
She tucked the half-eaten jar of jam into her bag, along with one small fairy ring mushroom to press into her field notebook, and smiled, knowing she’d add a new entry to her folklore collection that night, one about how the things we lose always find their way back to us, if we’re curious enough to look for them in the gaps between the stars and the habits we’ve always known. [750]
