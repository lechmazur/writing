The salt-stung wind curled through the rusted Ferris wheel spokes of the abandoned carnival ship drifting in a foggy bay, carrying the faint, distorted jingle of a long-dead calliope as Lira, the chimera twin discoverer, leaned against a splintered cotton candy stand, her lion’s paw curled tight around a frayed emerald swatch card. [53]
She was blissfully aberrant, half feathered avian with wings that caught the fog’s faint silver glow, half feline with tawny stripes that mapped the constellations she’d memorized as a child, and her only goal was to find the garden of forgotten names, the place where every lost piece of identity discarded by the world grew back as wild, sweet-smelling blooms. [113]
The emerald swatch card was the last memento of her twin, Kael, who’d vanished three years prior chasing the same myth, and the faint, grassy stain on its corner matched the exact shade of the garden’s rumored foliage, a clue no other seeker had ever managed to obtain. [161]
She was working within the strange, slippery timeframe as memories alter, where each step she took across the ship’s warped deck shifted the carnival’s layout, sometimes replacing the ring toss booth with a childhood bedroom, sometimes turning the haunted house entrance into the overgrown stone arch she’d seen in Kael’s final letter. [213]
This space held a strange, unkindled solace, a comfort that never quite sparked into joy, never quite curdled into grief, because every lost memory lingering in the fog kept Kael one breath closer, even when she couldn’t see him. [252]
The tone of trapped freedom wrapped around her like her own wings, for while she could wander every inch of the drifting ship and every thread of memory tied to it, she could not leave until she found what she was looking for, and that limitation felt like the first real choice she’d been given since Kael vanished. [310]
She knew that to recapture the trail Kael had left, she could not rely on physical clues alone, for the ship’s magic warped every solid thing the longer it drifted in the fog. [343]
Instead, she had to work through narrative threads, pulling on the half-remembered stories Kael had told her as children, the tales of hidden gardens and lost names that they’d scribbled in the margins of their school notebooks, the jokes they’d shared while sneaking into county carnivals after dark, each story a glowing, golden thread she could follow through the shifting fog. [404]
Each thread she pulled shifted the deck under her paws, until the cotton candy stand melted away into a patch of clover, the Ferris wheel’s creak softened into the sound of bees buzzing, and the stone arch she’d seen in Kael’s letter loomed right in front of her, its edges draped in the exact emerald green from her swatch card. [464]
When she stepped through, she found Kael sitting on a stone bench surrounded by flowers that each bore a tiny, handwritten name tag, grinning as he held up a matching emerald swatch card, his own half-avian, half-feline form glowing soft in the garden’s golden light. [509]
He explained that he’d gotten lost when the memory threads shifted under him, and he’d been waiting for three years, tending to the flowers that held the names of people and things the world had forgotten, too afraid to try and find his way back alone. [555]
Lira sat beside him, pressing her swatch card next to his, and for a long while they just watched the bees drift between the blooms, the unkindled solace she’d carried for three years finally sparking into warm, bright joy that settled in both their bones. [600]
They did not leave right away, for the garden sat right at the heart of the drifting carnival ship, and the trapped freedom they’d both felt separately now felt like a gift, a chance to stay among the forgotten names as long as they wanted, no longer alone. [648]
Every so often, they would pull on a new narrative thread, recapturing a lost memory or a forgotten name to add to their garden, their blissfully aberrant existence a quiet reminder that no one ever truly vanishes as long as someone is willing to tell their story. [695]
