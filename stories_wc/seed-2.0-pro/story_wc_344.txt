Under the soft hush of a distant morning, Maren, the lunar-eclipse locksmith, picked her way across the frost-filigreed mezzanine of the frozen library above clouds, her boots crunching thin layers of ice that glinted like crushed pearl. [37]
She had traveled three months through wind-scattered mountain passes to get here, her motivation sharp and steady: to learn the rhythm of repair, a skill no locksmith on the lowland plains had ever mastered. [71]
She knew the knowledge she sought was not in the leather-bound tomes lined along the main shelves, but through scraps of prophecy hidden in a library's corner, tucked behind a stack of frozen travelogues no one had touched in two hundred years. [113]
When she pulled the stack aside, she found a dented oak box, its surface etched with eclipse patterns that matched the silver tattoo curling around her left wrist, and inside sat a full clockmaker's gear assortment, each tiny cog and wheel frosted with a thin layer of ice that glowed faintly blue. [165]
Tucked under the gears was a scrap of yellowed parchment that directed her to the library’s west wing, where a hall of mirrors had been sealed since the last great eclipse, when the library had risen above the cloud line to escape the lowland wars. [210]
A driftwood nostalgia wrapped around her as she walked, the air thick with the faint, familiar scent of salt and old wood, as if the library had carried pieces of her childhood coastal home with it when it lifted into the sky. [252]
When she pushed open the oak door to the wing, the first thing she saw was the hall of mirrors, each pane polished to a hauntingly bright sheen, reflecting hundreds of versions of herself holding the oak box of gears, each reflection slightly out of step with the next. [301]
She set the box on a small stone table at the hall’s entrance, and reached up to uncap the small glass vial of eclipse dust she wore around her neck, the fine silver powder swirling inside as if it had a will of its own. [346]
The prophecy had said that the mirrors held the broken rhythms of every thing that had ever been damaged beyond fixing in the lowlands: cracked clocks, split door locks, broken promises, fraying family bonds, all frozen in the glass waiting to be mended. [389]
She sprinkled a pinch of the eclipse dust over the clockmaker’s gear assortment, and watched as the ice on the cogs melted, each tiny piece lifting into the air and floating down the hall of mirrors, slotting into gaps in the glass that she had not noticed before. [437]
As each gear slotted into place, the reflections in the hauntingly bright glass began to sync up, each version of Maren moving in the same slow, steady rhythm, their hands moving as if turning a lock or adjusting a clock spring, a gentle hum thrumming through the floor under her boots. [488]
She recognized the rhythm instantly: it was the same lullaby her grandmother, the last eclipse locksmith before her, had sung to her as a child while teaching her to rekey old locks, the same beat the tide had followed outside their coastal cottage every evening. [533]
She had spent years thinking repair was about forcing broken pieces back into their old shapes, but as she watched the gears turn in the mirror glass, she realized it was instead about meeting each broken thing exactly where it was, and matching its unique rhythm before you tried to put it back together. [587]
The driftwood nostalgia settled warmer in her chest then, and she knew her grandmother had been here too, decades earlier, leaving the scraps of prophecy and the gear assortment for the next locksmith who would come looking for the same truth. [628]
When the sun finally peeked over the distant cloud line, painting the ice of the library in soft gold, she closed the oak box of remaining gears, tucked it into her pack, and turned to head back down the mountain, the rhythm of repair thrumming steady in her bones. [677]
