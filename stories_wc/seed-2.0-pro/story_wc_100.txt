Three days after the waterlogged, unlabeled mysterious package arrived on his back porch, Elias Hale, a weary undertaker who’d spent two decades tending to the unclaimed dead of his remote coastal town, loaded the crate and his scuffed scuba gear into his dented aluminum skiff. [45]
For years, he’d carried the quiet, unshakable motivation to give rest a permanent address, instead of letting unmarked graves erode into the tide each storm season. [71]
He was inadvertently stubborn, too, the kind of man who’d dig out a grave in a hailstorm rather than push a funeral back an hour, even when no one asked him to make the effort. [106]
The package held no note, only a cracked, frosted broken lighthouse lens, and a tattered 1890s nautical chart marking the site of a forgotten underwater city library, sunk during a hurricane that had killed 117 people whose bodies were never recovered. [147]
He anchored 12 miles out, adjusted his oxygen tank, and slipped into the frigid blue, where a strange, chased stillness hung thick, as if the ocean itself was holding its breath to avoid drawing the attention of something sharp and hungry lingering far below. [191]
Elias had always had a knack for occupying impossible spaces, squeezing between craggy cliff faces to retrieve lost remains, fitting 10 tiny caskets into a plot too small for three, so navigating the sunken library’s crumbled marble archway felt almost second nature. [233]
The air pockets trapped between the library’s intact upper shelves hummed with faint, unplaceable sound, the faded remnant of distress signals sent from the lighthouse the night the storm hit, never seen by passing ships because the lens had shattered mid-squall. [274]
He hauled the broken lighthouse lens down the last of the crumbled stone steps, to the library’s central reading nook, where rows of waterlogged poetry collections lined the shelves, their pages fused together into soft, weighty blocks that felt like folded hands. [316]
He’d spent weeks researching the sunken city, learning that the library had been the heart of the community, a place where children gathered for storytime and widows came to write letters to their lost spouses, so he knew it was the perfect place to revitalize the idea of rest for the 117 people who’d never been given a proper grave. [376]
He mounted the lens on a chipped marble plinth at the center of the nook, adjusting it until it caught the thin slant of sunlight filtering through the ocean surface, casting faint, flickering prisms across every wall of the library, turning the old distress signals trapped in the air pockets into soft, warm beacons instead of cries for help. [435]
He’d never told anyone about his plan, partly out of the inadvertently stubborn urge to see it through without fanfare, and partly because he feared the town council would dismiss the idea as a silly waste of time better spent fixing the town’s crumbling new graveyard fence. [482]
He pulled 117 tiny hand-carved wooden name tags from his dive suit pocket, tucking one into each crevice of the library’s shelves, each marked with the name of a person lost that night, finally giving rest a permanent address that no storm could ever wash away. [528]
The chased stillness of the deep softened as he worked, the hum of the old distress signals fading into a low, contented murmur, as if the people who’d been lost for 130 years were finally letting themselves breathe easy. [567]
Elias had lost his own wife to a riptide 10 years prior, her body never recovered, so occupying impossible spaces like this sunken library had always been his way of clinging to the parts of loved ones that the ocean tried to steal. [610]
When he finished tucking the last name tag, he paused, running a gloved finger over the broken lighthouse lens, and realized he’d not only come to revitalize the rest of the lost 117, but to finally let his own grief rest too, instead of carrying it with him every time he dug a new grave. [665]
As he kicked his way back up to the skiff, he glanced over his shoulder, watching the prisms from the lens dance across the library walls, no longer distress signals calling for rescue, but gentle markers letting everyone know the lost were home at last. [710]
That night, when he returned to his small cottage, he slept for 12 straight hours, the first time he hadn’t woken up gasping from nightmares of his wife calling for him in the waves since she’d disappeared. [747]
