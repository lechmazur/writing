Lila, the only honest child of a three-generation con artist family, leaned against the cold stainless steel prep table in the city’s oldest funeral home, counting the faint scuffs on the toe of her work boots while she waited for her shift to end. [44]
Tucked in her canvas bag was the dented vintage ink pot she’d found tucked in her father’s old briefcase three days prior, after he’d skipped town to avoid testifying against a city councilman he’d helped embezzle public housing funds. [83]
She’d tested it once that morning, dipping a scrap of receipt paper in its deep indigo ink, and found the substance held a strange, uncategorized property that liquefies rigid thinking, turning the inflexible rules her father had taught her about grifting and survival into soft, negotiable lines she could rearrange however she wanted. [136]
Her whole life, she’d chased the quiet, unspoken goal to find the mathematics of a fair trade, a formula where no one lost more than they gained, no one was tricked out of what they deserved, and every exchange left both parties better off than they had started. [184]
Three weeks prior, she’d helped a refugee from a central African nation slip into the funeral home’s back prep room, a planned breach of the city’s harsh immigration enforcement policies that she’d executed by waiting actively, lingering at the front desk for four hours to mislead the border patrol agents who’d been trailing the man after he’d applied for political asylum at the local port of entry. [251]
The room hummed with a strange deathly vitality, the sharp, clean scent of embalming fluid mixing with the faint, sweet smell of lilacs from the casket display next door, and the quiet thrum of life from the break room down the hall where her coworker made tea for the family of a deceased grandmother who’d passed the night before. [310]
The man she’d helped, a mathematics professor who’d fled his home after criticizing a corrupt authoritarian regime, had promised to teach her the fair trade formula she’d spent years chasing, if she could get him the vintage ink pot her father had stolen from the university archive during a grift two years prior. [363]
He needed the ink, which he’d made himself decades earlier, to sign the final paperwork for his political asylum application, as the immigration officials refused to accept any signature that didn’t match the one on his old university ID, which had been signed with the same indigo mixture. [411]
She’d told him to meet her here after closing, when the building was empty save for the quiet dead and the hum of the refrigeration units holding the bodies waiting for services, confident that no immigration agent would ever think to search a funeral home prep room for a man seeking safety. [463]
A soft knock on the back door pulled her from her thoughts, and she opened it to find the professor standing there, rain dripping off his wool coat, a thick stack of paper tucked under one arm and a small, beat-up calculator held in his other hand. [510]
She pulled the vintage ink pot from her bag and set it on the stainless steel table, watching as he twisted off the cap and dipped a fountain pen into the thick indigo fluid, his hand steady as he signed his name on the asylum paperwork exactly as it had appeared on his ID twenty years prior. [567]
Once he finished signing, he pushed the stack of signed paperwork to the side and slid a crumpled sheet of notebook paper covered in scribbled equations across the table to her, explaining that the math of a fair trade didn’t rely on strict numbers, but on the willingness of both parties to give what they could spare to meet the other’s most urgent need. [631]
She traced the equations with her finger, the memory of the ink’s strange property that liquefies rigid thinking settling warm in her chest, as she realized she’d already lived the formula that day: she’d given him the ink he needed to secure his safety, and he’d given her the answer she’d spent her whole life chasing, no tricks, no losses, no fine print. [694]
Outside, the rain slowed to a soft drizzle, and the hum of the prep room’s refrigeration units mixed with the distant sound of a jazz band playing at a bar down the street, the deathly vitality of the space wrapping around them like a blanket as they both sat for a minute, savoring the rare, perfect fair trade they’d just completed, no con, no catch, just two people getting exactly what they needed. [767]
