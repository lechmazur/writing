<story>
Solemn civil-rights archivist Elara Voss leaned against the oak rail of the zeppelin-mounted floating library, her calloused fingers brushing the frayed edge of the recently unearthed encrypted diary that had launched her current mission. [34]
This work unfolded three weeks after the trove of lost Freedom Rider letters were found tucked in the floorboards of a demolished Montgomery bus depot, their contents hinting at a never-documented case of a Black activist who had vanished mid-protest in 1962. [76]
Elara’s core motivation, sharpened by decades of pushing past whitewashed institutional records to uncover truth in tension, had driven her to petition the archive’s board for access to the rare retrocognitive projection gear mounted on the zeppelin library’s lower deck. [116]
The gear had malfunctioned mid-projection, leaving her retrocognitively trapped, her physical body suspended in a cryo-pod on the zeppelin while her consciousness drifted across the sun-scorched coastal dunes where the activist was last seen. [150]
Beneath the dunes, half-shielded by sea grass and wind-carved sand shelves, glinted the obsidian lagoon the activist had referenced repeatedly in the ciphered margins of the encrypted diary, its surface so dark it swallowed sunlight without a glimmer. [188]
A searing reverie washed over her as she spotted a figure in a frayed denim jacket, the activist, huddled at the lagoon’s edge, flinching at the distant wail of 1960s police sirens that cut through the hum of the zeppelin’s propellers bleeding through from her own timeline. [235]
She knew she could not touch the figure or speak directly, so she resolved to parley the only way she could: arranging beach debris as messages that would register as strange, familiar signs in the activist’s line of sight, not enough to disrupt the timeline but enough to signal she was an ally. [288]
First she stacked sun-bleached driftwood posts into the shape of the freedom fist the activist had drawn on every page of the diary, then scattered crushed white shell fragments to form the code name “Moth” she had used to sign her cryptic entries. [331]
The activist’s head snapped up, her dark eyes scanning the dunes, her hand flying to the worn leather diary tucked in her jacket pocket, the same diary Elara held in her physical hands 60 years in the future, floating 300 feet above the same stretch of coast. [378]
When Moth spoke, her voice was soft but steady, directed at the empty dunes where she could sense Elara’s presence, and Elara learned the activist had not been killed, as historians had speculated, but had hidden in a cave behind the lagoon for three days before fleeing to Mississippi to continue organizing under a new name. [434]
Moth had intentionally left the encrypted diary and the bundle of letters in the bus depot floorboards, she explained, so that someone who cared enough to crack the code would find her story, and the stories of the 12 other organizers who had been erased from official records after the protest. [485]
As the sirens faded into the distance, Elara felt the hum of the zeppelin’s gear grow louder, the tugs of her own timeline pulling her out of the retrocognitive trap, and she used the last of her energy to arrange a single piece of red sea glass at the center of the freedom fist, a sign that she had received the message. [547]
When she jolted awake in the cryo-pod, the encrypted diary still in her lap, she flipped to the final page and found a tiny, previously unnoticeable drawing of a red sea glass shard tucked next to the freedom fist, proof that their parley had been real, not just a trick of the projection gear. [601]
Over the next month, Elara cracked the rest of the diary’s code, publishing the stories of Moth and the 12 erased organizers, forcing state archives to amend their records and add the names to the official civil rights registry. [640]
Sometimes, when she leans over the rail of the floating library as it drifts over the Southern coast, she can still feel the hot sand under her feet and hear Moth’s voice, a reminder that the truth only stays buried if no one is willing to reach across time to dig it up. [693]
