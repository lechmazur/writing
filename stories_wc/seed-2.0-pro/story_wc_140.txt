No one knows their name, not even them, ever since the hospital clerical error filed a death certificate under the only social security number they ever had, wiping their official existence from every state database overnight. [36]
They live in the towering labyrinth of stacked shipping containers at the city’s industrial port, where rusted steel walls absorb sound and seagulls drown out the hum of the distant downtown, and where the rhythm of the tides marks their days instead of clocks that no longer recognize their ID. [86]
They filter reality differently than most, seeing the faint, glowing traces of the lives that passed through each space instead of just cold metal and salt spray. [113]
Their only goal is to discover the texture of memories, the little, unrecorded moments that official histories erase when they reduce people to names on forms or, in their case, no name at all. [147]
They work by collecting fragments of overheard stories, lingering by docked cargo ships at high tide when crews swap tales on deck, hiding in empty container gaps when longshoremen stop for lunch and gossip about old jobs and lost loved ones, tucking every sliver of conversation into a tattered notebook they carry in their coat pocket. [203]
Three tides back, they found a crumpled, faded label from a poison bottle tucked in the crack of a dented container marked for agricultural shipments, its red skull and crossbones smudged but still bright enough to catch their eye as the low tide sun slanted through the stack gaps. [252]
They began to layer fragments of stories they had collected over the past months with the glowing traces they saw clinging to the container’s walls, trying to piece together the erased history tied to the small, discarded label. [290]
Two high tides later, they caught a fragment of a story from a gray-haired longshoreman leaning against a piling, talking about a new hire 10 years prior who had been exposed to a hidden shipment of illegal pesticide when he broke open a dented container, the company covering up the incident so thoroughly no record of the man or the spill ever existed. [353]
They added this fragment to their notes, matching the time of the incident to the shipping date printed on the faded poison label, and watching as the glowing traces around the container sharpened into a faint image of a young man with a scar on his cheek, laughing as he pried open the container’s rusted lock. [409]
Over the next three tide cycles, they collected more pieces: a cook on a grain freighter mentioning a boy from her village who moved to the port to send money home and never wrote back, a retired dock worker muttering about a company payout to a woman with a young son who never filed a formal missing person report, a street vendor by the port gates remembering a young man who always bought a tamale with extra hot sauce every morning before his shift. [493]
Each new fragment made the young man’s memory more vivid, the texture of his life as real to them as their own vague, half-remembered flashes of a hospital bed and a clerk apologizing for a “terrible mistake” that left them without a past. [536]
They did not need a name for the young man, or for themselves, to know that collecting these fragments was a way to push back against the systems that erase inconvenient lives, the ones that write off dead workers as clerical errors or non-existent people as unworthy of record. [585]
Last low tide, they taped the poison bottle label to the inside of their notebook, next to the scrawled fragments of the young man’s story, layering one more piece of an erased history into the growing collection of memories they had curated, one that felt as much a part of their own story as any of the half-forgotten moments they could almost reach. [648]
As the next high tide rolled in, carrying the sound of new crew stories across the container stacks, they turned to a blank page in their notebook, ready to catch the next fragment, the next memory, the next piece of a life someone had tried to throw away. [696]
