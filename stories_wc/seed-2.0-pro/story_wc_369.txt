Kael, a labyrinth maintenance worker whose nightly shifts usually consisted of prying loose flagstones and clearing overgrown ivy, froze mid-step when he heard a pin clatter across the stone floor of the tower’s silent corridor. [35]
The storm outside raged, sending sheets of rain skittering against the tower’s leaded glass, but thin slivers of cold silver moonlight still pierced the clouds to pool at his scuffed work boots. [69]
The pin had bounced off a dented, brass object half-buried in a crack in the wall: an astronomer’s broken sextant, its glass lens cracked, its curved arc bent almost in two. [107]
Kael had taken this low-paying, isolated job three months prior for one quiet, unshared reason: he wanted to discover what trees witness, the old stories they held in their rings, the secrets they had watched unfold in the labyrinth’s shaded paths for centuries. [159]
The old tower’s former resident, an astronomer who had vanished the winter before, had left scattered notes across the labyrinth referencing “quantified sorrow,” a measurement of unspoken grief he claimed clung to every living thing in the woods. [207]
When Kael knelt and brushed the dust off the sextant, he noticed the tiny crack in its lens was shrinking, the brass glinting brighter where the moonlight touched it, confirming the note he’d found last week that said the device heals in moonlight. [255]
The notes had also explained that to deconstruct the layers of quantified sorrow clinging to the labyrinth’s trees, and unlock their memories, one had to work by mapping scattered dreams, the half-remembered visions that drifted through the tower’s upper halls each night. [305]
He tucked the sextant into his work jacket pocket, climbed the winding stone stairs to the tower’s open observation deck, and spread out the tattered stack of the astronomer’s maps he’d collected from labyrinth clearing shifts over the past months. [352]
As the moonlight continued to mend the sextant, Kael closed his eyes and let the faint, drifting dreams of the labyrinth’s visitors wash over him: a child losing a stuffed rabbit in the hedges, a teen sharing their first kiss under an oak, an elderly woman visiting the spot where her husband had proposed fifty years prior. [408]
He marked each tiny, vivid memory on the astronomer’s maps, matching the location of each vision to the corresponding tree in the labyrinth, and when he adjusted the now fully mended sextant to align with the brightest star above the oak at the labyrinth’s center, the device hummed softly. [462]
The layers of quantified sorrow that had hung thick around the labyrinth for years began to unravel, each unit of grief lifting off the trees like dandelion fluff, carried away on the cool storm wind that swept over the observation deck. [508]
Kael opened his eyes, and for a single, glowing moment, he could see every memory the oak at the labyrinth’s center had held for three hundred years: first plantings, generations of visitors, small joys and quiet griefs, all woven together in the tree’s slow, steady rings. [557]
It was not the grand, world-altering secret he had half-expected, but a collection of small, tender moments, the kind of mundane miracles that most people walked past every day without ever noticing. [599]
He tucked the now functional sextant back into his pocket, folded the maps carefully, and made a note in his work log to trim the ivy off the old oak’s lower branches the next day, a small offering for the tree that had shared its stories with him. [645]
When he walked back down the winding stairs to the corridor, the storm was fading, and the faint glow of dawn was just starting to bleed over the tops of the labyrinth’s hedges, soft and golden. [688]
