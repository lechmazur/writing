In the mirror world’s alchemical orchard greenhouse, where endless night clung to glass panes thick with frost, the scattered memory keeper huddled on a crate of half-grown starfruit saplings. [29]
Her only motivation, the quiet fire that had kept her walking for ten mirror-years, was to rekindle the dying realm bleeding its light into the void outside the greenhouse walls. [59]
Directly across from her, set into the greenhouse’s northern wall, was the mica window, its translucent, shimmery surface clouded with decades of unlit ash, no longer glowing with the faint golden hue she’d read about in old scribe scrolls. [98]
She’d come to perform the old ritual, the one that birthed woven dawns, soft, iridescent streaks of light stitched from shared joy that once lit every corner of the mirror world before the great forgetting. [133]
The ritual worked through forgotten songs, fragments of lullabies, harvest hymns, and wedding chants she’d collected from half-erased ghosts and crumbling stone markers across the realm. [159]
Each line of the songs held the power of the bonds they represented, and she knew the ritual knits communities together even across the gaps of lost time and forgotten names. [190]
She stood, brushing frost off her wool shawl, and moved to the mica window, pulling a length of sun-dyed cotton from her pack to swaddle the base of the pane where cold air seeped through the rotting wooden frame. [229]
When the frame was sealed tight, she pressed her forehead to the cool mica surface, closed her eyes, and began to sing, starting with the lullaby a ghostly grandmother had hummed to her outside a long-abandoned village last winter. [268]
The first note hung in the frosty air, soft and warm, and a tiny fleck of gold appeared on the mica, growing brighter as she wove in the harvest hymn she’d found carved into the lintel of a burned-down granary three months prior. [311]
As she sang, she could feel the echoes of the people who had once sung these songs, their laughter, their grief, their quiet hopes, winding around each other like threads, no longer isolated by the great forgetting that had snuffed out the realm’s light. [355]
The gold on the mica spread, painting streaks of pink and orange across its surface, the first of the woven dawns seeping through the pane to wrap around the saplings in the greenhouse, their leaves unfurling for the first time in decades. [397]
She sang on, adding the wedding chant a pair of translucent lovers had whispered to each other when she’d stumbled on their hidden cottage deep in the orchard’s overgrown southern edge, and the light grew so bright she had to squint, even through her closed eyelids. [443]
Somewhere outside the greenhouse, she heard a faint, answering hum, then another, then a dozen, the scattered remnants of the realm’s people hearing their old songs, feeling their lost connections reknit, drawn toward the greenhouse by the light spilling through the mica. [485]
When she finished the last line of the final song, she pulled back from the mica window, her chest light with a sense of bound release, the weight of ten years of searching and collecting falling away as she saw the first full woven dawn spill over the greenhouse’s ceiling, painting the frost on the panes in every shade of peach and amber. [548]
The door of the greenhouse creaked open, and a small girl with ash on her cheeks stepped inside, followed by an old man leaning on a carved wooden cane, then a young mother holding a swaddled infant, all of them smiling, their faces lit by the warm glow of the dawn. [599]
She stood watching as they gathered around the saplings, touching their leaves, greeting each other like long-lost family even if they could not remember shared histories, the magic of the songs already working to knit their fractured community back together. [639]
Beyond the greenhouse walls, the endless night was already fading, streaks of the woven dawn spreading across the mirror world’s sky, touching every abandoned village and overgrown field, waking more people from their half-forgotten slumber. [674]
The scattered memory keeper smiled, knowing her work was only just beginning, but that the realm she’d fought so hard to save was finally on the path to being whole again. [705]
