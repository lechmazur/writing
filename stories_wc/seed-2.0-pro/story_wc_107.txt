Mara, the contemplative glassblower, kicked a slush puddle by the rusted access panel of the abandoned control room, her boots squelching as she hauled her portable kiln over the threshold. [30]
It was the third week of spring ice melting, and icicles dripped from the exposed rafters onto dials that had not turned in 40 years, their faces crusted with frost and grime. [62]
She was thoughtfully spirited, the kind of craftsperson who saw potential in every discarded space, and her current motivation to craft safe zones for inquiry had led her to this forgotten outpost perched at the edge of the national park. [102]
Tucked in the collar of her fire-resistant coat was a cracked leather bookmark, frayed at the edges, stamped with the constellation Orion, a gift from her grandmother who had first taught her to melt sand into light. [139]
She had a silly, beloved habit of dramatically narrating mundane tasks to pass the time, so she cleared her throat and addressed the empty control room as she laid out her glass rods. [172]
“Behold, the great artisan prepares the sacred raw materials, soon to be forged into vessels that hold space for every question no one dares to ask out loud,” she boomed, her voice echoing off the tile floors and making a sparrow dart out of a broken window. [219]
This project was tied to the quiet thawing heart she’d carried since her grandmother’s death two years prior, when she’d almost abandon her craft entirely, convinced no one cared about the small, gentle spaces she made. [255]
She’d spent those first 18 months of grief locked in her tiny city workshop, making only plain, functional glassware for local cafes, never the colorful, lumpy curiosity jars she’d once made for kids to store rocks and secrets and half-formed ideas. [296]
Then, last month, she’d found the cracked leather bookmark tucked in the back of her grandmother’s old astronomy textbook, and the note scrawled on its back: “The world needs more places to be messy, no answers required.” [333]
That was when she’d decided to abandon the safety of paid commissions, pack up her kiln, and travel to abandoned spaces across the state to leave small, heat-resistant glass orbs, each holding a slip of paper that invited anyone who found it to write down a question they were afraid to share. [385]
As she heated the first glass rod in the kiln, she kept up her running narration, describing the way the clear sand turned honey-gold as if it were a grand cosmic ritual, her tone holding that perfect weighty lightness that made even the most ordinary act feel like a gift. [435]
Outside, the ice melting picked up, sending small cascades of slush down the side of the control room, and Mara smiled as she twisted the molten glass into a round, pocked orb, intentionally leaving small bubbles trapped inside to look like distant stars. [478]
She set the orb on the cold metal control panel to cool, right next to a dial labeled “WIND SPEED,” and tucked the cracked leather bookmark under it to hold down the small slip of paper she’d printed that morning, which read “What’s a question you’ve been too scared to ask?” [529]
She would leave three more orbs in this space, one by the broken window, one by the rusted desk in the corner, one tucked into the space between two circuit boards, each a tiny safe zone for inquiry for any hiker, explorer, or curious kid who stumbled into the control room in the coming months. [584]
By the time she packed up her kiln to head to the next abandoned site, the sun was breaking through the clouds, gilding the edges of the melting icicles, and Mara could feel the last of the frost around her own thawing heart melt away, soft and warm. [632]
She paused in the doorway for a minute, grinning, and called out a final dramatic narration to the empty room, “And so the great artisan leaves this sanctuary of questions, waiting for the first brave soul to step inside and speak their truth.” [675]
She touched the faint indent of the cracked leather bookmark’s stamp through her coat, already looking forward to the next space she could fill with soft, unstructured possibility, no rules, no pressure, just room to wonder. [711]
