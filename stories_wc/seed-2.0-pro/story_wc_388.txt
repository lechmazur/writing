Jessa, a veteran conspiracy debunker with a sharply observant eye for gaps in official records, had driven three hours through radioactive dust storms to reach the wasteland orchard, the only patch of green left within 50 miles of the old corporate mining town. [43]
This trip came three years after the liquidation of the mining company’s assets, when local leaders had buried all reports of a catastrophic radiation leak that killed 12 workers, framing their deaths as a random dust storm accident. [81]
Jessa’s driving motivation, honed over a decade of calling out cover-ups, was to excavate truth from comfortable lies that let powerful people walk away from harm they’d caused. [109]
The orchard’s owner, Mara, was the widow of the mine’s lead foreman, who’d died in the leak; she’d told Jessa over a static-filled call that she’d shredded her husband’s final letters years prior, too scared to share what they revealed, and left the fragments in a rusted lockbox under the largest apple tree at the property’s center. [166]
Jessa knelt beneath the gnarled, fruit-heavy branches, pulled the lockbox from the dirt, and spent the next two hours by reassembling shredded letters, matching water-stained edges and scrawled handwriting to piece together the foreman’s final days. [202]
One partial paragraph mentioned the foreman had hidden a flawed diamond rumored to be cursed, stolen from the mine’s executive safe the day before the leak, when he’d realized the company planned to blow the containment wall to cut costs. [242]
The letters said he’d stashed the diamond at a hidden stillpoint in the orchard, a small depression where radiation levels were inexplicably zero, shielded by a natural formation of dense, mineral-rich rock beneath the soil. [277]
Her sharply observant gaze darted between the lines of text and the curve of the orchard around her, and she began to intuit exactly where the stillpoint was, noting how the apple trees grew thicker and brighter in a small circle 20 feet west of where she knelt. [325]
She brushed loose topsoil away from the spot with her gloved hands, and after 10 minutes of careful digging, her fingers closed around a small velvet pouch that held the cloudy, cracked diamond, and a folded microSD card tucked beneath it. [366]
The local rumor that the diamond was cursed had sprung up after three company executives who’d previously owned it suffered fatal accidents, but the letters revealed each death was tied to the executives’ efforts to cover up the mine’s long list of safety violations, not any magical property of the flawed stone. [418]
The microSD card, the foreman wrote, held footage of the executive meeting where they approved the deliberate containment breach, plus payroll records proving they’d hidden the radiation exposure of 72 other workers for two years prior to the leak. [457]
For years, the town’s residents had clung to the comfortable lie that the leak was an unforeseeable act of god, too scared of losing the small payouts the mining company had given them to push for further investigation, even as more locals developed rare radiation-related cancers. [503]
Jessa slipped the microSD card into her protective gear’s sealed pocket and left the diamond tucked back in the stillpoint, knowing the stone’s cursed reputation would keep looters away long enough for her to publish her full report, complete with the reassembled letters and the footage as evidence. [551]
Mara was waiting by the orchard’s gate when Jessa emerged, dust sticking to the hem of her radiation suit, and nodded when Jessa held up the small stack of taped-together letter fragments, a silent confirmation that she’d found what she’d come for. [593]
As Jessa drove back through the dust storms, she already knew her report would upend the town’s fragile peace, but she also knew the truth would let families whose loved ones had gotten sick or died finally get the compensation and accountability they deserved. [637]
She’d never been interested in the flashy conspiracies of online grifters, preferring the slow, unglamorous work of digging through scraps and lies to find the small, sharp truths that could actually change people’s lives. [671]
The wasteland orchard, thriving where everyone thought nothing could grow, felt like a perfect metaphor for the truth she carried: even in the most poisoned ground, something good and lasting could take root, if you were willing to dig for it. [712]
