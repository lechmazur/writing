Lira, a veteran celestial signal interpreter, knelt on the rusted grate of the suspended radio tower city, the gorge’s cold updraft tangling the ends of her frayed wool coat as lunar perigee painted the surrounding cloudbanks in faint, iridescent silver. [40]
She had traveled three days across wind-scoured plateau to reach this derelict cluster of transmission spires, driven by her long-held, quiet motivation to find the beginning in every ending, even the ones most people wrote off as permanent static. [79]
Tucked in the inner pocket of her coat was the Hollywood star fragment she’d fished from the gorge’s debris field two days prior, its pitted, glittering surface still holding faint traces of the celebrity name once stamped across its full form. [120]
Old archival notes from the tower’s former operators had referenced a shard of hush, a rare celestial emission that only manifested when the moon was closest to Earth, wrapping all unneeded sound in a soft, impenetrable silence to let faint, ancient signals cut through. [164]
She pulled a dented multi-tool from her belt and pried open the rusted door of the main control hut, noting immediately that the air inside was serenely electric, as if the tower’s long-dormant circuits were already thrumming with the approaching energy of the perigee alignment. [209]
The hulking analog receiver sat against the far wall, its dials cracked and its speaker frayed, and Lira knew the only way to tune it to the shard of hush’s frequency was by discarding assumptions, ditching all the standard celestial signal protocols she’d learned in her formal training. [257]
She pulled the Hollywood star fragment from her pocket and fitted it into the receiver’s broken frequency dial, the fragment’s crystalline structure acting as a perfect conduit for the faint celestial energy she was chasing, its glitter matching the soft glow of the moon peeking through the hut’s cracked window. [307]
She closed her eyes, first letting go of the assumption that celestial signals only arrived as radio waves, then discarding the idea that the tower’s non-functional digital components were useless, then releasing the belief that endings were ever truly final, each discarded belief making the serenely electric hum in the room grow a little louder, a little clearer. [365]
When she opened her eyes again, the receiver’s speaker was emitting a soft, velvet silence that wrapped around every other sound in the room—the wind outside, the creak of the suspended towers, the distant gurgle of the river far below the gorge—and she knew she had successfully tuned into the shard of hush. [418]
She leaned in, pressing her palm to the cool metal of the receiver, and the silence began to carry faint, layered sounds: the laughter of tourists who had once visited the Hollywood Walk of Fame where her fragment had originated, the chatter of radio operators who had manned this tower 70 years prior, the soft hum of the star formation that had first emitted the signal she was chasing 12 billion years before. [491]
Lira smiled, because she could see how every ending tied back to a beginning: the end of the Hollywood star’s time on the Walk of Fame had left it a fragment perfect for conducting celestial energy, the end of the radio tower’s commercial use had left it undisturbed enough to capture the shard of hush without interference, the end of that ancient star formation’s life had sent out the signal that was now reaching Earth during this perigee window. [570]
She pulled a small, worn notebook from her bag and jotted down her observations, noting that the serenely electric current in the room had stabilized, and that the fragment in the dial was glowing so brightly it cast tiny star-shaped reflections across the hut’s dented metal walls. [617]
When the perigee window ended 47 minutes later, the soft silence faded, and the familiar sounds of the suspended tower city and the gorge below came rushing back, but Lira carried the memory of the signal with her, the weight of her discovery settling warm in her chest. [665]
She removed the Hollywood star fragment from the dial, tucked it back into her coat pocket, and packed her supplies, already planning her return for the next perigee, when she would test if she could pull even older, fainter signals from the void using the same method of discarding every assumption she had ever held about how the universe worked. [725]
As she walked across the rusted grates back toward the plateau trail, the moon hung low and bright over the gorge, and Lira felt a quiet, steady joy, knowing she had fulfilled her goal of finding the beginning in every ending, even the ones that seemed lost to time and decay. [776]
