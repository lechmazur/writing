Mara, a committed caretaker who had tended the cloud-strung orchards of the umbral grotto above the clouds for three decades, stood at the gnarled base of the oldest silver pine, her boots dusted with pale, pollen-like cloud residue. [38]
This was her first week back in the grotto after forgiving the unforgivable, after letting go of the rage she’d carried since her sister’s reckless prank had left their mother’s grove scorched to ash two decades prior. [75]
Her only goal now, soft and unshakable, was to find the source of contentment she had felt only once before, sitting on this same pine’s highest branch as a seven-year-old. [105]
She had long favored climbing trees to think better, the pull of gravity and the rustle of wind through needles clearing the static from her mind far faster than any quiet sit in the grotto’s stone halls. [142]
Her staff knew her as kindly decisive, quick to offer a warm honey cake to a tired worker but unflinching when it came to culling blighted branches or reassigning tasks to suit each caretaker’s strengths. [177]
The entire grotto held a vineyard hush this time of year, the air thick with the sweet, green scent of ripening cloudberries, no sound louder than the distant call of a wren or the soft plink of dew dropping from cave stalactites. [219]
Halfway up the tree, her sleeve caught on a knotted branch, yanking the antique hairpin from her bun so her silver-streaked brown hair spilled down her back. [246]
She watched it tumble until it caught on a narrow stone ledge a dozen feet below, its carved mother-of-pearl edge designed to scallop the edge of any updo it secured glinting in the pale, diffused sunlight that filtered through the cloud layer. [288]
The hairpin had belonged to her mother, who had taught her that the cycle of rebirth did not only apply to living things, but to grief, to anger, to love that felt like it had burned to ash. [326]
She climbed carefully down to the ledge, her calloused fingers finding easy purchase on rough bark and rough stone, and when she knelt to pick up the hairpin, she spotted a tiny cluster of golden cloudberries growing from a crack in the rock. [369]
These were the exact strain her mother had grown in the scorched grove, a variety everyone had assumed was lost forever when the fire swept through. [395]
She plucked one, popping it into her mouth, and the bright, honey-tart flavor burst across her tongue, exactly as she remembered from childhood afternoons spent helping her mother tend the grove. [426]
In that moment, she realized the source of contentment she had been searching for was not a hidden spring or a magical relic, but the quiet choice to let old grief make space for new joy, to recognize that what felt lost could always return in a new form. [475]
She slipped the hairpin back into her hair, adjusting it so it would scallop the edge of her braid just as her mother used to wear it, and smiled, noticing for the first time that the wren calling in the distance was the same species her mother had kept as a pet when she was a girl. [532]
Over the next month, she carefully transplanted the cloudberry sprouts from the ledge to a new plot near the grotto’s main hall, inviting her sister, who had returned to the grotto to apologize a month prior, to help her tend the new grove. [575]
The vineyard hush of the grotto wrapped around them as they worked, no words needed to mend the rift that had stood between them for twenty years, only the quiet rhythm of tucking soil around small, fragile roots. [613]
She would climb the silver pine once a week after that, sometimes bringing her sister, sometimes coming alone, to sit in the high branches and watch the new grove grow, each new leaf a reminder that the cycle of rebirth was always working, even when no one was watching. [662]
By the end of the first year, the grove was thick with bright green foliage, and the first crop of golden cloudberries was shared between every caretaker in the grotto, each bite carrying the same warm, familiar flavor that had tied Mara to her mother, to her sister, to the quiet joy of second chances. [717]
She still wore the antique hairpin every day, its scalloped edge catching the light as she moved through the grove, a small, tangible reminder that contentment was never something you had to search for, only something you had to let yourself see, right in front of you. [764]
