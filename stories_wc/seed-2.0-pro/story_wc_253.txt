Two years after the federal happiness mandate made unregulated displays of sadness a misdemeanor punishable by community service, the blank-faced performer arrived at the remote farmhouse, its siding bleached pale by sun and the constant, low thrum of the giant wind turbines that overshadowed every inch of the property. [49]
They were morally ambiguous by trade, their act a loophole in the mandate that classified structured performance as exempt from emotion monitoring, even when the content brushed up against forbidden expressions of loss. [82]
Their client, a widowed farmer who’d lost his wife to a turbine maintenance accident six months prior, had paid three times their usual rate to have them host a small, unregistered service for her, free of the forced cheerful narration required for legal memorials. [126]
The performer carried two items through the screen door: a crumpled paper bag of pine kindling, and a dried flower bouquet the farmer had left on his porch the night before, made of the wild clover and daisies his wife had planted along the turbine access road. [173]
The farmer sat at the kitchen table, hands folded, and the performer said nothing at first, taking a seat across from him to whittle a small wooden clover sprig from a block of cedar, their face flat and unreadable as they began their act. [217]
Their method, honed over dozens of these off-the-books services, was dramatically narrating mundane tasks, framing every small, ordinary movement as a ritual of release that required no forced joy, no performative gratitude for the time shared. [253]
“I am now running my thumb along the edge of the knife, smoothing the rough edge of the clover’s first petal, a motion as slow and careful as the way you used to leave a mug of peppermint tea on her nightstand before you left for morning turbine checks,” they said, their tone flat, an emotional void that let the farmer project his own memories onto the words without fear of judgment or a report to the happiness bureau. [332]
They kept narrating, describing the way they adjusted the dried flower bouquet to catch the weak autumn light slanting through the kitchen window, the way they twisted a length of twine around the base of the wooden clover to tie it to the bouquet’s stem, each small action inflated into a quiet tribute that slipped under the mandate’s radar. [391]
The remade hush of the kitchen, broken only by the performer’s flat narration and the low thrum of the turbines outside, was a miracle of sorts, a space where sobs did not have to be masked as laughter, where grief did not have to be wrapped in false statements of joy to be acceptable. [445]
The performer never asked for details of the farmer’s loss, never offered empty comfort, because their only motivation was to cultivate lightness in grief, to give people a space to carry their sadness without the weight of hiding it, even if it meant risking a fine or a revocation of their performance license if they were caught. [502]
When they finished tying the wooden clover to the bouquet, they slid it across the table to the farmer, narrating the movement as “the transfer of a small, permanent piece of the care you held for her, no frills, no lies, just something you can set on the windowsill to remind you that you do not have to be happy to be okay.” [565]
The farmer nodded, his shoulders shaking as he picked up the bouquet, and the performer did not look away, did not comment, just sat in the remade hush of the kitchen while the turbines thrummed outside, their blank face a safe screen for whatever emotion the farmer needed to release. [615]
When the farmer had composed himself enough to slide the payment across the table, the performer tucked the cash into their jacket pocket, narrating the motion as “the exchange of value for a space that the state says you should not have, a small act of rebellion wrapped in a simple business transaction.” [668]
They left as the sun dipped below the turbine line, the shadow of the blades sweeping across the farmhouse porch behind them, already mentally planning their next stop, another off-the-books service for a family who’d lost a child to a turbine-related asthma attack the month before. [714]
They did not allow themselves to feel anything for the people they helped, not because they were cruel, but because the emotional void they carried was their greatest protection, keeping them from accidentally violating the mandate themselves, keeping them able to show up for the next person who needed a space to grieve without pretense. [769]
