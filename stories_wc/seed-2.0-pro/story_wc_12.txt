The loop pedal priest knelt on the cold basalt slab of the dimensional anchor, his posture loose and focused as he tuned the small device slung over his shoulder. [28]
The space around him hummed with muffled clarity, every sound softened by the thin veil between worlds but every truth sharp enough to cut if he reached for it. [57]
He had always decorated with silence in high-stakes spaces, letting gaps in sound soften the edges of anxious minds before he spoke. [79]
He had arrived with ten minutes left on the clock, enough time to work before the possession completes and the entity locks itself into the artist’s body for good. [107]
Beside the crumpled, semi-conscious artist rested an artist's palette with one color, a deep, inky indigo that glowed faintly where it touched the stone. [131]
The room was suspended in unraveled stillness, no dust motes moving, no breath fogging the air, time stretched thin between what was and what could be. [157]
His core motivation was to illuminate hidden assumptions the artist held, the false beliefs that had let the parasitic entity slip through the cracks of her self-perception. [184]
To do this, he would plumb the depths of her subconscious, pulling the unspoken fears to the surface where she could see them clearly. [208]
He worked through rhetorical devices, not spells, using layered questions and deliberate metaphors to lead her to her own realizations instead of forcing truth on her. [234]
He tapped the loop pedal once, laying down a soft, sustained tone that matched the low thrum of the dimensional anchor, then left the rest of the track empty to hold the silence he had curated. [269]
The artist’s eyes fluttered open, unfocused at first, then fixed on the indigo palette at her side, her fingers twitching as if she ached to reach for it. [299]
He did not greet her, did not offer empty comfort, instead asking his first question, slow and steady, “If that color could only paint one thing, what would it be?” [330]
She blinked, the entity’s low snarl echoing faintly in the back of her throat as she fought to form words, the first coherent thought she had managed in three days. [362]
“I would paint my own worth,” she said, her voice cracking, “but I don’t deserve to, not when everyone else’s work is so much brighter, so much more varied.” [392]
That was the hidden assumption he had come to find, the lie she had repeated to herself so often it had torn a hole in her aura large enough for the entity to slip through. [427]
He asked his next question, the words looping softly through the pedal’s speaker so she could hear them repeated, slow and unrushed, “Who told you worth requires more than one color?” [461]
Her breath caught, and she stared at the indigo palette, the glow of the ink seeming to brighten as she thought, for the first time, that the question might have a valid answer. [495]
The entity screamed, thrashing in her chest, trying to force her to lash out, to reject the question before it could unravel the hold it had built on her self-doubt. [528]
He leaned forward, keeping his voice even, letting the silence he had curated wrap around her to muffle the entity’s rage, making space for her own thoughts to rise. [561]
She reached out, her fingers wrapping around the palette, and said, clear and steady for the first time, “No one told me that. I told myself that, and it’s not true.” [594]
The unraveled stillness around them fractured for half a second, the entity’s form dissolving into thin air as the assumption that had fed it crumbled to nothing. [625]
The loop pedal priest tapped the device again, cutting the sustained tone, letting the normal sounds of the room seep back in as the artist collapsed back, breathing hard, free. [658]
She looked down at the indigo palette in her hand, then up at him, a small, tentative smile tugging at her lips as she realized she had never needed more than that one color to make something extraordinary. [701]
He nodded, gathering his pedal to leave, knowing his work was done; he had not fixed her, had not cast a spell, but had simply asked the right questions to help her see the truth she had hidden from herself all along. [746]
