After the first snowmelt, the fractal weaver picked her way down the moss-slick limestone ledges to the bronze grotto under starlight, her linen satchel slung heavy across one shoulder. [29]
The air held a shingled hush, each layer of stillness stacked soft as cedar shakes, muffling the distant gurgle of meltwater seeping through the mountain’s cracks. [55]
She traveled these remote places to collect the weights of different burdens, each unspoken grief, unfulfilled wish, and forgotten regret woven into tiny, repeating fractal threads she spun from the edge of her thumb. [89]
Half-buried in the grotto’s bronze-hued silt, she spotted the rusted rim of a kerosene lamp burner, its wick holder crusted with decades of old lamp oil and mineral deposits. [118]
She knew immediately what clung to it: the spirit of a trapper who’d gotten lost here a century prior, boundary-lessly trapped between the world of the living and the quiet after, his burden the weight of never returning to his wife and infant daughter back in the valley. [166]
The fractal weaver had freed dozens of such lingering spirits, and she knew she could release him only by orienting properly, aligning her body with the slant of starlight leaking through the grotto’s narrow ceiling vent so her shadow fell directly across the lamp burner’s rusted opening. [213]
As she held the position, she slipped into the whalebone reverie that always accompanied this work, her mind drifting through the trapper’s memories: the rough feel of his daughter’s knit cap, the sharp tang of his wife’s apple cider, the panic that had set in when the unexpected blizzard hit and blocked the grotto’s entrance. [268]
She began to spin her fractal threads, weaving them around the trapper’s grief, guiding it to calcify into a small, dense, iridescent bead no bigger than a pea, which settled into the hollow of the lamp burner’s wick holder. [307]
The shingled hush shifted for a heartbeat, softening further as the trapper’s spirit sighed and slipped away, no longer tied to the cold stone of the grotto or the regret that had anchored him there for so long. [345]
The weaver reached down, brushing silt off the lamp burner to pluck the bead free, tucking it into the small velvet pouch in her satchel where she kept all the collected burden weights, each labeled with a tiny scratch of charcoal marking the place and the name of the person it belonged to. [398]
She did not collect these weights to hoard pain, but to carry them to the ancient weave at the top of the highest mountain, where each burden would be stitched into the fabric of the world to feed the growth of wildflowers, the warmth of summer sunsets, and the soft comfort of lullabies sung to fussy infants. [455]
She sat for a while on a smooth bronze rock, letting the last echoes of the trapper’s memory fade, the shingled hush wrapping around her like a well-worn shawl as starlight shifted through the vent and painted small shifting patterns across the silt at her feet. [501]
She left the kerosene lamp burner half-buried where she found it, knowing it would hold the faint warmth of the trapper’s last moments of freedom, a small marker for any future traveler who might stumble into the grotto looking for shelter. [542]
When she stood to leave, her satchel felt only slightly heavier than it had when she arrived, though she knew the weight of the trapper’s burden was far greater than the tiny bead suggested, dense with a hundred years of longing and regret. [585]
She climbed back up the mossy ledges slowly, pausing once to look back at the grotto’s dark mouth, the shingled hush still clinging to her clothes even as the wind off the snowmelt streams brushed her cheeks. [622]
She had dozens more stops to make before she reached the ancient weave, dozens more burdens to collect, each one a small piece of the world’s soft, aching heart waiting to be turned into something gentle and bright for those who would come after. [666]
As she walked toward the next valley, the faint glow of pre-dawn light touched the edge of the horizon, soft and golden, a promise of the good the weight she carried would soon bring. [700]
