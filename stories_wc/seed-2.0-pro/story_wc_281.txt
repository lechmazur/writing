Ten minutes after the static shock fried the edge of her custom LED light wand, Jules leaned against the scuffed workbench of her light painting studio, twisting the small muscovite geode sliver she kept on her keychain between calloused fingers. [40]
As an affectionate skeptic, she’d never bought into her late grandmother’s claim the shard amplified unspoken thoughts, but she’d carried it every day since the woman’s funeral as a quiet, silly tribute. [72]
A year prior, she’d volunteered for a biotech trial testing a telepathy pill designed to let creative teams share unfiltered ideas, only to get a formal recall notice three months later warning of lingering, unregulated neural echoes. [109]
For months, those echoes had been fractally precise, tiny snippets of other trial participants’ half-formed dreams, fears, and half-baked creative concepts flashing behind her eyes when she least expected them, sharp enough to map but too disjointed to make sense of. [150]
Jules, who built her career helping marginalized emerging artists turn vague, unspoken ideas into tangible light art installations, had long wondered if those echoes held hidden pathways for collaborative projects no one had ever dared to voice. [187]
She’d spent weeks trying to devise a way to capture the echoes coherently, and had recently settled on working through somatic markers, pairing each flash of an echo with a specific physical sensation she could trigger on command to anchor the memory. [229]
The static shock, which had jumped from a frayed power cord to her wrist when she’d reached for the geode sliver mid-test of her marker system, had felt different, warmer, less sharp than a regular electrical jolt. [266]
When she twisted the sliver again, the silvery, flaking layers of muscovite caught the soft glow of her studio’s ambient strip lights, and she felt the familiar tingle in her left wrist that she’d assigned as her anchor marker for creative ideas. [308]
This time, the echoes didn’t flash in disjointed bits, but unfolded in a continuous, flowing stream: a teen painter who’d been scared to share her concept for a mural about foster care belonging, a disabled photographer who wanted to build a light exhibit that let visually impaired visitors feel light as texture, a poet who dreamed of turning spoken word into glowing, walkable lines through public parks. [375]
Jules fumbled for her notebook, scribbling every detail as fast as she could, her chest tight with that quiet willowglass hope she’d only felt a handful of times before, thin and delicate as blown glass but steady as the willow trees that grew outside her grandma’s old cottage. [423]
She didn’t waste time questioning if the geode had actually amplified the signals, or if the static shock had just recalibrated the lingering neural pathways from the recalled pill, because the ideas were real, the voices of other creators who’d been too scared to share their work were clear, and she had a way to bring them to life. [482]
Over the next three hours, she tested her system, pressing the muscovite geode sliver to her wrist before each run, letting the somatic markers guide the sweep of her light wand as the long-exposure camera mounted to the studio ceiling captured every stroke. [525]
When she pulled the first raw image up on her monitor, she gasped: the light trails weren’t just random swirls, they were a fractally precise map of interconnected public art installations, each location marked by a small glowing dot, each pathway between them lined with tiny, legible snippets of the creators’ ideas she’d pulled from the echoes. [582]
She set the geode sliver on her desk next to the monitor, grinning as she typed up an email to the list of trial participants she’d tracked down over the past months, inviting them to collaborate on the project that would illuminate hidden pathways for underheard creators across the city, turning a recalled experimental pill and a silly little crystal gift into something tangible, hopeful, and real.
