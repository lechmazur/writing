The emerald station’s vaulted glass walls glowed honeyed under the last sliver of fleeting golden sunset, streaks of moonrise already seeping through the gaps where the sky blushed indigo at the edges. [32]
Lila, the golden-hour portrait thief, perched on a weathered marble bench by the ticket booth, her canvas bag slung tight across her chest holding the chipped ceramic jar of captured moonbeams she’d stolen from a vintage apothecary the week prior. [72]
She was quietly savvy, having spent three years learning the codes in flower arrangements the station’s message runners used to pass notes to passengers without transit security catching on, no two bouquets ever reading the same even if they shared the same stems. [115]
Her work, stealing only the split-second, unposed portraits of strangers bathed in golden hour light, was never about profit; she was chasing the quiet, unspoken urge to press your ear against destiny’s door, to catch the split second where a life might pivot into something entirely unrecognizable. [162]
She called these split seconds illuminated moments, the ones most people let slip by without noticing: a teenager squeezing a college acceptance letter in their coat pocket, a grandparent spotting their grandchild for the first time in two years, a stranger locking eyes with someone across the platform who would become their spouse ten years later. [218]
Tonight, she’d come to the station to reinterpret the code in the bouquet tucked into the handle of the vintage rail car parked at the far end of Platform 7, a code she’d only ever seen referenced in old runner journals, one rumored to mark the arrival of a passenger carrying a life-altering secret for anyone brave enough to seek it. [279]
When she reached the rail car, the last of the sunset faded fast, and the emerald station by moonlight took on a soft, otherworldly glow, the jar of moonbeams in her bag thrumming so hard she fumbled to pull it out, unscrewing the lid to let the pale silver light spill over the bouquet of white clover, blue hydrangea, and dried wheat. [341]
The light hit the stems, and hidden markings she’d never noticed before glowed to life, recontextualizing the code she thought she knew: it wasn’t a marker of an arriving passenger, but an invitation, one that asked if she wanted to step inside the rail car to see every illuminated moment she’d ever captured, laid out like a tapestry of all the small, perfect fates she’d guarded over the years. [410]
She froze, her chest tight with luminous disbelief, because she’d spent so long chasing other people’s fates she’d never stopped to think her own might be waiting for her, the door to the rail car creaking open on its own as if welcoming her home. [455]
She held the jar of moonbeams out in front of her like a lantern, stepping over the threshold, and the car was lined with hundreds of tiny framed portraits, each of the illuminated moments she’d stolen over the years, their subjects smiling, crying, pausing mid-step exactly as she’d captured them. [505]
At the far end of the car, a small wooden table held a single blank canvas, a note tucked under its edge written in the same flower code she’d spent years learning, telling her she’d earned the right to capture her own illuminated moment next, to stop chasing other people’s destinies and step fully into her own. [562]
She laughed, soft and disbelieving, as she set the jar of moonbeams on the table, its light spilling across the blank canvas to reveal the faint outline of her own face, bathed in the same golden hour light she’d spent so long chasing in others. [607]
For the first time in her life, she didn’t feel like a thief stealing moments that didn’t belong to her, but a curator of the small, magical bits of life that make all the hard days worth living, her urge to press against destiny’s door finally sated because she’d already stepped through it. [660]
She pulled her camera from her bag, adjusted the settings for the soft mix of moonlight and golden hour afterglow seeping through the rail car window, and took the first photo of her entire career that she knew was fully, unapologetically hers. [702]
