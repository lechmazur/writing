Mara, the sage camera repairer who had tended the lunar observatory above the clouds for 47 years, felt the first low rumble of shifting bedrock the moment she set down her jeweler’s screwdriver. [33]
This was the timeframe when foundations shake, the early tremors of the controlled demolition scheduled to raze the decommissioned observatory before sundown, and she had refused the evacuation order to finish the project she had been planning for three years. [67]
Her motivation to harness the ephemeral had grown with every passing decade she’d spent here: every meteor trail that blazed and vanished in two seconds, every wisp of iridescent cloud that caught the moon’s glow for a minute before dissolving, every distant supernova’s faint light that touched the telescope lens just once, deserved to outlast the concrete and steel of the building that had framed them. [130]
Bolted to her workbench beside a half-repaired 1970s astrophotography camera, a vintage brass observatory clock bore one hour hand that won’t stop spinning, its whir a quiet, frantic counterpoint to the deep groan of the shifting walls around her. [170]
She had tried a dozen times to fix it, to reset its gears and align it to the rotation of the moon, but it had spun unceasingly from the day the demolition notice arrived, as if it could outrun the end of the only home it had ever known. [213]
The only thing that remained changelessly constant in the shaking building was the soft, dry thud of a leaf falling from the potted sugar maple she had carried up the mountain as a sapling when she first took the job, a leaf dropping exactly once every 17 minutes, as regular as the pulse of the stars she tracked. [268]
She had timed the fall for 30 years, marking each thud in a tattered leather notebook, and she knew it was the only timer she could trust now, when every digital clock in the observatory had flickered out and the spinning brass hand refused to hold a single second still. [317]
Her next task was to restructure the bank of 12 linked camera lenses she had mounted to the main telescope’s tracking arm, calibrating each to capture a different wavelength of light, so their combined exposure would hold every ephemeral moment she had ever loved about this place in a single, glowing frame. [372]
She had long ago learned to work by the sound of a leaf falling, tuning out every other noise to adjust each lens’s aperture and shutter speed in the exact split second the thud reached her ears, ensuring each lens would fire in perfect sync even as the floor beneath her boots trembled. [426]
The core concept guiding her work was escape velocity: she knew the digital file the cameras would produce needed enough weight, enough quiet magic, to outrun the demolition’s blast, to not be erased with the rest of the observatory’s equipment, to break free of the pull of loss that would make every memory of this place fade for everyone but her. [485]
She ran a calloused finger over the spinning clock hand as she waited for the next leaf to fall, a luminous ache settling in her chest, soft and bright as the moonlit clouds she had spent half her life watching, equal parts grief for the home she was losing and joy that the moments she cherished would not vanish with it. [543]
The soft thud came right on schedule, and she pressed the master shutter button the same instant, the 12 lenses clicking in perfect unison as a larger tremor shook the workbench, sending a stack of old photo negatives skittering to the floor. [587]
She stuffed the memory card holding the exposure into the inner pocket of her flannel shirt, slung her worn repair bag over her shoulder, and headed for the exit just as the first crash of the demolition crane’s wrecking ball hit the outer dome. [631]
The spinning clock hand whirred behind her as she stepped out into the cold mountain air, and she did not look back, knowing the frame she had captured held the entire soul of the observatory, bright and alive, long after the concrete and steel turned to dust beneath the clouds. [684]
