Jesse, an ex-band roadie turned antiques appraiser, sank onto the scuffed vinyl bench of the abandoned quarry office trailer, still reeling from the public failure of his high-profile guest spot on a national antiques show that morning. [37]
He had misidentified a 1965 Fender amp as a replica, an error caught live on air, and he had fled the studio before the segment’s final applause had even faded, driving 45 minutes out of the city to the only spot that ever felt quiet when his head spun too fast. [88]
This quarry, with its steep stone walls that turned every shout into soft, lingering echo valley longing, had hosted his old band’s free outdoor shows every summer back when he was 20, hauling gear and taping down cables for the lead singer, Lila, who had been his best friend since middle school. [140]
He pulled a frayed speaker grille cloth scrap from the pocket of his flannel shirt, the last remaining piece of Lila’s favorite amp, destroyed in the car crash that had killed her six years prior and pushed him to leave the roadie life behind entirely. [185]
For years, he had coped with his grief by insulating emotionally with facts, memorizing production dates and serial numbers for vintage music gear instead of talking about Lila, treating every appraisal like a problem to solve rather than a chance to connect with the stories behind the objects. [233]
His public failure that morning had happened because he had overlooked the hand-drawn star Lila had scribbled on the amp’s interior panel, too focused on checking serial numbers to recognize the very marker she had stolen from his backpack at their first ever show. [277]
He sat for a long time twisting the scrap of grille cloth between his fingers, listening to the soft hush of wind bouncing off the quarry walls, the ghost of every cheer and encore they had ever earned humming in the gaps between gusts, a quiet reminder of life after the applause had died down. [332]
His motivation, for so long narrowed to just getting through each day without breaking down, now shifted to the far softer, scarier goal: to discover purpose after loss, to stop hiding from the stories behind the gear he spent all day evaluating. [374]
He was confidently uncertain about what came next, sure only that he could not go back to treating every object like a collection of data points, that he had to wrest the good parts of his old life and his new life together instead of keeping them locked in separate boxes. [425]
He pulled out his phone and called the show’s producer, apologizing for the early exit and offering to come back for a follow-up segment, where he would talk not just about the amp’s market value, but about the girl who had drawn that star, about the shows they had played in this very quarry, about the way objects hold pieces of people long after they are gone. [492]
When the producer agreed, he tucked the grille cloth scrap into his wallet, next to the crumpled photo of Lila mid-song that he had hidden there for years, and stepped out of the trailer to stare up at the quarry’s high stone edges. [535]
He yelled Lila’s name, and it bounced off the rock faces three times before fading, soft and warm, like she was cheering him on from somewhere just out of sight, her voice tangled in the wind that carried the faint, distant echo of a crowd’s cheer. [581]
He knew the work ahead would be messy, that he would mess up again, that letting himself feel the grief he had hidden for so long would hurt far more than memorizing serial numbers ever could, but for the first time in six years, he was looking forward to it anyway. [632]
