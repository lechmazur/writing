The topaz workshop glowed amber under the fraying constellations, its windows fogged with ink fumes and the quiet heat of a cast-iron stove, as the first of the season’s vanishing stars winked out overhead. [34]
Its sole occupant, the cryptic letterpress operator locals only called Moth, ran a calloused thumb over the pitted, iridescent halo fragment he’d fished out of a dried-up creek bed three weeks prior. [66]
Moth habitually borrows personalities from everyone he meets, tucking small snippets of their tics, speech patterns, and quiet griefs into the margins of his leather notebook, all part of his quiet quest to understand the mathematics of emotion, the unquantifiable weight of joy and sorrow that refuses to fit into any standard equation. [119]
The fragment hummed when he held it, a soft vibration that triggered transposed empathy, letting him feel the half-remembered joys and sharp regrets of every being that had ever touched the broken relic, no matter how many centuries had passed since the halo had split in a battle no living soul remembered. [171]
He’d spent the past three nights between vanishing stars printing broadsides layered with deliberate ambiguity, half-formed phrases and shifting symbols that were designed to disorient anyone who read them, forcing their minds to fill in the gaps with their own deepest fears and most guarded hopes. [217]
Tonight, he was testing the system on himself, pressing the halo fragment into the ink plate before running the card stock through the press, so each broadside carried the faint, iridescent glint of the relic’s surface, amplifying the transposed empathy he already felt when he held the fragment. [265]
The first wave hit him as he pulled the first broadside free, the borrowed personality of a 12th century nun who’d held the halo fragment for 40 years in a mountain convent washing over him, her quiet devotion to her community mixing with his own hunger to quantify emotion. [314]
The half-printed phrase “the weight of what you carry” shifted before his eyes, first reading “the weight of what you left” then “the weight of what you lost”, the deliberate ambiguity working to disorient him even further, blurring the line between his own memories and the nun’s. [361]
He scribbled numbers in his notebook, mapping the intensity of the nun’s grief at losing her sister to the rate of star fade overhead, trying to pin a numerical value to the sharp, hot ache he felt in his chest that was not his own, a perfect example of transposed empathy made tangible. [414]
A second personality washed over him next, that of a young soldier who’d stolen the fragment from a battlefield shrine, his overwhelming guilt at surviving a fight that killed all his friends mixing with the nun’s quiet devotion, leaving Moth reeling with the lucid turbulence of two conflicting emotional currents warring for space in his head. [470]
He kept scribbling, noting that the strength of the transposed empathy correlated directly to the number of words on the broadside the reader’s brain filled in for themselves, the deliberate ambiguity acting as an amplifier for unexpressed emotion that he could measure against the dimming of the stars above the topaz workshop. [523]
When the second vanishing star winked out overhead, marking the end of his allotted work window, he had half a page of equations that linked the intensity of borrowed emotion, the degree of disorientation from the ambiguous text, and the speed of stellar decay into a rough, working formula for the mathematics of emotion. [577]
He set the halo fragment on his workbench, letting the borrowed personalities fade back into the edges of his mind, the lucid turbulence of the past few hours softening into quiet satisfaction as he traced the edge of his formula, his quest no longer a vague, unanswerable question. [625]
He slipped the finished broadside into his notebook next to the formula, already anticipating the next stretch between vanishing stars, when he could refine his work further and unlock the last hidden variables of the heart. [661]
