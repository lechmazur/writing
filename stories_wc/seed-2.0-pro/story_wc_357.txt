Kaelin, the intrepid orchard pilot, adjusted her glider’s rudder as the ground beneath her fruit groves began to drift upward, the start of the single hour when gravity inverts for an hour on her home, an orchard planet riddled with floating rock arches draped in bioluminescent apple blossom. [48]
The usual hum of pollinator drones cut out entirely, leaving only the whirling silence of loose soil and stray petals spinning past her cockpit, no sound carrying through the odd, weightless air. [80]
For three seasons, her groves had survived blights and frost events that should have wiped out 90 percent of the crop, and she’d made it her mission to find the source of resilience that kept her trees thriving when every agricultural model predicted failure. [124]
She’d mapped the hidden subsurface streams of the planet years prior, so she flew her glider slowly, navigating by water flow that rose in thin, glinting ribbons toward the upper atmosphere during inversion, the streams tracing paths between the floating rock arches no scanner could pick up. [171]
Thirty minutes into the inversion, she reached a cluster of arches where the water ribbons converged, and she realized she had stumbled on a gathering of echoes, faint, overlapping recordings of the planet’s first inhabitants that clung to the porous rock during gravity shifts. [215]
She anchored her glider to a narrow rock ledge and drifted toward the center of the convergence, her gloved hand brushing the rock face to discover a small, jagged shard half-buried in moss. [248]
She pulled it free, turning it over in her hand to recognize it as a broken arrow tip, carved from the planet’s iridescent core stone, its edge worn smooth from centuries of exposure to the shifting gravity and flowing water. [288]
The echoes swirled around her as she held the tip, and she remembered the old oral histories that said the first inhabitants’ sacred tools held a unique attribute: it accelerates plant growth, and radiates a protective energy that shields crops from extreme weather and disease. [333]
The faint echoes sharpened for a moment, carrying the voice of a young farmer who had hidden the tip here during a catastrophic inversion thousands of years prior, hoping it would spread its benefit across the entire planet’s orchard systems over time. [375]
She held the tip up to a small, wilting apple sapling that had drifted into the arch cluster, and within ten seconds, the sapling sprouted new leaves and tiny, unripe fruit, confirming the old histories were not just myth. [414]
She looked back at the ribbons of water flowing past the tip, realizing the inversion carried trace particles of the core stone through the streams down to every grove on the planet, that the resilience she’d observed had seeped into every root system over centuries. [459]
She considered taking the tip back to her grove to amplify its effects, but the echoes softened, reminding her that the tip’s power worked best when it was shared, when it could feed every farmer’s crops, not just her own. [499]
Fifteen minutes remained before the inversion ended, so she carefully tucked the arrow tip back into the moss where she’d found it, leaving a small marker carved into the rock so she could return during future inversions to study it further. [540]
She climbed back into her glider and began to follow the water ribbons back down toward her grove, watching as small particles of core stone drifted past her cockpit, falling toward the soil below as gravity began to shift back to its normal direction. [584]
By the time she touched down on solid ground, the whirling silence had broken into the familiar rustle of leaves and buzz of pollinators, and she could already see faint new growth on the apple trees at the edge of her grove, bright green against the darker, older foliage. [633]
That night, she gathered the other orchard pilots from across the planet to share what she had found, explaining that their shared fortune was not random luck, but a gift from the people who had tended the land long before them. [674]
They made a pact to protect the hidden arch cluster and the broken arrow tip, ensuring that the planet’s gift of resilience would continue to feed every generation for thousands of years to come. [708]
