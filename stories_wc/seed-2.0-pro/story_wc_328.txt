Elara, a cryptid researcher working undercover as a seasonal herbalist’s assistant, knelt in loam scented with wild mint and moonwort, brushing frost off the edge of a raised stone planter. [30]
It had been three months after the missing return changed, when 17 hikers who’d vanished in the adjacent national forest wandered back to town speaking of singing stone circles and gravity that let them step between pine boughs 30 feet off the ground. [73]
She’d taken the undercover posting not to study the hikers, but to turn bitterness into a cosmic joke, after the same anomaly had taken her older sister five years prior, leaving no trace until the recent string of returns. [112]
The herbalist had left her with a strange gift two days prior: a chipped flint arrowhead, carved with swirling runes that seemed to glow faintly when the garden’s bioluminescent moss touched it. [144]
She’d already noticed the garden was gravitationally playful, with clumps of dandelion fluff drifting in lazy loops that defied normal physics, and tiny pebbles bouncing three feet off the ground before settling back into the loam. [180]
Tucked in the corner of the garden, half-buried under a patch of chamomile, was the dented old cordless phone the herbalist had told her to pick up when it rang, saying it carried messages from the space the missing hikers had stumbled into. [223]
It rang right as she set the arrowhead down on the planter ledge, its speaker fizzing with static that made the bioluminescent moss flicker brighter. [248]
She picked it up, and heard her sister’s voice, crackling through the static, pouring out static-laden confessions of grief, of regret that she’d left Elara alone, of the beautiful, wild world she’d found on the other side of the rift. [288]
Her sister rambled about a harmony untamed, a rhythm that governed the rift world, where nothing was caged, no grief was permanent, and time stretched like soft taffy to let lost souls heal. [321]
The herbalist had warned her that if she held the arrowhead while listening to those confessions, the energy of the call would let her shift the rift’s rules, if her intention was clear. [354]
She curled her fingers around the flint arrowhead, closed her eyes, and let her own bitterness rise to meet her sister’s regret, focusing on her wish to turn her years of cold anger into something light, something that would let both of them heal, and watched the world around her begin to metamorphose via the static-laden confessions on an old cordless phone. [416]
The frost coating the planters turned to tiny glittering butterflies, the floating pebbles shifted into glowing wildflowers that drifted in the playful gravity, and a thin, glowing rift opened in the middle of the garden, framed by climbing ivy dotted with star-shaped white blooms. [460]
Her sister stepped through, wrapped in a jacket dotted with pine needles, her smile the same as Elara remembered, no trace of the cold distance that had marked all the other returned hikers. [493]
Elara laughed, a rough, rusty sound, because her joke had landed: the universe that had stolen her sister just to study human grief had been forced to give her back, because Elara had turned her own pain into a wish so bright it couldn’t be ignored. [539]
She didn’t need to write a paper about the rift, or warn the town about its dangers, because she’d gotten what she’d really come for, turning the years of bitter, lonely grief into a punchline the universe had no choice but to laugh along with. [584]
As her sister pulled her into a hug, the cold of the underground garden seeped into Elara’s coat, but the warmth of her sister’s chest against hers carried that frostbitten hope she’d carried quietly for five years, no longer just a distant dream, but something real she could hold. [633]
The flint arrowhead in her palm had gone warm, its runes glowing the same soft blue as the rift behind her sister, a permanent link between the world she’d always known and the wild, harmonious space her sister had called home for half a decade. [678]
They stayed in the garden for hours, trading stories of the years they’d spent apart, while the floating wildflowers drifted around them and the old cordless phone lay silent on the chamomile patch, its work done. [714]
