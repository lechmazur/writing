The luminous skeptic perched on the edge of lavender bluff under swirling green and violet aurora, the hem of her threadbare 2190s hiking dress catching on wild sprigs of silvery lavender that smelled of old sun and distant rain. [39]
It was the quiet window while forests hold elections, when mycorrhizal networks tally votes for next season’s canopy density and storm routing, and human settlements slow to a crawl to avoid disrupting the count. [73]
She had traveled three continents to be here, driven by a decades-old itch to learn why some people carry weather with them, their pockets full of unseasonable frost or sudden warm breezes that follow their steps long after they leave a room. [115]
Twisted around her left index finger was a dented tin compass ring, a gift from her grandmother who had been one of the first weather-carriers documented after the atmospheric reconfiguration of 2172. [147]
She had trained for two years to rely on sensing through taste changes to track weather-carrier energy, her tongue tuned to pick up the sharp tang of thunder, the sweet cream of high summer, the metallic bite of snow on air even when no storm was in sight. [195]
What most researchers dismissed as a broken trinket was actually an emergent compass, its needle not pointing north but toward nodes of terrestrially cosmic energy, where the pull of distant stars tangled with the magnetic hum of the planet’s crust to open small, temporary pockets of atmospheric flexibility. [243]
She had watched the needle spin wildly for the last twenty minutes, a clear sign that dozens of weather-carriers were beginning to converge on the bluff, their individual atmospheric signatures weaving together into something thicker and more powerful than any single carrier could summon alone. [288]
The back of her tongue flooded with the bright, citrusy tang of spring rain mixed with the warm, nutty taste of autumn bonfire smoke, confirming the mix of carriers gathering just out of her line of sight, their energies buzzing soft enough not to disrupt the forest election underway below the bluff. [340]
She had grown up listening to her grandmother’s stories of gathering on this very bluff when she was young, before weather-carriers were regulated and forced to register their abilities with the global atmospheric council, and the nostalgia wrapped around her chest like an old wool shawl, sharp and soft all at once. [392]
When the first carrier stepped into view, she was not surprised to see they wore matching tin compass rings, each tuned to the same emergent energy that had guided her here, their faces soft with the same quiet hope she had seen in old holos of her grandmother’s cohort. [441]
The group’s spokesperson, a woman with snowflakes tangled in her curly silver hair, explained that weather-carriers did not create the weather they carried, but acted as conduits for the terrestrially cosmic energy that pooled in places like this bluff, tapping into the planet’s memory of past seasons and the stars’ predictions of storms to come. [496]
They gathered every year while the forests held elections to converge their energy, aligning with the mycorrhizal network’s vote to route storms away from vulnerable coastal communities and send rain to drought-stricken farmlands, no council permission required. [533]
The luminous skeptic, who had spent years dismissing weather-carrier abilities as a collective delusion, felt her skepticism melt like frost on a warm windowsill as her tongue flooded with the soft, sweet taste of jasmine, the signature of her grandmother’s own weather signature, left embedded in the bluff’s soil decades earlier. [584]
She twisted her tin compass ring, watching the needle settle dead center toward the gathered group, and realized the emergent compass had never been leading her to a place, but to a community of people who carried the weather not for power, but to care for the planet that cared for them. [636]
She stepped forward to join the circle, the aurora swirling brighter above as the group’s energies merged, and for the first time in her life, she could taste the future on her tongue: soft, warm, and stitched through with the old, familiar love of the grandmother she had missed for so long. [688]
