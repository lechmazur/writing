The hush before the county’s century-old apple orchard is cut down hangs thick enough to press into the crevices of the downtown sheet music archive, where Lila, the former gossip-column exorcist, hunts for a tattered 1927 copy of *Orchard Lullaby*. [40]
She quit writing the snarky anonymous exorcism column three years prior, after she mocked a small-town widow’s claim that her dead husband’s ghost lingered in a vintage perfume bottle, not knowing the widow was her estranged older sister, Mae, who’d left home after their parents’ car crash when Lila was 16. [91]
Her signature, both in the column and in her actual exorcism work, was that she never gave direct advice, always framing guidance through metaphors drawn from the natural world—rot that feeds new growth, bees that only sting when they mistake you for a threat, frost that preserves what it doesn’t kill. [142]
She’d long believed that exorcisms were just imperfect miracles—they never erased grief, never undid hurt, only gave lingering spirits and the people who loved them enough space to say what they’d left unsaid. [175]
The sheet music archive’s old copper wiring was ideal for her current work, because she could run small, targeted induced currents through the pages of handwritten scores to amplify faint ghostly signals, no loud rituals or expensive tools required. [214]
Mae had died of a sudden stroke six months earlier, leaving Lila the chipped rose-gold antique perfume bottle she’d written the column about, alongside a note that only said *you always thought ghosts were jokes, until they’re yours*. [252]
She found the *Orchard Lullaby* score tucked between two 1950s jazz manuscripts 20 minutes later, its edges stained with apple juice, the handwritten dedication scrawled in Mae’s 12-year-old handwriting: For Lila, when she has bad dreams. [288]
She hooked the wiring she’d brought to the archive’s exposed junction box, laid the perfume bottle on the center of the sheet music, and adjusted the dial to send low, thrumming induced currents through the paper, the hum matching the quiet vibration of the orchard’s soon-to-be-felled trees just two blocks away. [339]
The air grew thick with the sharp, sweet scent of apple blossom and old vanilla, and Mae’s ghost flickered into view, wearing the frayed flannel she’d had the last time they saw each other, her hands calloused from decades of tending the orchard they’d grown up in. [386]
Lila’s throat tightened, and she spoke in the metaphor-heavy cadence she’d used for years, no snark left, only raw regret: “I was the kid who shook the apple tree too hard in autumn, knocking down all the unripe fruit before it had a chance to sweeten, and I never got to say I was sorry I laughed at the part of you that still held onto the soft stuff.” [455]
Mae’s ghost smiled, and the currents thrumming through the sheet music picked up, making the notes flutter like tiny wings, as she signed back—she’d lost her voice to throat cancer two years before Lila wrote that cruel column, a detail Lila had never bothered to learn—using the same orchard metaphors they’d grown up with: “The unripe fruit makes the best cider, anyway.” [517]
It was an imperfect miracle, the kind Lila had spent her whole career facilitating for strangers: Mae didn’t come back to life, the orchard was still going to be cleared at dawn for a strip mall, and the rift between them hadn’t vanished entirely, the old hurt still sharp at the edges. [569]
But for 12 quiet minutes, they were sisters again, flipping through the rest of the archive’s orchard-themed scores, Mae’s ghost tapping the notes in time to a tune only they remembered, the perfume bottle on the table glowing soft gold with the current running under it. [615]
When the first distant rumble of the logging trucks rolled through the open archive window, Mae’s ghost faded, leaving only the faint scent of apple blossom on the sheet music and a single dried apple seed tucked in the margin of *Orchard Lullaby*. [658]
Lila tucked the seed into her jacket pocket, wrapped the perfume bottle in the folded sheet music, and walked out into the pre-dawn dark, already planning where she’d plant the seed far from the construction zone, a small, unperfect, perfect thing to carry the apology they’d both waited so long for. [709]
