The first breath Lira drew after the interregnum ban lifted tasted of frost and wild clover, even tucked inside the sun-dappled cobalt atrium perched on the edge of the ice-locked northern lake. [32]
Known across the northern territories as a cloud-shadow poet, she was unapologetically aesthetically driven, her life’s work rooted in capturing the quiet, fleeting magic of light and shadow that most people passed by without notice. [74]
For ten long years, the temple had banned all civilian access to the land beyond the atrium, framing the stretch of shore holding the humming quartz colonnade as a sacred space reserved only for high priests and their arcane rituals. [117]
Lira had watched the colonnade glint through the atrium’s frosted glass every winter of the ban, writing verses about its faint, thrumming glow while nurturing a quiet, fierce motivation: to stride through boundaries once thought sacred, and return the colonnade’s beauty to the people who lived in its shadow. [174]
The air in the atrium held a lantern-moth hush, no other visitors had arrived yet for the first public opening, leaving only the soft whistle of wind off the ice and the distant, low thrumming of the quartz to fill the space. [217]
She slipped her worn leather notebook out of her coat pocket, her boots making no sound on the atrium’s polished cobalt tile as she walked toward the wide glass doors that opened onto the frozen shore. [256]
When she pushed the door open, the cold bit at her cheeks, but she barely noticed, because a tremor of certainty hummed up through her soles, matching the frequency of the colonnade’s song and telling her she was exactly where she was meant to be. [305]
The colonnade stretched 20 feet along the shore, each quartz column pocked with tiny fossilized shells and crisscrossed with gold veins that caught the weak winter sun, thrumming so loud now she could feel it in her teeth. [346]
Old sacred verses were carved deep into each column, written in the archaic temple script that had been forbidden for civilians to learn for three generations, designed to keep the colonnade’s wisdom locked away from anyone outside the priestly class. [393]
Lira’s plan was to paraphrase every line of those verses, translating the dense, formal religious language into soft, accessible verse that any child or farmer or traveler could understand, carrying pieces of the colonnade’s magic with them wherever they went. [444]
She had spent years studying the forbidden script in secret, and she knew her method through dissolving boundaries between the sacred and the ordinary, the elite and the public, the written word and the natural world would be condemned by the remaining old guard priests, but she did not care. [499]
She knelt at the base of the first column, running a gloved finger over the first carved line, the hum of the quartz vibrating up her arm and settling warm in her chest as she scribbled the first line of her translation in her notebook. [545]
The first verse had previously been translated by the temple as a command to prostrate oneself before the power of the gods, but Lira reworked it into an observation of the way cloud shadows drift across the frozen lake, a reminder that beauty was not something to be feared, but something to be held gently. [603]
A faint breeze stirred the edge of her coat, and she looked up to see a small group of local children creeping toward the colonnade from the edge of the tree line, their eyes wide with wonder at the glowing quartz they had only ever heard stories about. [650]
She waved them over, patting the stone next to her, and when they settled close, their small hands pressed to the humming quartz, she read them the line she had just written, grinning when one of the smallest girls gasped and said it sounded like the lake was singing to them. [704]
Lira knew that over the coming weeks and months, hundreds of people would pass through the atrium to visit the colonnade, and many would bring their own interpretations of the verses, weaving the quartz’s hum into their own stories and poems and daily lives. [753]
The old sacred boundaries were already dissolving, one line of verse, one curious child, one quiet, unapologetic act of beauty at a time, and the colonnade’s song had never sounded brighter. [789]
