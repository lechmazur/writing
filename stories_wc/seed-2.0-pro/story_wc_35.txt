The thin crust of post-rain dew crunched under Lira’s scuffed leather boots as she stepped from the desert fortress’s stone arch, the last wisps of an unspoken dream clinging to the edge of her vision like melted sugar. [38]
As a chef gifted with the ability to taste memories in every ingredient she handled, she’d spent three years at this remote outpost chasing the only flavor she couldn’t seem to soften: the acrid tang of her own unaddressed remorse, sharp as burnt caramel. [82]
Her quiet motivation, shared with no one, was to sandpaper those rough memories down until they no longer scraped the back of her throat every time she closed her eyes, until she could cook without tasting the argument that had sent her sister running into a storm she never came back from. [134]
Last night’s rare desert rain had left dozens of shallow tidal pools dotted across the compacted sand between the fortress walls and the dune line, and she’d long ago learned that analyzing tidal pool formations was the only way to locate the hidden entrance to the structure the local nomads spoke of in hushed tones. [189]
That structure was the coral icebound pavilion beneath the dunes, a vault carved from fossilized sea coral and lined with permafrost that had somehow survived millennia in the desert heat, built to hold the stored remorse of every person who’d ever stumbled across its threshold. [234]
She’d been warned that the pavilion’s stored remorse was emotionally contagious through touch, that even brushing a finger against its coral walls could flood a person with centuries of unprocessed grief, but the sharp burn of her own regret had long since overridden any fear of what she might find there. [285]
The tidal pools’ swirling salt patterns pointed to a narrow crack half-buried at the base of the tallest dune, and she crawled through it on her hands and knees until the sand gave way to smooth, cold coral stone, the air sharp with the smell of old salt and frost. [335]
The pavilion stretched out before her, its arching coral columns pocked with tiny frost bubbles that glinted in the faint blue light seeping through the sand above, its whole energy sturdy brittle, as if a single too-loud word could shatter the frozen quiet into a thousand shards of unspoken pain. [385]
She stepped forward and pressed her palm flat to the nearest column, letting the cold seep into her bones as she willed the pavilion to subsume her own remorse, to tuck it away with all the other griefs that had outstayed their welcome in the hearts of those who’d come before her. [437]
A flood of flavors hit her tongue immediately: burnt bread from a baker who’d missed his daughter’s birthday to finish a wedding order, salted fish from a sailor who’d abandoned his crew mid-voyage, burnt honey from a poet who’d lied to the only person who’d ever loved his work, all tangled with the sharp acrid tang of her own regret. [497]
For a long minute, she sobbed, the weight of every one of those stored griefs pressing against her chest, but she didn’t pull away, because she knew that to sandpaper her own rough memories, she had to first let them be seen, even by a vault of frozen coral and forgotten pain. [549]
Slowly, the acrid taste of her own remorse pulled away from her tongue, leaving only the faint briny sweetness of the coral behind, as the pavilion absorbed the last of the guilt she’d carried for ten long years. [587]
She pulled her hand back from the column, surprised to find no frost burn, no lingering weight on her chest, only a quiet lightness she hadn’t felt since she was a child, chasing her sister through the sand behind their family’s restaurant. [629]
When she crawled back through the narrow crack to the desert surface, the sun was peeking over the horizon, the last traces of her unspoken dream dissolving with the fading dew on the sand, the tidal pools already shrinking under the growing heat. [672]
She walked back to the fortress’s small kitchen, pulled out a jar of wild honey and a handful of dried desert berries, and started mixing a tart, sweet jam, tasting only sun and sand and the soft memory of her sister laughing, no trace of the old guilt left behind. [722]
She knew she would never tell anyone about the coral pavilion, or the way it had taken her remorse and held it close, but she would carry that lightness with her for the rest of her days, cooking flavors that felt like healing for anyone who ate her food. [771]
