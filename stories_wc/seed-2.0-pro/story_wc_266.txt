The frayed leather leash Lila had tucked in her jacket pocket for speech day luck snapped clean in two when she caught it on a bramble, ten steps from the main road to her high school. [36]
She’d carried the leash for three years, ever since her grandma, a former ferry captain, had passed, and its break felt like a sign she was destined to freeze up mid-speech like she always did. [67]
Instead of turning toward the school, she followed the faint, mossy path cutting uphill, letting the soft hush of the distant shore wrap around her like the lullabies her grandma used to sing while mending nets. [104]
Ten minutes of climbing brought her to the veiled ridgeway, a stretch of grassy bluff she’d never seen before, wreathed in thin, silver fog that blotted out the town below and glowed pink with the first hints of twilight. [147]
Two figures stood at the bluff’s edge, wrapped in navy wool coats stitched with constellations, and she realized they were the wise ferry guides her grandma had told her about, the ones who tended the crossings between the ordinary world and the places people rarely dared to look. [199]
When she approached, the taller of the two smiled, holding up a dented brass ticket punch with star stamp at its tip, the same one her grandma had kept on her lanyard for as long as Lila could remember. [239]
They explained that anyone who came to the ridgeway after a small, meaningful break in their routine was welcome to join the vigil in twilight, a quiet stretch of waiting where people could seek the parts of themselves they’d left unclaimed. [283]
Lila told them she’d spent years holding back, letting her fear of being seen keep her from speaking up for the things she cared about, and that she’d come to find where unused courage waits. [323]
The guides nodded, their manner gently persistent as they told her she would need to traverse the winding paths stretching out behind the bluff, and that she would have to take the way through indirect routes, no straight paths allowed, to earn what she sought. [373]
She set off immediately, stepping past clumps of sea thrift and wild blackberry bushes, following the faint glow of bioluminescent moss that lined the sides of the curving, looping trails. [412]
Each turn brought her a small, soft memory: her grandma teaching her to skip stones, the first time she’d volunteered at the local animal shelter, the quiet cheer of her best friend when she’d first mentioned writing the speech about protecting the town’s shoreline. [461]
She stopped once, when the path seemed to lead nowhere, and thought about turning back, but the faint sound of a lullaby drifting on the wind reminded her how much she wanted to stand in front of her classmates and say the words she’d written, loud and clear. [511]
When she rounded the last curve, she found herself back at the bluff’s edge, where the two guides waited, the twilight now deep enough that the first stars were pricking through the fog. [549]
The taller guide pressed the ticket punch to the palm of her hand, leaving a tiny, glowing star indent, and told her the courage she’d been looking for had been with her the whole time, stitched into the memories she’d revisited along the way. [595]
She tucked the two broken ends of the leash into her pocket, the star on her palm warm even through the fabric, and turned to walk back down the path toward the school, the sound of the shoreline lullaby still humming in her ears. [640]
By the time she stepped into the auditorium, her hands were steady, and when she walked up to the microphone, she didn’t fumble her notes, didn’t pause, didn’t let her voice shake for even a single word. [682]
When the audience erupted in cheers at the end of her speech, she touched the star mark on her palm, and smiled, knowing she’d found more than courage that day—she’d found a way to carry her grandma with her, every time she spoke up for what mattered. [730]
