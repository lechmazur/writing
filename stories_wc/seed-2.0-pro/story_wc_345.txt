The clementine orchards of Epsilon-9 stretched between honey-gold floating rock arches, their branches heavy with fruit for the first time since the ragtag crew of crashed freighter pilots had stopped being strangers and become family. [35]
No one knew the orchards’ secrets better than Lira, the altruistic ghost of the first settler who’d died trying to shield a sapling from a meteor shower three cycles prior. [65]
She carried only one object from her living days: a dented copper butterfly garden watering bell, etched with tiny monarch wings that glowed faintly when she passed through sunbeams filtering through the arches. [98]
The crew had never seen Lira, but they’d long found her strangely trustworthy, for her interventions always left their harvests larger, their leaky shelters patched, and their sick bay stocked with foraged medicinal herbs. [132]
Lira’s core motivation was to orchestrate collective effort to prepare the settlement for the incoming nebula of respite, a soft, iridescent cloud of cosmic dust that would bring three cycles of mild, crop-damaging radiation if they didn’t cover their orchards in time. [174]
She couldn’t speak to them directly, so she shared her warnings through messages chalked onto deserted sidewalks at dawn, her ghostly grip on the chalk leaving faint, glowing white script that faded by midday so passing scavenger ships wouldn’t spot the settlement’s location. [217]
The first message appeared exactly 60 cycles before the nebula was due, scrawled beneath a water spigot: “COVER ORCHARDS. USE ARCH FIBER.” [243]
The crew’s first reaction was skepticism, until Kai, their lead botanist, realized the silvery, web-like fiber growing on the undersides of the floating rock arches was exactly radiation-resistant enough to shield the clementine trees. [277]
Still, they hesitated to divert labor from their harvest, which was their only source of trade for medicine and spare parts for their broken comms array. [303]
Lira doubled down, leaving small gifts alongside her chalk messages: ripe clementines tucked beside notes listing fiber-harvesting routes, her copper watering bell left on the porch of the settlement’s communal kitchen each night to signal her presence. [340]
When the third message appeared, reading “HARVEST WILL ROT IF NEBULA HITS. WORK TOGETHER, NO ONE GOES HUNGRY,” the crew agreed to reexamine their seasonal work plans to make space for the fiber harvest. [374]
It was messy, tangled work, the kind that led to scraped knuckles, arguments over shift schedules, and nights spent huddled around the communal fire complaining about the stiff, scratchy fiber sticking to their clothes. [408]
This was brambled hope, after all: the kind that didn’t come easy, that grew thorns alongside its blooms, that required every member of their found family to show up even when they were tired. [442]
They finished hanging the fiber nets over every last orchard row exactly one day before the nebula of respite rolled over the planet, painting the sky in swathes of lavender and pale gold as it settled in for its three-cycle stay. [483]
The first week under the nebula was softer than anyone expected: the light was dim, the air smelled like rain and crushed clementine peel, and the nets held fast, blocking every bit of harmful radiation from reaching the trees. [522]
Lira left one final message on the sidewalk outside the communal kitchen at dawn on the first day of the second cycle, next to her glowing copper watering bell: “YOU DID GOOD. THE HARVEST WILL LAST.” [558]
The crew left an offering of fresh-baked clementine tarts beside the bell that evening, their small way of saying thank you to the ghost who’d become the quiet, unspoken member of their found family. [592]
Over the next three cycles, they tended the orchards carefully, sharing their harvest with passing wanderers who joined their settlement, expanding their family one tired, grateful stranger at a time. [622]
When the nebula finally lifted, the first thing they saw was a flock of bioluminescent blue butterflies, the same kind Lira had raised in her butterfly garden when she was alive, drifting between the floating rock arches. [659]
They knew, in that moment, that their brambled hope had been well placed, that the ghost who’d guided them was still watching, and that their small, growing family would thrive on the orchard planet for generations to come. [697]
