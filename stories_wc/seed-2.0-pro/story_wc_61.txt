The otherworldly sentimentalist glided through the nebula garden, their form woven from condensed star mist that cast faint, cool luminescence across every drifting bed of iridescent petals. [27]
Their core motivation, honed over a dozen lifetimes of inherited purpose, was to share light without using fire, for even the smallest plasma burst would incinerate the fragile, ionized blooms that made the garden a sanctuary for lost star travelers. [59]
They had embarked on their transit of the garden’s outer beds at the start of the annual bloom cycle, their journey unfolding across the quiet sigh of falling petals that marked the only standard unit of time the garden’s tenders had ever recognized. [96]
To avoid disrupting the slow, drifting paths of the falling petals or rousing the slumbering micro-life tucked in their folds, they navigated not with light beams, but with gentle sonar pinging that sent soft, rippling vibrations through the nebula’s thin gaseous medium to map every cluster and empty drift in their path. [149]
Strung on a thin cord of spun comet ice around their glowing core was the only physical object they carried: a dented, pitted calculating machine gear forged by their first ancestor to tend the garden, nearly eight thousand standard years prior. [190]
The core concept of generational patterns had shaped every choice made by the line of tenders, each adding a single tiny, precise notch to the gear’s inner edge to mark their own turn caring for the blooms, and logging their observations of the garden’s cycles into the gear’s etched grooves to be read by those who came after. [247]
The gear’s outer teeth were cut in a repeating logarithmic spiral that approximates infinity, a deliberate design choice by the first tender to remind every future keeper that their care was part of a cycle far larger than any single lifetime. [289]
The sentimentalist ran a wispy, misty finger along the gear’s teeth as they drifted, their sonar pings bouncing off a particularly dense cluster of deep rose petals ahead to alert them of a group of frozen, unconscious travel pods tucked in the cluster’s center. [336]
They drifted closer, letting their cool, soft glow spread across the pods to warm their frozen outer hulls without the risk of fire that conventional heating systems would carry, careful not to jostle the petals around them as they worked. [378]
When the pods’ lights flickered to life, signaling their occupants were safe and would soon wake to the garden’s beauty, the sentimentalist smiled, their form glowing a little brighter before they turned to continue their transit of the outer beds. [422]
Beneath the overwhelming, gentle serenity of the drifting petals and soft hum of their sonar pings, however, ran a sharp, quiet undercurrent of worry that lent the moment its troubling serenity, for the data etched into the calculating machine gear told a story no previous tender had ever had to face. [474]
Generations of logged observations showed the white dwarf at the garden’s center was dimming faster than expected, its light fading to the point that next year’s petals would not have enough energy to form, let alone glow, breaking the cycle of bloom that had run unbroken for longer than most human civilizations had existed. [531]
The sentimentalist paused for a moment, holding the gear up to catch the last faint rays of the dwarf’s golden light, and ran their finger over the twelve tiny notches cut into its inner edge, one for each tender that had come before them. [575]
They knew the generational pattern did not have to end here, for the spiral that approximates infinity on the gear’s teeth was not a fixed rule, but a promise that every cycle could be extended, if those tending it were willing to adapt. [621]
They adjusted their course, sending a new pattern of sonar pinging out toward the dwarf’s core to measure its remaining energy, their cool glow spreading wider as they moved, committed to finding a way to share their light with the garden for generations to come, without ever needing to strike a single flame. [677]
