Beyond the murmur of lost languages, the crystalline orchard perches at the edge of a shimmering crater, its frost-faceted fruit glowing pale silver when the thin, post-anthropocene wind stirs. [29]
Mara, a marsh-reed sculptor who traveled three sun cycles from the half-submerged lowlands to settle here, carries a bundle of dried reed stalks tucked under one arm and a faint, thrumming longing to graft herself onto the tight-knit community that tends the orchard’s delicate crops. [74]
Boldly sympathetic, she had spent her first two moon cycles listening more than speaking, learning that the orchard’s beating heart was the glimmering obsidian arbor that stood at its center, its polished surface marked with hundreds of tiny, swirling tags left by an elusive street artist no one had ever named. [125]
The tags, she learned, marked every milestone of the community’s webs of kinship: a new twist for a birth, a slanted loop for a marriage, a soft dot for a life well lived, each mark tying the person named to every other person who called the orchard home. [173]
When the elders mentioned that no new tags had appeared in six moon cycles, their voices heavy with the luminescent ache of fearing they had lost the quiet thread that held their shared history together, Mara proposed a shared search for the elusive street artist’s tag, hoping the joint effort would let her prove she cared as deeply about their traditions as the families who had lived here for generations. [235]
Groups spread out across the crater’s sloping edges, checking the undersides of crystalline fruit boughs, the cracked stone of the old crater rim paths, and the weathered walls of the small stone cottages that huddled at the orchard’s lower border. [273]
Mara joined the group of teens who tended the orchard’s new saplings, and it was one of them, a quiet girl with ink-stained fingers who flinched every time someone mentioned the tags, who finally led them to a small hidden cave tucked behind a cluster of glowing blue fruit trees. [320]
The artist, Kael, was 17, orphaned two cycles prior, who had stopped leaving tags because he thought no one noticed his work, that he was too much of an outsider to contribute to the community’s shared story. [352]
Mara’s chest ached with recognition, remembering how she had felt when she first arrived, sure she would never be allowed to add her own thread to the community’s web of kinship, and she pulled out her reed stalks to show Kael the small woven sculptures she made to honor the people she had met in her travels. [401]
Over the next three moon cycles, Mara worked with Kael and the rest of the community to restore the glimmering obsidian arbor, weaving reed frames around each existing tag to protect them from wind and weather, while Kael added new tags, including one marked with Mara’s name and one marked with his own, each tied with a small woven reed knot. [455]
The day the work was finished, the entire community gathered at the arbor, bringing baskets of crystalline fruit, warm spiced bread, and small mementos to lay at its base, and the elders asked Mara to speak at the ceremony to dedicate the newly restored arbor to the community’s ever-growing webs of kinship. [507]
Mara stood in front of the glowing black stone, the light of the setting sun bouncing off the polished surface to paint the crowd in streaks of pink and gold, and she told them that belonging was never about being born in a place, but about choosing to show up, to listen, to add your own small mark to the stories of the people around you. [555]
When she finished, Kael stepped forward to add one last tag, a looping, interwoven design that wrapped around the base of the arbor, linking every other existing tag together into a single, unbroken pattern that glowed faintly when the wind stirred the reed frames strung along the stone. [601]
As the crowd cheered, Mara ran a finger over the small tag marked with her name, the old luminescent ache of being an outsider fading into soft, warm joy, knowing she had finally grafted herself onto the community she had grown to love, her reed sculptures and Kael’s tags now permanent parts of the orchard’s shared history. [658]
