The scuffed neon sign for Gateway 7 flickered above the abandoned spaceport passenger terminal, its faded lettering half-obscured by crumpled carnival streamers left three days prior, when the pop-up interstellar fair had packed up its gyroscopic rides and bioluminescent cotton candy stalls and vanished into the warp lanes. [48]
Lila, a curious civil engineer intern in her second year of galactic infrastructure training, had snuck past the rusted perimeter fence that morning, her work bag slung over one shoulder and a half-finished mug of thermos-stored chai peeking out of its side pocket. [91]
She’d spent the last six months drafting failed proposals to repurpose derelict transit hubs across the sector, and her core drive now was to puzzle out new equations for hope that could turn empty, forgotten spaces into community anchors rather than scrap yards slated for demolition. [137]
Kicking a crumpled carnival ticket stub across the scuffed tile floor, she spotted a dented brass disc half-buried under a pile of discarded foam glow sticks, which she quickly realized was an explorer's sextant piece, its etched radial lines still sharp despite decades of exposure to the terminal’s fluctuating temperature and humidity. [189]
She’d long thought of abandoned civic spaces as a maze of intentions, each scratch on the wall, each faded handwritten sign, each discarded trinket a marker of a desire someone had left unfulfilled when the space was shuttered. [227]
As a functionally creative designer who specialized in adapting existing structural frameworks rather than tearing them down, she ran a finger along the sextant’s etched lines, realizing they mapped almost perfectly to the terminal’s original layout of foot traffic corridors, ventilation shafts, and unused utility tunnels. [273]
For weeks she’d been brainstorming how to resurrect the terminal without sinking millions of credits into full demolition and reconstruction, and the sextant piece gave her the key: through redirected flows of foot traffic, air circulation, and even public programming, she could turn the space’s confusing, overlapping layout into a feature rather than a flaw. [328]
She pulled her tablet out of her bag, cross-referencing the sextant’s markings with the terminal’s blueprints she’d pulled from the public archive the night before, a quiet, steady thrill rising in her chest that she didn’t dare name out loud, lest it vanish like the carnival had only days before. [378]
The sextant had once been used to navigate unknown stretches of deep space, and she realized it could now be used to navigate the maze of conflicting intentions that had stalled the terminal’s redevelopment for 12 years: local residents wanted a community gathering space, the planetary government wanted a low-cost trade hub, youth groups wanted a venue for art and performances, and environmental groups wanted zero new structural waste from the project. [450]
Her functionally creative approach solved every conflict at once: through redirected flows of foot traffic that separated trade vendor corridors from public gathering nooks, repurposed ventilation shafts that doubled as acoustic channels for live performances, and solar-powered air filtration systems that eliminated the need for new construction, she could meet every stakeholder’s request without compromising any of her core sustainability goals. [511]
She scribbled the first draft of her new proposal on the back of a discarded carnival flyer, mapping the corridors directly to the sextant’s etched lines, marking the old main gate as the central navigation point that would open out to a new public plaza planted with native wildflowers that had grown through cracks in the terminal’s outer parking lot over the years. [574]
The stoic wonder she felt as she finished the draft wasn’t the giddy, fleeting joy of the carnival’s spinning rides, but the quiet, steady certainty that she had cracked a problem no one else had bothered to look at closely enough, turning a pile of forgotten, discarded pieces into something that could serve the entire community for decades to come. [634]
She slipped the explorer's sextant piece into her work bag next to her tablet, already mentally drafting the presentation she would give to the city council the following week, knowing that the equations for hope she’d been searching for had been hidden in the terminal’s walls all along, just waiting for someone to connect the lines. [690]
