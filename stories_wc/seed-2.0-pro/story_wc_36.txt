The driven relic keeper picked her way down the scree slopes of Thunder-echo Range, the thin mountain air stinging her cheeks three years after the guardianship ended. [27]
The canyon hush wrapped around her so thick she could almost touch it, no bird calls, no wind rustle, only the faint thrum of rock that had given the range its name centuries prior. [61]
She had come to harvest insights from failure, to unpack the mistake that had cost her the role she had trained for her entire adult life, when the veil between worlds had frayed and she had been too slow to mend it. [103]
First, she knelt beside the half-frozen creek cutting through the canyon floor, arranging driftwood as directional markers to anchor her path between the mortal realm and the half-visible world of echoes that clung to the range’s stone walls. [141]
The work was avalanchingly quiet, each small piece of wood placed with deliberate care, no sound louder than her steady breath and the faint gurgle of water moving under thin ice. [172]
Weaving between worlds required more than just intent, she had learned the hard way; it required anchors that existed in both realms, driftwood that had been touched by the mist of the echo world and worn smooth by mortal water. [212]
When the last marker was set, she pulled the worn leather case from her pack and began to uncase the gilded reed flute, the relic she had been tasked with guarding for ten years before her failure. [249]
The flute’s golden surface glinted in the pale winter sun, its carvings of mountain peaks and wispy veils worn soft from decades of careful handling by keepers before her. [278]
She lifted it to her lips, and the first note hung in the canyon hush, low and thrumming, matching the pulse of the stone under her boots. [305]
Slowly, the world around her softened at the edges, the stone walls shifting to show glimpses of the echo realm, where lost memories and missed possibilities drifted like dandelion seeds on unseen wind. [338]
She followed the line of driftwood markers, her boots making no sound on the stone as she stepped between worlds, her focus fixed on the day of her failure three years prior. [370]
That day, a series of small avalanches had buried the marker stones she had set to hold the veil steady, and she had panicked, fumbling the flute so badly she could not play the stabilizing note in time. [408]
The veil had frayed for three hours, letting dozens of echo spirits cross into the mortal realm before the senior keeper had arrived to repair the damage, and she had been stripped of her title immediately. [444]
She had spent every day since then studying the range’s weather patterns, learning that driftwood, unlike stone, would float to the surface even after being buried under snow and rock, making it a far more reliable anchor for cross-world work. [484]
As she reached the spot where the old stone markers had been buried, she spotted the echo of her younger self, frozen with fear, flute trembling in her hands, and she paused, feeling no shame, only gentle curiosity. [522]
She lifted her own flute again, playing a soft, steady note that wrapped around her younger self’s panic, and watched as the echo relaxed, finding the note she had missed all those years before. [556]
The veil in the echo solidified, and she knew her insight was not that she had been unskilled, but that she had relied on the wrong tools, and let the pressure of her role override her training to adapt to the range’s unique quirks. [600]
She turned to walk back along the driftwood markers, the note still humming in her chest, and stepped fully back into the mortal realm as the last light of the sun dipped below the range’s highest peaks. [637]
She slipped the gilded reed flute back into its leather case, her fingers brushing the carved mountains with new affection, knowing she would bring her findings to the council of keepers the next week. [671]
She did not need her old title back, she realized; her only goal was to make sure no other young keeper would make the same avoidable mistake, and that the veil between worlds would stay steady for generations to come. [711]
The canyon hush held as she climbed back up the scree slope, her pack light on her shoulders, the weight of three years of guilt finally gone, replaced with the quiet, burning purpose that had driven her every step since she lost her role. [755]
