Three years after the story famine, diligent orchestra copyist Elara huddles in her ember alcove, the coastal mist curling thin around the chipped stone edges of her workbench. [28]
The famine erased every printed and spoken story across the archipelago, turning ink to ash and memory to static, leaving communities adrift without the shared tales that once bound them together. [59]
As a copyist who once transcribed symphony scores for the archipelago’s only surviving orchestra, she has spent every spare hour for two years chasing a quiet, seemingly impossible goal: to teach dust to tell stories, so no blight could ever erase narrative again. [102]
The only tool she has besides her pen and stacked sheaves of blank parchment is a beat-up shortwave radio, its dial glow secretly luminous even when the device is switched off, a quirk she discovered six months prior when she found the set discarded in a tide pool. [150]
That faint, cool glow is the marker of a pirate broadcast, run by a group of anonymous storytellers who transmit snippets of lost tales disguised as old folk songs, to avoid triggering the blight’s lingering static sensors. [187]
She has learned to cloak the stories she pulls from the broadcast within the musical notations she copies for the orchestra, weaving each phrase into the spaces between violin and cello lines so no casual observer would spot them. [226]
The broadcasters share their full tales via elusive song lyrics, scattered across weeks of transmissions, so only someone listening consistently, like Elara, can piece together the full narrative arc of each lost story. [259]
Tonight, the mist is so thick it seeps through the alcove’s cracked window, carrying fine, iron-rich dust that catches the shortwave’s glow as it settles on her open score pages. [289]
She works with thistled calm, her pen moving steadily as she transcribes both the orchestra’s new commission and the latest snippet of a story about a lighthouse keeper who talked to seabirds, tucking each line of lyric into a grace note mark on the page. [334]
When she pauses to adjust the radio dial, the dust on the page lifts slightly, swirling in the glow, and she sees the words she just wrote shimmer on each tiny particle, as if the dust is memorizing them. [373]
She blows gently, and the dust drifts across the alcove, settling on the stone wall where faint, glowing lines of the lighthouse keeper’s story appear for three slow seconds before fading, soft as a breath. [408]
The shortwave’s secretly luminous glow is the key, she realizes, because it charges the dust with the same frequency as the pirate broadcast, letting the particles hold the narrative embedded in the song lyrics. [442]
Over the next three months, she refines the process, copying every story she pulls from the broadcast into her scores, letting the dust settle on each page for an hour before sealing the notations into the final copies the orchestra uses for performances. [485]
The first time the orchestra plays the lighthouse keeper symphony in the town square, a fine dust drifts from the sheet music stands as the musicians play, and members of the crowd gasp as faint images of the lighthouse, the seabirds, and the keeper’s weathered face flicker in the air around them. [537]
Elara stands at the back of the crowd, the shortwave radio tucked in her coat pocket, its dial glow seeping faint and warm through the fabric, and she smiles, her thistled calm unbroken even as tears prick the corners of her eyes. [579]
No blight could erase these stories now, not when they were held in every speck of dust that drifted across the archipelago, carried on wind and tide, shared in every performance of every symphony she transcribed. [615]
The pirate broadcasters sent a hidden lyric a week later, congratulating her on the successful first transmission, and she knew the age of the story famine was finally, irrevocably, over. [645]
