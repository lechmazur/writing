The overhead fluorescent’s low, thrumming buzz cut out mid-hum, drenching the abandoned circus ticket office in thin, gray winter light that seeped through cracked paint and smudged Plexiglas windows. [29]
Javi, an elevator repair technician who’d seen ghosts since he was 12, froze mid-step, his utility belt clinking against his hip as he adjusted his flashlight. [55]
He’d taken the odd after-hours gig for one reason, the same one that drove every side job he picked up in derelict buildings: to discover what ceilings hide, the things left behind when everyone else walks away. [92]
He’d long learned that cracks in the façade, whether in elevator shaft paneling or the plaster of a 100-year-old ticket booth, almost always held more than water damage and loose drywall. [123]
Javi was skillfully observant, a trait that made him one of the best techs in the city, able to spot a frayed cable or misaligned sensor before anyone else even pulled their tools out. [157]
His go-to trick for finding hidden spaces, one he’d refined over 15 years, was analyzing dust accumulation patterns, the faint stripes and thinner patches that marked where a panel had been moved or a ceiling tile lifted recently, even if it had been decades. [201]
A faint, translucent figure of an older man in oil-stained overalls stood by the ticket booth’s back wall, his hand hovering over a warped wooden panel that Javi had almost missed. [232]
Javi knew better than to run from ghosts; he’d learned to engage, to follow their silent cues, because they almost always led him to the things no one else had bothered to find. [265]
Running his flashlight along the edge of the panel, he confirmed his hunch: the dust along its seams was half as thick as the surrounding plaster, meaning it had been opened at least once in the last 20 years, far more recently than the circus had shut down. [313]
He slipped a flathead screwdriver into the seam and popped the panel free, and a small, heavy brass object clattered to the linoleum at his feet, dented but still intact. [343]
It was a horologist’s mainspring barrel, the casing that held the coiled spring that powered old clocks, and Javi recognized it from the vintage grandfather clock he’d repaired for his grandma as a teen. [377]
The ghost, he realized, was Elias, the circus’s in-house horologist and calliope repairman who’d gone missing in 1998, a cold case no one had ever solved, his name scrawled on a faded poster taped to the ticket booth’s front window. [417]
Javi felt that familiar weightless burden settle in his chest, the soft, unpressured weight of carrying a story no one else had bothered to listen to, of being the only person who could tie up a loose end left dangling for 25 years. [460]
He twisted the lid off, and a folded slip of paper tumbled out, covered in Elias’s looping handwriting, explaining he’d hidden evidence of the circus owner’s embezzlement inside the barrel, and had hidden the barrel before the owner could make him “disappear” for threatening to go to the police. [509]
Elias gave him a small, grateful nod, his form starting to fade at the edges, the tension he’d carried for decades finally slipping away, because he’d finally found someone who would listen. [541]
Javi tucked the barrel and the note into his tool bag, already planning to drop them off at the local police station the next morning, along with the location of the unmarked grave he’d spotted under the circus’s bleachers a half hour earlier, marked only by a patch of wild daisies that didn’t grow anywhere else on the property. [600]
He didn’t mind the extra work, or the quiet responsibility of carrying the voices of people who’d been forgotten, because it was the only part of his job that ever felt like it mattered more than fixing a broken lift or a stuck door. [644]
As he turned to head back to the freight elevator he’d come to inspect, he glanced up at the ceiling tiles, wondering what other secrets the old building held, what other stories were waiting for someone to take the time to look for them. [688]
