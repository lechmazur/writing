The arcane linguist, Mara, ran a gloved finger along the cracked spines of leather-bound codices lining the extinct language archive’s stone shelves, her chest thrumming with the cautious exuberance of someone about to fix a mistake three years in the making. [41]
She and Kael, her once inseparable collaborator, had not spoken for nearly half that time after she’d published a paper on symphonies of truth without crediting his field work, a choice that had shattered their friendship and left her hollow with guilt. [83]
This was her last chance to make things right, before paths diverge at the end of the week when Kael accepted a permanent position at a distant arcane research outpost and she took a fellowship on the opposite side of the continent. [125]
She’d always been carefully reckless in her work, cutting corners on bureaucracy to prioritize discovery, but this time her haste had hurt someone she loved more than her research. [154]
In the center of the oak table between them sat a dented glass ink well, its sides etched with the swirling runes of Lyridian, the dead language they’d spent four years decoding together. [187]
She’d tracked it down for six months, learning it was the last surviving artifact that could unsnarl the tangled knot of their fractured bond, if she used it correctly. [216]
Lyridian magic worked via interconnected promises, each phrase a binding vow that amplified shared truth rather than forcing compliance, which is why symphonies of truth – sequences of Lyridian verse sung in harmony – were used to resolve unresolvable rifts between trusted parties. [259]
She pushed the ink well across the table, her throat tight as she met Kael’s guarded gaze, and explained her plan: they would each dip a quill in its enchanted ink, write their unvarnished truths about the rift between them, then sing the verses together to seal the promises they both needed to make. [313]
For a long moment, Kael said nothing, his fingers brushing the faint crack on the ink well’s side that matched the crack in the custom codex binding they’d made together their first year in the program. [349]
When he finally nodded, Mara’s breath caught, the cautious exuberance she’d kept buried for weeks bubbling up so fast she had to bite her lip to keep from grinning too wide before they’d even begun. [384]
She wrote first, scrawling her regret for cutting him out, her fear that his work was stronger than hers and that she would be left behind if she didn’t claim the discovery first, her deep desire to fix what she had broken no matter what it cost her career. [433]
Kael wrote next, his hand steady as he described the hurt of seeing his years of work uncredited, the grief of losing the person he’d considered his closest friend, the small, stubborn spark of hope that had made him agree to meet her in the archive at all. [481]
When they were done, they leaned over the table and sang, their voices weaving together in the soft, lilting cadence of Lyridian, the ink on their pages glowing faint blue as the interconnected promises they’d written wrapped around the rift between them, softening its sharp edges and unsnarling the tangled resentment that had festered for years. [537]
The glass ink well hummed softly in time with their symphony of truth, its etchings glowing the same bright blue as the ink, a quiet testament to the fact that even dead languages could hold enough magic to mend what was broken. [579]
When they finished singing, Kael looked up at her, a faint smile tugging at the corner of his mouth, and said he would turn down the outpost position if she would agree to co-author a corrected paper on the symphonies of truth, and to collaborate on all their future work, no exceptions. [631]
Mara laughed, bright and relieved, as she nodded so hard her glasses slipped down her nose, the careful recklessness that had gotten her into this mess now feeling like a gift, because it had pushed her to chase this last chance instead of letting Kael walk away forever. [679]
She reached across the table to curl her fingers around his, the hum of the glass ink well still thrumming between them, and knew she had done what she came here to do: restore trust after silence, and erase the future where their paths diverged for good. [726]
Outside the archive, the sun dipped below the stone spires of the city, painting the sky in streaks of pink and gold, and for the first time in three years, Mara felt no guilt, no fear, only the warm, bright buzz of cautious exuberance for all the work, and all the joy, they had yet to share. [783]
