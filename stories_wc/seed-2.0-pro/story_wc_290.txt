Elara, a prudently adventurous claustrophobic explorer who avoided tight cave systems even when they promised untold relics, had waited three months for the fallow period, when the rift nexus along the old abbey’s grounds lay dormant enough to access without risking instantaneous collapse. [43]
Her goal, sharp as the blueprint drafting compass point she’d inherited from her cartographer grandmother, was to uncover a vital key lost to time, one that could realign the warped spatial pockets that had swallowed her older brother mid-expedition five years prior. [85]
She picked her way through gnarled hawthorn thickets until she reached the shadowed nexus under starlight, a circular clearing ringed with weathered standing stones that hummed with faint, disorienting energy, its edges warping so that stepping left sometimes sent her lurching three feet right. [129]
This spatial mutiny, the tendency of the nexus to rearrange its own geometry without warning, was why most explorers wrote the site off as too dangerous, but Elara had spent years compiling notes from locals who’d heard strange, resonant music drifting from the ruin on clear fall nights. [177]
She’d brought no gear besides the compass point strung on a leather cord around her neck, because she knew the only way to cut through the nexus’s chaotic geometry was through the reverberation of a lonely church organ, the one that had sat undamaged in the abbey’s roofless nave since the 17th century. [230]
Her fingers brushed the cold metal of the compass point as she climbed the crumbled stone steps to the nave, her chest tight with the faint prickle of her claustrophobia, even though the space was open to the sky, because she could feel the nexus folding in on itself around her, pressing in like invisible walls. [286]
She’d taught herself to play the organ’s only surviving hymn, a slow, mournful tune her brother had loved as a kid, and she pressed her feet to the pedals and her fingers to the yellowed ivory keys, letting the sound swell out across the clearing, deep and thrumming enough to vibrate in her molars. [340]
Within seconds, the chaotic shifts of the nexus slowed, the twisting edges of the clearing seeming to transfix in place, locked into stability by the steady thrum of the organ’s reverberations, the spatial mutiny quieted by the consistent, predictable wave of the sound. [383]
Elara’s throat tightened with a wistful rebellion as she played, fighting against the natural laws of the nexus that had stolen her brother, rejecting the idea that some losses were permanent even as tears blurred her vision. [420]
When the last note faded, she pulled the compass point from around her neck, holding it out in front of her, and watched as it spun once before pointing directly at a spot in the center of the nexus, where a faint, glowing brass key half-buried in moss pulsed with soft gold light. [473]
She crossed the clearing slowly, her feet landing solidly on even ground for the first time all night, no longer lurching sideways as the nexus’s geometry stayed fixed, the organ’s echoes still lingering in the cool night air to hold the spatial mutiny at bay. [518]
When she knelt to pick up the key, she saw a faint, translucent hand resting beside it, wearing the frayed leather bracelet she’d made her brother for his 19th birthday, his face soft and smiling as he nodded at her, visible for only a few seconds before the organ’s echoes faded completely. [570]
She closed her fingers around the key, its warmth seeping into her palm, and she did not flinch when the nexus began to shift again around her, the spatial mutiny returning now that the sound had faded, because she knew she held the tool to bring her brother home for good. [621]
As she walked back down the abbey steps, the compass point warm against her chest under her jacket, she smiled through the lingering tears, equal parts sad for the years they’d lost and fierce with the quiet, wistful rebellion of someone who’d refused to let the universe decide what was gone forever. [673]
