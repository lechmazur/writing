Lila, a camera obscura artist and spiritually curious pilgrim of sparks, had wandered to the twilight viaduct in bloom three days after a past life memory surfaced unbidden during her morning tea. [32]
For months, her quiet motivation to find where unused love is stored had guided every step of her travels, leading her to forgotten shrines, coastal cliffside caves, and now this ivy-choked stone structure strung with climbing rose and wisteria that perfumed the mist curling around its arches. [79]
Slung over her shoulder was her custom-built camera obscura, its only lens a milky blue opal called Kwan Yin’s Tear, a gift from an elderly monk she’d met in a mountain temple who told her the stone held the quiet power to show what the naked eye could not. [128]
The memory that had surfaced was faint, filtered through the same foggy lucidity that hung over the viaduct now: she was a weaver in 14th century Hangzhou, and she’d spent three years weaving a silk tapestry for her childhood friend, only to die of a fever before she could give it to her, the love she’d poured into every thread never spoken, never received. [192]
She set up the camera obscura on a flat stone ledge halfway up the viaduct’s central arch, adjusting its wooden panels to face the sun dipping below the tree line, pink and gold light seeping through the petal-strewn mist. [231]
The monk had told her that the method of revealing what was hidden with Kwan Yin’s Tear came not from light alone, but through breathing changes, the warmth and rhythm of her own exhale aligning with the stone’s energy to lift the veil between past and present, seen and unseen. [281]
She closed her eyes, slowing her breath until each inhale lasted seven counts and each exhale lasted twelve, the scent of wisteria filling her lungs, the hum of crickets starting up in the grass below the viaduct matching the steady thud of her heart. [325]
When she leaned forward and breathed directly onto the opal lens, the surface fogged over for three long seconds before clearing to show an image that was not the sunset and viaduct in front of her, but the weaver’s cottage from her memory, the finished tapestry rolled up on a wooden table, glowing with soft, golden light. [382]
She kept breathing, slow and intentional, and the image shifted to show her old friend, her hair streaked with gray, hanging the tapestry on her cottage wall decades later, a note tucked into its edge that said “I knew you loved me, I always did”, the unused love Lila had spent months searching for spilling out of every thread, soft and warm and not wasted at all. [449]
She had always thought unused love was locked away in some dark, forgotten corner of the world, but here it was, stored in the quiet knowing of the person it was meant for, carried forward across centuries even when words were never spoken, goodbyes were never said, and gifts were never handed over. [502]
As the last sliver of sun dipped below the horizon, she felt the old, quiet grief she had carried since the memory surfaced begin to heal, the weight in her chest lifting so slowly she barely noticed it at first, until she realized she was smiling, tears rolling down her cheeks that felt like they belonged to both the weaver and her current self. [566]
She sat there for another hour, watching the stars come out behind the mist, the camera obscura still glowing faintly with the image of the tapestry, and she knew her work as a pilgrim of sparks was not over, but she had found the answer she was looking for that day. [617]
Unused love was never stored away, hidden and lost, but woven into the fabric of every life it touched, lingering in the small, quiet moments of recognition that cross lifetimes, soft and unshakable, even when no one remembers exactly where it came from. [660]
She packed up her camera as the mist grew thicker, tucking a fallen wisteria bloom into her coat pocket, and headed down the viaduct path, lighter than she had been in months, already curious what other hidden, luminous truths her journey would bring her to next. [708]
