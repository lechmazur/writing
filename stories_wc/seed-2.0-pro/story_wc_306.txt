The last spark of the shore campfire sputtered and died ten minutes before dawn, when Lira stepped into the gusting spiral of the breezy lighthouse’s stone stairwell, her satchel slung tight across her chest. [34]
She worked as a conscience for hire, a rare trade for folks tired of carrying the weight of choices they’d already convinced themselves were perfect. [59]
Her client tonight was Kael, a small-town mayor who’d spent three years promising to bulldoze the local wetland for a luxury housing development, and whose public persona was so sincerely insincere even his closest aides couldn’t tell when he was lying. [100]
He’d reached out three days prior, saying he was tired of his own unshakable, unchallenged certainty, and wanted to welcome nuance into certainty before he signed the wetland demolition order the following morning. [133]
The lighthouse’s top landing was strung with 12 separate wind chimes, each tuned to a different frequency of human regret, and Lira’s primary working method was deciphering wind chime selections to map the unspoken conflicts her clients hid even from themselves. [174]
From her satchel she pulled the hollow mirror shard, a jagged piece of glass that didn’t reflect what was in front of it, but what a person most feared they’d lose if they stayed on their current path. [212]
As the wind picked up, Kael shifted, and Lira spotted the faint, shimmering edge of the cloak of doubt he’d been wearing under his wool coat, the weight of his unacknowledged second thoughts stitched into every thread. [249]
Kael had expected a harsh lecture, but Lira’s manner was soft, the lighthouse’s thick stone walls wrapping them both in a bubble of protected vulnerability where he didn’t have to perform the confident, unflappable mayor he showed the world. [288]
The first wind chime sang out, high and clear, followed by three more in quick succession, and Lira nodded, telling Kael the chimes had picked up his regret over abandoning the childhood frog ponds he’d played in as a kid, the same ponds the development would pave over. [336]
He took the hollow mirror shard when she held it out, and gasped when he saw not his own face, but the flocks of migrating birds, the otter families, the kids from the local elementary school who visited the wetland every spring for their science field trip. [383]
For the first time in years, he let himself admit he’d known the development was a bad idea from the start, but had clung to his certainty because he was scared of being seen as weak for changing his mind. [423]
Lira stepped closer, and gently reached up to unhook the clasp of the cloak of doubt from around his neck, letting the shimmering fabric fall to the stone floor at his feet, no longer a weight he had to hide. [463]
The wind chimes sang out again, a softer, warmer chorus this time, and Lira explained that the cloak didn’t have to be discarded entirely, but could be folded up and kept as a reminder that doubt wasn’t a failure, but a signal that there were more angles to consider than the ones he’d first settled on. [519]
He picked the cloak up and folded it carefully, tucking it into the inner pocket of his coat, right next to the folded demolition order he’d been carrying for weeks, now feeling far less heavy than it had an hour before. [560]
The first pink streaks of dawn painted the horizon over the ocean, and the breeze coming through the lighthouse’s open windows carried the faint, earthy scent of the wetland, thick with the croak of frogs and the chirp of early waking birds. [602]
He asked Lira if he could keep the hollow mirror shard, and she nodded, telling him it would help him check in with himself any time he felt himself slipping back into the rigid, uncompromising certainty that had gotten him into this position in the first place. [649]
When he left the lighthouse, he had a plan: he’d call an emergency town council meeting that afternoon, present the data he’d hidden for months about the wetland’s flood protection and ecological value, and propose a public park and nature education center instead of the housing development. [696]
He knew there would be pushback from the wealthy developers who’d funded his campaign, and that some residents would call him a flip-flopper, a liar, a man who couldn’t keep his promises, but for the first time in years, he didn’t care. [738]
He had finally welcomed nuance into the hard, sharp certainty he’d hidden behind for so long, and that was enough to carry him through whatever came next. [765]
