The salt flat stretched bleached and endless under bruised purple storm clouds, the distant thunder’s low rumble fading to a hush that held the plain in a fragile, waiting lull. [30]
Elias Voss, the billionaire amoral philanthropist who’d made his first hundred million diverting the region’s river systems to feed corporate agribusiness and create the manufactured drought that had turned this basin to salt, stood at the edge of the former riverbed, leather boots crusted over with white crystalline residue. [79]
No one who’d seen him bribe regulators to cut residential water supplies or donate half his fortune to clean-water nonprofits to launder his reputation would have guessed he was secretly sentimental, his private safe holding a single fossilized extinct bird’s egg he’d found as a child, before the rivers ran dry. [130]
His quiet, unspoken motivation was to preserve the echo of extinct birds, the same Carolina parakeets he’d once seen flit over the riverbanks with their bright green and yellow plumage, before the last of them died out in the wild in the 1910s, before he’d decided the world’s only lasting goods were the ones you could control and leave behind. [190]
He’d spent three years studying the pattern of cracks snaking across the exposed riverbed floor, realizing they formed a perfect map drawn by cracks in dried riverbeds, of the old parakeet migratory paths, the fissures tracing every stop the flocks had once made to drink, nest, and raise their young. [240]
The egg he held was smooth, pocked with tiny mineral deposits, and fitted with a small, custom-made audio device he’d commissioned, preloaded with every surviving recording of Carolina parakeet calls, calibrated to broadcast on a loop that would carry for miles across the flat, unbroken terrain. [286]
He followed the map’s paths slowly, his boots crunching over salt and cracked clay, the thunder rumbling again far off before fading once more, the air thick with the sharp, metallic tang of coming rain. [321]
When he reached the intersection of cracks that marked the old nesting ground, the center of the migratory route where he’d first spotted a parakeet flock at seven years old, he knelt, brushing salt off the clay, and prepared to release the egg into the small hollow at the crossroads. [371]
He knew the coming rain would seep into the clay, activating the device’s moisture sensor and triggering the calls, that the sound would drift across the salt flat for weeks until the battery died, that no one would likely ever hear it but him, that this act was as useless as it was precious to him. [427]
He didn’t care that he’d destroyed the region’s ecosystem to get here, that families had gone thirsty for decades because of his greed, that his philanthropic work was just a band-aid over the gaping wound he’d carved into the land to line his pockets. [471]
All that mattered was that for a little while, the parakeets would sing here again, their calls bouncing off the salt crystals, mixing with the rumble of distant thunder, filling the empty space he’d helped create with something soft and alive that he couldn’t sell or trade or take back. [521]
He lowered the egg into the hollow gently, pressing it into the soft clay until it sat level, the pale blue-gray fossil blending almost perfectly with the muted tones of the cracked riverbed. [554]
As he stood, the first fat drop of rain hit his cheek, cool and sudden, and the lull of distant thunder broke for half a second with a low, sharp crack that sounded almost like a bird’s cry. [592]
He smiled, a rare, unguarded expression no public relations camera had ever captured, and turned to walk back to his car, not looking over his shoulder as the rain picked up, seeping into the clay around the egg. [630]
There was a fractured grace to the moment, equal parts atonement and selfishness, equal parts loss and hope, the ghost of a flock of birds coming home to a land he’d both ruined and given back a small piece of. [670]
