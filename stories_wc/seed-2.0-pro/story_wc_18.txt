The fog clung to the gnarled, fruitless boughs of the ruined orchard district when Elara, the firmly grounded mirror maker, picked her way past crumbled stone walls as the first shimmer of twilight frost gilded the tips of the dead apple trees. [42]
Tucked in the lining of her wool coat was an astronomer’s broken sextant, its brass frame dented, its split glass lens crisscrossed with faint cracks that caught the pale light like shards of ice. [76]
Three nights prior, her most precious creation— a dream she’d forged into a physical reflection of the constellation Orion, meant to hang in the village square as a guide for lost travelers— had slipped from its frame and fled into the fog-choked orchard, vanishing before she could grab it. [125]
The air held the crisp, unyielding edge of glass mountains, no sound seeping through the thick fog save for the soft crunch of frost under her leather boots, every surface glinting as if coated in fine, sharp crystal. [163]
The broken sextant had belonged to her grandfather, the astronomer who first taught her that the boundaries between the waking world, the realm of dreams, and the stretch of the stars were not solid, but thin enough to erase if you knew how to look past them. [210]
She’d come here at the exact shimmer of twilight frost to recreate the exact conditions under which the dream had been forged, using the method her grandfather had passed down: matching her breath to the slow pulse of the stars visible through the fog, synchronized breathing that would soften the lines between what was real and what was made of thought. [271]
She cleared a patch of frost from a flat, sun-bleached stone at the center of the orchard, set the broken sextant on its surface, and settled cross-legged on the cold grass, her posture straight and steady, true to her firmly grounded nature. [313]
She closed her eyes, slowed her inhale to match the faint drift of the fog, held it for the count of three faint star pulses, then exhaled at the same pace the frost melted from the sextant’s cracked lens, syncing every rise and fall of her chest to the world around her. [365]
Slowly, the edges of the fog began to blur, the line between her breath and the mist vanishing, the cracks in the sextant’s lens glowing soft silver as the boundary between waking and dream thinned to nothing, just as her grandfather had promised. [408]
When she opened her eyes, the orchard was lit with faint, shifting starlight, and she spotted her runaway dream bobbing between the apple boughs, its three bright belt stars glinting as it drifted just out of reach, as if testing her resolve. [450]
She lifted the broken sextant, holding it to her eye, and though its lens was cracked, it aligned perfectly with the dream’s brightest star, the brass frame warming in her palm as if her grandfather’s own hand rested over hers. [490]
She kept her breathing synchronized, slow and steady, never moving too fast, never letting the cold seep into her bones, calling the dream closer with every exhale, promising it a safe, permanent home in the mirror she’d carved for it back in her workshop. [534]
The dream drifted closer, its light wrapping around her wrist like a cool, glowing ribbon, and when she closed her fingers around it, it didn’t slip away, fitting perfectly into the hollow of her palm where she’d first held it when she forged it months prior. [580]
As the last shimmer of twilight frost faded from the boughs, the fog began to lift, the solid lines of the waking world settling back into place, the boundary she’d erased now firm again, the dream safe in her pocket, tucked right next to the broken sextant that had helped her find her way. [634]
Her boots crunched over the leftover frost as she walked back toward the village, her step firmly grounded, the sharp, glinting air of the glass mountains still clinging to her coat, knowing she’d not only reclaimed her dream but proven that even the most solid-seeming lines could be crossed if you moved slowly, breathed in sync with the world, and refused to let what you loved slip away.
