The drift-ice marina breathed sharp, briny air the morning Mara, a euthanasia tourist who’d woken up changed three weeks prior, laced up her steel-toed boots for her volunteer shift. [29]
She’d traveled to this remote northern port after her stage four ovarian cancer spread to her bones, planning to check into the country’s legally sanctioned end-of-life clinic as soon as she’d tied up the last of her loose ends. [68]
The wake-up call that left her changed came when she’d woken from a pain-induced coma convinced that she didn’t want to step into death blind, that she first needed to learn the sound of approaching endings. [104]
Her chosen method to study this quiet cue was through disaster relief volunteering, helping families recover small, precious belongings from the slush-choked docks after a late winter storm had shattered half the marina’s moorings and swept dozens of personal items out into the floating ice. [149]
Mara grips reality loosely these days, her pain medication and the constant, sharp wind making it easy to blend memories of her grandmother’s sunlit porch with the pale, glittering expanse of ice stretching out to the horizon. [186]
When her shift ends each afternoon, she trawls the edges of the ice floes with a long hooked pole, sifting through half-frozen debris for anything the official recovery teams might have missed. [218]
She’s been searching, mostly unconsciously, for the heirloom lace parasol tip that broke off her grandmother’s favorite sunshade when Mara was seven, the day her grandma told her she’d chosen to end her own cancer treatment rather than waste away in a hospital bed. [262]
This slow, deliberate work has taught her a fold in patience, the kind of quiet pause that lets you feel the world shift around you instead of fighting to outrun every hard, inevitable thing coming your way. [299]
On the sixth day of her volunteering, the hook on her pole catches on something small and cold half-buried in a chunk of slush, and when she pulls it up, the tiny, carved ivory parasol tip glints in the low northern sun, perfectly intact. [343]
She sits down on a frozen mooring post, turning the tip over in her palm, and for a second she swears she can smell her grandma’s lavender perfume on the wind, mixing with the salt and ice of the marina. [383]
She’d spent so long rushing toward the end, convinced that the only choice she had was to schedule her death like an appointment, that she’d missed the soft, low hum of endings all around her: the creak of ice melting as spring approaches, the sigh of a family who’s just found a lost child’s stuffed animal in the slush, the quiet release of a breath when you stop fighting what you can’t change. [456]
That’s the sound she’d been searching for, she realizes, not a single clear warning bell, but a thousand small, soft signals that wrap around you like wind, gentle enough to miss if you’re moving too fast. [492]
She calls the end-of-life clinic that evening, not to schedule her appointment, but to tell them she’s going to stay at the marina for a few more weeks, helping with the relief efforts for as long as her body will let her. [534]
She tucks the parasol tip into the inner pocket of her coat, right over her heart, where she can feel its cool weight whenever she reaches for it, a small reminder that endings don’t have to be rushed, that patience can be folded small enough to carry with you even when you’re walking into the unknown. [590]
The wind off the drift ice picks up as she walks back to her small rented cabin, carrying the distant laugh of a child who just got their lost toy back, the faint call of a seagull heading north for the summer, the quiet, warm comfort of a choice that feels like her own for the first time in years. [650]
She doesn’t know how much time she has left, but for the first time since her diagnosis, she doesn’t care to count, content to listen to the wind, to trawl the ice, to let each day unfold at its own soft, unhurried pace. [693]
