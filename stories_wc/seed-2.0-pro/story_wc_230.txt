Three years after the empathy virus swept through the coastal valleys, Elara, a rational truffle forager who’d never once followed folk wisdom without cross-checking three sources, tended her grafted apple boughs strung across the storm-battered ravine’s hanging orchard, her boots planted on planks bolted to granite outcroppings. [47]
The virus had left 72 percent of the surviving population involuntarily truthful, unable to lie, omit, or soften hard truths even when doing so would keep a neighbor from spiraling into grief, and Elara had fled to the ravine after accidentally telling her sister her newborn’s heart defect was almost certainly fatal mid-baby shower. [101]
Her sole goal now was to cultivate psychological safety for the small group of similarly afflicted people who visited the orchard once a month, a quiet space where no one would be shamed for blurting a truth they’d spent years hiding. [142]
She’d spent six months digging through discredited pre-virus medical textbooks at abandoned library sales, chasing rumored cures for the involuntary truthfulness that no official research would acknowledge, since the government had framed the trait as a public good to reduce corruption. [183]
Last week, a border runner had left her a smuggled relic at the orchard’s drop point: an antique glass eye, hand-blown in 1892 for a lighthouse keeper who’d lost his eye to a rogue wave, its iris a swirled deep amber the color of the truffles she hunted in the ravine’s mossy undercrofts. [236]
The tattered 1972 psychiatry textbook she’d found in a flooded basement claimed holding the glass eye to one’s dominant eye for three minutes at dawn would occlude the parts of the brain that forced unplanned truth-telling, without removing the capacity for empathy entirely. [279]
As a rational forager who always tested new mushroom varieties on herself before sharing them with the community, Elara had tested the ritual first that morning, standing on the highest plank of the orchard as wind whipped rain through the apple boughs, the storm that had been building for three days finally breaking. [332]
When the timer on her old pocket watch went off, she’d deliberately tried to say the sky was bright pink, and the words came out easily, no tightness in her chest, no involuntary flinch that had always preceded forced truth-telling before. [373]
By mid-morning, the 12 members of her community had picked their way down the ravine’s slippery paths, carrying jars of jam, loaves of sourdough, and small trinkets they’d traded for the chance to try the cure themselves. [410]
The first to try was Mara, a third-grade teacher who’d lost her job after telling a student his father would never come home from the war, and she cried when she successfully told her friend her new haircut looked great, even though she privately thought it was far too short. [460]
There was a fractured grace to the scene: rain dripped through the boughs to leave dark splotches on their coats, the wind shook the planks under their feet, and every success was met with quiet, shaky laughter, no fanfare, no loud celebration, just the quiet relief of people regaining control of their own voices. [514]
Elara, ever the rational forager, had already calculated the risk of the government finding their small operation: they’d hung thick canvas tarps between the boughs to occlude the view from the ridge above, and they only met on storm days when drone patrols were grounded due to high winds. [563]
She ran a finger over the smooth surface of the antique glass eye as she waited for the last member of the group to finish their turn, wondering if the lighthouse keeper who’d first owned it had ever imagined it would be used to give people their words back, a smuggled relic of a past no one had thought held any value for the present. [628]
By the time the last person finished, the storm had softened to a light drizzle, and the group gathered to share food, telling small, gentle lies to each other: that the jam was the best they’d ever tasted, that the walk down had been easy, that they felt safe enough to come back next month, no matter the risk. [687]
Elara bit into a slice of sourdough and smiled, knowing that the psychological safety they’d built here, tangled in the apple boughs of a storm-battered ravine, was far more valuable than any truffle she’d ever found, and far more resilient than any government ban on unapproved cures. [734]
