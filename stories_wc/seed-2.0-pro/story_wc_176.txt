Elara, a lost wax caster who had spent the past three months working through her guilt over botching a community memorial cast a decade prior, finally stepped into her icebound sanctum under the swirling green aurora the morning after forgiving herself. [41]
Her core motivation, now that the weight of self-reproach had lifted, was to preserve the sound of forgotten names from the town’s first ice settlers, names no living person had spoken in over two hundred years. [77]
Tucked in the lining of her wool parka was the tattered street organ punched card scrap she’d found tucked in a melted ice crevice the previous winter, its tiny punched holes holding fragments of a tune settlers had played at naming ceremonies. [119]
She’d spent months researching the artifact, learning that each punched hole corresponded to a transcribed memory, the tune’s notes tied directly to the phonetic sounds of the settlers’ given names, encoded for the organ to play aloud when fed through its pins. [161]
Elara was known across the territory for being technically gentle, her hands steady enough to carve minuscule details into wax casts without breaking even the thinnest edges, a skill she knew would be critical for the task ahead. [199]
Her method for pulling the fragmented memories from the card and fixing them permanently into a cast that could play the names on its own involved reconfiguring the braids of a centuries-old ceremonial rope she’d inherited from her grandmother, each fiber woven from dried arctic grass dyed with settler berry juice. [250]
The rope, when braided to match the pattern of holes on the card scrap, would illuminate the aurora’s faint magnetic energy trapped in the sanctum’s ice walls, converting that light into sound waves that would resonate through the wax as she poured it into her mold. [296]
The air in the sanctum hummed with that quiet, eager snowmelt ardor, the kind that makes icicles drip at midday even when the air still holds a sharp chill, hope seeping through cold long held stagnant. [332]
Elara laid the card scrap flat on her ice workbench, running a finger over the punched holes as she mouthed the half-remembered name fragments she’d decoded from the tune’s snippets, soft sounds that felt like frost melting on her tongue. [372]
She unspooled the ceremonial rope, its three thick braids frayed at the ends but still strong, and began separating the fibers, rebraiding them in a pattern that matched the spacing of the holes on the scrap, each cross of fiber corresponding to a note, each gap to a pause between syllables of a name. [426]
Her technically gentle touch kept the fragile fibers from snapping even as she pulled the braids tight to match the exact dimensions of the scrap, her breath fogging in the cold as she worked, the aurora swirling brighter above the sanctum’s ice ceiling as if responding to her careful work. [476]
When the last braid was tied off, she laid the reconfigured rope across the punched card scrap, and as she’d hoped, the faint glow of the aurora began to illuminate the rope’s fibers, turning the berry-dyed grass a soft, glowing pink that cast warm light across the ice walls. [525]
She could hear the first soft notes of the tune hum up through the rope, the transcribed memories rising to the surface, names she’d never heard spoken aloud wrapping around her like a warm shawl, each syllable clear and bright, full of the lives the settlers had built on the frozen land. [577]
She poured her pre-warmed beeswax slowly into the circular mold she’d carved earlier, letting the liquid wax settle around the rope and the card scrap, trapping the hum of the names inside as it cooled and hardened, the snowmelt ardor in her chest growing warmer with every second. [625]
When the wax had fully set, she pulled the cast from the mold, running her thumb over its smooth surface, and when she tapped it lightly with her casting tool, the sound of a dozen forgotten names spilled out, clear and loud enough to carry through the sanctum and out into the frozen valley beyond. [680]
She smiled, knowing she’d finally fulfilled the promise she’d made to her grandmother years before, the guilt that had weighed on her for a decade fully melted away, replaced by the quiet joy of returning lost voices to the community that had loved them. [724]
