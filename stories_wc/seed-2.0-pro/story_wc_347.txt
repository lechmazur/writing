Lila kicked a loose stone across the mossy flagstones of the cliffside labyrinth patrolled by clinking mechanical birds, her boots scuffing the frayed edge of the cursed apple orchard she’d been stuck tending for three years after her grandmother’s will. [40]
She’d never asked for the post, never wanted the weight of guiding lost hikers who strayed off the coastal trail into the labyrinth’s twisting paths before the night frost forms to ice over the narrow ledges and send them tumbling into the surf 100 feet below. [86]
Her grandmother’s old notes had mentioned a forgotten blueprint for a set of stone markers that could point travelers to the orchard’s safe central clearing without her having to patrol the paths every evening, but Lila had written it off as a fairy tale until last week, when she’d dug a small, dented bronze nut out of the orchard’s frost-damp soil. [147]
The nut fit perfectly into the notch at the base of the first stone slab she’d hauled to the labyrinth’s entrance, and she’d quickly realized her grandmother’s offhandedly exact measurements scrawled in the margins of the old journal lined up exactly with the gaps between the labyrinth’s weathered stone walls. [197]
She only had two hours left before the frost settled, and she needed to assemble the full set of eight markers before the first lost hiker of the night stumbled into the labyrinth’s dead-end paths that dropped straight off the cliff edge. [239]
The mechanical birds patrolling the labyrinth whirred overhead, their brass wings catching the golden sunset, and Lila had learned early on that their sensors responded only to physical touch, so she could place the markers by leaving impressions without touching the surrounding stone, pressing only the nut against the slab to etch the glowing directional runes into its surface. [298]
It was messy work, the runes lopsided and the markers wedged crookedly into the gaps between walls, but there was a graceless beauty to the way they glowed soft amber against the gray stone, no fancy craftsmanship needed to point a scared traveler toward warmth and safety. [345]
She raced from one junction to the next, the small nut warm in her palm, double-checking each measurement against her grandmother’s scrawled notes, the offhandedly exact numbers fitting so neatly into the labyrinth’s layout she wondered if her grandmother had built the maze herself decades earlier. [391]
The mechanical birds circled lower as the sky turned pink and purple, their tiny camera lenses glinting, but they didn’t trigger their warning alarms, because Lila never brushed against the stone walls, only pressing the nut to each marker slab to leave the glowing impression before moving on. [439]
When she reached the final junction 20 minutes before the first frost was due, she fumbled for the last slab, fitting the small nut into its pre-drilled notch, the final piece of the forgotten blueprint clicking into place as the full trail of glowing runes lit up from the labyrinth entrance all the way to the orchard’s clearing. [497]
She leaned against the gnarled trunk of the oldest cursed apple tree to catch her breath, watching as a group of three lost college students spotted the first glowing rune and followed the path, their laughs carrying through the maze as they avoided the deadly cliffside drops without even knowing how close they’d come to disaster. [553]
For the first time since she’d inherited the orchard, Lila didn’t resent her role as its reluctant caretaker, because the graceless beauty of the lopsided, roughly etched markers did more to keep travelers safe than any perfectly polished sign ever could. [594]
The first wisp of frost curled off the stone at her feet right as the students stumbled into the clearing, rubbing their cold hands and calling out thanks to whoever had left the glowing path for them. [631]
She tucked the small nut into her jacket pocket, making a mental note to follow the rest of her grandmother’s offhandedly exact notes to add more markers for stormy days, when even the glowing runes might be hard to spot through rain or fog. [675]
The mechanical birds perched on the labyrinth walls, their whirring slowing as the temperature dropped, and Lila smiled, knowing she didn’t have to race the frost every night anymore, because the marker system she’d assembled from the forgotten blueprint would keep travelers safe for years to come. [722]
