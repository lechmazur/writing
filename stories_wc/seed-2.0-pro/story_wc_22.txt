The gloom-hill coachman stepped onto the blooming jetty at twilight, his scuffed leather boots squelching over salt-kissed stone tangled with pink sea thrift and sea lavender. [26]
Three years had passed since the nearshore reef died, its once-vibrant coral bleached to bone white that crumbled to fine sand under every incoming tide. [53]
He slung a frayed burlap sack from his shoulder, its contents a carefully curated collection of dried seed pods he had gathered from every coastal meadow within fifty miles of Gloom Hill. [85]
Raised by a lighthouse keeper who studied lost coastal cultures, he spoke dead languages no living scholar could parse, their soft, guttural cadences fitting the quiet hush of the shore at dusk. [117]
This stretch of shore had once been a battlefield, where two fishing clans had fought over fishing grounds a century prior, their anger and grief settling into a submerged concord that kept even hardy shore plants from taking root in the sand above the sunken battle debris. [164]
His sole mission, the reason he drove his rickety coach across muddy moors and rain-slicked coastal roads every month, was to let a garden reclaim a battlefield, to turn the site of old pain into something that fed local wildlife and passing travelers alike. [208]
The air held that strange narrow vastness specific to coastal twilight, where the horizon bled into the sea so completely you could not tell where sky ended and water began, the whole world feeling both small enough to hold in your palm and infinite enough to swallow every old hurt. [258]
He knew he could not just scatter the seeds and hope they grew, so he had learned to fertilize the cursed ground by understanding bird calls, translating the trills and warbles of shore birds that carried memories of the land before the bloodshed. [301]
The dead languages he spoke aligned perfectly with the structure of the birds’ calls, allowing him to weave their shared memories of the land’s gentle past into the very soil as he broke open each seed pod and scattered its contents across the damp sand. [345]
Since the reef died, the shore birds had grown more frequent in their visits, their calls carrying more vivid memories of the days when schools of silver fish darted through the reef’s crevices and families picnicked on the sand without fear of conflict. [388]
Tonight, he could feel the submerged concord shifting under his feet, the old anger seeping away as the bird calls wrapped around the soil like a warm blanket, the seeds drinking in the shared memories as soon as they touched the ground. [431]
He had come to this jetty twelve times before, each trip leaving him sore and discouraged as the seeds he scattered washed away with the next high tide, no sign of growth to show for his hours of careful work. [470]
But tonight was different, and he knew it as soon as he heard the first trill of a plover that carried memories of the old clan peace treaties, its call weaving seamlessly with the words of the dead language he murmured under his breath. [514]
By the time he had emptied the last of the seed pods, small green shoots were already pushing through the sand, their tiny leaves unfurling toward the fading pink and orange light of the twilight sky, proof that his work was finally taking hold. [558]
He sat on a weathered jetty piling for a few minutes, watching the shoots grow, and smiled, knowing that the narrow vastness of this shore would soon hold a garden thick with wildflowers and berry bushes, a place where children could run and birds could nest, no trace of the old battlefield left for future generations to find. [616]
When he stood to leave, he left a small handful of extra seed pods tucked into a crack in the jetty stone, a gift for the next traveler who might stumble upon the growing garden and wonder how it came to be on a shore that had once known nothing but grief. [668]
