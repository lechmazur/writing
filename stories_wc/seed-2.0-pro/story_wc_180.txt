Marnie, the endearingly feisty quartz-eye mender, picked her way up the rotting stone steps of the abandoned cathedral three weeks after the guild breached its contract to let her access the rare light-root apple saplings growing in the rafter orchard. [40]
The guild had claimed the orchard’s magical stores were too dangerous for unlicensed use, but Marnie had never cared much for rules that barred people from healing magic just because they couldn’t pay membership dues. [75]
She’d come to the orchard, which spanned every exposed rafter and arched ceiling beam like a lush, fruit-heavy tapestry, to honor the deciduous vows her grandmother had first taught her as a child. [108]
The vows, unwritten and passed only between menders, held that every magical gift given must fade and renew with the seasons, no more permanent than an oak’s leaves, so no healer could ever hold power over the people they served. [148]
Her motivation was simple: to bridge generational wisdom by teaching the vows to the 16-year-old apprentice she’d taken on last spring, the daughter she’d only recently reconnected with after years of estrangement. [180]
When she hauled herself over the final ledge to the first row of fruit trees, she realized she’d left her usual vials and grafting tools at home, so she’d have to persist using forgotten bag contents she hadn’t touched in almost a decade. [223]
She rummaged through the frayed canvas sack at her hip, pulling out crumpled receipts, a half-eaten peppermint, a chipped quartz lens, and her daughter’s hair ribbon, the frayed blue satin one the girl had worn to her first day of primary school before Marnie had left town. [270]
At first she thought the ribbon was useless, until she remembered that the light-root apple cuttings needed to be tied to host branches with a material that held sentimental value, a detail even the guild had erased from its official manuals decades prior. [313]
The accidental wisdom of forgetting her tools hit her all at once: she’d been so focused on proving the guild wrong that she’d nearly forgotten the vows’ core tenet, that connection and care always mattered more than formal, licensed tools. [353]
She clipped three small cuttings from the healthiest light-root trees with the sharp edge of her chipped quartz lens, tying each one to a common apple rootstock she’d tucked into her bag that morning, using the hair ribbon cut into three equal strips to secure the grafts. [400]
Each strip would fade as the graft took root, falling away at the end of the growing season just as the deciduous vows dictated, a visible reminder that no gift, no healing, no knowledge could ever be hoarded permanently. [439]
When she climbed back down the cathedral steps as the sun dipped below the horizon, she carried the three grafted saplings carefully against her chest, already planning how she would show her daughter how to tend them when she got home. [480]
Her daughter would learn that the quartz eyes Marnie mended for people with damaged magical sight didn’t hold power on their own, but drew their strength from the connection between the mender, the recipient, and the natural world around them. [520]
She would teach her that the breach of contract had been a gift in disguise, because it had forced her to set aside her pride and rely on the little, forgotten things that held far more meaning than any guild-issued tool or official seal of approval. [566]
A week later, when she and her daughter planted the saplings in their backyard community garden, the first small leaves were already unfurling, and the blue ribbon strips were just starting to fray at the edges, softening as they merged with the bark of the rootstock. [612]
Marnie’s daughter asked if they could get a new ribbon once the old one fell away, and Marnie laughed, telling her they would use whatever meaningful trinket they had on hand at the start of the next growing season, no special supplies required. [655]
That evening, as they sipped warm apple cider on their porch, Marnie realized she’d already done what she set out to do: she’d passed on the core of the deciduous vows not through lectures or formal lessons, but through the messy, unplanned act of showing up, making do, and loving loudly. [706]
She’d never planned to stumble on the perfect lesson that day, but that was the magic of the deciduous vows: they always bloomed best when you stopped trying to force them, and let them grow where they were planted. [745]
