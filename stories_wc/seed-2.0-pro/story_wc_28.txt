Three weeks after the great fire turned the old city’s minarets to ash and streaked the dome of the sky with permanent, cracked shards of smoke, Kaelin knelt in the dust of the ancient plaza, her polished steel prosthetic arms glinting faint blue in the weird, filtered light. [48]
A known gentle radical who’d spent ten years advocating for displaced religious communities before the fire, she’d come to the plaza chasing a rumor that a single intact tessera from the northern minaret’s iconic mosaic had survived the blaze. [87]
She was retrocognitively trapped, cursed to relive the final moments of any object she touched for hours after contact, a condition that had left her avoiding personal possessions for years but made her perfectly suited to this task. [125]
Her fingers brushed the raised, chipped patterns on a mosaic stepping stone half-buried in ash, and the world tilted, forcing her to watch as the fire swept through the minaret, the imam clutching the small cobalt tessera to his chest as he shepherded a group of orphaned children out of the burning building. [178]
Kaelin had spent months designing her steel prosthetics specifically to cradle empathy in arms of steel, to hold the weight of others’ grief without breaking, and when the vision faded, she dug through the ash with careful, unflinching fingers until she found the tiny, smooth tessera tucked under the stone. [228]
The surviving families of the fire had demanded the city rename the plaza after the general who’d ordered the fire suppression effort, but Kaelin knew an aberrant mercy was needed here, one that honored the uncelebrated dead rather than the powerful who’d arrived too late to save most of the neighborhood. [279]
The cold weight of the steel in her arms contrasted with the warm, glowing memory of the imam’s kindness she’d pulled from the stepping stone, a warm coldness that settled in her chest as she made her way to the city council’s temporary outpost at the edge of the ruins. [329]
She presented the tessera to the council, walking them through the vision she’d seen, describing the twenty-seven children the imam had saved before he’d collapsed from smoke inhalation just steps outside the plaza, his final act tucking the tessera under the stepping stone so someone would remember what had really mattered that day. [383]
The council members hesitated at first, arguing that naming the plaza after an ordinary imam would set an odd precedent, but Kaelin brought the surviving children, now housed in a temporary shelter, to the next meeting, each carrying a small shard of mosaic they’d found in the plaza ruins. [432]
Six weeks later, the council voted unanimously to rename the plaza Imam Karim’s Square, the small cobalt tessera set into the new stone plaque at the entrance, framed by the shards the children had collected, the chipped patterns of the old stepping stone reproduced around the edges as a reminder of the story locked in the ruins. [489]
Kaelin stood at the edge of the plaza for the dedication, her steel arms folded across her chest, the faint cold of the metal seeping through her thin jacket even as the warm laughter of the surviving children washed over her, the perfect echo of the warm coldness that had guided her every step of the way. [546]
She knew the retrocognitive flashes would come for hours that night, that she would relive the imam’s final moments over and over, but it was a small price to pay for the aberrant mercy of centering ordinary goodness over institutional acclaim, of giving the grieving community a name to hold onto instead of a reminder of how long help had taken to arrive. [609]
When a small girl who’d been one of the children the imam saved ran over to press a lollipop into her steel palm, Kaelin smiled, her heart light, knowing she had fulfilled the promise she’d made to herself when she’d first had the prosthetics built: to cradle empathy in arms of steel, to carry the stories no one else would hold, until the world was ready to listen. [677]
Above her, the shattered sky streaked pink and orange as the sun set, the minaret tessera glinting in the plaque below, a tiny point of light for everyone who passed through to see, a reminder that mercy often comes in the most unexpected of forms. [722]
