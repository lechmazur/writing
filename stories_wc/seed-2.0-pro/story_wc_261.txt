The air held the meteor hush she had grown used to in the three months since the celestial object, meant to be a miracle curing the blighted crops of a village 30 miles west, had crashed through her tower’s roof instead, a displaced miracle that rewired her brain to be accidentally telepathic with plants. [82]

The ivy curling up the tower’s stone walls hummed soft encouragement in the back of her mind, its green tendrils glowing faintly with residual meteor dust as she pushed herself upright, her wrist throbbing dully where the bandage had slipped loose. [180]
On the workbench beside her sat the frosted hourglass, its glass coated in fine meteor dust from the crash, its sand frozen mid-fall until she activated a memory dive. [214]
She reached for the half-empty mug of black coffee she had left out the night before, tilting it to let a single drop fall onto the raised, pale line of her wrist scar, where it spread and dried in under a minute into a series of faint, swirling rings. [265]
Her method worked by examining coffee rings, the unique swirls of each dried drop mapping directly to the hidden emotional threads woven into the tissue of every scar, each line a fragment of the memory that had caused the injury. [308]
She leaned in, squinting at the tiny ridges and whorls of the rings on her wrist, and felt the familiar tug in her chest as she began to tunnel into the memory she had spent half her life running from. [349]

She was seven years old again, standing on the tower’s front step, holding a crumpled drawing of a flower she had made for her mother, who was loading a canvas bag into the back of a beat-up pickup truck without looking back. [442]
She had run after the truck when it pulled away, tripping over a loose stone in the driveway and slicing her wrist open on a shard of broken glass, the pain sharp and hot enough to drown out the sound of her own screams. [485]
