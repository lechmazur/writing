Jax, a reckless abandoned-mill explorer with a religiously skeptical streak, huddled in the mouth of an overgrown abandoned train tunnel, riding the edge of drowsy alertness as the hush following his ex-exploration partner’s sudden confession hung thick in the damp air. [41]
Ten minutes prior, Mia had shown up unannounced, drunk on cheap whiskey, and admitted she’d been the one to jostle the beaded valve wheel at the Westbrook Cotton Mill during their 2020 trespass, the mistake that led to the pressurized pipe burst that drowned Jax’s 14-year-old brother Ty, who’d snuck along for the adventure. [95]
For three years, Jax had carried the blame entirely, swallowing the truth of his supposed negligence so deeply he’d forgotten how to breathe easy, and his sole motivation now was to find the weight of that swallowed truth, to learn if his years of self-flagellation had been for nothing. [144]
The local legend of the Westbrook Mill’s factory ghost shift had always felt like cheap small-town lore to him, the unsubstantiated claim that the 17 workers killed in the 1978 boiler fire still showed up for their overnight shifts, tending to rusted equipment and turning valve wheels long after the plant shuttered. [196]
But a week prior, he’d stumbled on a trick to appraise the validity of claims tied to the mill, one that felt far more credible to his skeptical mind than any seance or ghost hunt: by interpreting frantic calls to talk-radio psychics from locals who swore they’d had run-ins with the ghost shift crew. [250]
He’d spent the last three nights scrolling through archived episodes of the local late-night psychic call-in show, sorting through hundreds of rambling calls to pick out patterns specific to the beaded valve wheel, the only one in the mill etched with tiny glass beads by a 1970s line worker as a good luck charm for his pregnant wife. [308]
The calls that described the wheel being twisted hard to the left, its beads clicking like nervous knuckles even when no wind passed through the mill’s collapsed walls, lined up exactly with Mia’s confession that she’d twisted it that far when she’d tripped over a loose pipe on the night Ty died. [360]
He’d also flagged 19 separate calls from former mill workers who said the ghost shift crew would linger by that specific wheel for hours, as if trying to turn it back to the closed position to stop a disaster they could sense coming. [403]
His drowsy alertness sharpened as he pulled the crumpled transcript of the most frantic call he’d found from his jacket pocket, a sobbing woman who’d snuck into the mill two days after Ty died to leave flowers for the ghost shift workers, who she’d grown up hearing stories about from her grandfather. [455]
She’d told the radio psychic she’d heard a boy’s laugh near the valve, and watched as the ghost shift workers all stepped back from the wheel at once, as if the disaster they’d been trying to stop had finally, irreversibly happened. [496]
Jax had never believed in ghosts, in psychics, in any higher power that could rewrite the past, but when he cross-referenced the timestamp of that call with the police report for Ty’s death, they matched exactly, down to the minute. [536]
When he appraised all the collected call data against Mia’s confession, the conclusion was unavoidable: the blame he’d carried for three years, the swallowed truth that had felt like a 100-pound weight chained to his chest, had never been his to hold at all. [580]
A distant drip of water in the tunnel pulled him back to the present, where Mia was still waiting for a response, her shoulders hunched, her eyes glistening with unshed tears. [611]
He didn’t yell, didn’t storm off, just held up the transcript and told her what he’d learned, how the ghost shift he’d never believed in had given him the proof he needed to finally let go of the weight he’d been carrying for far too long. [657]
His religiously skeptical core still refused to believe in ghosts or psychic powers, of course, but he didn’t need to believe in the supernatural to recognize that the patterns in the calls, aligned with Mia’s confession, were enough to confirm what he’d secretly suspected for months: he wasn’t at fault. [707]
As he stepped out of the tunnel into the cool gray dawn, he felt lighter than he had in years, the swallowed truth that had once felt heavy enough to drown him now weighing almost nothing at all. [745]
