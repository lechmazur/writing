The mist clung to salt-kissed rock and damp pine boughs on the breezy islet after rain, three weeks after the severance that had left Elias, the slow-spoken bonsai tutor, adrift without the partner he’d tended miniature trees beside for twelve years. [41]
He’d come here chasing the tides of remembrance, old local tales that claimed the shoreline held fragments of words left unsaid, if you knew how to sift for them. [70]
His only tools were the gnarled, half-dead juniper he’d brought from their shared greenhouse, a ghost ship’s compass he’d found tucked in his partner’s workbench drawer after they’d parted, and a scrap of antique lace his grandmother had left him, stitched with hidden care instructions for both plants and long-buried grief. [121]
The air hummed with a rhythmically placid energy, the kind that seeped into your bones and made you slow down, speak softer, notice the way wave foam caught the pale midday sun like scattered beads of glass. [158]
Elias had long been the quiet, deliberate type, his bonsai lessons delivered in slow, measured phrases that never rushed, and he knew better than to force what would only reveal itself in its own time, including the color of unspoken apologies he’d carried in his chest since he’d walked out without explaining how afraid he’d been of losing their partnership to his own terminal illness diagnosis. [224]
He’d first heard of the method to diffuse that heavy, unspoken regret through instructions woven into an antique lace pattern, the same one his grandmother had stitched into the scrap he carried, when he’d been a teen helping her tend her own bonsai collection on this very islet. [272]
He set the juniper on a flat rock at the tide line, laid the lace flat on the soil around its base, and held the ghost ship’s compass in his palm, watching its needle spin wild, unmoored to any magnetic north, drawn instead to the pull of memory and unfinished business. [323]
The tides of remembrance lapped at his boots, carrying faint snippets of the fights they’d had, the quiet mornings drinking tea while they pruned new growth, the way his partner had held him when his first test results had come back, before Elias had let fear get the better of him and lied about falling out of love. [381]
A cerulean regret pooled in the hollows of the rock at his feet, bright as the hull of the fishing boat his partner had grown up on, soft as the sweater he’d left folded on their kitchen counter the day he’d moved out, and Elias felt the weight in his chest lighten just a fraction, like a knot coming loose after years of being pulled tight. [447]
He followed the stitched instructions in the lace, pinching off dead juniper fronds in time with the rhythmically placid lap of the waves, whispering the apology he’d never said out loud, admitting he’d left only to spare his partner the pain of watching him waste away, not because he didn’t love him more than anything in the world. [505]
The compass needle slowed, then pointed steady toward the mainland, where he knew his partner was still tending their greenhouse, still leaving a mug of his favorite chamomile tea out every morning just in case, and the cerulean hue of the pooled regret seeped into the lace stitches, then into the juniper’s new growth, turning the tiny tip of a fresh frond that exact soft, bright blue. [572]
He’d found it, the color of unspoken apologies, soft and bright and alive, not the cold, dead gray he’d always feared it would be, and he knew the process to diffuse his regret had worked, the old lace’s hidden magic doing exactly what his grandmother had promised it would, for anyone brave enough to speak their truth out loud to the tides. [634]
He wrapped the lace around the juniper’s pot, slipped the compass into his jacket pocket, and booked the next ferry back to the mainland, his pace slow but steady, no longer afraid to say out loud what he’d hidden for so long, no longer weighted down by the guilt of the lie he’d told to protect the person he loved most. [695]
When he knocked on their greenhouse door an hour later, his partner standing there with tears in his eyes, Elias held up the juniper with its cerulean frond tip and said he was sorry, finally, the words coming easy now that he knew their color was soft, forgiving, and entirely his to give. [748]
