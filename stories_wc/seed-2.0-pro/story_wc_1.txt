The luminous glass artist huddled on a wind-scoured ledge of the quiet canyon under stars, still trembling three days after surviving the attempt on her life that left her studio a heap of melted silica and ash. [37]
She’d fled to this remote stretch of red rock, a place she’d hiked as a teen, because she couldn’t bear the sympathy of neighbors who didn’t know half the secrets she’d tucked into the layered glass panels she sold at regional fairs. [79]
The only thing she saved from the fire was a tattered charcoal smuggler’s ledger strip she’d found tucked in a box of discarded stained glass scrap six months prior, its edges singed but its faint, smudged markings still visible along the grain of the thick paper. [125]
That strip was a scorched testament to the illegal operations that had been running through the canyon for years, operations she’d accidentally photographed on a hike two months before, leading the men behind it to target her home and work. [165]
She’d always been steadily adaptive, adjusting her glass designs when supply chains broke, reworking broken panels into sun catchers for local kids, so she refused to let this attack break the quiet rhythm she’d built for herself. [202]
Her main drive now wasn’t revenge, or even rebuilding her studio right away; it was to discover purpose in the pauses, the empty spaces between what she’d planned and what had just happened, that she’d always ignored when rushing to meet commission deadlines. [245]
At dawn, she planned to retrace the path she’d taken that day she took the photos, to find the drop spots marked on the ledger strip, and figure out how to turn what the smugglers saw as a threat into something that could protect the canyon she loved. [293]
She’d mapped the first two coordinates already using patterns in morning frost, the delicate, lacy lines that formed on the flat rock ledges overnight matching the odd, curved markings on the ledger strip that she’d first written off as random smudges. [334]
There was a drifting resonance to the way the pieces were falling into place, as if the canyon itself was guiding her, the same way light through glass reveals hidden layers of color you can’t see when you hold the panel in your hand. [378]
She pulled her wool shawl tighter around her shoulders, staring up at the dense spray of constellations spilling across the black sky, and thought about the glass panel she’d been working on when the fire started, a layered piece of the canyon at dusk, embedded with bits of charcoal she’d collected on her hikes. [432]
She’d wondered at the time why the charcoal felt heavier, denser than the wildfire residue she usually collected, and now she realized it was the smuggled, industrial grade product they were moving through the canyon to avoid interstate taxes and environmental regulations. [474]
Her steadily adaptive nature kicked in again as she sketched a new design in the dust at her feet, a series of small glass markers etched with the same frost patterns she’d matched to the ledger, each one marking a drop spot that she could alert park rangers to without leading the smugglers back to her. [530]
She’d already decided she wouldn’t press charges against the men who’d burned her studio, not because she was afraid, but because she wanted the purpose she found in these quiet pauses to be rooted in care for the canyon, not anger at people who’d chosen a harmful path out of desperation. [581]
When the first pale light of dawn seeped over the canyon rim, she tucked the scorched testament of the ledger strip into the inner pocket of her jacket, laced up her worn hiking boots, and started to retrace the path she’d taken months before, her eyes scanning the rock ledges for the familiar patterns in morning frost that would lead her to the next drop spot. [647]
The drifting resonance of the canyon wrapped around her as she walked, the quiet rustle of sagebrush and distant call of a raven reminding her that she didn’t need a fully built studio to make art that mattered; she only needed the land she loved, and the quiet purpose she’d found in the pause between her old life and the new one she was building step by step. [715]
