When clocks run sideways, the frayed edges of reality bleed into the spectral grotto under starlight, where Jessa, an ardent ferry deckhand, spends her off-shift hours sorting through half-forgotten passenger trinkets. [31]
She is resiliently positive, even after three years of watching the same commuter ferry vanish mid-crossing every third tide, leaving no trace of crew, passengers, or the chipped blue paint that marked its hull. [65]
Her most prized find is an empty hourglass, its glass frosted with sea salt, its wooden frame carved with swirling lines that shift when she holds it up to the starlight filtering through the grotto’s porous stone ceiling. [103]
Jessa’s quiet, unspoken motivation is to merge parallel timelines, to pull the vanished ferry and all the people on it back into the world where their families still wait for them every evening. [136]
The tone of the grotto, and of Jessa’s entire life these days, is permanent transient: nothing stays the same for more than a few minutes, from the shape of the rock formations to the faint, distant chatter of passengers that echoes when the tide is high. [182]
Last week, she found a waterlogged marine radio half-buried in the grotto’s sand, its dials still turning on their own, spitting out fragments of weather reports and ship calls from decades past and decades yet to come. [219]
She quickly learned that she could send signals across timelines via interconnected patterns: the carvings on the hourglass matched the frequency markers on the radio, and the way the starlight fell through the grotto’s cracks aligned exactly with the gaps between the radio’s preset stations. [264]
Every night when clocks run sideways, she sits cross-legged on the grotto’s cold sand, turns the hourglass over even though no sand falls, and twists the radio dial to match the line of starlight currently stretching across the stone at her feet. [306]
At first, she only got static, then faint snippets of the ferry’s captain announcing stops, then the sound of a kid laughing, the same kid whose lost stuffed seal she’d found washed up on the grotto shore two months prior. [346]
Tonight, when she adjusts the dial again, a clear voice cuts through the static, the ferry’s first mate, who she’d shared morning coffee with a dozen times before the vanishing, asking if anyone can hear them. [382]
Her throat tightens, but she keeps her voice steady, following the pattern she’d mapped over months: she names each passenger she remembers, traces the hourglass carvings with one finger, and describes the starlight patterns stretching across the grotto floor to guide them home. [425]
The first mate tells her they’ve been drifting through a half-space where every direction looks like open ocean, no land in sight, no clocks that run straight, and all their passengers are calm, trusting that someone is looking for them. [465]
Jessa smiles, her resiliently positive attitude never wavering even when she’d spent weeks crying after the ferry first vanished, even when other ferry staff told her to stop looking, that the passengers were gone forever. [500]
She tells the first mate to have the passengers line the ferry’s rail, to hold onto anything with a pattern, to look for the flicker of starlight that will soon cut through the fog around their boat. [537]
Outside the grotto, the tide rises, and the empty hourglass in her hand starts to glow, the carved lines matching the frequency of the radio, matching the starlight, matching the faint glow she can now see rising above the waves at the mouth of the grotto. [583]
A moment later, the familiar chipped blue bow of the vanished commuter ferry pushes through the grotto’s mouth, the passengers cheering and waving, the first mate leaning over the rail to wave directly at Jessa where she sits on the sand. [624]
Jessa stands, tucking the hourglass into her work vest and setting the radio down gently in the sand, as the ferry docks against the grotto’s small stone pier, the passengers stepping off, already pulling out their phones to call their waiting families. [666]
She knows the permanent transient nature of this space means the ferry might vanish again someday, that clocks might run sideways again and pull them into another timeline, but she will be ready, waiting in the grotto with her hourglass and her radio, ready to follow the interconnected patterns to bring them home again.
