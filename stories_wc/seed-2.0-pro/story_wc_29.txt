Beneath the flickering fluorescent lights of the Maplewood University radio station, Elara Voss, a celestial signal interpreter, hunched over frayed astronomical charts spread across a dented mixing board, her jaw tight as she tracked the spike of unidentifiable frequencies bleeding into local airwaves. [43]
It was mid-autumn, as the community divides over rumors of cursed harvests, sudden job losses, and unexplained public outbursts that had turned once-friendly neighbors into sniping strangers over the course of three weeks. [76]
Elara had figured out three days prior that the disruptions were the work of cosmic tricksters, interdimensional entities that feed on social friction, and her sole motivation now was to outwit cosmic tricksters before the tension boiled over into outright violence. [117]
The entities’ gambit was a classic power play: flood low-level frequencies into a populated area to erode trust, feed off the resulting chaos, and gain enough tangible energy to cross fully into the mortal plane, where they could wreak havoc indefinitely. [158]
She was cautiously hopeful, her fingers tracing the constellation maps on the astronomical charts that marked the exact window the tricksters would use to solidify their hold, a 47-minute gap starting in 12 minutes when the Orionid meteor shower would peak overhead. [200]
Her plan was to interfere with their frequency feed through subsonic frequencies calibrated to match the vibration of the meteor shower’s ion trail, a counter-signal that would scramble the tricksters’ connection to the mortal plane without any human listener noticing a thing. [242]
She had spent three all-nighters cross-referencing the charts with 10 years of archived signal data from the university’s astronomy department, surviving on gas station coffee and the faint, thrumming hum of the radio tower that had been her constant companion since she’d taken the part-time engineer job two years prior. [292]
A faint, static-laced cackle cut through the monitor feed, sharp enough to make her flinch, proof the tricksters knew someone was onto their game, their power play already shifting to push back against her interference before she could even flip the switch. [334]
She pressed her palm to the cold metal of the mixing board, the faint glow of the astronomical charts’ silver ink shifting under the overhead light like stardust trapped on paper, a reminder of the hundreds of small, good lives in the town below that were counting on her, even if they didn’t know it. [389]
When the clock hit 1:17 a.m., the exact start of the window marked on her charts, she flipped the three switches that activated the subsonic frequency broadcast, leaning back in her scuffed office chair as a low, thrumming vibration hummed up through the floorboards, faint enough that only a dog two blocks away could hear it, but sharp enough to cut through the tricksters’ static. [454]
For 37 long seconds, the air in the station felt thick enough to chew, the cackle rising to a piercing shriek that made her ears bleed thin trails of blood, the tricksters throwing every last scrap of their accumulated power into one final push to break through her signal. [503]
Then, just as suddenly as the shriek had started, it cut off, leaving nothing but the soft crackle of the station’s regular late-night jazz broadcast playing through the monitors, the pressure in the room lifting so fast she gasped, her hands shaking as she wiped the blood from her cheeks. [553]
She pulled up the town’s local social media feed on her phone, her chest tightening as she saw post after post from confused neighbors apologizing to each other for uncharacteristic outbursts, asking if anyone else felt like a heavy weight had just lifted off their shoulders, the rifts that had divided the community already starting to stitch themselves back together. [613]
Elara leaned forward, tracing the edge of the astronomical charts one more time, her mouth twitching into a small smile, still cautiously hopeful that she had won this round of the cosmic power play, knowing full well the tricksters would be back, that this was only one battle in a long, quiet war waged in the spaces between radio waves and starlight. [675]
She poured herself another cup of lukewarm coffee, adjusted the radio’s broadcast dial, and settled in to wait, ready to interfere again through subsonic frequencies whenever the next signal came, the faint glow of the charts lighting up her face in the dark of the station, a guardian no one knew existed, watching over the town one frequency at a time.
