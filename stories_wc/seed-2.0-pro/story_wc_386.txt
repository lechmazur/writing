The candle-lit shrine carved into glacier ice glows with borrowed starlight, the faint, cool glow seeping through translucent ice walls to gild the edges of weathered diving gear stacked against a frozen bench. [33]
This is the home of Lira, a wreck-diver ghost whisperer who spends half her year exploring sunken cargo ships off the glacier’s frigid coast and the other half tending to the spirits trapped in the wreckage she finds. [71]
Her core motivation, one she has held close since she found her grandmother’s name etched into the hull of a 1920s passenger ship wreck, is to salvage something pure from the wreckage of lives cut short far from shore. [110]
The shrine’s ice walls are not just structural; they are windows of connection, thin enough that the dreams of people passing through the nearby coastal tourist town seep through, carrying faint echoes of the spirits Lira works to help. [149]
Lira’s preferred method of tracing unresolved spirit grievances is by analyzing cameo appearances in strangers' dreams, noting small, repeated details that do not fit the dreamer’s own life to identify which spirit is reaching out. [184]
She weaves truth carefully when she shares these findings with dreamers, never overwhelming them with stories of ghosts and shipwrecks but framing her insights as gentle guidance that honors both the living and the dead. [219]
Three weeks prior, she had pulled a melted snow globe from the submerged wreck of the Hollis, a cargo ship that sank in 1978 during a blizzard, its glass half-melted from a boiler fire that broke out as the ship went down. [261]
Inside its cracked base, she found a tiny photo of a young boy holding a lamb, the print still intact despite decades under salt water. [286]
For weeks, she spotted the same young boy as a cameo in a dozen different tourist dreams, always standing at the edge of the dreamer’s vision holding a tiny, glinting snow globe, never speaking but always pointing toward the ocean. [326]
She tracked the snow globe’s original owner to Thomas Hale, a retired sailor who now ran a small coffee shop in the tourist town, who had been seven when his father died on the Hollis, and who had given his father the snow globe as a goodbye gift the day the ship set sail. [380]
The action Lira knew she had to take was to expose the truth of what had happened to Thomas’s father, who had not drowned in the cold as official reports claimed, but had stayed behind to free three trapped crewmates, dying in the boiler fire that melted the snow globe tucked in his coat pocket. [435]
She invited Thomas to the shrine one evening, when the borrowed starlight was brightest, and laid the melted snow globe on the ice altar between them, pulling up the dream transcripts she had collected to show him the cameos of his younger self, the boy he had been when he gave his father the gift. [490]
She weaves truth carefully in this conversation too, starting with the physical proof of the snow globe before sharing the stories his father’s spirit had shared with her through the dream cameos, of his final act of courage, of how he had carried the gift with him every day of the voyage. [542]
Thomas cried for nearly an hour, holding the melted snow globe to his chest, explaining that he had spent 45 years wondering if his father had even thought of him in his final moments, if the cheap gift he had saved up three months of allowance to buy had meant anything at all. [595]
For Lira, this was exactly the pure thing she worked so hard to salvage from wreckage: not gold or lost cargo, but the quiet, loving truths that bind people across decades and even death, carried through the windows of connection that the ice shrine and the dream cameos offered. [644]
That night, as Thomas walked back down the glacier with the melted snow globe tucked safely in his coat, Lira watched a tiny, glowing spirit figure wave from the ice wall before fading away, its long-unfinished business finally complete. [683]
She blew out the last of the candles, leaving the shrine lit only by borrowed starlight, and turned to her diving log to note the case closed, another small, precious piece of goodness pulled from the cold dark of the deep. [730]
