<story>
Lila, a vivid mural artist whose work lined alleyways and community center walls across the county, was quietly driven by a question she had carried since she was seven, when she found a moonflower unfurling in her grandmother’s garden long after the streetlights flickered on: why do flowers bloom at night? [51]
She set up her easel and paint crates in the sun-warmed amber pavilion at the edge of the city’s botanical garden in early autumn, intending to stay across the melting of winter’s last snow until she found her answer. [90]
Her most prized possession, the only thing she kept on the pavilion’s splintered wooden table beside her water jars, was a collection of old postcards her grandmother had sent her in the years before she passed, each one printed with a photo of a nocturnal bloom and a single line of messy, looping handwriting on the back. [147]
The final postcard, sent two weeks before her grandmother’s death, had no flower photo, only a faded snapshot of the stained-glass window of the old cathedral downtown, with the line: “The answer is where the light doesn’t only shine by day.” [188]
As the days shortened and maple leaves crumbled to rust underfoot, Lila visited the cathedral every Wednesday, leaning against the cool stone ledge below the west window to reckon with the puzzle her grandmother had left her, following her advice to seek meaning through the hidden layers of etched glass in a cathedral window. [242]
The glass was thick, pocked with tiny bubbles from centuries of aging, and when she held her phone’s flashlight up to it after dark, when the cathedral was empty, she could make out faint etched outlines of moonflowers, evening primrose, and night-blooming jasmine tucked between the more prominent depictions of saints and biblical scenes. [296]
Winter wrapped the city in thin, sharp ice soon after, and Lila kept to the pavilion most days, painting small studies of night blooms next to her stack of postcards, her fingers stiff around her brush as she waited for the last frost to lift. [341]
The dawning hush she had come to associate with the moments between night and dawn, when the world was still wrapped in half-light and even the wind held its breath, settled over the pavilion on the morning the last of the winter snow softened into slush and dripped off the pavilion’s amber roof tiles. [395]
That afternoon, she walked back to the cathedral, the last of the postcards tucked in her coat pocket, and held it up to the window where the low, golden spring sun slanted through the glass, catching all the hidden etched layers at once. [438]
What she saw made her chest feel soft and tight all at once, an umbral tenderness wrapping around her heart like the fuzzy velvet of a moonflower bud right before it unfurls: the etched blooms lined up perfectly with the sun’s slant to cast tiny, glowing flower-shaped shadows on the stone floor, each one bright enough to look like it was blooming even in the dim cathedral light. [506]
She realized then that night-blooming flowers didn’t grow for the sun, or for the crowds that wandered the botanical garden by day; they grew for the quiet, for the people who stayed up late grieving, for the insomniacs, for the ones who needed something soft to look at when the rest of the world was asleep. [562]
Her grandmother, who had spent the last two years of her life unable to sleep through the night from chronic pain, had known exactly what those flowers were for, had hidden the answer in plain sight both in the cathedral window and in the postcards she’d sent Lila all those years. [613]
When Lila went back to the amber pavilion that evening, she pulled out her largest canvas and started sketching the outline of a moonflower spanning its entire width, surrounded by tiny postcard-sized drawings of the etched cathedral blooms, each one glowing softly against a dark blue background. [660]
She knew exactly who this mural was for: the people who wandered through the world in the quiet dark, who thought no one had made anything beautiful just for them, who needed a reminder that softness could exist even when the sun wasn’t shining. [704]
As the first faint stars pricked the darkening sky above the pavilion, she smiled, knowing her grandmother would have loved every stroke of paint she laid down that night. [733]
