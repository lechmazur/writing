The migrating geese had paused mid-flight over the city of forgotten fountains, their wings held frozen in the soft indigo dusk as if captured by a photographer’s long exposure. [29]
Elara, the luminous skeptic, leaned against the cracked rim of a dry fountain ringed with rusted iron lion heads, her pale silver glow cutting through the faint mist that curled over the cobblestones. [62]
She was realistically imaginative, the kind of person who knew ghosts were just echoes of unprocessed grief but still left out small offerings for the strays that haunted the city’s empty alleyways. [94]
Her mission, from the moment the geese had frozen in the sky, was to liberate trapped memories locked away by the city’s long-dead builders, who had hidden them to keep residents from mourning the fountains that dried up a century prior. [135]
She moved in a defiant hush, her boots making no sound against the stone, refusing to announce her presence to the old protective spells that hummed just beneath the surface of every weathered wall and empty fountain basin. [173]
She had learned years ago that the only way to unstick the city’s locked truths was by following breadcrumbs: tiny shards of stained glass, half-buried pennies, and faint water stains that only her glow could make visible against the old stone. [214]
The trail led her to the largest fountain in the city’s central square, its basin overgrown with ivy and its central statue of a water nymph cracked down the middle, one hand outstretched as if still reaching for a stream that would never come. [258]
Tucked into the nymph’s outstretched palm was the stained glass box, no bigger than her fist, its panels depicting constellations that shifted and glittered even in the faint light of her glow. [290]
She had spent years studying sidereal patience, the practice of aligning one’s actions with the slow, unyielding rhythm of the stars, rather than the hurried pace of human life, and she knew this was the exact moment the box’s lock would yield. [332]
She brushed the ivy off the box’s surface, her fingers brushing the tiny latch shaped like a goose’s wing, and it clicked open without resistance, as if it had been waiting for her all along. [367]
A flood of soft, glowing memories poured out: children chasing fireflies around splashing fountains, elders sharing cool glasses of lemonade on stone benches, lovers carving their initials into the wet concrete of newly installed fountain rims. [403]
She had expected a grand reaction, a burst of light or a roar of sound, but instead the memories drifted like dandelion seeds across the city, seeping into the cracks of the dry fountains, settling into the pockets of sleeping residents, warming the cold stone under her feet. [451]
Above her, the geese began to move again, their wings beating soft and steady as they resumed their northward journey, as if the pause had only been to give her enough time to finish her work. [487]
By dawn, the first trickle of water would start to flow from the lion heads of the fountain behind her, and by midday, every forgotten fountain in the city would be running again, clear and cold, filled with the laughter and stories that had been locked away for a hundred years. [538]
The city’s residents would wake with faint, warm memories they could not quite place, but they would find themselves drawn to the fountains, bringing their own stories to add to the flow, rebuilding the community that the old builders had feared would break if they grieved their lost water. [587]
Elara smiled, her silver glow dimming to a soft spark as she tucked the empty stained glass box into her jacket pocket, already planning her next journey to a city that had locked away its own truths. [624]
She knew that some truths could not be rushed, and that the slow, quiet work of returning memories was far more powerful than any loud, flashy spell could ever be. [654]
As she walked away from the square, she left a single crumb of stained glass by the fountain’s edge, for the next person who might need a trail to follow. [684]
