The bedraggled prince perched on the rusted iron gantry of the suspended palace, anchored to glistening giant stalactites that dripped mineral slime onto the smog-choked mechanical world 3,000 feet below. [30]
For three years he had wandered the scrap yards and forgotten vaults of his father’s clockwork kingdom, his only drive to reawaken beauty in a mechanical world that had erased all living things to maximize industrial efficiency. [67]
His method of hypothesizing about everything had gotten him exiled from the royal court, where engineers dismissed his questions about pre-industrial life as dangerous, unproductive nonsense. [93]
This stretch of his quest, through the discovery of the vault tucked in the deepest crevice of the palace’s stalactite anchors, had felt less like a search and more like a memory he was finally remembering. [129]
Inside the dust-caked oak chest he pulled from the vault, wrapped in linen embroidered with the wings of the long-gone sky lark, sat the extinct bird’s egg, its shell swirled with faint blue and gold patterns that glowed when he brushed the grime away. [173]
He fell into a quaking reverie, his fingers hovering over the shell as he recalled the old lullabies his nurse had sung, of birds that filled the sky with song instead of the drone of factory exhaust fans that choked the lower cities now. [217]
His great-grandfather had built this palace and the industrial empire beneath it on the flawed genesis that nature was an obstacle to progress, not a partner worth nurturing, and every choice since had erased a little more of the world’s softness. [258]
The taste of that realization was bitterly sweet, sharp with the grief of all the lives and beauty lost, soft with the first spark of hope he had held since he was a small boy sneaking into the royal archives to read about living forests and flying creatures. [306]
Over the next three days, he began to assemble the crude incubation chamber he had designed months earlier, scavenging heat coils from broken palace heaters, tempered glass from old observation domes, and a tiny nutrient solution he had brewed from rare moss that still grew in the dark, damp cracks of the stalactites. [359]
He tested every variable twice, hypothesizing about everything from the optimal temperature to the amount of light the egg would need, cross-referencing his notes with the fragmented 200-year-old ornithology texts he had smuggled out of the archives before his exile. [399]
Each night he curled up next to the chamber, watching the egg glow faintly through the glass, wondering if his quest was foolish, if the damage done by his family’s flawed genesis was too great to ever undo. [437]
On the fourth morning, he was woken by a soft, tapping sound coming from inside the glass chamber, so quiet he almost mistook it for the distant clank of factory machinery far below. [470]
He leaned forward, his breath catching in his throat, as a tiny crack split the surface of the extinct bird’s egg, and a flash of bright blue feather peeked through the gap. [502]
The quaking reverie he had drifted into days earlier flooded back, sharper now, as he realized he was witnessing the first living, breathing thing that was not a clockwork drone or a genetically modified crop in his family’s kingdom in over a century. [545]
The feeling was bitterly sweet, as he thought of all the generations that had never gotten to hear a bird sing, but also of all the generations that would, if he could keep this small, fragile miracle alive. [583]
He already had plans to assemble a small team of like-minded outcasts, engineers and biologists who had been cast out of the court for questioning the kingdom’s obsession with mechanical efficiency, to help him breed more of the birds and reintroduce them to the smog-choked valleys below. [630]
His habit of hypothesizing about everything had led him here, to this moment, proving that even a world built on a flawed genesis could be healed, one small, beating heart at a time. [663]
He looked out over the edge of the suspended palace, watching the factory smoke curl up toward the stalactites, and smiled, knowing the first notes of bird song would soon cut through the constant mechanical hum, the first step in reawakening the beauty his family had erased so long ago. [713]
