Mara, the crestfallen portrait artist who’d not sold a canvas in three years, huddled on the dust-caked oak floor of the forgotten library inside the lighthouse, her knees pulled tight to her chest as the granite walls first began to groan inward. [42]
She’d come here chasing a rumor that the space held fragments of the loved ones she’d lost to the coastal storm six months prior, hadn’t she? [68]
The timeframe when the walls close in was supposed to be the only window where the veil between timelines thinned, where the fragmented reality of the lighthouse bent to the will of those who grieved hard enough. [105]
Beside her sat the broken tarot card box she’d pulled from a crumbling shelf ten minutes prior, its splintered cedar sides spilling iridescent silk threads woven with tiny, shifting portraits of strangers and lost loved ones alike. [142]
Who wouldn’t trade every last commission they’d ever earned for one more chance to see the face of the sister who’d vanished mid-laugh on that storm-wracked pier? [169]
Mara’s motivation was simple, even when the walls inched closer so she could feel the cold granite on her shoulders: to descend into necessary darkness, to pull her sister’s thread out of the chaos of fractured timelines, to bring her home. [210]
The lighthouse library hummed with a luminescent ache, every floating dust particle glowing soft gold with half-remembered moments: first kisses, last goodbyes, birthday cakes half-eaten on foggy porches. [238]
She’d learned from the scrawled notes tucked in the margins of the library’s leather-bound grimoires that the way to reshape what was broken was not to glue pieces back together, but to redesign the pattern they’d been stitched into in the first place. [281]
How could she have known that the portrait commissions she’d spent half her life perfecting were just practice for reweaving a life she’d thought was gone forever? [308]
The method she’d been instructed to use was straightforward, even as the walls narrowed so much she could barely stretch her arms out: by untangling threads, one by one, separating the strands that belonged to her sister from the thousands of other lives tangled in the lighthouse’s fractured reality. [357]
She wrapped her fingers around the first glowing thread, its surface shimmering with the memory of her sister at seven, covered in cake icing after their shared birthday party. [386]
Another thread held the memory of their last hike along the cliffs, when her sister had dared her to jump over a tide pool and Mara had tripped, landing in the frigid water to both their hysterical laughter. [424]
Wouldn’t anyone fight to pull those small, perfect moments back from the edge of oblivion when given the chance? [443]
As the walls closed to within a foot of her shoulders, Mara sorted through the threads, setting aside every strand that bore her sister’s face, every echo of her voice, every spark of her messy, bright joy. [480]
She took the pile of sorted threads and began to weave them into a new pattern, not the one that had ended in a storm and an empty pier, but a new one where her sister stepped off the lighthouse stairs, grinning, holding the half-empty box of tarot cards they’d bought together at a beach fair two years prior. [539]
The fragmented reality of the lighthouse rippled as she wove, the walls pausing their inward creep as the new pattern took hold, solid and bright and warm. [566]
When she finished the last stitch, the broken tarot card box at her side clicked shut on its own, its splinters mended, its contents holding a single new card with a portrait of two sisters, grinning, on its face. [605]
The luminescent ache that had filled the library for decades softened, no longer a sharp grief but a gentle glow, as Lila, Mara’s sister, sat down beside her and squeezed her hand. [637]
Isn’t that what grief always asks of us, in the end: to wade through the dark, to pick up the broken pieces, and to make something new that honors what we lost? [669]
Mara tucked the mended tarot box into her bag, then took her sister’s hand, and stepped out of the lighthouse library into the golden glow of the coastal sunset, ready to redesign the rest of their lives together. [707]
