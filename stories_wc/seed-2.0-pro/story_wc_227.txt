The uncertain alchemist hovered in the flooded lower level of her gears-and-cogs workshop floating on an acid sea, every muscle locked as she existed only between breaths held underwater. [29]
Clutched in her palm, the dented iron key ring charm glowed faint gold, its surface pocked with the same tiny, irregular craters that marred the weathered marble statue wedged between two spinning brass cogs above the waterline. [66]
For three months, the lapping acid had gnawed through the workshop’s foundation, erasing half her research, half her old life, and every last marker of the family she’d lost to the same corrosive tides ten years prior. [103]
Her kintsugi intentions had led her here, to this suspended breath, because she knew the magic that quilts memories together did not care for perfection, only for the honesty of the cracks left by grief. [138]
She had spent three days mapping every indentation on the centuries-old statue, and now, she would narrate each lost memory by matching the pockmarks on the key ring charm to the corresponding craters on the statue’s face, her only goal to conjure meaning from the edge of oblivion before the acid ate through the last of the workshop’s tethers. [197]
The air above the water hummed with the sharp, honeyed tang of magic that felt equal parts dangerous and holy, the dire enchantment wrapping around her like a shroud as she lifted the charm to the first crater, her lungs burning with the effort of holding her breath. [245]
“When I was seven, my father carved this charm for me from the hinge of the workshop’s main door, after I fell off the catwalk and scraped my knee,” she mouthed, no sound escaping, but the magic caught the words, weaving them into a thin gold thread that snaked between the charm and the statue. [300]
She moved to the next pockmark, recalling the time her mother had dropped the charm while carrying a tray of new alchemical vials, the small dent left by the falling glass a permanent marker of her mother’s sheepish smile as she apologized for days afterward. [345]
Each matching pair of pockmarks pulled another memory to the surface, gold thread piling up around the statue’s base, glinting bright against the gray marble as the workshop rocked with a particularly violent wave of acid, a spray of the corrosive liquid hitting the water inches from her face. [394]
For a split second, she faltered, the uncertain alchemist in her wondering if this was all a fool’s errand, if the magic would hold, if her memories were even worth saving when the world outside the workshop was already half dissolved into the acid sea. [439]
She squeezed the charm tighter, forcing herself to move to the final pockmark, the deepest one, left the day the tidal wave had swept her parents out into the acid sea, when she’d clung to the statue’s base for three hours waiting for help that never came. [486]
“They loved this workshop more than any place in the world,” she mouthed, the words sparking a burst of golden light that wrapped around the entire statue, every gold thread fusing the charm’s pockmarks to the statue’s craters, binding her memories to the stone so they could never be erased. [536]
The workshop stopped rocking, the acid tides pulling back from the foundation for the first time in months, and she realized she had conjured meaning from the edge of oblivion not by bringing her parents back, but by making sure their love would outlast the end of the world as she knew it. [589]
Her lungs screaming, she broke the surface of the water, gasping for air, as the statue glowed bright enough to cut through the thick, acidic fog that hung over the sea for miles around. [623]
The key ring charm was now embedded in the statue’s cheek, its pockmarks filled with the same gold that lined the cracks of every memory she had quilted together, a permanent testament that even broken, even on the edge of destruction, intention could turn loss into something unbreakable. [671]
She smiled, watching the gold light stretch out over the acid waves, knowing the dire enchantment had not just saved her workshop, but had given her a reason to keep going, even when everything else felt like it was dissolving beneath her feet. [714]
