In the hush of a hidden sunrise, Elara, an introverted clock scribe who’d spent ten years tending to the sanctuary’s ancient timepieces without ever sharing her own secret hopes, crossed the threshold of the soundless sanctuary, her lungs filling with the soft, earthy tang of recent rain. [47]
She was there to make peace with an abandoned dream, the half-finished clock chime she’d hidden in her workbench drawer two decades prior, when the sanctuary’s former head scribe had told her soft, experimental melodies had no place in a space dedicated to unchanging, unfeeling time. [93]
She followed the stone path to the crystal whispering forecourt after rain, its faceted quartz pillars glinting pink with the first slanted rays of sun that slipped over the sanctuary’s high, ivy-covered stone walls. [127]
The glacial hush of the space wrapped around her like a well-worn shawl, no sound of distant birds, no creak of floorboards from inside the sanctuary, no hum of wind through the trees outside its protective walls, just the quiet thud of her own heartbeat in her ears. [175]
She pulled the small, dented brass chime from her coat pocket, her excruciatingly gentle fingers brushing the tiny, hand-carved runes along its edge that she’d etched when she was 19, bright-eyed and sure she could make time feel soft, not just strict and unforgiving. [219]
For years she had tried to bear the weight of giving up on that dream, tucking the chime away in box after box, moving it from apartment to apartment when she left the sanctuary’s apprentice program, until last month when the new head scribe had invited her back, telling her the sanctuary now welcomed experimental timepieces. [275]
The petrichor nostalgia clinging to every damp stone around her made the memory of that first rejection feel softer, less sharp, like a bruise that had finally faded to a faint, unthreatening yellow. [308]
She held the chime up to one of the crystal pillars, letting the first full ray of hidden sunrise catch its surface, and watched as the quartz hummed to life, soft as breath, playing the exact melody she’d imagined all those years ago, light and warm and unapologetically gentle. [357]
No one else was there to hear it, no crowd to applaud or criticize, just the glacial hush of the sanctuary, the smell of wet stone and clover growing between the flagstones, and the quiet thrill of knowing she’d not been wrong to want something softer than the unyielding tick of standard clocks. [410]
She thought of all the nights she’d stayed up late in her tiny apartment, adjusting the chime’s tiny internal springs with her excruciatingly gentle touch, too afraid to let anyone hear it play, convinced she’d wasted years of her life on a silly, frivolous idea that no one would ever care about. [462]
The petrichor nostalgia wrapped tighter around her, bringing back the smell of her old apprentice workbench, the faint scratch of her scribe’s pen on parchment, the way she’d run to the crystal whispering forecourt after rain as a girl, to listen to the pillars hum with the sound of distant storms. [513]
When the melody faded, the glacial hush settled back over the soundless sanctuary, and she smiled, a small, private thing, as she realized she no longer needed to bear the weight of regret she’d carried for so long. [551]
The new head scribe had left a note for her taped to the forecourt’s central crystal, telling her there was a space for her chime in the sanctuary’s main hall, right next to the oldest grandfather clock in the collection, if she wanted to leave it there. [598]
She ran her excruciatingly gentle fingers over the chime one last time, then set it in the small iron hook mounted at the base of the central crystal, where it would catch every sunrise and hum its melody for anyone who came to the forecourt looking for a little softness in their day. [651]
As she turned to walk back toward the sanctuary’s main doors, the sun peeked fully over the wall, gilding every wet stone and crystal surface, and the petrichor nostalgia shifted from something sad to something warm, a reminder that abandoned dreams didn’t have to stay buried forever. [698]
For the first time in 20 years, she didn’t feel the urge to shrink away from attention, or to hide the parts of her work that felt too soft, too personal, too much her own. [733]
She stepped into the main hall, ready to talk to the head scribe about designing more soft, melodic timepieces, knowing she’d finally made the peace she’d come looking for that morning. [764]
