Finn, the reticent lighthouse supply skipper who only spoke when the tide turned against him, hauled his duffel up the spiral stone steps of Gull Rock Light three hours after the lamp flickered out, the salt of the autumn gale crusted in his eyebrows. [45]
He knew the lantern room was haunted by the old keeper Mara, who’d vanished in a 1978 storm while dragging a life preserver to a foundering fishing boat, but he’d never feared her, his warmly observant eyes having caught the small signs of her presence for a decade: teacups moved from windowsills, salt shakers left turned upright, no matter how he stowed them. [102]
Six months prior, he’d stumbled on the anonymous letter chain tucked into a hollow brick behind the lamp’s metal housing, notes scrawled on waterproof parchment dating back to 1912, no signatures, only warnings of rogue currents, notes of thanks for rescued fishermen, quiet laments for loved ones lost to the waves. [155]
He’d left his first note two weeks later, after pulling a teenaged cliff jumper from the frigid water, and had received a reply three days after that, tucked into the same niche, wrapped around a piece of frosted cobalt sea glass he’d later learn Mara had collected by the sackful during her 22 years as keeper. [208]
That sea glass now sat in the breast pocket of his oilskin, warm from the heat of his body, as he pushed open the rusted iron door to the lantern room, the wind howling through the cracked glass of the surrounding lens housing. [248]
His first priority was to protect the ships cutting through the dark strait, most of them small fishing boats run by families who’d worked these waters for generations, who relied on Gull Rock Light to steer them clear of the sharp, submerged reefs that jutted out half a mile from the shore. [301]
He’d already sent a message that morning via a way of stacking stones as messages, a code the letter chain participants had refined over decades, a three-stack of smooth granite rocks on the highest point of the shoreline that meant “lamp failure, repair incoming,” so any skipper passing through would know to throttle back and watch their depth finders closely. [361]
He pulled the spare gas lamp from his duffel, filled its reservoir, and struck a match, the small flame catching and blooming until it cast a soft, golden glow across the circular room, and for a second, he swore he saw the faint outline of a woman in a wool coat leaning against the lens frame, smiling. [414]
The luminescent ache that lived in his chest whenever he visited the lighthouse swelled then, half grief for the people who’d tended this light before him, half joy that he got to carry their work forward, that he got to be part of this quiet, unspoken network of care. [464]
His motivation, the quiet thing that drove him to make the 12-mile run from the mainland in even the worst storms, was to bottle the feeling of safety he’d first felt as a seven-year-old, huddled in his dad’s fishing boat cabin, watching Gull Rock Light cut through a blizzard, knowing they were going to make it home. [523]
He pulled the crumpled note he’d written that morning from his pocket, scrawled with a warning about the new sandbar that had formed at the strait’s mouth, and tucked it into the hollow brick, setting the piece of cobalt sea glass on top of it for the next person who found the chain. [576]
When he stepped back outside a few minutes later, he could see the faint flicker of fishing boat lights on the horizon, moving slow and steady, their skippers having seen his stone stack and adjusted their course accordingly, safe for now. [621]
He lingered on the gallery for a few more minutes, watching the light from the lamp spin out across the waves, feeling the faint, warm press of a hand on his shoulder that didn’t belong to anyone he could see, and for the first time in months, the ache in his chest felt less like a weight and more like a gift. [676]
He pulled his oilskin tighter against the wind, turned to head back down the steps, and smiled, knowing the light would hold, the letters would keep coming, and the people who relied on this stretch of coast would be cared for, long after he was gone. [722]
