In the hush of a hidden sunrise, Echo Gorge bleached gold where pale light slipped between rock walls that had trapped unspoken grief for three centuries. [26]
Mara, the modestly fearless lost soul collector, hunkered on a ledge, the polished crocodile’s tooth strung around her neck warm against her collarbone. [47]
She was here to pursue the lingering spirit of a 1920s circus clown, who’d wandered the gorge for 90 years refusing to offer the spectral confessions that would free him from his guilt over a performance accident that killed his young assistant. [88]
For years, Mara had followed the standard collector’s protocol, guiding souls to confess their pain so they could cross over, but she’d grown tired of how often healing felt cold, hollow, stripped of the joy that had once made those lives worth living. [133]
Her driving goal now was to integrate laughter into healing, to prove that release didn’t require only sitting with sorrow, but remembering the light that existed alongside it. [164]
The clown’s spirit had refused every traditional offering, so she’d brought her grandmother’s misaligned harp, its strings stretched just off-key enough to warp sound into the gorge’s unique echo frequency, planning to reach him through the distorted notes of a misaligned harp. [215]
She plucked the first string, and the warbling note bounced off the rock walls, folding in on itself until it carried the faint snort of a child laughing, the sound the clown’s assistant had made the first time he’d hit himself in the face with a custard pie for her. [261]
A faint, translucent shape flickered 20 feet below her, the clown’s ruffled collar faded, his face paint streaked with the same spectral tears he’d carried for decades. [290]
Mara plucked another string, and the distorted note shifted to carry the roar of the circus crowd, the sound of the assistant yelling her approval when he’d pulled a live rabbit out of his sleeve mid-performance. [328]
The crocodile’s tooth around her neck warmed further, a gift her grandma had given her when she’d first started collecting, saying it held the memory of every joy she’d ever laughed at, even when grief felt like it would bite her in half. [376]
The clown drifted closer, his translucent hand outstretched, and when Mara plucked a third note, he finally spoke, his confession soft enough that only the gorge and the harp could catch it: he’d been drinking before the show, had missed the catch that would have stopped his assistant from falling 30 feet into the circus pit. [437]
Mara didn’t rush him, didn’t push him to feel only remorse, instead plucking a fast, jangly sequence on the harp that brought forth the sound of the assistant sneaking him extra funnel cake after every bad show, the sound of them making faces at strict circus management during meetings. [493]
For the first time in 90 years, the clown laughed, a thin, wobbly sound that glowed gold as the sunrise spilling over the gorge walls, the luminous ache of his long grief softening into something lighter, something whole. [537]
Mara touched the crocodile’s tooth, letting her own memory of laughing with her grandma the week before she died weave into the sound, showing the clown that grief and joy could coexist, that healing didn’t mean erasing the people he’d lost, just carrying their joy alongside his regret. [593]
The clown’s form began to glow, the streaks of paint fading from his face, and he gave her a small, grateful bow before he turned to step into the golden light, no longer trapped by his guilt, no longer forced to wander the gorge alone. [638]
Mara sat for a while longer, plucking soft notes on the harp as the sun rose higher, watching the light spill into every crack of the gorge where grief had hidden for so long. [674]
She knew most collectors would have thought her method was unorthodox, that mixing laughter with confessions was a waste of time, but she’d seen the difference it made, how the souls that left with a memory of joy crossed over far more peacefully than those who only left with regret. [729]
The crocodile’s tooth cooled against her skin, its work done for now, and she slipped the harp into its worn canvas case, already thinking of the next soul she would track down, the next small piece of joy she could weave into their healing. [776]
