Elara, a cultured, historically oriented museum guide, stepped through the mist curtain of the monumental waterfall marking the entrance to Veyra, the grotto city she called home, three days after the beekeeper’s last visit. [34]
The spring thaw had swelled the falls to twice its usual size, turning the grotto’s stone walkways slick with condensation and sending faint, silver drips pattering against the museum’s frosted glass display cases. [67]
She’d spent the past six months working with Veyra’s community of displaced persons, most of whom had fled the collapsed coastal kingdoms three years prior, her core motivation to rescue dying arts their elders had carried across the border for generations. [108]
Today, she was meeting with Maren, an 82-year-old weaver, to accept the donation of a single emerald-tinted opera glass lens, the only possession Maren had saved from her family’s estate before the floods swallowed her hometown. [145]
Rosewater dread hung thick in the grotto’s cool air, the beekeeper’s warning that the falls’ annual shift would block the city’s only trade route for six full months having settled over every resident like a damp, sweet-smelling shawl. [183]
When Elara reached Maren’s small stone cottage tucked between the grotto’s mushroom farms and community oven, the older woman was pulling a tray of small, crusty loaves from the heat, each baked to the shape of a family member’s initials, a custom Maren had brought with her from the old country. [234]
Maren offered her a loaf marked with an E, still warm enough to melt the butter Elara spread across its crumb, before leading her to a small wooden table where the emerald lens sat on a folded velvet cloth. [273]
“My mother used this to watch my father sing opera in the coastal capital, before the storms came,” Maren explained, her fingers brushing the edge of the lens as if touching a memory rather than glass. [309]
“I don’t have long left, and none of my grandchildren know how to use it, or care about the operas we used to travel for days to see.” [337]
Elara picked the lens up carefully, holding it up to the slanted cave opening that let in filtered light from the falls, watching the green hue turn the cascading water into a stream of liquid emerald. [373]
She knew the lens would be the centerpiece of her new exhibit, which paired donated personal artifacts with lessons on the traditions tied to them, including weekly workshops on the initialed bread, coastal weaving, and even the old operas Maren’s family had loved. [416]
The rosewater dread lingered, though, as she thought of the trade route closing, of the beekeeper’s note that he would not be able to bring new sugar, yeast, or fabric dye until next winter, supplies they needed to keep the workshops running through the long closed season. [463]
Maren seemed to read her worry, tapping the edge of the bread tray with a gnarled finger and smiling, as she pointed out that the community had stored three times the usual amount of grain that year, and that many of the displaced persons had brought yeast starter that had been passed down for centuries, no imported supplies needed. [522]
Elara laughed, tucking the lens into its velvet pouch and slipping it into her bag, before taking another bite of the initialed bread, its nutty, warm taste a reminder that the arts they were fighting to save were not just artifacts in glass cases, but living practices tied to the food they ate, the stories they told, and the families they built even far from home. [588]
The dread did not vanish entirely, of course, the distant roar of the falls a constant reminder that the outside world was still dangerous, that they would face months of isolation and scarcity before the beekeeper could return. [626]
But as Elara walked back to the museum, the lens heavy in her bag and the taste of bread still on her tongue, she knew that their work to preserve these traditions would not only survive the coming months, but would grow stronger, rooted in the shared history and care of the community that had found a new home behind the falls. [688]
