Elara, a vivid mural artist who paints with light, drifts into a daydream mid-sip of cold chamomile tea, her studio desk still scattered with half-processed photo negatives. [27]
The fog of the daydream swallows the edges of her studio first, softening into the mist-shrouded ruins of the orchard district lost to three centuries of unbroken, cotton-thick fog. [56]
She’s here to rediscover lost joy, the spark that vanished after a gallery fire burned three years of her light-painted murals to ash before they could be shown. [84]
Her boots sink into mossy, rotting apple crates as she rounds the corner of a crumbled stone wall, and a flash of terracotta half-buried in underbrush catches her eye. [113]
She kneels, brushing away damp fern fronds, to reveal a chipped fresco fragment, its faded pigments still showing a cluster of children laughing beneath boughs heavy with pink apple blossom. [143]
She recognizes the style instantly: it’s the work of her great-great-grandmother, a muralist who vanished along with the orchard district when the fog rolled in in 1722, her life’s work thought entirely lost. [176]
As she runs a finger over the cracked paint, the district’s knowledge network hums to life beneath her fingertips, the collective memory of everyone who ever lived here woven into the damp stone and fog. [211]
The fog shifts, and she sees it: the entire orchard as it once was, every frame a strip of exposed film, bright with sunlight, the air thick with the scent of ripening fruit and honey. [246]
She remembers why she paints with light, how she developed the technique after finding old family journals that spoke of her ancestor’s trick of projecting painted images onto sunlit walls for village festivals. [279]
The knowledge network feeds her more memories, not just the ancestor’s, but those of the children in the fresco, of the farmers who tended the trees, of the baker who sold apple tarts from a cart at the orchard gate. [319]
Pensive mirth tugs at the corner of her mouth as she realizes the fire didn’t steal her work, it just cleared space for her to carry forward a legacy older than she’d ever imagined. [353]
She lifts the fresco fragment gently, brushing dirt off its edges, and holds it up to the slanted light filtering through the fog, watching as the knowledge network projects the full, lost mural across the entire expanse of the crumbled orchard wall. [395]
Hundreds of figures dance across the stone: farmers, children, bakers, her ancestor standing to the side, a brush in her hand, grinning like she knew someone would find her work centuries later. [427]
She knows exactly what she’ll do when she wakes from this daydream: she’ll scan the fresco fragment, combine its imagery with the memories pulled from the knowledge network, and use her light-painting technique to display the full, lost mural for the entire city to see. [472]
She’d spent three years grieving the work she lost, convinced her purpose as an artist had burned up in the gallery fire, but standing here in the foggy orchard, surrounded by the hum of centuries of joy, she feels that familiar spark flicker back to life in her chest. [521]
She traces the outline of a laughing child on the fresco fragment once more, and the light of the projected mural wraps around her like a hug, warm even through the damp chill of the fog. [557]
The edges of the daydream start to soften, the hum of the knowledge network fading into the distant sound of her studio’s window AC unit clicking on, and she tucks the memory of the fresco fragment tight against her chest so she won’t lose it when she wakes. [605]
When she blinks back to her studio, the half-processed negatives on her desk glow soft in the afternoon light, and she can still smell the faint, sweet scent of apple blossom clinging to her sleeves. [640]
She grabs her notebook, scribbling down every detail of the mural she saw, mapping out how she’ll project the light painting across the side of the city’s old public library, where anyone can stop and see it for free. [679]
Pensive mirth lingers on her face as she realizes she didn’t just rediscover lost joy in that daydream, she found a whole new reason to keep creating, one that ties her to every artist who ever picked up a brush to make their community smile. [724]
The exposed film negatives on her desk wait to be developed, and she knows the first strip she processes will be the test reel for the new mural, a bridge between the fog-shrouded orchard of the past and the bright, busy city of the present. [769]
