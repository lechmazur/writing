The baroque asteroid of Veyra, its spires carved from iridescent olivine and strung with aged silk pennants, hung quiet in the gap between two visible streams of looping, gold-tinged time, three hundred years after time itself became a tangible, observable force. [41]
Kael, once the firebrand leader of Veyra’s failed anti-corporate uprising, was now a defunct revolutionary, his calloused hands dusted with archaeological grit, and his poetically inclined mind always turning over fragments of the old songs his grandmother had taught him. [87]
The core of his quiet frustration was the ongoing archaeological dispute between the off-world corporate consortium that claimed Veyra as a mining asset, and the handful of remaining Veyran natives, who insisted the consortium’s dig reports erased the planetoid’s radical, community-focused history to justify stripping its resources. [139]
The only relic he had saved from his childhood home, destroyed in the revolution’s final crackdown, was a jagged crystal vase shard, etched with the swirling runes of Veyra’s old harvest festivals, that he kept tucked in the lining of his worn wool coat. [185]
He still followed the old Veyran custom of covering mirrors during storms, the thick, ion-charged tempests that made visible time warp so sharply that unprotected mirrors would project screaming echoes of the revolution’s dead, so sharp they could cut a person who stared too long. [237]
His deepest, most closely held motivation was not just to correct the consortium’s lies, but to reimagine an entire civilization, one that did not end in gunfire and betrayal, but grew from the best parts of the old Veyra that the corporate researchers refused to acknowledge. [286]
He had spent six months studying the consortium’s dig schedules, the patterns of the ion storms, and the way visible time settled in the hollow of the central baroque amphitheater, the very spot where he had given his first revolutionary speech when he was 19. [332]
Last week, he had begun to devise a plan that would not just win the archaeological dispute, but rewrite the narrative of Veyra for every researcher, tourist, and corporate auditor that stepped foot on the asteroid for the next century. [378]
It was simple, rooted in the customs he knew and the tangible properties of visible time he had studied for decades: he would hide his crystal vase shard in the deepest layer of the consortium’s main dig site, timed perfectly to be uncovered right as the next major ion storm rolled across Veyra’s surface. [428]
He had already coordinated with the few remaining Veyran families to leave every public mirror uncovered for the first hour of the storm, a deliberate break from the tradition of covering mirrors during storms that would let the time echoes flood the amphitheater and the dig site all at once. [479]
As a poetically inclined former activist, he had written a full, lyrical narrative of the reimagined Veyra, weaving the old harvest runes on the shard with stories of collective governance, universal care, and peaceful resistance that had been erased from official records. [527]
The burnished sorrow that had lived in his chest since he watched his closest friends die in the final battle softened, just a little, as he ran his thumb over the etched runes on the shard, knowing he was not just fighting for the past, but for a future where Veyran children would know their true heritage. [579]
The consortium’s lead archaeologist, a sharp-eyed woman from the core worlds, had already made public statements dismissing the remaining Veyrans as liars with no tangible proof of their claims, so the discovery of the shard, paired with the time echoes that would play out across every public mirror, would be impossible to dismiss. [632]
He knew the plan carried risk; if the storm shifted earlier than predicted, the time echoes could hurt innocent bystanders, and the consortium would almost certainly arrest him for tampering with the dig site, but he had already lived long enough to see his revolution fail, his people dispossessed, and his history rewritten by strangers. [685]
The night before the storm was due to hit, he sat in the small stone hut he called home, all mirrors already covered for his own safety, and traced the edge of the crystal vase shard one last time, whispering the old harvest songs under his breath, the words soft and worn as the pennants strung across the asteroid’s spires. [736]
When the first low rumbles of the ion storm rolled across the olivine ridges the next afternoon, he slipped the shard into his pocket and walked toward the dig site, the golden streams of visible time swirling around his ankles, his grief quiet and steady, his plan fully formed, and his hope for the reimagined civilization he had spent decades dreaming of finally within reach. [792]
