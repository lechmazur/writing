The alchemist’s market strung high above the fog-shrouded city rooftops hums with the sharp, sweet scent of crushed starflower and half-spoken incantations, the kind that linger on the edge of perception like a half-remembered lullaby. [35]
I am a between-worlds translator, my work bridging the quiet, unrecorded tongues of spirits who linger in human spaces and the living people who have long forgotten how to listen to them. [67]
My motivation to honor overlooked histories began when I was seven, standing on the rooftop of my grandmother’s apartment building, watching a lighthouse beam sweep across the bay even though the lighthouse itself had been torn down three decades prior to make room for a waterfront mall. [114]
The elder spirits who frequent the market told me that ghosts of lost structures only stay visible if they have anchors of intuition, small, meaningful fragments that people who once loved them carry unknowingly in their bones and their memories. [154]
Today, I am searching for a specific, elusively tangible object: a shard of the old lighthouse lens, last seen tucked into the frame of a 1952 oil painting of the bay that hangs in the market’s back stall of forgotten memorabilia. [195]
I have learned to locate such fragments through hidden details in paintings, tiny brushstrokes that shimmer with residual spirit energy, only visible to people who have spent years holding space for the stories no one else bothered to write down. [235]
The painting is just where the spirits said it would be, propped against a stack of rusted alembics, its sky streaked with the same deep indigo I remember from my childhood rooftop, a fleck of pale gold glowing at the spot where the lighthouse once stood. [281]
When I lean closer, I can feel the lighthouse lens fragment thrumming behind the wooden frame, warm as a living pulse, a tiny piece of a structure that once guided hundreds of fishing boats home through winter storms. [319]
My fingers brush the edge of the frame, where a thin brass clasp holds the canvas in place, and I know I need to unfasten it carefully, so I don’t disturb the spirit of the lighthouse keeper who painted the scene, still lingering in the brushstrokes. [365]
The clasp gives easily, as if it has been waiting for someone who knows what it holds, and when I pull the canvas slightly free, the shard of glass falls into my palm, cool and bright, holding a thousand tiny refractions of the market’s string lights. [411]
In this eternal instant, I can feel the space between childhood and now collapse, the seven-year-old girl watching the lighthouse beam and the woman holding its fragment standing in the same spot, connected by a thread of memory that no demolition could ever cut. [455]
I hear a soft, gravelly voice in my ear, the lighthouse keeper’s spirit thanking me for not letting his work be erased, and I tell him I plan to mount the shard in the new community center by the bay, so every child who visits can see the light he tended for so many years. [510]
He laughs, a sound like wind through old wooden railings, and I feel his energy fade gently, no longer tied to the painting, free to wander the shoreline he loved so much for as long as he wishes. [548]
I tuck the lighthouse lens fragment into the inner pocket of my coat, right over my heart, and pause to look out over the city rooftops below, where the sun is just beginning to paint the sky pink and orange over the bay. [591]
For a second, I swear I can see the lighthouse beam sweep across the water again, brighter than I ever remember it, casting a warm glow over every fishing boat, every waterfront home, every child playing on the beach below. [631]
These anchors of intuition are not just trinkets, I realize, they are bridges between what was and what is, between the people who built our world and the people who get to live in it, proof that no story is ever truly lost if someone is willing to look for it. [682]
As I turn to walk back toward the market’s rope bridge leading down to the city, I tuck my hand into my pocket to press the shard once more, grateful for the quiet work of translation, of unearthing the stories that make our world feel like home. [729]
