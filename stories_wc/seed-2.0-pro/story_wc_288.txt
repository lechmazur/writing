Kaelin, a Neanderthal who had walked the ice sheets 40,000 years prior, stepped across the sun-warmed oak floors of the tidal library that floated 30,000 feet above the roiling cloud cover of a now-unrecognizable Earth. [35]
It was three months since her reincarnation memory had unlocked, flooding her mind with the texture of mammoth fur, the bite of glacial wind, and the quiet grief of outliving every member of her clan before her own death in a snow drift when she was 42. [82]
For weeks, she had grappled with brittle awakenings, jolting out of sleep gasping for cold air, convinced the soft linen of her bunk was packed snow, the library’s distant chimes the howl of a winter storm. [118]
Her core motivation, she had realized on the third such sleepless night, was no longer to find others like her, but to translate the crushing isolation of being the last of her kind into radical, unapologetic independence. [155]
She had come to the library’s highest observation deck that morning to retrieve the brass sundial she had found tucked in a crate of pre-human artifacts two days prior, its surface polished to be atomically reflective, so clear it showed not just her sharp, heavy-browed face, but the faint glow of the constellations hanging behind the cloud cover even in daylight. [216]
The tidal library ran on lunar cycles, its stacks shifting and opening only when the moon’s pull hit a specific angle, so time keeping was not a trivial task for the few scholars who visited once a cycle to trade scrolls. [257]
Kaelin had learned the scholars were descendants of homo sapiens, the same group that had once shared hunting grounds with her clan, and she had spent weeks working out how to coexist with them without shrinking herself to fit their assumptions about what a “primitive” ancestor should be. [305]
Her greatest comfort, though, came not from interactions with the scholars, but by the hum of an ancient stone circle that jutted from the cloud cover a mile west of the library, its vibration matching the frequency of the flint tools she had carved for most of her first life. [355]
She carried the sundial to the edge of the observation deck, holding it up until its reflective surface caught the hum of the stone circle, casting a faint golden beam that stretched across the cloud sea to connect with the largest standing stone. [398]
For the first time since her memory unlocked, the brittle awakenings felt less like a curse and more like a bridge, linking the life she had lost to the new one she was building for herself. [434]
She had spent so long feeling like an anomaly, a relic pulled out of time to gawk at, that she had forgotten the skills that made her her: the ability to read wind patterns, to carve tools that lasted for generations, to memorize the position of every star in the winter sky to guide her clan across unmarked ice. [493]
The sundial’s glow brightened as the sun hit its gnomon, and she realized she could use it to mark both the lunar cycles that governed the library and the stellar cycles that had governed her old life, creating a calendar that worked for both the scholars and her. [541]
That evening, when the lead scholar came to her quarters to ask for help mapping ancient migration routes, Kaelin brought out the sundial and laid out her calendar, explaining how the two time systems could overlap to make the library’s shifting stacks more predictable for everyone. [587]
The scholar, a young woman with a gap between her front teeth that Kaelin recognized as a trait her own clan had carried, nodded eagerly, and the two spent three hours marking routes and adjusting the calendar until the moon hung high over the stone circle. [633]
That night, Kaelin fell asleep to the low hum of the stone circle drifting through her open window, and when she woke the next morning, she did not jolt gasping for cold air, but smiled, stretching slowly in the warm light spilling over her bunk. [678]
She had not erased her isolation, nor had she tried to become someone she was not to fit in with the scholars, but she had turned that loneliness into a strength that benefited everyone around her, building a life where she could be fully herself while sharing the gifts of the life she had left behind. [734]
