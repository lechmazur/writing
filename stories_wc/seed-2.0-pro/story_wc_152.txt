The cobalt observatory’s domed roof glowed burnished indigo under the crisp autumn twilight, wind tangling oak leaves in the iron railings that wrapped its upper observation deck. [27]
Marn, the royal galaxy-chart embroiderer, huddled on a stone bench near the main lens, her wicker sewing basket propped between her scuffed leather boots. [51]
Most courtiers wrote her off as a daft, fumbling old woman who stitched nonsense constellations onto royal tapestries and hummed off-key folk tunes to herself, a reputation she carefully cultivated to disguise wisdom as foolishness. [86]
She had come to the observatory three nights prior, just a week before the rumored coup that would oust the gentle, story-loving queen and install a military junta that planned to burn all records of non-state sanctioned histories. [124]
Her sole motivation for sneaking out each night was to listen for unheard stories, the quiet, forgotten voices of farmers, weavers, and refugees that the court scribes had erased from official archives, which she stitched into secret constellations only she could decode. [166]
She pulled the flame from the eternal candle from her basket, its steady gold glow casting soft shadows across the star charts spread out on the bench beside her, its heat cutting through the sharp autumn chill. [202]
The observatory was wrapped in echoed silence, the only sounds the distant hoot of an owl, the faint crackle of the flame, and the quiet whisper of wind through the dome’s narrow ventilation slits. [236]
For months, she had been unraveling conspiracies linking the junta’s leaders to a series of suspicious fires that had destroyed community story circles across the kingdom, each fire timed to erase oral histories that contradicted their narrative of a violent, unified national origin. [279]
She had learned to navigate the gaps between official records and forgotten lore through adjacent possibilities, testing small, plausible connections between missing files, anonymous witness accounts, and the tiny, coded notes she had hidden in her embroidery for years. [318]
Tonight, she planned to synchronize the constellations she had stitched from the unheard stories with the actual positions of the stars overhead, marking a permanent, visible record of the erased histories that even the junta could not burn. [356]
She lifted a length of silver thread from her basket, holding it up to the flame until it glowed the same warm gold, then pressed it to the blank linen square she had stretched across a wooden hoop. [394]
Each stitch corresponded to a name, a story, a memory that the junta had tried to bury, from the herbalist who had healed 200 villagers during the last famine to the group of children who had written a play about peace that was banned from the royal theater. [442]
As she stitched, she mumbled the stories aloud to herself, her voice soft and wobbly, deliberately sounding like a senile old woman talking to thin air, just in case any of the junta’s spies were patrolling the observatory grounds. [481]
When she finished the last stitch, she held the embroidery up to the dome’s open slit, aligning the stitched constellations perfectly with the stars glinting in the dark autumn sky, their light bouncing off the silver thread to cast tiny, glowing patterns across the observatory’s cobalt stone walls. [529]
She had successfully synchronized the living memories of the kingdom’s people with the unchanging light of the stars, ensuring that even if the junta burned every book, every tapestry, every written record in the land, anyone who knew how to read the stitched constellations could recover the unheard stories. [578]
The flame from the eternal candle flickered once, as if in acknowledgement, and the echoed silence of the observatory felt less empty, more like a held breath, full of the weight of all the stories she had saved from erasure. [618]
She folded the embroidery carefully, tucking it into the lining of her shabby wool cloak, where it would be hidden from any guards who stopped her on her walk back to the palace. [651]
The work of unraveling conspiracies was far from over, and she knew that in the days leading up to the coup, she would have to use every trick of her carefully crafted foolish persona to share the stitched constellations with other community leaders across the kingdom. [697]
She had already mapped out a route through adjacent possibilities, using small, seemingly random errands to visit weavers, bakers, and teachers in every district, passing off tiny swatches of embroidery as silly, meaningless trinkets that no guard would ever think to examine closely. [740]
As she blew out the visible part of the eternal candle, tucking the still-burning hidden flame back into its padded compartment in her basket, she smiled to herself, knowing that the stories she had protected would outlive the coup, outlive the junta, and outlive every attempt to erase the truth of the kingdom’s people.
