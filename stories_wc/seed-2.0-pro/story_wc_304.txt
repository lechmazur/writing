Lira, who worked both as a small-town beautician and part-time mortician, had climbed the mist-woven stone steps to the skyborne grotto under the rippling emerald aurora three nights in a row, waiting for the right moment. [36]
She was racing the slow creep of her own fading memory, a rare neurological condition that would erase every part of her by week’s end, and her only goal was to capture one last spark before oblivion: the echo of her late wife’s laugh, which she’d last heard in this very grotto on their tenth anniversary. [92]
She perched on the smooth limestone ledge at the grotto’s mouth, lucidly watchful, as the aurora shifted from lime green to tangerine to soft violet, painting the jagged cave walls in shifting, glowing hues. [126]
In her palm she curled the tiny emerald chip her wife had given her as a wedding gift, its faceted edge catching the aurora’s light to throw tiny, flickering rainbows across the grotto’s shallow, glowing pool. [162]
Her years of working with both the living and dead had taught her to recognize the threshold of stillness: that quiet, unnameable moment between one breath and the next, between a sound and its echo, when the world held perfectly still to let magic seep through. [208]
She’d spent months practicing the old folk magic her grandmother had taught her, finding patterns in static to pull lost sounds and moments out of the thin, charged air, and she’d brought nothing but the chip, a small vintage radio, and her own fraying focus to the grotto for this final attempt. [260]
The radio beside her hissed soft white noise, the static warbling as the aurora dipped lower, brushing the tops of the cloud banks that surrounded the floating grotto on all sides, cutting it off from the world below. [298]
She held her breath as the static softened, the hissing fading to a low, thrumming hum, and she knew she had crossed the threshold of stillness, that the air around her was thick enough to hold every lost moment that had ever happened in the grotto. [344]
Closing her eyes, she sifted through the hum, finding patterns in static that looked like the shape of her wife’s smile, the curve of her hand, the way she’d tossed her head back when she laughed so hard she snort-laughed, quiet and bright and warm. [389]
When she felt the spark of that laugh brush against her fingertips, she pressed the emerald chip to her chest, letting the warm, golden glow of the memory seep into the stone, willing it to infuse every facet of the tiny gem with the full weight of her love, her joy, the life they’d built together. [445]
For a long, stretched moment, the entire grotto was wrapped in a radiant hush, the aurora holding its glow steady, the static gone completely silent, the water in the pool so still it mirrored the sky above perfectly, no ripples, no movement, no sound at all. [491]
As a beautician, she’d spent years helping people hold onto their best versions, painting on smiles for wedding days, covering scars for graduation photos, making people feel seen and whole even when they felt broken. [526]
As a mortician, she’d spent even more years helping families hold onto the best versions of their loved ones, fixing the marks of illness and accident to give people one last perfect memory of the people they’d lost, and this felt like the same work, just for herself. [574]
When she opened her eyes, the emerald chip in her palm glowed soft green, warm to the touch, and she could hear the faint, familiar sound of her wife’s laugh hum just under its surface, clear and bright and unbroken. [614]
She left the grotto as the first pale streaks of dawn peeked over the cloud line, tucking the emerald chip into a small silver locket she wore around her neck, where it would sit even when her memories faded, even when she forgot who she was, even when oblivion finally came for her. [667]
She didn’t need to remember, not really, because the spark she’d captured would outlast her, a tiny, glowing piece of the love she’d held so close, floating just under the aurora, just under the surface of the stone, for anyone who might be lucky enough to find it one day. [717]
