The first jagged fork of lightning split the indigo sky above the cloud-perched courtyard, and Kael, a seldom-heard balladeer who’d drifted here half by accident, stilled his calloused fingers on his battered lute, counting the silent stretch between flash and rumble. [41]
Thick, pearlescent mist curled around the weathered stone flagstones of the space, which was built into the spine of a floating mountain range, strung with glowing paper lanterns that hummed faint, tinkersong wonder into the damp air. [78]
He’d found the dented brass flower press bolt plate tucked into the crack of a courtyard bench three nights prior, its surface etched with faint, looping patterns no botanist he knew would have carved into a work tool. [116]
Kael was driftingly alert, his gaze flicking between the bolt plate in his pocket and the faint, glitching edges of the lantern light, because he’d learned the week prior that this entire floating colony ran on old, layered code, and there was a ghost in code that had been trying to reach someone for decades. [171]
Most colonists wrote off the glitches as wind interference or old hardware, but Kael, who’d spent his whole career singing the stories no one else bothered to amplify, shared the ghost’s core drive to articulate unspoken needs that got lost in the noise of daily life. [217]
He’d figured out the ghost spoke in visual symbols, and that the only way to respond, to encode his message of willingness to help so the code could read it, was through messages encoded in graffiti art pressed into the soft, mist-dampened clay of the courtyard’s low border walls. [266]
The timeframe between lightning and thunder was the only window the ghost could interact with the physical world, when the atmospheric charge messed with the code’s firewalls just enough to let its residual consciousness seep through. [302]
He pulled the brass bolt plate from his pocket, running his thumb over the etched patterns that matched the ones the ghost had been flickering into the lantern light for days, each line a request for someone to listen to the stories of the colony’s long-dead founders, whose memories were trapped in the code’s earliest layers. [358]
He knelt at the nearest clay wall, pressing the flat, etched surface of the bolt plate hard into the damp material, twisting it slightly to leave a clear, deep impression that glowed faintly gold as soon as he lifted the metal away. [400]
The lanterns flared all at once, their hum swelling into a bright, wordless melody that tangled with the mist and made Kael’s lute strings vibrate on their own, echoing the tinkersong wonder he’d felt the first time he’d realized the ghost was real. [443]
Fragments of memory flickered across the courtyard’s mist: a group of engineers building the first floating platforms, laughing as they planted wildflowers in the first courtyard planters, a young woman carving the patterns into the flower press bolt plate as a gift for her partner, who’d designed the colony’s first code framework. [495]
The ghost’s unspoken need clicked into place: the founders had never wanted the colony to become the rigid, profit-driven outpost it was now, they’d wanted it to be a place of art and community, and they’d been trying to remind the residents for decades. [539]
Kael smiled, plucking a few tentative notes on his lute that matched the lantern hum, already composing the ballad he’d sing in the colony’s market the next day, weaving the founders’ story into every line so no one would forget it again. [581]
The first low rumble of thunder rolled across the cloudscape just then, marking the end of the window, and the glowing impression on the clay wall faded to soft terracotta, but the melody lingered, warm and bright in Kael’s bones. [621]
He tucked the flower press bolt plate back into his pocket, already planning to press more of the ghost’s symbols into courtyard walls across the colony, each piece of graffiti a bridge between the forgotten past and the hopeful future the founders had always imagined. [666]
For the first time in his career, he knew his ballad would be heard, not just by living colonists, but by the ghosts who’d built the home they all loved, their unspoken needs finally woven into the fabric of the world they’d made. [709]
