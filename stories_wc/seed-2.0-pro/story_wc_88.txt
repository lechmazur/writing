Elara stepped onto the pebbled shore of the underground bioluminescent lake six weeks after the procedure, the frayed cuff of her canvas jacket brushing cool, glowing cyan water that smelled of wet limestone and crushed starflower. [35]
She was a self-proclaimed clean slate seeker, a former smuggling ring boss who’d ordered violent cargo seizures and left families homeless to line her pockets, and the full memory wipe had been supposed to scrub that dirty past clean enough to let her start over. [75]
The only possession she’d refused to let the clinic incinerate was a scuffed regulation baseball signed by a time traveler who’d stumbled into her apartment when she was 19, bleeding from a cut on his forehead and rambling about branching timelines and second chances. [119]
The ball was delicately robust, its stitched leather worn smooth along the seams from a decade of being tucked in her jacket pocket, its silver ink signature glowing faintly when held up to any source of light, no matter how dim. [162]
She’d embarked on this journey three months prior, after realizing the memory wipe had only erased the specific memories of her cruelty, not the cold, empty hollow where her ability to care about other people had once been. [204]
She’d found her way to the hidden cave entrance by reading between newspaper lines, picking out coded phrases the time traveler had scrawled on the ball’s leather side from tiny, unmarked classified ads buried in the back of local papers across three states. [249]
Every step of the trek had left her equal parts exhausted and skeptical, convinced she was chasing a ghost of a promise made by a random stranger who’d probably been high, but the moment she’d rounded the final cave bend and spotted the glowing lake, that skepticism had warped into the sharp, unsteady feeling of jaded wonder she’d carried ever since. [306]
The lake’s surface rippled in endless, shifting patterns, each glowing arc and eddy representing a different choice she could have made at every turning point of her life, a living, breathing labyrinth of choices that held every possible version of the person she could have been. [356]
The time traveler’s note on the ball had told her that to get what she was looking for, she wouldn’t just have to pick a ripple to follow—she’d have to sit with every memory tied to that choice, the good and the terrible, without looking away. [399]
Her motivation was simple, even if it felt almost impossible to achieve: she wanted to kindle empathy where none remains, to feel the weight of the harm she’d caused so she could make sure she never, ever caused that kind of pain again. [441]
She sank down onto a smooth, flat stone half-submerged in the shallow edge of the lake, turning the baseball over in her palm and watching the silver signature glow brighter the longer she held it under the cyan water. [481]
For a long moment she hesitated, staring at the hundreds of shifting ripples spreading out around her boots, wondering if she even deserved to feel anything other than the cold empty hollow she’d carried since the procedure. [522]
She reached out eventually, her fingers brushing a ripple that flashed with an image of 27-year-old her slamming the door in the face of a single mother who’d begged for extra time to pay off a smuggling debt, and a sharp, burning ache bloomed in her chest, hot and foreign and real. [574]
Tears she didn’t know she could produce rolled down her cheeks, mixing with the cool lake water, as a flood of small, sharp memories washed over her: the sound of that mother’s sobs, the fear in the eyes of the teen she’d once threatened over a late payment, the quiet guilt she’d buried so deep even the wipe couldn’t fully get rid of it. [631]
She curled her fingers around the baseball again, holding it tight to her chest as the ache in her chest softened into something warm, something that felt like the first spark of the empathy she’d spent months chasing. [671]
She didn’t know how long she sat there, letting the memories wash over her, but when she finally looked up, the ripples on the lake had settled into a single, clear path leading across the water to a cave opening on the other side, glowing the same soft silver as the signature on her baseball. [722]
She stood up, brushing the dust off her jeans, and took the first step into the shallow water, knowing she still had hundreds of choices to make and hundreds of apologies to offer, but for the first time in her entire life, she felt like she was finally walking the right path. [771]
