The midnight glade above clouds breathed in slow, silver gusts, its grass thick with frost that glinted like crushed starlight when the crescent moon peeked through wispy fog. [28]
The onyx-ink cartomancer had hiked for three days to reach it, her cloak stiff with high altitude ice, her satchel stuffed with worn tarot cards edged with the same deep black ink that stained the tips of her fingers permanently. [68]
It was three months after the birth of her daughter, the first time she had left the cottage she shared with her partner, guilt coiled tight in her chest even as she knew this journey was non-negotiable. [105]
She was here to find where lost time accumulates, to claw back the years she had wasted running from her family’s gifts, the years she had missed with her own mother before the woman’s sudden passing two winters prior. [144]
This was cloudbound repentance, the old family term for treks above the cloud line to make amends for choices that had eroded the bonds between blood and magic. [172]
She pulled a frayed linen tablecloth from her satchel next, dotted with the inked outlines of dozens of hands, markers of a family custom of tracing hands on birthday tablecloths that dated back six generations. [207]
She reached up to unfasten the silver clasp of her cloak, letting the heavy fabric fall to the frost so she could move her arms freely as she spread the tablecloth across the flattened patch of grass she had chosen at the glade’s center. [251]
Next she pulled out a chipped porcelain saucer, its delicate pink rim crisscrossed with a dolls’ tea set saucer crack she had carried with her since she was seven, when she had dropped it while throwing a tantrum after her mother tried to teach her her first cartomancy spread. [300]
She laid the saucer in the center of the tablecloth, then fanned her tarot cards around it, willingly perplexed by the way the ink on the cards seemed to shift and glow under the moon, as if the stories held in the hand tracings were seeping into the card stock. [350]
The air around her grew thick with the scent of jasmine and frozen pine, a choking beauty that made her eyes burn and her throat tight, as if the clouds themselves were holding their breath to watch her work. [389]
She pressed her palm flat to the outline of her mother’s hand on the tablecloth, the inked edges fitting almost perfectly over her own, and whispered the old prayer her grandmother had taught her, the one for calling back lost moments. [430]
The crack in the saucer began to glow soft gold, and tiny, glowing motes of light started to drift up from the tablecloth, each one holding a sliver of memory: her mother teaching her to braid daisies into her hair, the way her mother had cried when she told her she was rejecting the cartomancy gift, the quiet way her mother had left the saucer on her windowsill the day she moved out, no note attached. [506]
She realized then that lost time doesn’t accumulate in some hidden pocket of the world, it accumulates in the cracks of the things we carry, in the spaces between the words we never said, and the love we never allowed ourselves to accept. [549]
She reached out and touched the glowing crack in the saucer, and felt her mother’s hand wrap around hers, warm and solid, even though she knew no one was there. [579]
She pulled her hand back a minute later, and tucked a small, glowing mote of light into her pocket, the one where her daughter’s tiny knit sock was folded, a gift she planned to give the girl when she was old enough to learn the family’s customs, old enough to trace her own hand on the tablecloth at her first birthday. [640]
She folded the tablecloth carefully, tucking the saucer back into its pouch in her satchel, and looked out over the sea of clouds stretched below her, the silver light turning the undulating waves of mist into something that looked like crushed silk. [682]
The guilt that had sat heavy in her chest for months had lifted, replaced by a soft, warm weight, the kind that comes from knowing you have made amends, and that the love you thought was lost was actually with you all along, tucked into the cracks of the things you carry. [734]
She slung her satchel over her shoulder, picked up her frozen cloak, and started down the mountain, eager to get home to her daughter and her partner, to hold them tight, and to never again let the time she has with the people she loves slip through her fingers unacknowledged. [784]
