Lila, the moonshine runner’s daughter, hunkered against a splintered piling of the rotting pier overhead, the salt-sting of bay air cutting through the thin wool of her jacket as she waited for the pirate market’s first vendors to unlock their crates. [41]
She’d snuck out at midnight with her father’s old field recorder tucked in her bag, her only goal until sunrise to capture the sound of trust breaking, the noise her mom had described the night she’d left, a sound Lila had never managed to track down in the quiet hollers or backroad stills of her hometown. [97]
The pirate market ran on an unspoken truce of hush, no questions asked about stolen cargo or hidden warrants, a rule that kept even the most reckless runners and smugglers from drawing the coast guard’s attention to the rickety, tide-washed aisles strung with fairy lights and strung up dried eel. [147]
Her gaze landed on a vendor polishing a dented brass antique watch fob etched with a heron, the same design her father had worn on his keychain the night he’d been arrested three years prior, the fob that had gone missing from the evidence locker before his court date. [196]
The vendor, a one-eyed man with a scar snaking up his jaw, was curiously steadfast in his refusal to name where he’d gotten the fob, even when Lila offered him twice the cash she’d stuffed in her pocket, his jaw tightening like he was bound by some unspoken promise. [245]
She’d spent weeks planning to confront the man everyone in the runner circuit said had ratted her father out, and she’d planned to thwart his attempt to sell the fob to a wealthy collector that night, working through false starts of small talk and half-asked questions to get him to admit what he’d done. [299]
The air hummed with the low buzz of haggling and the lap of tide against wooden posts, a strange, comforting unease settling in her chest as she realized she was finally about to get the answer she’d chased for years, the sound she’d come all this way to record. [348]
When she finally mentioned her father’s name, the vendor’s hands stilled, his one eye widening like he’d seen a ghost, and for a long minute the truce of hush hung heavier between them than the salt fog curling around the pier supports. [390]
He told her he’d not ratted her father out, that her dad had begged him to take the fob and sell it to cover the first round of her mom’s chemo, that he’d lied to everyone about being a snitch to keep her dad’s pride intact, to keep Lila from hating the father she’d thought was a hero. [448]
The sound of trust breaking didn’t come from the vendor’s mouth, didn’t come from a shouted confession or a slammed fist, it came from the soft crack of her own false belief splitting open, the quiet shatter of the grudge she’d carried for three years, and her recorder caught every second of it, the hush of her breath catching, the faint thud of her heart skipping a beat. [516]
He pushed the antique watch fob across the splintered crate he used as a counter, no charge, saying her father had told him to give it back to her if she ever showed up asking questions, if she ever got old enough to understand the choices people make for the people they love. [569]
The sky was starting to pale pink at the edges when she turned to leave, the timeframe she’d set for herself nearly run out, and she paused to look back at the vendor, who gave her a small nod before turning to help a customer haggling over a crate of stolen rum. [621]
The comforting unease had softened into something warm by the time she climbed up the rotting pier steps to the street above, the fob tucked in her jacket pocket and the recorder still running in her bag, capturing the faint crash of waves and the quiet pop of the first firecrackers set off by early-morning fishermen down the shore. [680]
She didn’t have the heart to turn the recorder off, not yet, because the sound of trust breaking, she’d learned, was also the sound of something new and softer starting, the kind of sound that was worth holding onto long after the sun peeked over the horizon and the pirate market below faded back into the shadow of the pier. [740]
