When coincidence becomes pattern, Elara, a broken-clock tinkerer who’d spent ten years fixing timepieces that could no longer tick, stopped dismissing the faint, thrumming pull she felt every time she descended the stone stairs behind her workshop. [37]
The stairs led to a subterranean waterfall, its mist catching the glow of her headlamp to paint tiny, shifting rainbows across the moss-slick rock walls. [62]
For months, she’d been chasing that quiet goal to befriend uncertainty in planning, tired of rigid repair checklists that left no room for the unexpected quirks that made broken clocks feel like old friends rather than discarded objects. [100]
That night, the pull guided her off the familiar stone path, through the swirling leaves in a neglected greenhouse tucked into the cave’s far corner, its glass panes cracked and overgrown with wild ivy that dripped with waterfall spray. [139]
In the center of the greenhouse sat a rusted iron fire pit, ringed with chipped ceramic mugs and half-carved wooden gears, and she knew without looking that whatever she sought lay beneath the ashes. [173]
She brushed the cold ash aside with her calloused fingers, and her palm closed around a dented brass disc etched with nautical stars, the admiral's lost compass rose she’d seen referenced in old port records a dozen times before. [212]
She was uncertainly certain that the compass had not been there the last time she’d explored the greenhouse, just as she was uncertainly certain it was meant for her, even if she had no idea how it had ended up buried under ash in a cave behind her shop. [261]
The compass rose was cracked clean down its center, its internal gears corroded beyond standard repair, so she knew she would have to improvise, using the spare clock parts she kept in her work apron pocket to bind its pieces back together. [303]
She wove tiny clock springs around the brass rim, slotted a broken watch’s tiny magnetic pin into the compass’s center, and breathed warm air onto the cold metal to clear away the last lingering dust. [338]
For a long moment, nothing happened, and she wondered if she’d wasted her time chasing a hunch that had no logical explanation, no paper trail to confirm it was anything more than a silly flight of fancy. [375]
Then the compass’s needle twitched, spinning once before settling not toward north, but toward the waterfall, where the mist swirled so thick she could barely see the stone ledge at its edge. [407]
She stood, brushing ash off her jeans, and walked slowly toward the ledge, holding the compass out in front of her like a lantern, its brass surface glowing faintly even in the dim, misty light. [442]
When she reached the edge, she realized the compass was pointing not at the falling water, but at a small crack in the rock behind it, just large enough for her to slip through if she turned sideways and held her breath. [484]
She squeezed through the crack, water soaking her sleeves as she pushed past the curtain of falling water, and emerged into a small, dry cavern lined with shelves holding dozens of broken timepieces, each tagged with a handwritten note describing its previous owner’s favorite memory. [529]
She’d spent years looking for a collection like this, ever since she’d started repairing broken clocks and wondering where all the discarded, unclaimed timepieces went when their owners no longer wanted them. [561]
She sat on a carved wooden stool in the center of the cavern, turning the improvised compass over in her hand, and smiled, realizing that her goal to befriend uncertainty in planning had paid off far more than she’d ever expected. [602]
For so long, she’d relied on rigid schedules and step-by-step repair guides, too scared to follow the quiet instincts that had drawn her to clock repair in the first place, too worried that straying from the plan would lead to failure. [643]
Now, holding the compass she’d pulled from beneath the ashes, the product of a dozen small coincidences that had stacked into an undeniable pattern, she knew she would never go back to planning every single detail of her days again. [683]
She pulled a small notebook and pencil from her apron, ready to catalog the first of the timepieces in the hidden cavern, letting the compass sit on the stone table beside her, its needle twitching occasionally toward a new, unexplored corner of the cave system. [728]
