That was the day her identity as a top-tier coast guard navigator shattered, the day she earned the quiet label she’d carried ever since: the broken navigator, too haunted to read a star chart or hold a compass without her hands shaking. [75]
She’d driven here an hour prior, right when her last expectation of outrunning her guilt collapsed, when she’d realized numbing herself with bar drinks and dead-end retail shifts would never stitch the gap between who she was and who she wanted to be. [117]
Her fingers curled around the dented astronomical compass tucked in her pocket, its glass cracked, its needle frozen mid-spin ever since the rescue boat crashed against the rocks in the storm she’d failed to predict. [155]
The backlash of regrets hit her then, sharp as the salt in the air: every choice she’d made to avoid the water, every time she’d lied to her friend’s mom that she was doing okay, every missed chance to honor the woman who’d loved kayaking more than anything. [201]
She huffed a merrily melancholic laugh, wiping at the wetness on her cheek as she watched a group of kids chase seagulls across the nearby sand, their shouts bright against the low hum of the tide. [238]
For months after the crash, she’d refused to use any navigational tools she’d trained with, instead navigating by utility poles when she walked the coastal backroads, counting their faded yellow numbers to keep her mind off the constellations she’d once known by heart. [283]
Her motivation to align heart and action had pulled her here today, her heart screaming that she owed it to her friend to get back on the water, even if her hands still shook when she looked at the floating kayaks bobbing by the dock. [324]

The tone of flickering faith hummed in her chest, bright one second as she remembered her friend laughing as they kayaked these waters as teens, dim the next as she remembered the sound of the storm siren blaring the day of the crash. [404]
She pulled the astronomical compass from her pocket, running her thumb over the crack in the glass, and for the first time in three years, she didn’t flinch when she saw the frozen needle. [441]
It didn’t matter if the compass didn’t work anymore, she realized, because she wasn’t navigating by stars or coordinates today; she was navigating by the memory of her friend’s voice, telling her to stop blaming herself for things she couldn’t control. [487]

For a second, her feet froze, the old fear roaring back that she’d mess up again, that she’d get lost, that she didn’t deserve to find peace after what had happened. [559]

Her faith flickered again when a small wave rocked the boat, but she steadied herself, remembering the way her friend had taught her to lean into the waves instead of fighting them, and she kept going. [724]
She didn’t know if she’d ever be the navigator she once was, but that didn’t matter anymore, because for the first time in three years, her heart and her actions were aligned, pointed exactly where they needed to be. [766]
