The last of the geese had vanished over the palace’s crenellated west wall three days prior, crisp autumn air sharp enough to sting the tips of Lila’s ears when she slipped through the cracked iron gate into the overgrown courtyard. [40]
Lila was a creeping normality noticer, the kind of person who caught when the neighborhood laundromat swapped its detergent for a cheaper brand, when a regular bus driver stopped greeting passengers by name, when the beloved corner bakery that had operated for 72 years shuttered overnight with no warning and no explanation. [92]
Tucked in her canvas bag was the last half-loaf of sourdough she had bought from that vanished bakery, frozen for three months and thawed that morning, still holding the faint, nutty sweetness of the starter the owner had tended for four decades. [134]
She wove past patches of wild dandelion and self-seeded tomato vines until she reached the secret tea house, a tiny cedar structure half-hidden under climbing rose bushes that still held a few stubborn, frost-kissed pink blooms. [170]
This was her third visit that month, driven by a quiet, unshakable motivation to find the sacred in the ordinary, to hold onto the small, precious things that the world seemed determined to erase without fanfare. [206]
The tea house keeper, Mara, was heuristically gentle, adjusting her tone and offers of honey or extra blankets based on small, unspoken cues from visitors, never pushing for stories or personal details if someone seemed closed off. [243]
When Lila set the loaf of sourdough on the wooden table between them, Mara smiled and reached up to uncloak the burlap cover that had been draped over the wide window facing the courtyard, revealing a row of flourishing wheat stalks swaying in the light breeze. [289]
Mara explained that a small group of local residents had tended the wheat over the past six months through guerrilla gardening, planting it under cover of night when the palace grounds staff had stopped patrolling the unused courtyard, using saved seeds from the vanished bakery’s owner, who had been a close friend of hers. [343]
Lila’s throat tightened, because she had carried guilt for months for not realizing the bakery’s owner had been ill, for not stopping by more often in his final weeks, for assuming the shop would always be there until it was gone, and the sight of the wheat felt like the first hint of tenuous absolution she had allowed herself to accept. [404]
Mara sliced the sourdough and toasted it over the tea house’s small wood stove, the scent of warm crust and fermented grain wrapping around them like a hug as she poured two mugs of chamomile tea sweetened with wild honey they had harvested from a beehive in the palace’s old apple orchard the week prior. [459]
Lila took a bite of the toast, and it tasted exactly like she remembered, bright and earthy, like weekend mornings and the owner’s quiet laugh when she asked for extra butter, like all the small, uncelebrated moments that made a neighborhood feel like home. [503]
Mara told her they planned to harvest the wheat at the end of the month, mill it by hand, and bake loaves to give away for free at the local food bank, using the same starter the bakery owner had shared with her years before, keeping his legacy alive one loaf at a time. [557]
Lila asked if she could help with the harvest, and Mara nodded, handing her a small packet of the same wheat seeds so she could plant a patch in the empty planter box outside her apartment, passing the legacy on even further. [599]
By the time Lila left the tea house as the sun dipped below the palace walls, the guilt that had sat heavy in her chest for months had softened, replaced by a quiet, warm hope that the sacred things she loved so much never truly vanished, they just changed shape, waiting for people to notice and nurture them back to life. [660]
