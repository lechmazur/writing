The first breath Elara drew after her fever broke tasted of rime and dried pine, sharp enough to cut through the fog of half-remembered visions she’d carried for three days trapped in her cot at the remote mountain weather station. [40]
As a fault-tolerant oracle, she was accustomed to parsing fragmented, conflicting prophecies without collapsing under the weight of their contradictions, but the fever had stripped away her usual filters, leaving every unspoken truth she carried raw and exposed. [78]
She was peripherally aware of the wind whistling through the station’s solar panel frames, of the static fizzing across the barometer screens, of the faint green glow of the aurora spilling through the frost-filigreed window above her desk. [116]
Her fingers curled around the tattered botanical illustration of alpine edelweiss tucked under her pillow, the only possession she’d brought with her when she’d retreated to the station six months prior, fleeing the quiet shame of a prophecy she’d gotten deliberately wrong. [158]
The illustration had belonged to her younger sister Lila, who’d fallen from a nearby trail three years before, a death Elara had foreseen three weeks before it happened but never mentioned, too terrified of being blamed for planting the idea of the hike in Lila’s head. [204]
For years, she’d carried that swallowed truth like a stone in her chest, and her current mission, the reason she’d come to this isolated outpost, was to find the weight of a swallowed truth, to measure how much of her power it had leached away. [249]
The fever had shaken loose her long-held certainties, leaving her with swaying certainties that shifted like the aurora outside, no longer fixed, no longer reliable, forcing her to find a new way to parse what she saw. [286]
She had always read the center of her visions, the bright, sharp core of what was meant to be, but her time at the station had taught her the most reliable answers came by occupying margins, the spaces between data points, the edges of prophecies most oracles dismissed as noise. [336]
She dragged her thumb across the edge of the botanical illustration, careful to smudge only the faint pencil mark Lila had left in the corner, a tiny heart she’d drawn the day before she’d left for her final hike, and let her vision blur, focusing only on what she saw out of the corner of her eye. [393]
The aurora’s green and purple glow painted the edges of the paper, and for the first time, she didn’t flinch away from the memory of Lila asking if she thought the hike would be safe, the way she’d lied and said yes, even though she’d seen the ice on the trail in her vision. [447]
She felt the stone in her chest shift, its weight no longer a cold, unnameable thing, but a measurable 1.7 kilograms, exactly the weight of the backpack Lila had been carrying that day, stuffed with her sketchbooks, her water bottle, and the pressed edelweiss she’d planned to give Elara for her birthday. [499]
The swaying certainties settled, not into the fixed truth she’d always chased, but into a softer, kinder truth: she had not killed Lila by staying silent, and Lila had not blamed her, even at the end. [535]
She ran her finger over the smudged heart on the illustration, smiling through the tears that froze on her cheeks in the cold station air, the auroral sorrow she’d carried for three years softening into something like peace, no longer a weight to bear, but a memory to hold gently. [585]
Outside, the wind died down, and the barometer screens stopped fizzing, their readings steady for the first time in three days, matching the steady beat of her heart as she finally let herself grieve properly, no longer hiding from the truth she’d swallowed for so long. [631]
She tucked the botanical illustration into the front of her notebook, the smudged heart visible at the edge, and made a note in the margin of her weather log: Truth weighs 1.7 kg, and it tastes like pine and forgiveness. [671]
