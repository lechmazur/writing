Lila, the sole survivor of a 2019 drone strike that incinerated her rural Yemeni wedding mid-vow, leaned against the cool carbon-fiber railing of the floating observatory 12,000 feet above the Gulf of Aden, waiting for the lab’s overtaxed circuit breakers to cool enough to reset. [45]
Three years prior, a relief worker had pressed a chipped, unpolished geode crystal into her palm at a refugee camp, telling her it held all the light and potential the strike had stolen from her, and she’d carried it in her pocket every day since. [90]
She’d won a radical quantum research grant six months earlier, driven by a ragged, unshakable motivation to find where unused potential pools – the half-lived lives of wedding guests, the unsaid vows, the children her best friend would never raise, all the energy the strike had snuffed out before it could unfold. [142]
Her work centered on the quantum hunt for these latent energy clusters, conducted through perturbation theory, which let her map tiny disruptions in dark matter that signaled concentrations of what could have been, a phenomenon she’d dubbed intangibly tangible, because she could feel its weight in her bones even when her sensors registered nothing at all. [198]
Ten minutes earlier, her sensors had spiked so sharply they’d tripped the entire observatory’s power grid, throwing her systems into emergency shutdown right as she’d locked onto a massive, dense cluster of latent potential directly below her, less than a mile from the site of her ruined wedding. [246]
She pulled the geode from her jumpsuit pocket now, running a calloused thumb over its pocked, matte gray surface, and thought about the moment the drone’s missile had hit, the split second of bright light before the pain, the way she’d woken up three days later in a hospital with no memory of the blast and 37 names of dead loved ones scrawled on a piece of paper beside her bed. [317]
That sensor spike had been her unfiltered awakening, the first time her data had matched the quiet hum she’d felt under her skin for years, proof that the grief she carried wasn’t just a weight in her chest, but a signal pointing to something real, something waiting to be found. [367]
A current of bleak exhilaration ran through her as she stared down at the hazy blue line of the coast, the rush of finally being close to answers tangled tight with the sharp, fresh grief of remembering the laughter that had filled the wedding tent only moments before the strike. [417]
The circuit breakers beside her control panel clicked softly, a sign they were almost done cooling, and she ran another quick calculation on her battery-powered handheld tablet, confirming that her perturbation theory models had correctly identified the cluster’s exact coordinates, no margin of error. [461]
She held the geode up to the faint midday sun, and for a split second, she swore she could see the light of her lost loved ones glowing inside its unopened core, the intangibly tangible potential she’d been hunting for finally within her reach, no longer just a theory on a screen. [513]
She’d spent so long thinking her grief was a flaw, a hole in her life that could never be filled, but this hunt had taught her that every lost possibility, every cut-short life, left a ripple in the fabric of the universe that could be found, could be measured, could be honored. [565]
The final click from the control panel told her the circuit breakers were fully cooled, and she slipped the geode into the reader slot built into her console, ready to run the final scan that would confirm she’d found the pool of unused potential she’d spent three years chasing. [614]
She didn’t know what she would find once she mapped the cluster fully, if it would bring her closure or just more questions, but the bleak exhilaration buzzing under her skin told her it didn’t matter, that the act of finding, of honoring the lost, was enough on its own. [664]
She pressed the scan button, and the console hummed to life, the screen lighting up with streaks of bright gold light that matched the glow she’d seen inside the geode, proof that the people she’d loved had never truly disappeared, just pooled somewhere quiet, waiting to be remembered. [712]
