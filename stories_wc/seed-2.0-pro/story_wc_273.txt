The last sliver of the sun slipped from behind the moon an hour prior, painting the wind-carved encampment in pale, gold-flecked shadow that pooled in the hollows of stacked stone shelters and tattered canvas lean-tos strung between red rock spires. [40]
Kael, the albino tracker who had spent twenty years cultivating a consciously wild way of being, unshackled from state surveillance and the rigid schedules of the lowland cities, knelt beside a crumpled canvas tarp where his research partner had been sitting three days prior, when a flash flood ripped through the canyon’s lower reaches. [94]
He’d come to this remote stretch of canyon after three separate survivors of local disasters reported time stretching into infinite, slow-motion increments as they fought to stay alive, a phenomenon no mainstream scientist would deign to study, let alone validate. [134]
The faint whir of his artificial hummingbird drone cut through the soft wind, its iridescent polymer wings catching the post-eclipse light as it dipped low to scan the ground for any trace of his partner, Lila. [170]
Beside the tarp, a cracked glass vial of disappearing ink Lila had been using to mark survey routes lay on its side, its contents spilled across a flat sandstone slab in swirling, lavender-tinged patterns that were already starting to fade into the rock. [213]
Kael’s face stayed smooth, no outward sign of the serene anger burning under his pale skin, anger at the officials who’d refused to send warning of the coming flood, anger that he’d been half a mile away checking a sensor when the water hit, calm rooted in his decades of living wild, knowing panic would get him nowhere. [271]
The drone hovered an inch above the slab, its tiny camera feeding high-resolution footage to Kael’s wrist comm, and he froze when he saw tiny, familiar indentations in the center of the ink splotch, the exact shape of Lila’s favorite leather boot toe. [314]
He remembered Lila rambling two nights prior, half-joking, that the disappearing ink’s light-activated molecules resonated with the weird time dilation they’d been measuring, that the ephemeral patterns it made as it faded might be a doorway, not just a mess. [354]
He didn’t hesitate, shrugging off his pack and stepping carefully into the center of the fading ink patterns, letting his bare soles press into the cool stone where the lavender hue was already bleeding into nothing, the drone darting back to hover a safe distance away as he focused on the quiet, thrumming anger in his chest, the drive to find Lila and answer the question that had brought them both here. [426]
The world around him softened, the sound of the wind fading to a low hum, and he began to disappear through ephemeral patterns in spilled ink, his pale hair and white linen shirt melting into the lavender swirls until only the faint indent of his feet was left on the stone, gone before the last of the ink faded from view. [487]
He blinked, and found himself standing in waist-deep, frigid water, the canyon walls frozen mid-crumble above him, droplets of water suspended in the air like tiny glass beads, Lila ten feet away, clinging to a rock ledge, her eyes wide with shock as she saw him. [533]
In that quiet, suspended moment, he understood the answer he’d been chasing: time slows in crisis not as a trick of the brain, but as a lifeline, a crack in the fabric of reality woven by the human drive to survive, a crack that could be crossed if you knew what to look for, if you had the right ink, the right anger, the right reason to step through. [602]
He reached for Lila’s outstretched hand, his serene anger softening just a little into relief, as the artificial hummingbird drone, still perched on a rock spire back in the post-eclipse encampment, captured the last trace of the ink before it vanished entirely, storing the footage of the swirling patterns for anyone who might come looking for the same answer, for anyone who had someone they’d cross a crack in time to save. [674]
