Lila, the raft city of Perpetual Fiesta’s only official culture shock absorber, ran calloused fingers over the chipped foundation stone she wore on a leather thong around her neck, the rough edge digging just enough to ground her as she wove through the crowd of fire dancers and arepa vendors at dusk. [52]
Her job, until the city council voted on privatization the following week, was to mediate tensions between lifelong raft dwellers and the flood of wealthy land developers who’d begun renting out luxury raft suites for weekend getaways, a role that required her unique orderly intuitive mindset to balance rigid bureaucratic rules with the unwritten, joy-first norms of the carnival community. [112]
She carried a constant quiet weight of dual allegiance, having grown up selling hand-painted masks on the raft’s main boardwalk before earning an urban planning degree on the mainland, making her the only person who spoke both the developers’ jargon and the carnival’s unspoken language of shared space and collective care. [163]
Her sole goal these days was to navigate gentrification without letting the city lose the raucous, inclusive spirit that had been its backbone since the first raft was lashed together by climate refugees 40 years prior, even as the developers promised steady jobs and improved flood barriers that the cash-strapped community could not afford on its own. [220]
The chipped foundation stone around her neck had been chipped from the very first cinder block used to anchor the original central raft, given to her by her grandmother who’d helped build it, a reminder that every part of the city’s structure relied on the community that built it, not just the materials holding it afloat. [276]
She’d spent three months devising a plan to reorganize the city’s shared public spaces to suit both dwellers and developers, communicating the fine print of the proposal only through messages encoded in graffiti art painted on the side of food carts, parade floats, and raft hulls, so that only long-term community members could decode the hidden clauses that protected their access to rent-controlled moorings and free public event space. [345]
The air hummed with hopeful desperation the night before the council vote, as Lila walked the full length of the raft city, tracing each graffiti tag with her eyes to make sure every clause of her proposal was spelled out clearly, from the guarantee that 70% of new jobs would go to local residents to the rule that no carnival parade route could be blocked for private events. [413]
The developers thought her proposal only gave them permission to build 20 new luxury suites and a waterfront cocktail bar, but they had no way of reading the swirling, neon-pink symbols tucked between cartoon mariachi skeletons and glittery mermaid murals that laid out the community’s non-negotiable demands, each one tied to city planning rules she’d learned in school that even the developers’ high-priced lawyers could not overturn. [480]
When she reached the central raft, she pressed the foundation stone chip to the weathered cinder block half-buried in the raft’s wooden deck, whispering a promise to her grandmother that she would not let the community be pushed out to make room for wealthy tourists who only cared about the carnival’s aesthetic, not its people. [535]
By dawn, hundreds of community members had gathered at the council meeting, each carrying a small printout of the graffiti clauses they’d decoded, ready to vote in favor of the proposal that would let the developers move forward while locking in protections for the next 50 years, a compromise Lila had pulled together by honoring both sides of her split identity. [596]
As the council called the vote, Lila twisted the foundation stone chip around her neck, watching the carnival’s acrobats wave at the developers from the back of the room, and felt the familiar tight mix of hope and fear soften into something solid, knowing that even if the city would change, its soul would stay intact, carried forward in the murals, the music, and the people who had built it from the first floating plank.
