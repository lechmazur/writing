<story>
Lila, a phobia-ridden stunt double who’d spent three years jumping off cliffs and avoiding enclosed spaces to keep her job, huddled on the corroded metal fire escape of the rusted library under stars, the crumpled adoption papers in her hand still burning from the hour-old discovery that she was not who she thought she was. [55]
The woman she’d spent three years doubling for, her supposed estranged older sister Mara, had orchestrated a transformative betrayal, stealing Lila’s birth identity as a toddler to access their late marine biologist mother’s seven-figure research grant. [91]
Tucked in her jacket pocket was the whispering seashell half her foster mom had given her when she was seven, the only memento she’d kept of the first few blurry years of her life, which hummed soft, unrecognizable lullabies when she held it to her ear. [137]
She didn’t care about the grant money, not really; her core motivation, sharp and unshakable even as her hands shook, was to prove that vulnerability is power, that the phobias Mara had mocked her for for years weren’t weaknesses, but clues that had led her here. [183]
Mara was set to burn the only remaining copy of Lila’s original birth certificate, locked in a hidden safe in the library’s sub-basement, in 45 minutes, but Lila, firmly adaptive even when her claustrophobia screamed at the thought of climbing down the narrow service stairwell, had a plan she’d spent the last three hours refining. [238]
Three days prior, she’d signed the neon pink cast of 10-year-old Javi, a neighbor of Mara’s who’d snuck into the library two weeks earlier to draw maps of the hidden nooks he’d found, and scrawled the combination clues to the old safe he’d seen Mara tamper with across the cast’s surface before he’d gone swimming and smudged half the ink. [298]
She pulled out the photo of Javi’s cast she’d taken after he’d asked for her autograph, leaning into the cold metal rail as she focused on decrypting smudged notes on a child’s cast, matching the faded lines to the blueprint of the library she’d pulled from public records that morning. [348]
It took 12 minutes to cross-reference the smudged numbers with the dates the seashell hummed when she held it to the photo, her breath catching when she realized the combination lined up with the day she’d been taken from the hospital, the numbers lighting up in her head with trembling certainty, no room for doubt. [403]
She slipped through the library’s broken back window, ignoring the tight squeeze that made her claustrophobia spike, and made her way to the maritime history shelf on the first floor, twisting the third copy of *Moby Dick* to reveal the safe embedded in the wall, the combination clicking into place on her first try. [457]
Mara walked through the front door 30 seconds later, a gas can in one hand and a lighter in the other, sneering when she saw Lila standing by the open safe, the birth certificate held loose in her left hand. [497]
Lila didn’t flinch, even when Mara stepped forward and threatened to light the entire building on fire, because she had already planned to outwit her older sister long before she’d stepped foot in the library that night. [534]
She’d sent a full digital copy of the birth certificate, the adoption papers, and a recording of Mara admitting to the identity theft to the grant board, the county clerk, and the local police 10 minutes earlier, the notification ping from the police department confirming they were two minutes away popping up on her smart watch right as Mara reached for her. [596]
Mara froze when she heard the sirens wailing outside, and Lila held up the whispering seashell half, the soft lullaby it hummed loud enough for both of them to hear over the noise, her hands still shaking but her voice steady as she told Mara that the weaknesses she’d spent years mocking were the exact things that had led Lila to the truth. [659]
As the officers walked through the front door to arrest Mara, Lila smiled, knowing she’d proven that vulnerability is power, that her phobias, her gaps in memory, her willingness to ask for help from a kid with a cast, were never flaws—they were her greatest strength. [705]
