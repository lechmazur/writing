The cast-lots decision maker leaned against the sun-warmed rail of the ambered observatory deck, his boots scuffing at faint craters left by the last round of cosmic rebirth. [28]
Long after the universe contracted into a dense, glowing pinprick before blooming again into its current slow, cool iteration, the old archives had carried a backwards prophecy: the first sign of the next stable cycle would not appear in the future, but as an echo of what had existed before the collapse. [80]
His entire adult life had been dedicated to one quiet goal: to wavelength align with purpose, to be the person who could parse that echo and guide the scattered settlements of the new universe away from the repeated collapse that had wiped out every prior cycle. [126]
He was quietly enthusiastic about the work, never prone to the dramatic outbursts or reckless experiments that had gotten three of his predecessors removed from their post, even when months of readings turned up nothing but static. [163]
His unusual gift for understanding static bursts, developed as a child listening to old pre-collapse transmission fragments from his grandmother’s archive, made him uniquely suited to the role of cast-lots decision maker, the person who cast weighted lots to greenlight every high-stakes cosmic research project. [208]
Tucked in the inner pocket of his thick wool coat was his most prized possession: a collection of foreign coins, each minted in a different pre-collapse civilization, each etched with tiny symbols that matched the faint markings he had started picking out in the static six weeks prior. [256]
That evening, he had brought his portable spectroscope out to the deck, planning to distill the latest 48 hours of static readings into a concentrated signal he could cross-reference against the symbols on each coin, hoping to finally confirm that the echoes matched the backwards prophecy’s specifications. [303]
Still, he could not shake the undercurrent of stellar doubt that hummed under every calculation, the quiet fear that he was misreading the static, that the symbols were just coincidence, that his purpose was not as clear as he had spent his whole life believing. [348]
He spread the coins out on the carved oak table bolted to the deck, their warm, worn edges glowing under the amber tint of the observatory’s curved protective dome, which filtered out the harsh radiation of the young universe’s unsteady stars. [389]
When he flipped the switch on the spectroscope, a soft fizz filled the air, and a stream of static bursts poured out of the tiny speaker, each pop and crackle aligning perfectly with the weight of each coin as he rolled them between his fingers, one by one. [437]
As the cast-lots decision maker, he had prepared three weighted lots earlier that day, each marked with the symbol of a different possible outcome: proceed, pause, abandon the project entirely. [467]
He held his breath as he tossed the lots onto the table next to the coins, and for a long, quiet moment, the only sound was the faint hum of the spectroscope and the distant glow of a newborn star flaring behind the amber dome. [512]
The lot that landed face up was the one marked “proceed”, and the static bursts cut out immediately, replaced by a soft, steady tone that matched the exact frequency of the metal alloy used to mint every single coin in his collection, a pattern that could not have been a coincidence. [563]
For the first time in years, the stellar doubt that had clung to him like frost faded, and he realized he had already wavelength aligned with purpose, that his entire life had led him to this exact moment, where he could confirm the backwards prophecy was true. [610]
The backwards prophecy had always said the signal would come from the past, not the future, and the coins, carried across the contraction by tiny pockets of stable spacetime, were the key to unlocking the path that would keep the new universe from collapsing again for trillions of years. [659]
He tucked the coins back into his coat, picked up the spectroscope readings, and headed down from the observatory deck to share the news with the council, his quiet enthusiasm brighter than ever, even as a tiny, healthy spark of stellar doubt lingered to remind him to check every calculation twice. [710]
