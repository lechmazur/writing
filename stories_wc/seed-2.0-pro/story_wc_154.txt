The mist-clad atrium at twilight smelled of frangipani and antiseptic, soft enough to blur the line between the field hospital Lila had left in the Solomon Islands and the pediatric clinic she now sat in, waiting for the anesthetic to kick in for the 10-year-old patient she’d been paired with for the day. [53]
Lila, a Pacific conflict-zone translator who’d spent three years mediating between displaced communities and aid groups, had volunteered at the clinic after returning home, still carrying the weight of unspoken grief she couldn’t name. [87]
When the nurse wheeled Koa out, his left leg wrapped in a neon-blue cast covered in smudged, half-erased pencil scrawls, Lila reached into her pocket and pulled out the chipped brain coral fragment she’d dug up from a bombed reef off Guadalcanal six months prior. [132]
Her quiet motivation for bringing the fragment everywhere, and for working with child survivors of displacement, was to graft new meaning onto old wounds, both her own and those of the people she’d spent years translating for. [169]
Koa blinked at her with a shrewdly innocent expression, like he knew exactly what the coral meant even before she explained it was a piece of a reef that had survived three separate bombing runs during the island’s decades-long resource conflict. [210]
Lila had learned early in her work that shared anxieties bound communities far tighter than shared joys ever could, and she could see the same fear in Koa’s eyes that she’d seen in hundreds of other children who’d lost their homes, their families, their sense of safety to violence no one had asked for. [264]
When Koa nodded at his cast and mumbled something in the dialect of his home island, Lila leaned in, deciding to unknot the tangled grief they both carried by decrypting smudged notes on a child’s cast, one scrawl at a time. [305]
The first smudge resolved into a drawing of a coconut tree, half fallen, with a tiny star tucked in its branches, and Koa explained he’d drawn it the night his village was evacuated, when his older sister had told him the stars would always point home. [351]
The second was a scribbled name, faded almost to invisibility, that Lila recognized as the name of Koa’s sister, who’d gone missing during the evacuation, a loss he’d never spoken about to any of the clinic staff before that night. [391]
Lila told him about the brain coral fragment, how she’d found it while searching for her own little brother, who’d been swept away by a tsunami triggered by a bombing run when she was 12, a loss she’d carried silently for 18 years. [434]
There was a lotus-hearted ache to the air, soft and bittersweet, as they sat together in the mist, two people who’d lost so much, finally speaking the words they’d hidden for so long behind silence and duty. [471]
By the time Lila had decoded every scrawl on the cast, Koa was drowsy from the anesthetic, its effects fully settled in, but he reached out and curled his fingers around the brain coral fragment, asking if he could keep it, to remind himself that things that break can still hold beauty. [522]
Lila nodded, her throat tight, as she realized that the knot she’d been trying to untangle for decades wasn’t just her own grief, but the collective grief of every person who’d watched their home be torn apart by conflict they didn’t choose. [564]
She pulled a pencil from her bag and drew a tiny brain coral on the empty space of Koa’s cast, next to his sister’s name, writing the word “home” underneath in both his dialect and English, so he’d never forget that home wasn’t just a place, but the people and memories you carry with you. [619]
As the nurse wheeled Koa back to the operating room, he waved the coral fragment at her, his shrewdly innocent smile softening the sharp edge of grief that had lived in her chest for so long. [655]
Lila sat in the mist for a long time after he left, watching the twilight seep through the atrium’s glass roof, and for the first time since her brother died, she didn’t feel like she was carrying her grief alone. [695]
She’d come to the clinic hoping to graft new meaning onto old wounds for the children she helped, but she’d ended up healing a little of her own, too, proof that shared anxieties, when spoken out loud, can turn into shared hope. [737]
