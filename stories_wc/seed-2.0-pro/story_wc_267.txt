The cobalt fortress under snow loomed at the edge of the abandoned alpine resort, its stone walls glinting like crushed lapis between drifts that had not shifted in three years. [30]
The silent-choir bellhop, whose vocal cords had been severed as part of his initiation into the order that tended the fortress, was three weeks into nerve regeneration treatments, the faint tingle in his throat the only sign his body was fighting to reverse the ritual. [75]
He had spent every afternoon of his recovery climbing the fortress’s narrow stairwells, driven by a quiet, burning motivation to understand the cost of safety the order had promised the region when they first sealed themselves behind the fortress walls a decade prior. [118]
Most of the fortress was frozen in time, its common areas filled with seraphically mundane objects: chipped teacups stacked on dining tables, half-read hymnals tucked into pew pockets, a dented brass piano pushed against the west wall of the second floor hall. [160]
The piano was missing its bench, the only remaining part a single rusted piano bench bolt half-screwed into the floor where the bench had once sat, its surface worn smooth from decades of feet brushing against it. [197]
The order’s leaders had always refused to speak of the day the bench vanished, or the young pianist who had played for the fortress every evening until that same day, a silence the bellhop had long considered unpardonable, even before he lost his own voice. [242]
He had almost given up on finding answers until last week, when he spotted a mark via the slightest discoloration on a dusty spiral staircase tucked behind the fortress’s kitchen, a faint smudge of polished wood where decades of foot traffic had worn away the grime, leading up to a locked attic door no one else had ever mentioned. [301]
He had picked the lock that morning, his fingers still clumsy from the lingering nerve numbness in his arms, and pushed the door open to find the missing piano bench tucked against a small window overlooking the snow drifts, the rest of the attic empty save for a stack of handwritten sheet music and a tattered journal bound in black leather. [362]
The journal belonged to the pianist, he learned as he flipped through its pages, the entries detailing how she had discovered the order was hoarding food and medicine while the villages below the fortress starved during the long, brutal winter ten years prior. [405]
She had planned to sneak the supplies down to the valley, but the order’s leaders had found out, and had given her a choice: be cast out into the snow to die, or remain silent forever about their betrayal, surrendering her ability to speak and locking herself and her piano bench in the attic for the rest of her life. [465]
She had chosen the attic, the journal revealed, and had spent the next seven years writing her music and leaving notes for anyone who might find them, before she died of a fever three years before the bellhop joined the order. [506]
The cost of safety the order had sold to its members, he realized, was the freedom to speak the truth, and the lives of everyone the order had abandoned to the cold below the fortress walls. [542]
The first tingle of returning speech pricked at his throat as he tucked the journal into his uniform pocket, wrapping the loose piano bench bolt in a scrap of sheet music to carry with him as proof of what he had found. [584]
He would take his time descending the stairs, letting the frostbitten hope warming his chest steady his steps, because he knew the order’s unpardonable silence was about to be broken for good. [616]
He did not know if he would survive confronting the order’s leaders, but he knew he could no longer stay silent, not when the truth of what had happened to the pianist and the villagers below was finally in his hands. [657]
