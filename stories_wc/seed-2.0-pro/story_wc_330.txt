The first high, clear note of the brass wind bells strung between the abandoned circus train’s rusted boxcars rippled through the air, marking the narrow window of time Luka, the silent carousel carpenter, called inside the chime of wind bells, when the line between what was lost and what could be reborn blurred thin as old parchment. [57]
Luka had not spoken a word since the circus collapsed three years prior, when a lightning strike burned down the big top and shattered the hand-carved angel carousel horse he’d spent six months crafting, its gilded halo fragment still tucked in the pocket of his frayed canvas work coat. [106]
He was quietly persistent, returning to the train every weekend to salvage cracked carousel poles, chipped paint jars, and splintered horse heads, driven by his quiet motivation to interpret silence as possibility, to turn the hollow gaps left by loss into something soft and bright. [151]
That afternoon, he’d wandered further than usual while carrying a load of reclaimed oak planks, and a wrong turn led to a hidden oasis tucked between two overgrown boxcars, a small, sun-dappled pool ringed with wild clover and glowing dandelion fluff that caught the golden hour light like scattered stardust. [202]
A misting hush hung over the space, so thick he could almost taste the cool, damp air on his tongue, no sound of distant traffic, no creak of rotting wood, just the faint, thrumming quiet he’d grown to love in the years since he stopped speaking. [248]
The tone of the space carried a whispering gleam, soft and hopeful, like a secret someone had left tucked under a stone for him to find, warm even when a faint breeze rippled the surface of the pool and sent tiny droplets of water drifting onto his work boots. [297]
He sat on a smooth limestone ledge at the pool’s edge and pulled the halo fragment from his pocket, running his calloused thumb over the jagged edge where the lightning had split it from the rest of the angel horse’s gilded crown, the metal still warm from being tucked against his chest all afternoon. [351]
For the first time in three years, he felt a pressure building in his chest that was not grief, not the heavy silence he’d wrapped around himself like a blanket, but something light, something that wanted to escape, so he let it, opening his mouth to sing the lullaby his grandmother had taught him when he was a small boy learning to carve his first wooden horse. [418]
His voice was rough at first, rusty from disuse, but it grew smoother as he sang, the notes wrapping around the misting hush of the oasis and making the dandelion fluff dance in small, swirling circles above the pool, as if the air itself was leaning in to listen. [467]
He held the halo fragment up to the light as he finished the last note, and saw that the jagged edge had smoothed over, the gilded metal glowing so bright it cast a small, perfect circle of gold on the surface of the pool, matching the sun’s reflection so well he could barely tell the two apart. [524]
The wind bells chimed their final clear note, marking the end of the window inside the chime of wind bells, and Luka tucked the halo fragment back in his pocket, already mentally drafting the plans for a new carousel, one that would sit right here, ringed with clover, where the quiet could wrap around every child who rode it and fill them with the same gentle hope he felt right then. [595]
He knew it would take months, maybe years, of careful carving and slow work to bring the new carousel to life, but his quietly persistent spirit had carried him this far, and he had no doubt it would carry him the rest of the way, turning the silence of his lost circus into a possibility more beautiful than anything he had ever imagined before. [659]
When he turned to walk back to his workshop tucked in the old circus train’s baggage car, he did not feel the need to speak, because he had already found his voice in the hum of his chisels, the lullaby he had sung, and the quiet magic of the hidden oasis that had been waiting for him all along. [718]
