Lila, a natural history illustrator who’d spent three years sketching bioluminescent rooftop canal flora, had always felt the pull of the place where echoes are born. [26]
The rooftop canal system weaving between the old port’s glass and sandstone towers was her usual hunting ground for rare moss and flowering water weeds, but she’d never dared sail past the weir marked with chipped giant runes. [64]
The old dockworkers said those runes marked the edge of the territory the slumbering port giant claimed, and any traveler who crossed while he napped would be trapped in the soft, stretchy pocket of time his dreams wove. [102]
She wore a dented infinity symbol pendant on a leather cord around her neck, a gift from her grandmother who’d taught her that every illustration captured a sliver of time that would otherwise fade. [136]
What her grandmother hadn’t told her was that the pendant didn’t just capture time, it sculpts time itself, bending moments to the will of whoever wore it with an honest enough desire. [168]
She’d first felt the pull of the echo birthplace two weeks prior, when a wren’s song she’d been recording for a field guide cut off mid-trill in an interrupted cadence, as if the sound had been snapped mid-flight and pulled somewhere out of reach. [212]
That morning, she’d woken with the certainty that she could cross the weir, by borrowing time from the giant’s nap to stretch the short window he slept into enough hours for her to sail to the far end of the canal and back before he stirred. [258]
She untied her tiny reed boat from the mooring outside her studio loft, tucked her watercolor sketchbook into its waterproof case, and let the slow current carry her past the rune-marked weir, the pendant glowing warm against her chest as time softened around her. [302]
The further she sailed, the more half-formed sounds drifted past her: snippets of lullabies, the creak of old ship masts, the laugh of a child she’d never met, all trapped in the thin air between the towers, waiting for someone to uncage them and let them become real echoes. [351]
When she rounded the final tower bend, she found a small, glassy pool tucked between two gargoyles, its surface rippling with every half-sound that drifted into it, and she knew immediately this was the place where echoes are born, the source of that interrupted cadence she’d chased for weeks. [400]
She pulled the infinity symbol pendant from under her shirt, letting it dip into the cool pool water, and felt its power to sculpts time itself wrap around her, stretching the current moment long enough for her to sketch every ripple, every half-formed note hovering above the surface. [448]
When she finished the last line of her sketch, the interrupted cadence of the wren’s trill burst from the pool, bright and whole, and she realized her act of capturing the moment on paper was what she needed to uncage the sound, to let it drift back down the canals and reach every ear that was meant to hear it. [508]
The pendant grew cool against her skin, a warning that the time she had gained by borrowing time from the giant’s nap was running out, so she tucked her sketchbook back into its case, pushed her reed boat away from the pool, and let the current carry her back toward the weir. [560]
She crossed the rune-marked weir barely ten seconds before the giant stirred, letting out a low rumble that shook the tower windows, and the soft stretch of time across a giant's nap snapped back into its usual tight, linear shape, leaving no trace that she’d ever sailed past the barrier. [610]
That evening, as she sat on her loft fire escape adding color to her sketch of the echo pool, she heard the wren’s full trill echo between the towers, followed by dozens of other sounds she’d recognized from her journey, all bouncing off the canal water and filling the air with a richer, warmer soundscape than the city had ever had before. [672]
She touched the infinity symbol pendant around her neck, smiling as another soft echo bounced off her window, knowing that the sliver of time she’d borrowed had gifted the city a thousand new sounds that would ripple through its streets for decades to come. [714]
