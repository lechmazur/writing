The alchemical orchard greenhouse, wrapped in an endless night that blotted out sun and moon alike, hunkered between stretches of winter-cracked fields where even wind dared not make a sound. [30]
Elias, the seventh-generation caretaker of the glass-walled space, knelt beside a row of frost-kissed nightshade vines, sorting dried seed pods while a small carved stone bead hung on a leather thong around his neck, passed down through his family for 400 years with no note of its origin. [78]
He had never questioned the trinket, not even when faint glimpses of a woman in an ashen veil, the ashen-veil ghost bride of local folk tales, began to flicker at the edges of his vision three weeks prior. [116]
She never spoke, never drew close, only made ephemeral cameo appearances in the gaps between greenhouse panes, behind fruit tree boughs heavy with glowing alchemical pears, her form dissolving the second he tried to turn his head to look at her directly. [158]
What he did not know was that 400 years prior, his great-great-great-great-grandfather had found a dying young bride left for dead in these same fields, poisoned by a lovingly venomous betrothed who had married her only to steal her family’s alchemical recipes. [200]
The old healer had saved her life for three short days, long enough for her to carve the stone bead from a piece of ore pulled from her family’s mine, infusing it with the last of her power to protect his line until she could repay the debt across lifetimes. [250]
When she died, she chose to ghost through the centuries rather than pass into the afterlife, anchored to the land where the healer had built the first foundation of the greenhouse that now stood in his family’s care. [288]
Now, the time for resonant awakenings had come: the blight that had been creeping across the region’s crops for two years, a remnant of her betrothed’s cursed magic, was about to reach the greenhouse, and only her power fused with the bead could destroy it for good. [335]
That night, as Elias sat on a wooden stool polishing the stone bead by the glow of a tallow candle, the ghost bride stepped fully into view for the first time, her face half-hidden by the ashen veil, her eyes shining with a mournful elation that made his chest ache even though he did not know why. [392]
She held out a translucent hand, and Elias found himself lifting the thong over his head without hesitation, pressing the warm carved stone into her palm as if he had done it a hundred times before. [428]
When her fingers closed around the bead, a soft golden light exploded outward, wrapping around every vine, every tree, every pot of alchemical herbs in the greenhouse before seeping out through the glass to spread across the hush of winter-cracked fields beyond, burning the blight to ash before it could take another step. [481]
Elias stood stunned as the light faded, fragments of old memories flooding his mind: of mixing herbal remedies in a small cottage, of pressing a cup of warm poppy milk to a dying girl’s lips, of her pressing a rough carved stone into his palm and whispering a promise to pay him back. [534]
The ghost bride smiled, the veil slipping from her face to show soft, freckled cheeks, no trace of the poison’s blue tinge that had marred her skin in her final days of life 400 years prior. [570]
She held the bead out to him again, and when he took it, he could feel its warmth thrumming with leftover power, enough to keep the greenhouse and the surrounding lands safe for another four centuries if needed. [608]
Her form began to glow, translucent edges turning soft gold as the weight of centuries of waiting lifted from her shoulders, the mournful elation in her gaze softening into pure, quiet peace now that her debt was finally paid. [647]
Before she faded entirely, she gave him a small nod, a silent thank you for the kindness his ancestor had shown her, a promise that if his line ever needed help again, she would find her way back to the greenhouse shrouded in endless night. [692]
Elias stood there long after she was gone, the carved stone bead warm in his palm, the first hint of starlight peeking through the endless night above the winter-cracked fields, proof that even the oldest debts could be repaid, and even the longest waiting could end in joy. [740]
