The first pale gold light of dawn seeps through the feathered leaves of the siskin alder grove, gilding the small hedgehog that has just uncurled at the base of the oldest tree, its quills dusted with last night’s moss spores. [40]
Lira, the symphonically minded tree-song sculptor, huddles on a fallen log a few feet away, her calloused fingers curled around the chipped porcelain insulator she dug up last week from a collapsed 19th-century telegraph line half-buried under pine needles. [79]
The world has been silent for three years, ever since the ash blight swept through every valley and plain, silencing bird calls, stilling wind chimes, even deadening the crash of ocean waves against shoreline rock, leaving only the faint, muffled thud of living things moving through dust and grass. [128]
Lira’s quest to create music in a silent world has led her here, to this grove where her old mentor carved a tree-song sculpture of intertwined alder and siskin wings 40 years prior, left forgotten when the blight drove most survivors from the region. [172]
For three days she has sat studying it, her small magnifying glass tracing every thin, silvery stress crack that spreads across its weathered surface, mapping how the wood’s tension holds the faint, buried resonance of the day it was carved, when siskins sang and wind hummed through the grove’s branches. [222]
She calls this work empathy in motion, the act of listening to what a broken, forgotten thing holds, rather than forcing it to make the sound she wants, meeting its history exactly where it is instead of demanding it fit her vision. [264]
The hedgehog waddles toward the sculpture to nibble a patch of clover growing at its base, and its small paw brushes the lowest edge of the carving, sending a faint, almost imperceptible hum through the wood that Lira feels through the soles of her boots. [309]
In that instant, she sees the pattern she has been searching for, the way the stress cracks align exactly with the raised ridges along the edge of the porcelain insulator, each ridge shaped to catch and amplify the vibration trapped in the wood. [352]
She leans forward, presses the insulator’s ridged edge gently against the largest stress crack, and uses the tip of her index finger to strum the thinnest edge of the sculpture, the same way she used to strum her grandmother’s old ukulele before the blight took it. [398]
The first note that rises is so quiet she thinks she might have imagined it, a high, clear trill that sounds exactly like the siskins she remembers from childhood, warm and bright and alive, wrapping around the grove like a soft blanket. [440]
The hedgehog freezes mid-nibble, its tiny black eyes widening as the note swells, the insulator carrying the vibration of the wood through each crack, layering in the hum of old wind, the rustle of long-gone leaves, the faint chirp of baby siskins that nested in the grove the year the sculpture was carved. [493]
As a symphonically minded artist, she has spent years mapping how every living and once-living thing holds a note, but she never thought she would hear them woven together so perfectly, a symphony written not by her hand, but by the slow, patient work of time on wood and clay. [543]
She does not cheer or jump for joy, only closes her eyes and lets the sound wash over her, the intensity of the quiet moment so thick she can almost touch it, no loud fanfare needed to mark the first music the world has heard in three years. [591]
She strums again, softer this time, and the note shifts to the low, warm rumble of her mentor’s laugh, the one he used when he told her that the best music is not made, but found, if you are willing to listen first. [634]
The hedgehog sits back on its haunches, its tiny nose twitching as it listens, and for a second Lira swears she sees a siskin, faint and translucent, flit across the top of the sculpture, its wings beating in time with the rising notes. [677]
She knows she will carry this sound with her when she leaves the grove, using the same method of reading stress cracks to pull music from every forgotten broken thing she finds, stitching the world back to life one quiet, gentle note at a time. [721]
