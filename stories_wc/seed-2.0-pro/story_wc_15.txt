Long after the tide pools empty of periwinkles and silver minnow, Lila, the poetic wharf weaver, picks her way up the weathered stone steps leading to the centuries-old labyrinth carved into red canyon walls. [34]
She carries two things tucked into the pocket of her linen apron: a spool of indigo thread twisted from sea grass, and a cracked storybook illustration plate she fished from the bay last winter, its faded ink showing a girl weaving garlands along the same labyrinth paths she now walks. [84]
For three generations, the weavers of her coastal town have been forbidden from entering the labyrinth, a precedent set after a landslide crushed a group of travelers there in 1872, but Lila is hauntingly optimistic, convinced the space can be reclaimed rather than left to crumble as a memorial to grief. [135]
Her motivation, to carve a path from ruin to reverence, grew after she found the storybook illustration plate, which she recognized as the work of her great-great-aunt, the last weaver allowed to tend to the labyrinth’s wild orchid patches before the ban. [177]
For six months, she has tracked her own sleepwalking paths, noting that the routes she takes while half-asleep overlap exactly with the faint, worn footprints etched into the labyrinth’s stone floors, routes no living town resident has walked in over a hundred years. [220]
Tonight, she plans to sew a continuous line of indigo thread along these overlapping sleepwalking paths, marking a safe route through the labyrinth’s sharp turns and loose stone ledges, one that honors the old traditions while breaking precedents that have kept the community cut off from a place that once brought them so much joy. [275]
The air inside the labyrinth smells of dust and wild white orchids that grow in cracks in the red rock, wrapping the space in a soft orchid solace that makes her feel like her great-great-aunt is walking right beside her, holding the other end of her spool of thread. [324]
She pauses every dozen steps to tuck a small petal from the orchids into the twists of her thread, and to run a finger over the storybook illustration plate she keeps tucked in her apron, checking that the path she is following matches the one drawn in faded ink all those years ago. [377]
By the time she reaches the center of the labyrinth, the sun is dipping low over the canyon rim, painting the red stone in streaks of pink and gold that match the watercolor washes on the storybook illustration plate, and she ties the end of her thread around a thick iron hook embedded in the center stone, left there by her aunt decades before. [441]
She sits on the cool stone for a few minutes, breathing in the soft orchid scent, and thinks about how the town’s elders will react when she shows them the path she has sewn, how some will be angry that she broke the old precedent, but others will remember the stories their grandparents told of feasts and flower crowns woven in this very spot. [505]
Before she leaves, she tucks the storybook illustration plate into a small niche in the center stone, weighting it with a smooth piece of red rock so it will stay safe for other visitors to find, a small marker of the history that was almost forgotten when the ban was put in place. [558]
On her walk back down to the town, she passes a group of children playing near the tide pools, and she tells them about the thread path in the labyrinth, and the orchids that grow there, and the drawing of the girl weaving garlands that waits in the center. [607]
The children listen wide-eyed, and before the sun fully sets, Lila can see them climbing the first steps to the labyrinth, following the bright indigo thread she wove earlier, their laughter bouncing off the red canyon walls and carrying down to the shore. [650]
Later that night, when the town elders come to her cottage to ask her about the broken precedent, she does not argue, but simply shows them the small orchid petal she tucked into her apron, and tells them about the overlapping sleepwalking paths that led her straight to the center of the labyrinth, as if the land itself was asking to be welcomed back into the community’s life. [718]
By the end of the week, the labyrinth is no longer a forbidden ruin, but a gathering place where families bring picnics, weavers make garlands to hang on the stone walls, and children leave small drawings beside the storybook illustration plate in the center, building new traditions that honor the old without being trapped by them. [774]
