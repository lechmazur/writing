Crisp amber maple leaves skittered past the leaded bay windows of the small town’s velvet-lined library, where the air smelled of old paper, cinnamon potpourri, and the faint, sharp tang of wood polish on vintage string instruments. [37]
Elara Voss, the town’s harmonious choir teacher, sat at a carved oak table, running a calloused thumb over the inlaid pearl zither soundhole rosette she had inherited from her grandmother, a refugee who had brought the instrument across three borders before the zither itself was destroyed in a 1990s bombing. [87]
She had spent the last two decades working across the timeframe of unlearning hatred in her community, her core motivation to braid diverse voices into harmony rather than forcing them to match a single, rigid pitch. [123]
The room hummed with a traveled stillness, holding the echoes of folk songs from 12 different cultural groups that had made this town home, and Elara was expectantly resigned, knowing most of the traditional instruments and vocal techniques her students brought to her were vanishing arts, fading faster than she could document them. [176]
Today, she was preparing to lead a workshop for her intergenerational choir, where she would teach them that harmony does not require erasing difference, and that the best way to denote the unique timbre of each voice is by subtracting noise, the unnecessary pressure to conform to a mainstream sound that had erased so many cultural musical traditions over the years. [237]
When the first group of students filed in, ranging from a 16-year-old Hmong teen who played the qeej to an 82-year-old Ukrainian grandmother who sang traditional kobzar ballads, Elara set the zither soundhole rosette in the center of the table, so everyone could see the delicate, swirling pearl patterns that had survived war, displacement, and decades of neglect. [295]
She told them the rosette had once framed the soundhole of her grandmother’s zither, and that its pattern was a map of the villages her grandmother had fled, each swirl representing a different folk song she had carried with her instead of material belongings. [339]
For the first exercise, Elara asked each participant to sing a 10-second snippet of a song from their cultural background, no adjustments or apologies allowed, while the rest of the group listened without speaking or interrupting. [375]
She explained that this practice of quiet attention was exactly what she meant by subtracting noise, because the pressure to perform for a mainstream audience was often the loudest barrier to keeping vanishing arts alive for the next generation. [414]
The notes each person sang would denote their own place in the larger harmony they were building, just as each swirl in the zither soundhole rosette had its own place in the larger pattern that framed the zither’s sound. [453]
Over the next two hours, the group wove their snippets together, adjusting tempo and volume only to lift each other up rather than overshadow one another, creating a sound that felt like a perfect reflection of the work Elara had done across her years of unlearning hatred and teaching others to do the same. [507]
When they finished their first full run-through of the combined piece, the room fell back into that soft traveled stillness, and Elara felt that familiar expectantly resigned tug in her chest: she knew she would never be able to save every vanishing art, every folk song, every traditional instrument that was fading as elders passed and younger generations assimilated. [566]
But she also knew that every time she helped braid diverse voices into harmony, she was carrying forward the same work her grandmother had started when she tucked that zither soundhole rosette into her coat pocket before fleeing her home, keeping a small piece of her culture alive to share with others. [618]
One of the youngest students, a 7-year-old whose parents were from Somalia, reached out gently to touch the edge of the rosette, and asked if she could add her own mother’s lullaby to the piece they were building for the town’s winter festival. [661]
Elara smiled and nodded, knowing that even if the zither that had once held the rosette was gone, the sound it had carried would live on in the harmony they were building, one voice, one song, one small act of listening at a time. [705]
