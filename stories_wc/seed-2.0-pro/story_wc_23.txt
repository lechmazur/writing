The patchwork navigator huddled on the rusted catwalk of the starlit signal station, three days after the hydraulics failed and left the tower stranded 200 feet above the coastal marsh. [30]
Her coat was stitched from scraps of flannel, rain slicker, and old linen tablecloths scavenged from every home she’d ever had to leave, the uneven patches mapping every gap in her memory of the people she’d lost. [67]
Clutched in her left palm was the patina bronze censer foot, the only fragment of her grandmother’s altar that survived the storm that washed her hometown off the map five years prior. [99]
She’d come to the station to build a signal for the reverse migration she’d spent years chasing, the slow, quiet drift of displaced coastal families moving back to the sunken lands their ancestors had called home, not just to rebuild houses, but to reclaim the grief they’d left buried under rising tides. [151]
The signal’s strength fluctuates with moon phases, she knew, the pull of the lunar cycle tugging at the salt water that saturated every inch of the sunken coast, warping radio waves and carrying the echoes of voices lost to storm surges. [192]
Her method of calibrating the signal came through cryptic phone calls at midnight, from strangers scattered across the coast who’d also found small, unbroken fragments of the lives they’d lost—a child’s ceramic mug, a grandfather’s pocket watch, a locket with a faded photo of a couple standing on a now-submerged pier. [244]
Her core motivation was not just to guide families home, but to navigate the geography of loss herself, to find the stretch of marsh where her grandmother’s cottage had stood, where she’d learned to burn sage in that bronze censer and listen to stories of the tides carrying lost loved ones back to those who waited for them. [302]
When the hydraulics failed three days prior, she’d thought her plan was ruined, the station’s old signal array dead, no way to broadcast the frequency she’d spent months tuning with those midnight calls. [335]
But she’d found a way to rig the signal using the censer foot as a grounding pin, its heavy, patinaed bronze conducting the faint static from the tower’s backup battery better than any official part she’d ever ordered. [373]
Tonight, the moon was full, so the signal was at its strongest, the air thrumming with the faint, distorted voices of the strangers who’d called her over the months, each sharing a line of a lullaby, a memory of a meal, a description of a landmark that only existed in their collective grief. [426]
She leaned against the station’s rusted railing, staring out at the dark expanse of marsh, and thought she saw tiny pinpricks of light bobbing just above the water, the first of the reverse migration travelers wading through the muck with their own small fragments of lost lives held tight to their chests. [478]
There was no sharp, triumphant rush of success, only the raw solace of knowing she wasn’t alone in her longing, that the grief she’d carried for so long wasn’t a weight to bury, but a compass to follow. [516]
She picked up the old payphone she’d rigged to the signal array, dialled the first number in her notebook, and whispered the first line of her grandmother’s favorite lullaby, the words carrying out over the marsh on the back of the moon-boosted signal. [559]
A voice on the other end picked up immediately, humming the next line, and she realized she could see the light from that caller’s lantern bobbing less than a mile from the base of the station, moving steadily closer through the tall, salt-crusted reeds. [603]
She ran a finger over the dents in the bronze censer foot, still warm from conducting the signal, and smiled through the tears stinging her eyes, knowing she’d already found her way to the part of the geography of loss that didn’t feel like empty space anymore. [650]
By dawn, the small crowd at the base of the station would be bigger, each traveler carrying their own small memento, each ready to help map the sunken lands and build new homes that honored the ones they’d lost, not erased them. [692]
The signal would dip and swell over the coming weeks as the moon waned and waxed, but it would never go out again, not as long as there were people willing to call in at midnight, to share their memories, to walk back into the lands that loved them even when the water tried to take them away. [750]
