The cryptic medium waded through the mangrove labyrinth’s brackish, knee-deep water, the air thrumming with the soft, warbling harmonies of the singing spirits that nested in the knotted aerial roots. [30]
She was here in the fragile, liminal space where an apology waits unspoken, her boots squelching over soft peat as she clutched the smooth, pitted gargoyle’s rainspout shard she’d pried from the ruins of her grandmother’s coastal chapel the week prior. [71]
She’d spent three nights leaving the shard on her windowsill to charge, because she’d learned as a child it heals in moonlight, its chipped edge softening and its core thrumming with warm, gentle energy each time silver light touched its porous surface. [113]
Her motivation was simple, sharp as the shard’s still-sharp tip: to find the moment before deciding, the split second three years prior when she’d chosen to hang up on her sister mid-fight instead of listening, when she’d let pride swallow the apology sitting heavy on her tongue. [160]
The singing spirits swirled around her wrists as she walked, their voices light as mist, guiding her toward the gnarled, glass-paned hut half-sunken in the mangrove roots at the labyrinth’s center, the only structure in the liminal space that held echoes of unspoken regret. [204]
She’d brought with her a stack of unsent letters, all addressed to her sister, all crumpled and rewritten a dozen times, words of regret and affection she’d never had the courage to send after the fight that had left them estranged, after her sister had moved across the country and cut off contact six months later. [260]
When she reached the hut, she pressed the gargoyle shard to the cold glass of the front window, closing her eyes and focusing all her energy to evoke the memory of that phone call, to pull the split second of choice out of the fog of the past so she could hold it, examine it, understand what she could have done differently. [322]
The singing spirits hummed louder, their voices weaving through her focus, as the shard’s warm energy seeped into the glass, and soon fine, dewy condensation bloomed across the pane, the method she’d been taught as a medium to pull lost moments into the present, written line by line in fog. [372]
First, the words of her unsent letters appeared, looping and shaky in the condensation, the apologies she’d written a hundred times, the memories of shared childhood trips to the mangroves, the promises to do better, to listen more, to never let pride win again. [416]
Then the moment itself appeared, a moving scene in the fog: her 26-year-old self holding a phone, jaw tight, finger hovering over the end call button, the sound of her sister’s voice cracking on the other end of the line, asking her to just wait, to hear her out. [465]
The medium pressed her palm to the glass, tears spilling down her cheeks, as she felt the fierce tenderness she’d buried for three years bubble to the surface, soft and unyielding, no longer hidden behind anger and regret. [503]
She’d thought she wanted to change the past, to reach through the glass and push her younger self’s finger away from the button, but she realized in that moment that she didn’t need to change anything, she just needed to see the choice clearly, to carry that softness back into the present with her. [557]
When the condensation began to fade, she pulled the first of her unsent letters from her bag, copied the address she’d memorized three years prior onto the envelope, and tucked the gargoyle shard inside along with the note, so it could carry the moonlight’s healing energy to her sister too. [607]
The singing spirits trilled softly as she turned to leave the labyrinth, their harmonies wrapping around her like a hug, and for the first time in three years, she didn’t feel the weight of the unspoken apology sitting heavy in her chest, just the quiet, bright hope that her sister would feel the same fierce tenderness when she opened the envelope. [668]
She stopped at the edge of the mangroves to drop the envelope in the floating post box tied to a cypress root, already planning what she would say when her sister called, already ready to listen, to hold space for whatever anger or hurt her sister might still carry. [717]
