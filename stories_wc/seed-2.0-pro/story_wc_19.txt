Lila, the bunker builder’s claustrophobic 12-year-old, counted the seconds until the annual gravity inversion, when the world would tip up for one full hour and she could escape the windowless concrete warrens her father spent every workday carving into the earth. [41]
Her secret motivation, one she’d never shared even with her dad, was to collect the flavors of different silences, each as distinct as wild honey, rain on pine, or the burnt iron tang of the bunker draft that stuck in her throat whenever he brought her to his job site. [91]
She’d learned to capture them by limiting exposure, slipping small, airtight glass vials into quiet spaces for exactly seven seconds before twisting the caps shut, enough to trap the essence without diluting it with the noise of her own breath. [131]
Tucked in the pocket of her overalls was the tiny porcelain doll her mother had left behind when she moved away three years prior, its painted cheeks rosy, its lace frayed at the cuffs, the only thing Lila ever brought on her inversion trips for luck. [177]
The first thing that happened when gravity flipped at 3:17 PM was her father’s heavy bunker blueprints lifting off the kitchen table, followed by her own light body, drifting up through the open skylight she’d left unlatched that morning, straight toward the xenial trellis above the clouds she’d spotted last year, woven from glowing ivy and strung with tiny white blooms that looked like they were waving her closer. [246]
The tone of the hour was stretching contracted, every second feeling both endless and fleeting, like taffy pulled thin enough to see through but still holding its sweet, dense core, and she knew she had to move fast if she wanted to capture the silence she’d been daydreaming about for 12 full months. [299]
When she curled her fingers around the trellis’s warm, vine-woven bars, she was hit first by the gilded hush that wrapped around her like sun-warmed linen, no hum of bunker drills, no crackle of the house’s old radiator, no distant roar of traffic from the valley below, just a quiet that glowed soft and gold. [354]
This silence was intangibly tangible, so thick she could almost taste it on her tongue, sweet as wild clover and rich as melted butter, and she fumbled in her overalls pocket for a vial, her fingers brushing the cool porcelain of the doll before she pulled the small glass tube free. [405]
She held the vial open for exactly seven seconds, as she always did, then twisted the cap shut tight, tucking it into the inner pocket of her jacket where it would stay safe even when gravity flipped back and pulled her down to earth again. [450]
As she drifted along the trellis, admiring the small blooms, she noticed a tiny, hard seed caught in the doll’s lace cuff, where it must have gotten stuck when she’d reached for the vial, and she tucked the doll back into her pocket carefully so she wouldn’t lose it. [499]
The remaining 20 minutes of the inversion passed in a blur of soft light and quiet, and when she felt the first faint tug of normal gravity pulling her back down, she waved at the trellis, already counting the days until she could return. [543]
That night, when she pulled the doll out of her pocket to set it on her windowsill, the tiny seed fell into the small terracotta pot she kept on the sill for succulents, and she decided to leave it there, curious what might grow from something that came from the trellis above the clouds. [597]
Three days later, she woke up to see a tiny, glowing green shoot begin to sprout from the soil, its leaves glowing the same soft gold as the gilded hush she’d captured, and she grinned, knowing she’d brought a piece of that perfect quiet back with her, even when she was stuck in the house with her dad’s bunker blueprints spread across the kitchen table. [662]
She pulled the vial of gilded hush out of her jacket pocket that afternoon, holding it up next to the small shoot, and she swore she could smell the trellis’s wild clover blooms drifting out of both, a small piece of the sky she could keep even on the days the valley was wrapped in thick gray rain and the bunker drills hummed so loud she wanted to scream.
