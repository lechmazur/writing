Fifty years after first contact with the silicon-based aeronauts of the Tau Ceti flotilla, the sunken benthic city of New Veyra still glowed with bioluminescent coral strung between rusted skyscraper spires, its residents long accustomed to filtering oxygen through gill implants and measuring time by the slow drift of deep-sea currents. [51]
Kael, a 62-year-old navigation instructor with a scar slashing across his left cheek from a childhood submersible crash, was balancedly curious by nature, never rushing to dismiss unproven theories but never gambling the lives of his students on untested tricks either. [92]
For three years, he had chased a quiet, stubborn motivation: to learn to navigate by starlight, a skill no resident of the deep sea had needed in the two centuries since the climate collapse flooded all surface land, until the aliens shared that their ships used cosmic positioning to cross light years without relying on manmade beacons. [149]
The only physical memento he kept of his surface-born grandfather, a fisherman who had sailed the last remaining open oceans before the flood, was a pitted, rusted boat anchor chain link, worn smooth on one side where generations of his family had rubbed it for luck before voyages. [197]
The aliens had also shared the mathematical method of edge geometry, a framework that maps the thin, permeable boundaries between layers of reality, allowing navigators to calculate the path of light as it bends through kilometers of ocean water, refracting off plankton, sediment, and thermoclines to reach the seabed intact. [247]
Kael spent six nights a week in his tiny spire apartment, running calculations on his waterproof datapad and holding the chain link up to the faint shafts of refracted starlight that seeped through the water, testing how the metal’s edges bent the light to match the edge geometry formulas he had memorized. [299]
The somber marvel of his work settled over him each time he spotted a pinprick of light that matched the coordinates of Alpha Centauri, a reminder that the world his grandfather had known, and the worlds beyond the sky, were not as out of reach as his people had believed for generations. [351]
Last month, he had finally gathered enough data to lead a small team of his top students on a test voyage, using only refracted starlight and edge geometry to navigate 300 kilometers along the seabed to the sunken ruins of old Miami, no GPS, no sonar beacons, no pre-plotted route to guide them. [404]
When they pulled into the ruins’ submerged docking bay right on schedule, the students cheered, but Kael only pulled the worn chain link from his pocket and held it up to the shaft of Vega’s light cutting through the water, feeling the weight of his grandfather’s unspoken wish to see his people sail again, even if it was beneath the waves instead of on top of them. [471]
The core concept of unfolded horizons had been a throwaway phrase in the aliens’ first cultural exchange packet, a description of how contact with new civilizations breaks down the assumed limits of what a species thinks is possible, but for Kael, it was tangible, a thing he could hold in his hand in the form of the chain link, a thing he could see in the starlight that reached him 4 kilometers below the ocean surface. [547]
Over the next year, he worked to expand the program, teaching every navigation student in New Veyra the principles of edge geometry and starlight navigation, helping them build their own small light sensors, encouraging them to look up through the dark water instead of only looking down at the seabed below their submersibles. [600]
He still kept the anchor chain link on a leather cord around his neck, rubbing its smooth edge before every test voyage, a quiet tribute to the grandfather who never lived to see the aliens arrive, never lived to see his people learn to sail the stars even from the bottom of the sea. [654]
On the day the first student crew navigated all the way to the Mariana Trench using nothing but starlight and edge geometry, Kael stood on the observation deck of the city’s central spire, watching the submersible’s lights blink into view as it pulled into the dock, the chain link warm against his chest under his diving suit. [711]
He thought of how his grandfather had once told him that horizons only feel closed if you stop looking for ways to reach them, and in that moment, he knew that the unfolded horizons stretching out before his people were endless, ranging from the deepest trenches of the ocean to the farthest stars in the galaxy, waiting for them to sail toward them one calculated path at a time. [780]
