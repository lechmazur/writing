The mist clung to Zephyr Bluff like crushed tinsel at the tilt of the earth toward spring, when the last of winter’s snow lingers soft at the north-facing ledges and the air smells of damp pine and unthawed possibility. [39]
Elara, a burned spy who’d been cast out of her agency three years prior after a mission gone wrong left her handler dead and her reputation in ashes, had climbed the bluff before dawn, her boots crunching soft over the slush-fringed snowpack. [82]
She’d spent those three years haunted by the choice she’d made to freeze instead of act, and she’d come here today motivated to find where unused courage waits, the same drive that had pushed her to dig through old agency archives for six months to find the coordinates her handler had hidden before his death. [137]
The bluff was wrapped in a silver hush, the mist muffling all sound of the town 300 feet below, so that the only noise was the faint whistle of wind through rock crevices and the soft thud of her pack hitting the frozen dirt at her feet. [184]
She pulled a dented engraved pillbox from her coat pocket, its surface etched with the small raven that had been her handler’s call sign, the same pillbox he’d pressed into her palm two weeks before the mission that broke them both. [225]
Her core concept for this trip had been simple: fixing what’s broken, even if the only thing she could fix was the splintered trust she’d broken in herself when she’d failed to pull the trigger that day. [262]
She’d always been prudently daring, the kind of agent who planned three escape routes before entering a room but never hesitated to take a calculated risk when lives were on the line, until that final mission when fear had frozen her solid. [304]
She’d learned last week that her handler had left a final message for her, hidden in codes in accumulated snow on this very bluff, marked only for the spring tilt when the snow would melt just enough to reveal the indentations he’d hammered into the rock below the snow line the winter before his death. [359]
She knelt, brushing the top layer of slush off the wide, flat rock ledge at the bluff’s edge, and the familiar pattern of dots and dashes blinked up at her, translated instantly by the muscle memory of years of spy work. [400]
The message read that he’d known the mission was compromised, that he’d chosen to stay behind to take out the target himself, that her choice to hold her fire had kept her alive so she could expose the double agent inside their agency who’d set them up. [447]
He’d also written that he would vouchsafe her place in the agency if she brought the evidence he’d hidden in the pillbox to the oversight board, the evidence he’d stashed there months prior, knowing he would not survive the mission. [487]
Elara’s breath caught in her throat, and for the first time in three years, the heavy weight of guilt that had settled on her chest lightened, because she realized her unused courage had never been missing—it had been waiting for her to stop blaming herself long enough to see it. [537]
She pried open the pillbox, and the small data drive tucked inside glinted in the faint gray light filtering through the mist, solid proof of the double agent’s crimes, solid proof that her handler had trusted her all along. [576]
She stood, brushing slush off her knees, and tucked the pillbox back into her inner coat pocket, right over her heart, where it would stay safe until she could present the evidence to the board the next morning. [614]
Fixing what was broken would not bring her handler back, but it would honor his sacrifice, clear her name, and take down a traitor who had put dozens of other agents in danger over the past five years. [652]
She paused for a moment at the edge of the bluff, watching the mist curl over the valley below, and smiled, feeling the old spark of her prudently daring self flicker back to life, sharp and steady as it had ever been. [694]
The silver hush of the bluff wrapped around her like a blessing, and for the first time in years, she did not feel like a burned spy cast aside by the only life she’d ever known—she felt like the agent her handler had always believed she was, ready to finish the job he’d started. [748]
