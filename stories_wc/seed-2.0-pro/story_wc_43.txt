The air in the radiant haven above the clouds hummed with pulsing stillness, thin and sweet with pine and the faint, unplaceable scent of hope that lingered whenever a patient’s final days drew near. [34]
Lila, the serene hospice music therapist who worked the cloud ward, had been waiting an hour for Mr. Hale’s results, and the second the blood work came back she slipped the crumpled envelope from the nurse’s tray without a word. [74]
She was decisively kind, never wasting a second on gentle lies or prolonged small talk when a patient only had 72 hours left to tie up the loose ends they’d carried for 87 years. [108]
Her core duty, as she saw it, was to execute a flawless goodbye for every patient under her care, one that felt less like an ending and more like a gentle closing of a book they’d loved reading. [146]
Tucked in the pocket of her linen scrubs was the piece of a broken vinyl record she’d dug out of Mr. Hale’s old storage box when he was first admitted, a shard of the 1952 hymn record he’d shared a first dance to with his late wife, Elara. [194]
Her preferred method to smooth these goodbyes was hymning the mundane, the small, ordinary moments her patients had cherished, rather than grand, formal prayers that never felt rooted in the lives they’d actually lived. [228]
She knocked softly on Mr. Hale’s door, stepping inside to find him propped up on pillows, watching cloud drifts float past his window, and she held up the vinyl shard before explaining her plan to reunify him with the memory he’d grieved since Elara’s death 12 years prior. [277]
He blinked at the shard, his weathered hands trembling as he ran a finger over the cracked edge, the faint groove of the hymn’s first verse still visible under the scuffs of decades of use. [312]
She pulled a small record player from the cart outside his door, the other half of the broken record already secured on its platter, and she nodded for him to set his shard in the empty gap along its edge. [352]
When he did, she dropped the needle, and instead of the static anyone would expect from a broken record, the soft, waltzing tune of their wedding hymn spilled out, warm and clear, as the scent of hope in the room grew thicker, tangled now with the lavender perfume Elara had worn every day of their marriage. [408]
She began hymning the mundane over the music, naming the small moments they’d spoken of in their sessions: burnt toast on Sunday mornings, dancing in the kitchen during rainstorms, the way Elara used to tuck daisies behind his ear before they went to church. [452]
Mr. Hale’s eyes fluttered shut, and a soft smile spread across his face as he swore he could feel Elara’s hand in his, her thumb brushing the back of his knuckles the way she always did when they danced. [491]
Lila stayed quiet, letting the pulsing stillness of the haven wrap around the two of them, the music looping soft and low as the clouds outside glowed pink with the start of sunset. [524]
When the song ended for the third time, his breathing had softened to a slow, steady rhythm, and he opened his eyes to tell Lila he was ready, that he didn’t have any more loose ends to tie up, any more regrets to carry. [568]
She squeezed his hand gently, her decisively kind smile soft as she told him he could go whenever he wanted, that Elara was waiting, that the dance floor up ahead was always open for them. [603]
He nodded, his eyes drifting shut again as the record continued to play, and by the time the sun dipped fully below the cloud line, he was gone, his hand still curled loosely around the vinyl shard, a faint smile still on his face. [647]
Lila tucked the other half of the record into the small wooden box she kept under her cart, a collection of broken, memento pieces from every patient she’d helped have a flawless goodbye, each one a reminder that the scent of hope didn’t fade when a life ended, it only shifted, wrapping around the ones left behind and the ones moving on. [709]
She paused for a minute to listen to the faint echo of the hymn still lingering in the hallway, the pulsing stillness of the haven settling back over the ward as she prepared for her next patient, ready to meet whatever small, cherished memory they needed to reunify with before they said their last goodbye.
