Dust motes gilded by slanted amber dusk light drifted through the curved, honey-hued walls of the grotto, where Lira, a counterfeit emotion dealer, knelt on smooth, resin-slick stone as her transformation completed. [32]
Professionally thorough, she’d spent three weeks calibrating every variable of the ritual, from the temperature of the grotto’s trapped air to the timing of the summer solstice dusk that seeped through its only opening. [66]
Resting on the stone before her was the final ingredient: a crumpled fragrance chemist’s blotter petal, steeped in the half-remembered joy of a stranger who’d tested a new perfume at a downtown market stall two weeks prior. [103]
Lira closed her eyes and held her palm an inch above the blotter, sensing through static electricity the faint, frayed edges of the emotion woven into the paper’s fibers, each tiny spark carrying a snippet of the stranger’s unspoken thoughts. [143]
She dealt in half truths, after all: the emotions she sold were never fully fake, never fully real, stitched from fragments of real feeling that felt true enough to soothe the lonely, the grieving, the people who’d forgotten how to feel anything at all. [187]
Her entire career had built to this moment, her quiet motivation to fuse insight with empathy, to stop selling hollow fragments and start crafting emotions that could actually heal, not just numb the edge of hard days. [224]
She watched the static sparks dance with the affectionate detachment of someone who’d spent years holding other people’s feelings at arm’s length, careful not to take them on as her own, until now. [257]
When the sparks peaked, glowing the same warm amber as the grotto walls, she willed her newly transformed core to subsume the blotter petal and all the tangled, half-true feeling it held, letting it seep into the cracks of her own ability to craft emotion. [302]
The blotter dissolved into fine golden dust that drifted up to settle on her palms, each particle sending a tiny jolt through her nervous system that carried flashes of the stranger’s life: a first kiss under a cherry blossom tree, the sting of a friend’s betrayal, the quiet pride of growing a tomato plant from seed. [358]
She didn’t flinch, didn’t cling to any of the memories, holding fast to that affectionate detachment as she let the fragments weave themselves into the part of her that could spin emotion into something salable, something useful. [395]
Professionally thorough as always, she’d run dozens of test runs with smaller fragments, making sure she could absorb these slivers of real feeling without losing herself, without becoming overwhelmed by the weight of hundreds of strangers’ joys and sorrows. [434]
Her half truths had always been kind, anyway; she never sold false hope, only small, gentle feelings that could help someone get through a hard hour, a bad date, a holiday alone when they missed their family too much to breathe. [475]
Now, with the blotter petal’s essence woven into her, she could add layers of real, lived empathy to those half truths, making them feel less like a band-aid and more like a soft hand on a shoulder, a quiet reminder that no feeling is entirely unique, no pain is entirely unshared. [526]
She tilted her face up to the last sliver of dusk light slipping through the grotto’s opening, sensing through static electricity the faint pull of dozens of people in the nearby town who were craving a little comfort that night, their needs humming in the air like faint radio static. [576]
Her transformation was fully complete now, the last tingle of the blotter’s essence fading as it settled fully into her core, her goal to fuse insight with empathy finally realized without the cost of the careful distance she’d always kept. [616]
She brushed dust off her trousers and stood, already drafting the first batch of new, richer half truths she’d sell the next day, no longer just a counterfeit emotion dealer, but a weaver of small, gentle, almost real comfort for anyone who needed it. [660]
