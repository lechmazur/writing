Upon the hush of budding hope, when winter’s last frost had melted into the moss of the verdant parapet perched beside Silverthread Falls, Mara, the wise orchard pruner of the border villages, knelt to adjust the wicker basket slung at her hip. [42]
She was insatiably creative, her long years tending to the valley’s fruit trees having taught her that growth required as much imagination as it did precision, and today she had a task more sacred than any seasonal trim. [80]
Her motivation was simple, if extraordinary: to preserve the color of wonder that had begun to fade from the mortal world, as children stopped chasing fireflies and adults forgot how to gasp at the first spring bloom. [117]
The veil between the mortal realm and the world of fey wilds was thinnest here, the waterfall’s constant mist acting as a permeable barrier that allowed weaving between worlds for those who knew how to read the currents of light and air. [159]
She pulled the small velvet pouch from beneath her wool shawl, and tipped its only content into her palm: an heirloom seed, passed down through seven generations of orchard tenders, its surface swirled with flecks of iridescent blue that glowed faintly in the dappled sunlight. [204]
Mara had spent three months perfecting her method, by capturing essence of the fey wilds’ most vibrant joys to activate the seed, so that when planted in the mortal world it would bloom into a tree that bore fruit infused with that forgotten wonder. [248]
The air hummed softly around her, and she closed her eyes, letting the mist kiss her cheeks as she reached across the veil, her fingers brushing first the soft petal of a fey poppy that glittered like crushed starlight, then the downy wing of a glowing sparrow that darted through the mist. [300]
She pulled each wisp of bright, unburdened joy into the seed, which grew warmer in her palm, its blue flecks spreading until the whole surface shimmered like a tiny piece of the summer sky. [334]
A soft whimper pulled her from her focus, and she opened her eyes to see a small fey fox, its paw caught in a crack of the parapet’s stone, its golden fur matted with mist and blood, its big eyes wide with fear. [377]
Without hesitation, Mara shifted to succor the tiny creature, working the sharp stone edge loose with her pruning knife before wrapping the injured paw in a scrap of soft linen from her basket, humming the old lullaby her grandmother had taught her to calm skittish fawns. [423]
The fox nuzzled her palm after she finished, and a warm, golden spark of gratitude drifted from its nose straight into the heirloom seed, which flared so bright for a second that Mara had to squint against the light. [462]
She realized then that she had been missing the most important essence of all: the joy of connection, of kindness freely given and received, which was the root of all true wonder in any world. [497]
The fox darted off into the mist a moment later, its paw already healing, and Mara turned back to her work, her heart lighter than it had been in years, the radiant hush of the space between worlds wrapping around her like a blanket. [541]
When she felt the seed hum with enough energy, she tucked it back into its velvet pouch, knowing that when she planted it in the center of the village square the next morning, it would grow into a tree whose blossoms would glow with fey light, whose fruit would taste like summer afternoons and firefly chases and the warm fuzz of a fox’s fur against your palm. [608]
She stood, brushing moss from her trousers, and looked out over the waterfall, where the sun was beginning to dip low, painting the mist in streaks of pink and orange that blurred the line between the mortal and fey worlds even more. [650]
Children would soon start to see the fey lights dancing in the mist again, she knew, and adults would pause on their way to market to stare at the tree’s glowing blossoms, remembering the wonder they had forgotten how to feel. [691]
She had not only captured the essence of fey joy to fuel her seed, she realized, but had rekindled a little of that wonder in herself, too, in the quiet act of stopping to help a small creature in need. [731]
As she turned to walk back down the path toward the village, the seed hummed softly against her chest, a tiny, glowing promise that the color of wonder would not be lost, not while there were people willing to look for it, and to share it with anyone who needed a reminder. [783]
