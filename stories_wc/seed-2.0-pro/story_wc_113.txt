Elara, a viola player who’d served ten years in the downtown opera house’s orchestra pit, waded calf-deep through murky, cold water sloshing over the lowered stage’s edge, the air thrumming with the hush of unstruck chords left hanging when the evacuation order cut the final dress rehearsal short. [48]
She’d been lately inspired by old tales of the opera house’s resident ghost, a 1920s soprano who’d died mid-performance when a collapsed balcony crushed her mid-aria, leaving behind a rumored coded message only another musician could decode. [85]
The only light came from flickering lanterns strung by the fire department before they’d cleared out, their golden glow bouncing off the water’s rippling surface to paint wobbly gold splotches across velvet seat cushions and frayed red curtain hems. [124]
Her mission tonight, the reason she’d snuck past the barricade, was to unravel the code left by the ghost, who was said to have hidden clues in a 1927 antique opera program printed for the performance where she’d died. [163]
She spotted the program tucked in the lip of the conductor’s podium, its faded cream edges peeking out just above the water line, half-swallowed by the slow-rising flood. [191]
She’d long been fascinated by the in between spaces of the opera house: the gap between curtain rise and first note, the breath between a singer’s phrase and the audience’s applause, the liminal stretch of time where the living and dead could share the same air. [237]
She carefully lifted it, heart thrumming, but a sudden ripple from her movement sloshed over the cover, and she watched, mesmerized, as water damage creating images she’d never seen before bloomed across the pages, blurring the old cast lists into smudges of dark ink that formed musical notation. [285]
It felt like a wordless conversation, the ghost responding to her presence by revealing what she’d hidden for almost a century, each smudged note a greeting, each bar of melody a secret shared only with someone who cared enough to stay behind when everyone else ran. [331]
She realized the notation was a short viola solo, the very piece she’d been fumbling to write for weeks, the one she’d felt pulled to compose for no clear reason, and she knew she was meant to play it to disrupt the ghost’s endless cycle of being stuck in that final, unfinished performance. [384]
She reached for her viola case, propped on the highest step of the pit where the water hadn’t reached, and pulled out the instrument, running her fingers over the strings to tune it quickly by the light of the nearest lantern. [425]
The first note rang out clear and warm, cutting through the damp, cold air, and the water around her rippled even without a breeze, the lantern light flickering faster as if the ghost was leaning in to listen. [463]
When she reached the final bar, a soft, wordless sigh echoed through the empty house, and she looked up to see a faint, glowing figure of a woman in a silver 1920s gown standing at the center of the stage, smiling, before the figure faded away entirely, leaving only the soft lap of water against the podium. [520]
She looked down at the antique opera program in her hand, and the water damage had settled into clean, crisp notation, no longer smudged, the full score of the solo she’d just played printed perfectly across every page, the ghost’s final gift to her. [564]
She’d come to unravel a ghost’s code, but what she’d found instead was the missing piece of her own artistic voice, the piece that would make her upcoming solo debut the most talked about performance the opera house had seen in decades. [606]
She slipped the program into her waterproof bag, slinging her viola over her shoulder as she waded back toward the exit, the sound of the ghost’s sigh still lingering in her ears, soft and grateful. [641]
When she stepped outside into the cool night air, the rain that had caused the flood had slowed to a soft drizzle, and she felt lighter than she had in years, like a weight she hadn’t known she was carrying had lifted right along with the ghost. [688]
Three months later, when she stepped onto the stage for her debut, she held the antique program in her bag, and as she played the first note of the ghost’s solo, she swore she heard a soft, cheering voice in the back of the hall, quiet enough only she could hear. [739]
