Marnie, the lion tamer’s widow, perched on a scuffed oak stool beneath her rain-beaded skylight loft, waiting for the kettle to boil as she ran a calloused thumb over the grain of an ash wood carving blank. [37]
The blank had been tucked in her late husband Elias’s tool chest for three years, ever since the arena fire that took him and the three big cats he’d raised from cubs, and she’d only worked up the courage to pull it out that morning, driven by the quiet urge to harvest lessons from loss instead of letting grief fester unacknowledged. [98]
The rain streaking the skylight filtered the late autumn sun into a rust-hued peace that wrapped around her like the well-worn wool blanket Elias had crocheted for her during their first winter on the carnival circuit, soft enough to soften the sharp edges of memory without dulling their warmth. [147]
Elias had always been hauntingly practical, even when the carnival’s bookkeepers warned him they couldn’t afford extra fire extinguishers for the cat enclosures, even when Marnie begged him to skip the final show of the season when the electrical wiring had started spitting sparks the night before. [194]
He’d loved whittling small figures of the cats in his off hours, saying it reminded him that everything worth loving was here and gone, that the time you had with a thing was far more important than how long the thing itself lasted. [237]
She’d spent weeks researching old folk carving techniques after his death, looking for a way to contain the pieces of him she still carried in every quiet moment, and she’d finally found it: a method of sealing memory into wood through crystallized time, the practice of carving only in the small, unrushed windows of quiet routine where memory sat thick and unhurried. [299]
The kettle on the small hot plate by her workbench had started to hum, the first sign it would be screaming in three more minutes, exactly the window she’d set aside to carve the first marks into the ash blank. [339]
She picked up Elias’s favorite whittling knife, its handle worn smooth where his palm had rested against it a thousand times, and traced the first curve of Zara’s mane, the tiny tawny cub that used to sneak out of the enclosure to curl up on their trailer’s couch while they ate dinner after shows. [393]
She could almost smell Elias’s pine soap and the cub’s faint hay-streaked fur as she carved, the lines coming easy, no hesitation, as if his hand was resting over hers guiding the blade through the soft ash grain. [431]
She didn’t grieve the fact that they were gone anymore, not in the sharp, tearing way she had the first year; instead, she held the warmth of their presence in every cut, knowing that the time they’d shared was real, even if it couldn’t last forever. [477]
By the time the first wisp of steam curled over the kettle’s rim, she’d carved the outline of Zara’s face, the tiny tuft of fur between her ears, and the faint curve of a smile that matched the one the cub always wore when she’d stolen a bite of Elias’s sandwich. [528]
The lesson she’d been chasing for three years clicked into place right then: loss didn’t erase the good parts, it just gave you the chance to carry them with you, to make something soft and lasting from the pieces they left behind. [570]
The kettle screamed right as she finished that thought, and she set the half-carved figure on the workbench, running her thumb over the smooth curve of Zara’s cheek before reaching over to turn off the hot plate. [607]
She poured boiling water over the chamomile tea Elias had loved, smiling as the steam curled up to fog the edge of the skylight, mixing with the rain drops to make soft, blurry patterns of light on the workbench. [646]
She’d carve the rest of Zara the next time the kettle boiled, then the other two cats, then Elias, each small figure a little piece of the love that had never really left, just changed form. [682]
For the first time since the fire, she didn’t feel like she was missing pieces of herself, just holding them closer, preserved in ash and wood and the quiet, golden minutes of ordinary days. [716]
