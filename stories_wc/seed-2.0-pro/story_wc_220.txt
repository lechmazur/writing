Moss, a bloodhound retired from finding bodies in the rubble of the last resource war, patrolled the perimeter of the scrapyard settlement spanning a cracked wasteland every dusk, his paws scuffing rusted sheet metal and crumbled asphalt. [37]
Tucked in the frayed canvas pouch slung around his neck was an autumn leaf preserved in wax, a trinket he’d pulled from the pocket of a young mother he’d located three years prior, long after her family had given up hope of finding her alive. [82]
The settlement hung in a wild hush most evenings, the wind carrying only the faint creak of stacked scrap and the distant whistle of toxic dust devils that no one dared approach. [114]
Moss’s long, drooping ears still twitch at the faintest trace of decay, but these days he uses his intuitively descriptive sense of smell for smaller, softer jobs: tracking lost trinkets, locating stray settlement kids, even sniffing out mold in stored grain supplies before it can make anyone sick. [162]
His most self-appointed mission, though, is driven by a quiet motivation to translate distance into closeness, helping settlement residents connect to loved ones they lost to the war, long before their remains could be found or their final words shared. [202]
The old crank phonograph he’d dragged out of a crushed media storage unit six months prior is his most cherished tool, its brass fittings pitted with rust but still functional when he greases its gears with rendered scavenged animal fat. [242]
Most residents bring him cracked, dust-caked phonograph cylinders they find in scrap piles, convinced they hold nothing but static and long-dead music, but Moss knows better, his sensitive nose able to pick up the faint impression of ink and pressed paper left in the cylinder grooves, letting him read secret texts in the grooves of a phonograph cylinder that no human eye could ever parse. [307]
Last week, a teen girl who’d been orphaned as a toddler brought him a dented cylinder she’d found tucked in the frame of a busted pickup truck, her hands shaking as she explained the family name etched on its side matched the one stitched into the baby blanket she’d been found wrapped in. [360]
When Moss slid the cylinder onto the phonograph and cranked it slowly, he didn’t just pick up the grain of an old folk song: he caught the faint, faded impression of a handwritten note pressed into the wax during manufacturing, a riddle scrawled in looping cursive that referenced a stand of maple trees that had stood on the edge of the wasteland before the war. [425]
The wild hush of the settlement thickened as he worked, the small crowd that had gathered to watch holding their breath as he described the words one by one, his nose twitching as he traced each faint indent in the cylinder’s grooved surface with a careful paw. [472]
The riddle’s answer pointed to a hollow boulder half a mile west of the settlement, and when the girl led a small group there at sunrise, they found a dented metal box tucked in the rock, full of family photos, a handwritten journal, and a handful of pressed autumn leaves just like the one Moss carried in his pouch. [531]
These tinted revelations, softened by decades of dust and decay and never quite as sharp or complete as anyone hopes, are the closest most settlement residents ever get to closing the gap between the lives they lost and the hard, quiet lives they’ve built in the scrapyard’s shadow. [579]
Later that day, the girl brought Moss a loose autumn leaf she’d found tucked between the pages of her mother’s journal, and he helped her dip it in beeswax melted from scavenged old candle stubs, letting her hang it next to the string of other preserved leaves he strung outside his scrap wood hut to mark every family he’s been able to reconnect across years of loss and distance. [648]
As dusk falls again, he tucks the girl’s mother’s leaf next to his own memento in his pouch, his tail giving a slow, soft wag as he listens to the faint hum of the settlement settling in for the night, no longer tasked with finding the dead, but with finding the small, warm traces of the people who once loved the things the wasteland left behind. [714]
