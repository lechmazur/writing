Three weeks after the common household spoon began warping small pockets of local reality, Elara, a budding rune scholar, perched on the edge of a scuffed oak workbench in the nocturnal observatory perched on a floating isle 3,000 feet above the mist-shrouded lowlands. [43]
She was quietly deliberate in every movement, brushing crumbs of crushed moonstone from her notebook rather than swiping them away, as if even small, careless motions could unravel the already fraying fabric of the world around her. [80]
For the past month, she had been fixated on a single, seemingly trivial question that no other scholar would deign to investigate: where shadows go at noon, when the sun hangs directly overhead and every dark edge seems to vanish without a trace. [123]
The only clue she had found so far was a dented brass clockwork automaton key, tucked in the binding of a 200-year-old treatise on runic sealing that had been donated to the observatory’s archives the week before the reality-bending event began. [164]
The treatise outlined a long-forgotten magical principle of sealed awakenings, which held that dormant energies hidden in the fabric of the world could be roused only by mending broken cycles rather than casting new spells. [199]
Elara had quickly realized that the standard rune casting she had spent three years studying was useless in the warped post-spoon reality, and that she would have to relearn the entire craft from the ground up, using the treatise’s suggested method of working by closing loops of unfinished magical business left by past scholars. [253]
The first loop she intended to close was the unfinished work of the treatise’s author, a 19th century rune scholar who had built a clockwork observatory automaton designed to track shadow movement across all hours, but died before he could wind it for its first trial. [299]
After three nights of searching the observatory’s cluttered storage towers, she had found the automaton tucked beneath a tarp, its brass joints untarnished, its face etched with tiny runes for light and shadow, its keyhole perfectly shaped to fit the dented key she carried. [343]
On the night before the first solar eclipse since the spoon phenomenon began, she carried the automaton to the observatory’s main viewing platform, set it on the stone railing, and inserted the key, turning it slowly, one small click at a time, in keeping with her quietly deliberate nature. [392]
When the last click sounded, the automaton whirred to life, its glass eyes glowing soft silver, a small panel on its chest sliding open to reveal a blank runestone that slowly filled with swirling dark ink, the first sign of the sealed awakening the treatise had promised, as the old scholar’s unfinished work finally reached its intended start. [450]
She left the automaton running through the night and into the next day, checking its readings every hour, watching as it recorded the length and angle of every shadow that crossed the observatory’s stone floors, waiting eagerly for the clock to strike noon so she could finally get an answer to her question. [503]
When the bells chimed twelve, every visible shadow in the observatory vanished, as expected, but the automaton’s runestone continued to swirl, its tiny mechanical pen scratching across a roll of parchment tucked in its base, mapping a realm of compressed shadow that existed just beneath the layer of visible reality, a pocket dimension that stored all shadows when the sun was directly overhead. [566]
Elara gasped, realizing that the shadow realm was also the place where the reality-warping energy from the bent spoons was leaking from, a rift that had opened when the old scholar’s work was left unfinished for two centuries, its loops left open long enough to fray the edges of the physical world. [618]
Now that she had closed the first loop by awakening the automaton, she knew she could use the relearned runic techniques from the treatise to seal the rift, one closed loop at a time, mending the damage the bent spoons had caused while also learning more about the hidden shadow realm she had spent months searching for. [675]
That evening, she sat back down at her workbench, the clockwork automaton key resting on a velvet cloth beside her notebook, and began to outline the next loop she would close, her quietly deliberate approach the perfect tool to untangle the messy, frayed edges of reality one small, careful step at a time. [728]
