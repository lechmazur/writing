After the cloud-strung carousel, its horses carved from bleached coral and strung with discarded film canisters, ground to a permanent, whirring halt, the resolute scavenger stepped onto the glossy, shifting surface of the kinetic lagoon floating three miles above the Pacific’s storm-wracked surface. [43]
She was chronically chronologically confused, some days waking convinced she was 17 and riding the carousel for the first time, other days swearing she’d already spent 70 years picking through the lagoon’s debris, no calendar or sun clock ever holding steady long enough to confirm either. [89]
The air hummed with fractured stillness, the lagoon’s slow, sloshing movement the only sound, no wind, no bird calls, no distant hum of the world below cutting through the thick, cotton-soft quiet. [121]
Her only consistent motivation, the one that pulled her out of bed even on the days she could not remember her own name, was to negotiate with her own ghost, the wispy, half-visible figure that drifted across the lagoon’s farther spits, always just out of reach. [167]
She had learned, over months or decades of wandering, that the only way to draw the ghost close enough to speak to was by collecting fragments of overheard stories, snippets of half-remembered conversations, old jokes, whispered regrets that had floated up from the world below and caught in the lagoon’s cloud-fog. [218]
She stored each story snippet wound around the disposable camera wheel she’d pulled from the carousel’s control panel the day it stopped, the cracked plastic rim catching the faint glow of the cloud-steam as she turned it to make space for each new memory. [262]
The stories she collected were not random; she sought out ones that matched her own eroded ambitions, the half-forgotten dreams of becoming a wildlife photographer that she’d abandoned at 22, when a car crash had stolen the use of her dominant hand and left her convinced she would never hold a camera steady again. [316]
Regret sat heavy in her chest every time she turned the camera wheel, each snippet a reminder of all the trips she’d never taken, all the photos she’d never shot, all the versions of herself she’d let die when she’d packed her gear away in a storage unit and never looked back. [368]
Today, as she knelt to tuck a snippet of a photographer describing a shot of a humpback whale breaching at dawn into the camera wheel’s grooves, she looked up to see her ghost standing three feet away, its face soft, familiar, no longer out of reach. [414]
She held out the camera wheel, the edges of the stories peeking out like the edges of undeveloped film, and spoke first, her voice rough from disuse. [441]
“I know I gave up on the thing we wanted most,” she said, no longer confused about the time that had passed, no longer ashamed of the years she’d spent hiding from the choices she’d made. [477]
The ghost’s voice sounded like wind through old film canisters, soft, tinged with the same regret she carried, but no anger, no judgment. [500]
“You don’t have to negotiate for forgiveness,” the ghost said, reaching out to tap the disposable camera wheel, the stories glowing bright as it made contact. [526]
“You just have to admit that the ambition didn’t erode because you couldn’t hold a camera, it eroded because you were scared you’d never be good enough, even before the crash.” [557]
She sucked in a sharp breath, the truth hitting her harder than any grief she’d ever carried, the fractured stillness of the lagoon wrapping around her like a blanket as she nodded, tears sliding down her cheeks. [594]
For the first time since the carousel halted, the chronological confusion lifted, and she could see every year of her life clearly, the mistakes, the small joys, the abandoned dreams all fitting together like the snippets of stories wound around her camera wheel. [637]
She didn’t need to negotiate with the ghost anymore, she realized, because the ghost was just the part of herself she’d left behind, the part that still believed she could pick up a camera again, even if she had to hold it with her left hand, even if her first shots were blurry, even if she was 62 years old and had wasted decades being scared. [703]
When she looked up again, the ghost was gone, and the kinetic lagoon’s surface had stilled, no longer shifting under her boots, the air clearing so she could see the blue curve of the ocean far below, glinting in the sun. [744]
She slipped the disposable camera wheel into her pocket, the stories warm against her thigh, and turned toward the stairs leading down from the carousel platform, ready to go home, ready to buy a new camera, ready to stop letting regret rule the rest of her days. [791]
