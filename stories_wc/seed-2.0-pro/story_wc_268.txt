Deep in the ancient temple library lost in time, where dust motes danced in slants of sunlight that had not shifted in three eons, Kael, a diminutive titan barely taller than the oak reading desk at the chamber’s heart, ran a calloused finger along the spine of a leather-bound astronomy tome. [51]
Regally unpretentious, he wore no crown save for a thin braid of starflower woven into his silver hair, and carried no scepter, only a chipped clay vial holding the single iron gall ink pellet he had inherited from his father, the rightful high titan. [95]
His quest, to unmask a pretender to the throne who had seized power by forging his father’s name on a false succession edict, stretched across the pause of shifting constellations, the brief 200-year window when the sky’s star patterns held still enough for ancient runes to retain their magic. [144]
Kael had chosen to spread his truth through cryptic messages left in library books, knowing only the titan realm’s most dedicated scholars, who still made the pilgrimage to the lost library once per generation, would stumble on his clues and carry them back to the realm. [190]
Each message was marked with a rune of drift, a tiny, swirling sigil that only glowed for those with untainted royal blood in their veins, ensuring the pretender’s spies would see nothing but blank parchment if they intercepted the volumes. [230]
He mixed a drop of his own blood with crushed pigment from the iron gall ink pellet to draw each rune, the slow, deliberate work taking him nearly a century to mark every relevant text in the library’s vast collection. [270]
As the final year of the constellation pause arrived, Kael climbed the library’s highest observatory to wait, watching the first group of scholars arrive, their faces lighting up as they pulled a history of titan succession from the shelves and spotted the glowing rune on its first page. [318]
Word spread back to the titan realm faster than Kael had dared hope, the scholars sharing the hidden proof of the pretender’s forgery at every council and village gathering until the entire realm knew the truth of who had stolen the throne. [360]
The tone of the pretender’s fall was one of graceful collapse, as he gave no violent resistance when Kael returned to the realm, his only protest a quiet mumble that he had seized power only because he believed the rightful heir had died in the library’s collapse centuries prior. [409]
Kael did not seek to punish the pretender, but he did need to sunder the false edict the man had nailed to the realm’s central spire, a public act that would formally erase the false claim to power once and for all. [451]
He took the remaining shards of the iron gall ink pellet with him to the spire, grinding them into a fine dust that he sprinkled along the edges of the false edict before running a single finger along the rune of drift he had drawn on the document’s corner. [500]
The paper split cleanly down its center, the two halves drifting to the cobblestones below as the gathered crowd cheered, their voices echoing off the spire’s marble walls and carrying all the way to the edge of the realm. [539]
Kael refused the heavy golden crown the court attendants tried to place on his head, asking only that they reweave the starflower in his braid and arrange for the ancient temple library lost in time to be restored and opened to all citizens of the realm, not just scholarly pilgrims. [589]
In the years that followed, he made regular trips back to the library, leaving new cryptic messages in library books for future generations, each marked with the rune of drift to ensure only those with pure intentions could access the truths he had worked so long to protect. [637]
The pause of shifting constellations ended a few months after his coronation, the stars sliding back into their slow, eternal dance, and Kael knew he had used the small window of magic exactly as his father had intended when he left him the iron gall ink pellet all those centuries before. [688]
The former pretender, for his part, took a post as the library’s first head steward, spending his days mending old tomes and guiding visitors to the texts that held the hidden messages, his own graceful collapse into a quiet life of service a reminder that even the worst mistakes could be redeemed. [740]
