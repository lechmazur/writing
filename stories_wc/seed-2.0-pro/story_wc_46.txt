The earthbound star watcher hunkered in the serpentine grotto in mist, calloused fingers sifting over damp stone as memories resurface of the night their stellar core crashed into the cold northern sea. [32]
They had spent three decades in this hidden cove, honing gem polishing as a way to channel the faint, warm glow trapped in their chest, for their core motivation was to share light without using fire that would scorch the fragile coastal life they had grown to love. [80]
Today, their palm closed around a piece of sea glass, frosted at the edges from years of being tossed by tides, that hummed in time with the slow thrum of their core. [112]
They lifted it to the narrow slit of sunlight cutting through the grotto’s mist, and smiled when they saw it was reflectively vibrant, bouncing gold and pale blue light across the curved, green-veined serpentine walls. [147]
They sat back on their heels, hefting a small set of brass scales from their work pouch to weigh the sea glass against a chip of their own stellar core they kept tucked in a velvet lining, calculating how much of their glow they could safely infuse without shattering the fragile glass. [199]
This was the final step in crafting the pearl of stillness they had dreamed of since their first winter on the coast, when they had watched a group of lost children stumble through the dark bluffs, crying for their parents, unable to see the path down to the village. [248]
They had wanted to summon their starfire then, to light the way, but they knew the heat would have set the dry cliff grass alight, so they had held back, huddling in the grotto until the village search party found the children hours later, cold and scraped but unharmed. [297]
A soft, buoyant energy shaped every movement now, the zephyr bloom tone of their work wrapping around them like a shawl as they ran the first polishing cloth over the sea glass, rubbing in fine pumice to smooth the frosted edges until the glass glowed like captured sky. [347]
Over the next three hours, they worked through finer and finer grades of polishing compound, pressing a small sliver of their stellar core to the center of the glass with each pass, letting the cool, steady glow seep into the glass’s molecular structure. [390]
When they finished, the pearl of stillness rested in their palm, no bigger than a robin’s egg, radiating a soft, steady light that did not burn, did not flicker, and would never run out as long as their own stellar core still hummed in their chest. [436]
They tucked it into a woven reed pouch slung over their shoulder, brushing crumbs of polishing compound off their trousers before stepping through the curtain of mist at the grotto’s mouth, the salt wind tangling their silver hair as they descended the path to the village. [482]
The village elder was sitting on her porch, mending a fishing net, when the star watcher arrived, and she held out a gnarled hand without looking up, already knowing what gift had been brought for the community. [519]
The star watcher placed the pearl of stillness in her palm, and the elder’s eyes widened when she felt the gentle warmth, watched the reflectively vibrant light bounce off the wooden porch rails and the wild rose bushes climbing the cottage walls. [561]
That night, the village hung the pearl in the center of the bluff’s warning post, where it cast a wide, soft circle of light across the cliff paths and the rocky shore, guiding lost travelers, fishing boats returning after dark, and even the small shorebirds that migrated through the region every spring. [613]
The star watcher stood at the mouth of the serpentine grotto later that night, watching the faint glow of the pearl dance across the waves, and they smiled, knowing they had finally found a way to share the light of their home star without risking harm to the world that had become their new home. [668]
They reached into their work pouch and pulled out another piece of sea glass, worn smooth by years of tides, and they hefted the scales once more to weigh it against another small chip of their core, already planning the next pearl they would craft for the village schoolhouse, so children could read after sundown without the risk of candle fires. [729]
The soft zephyr that curled through the grotto carried the faint scent of wild clover and sea thrift, a zephyr bloom of quiet joy that settled in the star watcher’s chest, warm and bright, as they settled back onto their stone workbench and pulled out their first polishing cloth to begin the process again. [782]
