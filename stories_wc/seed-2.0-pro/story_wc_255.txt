Three days after the phoenix rises, the colorblind painter slips into the glassblower’s courtyard, his canvas bag slung over one shoulder stuffed with dried fern fronds, clover sprigs, and bleached wheat stalks. [32]
He is innocently cunning, having spent the last week researching the glassblower’s hidden grief over his late wife, a folk musician who had played the concertina at every village fair for 40 years. [65]
His core motivation, the quiet drive that has guided every piece he has made since he learned of his red-green color blindness as a teen, is to find beauty in imperfection, and he knows the glassblower holds something he needs to unlock that goal. [109]
The courtyard is dotted with half-blown glass vases warped by the phoenix’s passing heat, shards of iridescent glass glinting in the ash-tinged sunlight, and a weathered wooden workbench where a cracked leather concertina rests, its innards exposed where the glassblower had tried and failed to repair it after the fire. [159]
The key component he has come for is the concertina reed tongue, thin and bent from the fire, stuck fast in its frame so it can no longer hum when air passes over it, a perfect metaphor for the beauty of broken things he has been chasing his whole career. [209]
He does not knock or call out, because he has long preferred communicating through plant arrangements, his color blindness making the texture, shape, and placement of foliage far more meaningful than any verbal message or painted color could ever be. [249]
On the edge of the workbench, he arranges sprigs of clover to spell out “memories”, fern fronds curled to look like tiny concertinas, and wheat stalks crossed to mark a fair trade, no money exchanged, only the quiet magic of bartered awakenings the village had practiced since the first phoenix rose a century prior. [303]
He tucks the bent reed tongue into his bag as the glassblower rounds the corner from the workshop, and he holds up a hand to stall the older man’s angry shout, nodding at the plant arrangement and then at the warped glass vases stacked against the courtyard wall. [351]
The glassblower’s anger softens when he sees the arrangement, recognizing the clover as his wife’s favorite flower, and he nods slowly, understanding that the painter will trade him a piece of art that honors his wife in exchange for the useless broken reed. [394]
That night, the painter works in his small attic studio, mounting the bent concertina reed tongue at the center of a canvas woven through with dried foliage and dotted with tiny shards of the warped glass he had collected from the courtyard floor earlier that day. [440]
He does not use any paint, relying instead on the contrast of rough reed, soft foliage, and sharp iridescent glass to create depth, knowing that his inability to see color lets him focus on the beauty of the objects’ inherent flaws rather than the distraction of their hues. [488]
When he brings the finished piece to the glassblower three days later, the older man bursts into tears at the sight of the reed that had once made his wife’s favorite songs, surrounded by foliage that reminds him of the meadow where they had their first date. [535]
As the glassblower runs a finger along the bent edge of the reed, a soft, quiet hum seems to drift from the canvas, as if the reed has begun to vibrate on its own, and they both feel the long-dormant memory of the glassblower’s wife awaken, warm and bright, between them. [586]
The painter leaves the courtyard that afternoon with his chest light, knowing he has finally found the beauty in imperfection he has spent his whole career chasing, turning a broken, useless reed into a vessel that holds more love and meaning than any perfectly painted, full-color portrait ever could. [635]
Word of the piece spreads through the village quickly, and soon other residents bring him their own broken, discarded treasures, asking him to create works that awaken their own lost memories, each trade another small example of the quiet magic of bartered awakenings that binds the community together after every phoenix rise. [687]
