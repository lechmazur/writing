The year thoughts became taxable, Kael, a charming rogue scholar with a penchant for smuggling unregistered ideas across district borders, stumbled into an abandoned entomologist’s greenhouse tucked between two tax audit offices. [32]
He’d been hiding from a thought audit patrol when he spotted a scrawled note tucked under a potted milkweed, written in the signature candidly cryptic script of the Hushvine, the city’s underground secret language club he’d been trying to track for six months. [75]
The note invited him to join their network, whose core method of evading thought tax was joining community gardens to plant message patterns only visible to members who knew to track the bloom times of specific native flora. [113]
Tucked in the fold of the note was a single gourd rattle seed, its shell etched with a tiny bee, the Hushvine’s official symbol of unmonitored communication, since the tax bureau had never figured out how to audit the thoughts exchanged between pollinators and plants. [158]
Kael had spent three years dodging repeated attempts by the tax bureau to harvest and monetize his most subversive thoughts, so his core motivation, to stand guard over your own spark of defiance instead of letting the state siphon it for profit, aligned perfectly with the club’s mission. [206]
The first task he was assigned required him to warp the standard layout of the downtown community garden, mixing gourd rattle seeds in with sunflower and tomato plantings to spell out a warning about an upcoming citywide thought audit sweep. [246]
The week of the planting, half a dozen unrelated disruptions began piling up at once: the city announced plans to pave over three community gardens for a new tax processing center, a rogue beekeeper released 10,000 unregistered pollinators downtown, and a leak revealed the tax bureau had started auditing gardeners’ private planting journals for hints of unreported ideas. [304]
Kael retreated to the old entomologist’s greenhouse to meet with the Hushvine leadership, only to find the space buzzing with dozens of club members, beekeepers, local gardeners, and even a handful of disgruntled tax bureau clerks who’d grown sick of harvesting other people’s creativity for a paycheck. [351]
The converging chaos of the moment felt like a spark catching tinder, every separate group’s frustration folding into a single, unquantifiable thought no tax algorithm could possibly assign a dollar value to. [383]
Kael pulled the gourd rattle seed out of his pocket, holding it up to the greenhouse’s sun-streaked glass, and the group quickly agreed to adjust their plan, expanding the planting pattern to cover every community garden in the city instead of just the downtown plot. [428]
Over the next three days, they snuck out under cover of night to warp garden rows, tuck gourd rattle seeds into raised beds, and scrawl candidly cryptic clues on fence posts to guide other unregistered thinkers to their cause. [467]
When the tax audit sweep launched the following Monday, auditors found dozens of community gardens full of unidentifiable plant patterns, thousands of pollinators swarming the plots to carry the hidden messages across district lines, and crowds of local residents gathering to tend the gardens, all openly admitting they were there to tend to thoughts the state had no right to tax. [528]
The auditors tried to assign a value to the mass act of defiance, but every formula they ran crashed, every thought-tracking device they brought to the gardens short-circuited when it got close to the blooming gourd rattle plants, and the public backlash against the thought tax grew so loud that the city council was forced to hold an emergency hearing to repeal the law entirely. [593]
Kael stood in the old entomologist’s greenhouse a week later, shaking a dried gourd rattle grown from the first seed he’d planted, as new members of the secret language club lined up to get their own seeds and learn how to plant message patterns in neighborhoods across the region. [642]
He’d long believed that the only way to protect a spark of defiance was to share it, and the converging chaos of the past month had proven that even the most oppressive tax system could not put a price tag on a community dedicated to speaking its mind, out loud and in plain sight, no matter who tried to silence them. [703]
