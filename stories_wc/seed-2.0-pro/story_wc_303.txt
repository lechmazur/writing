The velvet observatory smelled of crushed mulberry and ozone on that midsummer evening, Elara’s knees pressed to the threadbare plum upholstery of her workbench as she sorted through heirlooms for a noble client who claimed lineage to the old frost elven dynasties. [42]
She’d never asked for the quiet, unshakable telepathy she shared with all plant life, a side effect of a childhood lab accident with enchanted mulberry sap that had left her fingers stained deep purple year round, a constant hum of root and leaf and bloom in the back of her skull at all times. [96]
The most unusual artifact in the lot was a violin carved entirely of flawlessly transparent ice crystal, its strings spun from frozen starlight, no melting at room temperature, no maker’s mark anywhere on its curved, glassy surface. [133]
Her client had promised her enough gold to buy back the forest her family had lost to the imperial logging syndicate if she verified the violin belonged to his ancestor, the last frost queen, but she knew confirming the lineage would let him lay claim to the northern ice territories, letting the empire expand its mines and displace thousands of indigenous frost clans. [196]
She had three days to deliver her verdict, before the noble’s promise of gold lost its glow and he handed the contract off to a less scrupulous researcher who would falsify the provenance without a second thought. [233]
Her usual provenance checks had turned up nothing, so she turned to the strange divination method her grandmother had taught her, swirling black oat coffee in a chipped ceramic mug before upending it over a sheet of parchment, waiting for the dark, sprawling stains to settle into visions of the past and future. [286]
What she saw made her chest tight with that familiar mulberry ache, the sweet, bruised pain of a perforated destiny, full of holes where choices could slip through and rewrite every outcome she’d been taught was fixed. [324]
The violin had not been carved by the frost queen, but by her human partner, a herbalist who had imbued the ice with the seeds of ten thousand ancient tree species, designed to propagate old growth forest across any barren land when played with a bow strung with mulberry wood. [374]
The potted mulberry sapling on her workbench hummed in agreement, pushing a single ripe berry off its branch to roll across the parchment and land directly on the coffee stain that showed the violin played over the imperial strip mines, trees erupting from cracked rock in seconds. [421]
She knew she could turn the violin over to the noble, get the gold, and save her family’s small forest, but that would condemn hundreds of thousands of acres of northern land, and tens of thousands of people, to destruction at the empire’s hands. [465]
This was the hole in her perforated destiny, the choice no one had ever told her she was allowed to make, that she could put the needs of the many above the grief of her own family’s lost legacy. [504]
She sent the noble a forged report saying the violin was a cheap tourist replica, packed the ice crystal instrument into a padded case, and snuck out of the observatory before the sun peeked over the horizon, heading north to meet the frost clan elders she’d communicated with through the pine trees lining the road outside her cottage months earlier. [564]
When she played the violin for the elders two days later, the ice hummed against her neck, and the first saplings pushed through the frost-covered soil of the abandoned imperial mine site, spreading faster than she could have imagined, enough to cover her family’s lost forest, and every other barren stretch of land in the empire, in a matter of years. [625]
The mulberry stain on her fingers tingled as she watched the saplings grow, that soft mulberry ache lingering not from grief, but from the quiet joy of choosing a destiny that fit in the holes of the one she’d been given, long before the noble’s empty promise ever had time to lose its glow. [679]
