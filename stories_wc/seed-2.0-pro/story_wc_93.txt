<story>
Three days after friction vanished from every surface and particle of the known world, the elusive eel herder Kael picked his unsteady way up the sloped face of the tidal bluff, pale green and deep violet auroras rippling above the choppy silver sea, the entire landscape bathed in the soft, pale gold of borrowed dawn, a light that never dipped below the horizon no matter the hour, as if the sky had borrowed the first hour of sunrise to hold back the dark forever. [83]
Kael was precognitively cursed, each vision of small, unavoidable future pain seared into his bones the second he caught sight of another living thing, so he’d retreated to this stretch of coast ten years prior, only ever seen by passing fishermen as a wisp of gray wool against the rocks, his reputation as elusive well earned. [139]
He’d come to this bluff tonight because a lingering promise he’d made to his grandmother, the last person he’d allowed himself to love before the curse showed him her slow, painful death from lung cancer three months before it happened, hummed in the back of his mind, pushing him to finally act on the sacred patterns she’d taught him as a small boy, patterns she said held the power to fix even the most broken of worlds. [216]
In the leather pouch slung over his shoulder, he carried two things: a small glass vial of collapsed star dust his grandmother had given him the day she’d made him promise to use the patterns if the world ever broke beyond ordinary repair, and a pouch of smooth river rocks he’d painted with iridescent blue and silver stripes the week before, when he’d first had a vision of friction vanishing. [286]
His goal tonight was to harness the faint, residual cosmic energy tangled in the auroras above, to stitch friction back into the fabric of the world, and the only method he knew to do that was placing painted rocks in mathematical sequences, each rock aligned to the Fibonacci spiral his grandmother had traced in the sand for him a hundred times as a child. [348]
He knelt carefully, his boots sliding a small hollow in the soft, moss covering the bluff top first, then laid the first rock at the center of the spiral, then the next, spacing each a foot to the left, then the next to the right, each placement precise, his fingers brushing the cool stone warm from hours of memorizing the sequence, no mistakes allowed, if he messed up the energy would slip away forever. [422]
When the last rock was placed, he unscrewed the vial of collapsed star dust, sprinkled the fine, shimmering gray powder over the center of the spiral, the dust caught the borrowed dawn light and the aurora glow, glowing bright silver for a heartbeat, then a soft hum filled the air. [472]
For a split second, his curse flared, showing him a future he’d never dared to hope for: people walking without slipping across polished floors, mugs staying perched on table edges, children running across pavement without sliding into traffic, his own hands holding a stranger’s without flinching away from the cold knowledge of their inevitable pain. [528]
He held his breath, waiting for the hum to grow louder, the ground under his boots no longer sliding, friction sewn back into the world’s fabric, when he pressed his hand down onto the moss rough bark, he knew his grandmother’s lessons had worked, the lingering promise honored at last. [580]
The auroras faded slowly, the borrowed dawn glow softening into real pink and tangerine sunrise spilling over the horizon, the salt wind carrying the faint splash of eels leaping in the tide pools below the edge of the bluff, the familiar silver flashes of their scales catching the new light. [630]
He smiled, for the first time in ten years, no vision of pain clouding his view, the sacred patterns he’d spent his whole life studying finally making sense, the elusive eel herder no longer hidden from the world he’d helped save. [671]
