The son of the boy who cried wolf had traveled three days across choppy, salt-crusted waves to reach the lighthouse prison perched on a jagged reef, where the air hummed with the quiet weight of unspoken mistakes. [37]
He was there chasing hush, the kind of silence that could scrub the stain of his father’s legacy of false alarms from his name, before they find the bodies of the two fisherman who’d vanished near the reef three weeks prior, the ones the local patrol had written off as his latest bad joke. [91]
Copperlight repose flooded the prison’s lower halls as the sun dipped low over the water, gilding rusted iron bars and scuffed floorboards with the warm, worn sheen of old pennies. [121]
His motivation was simple: to thread a labyrinth with his own story, separate from the punchline that had clung to his family for generations, to prove he could be trusted when he said something was very, very wrong. [159]
He found his way to the back of the abandoned prison chapel, where a lattice from a confessional leaned against a rotting oak pew, its carved oak slats worn smooth by decades of whispered secrets and pleas for forgiveness. [198]
Old timers from the mainland had told him the lattice sustains impossibilities, holding onto every story ever spoken near it, even the ones the rest of the world had discarded as lies or fantasy. [232]
The only way to access the lattice’s stored memories, they’d said, was through the scuffs on a narrow door left ajar for decades, tucked between the chapel’s back wall and a stack of moldering hymnals. [267]
He knelt before the door, running his fingers over the shallow, boot-shaped scuffs that crisscrossed its lower edge, and breathed out the first true sentence he’d ever spoken about the fishermen: he’d seen a rogue wave sweep their boat against the reef, not a drunk captain steering off course. [316]
The scuffs glowed the same soft copper as the light flooding the room, and the door swung open just wide enough for him to step through, into a winding corridor lined with floating slips of paper, each holding a fragment of a forgotten story. [360]
This was the labyrinth he’d come to thread, and as he walked, he spoke his truth out loud, each sentence attaching itself to an empty slip of paper, lighting it up with the same copper glow. [396]
Halfway down the corridor, he saw the lattice hovering in front of him, its slats glowing so bright he had to squint, holding all the stories he’d ever told, and all the ones his father had told before him. [435]
He reached out, and the lattice pulled his father’s first false wolf cry forward, then the next, then the last, laying them bare not as jokes, but as the panicked lies of a lonely boy who’d wanted someone, anyone, to pay attention to him. [479]
Next came his own story of the fishermen, stitched together with the fragments of other witnesses’ forgotten accounts: a lighthouse keeper who’d seen the wave, a vacationer who’d taken a blurry photo of the wreckage, a teen who’d heard the men’s cries for help. [523]
When he reached the end of the corridor, he stepped back out into the chapel, and the lattice drifted behind him, settling against the pew again, now holding the full, unbroken record of his truth, unfiltered by his family’s bad reputation. [564]
He walked back out to the lighthouse’s edge, where he could see the coast guard’s boat speeding toward the reef, and he smiled, knowing that when they found the bodies, they would find the lattice’s stored memories waiting for them too, proof that he had told the truth this time, that his story was his own. [620]
The copperlight wrapped around him like a blanket, soft and steady, as he waited for the patrol to dock, finally free of the shadow of his father’s mistakes, finally able to stop chasing hush long enough to be heard. [659]
