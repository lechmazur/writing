The cryptic fortune cookie writer huddled on the frosted observation deck of the glass spire by a frozen lake, breath pluming in the sharp, still air as pale pink light began to bleed over the edge of the world at dawn. [41]
She was chronologically homesick, not for a place, but for the soft, uncomplicated timbres of her childhood, when every rustle of wind through pine boughs sounded like a snippet of a song no one else had ever bothered to name. [81]
For years, she’d tucked half-formed verses and snippets of these forgotten melodies into fortune cookies, but she craved more: her motivation, sharp and unshakable, was to become the conductor of unheard symphonies, to weave all those half-remembered sounds into something tangible for the whole world to hear. [128]
She knew she had to finish her work before exhaustion hit, the cold seeping through her wool coat already making her fingers stiff and her eyelids heavy, so she pulled her most prized possession from her coat pocket: a smooth, pearlescent nautilus shell segment, ridged with tiny, evenly spaced grooves that looked like the bars of a sheet of music. [188]
The tone of the pre-dawn world was an upturned hush, every sound muted by the thick layer of ice covering the lake, the surface so perfectly smooth that it created a mirrored horizon, where pale pink sky and frosted white ice blurred into one indistinguishable expanse of light. [236]
She’d spent weeks researching how to bring her symphony to life, and landed on her method: by mapping childhood memories to the ridges of the nautilus shell, then running her finger along the grooves as she stood in the perfect acoustic pocket of the glass spire, she could pull each memory’s sound into the air, where it would hang and mix with the others into a coherent composition. [304]
She closed her eyes and pressed her index finger to the outermost ridge, and the first sound bloomed: the high, tinkling laugh of her seven-year-old self, chasing fireflies through her grandmother’s backyard, light and bright enough to make the frost on the spire’s glass panels glitter. [350]
She slid her finger inward to the next ridge, and the low, rumbling purr of her old tabby cat joined in, followed by the patter of summer rain on a tin roof, the crunch of pine needles under snow boots, the soft lilt of her grandmother’s voice singing old lullabies off-key. [401]
Each sound tangled with the next, wrapping around each other like threads in a tapestry, echoing off the glass walls of the spire and bouncing out over the frozen lake, where the mirrored horizon doubled each note, turning a single melody into a layered, sweeping chorus that felt alive, thrumming with the weight of all the small, precious moments she’d almost forgotten. [463]
She kept moving her finger along the ridges, her pace steady even as her limbs grew heavier, the cold seeping deeper into her bones, and she smiled as she realized the symphony was exactly what she’d imagined, no gaps, no wrong notes, every memory fitting perfectly into the structure of the nautilus’s spiral. [516]
When she reached the innermost ridge, she held her finger there for a long beat, letting the final note, the quiet thud of her grandmother’s heart as she held her as a small child, hang in the air before she pulled her hand away, the symphony continuing to echo across the lake on its own, self-sustaining. [572]
She leaned against the glass wall of the spire, watching the sun climb higher over the mirrored horizon, the symphony wrapping around her like a warm blanket, and she knew she’d succeeded, no longer just a writer of tiny, hidden fortunes, but the conductor of unheard symphonies, the keeper of all the small, beautiful sounds no one else had thought to save. [634]
Exhaustion was beginning to tug at the edges of her vision now, but she didn’t mind, because she’d finished her work right on time, the symphony echoing on, immortal, for anyone who bothered to stop and listen to the quiet magic of the frozen lake at dawn. [681]
She tucked the nautilus shell segment back into her coat pocket, her fingers brushing the crumpled stack of fortune slips she’d brought with her, each one now marked with a line of the symphony’s sheet music, ready to be slipped into cookies and sent out across the world to anyone who needed a little bit of hidden magic.
