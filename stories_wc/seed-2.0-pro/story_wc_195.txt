The withdrawn knight leaned against the frost-fogged pane of the alchemical orchard greenhouse, shrouded in endless night, his dented pauldrons dusted with crushed starflower pollen. [25]
He had come hours earlier, right as reality shifts began to warp the edges of the potted quince trees, their leaves bleeding faint gold when he brushed past them. [54]
His quiet mission, shared with no one, was to capture the shape of possibility, the ghost of the life he had abandoned when he swore his first doomed vow to a fallen king. [87]
Half-buried under a tray of moonwort cuttings, he spotted a crumpled, abandoned marionette theater program, its ink faded to pearlescent silver where the greenhouse’s mist had seeped into the paper. [117]
He knew better than to dismiss the paper as trash; the greenhouse’s alchemical infusions made every discarded item here factually mystical, charged with the echoes of the choices that led to it being left behind. [152]
He knelt, brushing the starflower dust off his gauntlet first, then reached up to unsnap the leather clasp of the scroll case slung at his hip, tucking the program inside carefully so he did not smudge the silver ink. [191]
To draw out the possibility trapped in the program, he knew he had to work through forgotten patterns, the old weaving chants he had learned as a boy before his family sent him to the royal barracks. [228]
As he pulled the program back out and spread it on a stone workbench dusted with dried sage, he let slip a murmured allegiance not to any crown, but to the quiet, unchosen lives he had never gotten to live. [268]
The silver ink on the program flared to life, projecting faint, wispy figures of marionettes dancing across the greenhouse, their strings tangled with the branches of the apple trees that grew golden fruit glowing like tiny suns. [305]
He watched, his mouth softening for the first time in ten years, as the marionettes acted out scenes from the life he could have had: a small cottage, a workbench full of carved wooden dolls, a hound curled by a fire, no armor, no oaths, no blood on his hands. [355]
The reality shifts warped the vision further, blending the greenhouse’s endless night with the warm glow of the cottage hearth, and for a long moment he could feel the heat on his face, the rough texture of the doll carver’s knife under his fingers, the hound’s soft breath on his boot. [406]
This was the shape of possibility he had chased for so long, not a fixed path to step into, but a soft, malleable ghost that he could carry with him, even when he returned to the cold world outside the greenhouse. [447]
A surge of shivering bliss ran up his spine, making his shoulders shake, as he realized he did not have to choose between the knight he was and the carver he could have been; he could carve small wooden trinkets for the village children on his travels, he could tend to potted starflowers on his windowsill, he could let the oaths that bound him loosen, just a little. [515]
When the marionette vision faded, the ink on the program had turned a soft, warm brown, no longer glowing, but still holding the faint hum of the life it had shown him. [547]
He folded the program carefully, tucking it back into his scroll case before he snapped the clasp shut again, his fingers lingering on the worn leather for a long moment. [577]
As he turned to leave the greenhouse, he reached up to pluck a single golden apple from the nearest tree, its skin warm to the touch even in the endless, misty dark. [609]
He did not know when the reality shifts would settle, or if the world outside would look the same as it had when he arrived, but for the first time in as long as he could remember, he did not fear whatever came next. [653]
He had captured the shape of possibility not by stealing a new life, but by learning to carry the ghost of it alongside his own, and that was more than he had ever dared to hope for. [690]
