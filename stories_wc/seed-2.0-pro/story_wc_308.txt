The harmonic stone skimmer hunched on a weathered ledge of calcified bighorn rib, surrounded by the towers of bone in a canyon of endless echoes, where even the softest breath ripples through centuries of layered dust clinging to every jagged surface. [41]
They were a being of cracked, porous sandstone crusted with petrified moss, inherently historically reverent, their existence tied to every being that had fallen, hunted, or sought refuge in this canyon since the first human clans carved the first hunting trails into its red walls. [86]
For three hundred cycles of sun and snow, their core motivation had been to learn to navigate without moving, to reach the scattered pockets of lost memory tucked in the canyon’s farthest crevices without shattering their brittle, aging stone body on the sharp, bone-strewn rock below. [132]
The previous winter, a storm had dislodged a shard of mastodon tusk from the highest bone tower, revealing a chipped, patinaed stone arrowhead tucked in a hollow of the tusk’s core, its edges still sharp enough to slice through the thin layer of dust that settled on it each moon. [182]
They recognized it as the work of the first clan that had worshipped them as a guardian of the canyon, and they knew the object held a pulse left wandering, the echo of the young hunter who had fired it at a bighorn sheep twelve thousand years prior, only to miss and watch it clatter into the tusk as the herd fled. [244]
To access the pulse’s memory, they first needed to appraise its full history, to trace every moment it had lay hidden, every shift of dust and crack of bone that had surrounded it through the millennia, a task they had long learned to complete through the patterns in a flock of birds in flight, the only moving force in the canyon that carried echoes of every era in their wingbeats. [314]
They waited three days for the flock of canyon wrens to return, their weathered resolve holding even as a harsh wind scoured more tiny flakes of stone from their shoulders, even as the echoes of distant rockslides rumbled through the canyon’s throat for hours on end. [360]
When the wrens arrived, darting between the spires of fossilized mammoth bone and ancient human skulls stacked by centuries of flash floods, the skimmer tuned the low, thrumming vibration of their core to the frequency of the birds’ wingbeats, letting the birds’ swirling, shifting formations map every layer of time the arrowhead had existed through. [416]
Each dip of a wren’s wing marked a decade of dust settling on the arrowhead, each tight turn of the flock marked a shift of the tusk it was tucked inside, each synchronized dive marked a season where the canyon had held enough rain to feed small flowers at the base of the bone towers. [471]
As they finished the appraisal, the wandering pulse of the hunter stirred, reaching out to the skimmer’s core, and the skimmer felt the weight of the hunter’s unfulfilled wish to find his way back to his clan after he’d gotten lost chasing the bighorn herd, a wish that had kept the pulse tied to the arrowhead for all those millennia. [531]
In that moment of connection, the skimmer learned the first lesson of navigating without moving: that movement was not tied to physical form, but to the threads of memory and longing that wove every being, every object, every moment in the canyon together into a single, interconnected web. [579]
They guided the hunter’s pulse along those threads, carrying it through the layers of time to the spot where his clan had set up their winter camp twelve thousand years prior, letting the pulse finally rest where it had belonged for so long, while the wrens swirled overhead, their formation shifting to mark the end of the hunter’s long wandering. [639]
Now, the skimmer could feel every thread in the web stretch out before them, leading to every hidden memory pocket, every lost pulse, every corner of the canyon, no physical step required to reach any of them, their centuries of patience and reverence finally paying off in the quiet, dust-choked air of the canyon. [693]
They tucked the stone arrowhead into a small hollow in their own stone chest, where it would sit as a marker of their first journey without moving, as the echoes of the hunter’s quiet thank you bounced off the bone towers for hours, merging with the endless hum of the canyon that had been their home for as long as they could remember. [756]
