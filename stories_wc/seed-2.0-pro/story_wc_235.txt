The lucid dream chronicler dragged his scuffed hiking boots over the threshold of the weathered mountain ranger cabin, his throat still tight from the shock of stumbling on his own moss-choked grave ten minutes prior, tucked between two spruces at the edge of the treeline. [45]
Tucked under his arm was the cracked, resin-sticky birch bark scroll he’d pried loose from the grave’s crumbling headstone, its edges frayed from decades of rain and frost, marked with his own messy, looping scrawl he’d thought he’d outgrown at 22. [86]
He’d spent 12 years documenting every sharp, vivid detail of his lucid dreams, and the last three chronologically confused, unable to tell if the memories knocking around his skull were from waking life, a dream three nights prior, or a version of himself that lived 50 years in the past. [136]
His whole work was built on the promise to distill chaos into narrative, to pull frayed, disjointed threads of impossible experiences into something linear, something that made sense, even if it only made sense to him. [172]
Unrolling the birch bark scroll on the cabin’s splintered pine table, he was hit with a wave of feral nostalgia, sharp and hungry enough to make his teeth ache, as he read accounts of hikes he’d never taken, of stargazing nights on this very cabin’s porch he had no memory of living. [231]
He’d learned long ago that the easiest way to access lost timeline threads was getting jealous of past selves, coveting the joy they’d gotten to live that had never made it to his version of the timeline, letting that envy pull him through the thin veil between what was and what could have been. [289]
The jaded wonder he’d carried through every dream, every weird timeline glitch, every quiet discovery of a life he hadn’t lived, flared to life in his chest as he traced the scroll’s description of a flight over the snow-capped peaks that loomed outside the cabin’s smudged window. [344]
He closed his eyes, let the envy of the self that had written the scroll wrap around him like a blanket, let the thrum of the cabin’s old wood, the faint scent of pine resin and old wood smoke, seep into his bones until he felt the familiar lightness that preceded a lucid shift. [398]
When he opened his eyes, he was no longer hunched over the table, his bare feet hovering an inch above the scroll, the cold mountain air whipping through his hair as he began to soar out of the cabin’s open window, over the treeline, past the spot where his grave sat half-buried in moss. [456]
Below him, he spotted the other version of himself, the one that had written the scroll, sitting on the cabin’s porch strumming a beat-up guitar, a half-empty mug of coffee beside him, his face soft with the kind of peace the chronicler hadn’t felt in years. [506]
He didn’t try to land, didn’t try to call out to the other him, because he knew this was how the threads wove together, how the stories wrote themselves, each version of him leaving breadcrumbs for the next to find. [549]
The scroll’s words were already rearranging in his head, stitching together the disjointed timelines, the grief of seeing his own grave, the sharp joy of flight, into a narrative that didn’t need to follow linear time to feel true. [597]
He looped once over the highest peak, the snow glinting pink in the setting sun, and wondered if the next version of himself, the one that would find his grave 50 years from now, would feel this same quiet, aching wonder when they read the words he would leave tucked under his own headstone. [656]
When he landed back on the cabin’s floor, his fingers were already stained with pine resin, his notebook open on the table, ready to write the story that would tie all the loose ends together, for the him that had come before, and the him that was still to come. [704]
He paused for a second, tracing the edge of the birch bark scroll one last time, and smiled, knowing that for the first time in years, the chaos in his head finally felt like it belonged somewhere. [737]
