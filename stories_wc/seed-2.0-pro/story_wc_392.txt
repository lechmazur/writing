The preventable death investigator stepped into the damp, moss-furred subway maintenance tunnel three days after the drought ended, the hem of his frayed work coat catching on rusted rebar jutted from the cinder block wall. [37]
He clutched a dented, glass-paneled lantern from the Underground Railroad, a family heirloom his great-great-grandmother had carried north to freedom, instead of the standard-issue LED flashlight his department required for tunnel inspections. [74]
His demeanor was watchfully calm, his eyes sweeping the uneven floor and cracked ceiling for signs of structural failure, his boots making soft squelching sounds in the puddles left by the first heavy rains in four months. [112]
He was here to investigate the death of a 19-year-old maintenance worker who’d been crushed when a section of the tunnel ceiling collapsed three days prior, a death the city had already ruled an unavoidable natural disaster tied to post-drought soil shift. [157]
His core motivation for taking the case, even when his supervisors told him to drop it, was to break free from a controlling influence: his late mother, a former transit worker who’d spent 30 years enforcing the department’s code of silence around preventable accidents, even after her own brother died in a 1998 tunnel collapse the city wrote off as “user error.” [219]
The tunnel hummed with entangled memories, every glint of rust on the rails pulling up overlapping moments: his grandmother sitting on his childhood porch telling him stories of hiding in root cellars by the lantern’s light, his mother slamming the lantern in a closet when he was 12 and telling him to stop clinging to “ancient nonsense” and focus on following the rules she set for his future. [281]
He had always conducted his investigations through empirical observation, rejecting guesswork and hearsay in favor of hard, measurable evidence, a habit he’d developed to push back against his mother’s demand that he accept whatever the department told him as fact. [328]
He knelt to run a caliper along a jagged crack in a wooden support beam, noting the rot that ran three inches deep into the wood, a flaw that would have been visible at least 18 months prior if anyone had bothered to inspect the beam properly. [373]
The slow drip of rainwater seeping through the porous tunnel ceiling began to reverberate off the curved concrete walls, matching the thud of his own pulse, and he could almost hear his mother’s voice reverberating through his childhood kitchen, yelling that he would get himself fired if he kept questioning the department’s rulings. [429]
He let himself pause for 10 seconds, letting the tight knot of grief and anger in his chest soften, his fortified vulnerability letting him acknowledge how scared he’d been his whole life to disappoint her, how much he’d wanted to make her proud even when she was wrong. [481]
He pulled out his camera to photograph the rotted beam, then the forged inspection sticker nailed to its side, dated just six months prior and marked “fully sound,” the exact evidence he needed to prove the city had ignored clear warning signs and the young worker’s death was entirely preventable. [537]
He lifted the Underground Railroad lantern high, its warm, golden light stretching further down the tunnel than his work flashlight ever could, illuminating more cracks in the support beams, more evidence of decades of neglect the city had tried to bury. [582]
For the first time in his life, he didn’t feel the weight of his mother’s expectations pressing down on him, didn’t feel like he had to choose between keeping her happy and doing what was right. [620]
He knew the evidence he’d collected would reverberate through every level of the transit authority, that it would force policy changes, that it would keep other workers from dying avoidable deaths, just like his grandmother’s choice to carry that lantern north had reverberated through generations of his family to give him the freedom to choose his own path. [683]
He tucked his notebook back into his coat, adjusted his grip on the lantern, and started walking back toward the tunnel entrance, the soft glow of the flame lighting his way, no longer afraid to step out of the shadow of his mother’s control and into the life he had chosen for himself. [736]
