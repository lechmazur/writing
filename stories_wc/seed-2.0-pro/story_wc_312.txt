The last silver heron had flown off with its catch ten minutes prior, marking the hour after the last fish was caught in the shallow pools edging the sapphire viaduct at dawn. [32]
Kael, a lukewarm mage whose spells never burned hot enough for battle nor cooled enough for healing, perched on the viaduct’s weathered stone parapet, his scuffed leather satchel slung over one shoulder. [64]
He was earnestly naive, the sort of person who still left crumbs for the forgotten house sprites of the capital even after they’d stolen his favorite wool scarf three winters running. [95]
He’d come to this remote spot with one purpose: to right a cosmic imbalance that had left the coastal villages of the north trapped in endless, rain-soaked twilight for three long years. [127]
From his satchel he pulled the dented, cracked relic he’d recovered from the sunken trade ship off the rocky shore: a broken ship’s bell, its surface etched with the faded runes of the old sea gods. [163]
The imbalance had been sparked by the death of the ship’s captain, whose brittle devotion to the sea gods had led him to sink with his vessel rather than abandon the offerings he’d promised to deliver to their underwater shrine. [203]
He’d spent six months studying to rebuild the bell, his only guide his careful notes on understanding dripping rates from the viaduct’s carved gutters, a quirk of water magic that let him map the tiny, invisible fractures in broken metal by the rhythm of moisture passing through them. [251]
Most senior mages had laughed when he’d shared his plan, arguing that a lukewarm mage could never reforge a sacred relic bound to gods, but Kael never paid their doubt any mind. [283]
He set the bell on the stone between his feet, then held his palms over its cracked surface, counting each drip that fell from the viaduct’s edge onto the metal, matching his pulse to their steady, even cadence. [321]
As he counted, the cracks in the bell began to glow pale blue, the fractures knitting themselves shut one by one, each seal matching the interval between two consecutive drips from the viaduct above. [355]
The air hummed with that specific, vivid sense of plausible impossibility, the kind of magic that only works when no one is around to tell you it cannot possibly be real. [386]
When the last crack sealed shut, Kael lifted the bell and rang it once, the sound clear and bright, carrying over the coastal marshes far beyond the viaduct’s edge. [415]
High above, the heavy grey clouds that had hung over the north for three years began to part, golden sunlight spilling through the gaps to paint the sapphire stone of the viaduct in streaks of honey and amber. [453]
Somewhere far below the waves, the captain’s lost spirit heard the bell’s ring, and the weight of his brittle devotion, which had anchored him to the sunken ship for years, finally lifted, letting him pass on to the afterlife he’d earned for his loyalty. [497]
Kael sat back on his heels, grinning, as he watched the last of the clouds drift away, the cool dawn air now carrying the distant sound of villagers cheering as they saw the sun for the first time in years. [537]
He’d known all along that he didn’t need flashy, powerful magic to fix the problem, just the patience to study the dripping rates long enough to understand how the pieces of the bell fit back together, and the faith that even the smallest, most overlooked magic could shift the fate of an entire region. [591]
The senior mages would never believe he’d pulled it off, of course, they’d blame the shift in weather on a random atmospheric anomaly, or a delayed blessing from the high temple they’d forgotten they’d requested months prior. [628]
Kael didn’t care if they ever learned the truth, he’d done what he set out to do, and the people of the northern coast would get their summers back, their fishing boats would return to the water, and their children would grow up knowing what it felt like to feel warm sunlight on their faces. [683]
He tucked the now-whole bell back into his satchel, already planning his next trip to a village on the other side of the kingdom that had been struggling with a curse of blighted crops, sure that his quiet, unassuming magic would find a way to fix that problem too. [732]
As he stood up to leave, a single drop of water fell from the viaduct’s edge, landing lightly on the top of his satchel, a quiet sign that the sea gods had seen his work, and were grateful. [770]
