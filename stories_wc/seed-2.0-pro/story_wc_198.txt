The morning fog saxophonist, whose real name no one in the coastal town remembered, picked his way up the overgrown cliff path as dewdrops form, his scuffed leather case slung over one frayed wool shoulder. [35]
He had returned to this spot every October for 47 years, chasing the eerie nostalgia of the night he’d first heard the coded prophecy spill from the lips of his old mentor, the town’s last lighthouse keeper. [72]
Halfway up the path, the mist thickened until the world softened the sharp edges of rock and pine, revealing the first edge of the radiant grotto in mist he’d only ever seen once before, when he was 19 and foolish enough to ignore his mentor’s warnings. [118]
His mouth went dry as he stepped inside, the air thick with the smell of damp stone and the jasmine his mentor used to tuck behind his ear before every gig, and his eyes adjusted to the quietly vivid glow of bioluminescent moss clinging to every curved rock face. [167]
At the far end of the grotto, the prism atrium in mist rose from a shallow pool of crystal clear water, its cut glass facets catching the faint glow of the moss to throw streaks of emerald and amber across the stone floor. [211]
He knew this structure was the source of the coded prophecy, its refracted light that spilled out into the town square on storm surges would do so again at dawn, spelling out a warning of a flood that would wipe the small coastal town off the map if left unstopped. [261]
His goal was simple: to hush a prophecy spoken in code before it reached the ears of panicked townsfolk, who would abandon their homes and flee into the hills, triggering mass panic that would cause far more death than the flood itself, as his mentor had explained decades prior. [310]
He unlatched his saxophone case, pulling out the dented brass instrument he had played at every funeral, wedding, and town festival for half his life, its keys worn smooth from decades of use. [343]
He had spent years studying the spell his mentor had left him, learning that low, resonant sound could bend light through negative space, sealing the prophecy away for another generation. [373]
He closed his eyes, held the memory of his mentor’s laugh, and began to play a slow, soft melody that sounded like rain on a lighthouse window, each note wrapping around the prism facets, and hung in the misty air. [413]
When the final note faded, he waited, and then he began to whisper the final line of the spell, his voice barely audible over the faint hum of the glowing moss. [444]
The light that had been spilling sharp and shifting across the walls stilled, wrapping into the core of the prism atrium, a vested hush settling over the entire grotto, so complete he could hear the faint thrum of the trapped prophecy under his feet. [485]
He sat down on a smooth stone near the pool, watching as the dewdrops continued to form on the edge of his saxophone keys, each one catching the faint glow of the moss. [518]
He thought of all the people down below the cliff, still asleep in their beds, unaware they had been spared another decade of peace ahead of them, no idea the old saxophonist who played at their festivals had just saved their lives that morning. [562]
He traced the scratch on the side of his saxophone, a mark his mentor had left there the day he gave him the instrument, telling him he would know when the time came to use it for something greater than playing for crowds of drunk fishermen at the bar downtown. [612]
The sun began to peek over the edge of the cliff, casting faint gold light filtering through the mist, and he smiled, knowing he had kept his promise to the man who had raised him after his parents died at sea when he was a boy. [658]
He packed up his saxophone back into its case, slung it over his shoulder, and began to walk back down the cliff path, leaving the vested hush of the grotto lingering behind him, the prophecy safely trapped for another generation. [698]
Somewhere far below, a rooster crowed, and he laughed, knowing the town would wake soon to another quiet, uneventful morning, none the wiser to the magic that had saved them. [727]
