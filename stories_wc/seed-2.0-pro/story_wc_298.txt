Elara picked her way through the catacomb orchard, where gnarled apple tree roots snaked between sun-bleached human bones tucked into crannies of the half-lit corridor. [25]
A reckless idealist who had traded three years of her own memory for entry to this space, she moved with the fiercely gentle care of someone tending to a wounded animal. [56]
Her coat pocket held a crumpled, burned tarot card, the Lovers, only half its face still visible beneath black, bubbled scorch marks. [78]
This was the realm of bartered destinies, where every choice people had traded away for an easier path grew into twisted, fruit-bearing boughs heavy with the lives they never lived. [108]
Elara’s mission was simple: to harvest the small, glowing gold fruits to patch holes in the souls of people who had given too much of themselves away, including her little sister, who had traded her ability to feel joy to pay for their mother’s cancer treatment two years prior. [157]
She had spent three months preparing for this journey, inlaying meaning everywhere she could: stitching star patterns into her cuffs to represent her sister’s love of astronomy, tucking dried lavender from their mother’s garden into her boots, carving their family initials into the handle of her harvesting knife. [205]
Dusky resolve settled over her as she rounded a bend and spotted the gnarled tree holding the fruit she needed, its branches wrapped tight around the shattered remains of what looked like a teen girl’s skateboard, a half-finished art portfolio, and a stack of unopened love letters. [252]
Before she could take another step, the tree’s thick, coiling roots snapped up to trammel her ankles, yanking her off balance and pinning her to the cold stone of the corridor floor, the burned tarot card slipping out of her pocket to land face-up beside her hand. [299]
The card had belonged to the sister, a gift from Elara for her 16th birthday, burned in the same kitchen fire that had nearly killed their mother only a week after the girl had made her fateful trade. [337]
Instead of cutting at the roots with her knife, she spoke softly to them, telling them of her sister’s quiet afternoons painting constellations on her bedroom ceiling, of the way she used to leave sun-warmed apples on their neighbors’ porches, of how she still hummed old lullabies even when she couldn’t feel the happiness they used to bring her. [396]
The roots hesitated, pulsing faintly with dim golden light, before slowly releasing their hold on her ankles, curling back into the gaps between the bones like shy, curious snakes. [425]
She reached up and plucked three of the small, glowing apples from the bough, tucking them carefully into the inner pocket of her coat, right beside a folded photo of her sister grinning at the camera, paint smudged on her cheek. [466]
She knew bartered destinies could never be fully returned, that the sister would always carry a small scar from the choice she had made, but the fruit would stitch enough of her lost joy back into her soul to let her laugh again, to let her paint, to let her feel like herself once more. [521]
She picked up the burned tarot card from the floor, tucking it back into her pocket before leaving a small, carved wooden star she had made for the journey at the base of the tree, a thank you for the gift it had given her. [566]
Dusky resolve softened into quiet hope as she turned back toward the corridor entrance, her steps steady even as the dim light flickered around her, the fruit warm against her chest where her own heart beat, full of the meaning she had stitched into every step of her journey. [615]
She had been warned a hundred times that this journey was too dangerous, that the guardians of the catacomb orchard never let intruders leave with their fruit, but her reckless idealism had kept her going even when every logical part of her had told her to turn back. [663]
When she stepped out of the catacombs and into the cool twilight of the city, the first person she saw was her sister, leaning against a lamppost, waiting for her, a small, tentative smile tugging at the corner of her mouth even though she couldn’t yet feel the joy behind it. [714]
Elara pulled one of the glowing apples from her pocket, held it out to the girl, and knew that the holes they both carried would never be fully gone, but they could be patched, slow and gentle, with every small, loving choice they made for each other from that day forward. [765]
