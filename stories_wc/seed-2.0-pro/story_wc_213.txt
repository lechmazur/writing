Mara, a humble storm siren tester who’d spent ten years tending to the cliffside warning systems of the coastal lowlands, had climbed three thousand feet of frost-dusted stone to reach the windblown sanctuary above the clouds before dawn on midwinter solstice. [41]
She was credibly curious about the old tales that claimed the sanctuary’s bell could call storms rather than just warn of them, a rumor that aligned perfectly with her quiet motivation to favor listening over noise in every part of her work. [83]
She’d arrived 12 hours earlier to mend a fray in the thick cathedral bell rope, and was waiting out the timeframe after the glue dries to test the repair, wrapped in a wool shawl as the solstice hush settled over the peak, no sound but the faint whistle of wind through the sanctuary’s arched stone openings. [139]
Her usual work relied on tracking microclimate variations to predict incoming gales hours before standard weather models picked up their first stirrings, a skill she’d honed by sitting silent for hours on end instead of blasting test siren blasts at random intervals like her former coworkers. [185]
She’d calculated that zero hour, the exact moment the solstice sun would peek over the cloud line to kiss the sanctuary’s stone altar, would fall in three minutes, and her fingers itched to reach for the bell rope even as she forced herself to stay still and listen. [233]
The old tales had said that to make the bell work for you instead of against you, you had to switch your focus from what you wanted to say to what the wind wanted you to hear, a shift that felt far harder than flipping the toggle on her siren control panel ever had. [287]
When the first sliver of gold light slipped over the clouds and painted the bell’s bronze surface, she reached out and wrapped both hands around the cathedral bell rope, feeling the cured hide and twisted hemp fibers under her palms, steady and warm from the small heater she’d set beside it while waiting for the glue to set. [345]
Instead of yanking it hard to ring a warning, she pulled slowly, matching the rhythm of the wind that billowed through the sanctuary’s open rafters, letting the bell hum softly rather than clang, its low tone rippling out over the sea of white clouds below her. [391]
As she tracked microclimate variations in real time, she felt the air shift, warm and damp, as a small, gentle storm began to form right above the sanctuary, not the raging gale she’d expected, but a soft snowfall that would dust the lowlands’ parched farmlands just enough to save the winter wheat crop. [444]
She’d spent so many years warning people to run from storms that it felt like a gift to call one that would care for them instead, proof that her choice to favor listening over noise had not just changed her work, but had changed the impact she could have on the communities she served. [498]
When zero hour passed and the sun hung full and bright above the cloud line, the snowfall slowed to a light drift, and she let go of the bell rope, brushing flecks of frost off her sleeves as she smiled, the solstice hush still wrapping around her like a hug. [548]
She knew that when she went back down the mountain the next day, she would switch out her old siren testing schedule to add weekly trips up to the sanctuary, where she could listen to the wind, track small shifts in the air, and ring the bell only when the land needed a gentle storm rather than a loud warning. [608]
Her former coworkers would likely tease her for wasting time on old fairy tales, but she didn’t care, because she’d seen the proof in the soft snowflakes that were already drifting down to the lowlands, far softer and more useful than any siren blast she’d ever triggered. [655]
She sat down on the stone altar, still warm from the sun, and closed her eyes, letting the solstice hush wrap around her as she listened to the wind shift, already tracking the next microclimate variation that would tell her when the land needed her help again. [702]
