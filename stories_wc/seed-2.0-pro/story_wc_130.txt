Long after the cavern orchard grew silent beneath the moon, the secretive conjurer knelt beside the low boughs of bioluminescent apple trees, his boots pressed to damp, mossy stone. [24]
Dripping stalactites above dripped slow, mineral-rich water onto the fruit, leaving faint, glowing dewdrops that rolled down to feed the gnarled roots curled between the stones lining the path. [48]
For three years, he had tended this orchard alone, his gently inventive experiments with bioluminescent grafts and mineral nutrient blends the only sign he ever spoke aloud in the empty space where his partner, Elara, had once stood. [79]
Elara, the taxidermist who had loved blending natural oddities almost as much as she loved him, had vanished three years prior without a note, no trace left save the taxidermist’s glass eye pair she kept on their workbench, and the mosaic stepping stone they had laid together at the orchard’s entrance, its chipped patterns spelling out their initials tangled in apple blossoms. [131]
That ambiguous loss had gnawed at him ever since, the lack of confirmation of whether she was lost forever or would one day walk back through the cavern’s hidden entrance, had led him to his quiet, obsessive goal: to understand the mathematics of emotion, to map the gaps between hope and grief, joy and despair, into something he could measure, something he could hold. [188]
He pulled the small velvet pouch from his tunic pocket, opening it to reveal the pair of glass eyes, their irises the exact shade of Elara’s hazel, flecked with gold, identical to the flecks of feldspar in the mosaic stepping stone at his feet. [233]
He had spent months working out the ritual, designed to dovetail the fragments of memory tied to the eyes, the magic of the orchard, and the traces of Elara’s energy left in the stone, to pull forward a sign of where she had gone, not to bring her back, not yet, just to know. [278]
Per the ritual he had designed himself, he would access the traces of her essence via the chipped patterns on a mosaic stepping stone, tracing the cracks where the grout had worn thin, the edges of the apple blossom motifs chipped from years of their boots scuffing over the surface. [325]
He set the glass eyes into the two circular chipped gaps in the stone where the centers of the largest mosaic apple blossoms had fallen out years before, their size a perfect fit, as if they had been carved for that exact purpose. [367]
The orchard’s faint blue glow shifted to warm hazel, seeping up from the stone into the roots of the trees, sending pulses of light up the boughs, making the glowing apples hum softly, a sound that matched the lullaby Elara used to sing while she worked mounting small birds for her taxidermy commissions. [417]
For a long moment, the air smelled like her favorite jasmine tea, like the pine resin she used to seal her work, and a soft, disembodied voice drifted through the cavern, quiet, clear, saying only, “I’m safe, I’m looking for the same answer you are, meet me at the crossroads of the star fall in six months’ time.” [472]
He sat back on his heels, his shoulders slumping for a moment, relief flooding through him, not the full answer he had craved, but enough, the ambiguous loss no longer a yawning chasm of unknown, just a gap he could cross, a promise he could hold. [517]
He tucked the glass eyes back into the velvet pouch, pressing the pouch to his chest, watching as the glow from the stone faded back to the soft blue of the orchard’s fruit, the dripping stalactites resuming their steady, comforting rhythm. [561]
He had not mapped the full mathematics of emotion that night, but he had found a new variable to add to his equations: hope, quiet, steady, buried optimism he had not dared to let rise to the surface for three long years, now a tangible thing he could calculate around, build toward. [609]
He stood, brushing moss from his trousers, and turned to walk back toward his small cottage carved into the cavern wall, already planning the next round of grafts, the next round of equations, the day he would see Elara again, the gaps between them finally closing. [658]
The orchard fell silent again beneath the moon, the stone glowing faintly under the drips, holding the memory of her voice, the promise of a future he had not dared to imagine until that night. [694]
