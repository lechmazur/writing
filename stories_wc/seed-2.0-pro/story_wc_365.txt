In the murmur of half-remembered dreams, Elara, a sensory map maker who charted the texture of unspoken memories and the hum of forgotten places, woke propped against a rusted plasma weld bench in an abandoned spaceport repair bay, the acrid tang of old hydraulic fluid clinging to her wool coat. [45]
Curled tight in her left palm was a pitted green glass shard, its edge warm to the touch even though the bay’s cracked viewports let in the frigid bite of high-altitude wind that whipped across the unused launch pads outside. [90]
Her gift for weaving between worlds was cyclically spontaneous, flaring to life without warning every three to seven weeks, yanking her out of her quiet studio apartment and dropping her in moments or places that felt tied to the half-truths her late grandmother had never dared share. [145]
She’d spent three years chasing these jumps, her only constant goal to discover the weight of a withheld truth: why her grandmother, a former spaceport engineer, had hidden every trace of her work at this very bay and fled the colony in the middle of the night when Elara was an infant. [200]
The green glass shard, she’d learned on her last three jumps, was an anchor that let her nudge the path of her jumps instead of being tossed entirely at the mercy of her ability’s random cycles. [241]
When the shard began to glow a soft, neon emerald, she knew she would soon have to redirect her path through parallel universes, each iteration of the repair bay holding a small piece of the story her grandmother had buried. [288]
She pushed off the weld bench, her boots crunching over discarded fasteners and crumpled maintenance logs, and held the shard up to the nearest viewport, letting the faint pink light of the colony’s binary sunset filter through its pitted surface. [338]
The first universe she stepped into smelled like fresh welding fumes and jasmine tea, her grandmother, 20 years old and laughing, perched on the same bench Elara had just left, running a cloth over a small scout ship she’d modified to carry refugee children off the war-torn main continent. [392]
Elara stayed quiet, not wanting to disrupt the thread of the memory, and noted the sticker of a lark on the ship’s hull, the same sticker her grandmother had taped to Elara’s first backpack when she started school. [435]
When the shard dimmed, she stepped back into her home timeline’s bay, then tilted the shard again to redirect to a second parallel universe, this one holding the day the colony’s governing council found out about her grandmother’s unsanctioned refugee runs. [486]
Her grandmother stood in the middle of the bay, face set with defiance, as two council officers ordered her to decommission the ship and turn over the list of children she’d already transported off-world. [529]
When she refused, one of the officers slammed his baton into the control panel of the ship’s navigation system, shattering the green glass display that sat at its center, and Elara realized the shard she’d been carrying all this time was a piece of that very panel. [581]
She tilted the shard a third time, jumping to the last universe, the one that held the final piece of the truth: her grandmother sneaking back into the bay the night she fled, tucking the shard into a crack in the weld bench, where Elara would find it 32 years later, guided by the cycles of her gift. [635]
As she stepped back into her own timeline, the shard grew cool in her palm, and she finally understood the weight of the truth her grandmother had withheld: she had not run out of fear, but out of love, choosing to keep Elara and the hundreds of children she’d rescued safe from the council’s retribution by never speaking of her work again. [698]
She pulled her leather-bound sensory map journal out of her coat pocket, pressing the green glass shard into the pages next to the sketch she’d made of her grandmother’s lark sticker, and marked the bay’s coordinates with a note of the courage she had never known her quiet, baking-obsessed grandmother had carried. [756]
When she woke up back in her studio the next morning, the shard still tucked in her journal, she no longer felt the restless tug of unanswered questions, knowing the spontaneous jumps that had once felt like a burden had led her exactly where she needed to be. [800]
