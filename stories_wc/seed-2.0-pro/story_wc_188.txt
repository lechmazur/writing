Lira, a wandering fireworks designer, picked her way up the moss-choked ramparts of the blooming fortress at dusk, her boots squelching over clover and wild poppy blooms that pushed through cracked stone battlements. [33]
She chanted while walking, an old street-performer’s lullaby her fire-spinner mother had taught her, the syllables soft enough to not scare the small lizards darting between stone crevices. [61]
The local villagers had begged her to come, explaining that a curse had blanketed the valley for three weeks, snuffing out all starlight and leaving the community crippled by a heavy, inescapable hopelessness that made even getting out of bed feel like climbing a mountain. [106]
Lira had agreed immediately, her sole driving motivation to defy the gravity of hopelessness that had settled over the valley, to remind the villagers that light could exist even when the sky refused to give it. [142]
Tucked in her leather satchel, alongside fuses and colored powder packs, was the tattered fire spinner’s kerosene rag knot her mother had left her before she passed, the fabric frayed at the edges and still holding the faint, acrid scent of campfire smoke. [185]
She was searching for the crumbling mosaic set into the fortress’s central watchtower, because the village elder had told her it held a note in mosaic left by the last traveler who’d tried and failed to lift the curse, a clue to what would cut through the valley’s heavy fog of despair. [237]
Rather than scanning the stone with her eyes alone, she began to filter out the hum of crickets, the rustle of poppy petals, and the faint thrum of the curse’s low energy through ambient awareness, letting her senses attune only to the faint, warm glow of the mosaic’s embedded glass shards. [288]
Dusk slipped away quickly, and soon she was working during the hush of a night with no stars, the only light coming from the small glow stick she’d tucked behind her ear and the faint, almost imperceptible glint of the mosaic’s colored glass. [331]
When she found the mosaic, she laughed softly at the message, a silly, scribbled thing made of red and yellow glass shards that read “LIGHT THE FIRE THAT FEELS LIKE SINGING”, the perfect mix of earnest advice and whimsy that matched her own approach of serious playfulness when working with light and flame. [384]
She pulled the fire spinner’s kerosene rag knot from her satchel, doused it in the fast-burning pyrotechnic gel she used for firework ignition, and held it up to the small flint she kept on her keychain, striking it until the knot burst into bright, dancing gold flame. [431]
She set the flaming knot in the center of the mosaic, and it cast warm light over the array of fireworks she’d laid out across the watchtower ledge: small bursts shaped like poppies, longer fountains that sprayed pink and blue light, and one large, central shell designed to explode into a pattern of a hundred tiny, glowing stars. [489]
As she lit each fuse one by one, she picked her chant back up, louder this time, the lullaby’s bouncing melody mixing with the fizz and pop of the fuses burning down, the sound carrying over the valley walls to the small cluster of villagers waiting at the fortress’s base. [539]
The first fountain burst to life, painting the stone walls of the blooming fortress in bright tangerine light, and Lira could feel the heavy weight of the curse lighten just a little, as if the sky itself was leaning in to watch the show she’d put together. [586]
When the final, largest shell exploded overhead, filling the empty black sky with hundreds of tiny, glowing fake stars that drifted slowly down toward the valley, the villagers below cheered, their voices loud and bright, the heavy cloud of hopelessness that had hung over them for weeks finally shattered. [635]
Lira leaned against the watchtower wall, watching the last of the star fragments drift down, the fire spinner’s kerosene rag knot still burning softly in the center of the mosaic, its flame casting a warm glow over the silly, hopeful message spelled out in colored glass. [681]
She didn’t need real stars to lift the curse, she realized, because the point of her work had never been to copy what the sky gave, but to make something warm and bright enough to remind people that they could carry their own light, even when the world felt dark and heavy. [733]
