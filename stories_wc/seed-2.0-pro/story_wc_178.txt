Dusk falls over the reimagined ancient Babylonian palace, its mud-brick spires gilded with fading amber, during the uneasy lull between the death of the old king and the coronation of his untested heir. [33]
The frugal astronomer, who had turned down three offers of fine linen robes and a private kitchen from the old king’s court to prioritize funding for his star charts, climbs the spiral steps to the abandoned top observatory, his only possession a small, chipped clay tile tucked in his belt. [83]
He has spent 20 years mapping the paths of the wandering stars, and the lull between regimes means no court officials will demand he focus on omens for the new ruler, leaving him free to pursue his quiet, long-held goal to seal a covenant with the unknown force he believes guides the celestial bodies. [137]
The tile in his belt is a magic square tile, its surface scored with 16 cells each holding a cuneiform numeral, arranged so every row, column, and diagonal adds up to the same sum, a pattern he discovered hidden in the orbital cycles of Jupiter over 12 years of observation. [187]
The dusky harmony of the fading light bleeds pink and indigo across the observatory’s open archway, softening the sharp edges of the clay star tables stacked along the walls, and he pauses for a long moment to breathe in the scent of date palm and baked earth drifting up from the palace gardens below. [241]
He sets the magic square tile on the flat stone altar at the center of the observatory, then reaches up to uncloak the tall wooden rack of star charts he had hidden under a linen tarp six months earlier, when the old king’s health began to fail and court intrigue seeped into every corner of the palace. [298]
His plan to reach the unknown relies on schematic representations of the star paths he has drawn, each line and dot corresponding to a numeral on the magic square tile, creating a code that carries the encrypted devotion he has held for the celestial order since he was a small boy watching shooting stars from his family’s mud hut on the edge of the city. [363]
There is an empirically ironic edge to his work, he knows: as a man who has spent his entire career demanding measurable, repeatable proof of every celestial movement, he is now relying on a pattern he cannot explain to reach a force he cannot see, let alone quantify. [411]
He arranges the star charts around the magic square tile, aligning the schematic lines of Jupiter’s orbit with the top row of the tile, Mars’ path with the left column, and the fixed constellations of the zodiac with the outer edge of the altar, his calloused fingers moving slowly, deliberately, as if he is handling something far more precious than gold. [472]
The last sliver of sun dips below the horizon, and the first stars prick through the deep purple sky, matching the dots he had drawn on his charts one by one, as if the sky itself is responding to his work. [513]
He does not ask for wealth, or power, or good omens for the coming regime, only that the unknown force that ordered the stars will accept his offering of decades of careful observation, his encrypted devotion woven into numbers and lines, and seal a covenant that will let him understand the true pattern of the universe before he dies. [572]
A soft, warm wind picks up in the observatory, swirling the fine dust on the altar, and when it settles, the numerals on the magic square tile glow faint gold, each one shifting to match the brightness of the star it corresponds to in the sky above. [619]
He smiles, a quiet, unhurried expression, and sits cross-legged on the stone floor, staring up at the stars, knowing his covenant is sealed, and that the empirical irony of his leap of faith has only deepened his love for the order he has spent his life studying. [666]
The dusky harmony of the night wraps around the reimagined palace, unbroken by the shouts of court officials or the clatter of preparation for the new king’s coronation, as the frugal astronomer sits, surrounded by his schematics and the glowing magic square tile, finally at peace with the unknown he has spent so long chasing. [721]
