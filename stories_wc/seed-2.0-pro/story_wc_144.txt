The mist-coated rail depot hunkered at the edge of the coastal shipyard, its tin roof glistening with fine dew as the first low blare of a new ship’s maiden voyage horn rolled over the weathered wooden docks. [29]
Elara, the heartfelt artisan who ran the depot’s tiny repair shop, sat on a splintered oak stool just outside her door, her unseeing eyes tilted toward the distant groan of the anchor lifting slow and heavy from the silty harbor floor. [64]
Perched on her outstretched palm was the artificial hummingbird drone, its iridescent polymer wings crumpled along one edge, dropped off the night before by the ship’s engineering team who’d crashed it during final systems checks. [101]
For three years, Elara had run her shop with a single quiet, unshakable goal: to decode feedback into direction, turning the faint hums, weight shifts, and even damage of broken tech into tangible fixes that served the people who relied on them. [142]
Even without sight, she oversaw every step of every repair that passed through her doors, her sense of touch, sound, and even heat tuned so finely she could spot a frayed wire faster than most techs with perfect vision. [183]
Beside her stool sat a shallow carved stone basin, a small water feature she’d installed shortly after losing her sight in a soldering accident, its surface rippled by the faint, salt-kissed harbor breeze. [221]
Most repair technicians relied on magnifying lenses and digital circuit testers for small drone repairs, but Elara had perfected her unique method of examining light refraction through water features to map even the tiniest, most hidden damage. [265]
She had learned to track how sunlight bent around cracks, warped components, and loose wiring based on the faint warm spots the refracted light cast on her wrists and palms, no sight required to see what needed fixing. [308]
The second ship’s horn blared, louder this time, signaling the anchor had fully lifted and the vessel would pull away in ten minutes, leaving the engineering team without the aerial survey tool they’d planned to use to map dangerous ice floes along their arctic trade route. [357]
Elara ran a gentle fingertip along the crumpled wing edge, feeling the tiny, almost imperceptible fracture where the internal wiring had pulled loose from the tiny motor housing, her breath as steady as the lapping tide against the depot’s foundation. [402]
She reached into the pocket of her oil-stained canvas apron and pulled out a thin sheet of heat-reactive composite, the same material she used to patch torn rail signal wiring and cracked drone casings for the yard’s rotating crews. [443]
She held the damaged wing over the stone basin, tilting it slowly until she felt the warm mid-morning sun hit the water’s surface just right, the refracted light casting a narrow, even warm strip across her wrist that told her the crack was aligned perfectly for the patch to seal evenly without blocking the wing’s full range of motion. [508]
She pressed the thin composite strip along the fracture, running her calloused fingertip along the edge to smooth out any air bubbles, waiting ten slow, deliberate seconds for the material to cure before lifting the drone to her ear and flicking the power switch with her thumbnail. [558]
The tiny motor whirred to life, its pitch even and steady, no rattle or drag to signal the wing was still damaged, and Elara smiled, lifting her palm to let the drone hover a few inches above her hand, its wings beating fast enough to stir the fine mist off her knuckles. [606]
A group of three harried engineers rounded the corner of the depot, their steps fast with worry, until they spotted the hovering drone and slowed, their tight shoulders slumping with visible relief. [642]
Elara held out her hand to let the drone land in her palm again, passing it over to the lead engineer, explaining that she’d adjusted the wing’s alignment slightly based on the refraction patterns, a small change that would cut down on wind drag during long survey flights over the arctic ice. [691]
The lead engineer thanked her profusely, tucking the drone into his padded carrying case before running back toward the dock, where the ship’s third and final horn blared, signaling its immediate departure. [728]
Elara sat back on her stool, tilting her face toward the sound of the ship’s engines fading into the thick coastal mist, her hands resting lightly on the edge of the stone basin as the sun shifted across the water, casting new refraction patterns she would use to guide her next repair. [775]
