Nectar dusk gilded the spire of the lightning collection tower, where Jax, the candid toll keeper, had stood post for twelve unbroken years after the city-wide blackout rewrote all local rules of infrastructure and routine. [35]
He carried the curse of being involuntarily truthful, a side effect of the blackout’s residual electromagnetic surge, so he never bothered with small talk or polished pleasantries with the handful of travelers who passed through his gate. [72]
The core issue plaguing the region lately was drone beehive failure, the automated pollinators that fed the nectar groves whose sap powered the tower’s backup cells short-circuiting one by one after the blackout’s lingering power surges. [108]
Today, a hiker in a frayed sunhat paused at the gate, holding a matchbox tucked in their outstretched palm, asking for passage up the tower to survey the groves for undamaged beehives, offering the matchbox as toll. [145]
Jax flipped open the matchbox to find a scrap of folded notebook paper scrawled with a cryptic riddle: “What holds honey that hums without a throat, stores light without a bulb, and outlasts every blackout, no matter how long? [184]
His involuntarily truthful nature kicked in before he could stop it, and he blurted out the answer immediately: a lacquer box tucked in the hidden compartment of his toll booth desk, the one he’d guarded for a decade. [222]
He’d never planned to reveal its contents to anyone, but the riddle had unlocked the quiet urge he’d carried all those years, his core motivation to preserve wonder in routine, the small magic no corporate or automated system could erase. [262]
He gestured for the hiker to follow him into the tiny booth, the sweet, sun-warmed nectar dusk light spilling through the screen window as he pulled out the lacquer box, its red surface polished smooth from years of careful handling. [302]
When he lifted the lid, the hiker gasped: inside were three thousand living, wild honeybees, the last wild colony in the region, unaffected by the surges that had fried every drone beehive in a hundred mile radius. [339]
Jax explained that he’d found the colony hiding in a crack of the lightning collection tower days after the blackout, and he’d tended to them in secret ever since, leaving small bowls of nectar on his desk every morning and clearing out dead comb once a week. [386]
He’d kept them hidden because the city council had ordered all wild beehives destroyed to make room for the drone units, which they claimed were more efficient, more reliable, and less prone to unexpected disruption. [421]
The hiker pulled off their sunhat to reveal a patch on their sleeve emblazoned with the city council’s logo, and Jax’s chest tightened for a second, but the truth kicking in to make him admit he’d been hiding the bees for years, but the hiker smiled and shook their head. [472]
They explained that they were a former drone engineer who quit when they saw how the drone beehive failure wiped out 90% of the nectar groves in three weeks, and they’d been searching for any wild colony to repopulate the groves for months, leaving riddles in matchboxes at every toll booth across the region to find someone who had a secret worth keeping. [535]
Jax let a soft laugh escape, the first he’d shared with anyone in years, as he handed over the lacquer box, the bees humming softly inside, the nectar dusk light gilding the edges of the box’s red surface like liquid honey. [576]
He realized his years of quiet, routine care had mattered, that the small wonder he’d hidden away was exactly what the region needed to recover from the blackout and the drone beehive failure, to rebuild a food and power system that relied on living magic as much as technology. [625]
When the hiker left, they promised to return once the groves were thriving again, to bring Jax the first jar of wild honey harvested from the new colonies, a reminder that even the most monotonous routine could hold the most extraordinary magic, if you were willing to guard it close. [675]
Jax leaned against the toll booth gate long after the hiker had vanished down the grove path, the low hum of the lightning collection tower mixing with the faint, distant buzz of bees he knew would soon fill the whole valley. [716]
