Elara, an insomniac painter who’d survived the decade-long water wars by scrawling propaganda murals for rebel outposts, trekked across the exposed mudflats to the tidal island an hour before dawn, her canvas pack slung over one frayed wool shoulder. [39]
She’d found her way to this remote spot by following rain trails etched into the baked coastal earth, the only consistent source of fresh unclaimed water left in the parched lowlands since the war’s end. [74]
For three years, she’d wandered the former conflict zones with no fixed home, driven to thresh meaning from experience that had left most of her friends dead, her family displaced, and her hands permanently stained with the indigo dye used for rebel messaging. [117]
The insomnia had set in six months before the war ended, after she’d watched a civilian supply convoy bombed by state forces, and she’d long since given up fighting the sleepless nights, instead using them to chase light and landscape no one else bothered to document. [163]
When she reached the island’s rocky western edge, she stumbled on the unearthed convergence she’d heard other wanderers whisper about: a patch of sand where every high tide spat out lost artifacts from the war, from before the war, from lives no one could name anymore. [209]
A haggard awe settled over her as she knelt, her fingers brushing the chipped edge of a child’s ceramic cup, a dented rebel canteen, a fragment of a porcelain teacup that looked like it had belonged to her grandmother, once, before the floods swallowed their hometown. [255]
Half-buried under a clump of sea grass, she spotted the amber brooch, its golden surface holding a perfectly preserved tiny sprig of pine, the kind that only grew in the mountain forests that had been burned to ash during the war’s final battle. [298]
“That belonged to my sister,” a rough voice said from behind her, and she turned to see a gray-bearded man leaning on a driftwood staff, his face crisscrossed with the same faint acid scars she bore from the water skirmishes in the lowlands. [341]
He was wryly earnest when he explained he’d come to the island every low tide for two years, looking for the brooch, which his sister had worn the day she’d volunteered to carry water across the front lines and never came back. [383]
Elara did not hesitate to agree to an exchange: she would hand over the brooch, and he would tell her every story he could remember about his sister, the mountains, the life they’d lived before the war turned everything to ash and salt water. [427]
They sat on a flat rock for the next three hours, as the tide crept slowly closer to the mudflats, his stories weaving through the sound of gulls and the faint crash of waves, while Elara sketched furiously in her notebook, capturing the line of his jaw, the way he held the brooch like a prayer when she passed it to him, the glint of amber in the rising sun. [497]
For the first time in years, she did not feel the sharp, empty pull of insomnia at the edge of her mind, just the soft, heavy weight of all that grief and all that beauty tangled together, the thing she’d been chasing across hundreds of miles of war-torn land. [546]
Before they parted ways to cross the mudflats before the tide cut the island off, he pressed a tiny, dried pine cone into her palm, the kind he’d carried from the burned forests for decades, and told her to use it as a model for the painting she planned to make of the brooch and his sister. [603]
She walked back to the mainland slowly, her notebook full of sketches, her pocket warm with the pine cone, her head full of stories that did not feel like pain, just like memory, like something she could finally put to canvas without flinching away from the weight of what had been lost. [655]
That night, for the first time in three years, she fell asleep at her easel, half-finished painting of the amber brooch and the pine sprig and the gray-bearded man spread out in front of her, the sound of distant rain tapping against her window like a promise that all lost things eventually find their way back to someone, somewhere.
