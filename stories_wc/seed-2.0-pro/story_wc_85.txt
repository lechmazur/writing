The cobalt terrace at dusk bleached soft indigo over the chipped white rail where Kael, a guilt-ridden black market genealogist, leaned his elbows, staring out at the fog-shrouded shore line. [30]
For seven years, he had manufactured false memories for clients who wanted to outrun criminal records, disown abusive families, or lay claim to fortunes they had no right to inherit, each lie a small stone he had tucked into the pocket of his own conscience. [75]
Twisted between his thumb and forefinger was the dented brass watch hand he had pulled from his little sister’s pocket the night she vanished, the only piece of her he had left after he had sold a false alibi for the man who had hurt her. [121]
His motivation, the only thing that had pulled him out of a months-long drunken stupor after she disappeared, was to discover the weight of a withheld truth: what she had seen that night, and whether she had run because she thought he had chosen the money over her. [169]
He operated now under the weight of unspoken words, the apologies he never got to say, the questions he never got to ask, the confession of his greed he had buried so deep it ached in his bones every time he breathed. [211]
The town around him had wrapped the entire incident in a sieved hush, half-truths and whispered assumptions filtering through the gaps in silence, no one willing to admit they had seen the man leaving Kael’s apartment that night, no one willing to tell him where his sister had gone. [260]
He had tried every trick he knew to quell the screaming in his head, to quiet the part of him that replayed that night on loop, but the false memories he wove for others never stuck to his own mind, no matter how hard he tried to rewrite his own past. [311]
An old client, a fisherman who had grown up on the coast, had told him the only way to find what he was looking for was by sifting through driftwood carvings along a forgotten shore three miles out of town, where runaways had left markers for each other for decades. [361]
Tempered regret clung to him like salt spray as he grabbed his jacket off the terrace rail, tucking the watch hand into the inner pocket next to the crumpled photo of his sister he carried everywhere, the sun dipping below the horizon and painting the sky in bruised purple and gold. [412]
The walk to the shore took an hour, the path overgrown with blackberry brambles that snagged his sleeves and left tiny scratches on his wrists, each one a small penance for the choice he had made all those years ago. [452]
When he reached the shore, the tide was low, exposing half-buried pieces of driftwood half-carved into birds, flowers, tiny faces, each marked with a small notch at the base, the signature his sister had used for her carvings since she was eight years old. [496]
He knelt in the cold damp sand, turning over carving after carving, until his fingers landed on one shaped like a tiny watch, the notch at its base, a slip of rolled paper tucked into a crack in the wood. [536]
He pulled the paper out, his hands shaking so bad he almost dropped it, the handwriting messy and smudged with salt water, clearly written in a hurry the night she left. [567]
The note said she knew he had not meant to take the man’s money, that she had heard him turn the man down first, that she had run to keep the man from hurting him too, that she was safe with a family friend up north, and she would come home when it was safe. [622]
The weight of the withheld truth settled soft on his shoulders, lighter than he had expected, the sieved hush of the last seven years breaking open as he laughed through tears, the guilt that had gnawed at him for so long finally beginning to ease. [667]
He pulled the brass watch hand from his pocket, tucking it into the crack in the driftwood carving next to the note, leaving it there for her to find when she came home, a sign he had stopped lying for other people, that he was ready to tell the truth at last. [719]
Tempered regret still lingered, soft and familiar, but it no longer burned, no longer kept him up at night, because he knew she had never blamed him, that the only lie he had ever needed to unlearn was the one he had told himself about being unforgivable. [766]
