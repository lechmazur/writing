Lila, an experimental treatment guinea pig for a memory entanglement study, slipped through the chain-link fence surrounding the coastal sound wave garden as the hush of the universe inhaling settled over the coast. [33]
She was supposed to stay in the lab dorm under 24-hour observation, but her quietly rebellious streak had only grown sharper in the three months since the treatment had activated the entangled memories she shared with her late partner, Kael, who’d helped design the garden’s resonant sculptures. [80]
Kael had died when a test of the garden’s frequency modulation system shorted out, before he could finish the project they’d dreamed up together: tuning the sculptures to project holograms of the shared memories of visiting couples, turning the garden into a living archive of love. [126]
Lila’s motivation, to breathe life into that shared dream, had kept her volunteering for the high-risk treatment even when the researchers warned the entangled memories might overwrite her own identity entirely. [157]
She’d discovered weeks earlier that the resonant metal sculptures in the garden responded to rhythm, and that drumming on everything from the tubular chimes to the curved disk installations fed the same frequency the treatment used to anchor her entangled memories to Kael’s. [200]
Tonight, she climbed the tallest spiral sculpture at the garden’s center, its ridged surface warm from the day’s sun, and found a flat ledge to perch on, her scuffed sneakers swinging loose over the drop to the shallow auric lagoon under starlight that curved around the garden’s base. [248]
The auroral sorrow she’d carried since Kael’s death wrapped around her like the faint green and pink glow brushing the horizon, soft but unshakable, as she curled her fingers around the chipped wooden drumstick she’d stolen from Kael’s old workbench. [288]
She began tapping the drumstick against the spiral’s metal ridges first, the deep thrumming note vibrating up her arm and out across the garden, then shifted to tap the chimes strung along the sculpture’s edges, the flat metal disks bolted to its sides, even the small hollow glass orbs tucked into its crevices, drumming on everything she could reach in the steady, looping rhythm Kael had taught her on their first date. [360]
The sound built, soft and layered, and the auric lagoon under starlight began to glow in time, its surface rippling with the same frequency as the treatment, pulling the tangled threads of her and Kael’s memories out of her mind and weaving them into the air above the water. [409]
She saw the first memory flicker to life ten feet above the lagoon: the two of them at 19, covered in paint, laughing as they dragged the first sculpture prototype to the garden’s edge, the same drumstick tucked behind Kael’s ear. [450]
More memories followed: their first kiss under a rainstorm, late nights troubleshooting sculpture wiring in their cramped apartment, the day Kael had asked her to help him build the memory archive, his eyes bright with the same dream she was now chasing alone. [493]
She kept drumming, her palms sore, her throat tight with the auroral sorrow that felt both like grief and like Kael standing right beside her, matching her rhythm with his own ghostly taps on the sculpture next to hers. [532]
The garden’s system, long dormant, hummed to life all around her, the sculptures glowing in time with the lagoon, and she realized the entangled memories weren’t just hers to hold—they were being written into the garden’s core, exactly the way she and Kael had planned. [577]
The hush of the universe inhaling began to lift, the first pale edge of dawn painting the sky, and Lila slowed her drumming, watching the holograms of her and Kael’s memories loop softly above the lagoon, visible now to anyone who walked the garden paths. [622]
She’d known the treatment might erase her own memories eventually, but as she leaned back against the cool metal of the sculpture, she didn’t care—she’d breathed life into their shared dream, and it would outlast both of them, glowing softly for every couple that came after. [668]
The auroral sorrow faded just a little, warm now instead of sharp, as she watched the auric lagoon glint under the growing light, her drumstick still tapping a quiet, steady rhythm against the sculpture’s edge. [703]
