Lila, a diaspora returnee whose grandmother had fled their ancestral valley mid-harvest three generations prior, knelt at the edge of the catacomb orchard, where gnarled apple tree roots snaked between weathered human half-bones half-buried in loamy soil. [37]
She was warmly curious, not reckless, as she brushed crumbled stone dust from a fragment of carved limestone that had caught her eye, part of a larger dig she’d signed on to across excavating a tomb thought to hold her family’s lost community relics. [81]
Her grandmother had always spoken of interrupted journeys, of the way their people had left mid-ritual, mid-meal, mid-life, and how the break had left a hollow in every descendant’s heart that no foreign home could fill. [117]
Lila’s primary motivation, beyond reconnecting with her roots, was to restore lost faith in her community’s scattered diaspora, to prove that their stories were not just myths passed down over kitchen tables. [149]
The limestone fragment she held was the curved edge of a sundial base, etched with the faint outline of apple blossoms and the faded initials of her great-great-aunt, a girl who had tended the orchard before the war. [187]
She had opted to work outside the strict dig grid the lead archaeologist had set, choosing through purposeful wandering to trace the paths her grandmother had described as a child, each step feeling like a conversation with the relatives buried beneath the orchard floor. [231]
A sense of cautious enchantment wrapped around her as she brushed more dirt away, aware that every fragment she uncovered could rewrite the stories her family had carried for decades, but also careful not to disturb the resting places that had been undisturbed for so long. [277]
Over the next three weeks, the timeframe across excavating the tomb stretched out slowly, as she and the dig team uncovered the rest of the sundial, along with clay vessels, woven baskets, and handwritten notes tucked into crevices in the tomb wall. [319]
The notes, written by her great-great-aunt, detailed the rituals of the orchard, how the sundial had marked the times for harvest festivals, for remembrances of the dead, for welcoming new members into the community. [353]
Lila realized the interrupted journeys of her people had not just cut them off from their home, but from the rituals that had bound them together, the small, steady practices that had kept their faith in each other and their history alive. [395]
She decided she would rebuild the sundial first, setting its base back in the exact spot it had been found, between two apple trees whose roots curled around the edges of the tomb’s entrance, so it could once again mark the sun’s path for anyone who came to the orchard. [445]
Her warmly curious nature led her to record every detail of the dig, posting photos and translations of the notes to a shared group for her diaspora community, where hundreds of people who had never seen their ancestral home started commenting, sharing their own family stories of interrupted journeys and lost rituals. [497]
The cautious enchantment she had felt on her first day grew stronger as more members of the diaspora started traveling to the orchard, bringing small offerings of their own, recipes from their grandmothers, pieces of fabric dyed with the traditional indigo their people had once made, to add to the small shrine they were building beside the sundial. [555]
By the time the tomb excavation was complete, Lila knew her work to restore lost faith was already bearing fruit, as members of the diaspora were planning their first annual harvest festival at the orchard in over seventy years, a chance to pick up the threads of their interrupted journeys and weave them into something new. [611]
She knelt beside the fully restored sundial base one last time before leaving the orchard for the first time since the dig started, running a finger over the apple blossom etching, as a breeze rustled the apple tree branches and carried the faint, sweet scent of ripening fruit over the rows of graves tucked between the roots. [668]
She knew the work to rebuild their community would take years, that purposeful wandering would be required of all of them as they learned to fit their scattered lives back into the land their ancestors had tended, but for the first time in her life, she felt fully rooted, fully home, fully connected to the people who had come before her and the people who would come after. [736]
