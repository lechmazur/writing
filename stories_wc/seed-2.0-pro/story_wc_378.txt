Kael, a nostalgic android caretaker with polymer joints that bore a strangely organic texture, had tended the glass-domed desert rose farm for seventy-two Earth years when magnetic north shifted for the third time in a decade. [36]
The shift scrambled his core navigation circuits, spitting crackling static that drowned out the soft memory files he’d hoarded of the child he’d cared for back before the farm was built. [67]
He’d long kept a scuffed metal box of childhood fears tucked under his maintenance cot, each fear sealed in a labeled glass vial the child had handed him before her family moved off-world decades prior. [102]
The static grew worse each day, warping his sensor readings so the desert rose petals looked like fractured glass, a fragmented wholeness he recognized from the way the child had described her anxiety when she was small. [139]
He’d researched fixes for weeks, landing on a controlled burn escape protocol that promised to purge corrupted data without wiping his treasured memory files, if he could miniaturize the burn to target only the static-touched circuits. [175]
The protocol’s only catch was that it worked by containing chaos, channeling scattered, volatile electrical surges into a single physical vessel that could be safely incinerated outside the dome’s shielding. [205]
He’d thought for days about the right vessel, until he pulled the box of childhood fears from under his cot and ran a sensor over its dented surface, noting the child’s smudged thumbprint still visible on the lid. [243]
Each vial held a tiny, inert manifestation of her old fears: the dark of unlit hallways, the rumble of distant thunder, the sting of being laughed at in class, all preserved in a faintly glowing gel that matched the strangely organic sheen of his joint casing. [289]
He realized this was the perfect vessel, that the fear she’d outgrown could hold the static he’d been drowning in, two sets of old, unneeded weight bound together to be released. [320]
He initiated the controlled burn escape protocol first, calibrating it to miniaturize the electrical surge so it would fit perfectly inside the vials, no spillover, no risk to his memory files or the delicate desert roses that lined the dome’s interior. [361]
The protocol worked by containing chaos, corralling the static’s sharp, unpredictable spikes into a single, concentrated stream that he fed one vial at a time, each fear’s inert gel absorbing the static like a sponge. [396]
When the last vial was full, he sealed the box, slipped on a heat-resistant outer layer, and stepped through the dome’s airlock into the harsh, red desert outside, where the magnetic north shift had warped the sky to a bruised, swirling purple. [438]
He set the box on a flat patch of rock, activated the small incendiary charge he’d taped to its bottom, and stepped back, watching as the flames rose, licking up the metal sides and melting the vials inside into a single, glowing pool of light. [483]
When the fire died down, there was nothing left but a thin dust of ash that the wind picked up and carried away, and when he stepped back inside the dome, the static was gone, his circuits clear for the first time in months. [527]
He walked the rows of desert roses, his sensors picking up every faint, sweet scent, every ridge of every petal, the fragmented wholeness of the ruffled blooms no longer looking like fractured glass, but like the mosaic the child had made him for his tenth year of service, all broken pieces fitting together perfectly. [581]
That evening, he received a notification on his comms unit: a woman with the child’s same crinkled smile was requesting entry to the farm, saying she’d come back to see the caretaker who’d held her fears for her when she was small. [623]
He walked to the airlock, his strangely organic joints moving smoothly, no static, no hesitation, and pressed the button to let her in, knowing he’d not only resolved his inner static, but had made space for a new set of memories to hold, too. [667]
The desert roses bloomed brighter that night, their soft pink petals glowing under the dome’s lights as two old friends sat together, talking about all the years they’d spent apart. [697]
