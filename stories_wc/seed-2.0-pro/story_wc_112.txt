The xenobiology research station perched on the frost-edged rim of a gas giant’s moon, its solar arrays crusted with faint blue ice that glowed soft as embers as the sun dipped below the curvature of the swirling gas giant above. [40]
Kael, the station’s obliging sky mechanic, had spent three months tinkering in the off-hours between maintenance calls, chasing a half-formed hypothesis most of the research team wrote off as whimsical nonsense. [77]
His core motivation, the quiet fire that kept him up long after the station’s lights dimmed to night mode, was to prove that time moves sideways, not linearly, a possibility he’d first suspected when he’d watched the station’s resident avian xenobes shift position in two places at once during a magnetic storm. [132]
Tonight, he ran his final test within the hush of beating wings at rest, the dozen avian xenobes curled in their glass enclosure beside his workbench, their soft, downy chests rising and falling in slow, synchronized rhythm that matched the low hum of the station’s life support. [188]
The core of his setup was the magnetic nexus at twilight, a fist-sized chunk of crystallized magnetic ore he’d harvested from the moon’s surface an hour prior, when the sky streaked violet and tangerine and the gas giant’s magnetic field hit its daily low ebb. [237]
He’d coated the nexus in a layer of his own custom composite, engineered to stay mechanically warm even when exposed to the moon’s minus 120 degree surface temperatures, its heat seeping through his work gloves as he adjusted its alignment. [282]
He’d mapped the nexus’s magnetic resonance to the station’s hologram array, planning to bend local spacetime through holographic principles, projecting overlapping layers of magnetic resonance to create a fold where past, present, and future overlapped for a fraction of a second. [334]
This test wasn’t meant to create a grand explosion or flashy time rift; it relied on subdued awakenings, the slow, almost unnoticeable shift of particles into a new state of being, too quiet to trigger the station’s alarm systems or rouse the sleeping research team down the hall. [386]
All he had to do now was pull the heavy iron lever he’d salvaged from a decommissioned cargo shuttle, its grip worn smooth from decades of use by pilots and cargo handlers across half a dozen star systems. [423]
The air around the workbench held that strange icy warmth the station always carried at this hour, the cold seeping through the thin metal hull mixing with the recycled, heated air pumped through the vents, carrying faint notes of the citrus cleaning solution the research team used on their lab equipment. [476]
He took a deep breath, glancing first at the sleeping xenobes, then at the readouts on his tablet that showed all parameters were within the safe range, his fingers wrapping around the cool metal of the lever until his knuckles whitened slightly. [520]
He pulled it hard, the lever clanking into its locked position with a low thud, and for a split second, the room went completely silent, the hum of life support cutting out, the soft breathing of the xenobes pausing mid-inhale. [563]
Then he saw it: two versions of himself standing side by side at the workbench, one holding the lever mid-pull, the other watching, their faces identical down to the faint smudge of grease on Kael’s left cheek, the overlap lasting no longer than the blink of an eye. [608]
He laughed softly, giddy relief flooding his chest as the readouts on his tablet spiked then settled, storing the data he’d spent three months chasing, hard evidence of the sideways shift he’d long suspected existed. [649]
One of the xenobes chirped softly in its sleep, shifting its wing to tuck it closer to its body, and Kael reached out to tap the glass gently, a quiet thank you to the creatures that had given him the idea in the first place. [692]
He didn’t run to wake the research team, didn’t fire off a comms message to the central galactic science board right away; instead, he sat down on his worn work stool, sipping a lukewarm cup of coffee he’d poured hours earlier, and stared at the glowing magnetic nexus, still radiating that soft, mechanically warm heat through the glass case he’d placed it in. [751]
Tonight, he just wanted to sit with the knowledge that he’d been right, that time didn’t drag you forward kicking and screaming, but wrapped around you like the station’s strange icy warmth, opening up paths you’d never noticed if you only took the time to look. [798]
