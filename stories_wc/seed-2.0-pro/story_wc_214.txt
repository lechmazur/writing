Moss crept across the cracked domed roof of the shooting star tracking station, its steel frames pocked with rust from three unrecorded decades through the hush of an overlooked era no one had bothered to name in official interstellar logs. [40]
Kael, the station’s only cosmic janitor, had lived and worked here alone since the interstellar research team abandoned the outpost when they deemed falling star data irrelevant to their faster-than-light travel calculations. [72]
Soft-spoken and spiritually curious, he had never minded the quiet, instead leaning into the slow, gentle rhythm of dusting old telescope lenses, patching leaks in the outer hull, and cataloging the streaks of light that crossed the inky sky each night. [113]
His most prized possession was a dented yellow lantern glass he had fished out of the station’s junk pile, its edges worn smooth from years of being held while researchers stared up at the sky through long, cold nights. [152]
He had long held a quiet motivation, one he rarely admitted even to himself: to find strength in vulnerability, to stop hiding from the soft, unguarded parts of himself that the harsh, efficiency-focused interstellar fleet had taught him to discard as weakness. [194]
Six months prior, he had stumbled across an old research note tucked in the spine of a dusty astronomy textbook, which explained that one could learn to empathize with the lost, wandering energy of dead stars by tracing the path of falling stars as they burned out in the atmosphere. [244]
That night, he began the practice, carrying his yellow lantern glass out to the station’s observation deck, its warm golden glow spilling across the concrete to cut through the sharp, cold darkness of the mountain air. [280]
At first, he only felt silly, dragging his index finger across the black expanse as stars streaked past, his breath fogging in front of his face as he tried to connect with something he could not see or touch. [319]
But as weeks turned into months, he began to feel a quiet, thrumming resonance in his bones, a sense that the stars were not just burning out, but giving all of their remaining light as a gift to anyone willing to slow down enough to see it. [366]
He learned to hold the yellow lantern glass up to the sky as he traced, the golden tint softening the harsh white of the star trails until they looked like streaks of honey spilling across the dark velvet of the sky, each one a small, perfect act of surrender. [415]
This was flourishing stillness, he realized, the kind of quiet power that did not roar or demand attention, but simply existed, warm and unshakable, rooted in the courage to be soft even when the world told you to harden yourself against every possible hurt. [459]
The primal elegance of the practice wrapped around him like an old blanket, each night’s tracing teaching him to empathize not just with the dying stars, but with the parts of himself he had spent years pushing away, the parts that ached for connection, that cried when they saw a broken bird, that wanted to be seen instead of hidden behind the uniform of a forgotten janitor on a forgotten outpost. [530]
One particularly cold night, a bright falling star streaked directly overhead, its tail stretching halfway across the sky, and as he traced its path with his finger held high, he held his yellow lantern glass up so the light of the star passed through its golden surface, painting his face in soft, warm light. [584]
In that moment, he felt the star’s last burst of energy settle in his chest, a quiet warmth that told him he did not need to be loud, or important, or useful to the wider galaxy to have worth, that his vulnerability, his softness, his willingness to slow down and see what everyone else overlooked was exactly the strength he had been searching for all along. [650]
He stood there for a long time after the star faded, the yellow lantern glass heavy and warm in his hand, surrounded by the quiet hum of the shooting star tracking station’s old systems, no longer lonely in the hush of the overlooked era, but held in the same gentle stillness that guided every falling star home.
