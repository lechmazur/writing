Three months after going off the grid, the former lending library arsonist slipped through the cracked marble entrance of the lost civilization archive, her boots crunching on shards of weathered clay tablets. [32]
She was analytically intuitive, the kind of person who could spot a pattern in half-erased glyphs before most scholars could even identify the writing system, and that gift had gotten her both her old job and her current fugitive status. [72]
The fire she’d set at the small neighborhood lending library two years prior had not been random, though every official report had labeled it senseless vandalism. [98]
She’d burned only the back storage closet, where the city had stashed hundreds of artifacts from this same lost civilization, marked for destruction to make room for a luxury development. [128]
Her motivation, unshared with anyone but the ghost of her grandmother who’d first taught her to read the civilization’s swirling glyphs, was to rescue a forgotten promise the ancient people had inscribed for their descendants. [163]
She’d been tracking a trail of artistic creations leading to a hidden message for six years, ever since her grandma had given her a faded tapestry fragment stitched with a tiny self-portrait of the ancient scribe who’d crafted the promise. [203]
Halfway down the main archive corridor, she spotted the first familiar mark: a vandalized self-portrait of the same scribe, his face scored with three deep lines that matched the notches on the edge of her tapestry fragment. [240]
The scribe had deliberately defaced his own work to mark the path, she realized, her chest thrumming with that volcanic tenderness she always felt when she uncovered another piece of the story her grandma had sworn she’d help bring to light. [281]
She followed the string of half-destroyed murals, carved stone figurines, and dyed cloth scraps deeper into the archive, each piece matching the symbols stitched into the edge of her fragment, until she reached a sealed stone door etched with the scribe’s initials. [323]
She pressed the faded tapestry fragment into a small indentation in the door’s center, and the stone rumbled open, releasing a gust of cool, jasmine-scented air that smelled exactly like her grandma’s old kitchen. [357]
The room beyond held a single raised stone plinth, on which rested a fully intact tapestry, its threads bright even after two thousand years, woven to portray the scribe standing beside a field of grain, holding a scroll out to a small girl with her grandma’s curly dark hair. [406]
She stepped forward slowly, her hands shaking as she unrolled the scroll beside the plinth, its glyphs glowing faintly under the thin beam of her headlamp. [432]
The promise was written across its pages: the ancient people had developed a method of drought-resistant farming that could save the dying agricultural communities in her grandma’s home village, which had been facing famine for the past three years. [471]
She’d grown up hearing stories of the village’s failing crops, of children going hungry, of her grandma’s dying wish that she find the ancient knowledge the old stories said was hidden somewhere in the surrounding hills. [507]
The volcanic tenderness in her chest swelled until it felt like it might burst through her ribs, hot and bright and unstoppable, as she copied every glyph into her waterproof notebook, her pen moving fast enough to keep up with the rush of relief and joy rushing through her. [556]
She didn’t care that she was still a fugitive, that the police were still looking for the person who’d burned the library closet, that she’d have to stay off the grid for months more to get the information to the village without being caught. [600]
She folded the faded tapestry fragment back into her inner jacket pocket, pressed a kiss to the intact tapestry’s edge, and turned to leave, the weight of the saved promise sitting light and warm in her bag, next to the photo of her grandma she carried everywhere. [647]
Outside, the sun was rising over the hills, painting the sky in streaks of the same burnt orange as the threads of the tapestry, and she smiled, knowing she’d finally kept the promise she had made to her grandma all those years before. [690]
