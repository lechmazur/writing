The faint blue glow of bioluminescent coral seeped through the cracked marble columns of the forgotten underwater temple, painting streaks of icy light across Elara’s scuffed dive suit as she fumbled to trigger her sub’s last remaining distress beacon. [39]
Her oxygen gauge blinked a frantic red, reminding her she was working as time runs short, with barely twelve minutes of breathable air left before her tanks ran dry. [68]
No sound penetrated the thick dome of the temple’s central chamber save for the slow gurgle of water through fissures and the faint whir of her rebreather, a gathered hush so dense she could almost feel it pressing against the glass of her faceplate. [112]
Half-buried in a bed of pearlescent silt at the altar’s foot sat the crystal perfume vial she’d spent three years tracking, its facets catching the coral glow as if holding trapped fragments of the sun the temple’s ancient worshippers had once revered. [154]
She’d come here not for treasure or academic acclaim, but driven by a motivation to harness the ephemeral, to capture the fragile, lost scent of ritual that had vanished when the temple slipped beneath the waves 2,000 years prior. [193]
Old texts had warned the vial’s contents carried an attribute that touches memories, that anyone who breathed its scent would relive the exact moments the perfume had been sealed inside, unfiltered and unedited. [226]
As she stretched forward to curl her gloved fingers around the vial’s cool neck, her safety line snagged on a jagged piece of carved marble, yanking her back and tangling so tight around her left thigh she could barely shift her weight. [268]
Her first instinct was to thrash, to yank hard until the line snapped, but she knew that would dislodge the loose rubble above the altar and bury the vial forever, so she forced herself to focus on the action she needed to take: to unsnarl the line by overriding instinct, moving slow and deliberate even as her oxygen gauge ticked down another 60 seconds. [334]
When the line finally came free, she floated for half a second, taking in the carved friezes of processions and sun blessings wrapping around the chamber walls, the quiet glow of the coral, the complete absence of any other human soul for 120 miles in every direction, and the lonesome splendor of the space settled over her so sharply she almost forgot to breathe. [397]
She twisted the vial’s tiny stopper free, and a wisp of pale, golden scent drifted up, dissolving into the small bubble of air she’d released under her faceplate to breathe it in. [434]
Memories not her own flooded her mind: the heat of the sun on her shoulders, the sound of flutes and chanting, the weight of a flower crown in her hair, the quiet joy of laying the vial on the altar as an offering to the gods who watched over the coastal village. [485]
For a second she was no longer Elara, the archaeologist who’d spent half her life chasing lost stories, she was the young priestess who’d sealed the vial, grieving the coming storm that she knew would swallow her home whole. [529]
The distress beacon on her wrist beeped loud, cutting through the memory, and she checked her gauge: four minutes of air left, enough time to make it back to her sub if she moved fast, even with the small, carefully wrapped vial tucked into the inner pocket of her suit. [580]
She didn’t know if the rescue team would get her signal in time, if she’d even make it to the surface to share what she’d found, but for the first time in years, she didn’t feel the sharp, restless hunger that had driven her across every ocean on the planet. [635]
She’d captured the ephemeral, held a piece of a lost world in her hands, and even if she never got to tell anyone else about it, the memory of the priestess’s joy, of the sun on her shoulders, would stay with her for the rest of her life. [687]
She kicked off from the altar, glancing back once at the quiet, glowing chamber, the gathered hush still wrapping around it like a blanket, before she turned toward the faint glimmer of open water above, the vial warm against her chest even through the thick neoprene of her suit. [744]
The distress beacon blinked steadily beside her, and for the first time since she’d dived down, she didn’t feel afraid of what came next, no matter how short her remaining time might be. [781]
