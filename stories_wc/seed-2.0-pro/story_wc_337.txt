Lira, a broken mask restorer who had spent ten years gluing cracked wooden visages pulled from coastal shipwrecks, huddled in the prism sanctuary under starlight, the structure’s faceted quartz walls refracting distant constellations into tiny, shifting rainbows across the stone floor. [41]
She had come there across sharing a secret with the three lingering spirits of fishermen lost to a 17th-century gale, their faint, glowing forms hovering just above the crates of recovered wreckage stacked against the sanctuary’s back wall. [79]
Her motivation to ground restless spirits had been stoked six months prior, when her grandfather’s ghost had appeared at her workshop door, dripping saltwater and begging her to free the men who had died alongside him when their nets caught on a jagged reef mid-storm. [124]
The tone of lucid unrest hung thick in the sanctuary, no wind rustling the air, no sounds of the nearby shore drifting past the quartz walls, only the faint, unsteady hum of the spirits’ unresolved grief vibrating just under her skin. [165]
Before her lay the centuries-old fisherman’s net, its thick hemp strands tangled tight with fragments of bone, splinters of ship wood, and the frayed edges of the fishermen’s wool scarves, each knot holding a piece of the terror they had felt in their final moments. [210]
She would exhume their unspoken final words, the memories they had never gotten to pass to their families, by unraveling knots in a centuries-old fisherman’s net, a method she had learned from her grandmother’s old book of coastal folk magic. [250]
In her right hand, she held a tapestry needle, its dull steel tip worn smooth from decades of her grandmother using it to repair fishing nets and mend holes in the tapestries that lined their cottage walls. [287]
The core concept of interlaced hush guided her work, each silence tangled in the knots woven together with the strands of the net, every unspoken sentence tangled with the next until the weight of them had kept the spirits tied to the mortal world for 400 years. [334]
She knew the net accumulates silence, each unspoken word from every fisherman who had ever used it, every scream swallowed by storm waves, every wish for home never spoken aloud, tangled into its knots like tiny, trapped pearls. [372]
She pressed the tip of the tapestry needle into the first tight knot, working it loose slowly, careful not to snap the fragile hemp, and as the knot came undone, a soft puff of cool air brushed her cheek, carrying the faint sound of her grandfather’s laugh, the one he used to give her when she brought him fresh baked bread after his fishing trips. [437]
The second knot released the voice of the youngest fisherman, barely 19, telling his mother he loved her, that he was sorry he had not said goodbye before he left on the trip, and the glowing form of the boy flickered brighter for a moment before a small smile touched his translucent face. [490]
For hours, she worked, her fingers cramping, her eyes heavy, but she did not stop, each knot she undid releasing another piece of memory, another unspoken confession, another wish held in silence for centuries. [524]
The lucid unrest in the room softened slowly, the sharp hum of grief fading into a warm, soft murmur, as each spirit found their missing words, their unspoken truths finally free to drift out of the sanctuary and to the families that had grieved them for generations. [571]
When the final knot came undone, her grandfather stepped forward, his translucent hand brushing her hair even though she could not feel it, and he mouthed “thank you” before his form, and those of the other two fishermen, faded away into the starlight filtering through the prism walls. [619]
She sat back, staring at the now loose, untangled hemp net spread across the floor, the tapestry needle still held loosely in her hand, and she smiled, knowing she had kept her promise to her grandfather, that the men who had died at sea were finally home. [666]
The quiet of the sanctuary felt different now, lighter, no longer weighed down by trapped silence, and as she packed up her things to leave, she spotted a small carved wooden mask tucked under the edge of the net, its crack running right down the middle, a new project waiting for her when she returned to her workshop the next morning. [718]
