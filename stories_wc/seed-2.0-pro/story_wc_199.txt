The orderly seed sorter knelt on the moss-draped planks of the ashen skyborne arbor, her wicker sorting tray balanced across her knees as she waited for the hush where past meets prophecy to settle over the cloud-tethered realm. [38]
She had spent three cycles tending this outpost, her days spent sorting viable cloud-seed from chaff, but her quiet, unspoken motivation was to decode the message in falling leaves that drifted up to the arbor from the mountain villages far below. [79]
Regional folklore from those villages held that during this exact hush, the veil between what was and what could be thinned enough that forgotten things returned, carrying answers tucked in their seams. [111]
The sorter’s chosen method for drawing those answers out was building tiny monuments to forgotten things, each no bigger than her thumbnail, tucked into the crooks of the arbor’s ash-bark branches. [142]
She had built dozens already: a tiny replica of a mill that burned a century prior, a minuscule locket matching one lost by a village girl in 1872, an itty-bitty horseshoe from a mountaineer’s pony that fell to a crevice in 1921, each carved from moss and cloud-seed husk with painstaking care. [194]
Today, as the hush wrapped the arbor in soft, muffled stillness, a glint caught her eye: a single moonstone cabochon dust speck, no larger than a grain of pollen, resting on the edge of her sorting tray. [231]
She knew from the folklore that anything that arrived during this hush emerges transformed always, so she did not brush it away, but leaned in, her breath held tight with the kind of mossy delight that only comes from chasing small, secret magic. [274]
Curious, she used the sharpened tip of her seed-sorting tweezers to prod the speck, half expecting it to blow away in the faint arbor breeze, but it clung fast, glowing brighter as the tip made contact. [310]
In the next heartbeat, the speck unfurled, spreading into a thin, glowing film that coated every one of her tiny monuments, then drifted down to land on the crumpled maple leaf she had retrieved three days prior, the one she had been trying to interpret for weeks. [357]
Where the film touched the leaf, faint, silver text bloomed across its veins, written in the old mountain dialect the sorter had learned from her grandmother, who had first told her the stories of the skyborne arbor as a child. [397]
The message was simple: the villages below would face a harsh, six-month drought starting at the next full moon, but the seed sorter could prevent it by sowing the cloud-seed she had sorted in the patterns of the tiny monuments she had built, each tied to the memory of someone who had once protected the mountain valleys. [454]
She laughed, soft and warm, as the moss on the arbor planks glowed the same silver as the leaf’s text, the mossy delight in her chest swelling so bright she thought it might leak out into the clouds around her. [494]
She learned later that the moonstone speck had once been part of a pendant worn by her great-great-aunt, who had worked as a sky arbor tender three generations prior, lost to a cloud storm before she could pass on her own knowledge of the leaf messages. [540]
The folklore had been right: the speck had emerged transformed always, shifting from a forgotten fragment of jewelry to the key that unlocked the answer she had spent years chasing, a gift from a relative she had never known, sent through the thin veil of the hush. [587]
That evening, before the hush faded entirely, she sowed the cloud-seed in the exact patterns of her tiny monuments, watching as soft, cool rain began to fall toward the valleys below, carrying the protection of all the forgotten people she had honored with her carvings. [632]
When she returned to her sorting tray later, she found a tiny new carving resting where the moonstone speck had once sat: a minuscule replica of her great-great-aunt’s pendant, etched with the same silver script that had covered the maple leaf. [673]
She tucked it into the crook of the thickest ash branch, right next to the tiny mill carving, knowing that it would wait for the next hush, and the next seed sorter who came looking for answers in the falling leaves. [714]
