The time dilation observation deck hummed with the faint, ghostly vibration of dormant generators, its glass panels fogged at the edges three decades after the shutdown. [26]
Echoed silence wrapped around Kael, the blessed pariah of the old station, as he stepped over a crumpled warning sign left by the last evacuation crew. [52]
The anomaly that warped the deck’s core and forced the shutdown left him temporally stretched, one year of his time equal to seven for everyone else back on the planet below. [83]
He’d returned today not to mourn the station he’d once called home, but to follow his quiet motivation to turn confusion into exploration, to find the answer to the disorientation that had clung to him since the accident. [121]
He pulled a tattered leather notebook from his coat pocket, following a family custom of sketching the night sky on anniversaries—today marked 42 years since his parents first brought him to the deck, just six years by his own stretched timeline. [162]
As he leaned against the cold observation rail, his sleeve caught on a loose gilded compass rose applique bolted to the metal, its edges worn smooth by decades of curious hands. [193]
He pried it loose gently, turning it over in his palm, and caught a glimpse of a faint blue glow emanating from its etched center, pointing steady past the observation glass at a point far beyond the visible constellations. [232]
He’d spent years feeling adrift, his internal clock out of sync with every person he’d ever loved, never finding a fixed point to orient himself, but that glow aligned exactly with the true north his parents had taught him to map as a child, unmoored from planetary magnetic shifts or time’s distortion. [284]
He flipped open his notebook, flipping past dozens of sketches of constellations he’d drawn on previous anniversaries, each marked with two dates: one for the planet below, one for his own stretched timeline. [317]
He’d always assumed the disorientation he felt was a side effect of the time dilation, a permanent misalignment between his body and the world around him, but the steady glow of the applique told him he’d been looking for the wrong fixed point all along. [362]
The station’s old mission had been to map the edges of time dilation pockets scattered across the sector, a project abandoned when the accident left most of the crew dead and Kael as the only surviving witness. [399]
The planet’s council had declared him a bad omen, barring him from most public spaces and cutting off contact with the few remaining family members he had left, hence his title as a blessed pariah: blessed to survive, cursed to outlive everyone he cared for. [444]
He pulled a charcoal stick from his notebook’s spine, and began to sketch the point the applique pointed to, adding a tiny golden compass rose in the corner of the page to match the one in his palm. [482]
For the first time since the accident, he didn’t feel like he was stuck between two timelines, watching everyone he loved age past him while he remained frozen in a perpetual young adulthood. [515]
That true north wasn’t tied to any planet or any timeline, it was tied to the memory of his parents, to the joy of mapping the sky together, to the mission he’d been too young to take up before the shutdown. [556]
He slipped the applique into his coat’s inner pocket, securing it next to a faded photo of his parents standing on this very observation deck, grinning as they pointed out the north star to a seven-year-old version of himself. [595]
The echoed silence of the deck no longer felt like a prison, it felt like a launchpad, a quiet place to plan the exploration he’d spent decades craving, mapping the dilation pockets no one else dared to study. [633]
He tucked his notebook under his arm and turned toward the deck’s exit, already calculating the first jump coordinates he’d plot, guided by the steady, unchanging glow of the gilded compass rose and the fixed true north he’d finally found. [673]
For the first time in his life, the temporally stretched pariah didn’t feel adrift, he felt seen, anchored to a purpose that made his unusual existence feel like a gift rather than a curse. [707]
