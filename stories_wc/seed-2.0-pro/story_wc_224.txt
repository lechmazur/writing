Long after remembering her lost coastal village became too sharp to hold without bleeding, the mercury-glass seer wandered the labyrinthine bookstore carved into the entirety of the Ironspine Mountain, her mirrored palms catching the dim amber glow of floating shelf lamps. [41]
She had long learned to substitute reality nightly, wrapping herself in quiet, half-true visions of other lives to outrun the grief that gnawed at the edges of her waking hours, but tonight a faint hum tugged her past shelves of leather-bound star charts and handwritten recipe collections for storm bread. [91]
Her only steady motivation through three decades of wandering had been to understand the shape of home, not the crumbled stone of the village swept away by the hurricane, but the intangible, warm thing that had lived in the gaps between her grandmother’s stories and the salt wind that used to curl through her bedroom window. [147]
The hum led her to a crammed nook on the mountain’s seventh sub-level, where a smooth, pockmarked gray rock half-buried under a pile of vintage birding guides thrummed in time with the pulse under her mirrored skin, and she recognized it instantly as the lost language stone her grandmother had hidden in her backpack the week before the storm hit, the one she’d thought lost forever when she’d been pulled from the floodwaters. [220]
Her grandmother had told her the stone worked only for those who tended to their buried talents, and the seer had spent so long hiding her gift of seeing memory threads that she’d almost forgotten she could pull truths from ordinary objects, if she used the right conduit. [268]
Tucked in the collar of her wool coat was a single downy gull feather she’d found on a riverbank the year before, its soft, ridged texture holding just enough of the sky’s memory to bridge the gap between her mirrored hands and the stone’s quiet hum. [314]
She ran the feather slowly over the face of the stone, and via the texture of a feather, the stone unlocked the memories her grandmother had encoded into it, first images of the village’s annual herring run, then the sound of her grandmother’s lullaby, then the quiet instruction she’d been too young to understand before: seed the old words in every new place you stay, so home grows wherever you are. [385]
For the first time since the storm, the seer did not flinch from the weight of her memories, the quiet rumble of them under her skin like hidden thunder, promising no destruction, only the soft, steady nourishment of rain after a long drought. [428]
She pulled a leather-bound notebook from her bag, the one she used to jot down strange phrases she found in the bookstore’s forgotten texts, and copied the first of the old coastal words from the stone’s visions onto its first blank page, tucking the feather between the pages to mark the spot. [480]
She left the notebook on a low, heavily trafficked shelf near the bookstore’s main entrance, so every visitor who picked it up would carry a little piece of her village’s language out into the world, planting the first of many seeds she would scatter over the coming years. [528]
She no longer felt the urge to substitute reality nightly, because she had finally found that the shape of home was not a fixed place tied to painful memories, but the talent she had buried for so long: the ability to carry pieces of the people she loved and plant them wherever she went, making space for warmth even in the coldest, most unfamiliar corners of the world. [596]
The hum of the lost language stone pulsed softly in her bag as she wandered deeper into the bookstore’s mountain corridors, planning which of the old words she would plant next in a handwritten margin of a poetry collection, which in a note tucked into a jar of honey for the bookstore’s owner, which whispered to the stray cat that napped on the front desk every afternoon. [663]
Far below, the mountain’s core rumbled softly, matching the beat of her heart, the quiet promise of hidden thunder that grief could be turned into growth, and that home was always just a seed away from blooming. [700]
