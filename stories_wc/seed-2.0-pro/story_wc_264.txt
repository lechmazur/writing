After the last forest burns, the only color left on the charred continental shelf is the glowing dune in bloom, its violet sea thrift clinging to sand that hums faint blue when stepped on. [34]
Kael is a dreamless freight guard, who spent ten years patrolling the cross-continental supply trains that hauled ash away from the burn zones, never once catching a stray thought or half-formed memory when he slept. [69]
He left the trains three months prior, his only driving purpose to cultivate the sparks in the unlit corners of the world, the faint flickers of what was lost that no one else bothered to hunt for. [106]
The only memento he carried was a chess clock frozen at midnight, its glass face cracked along the 12, its buttons rusted shut, found tucked in the hollow of a burnt oak on the last day of the fire. [145]
He’d come to the dune chasing rumors of the divergent archive, a collection of every possible timeline that never came to pass, stored not in shelves or servers but in the vibration of the sand grains under his boots. [184]
The archive was occasionally everywhere, flickering into view at crossroads and burnt out clearings for a handful of heartbeats before vanishing again, but old travelers said it rooted itself to the dune for one week every year when the thrift bloomed. [225]
The air hummed with a fractured lullaby, half familiar tune half static, stitched from the lullabies parents in lost timelines sang to their children before bed. [251]
He’d spent three days learning to charm the archive into revealing its hidden sparks, the old traveler’s notes saying the only way to reach it was through wave functions, matching the vibration of his own pulse to the hum of the sand. [293]
He set the frozen chess clock in the sand in front of him, its cracked face catching the blue glow of the dune, and closed his eyes, letting his breath slow to match the soft sway of the thrift blooms in the wind. [336]
As a dreamless guard, he’d always had an unusually steady pulse, no flickers of dreams to jolt it off rhythm, and that steadiness made the wave function alignment far easier than the traveler’s notes had warned. [372]
When his pulse matched the sand’s hum exactly, the static in the lullaby softened, and he saw them: thousands of tiny golden sparks, tucked in the gaps between sand grains, each holding a memory of a timeline where the forests never burned, where fireflies still danced under oak canopies, where children never learned the taste of ash. [429]
He reached out, careful not to break the wave function alignment, and cupped a handful of sparks, feeling their warm, faint tingle against his palms, the first soft flicker of a dream he’d ever felt stirring in the back of his mind. [471]
The chess clock at his feet ticked once, just once, even though its mechanism had been frozen solid for ten years, as if the sparks were seeping into its rusted gears, breathing life into the long-still time it held. [510]
He tucked a handful of sparks into the inner pocket of his guard’s jacket, next to a handful of dune sand, and knew he would carry them to every burnt out corner of the continent, planting them where the ash was thickest, letting the possibility of better timelines take root. [560]
The fractured lullaby swelled as he stood, picking up the chess clock and tucking it back into his pack, the tiny tick now a quiet undercurrent to the hum of the dune, a promise that time did not have to stay frozen, that loss did not have to be permanent. [610]
When he walked away from the dune, he glanced back once, and saw the archive flickering at the edge of his vision, winking in and out of existence as it always did, already preparing to vanish to its next stop when the blooms faded at the end of the week. [660]
That night, when he curled up in the shell of a burnt gas station a hundred miles from the dune, he fell asleep and had his first dream: a forest full of oak trees, children laughing as they chased fireflies, a chess clock on a picnic table ticking steadily, not frozen, counting down the minutes until the first rain of spring. [721]
He woke up with a smile on his face, the sparks in his pocket warm against his chest, and knew that every unlit corner he visited from that day forward would be a little brighter, a little closer to the timeline he had seen in his dream. [768]
