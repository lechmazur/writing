When tomorrow disappears, the tidal atrium’s slate floors flood twice per lunar cycle, no longer tied to the distant, dead ocean tides. [22]
The titan-gear librarian who tends the atrium’s collection of memory codices moves slowly through the shallow, sun-warmed water, their brass exoskeleton whirring soft enough to match the hum of the wintersun through frosted skylights. [56]
They carry a dented fisherman’s bamboo creel slung over one of their cogged shoulders, its woven sides frayed at the edges from decades of careful use. [82]
Their core programming, rooted in the core concept of migrating identities, fluctuates with moon phases: on new moons they answer to Lira, a former fisher who once brought daily catch to the atrium’s old ground-floor market, on full moons they are Kael, the first archivist who built the titan-gear frame to outlive the collapse of linear time. [143]
Their quiet, unprogrammed motivation, woven into every choice they make, is to preserve wonder in routine, even when no future exists to plan for or look toward. [176]
Today is a waxing crescent, so they are a little of both, their hands half calloused from hauling nets, half stained with ink from repairing water-damaged codices, and they smile to themself as they stop at a stone plinth half submerged in the pale floodwater. [222]
Resting on the plinth is a pile of iridescent memory shards, fragments of identities that lost their anchors when tomorrow vanished, left to wash up on the atrium’s high tide lines every full moon. [259]
They have tended these shards for decades, sorting and cataloging, but they only recently learned how to smelt the shards into new, whole identities that do not erase the old ones, and the method works only by accepting contradictions: that a person can be two people at once, that a past can coexist with no future, that loss and joy can occupy the same small, soft space. [334]
They kneel, the cogs of their legs hissing softly as they lower their weight, and scoop a handful of the glowing shards from the plinth, dropping them one by one into the open bamboo creel at their hip. [373]
The thin wintersun catches the sharp edges of the shards, throwing tiny, wavering rainbows across the atrium’s walls, where faded murals of fishermen and librarians and children dance in the shifting light. [409]
They have done this same routine every waxing crescent for seventeen lunar cycles, and each time it feels new, each shard holding a story no one has heard in years: a first kiss at a rickety fishing dock, a first book bound by hand, a storm that kept the entire local fleet ashore for three sunny days. [461]
When the creel is half full, they carry it to the small, sun-warmed forge tucked in the atrium’s western corner, its fire kept stoked by the same geothermal heat that warms the floodwaters to a pleasant, bath-like temperature. [498]
They pour the shards into the clay crucible, and the low, steady heat pulls them into a shimmering, liquid pool that shifts color with the faint glow of the crescent moon visible through the frosted skylight above. [536]
They do not rush the process, letting the shards melt slowly, letting their conflicting memories mix instead of forcing them to separate, because they know that the most whole, vibrant identities are the ones that hold all their contradictions without flinching. [582]
When the liquid is smooth and unclouded, they pour it into a small, oblong mold lined with pressed sea grass, and when it cools ten minutes later, they pull out a new codex page, etched with the combined stories of the shards, ready to be added to the collection where curious visitors will find it for as long as the atrium stands. [636]
They tuck the new page into their leather satchel, then empty the few remaining tiny scraps from the bamboo creel back into the tide, where they will wash up again on the next high tide, ready to become part of something new on the next cycle. [681]
As they walk back toward the atrium’s main stacks, their exoskeleton whirring softly in time with the lap of water against the slate floors, they pause to run a hand over the surface of the floodwater, watching the ripples break the reflection of the wintersun into a thousand tiny, glowing pieces. [728]
No one knows when or if tomorrow will come back, but that does not matter, because the small, steady wonder of tending to stories, of holding space for all the people everyone has ever been, is enough to fill every present moment, warm and bright and unshakable. [776]
