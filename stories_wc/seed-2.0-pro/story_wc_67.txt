Mara, the city’s only charcoal portrait ash scatterer, slipped through the cracked side door of the Vanishing Point Gallery ten minutes before opening, her satchel heavy with two small ceramic urns and a hand-carved ash sifter. [31]
She had secured after-hours access three days prior, and like she always did, she weaves truth carefully when speaking to the curator, saying only that she planned to add a small memorial component to the gallery’s emerging artist group show. [59]
She made no mention of the ash, or the mask she had assembled by hand from the shattered porcelain shards pulled from the wreckage of her sister Lila’s pottery studio, which had burned to the ground six weeks earlier. [93]
In the far corner of the gallery, tucked beside the wall of unframed canvas submissions, sat Lila’s old oak rocking chair, which had been caught in the draft when the movers carried it in the previous afternoon and had been rocking softly ever since. [134]
Her timeframe for the entire installation was narrow: she had to finish every step as the old rocking chair finally slows to a stop, a small, private ritual she had designed to honor Lila, who had rocked in that chair while sketching every one of her pottery designs. [179]
The curator had granted her access without asking further questions, and that was exactly how Mara planned it, working through incomplete information to avoid the pushback she knew would come if she admitted she intended to leave ash on a piece displayed in the gallery’s main space. [224]
She pulled the porcelain mask from her satchel first, running a finger along the sharp, sanded edges of each shard, marveling quietly at how something so fragile could survive a fire that had melted even the steel kiln shelves in Lila’s studio. [265]
The core concept she was exploring with the piece, even if she never shared it with anyone, was the mirage of safety: Lila had signed a three-year lease on the studio two months before the fire, bragging that she had finally found a permanent, affordable space to build her career, never knowing the building’s wiring had been faulty for a decade. [322]
Her motivation for the entire project was simple: to crystallize a fleeting emotion she had carried since the last night she saw Lila, when her sister had held up the half-finished, unbroken version of the mask and laughed, saying she wanted her work to feel like a memory you could reach out and touch. [374]
The chair’s rocking had grown slower already, its creaks softening as it lost momentum, so Mara moved quickly, pulling the first urn from her bag and twisting off its lid to reveal the fine, black charcoal ash inside, left over from the portrait of Lila she had burned three days after the funeral. [427]
Her next action was to powder the entire surface of the porcelain mask with the ash, turning the bright white shards a soft, matte gray, the dark dust settling into the cracks between each piece to trace the mask’s jagged, uneven seams. [472]
The somber marvel of the moment made her chest tight, as she watched the ash catch the faint, slanted morning light seeping through the gallery’s skylight, tiny flecks of black glowing like scattered stars against the pale porcelain. [513]
She had spent weeks worrying the ash would look messy, or disrespectful, but instead it looked like the mask was holding Lila’s shadow, every crevice holding a piece of the sister she had lost far too soon. [550]
The rocking chair gave one final, soft creak, then fell completely still, right as Mara tapped the last of the ash off the sifter and set the mask on the small wooden shelf the curator had reserved for her installation. [591]
She stepped back to look at her work, smiling through the thin film of tears on her cheeks, as the sound of the gallery’s front door unlocking echoed from the other end of the space, signaling the curator had arrived for opening prep. [635]
No one would ever know the ash was there, not really, not unless they ran their finger over the mask’s surface and felt the faint, powdery residue, but that was okay with Mara; the memory wasn’t for the gallery patrons, it was for her, and for Lila, finally held still in the shards of what she had left behind. [697]
