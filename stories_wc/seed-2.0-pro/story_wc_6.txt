Lila, the arsonist’s daughter who’d served three years as a coastal firefighter, picked her way down the sand slope to the obsidian lagoon beneath the dunes, gracefully alert for loose shards of volcanic glass half-buried in the warm grit. [39]
It was the season of cloud migration, when puffy silver formations drifted hundreds of miles from the northern ice sheets to paint soft, moving shadows across the coast all day long. [70]
She’d come here today for one quiet, stubborn reason: to prove that scars can sing, that the marks of old harm don’t have to dictate the shape of the good someone can do in the world. [106]
In her pocket she carried a small pouch of black sand from the volcanic beach ten miles north, collected after she’d helped put out a wildfire sparked by a careless camper three months prior. [140]
Around her neck hung her good luck charm, a foreign coin drilled and strung on red thread, given to her by a survivor of the apartment fire her father had set when she was seven, the woman telling her she was braver than the harm her family had caused. [189]
For years she’d carried the weight of her father’s choices, but she’d built her life around unyielding compassion, showing up for every fire victim with the same patience she wished someone had given her dad when he was lost in his own rage and grief. [234]
Today, she planned to graft a sapling of fire-resistant coastal mangrove, grown from seeds she’d harvested from burned areas that had regrown on their own, to a native mangrove growing at the lagoon’s edge, its bark still marked with faint scorch lines from last summer’s fire. [280]
The water was still as polished glass, dotted with clumps of kelp that drifted lazily with the faint tidal pull, casting long, wavy shadows across the dark pebbles at the lagoon’s floor. [312]
She knelt on the soft bank, pulled a small folding knife from her belt, and made a clean, shallow cut into the native mangrove’s bark, the texture rough and warm under her fingertips. [345]
She sprinkled a pinch of the black volcanic sand into the cut, a trick she’d learned from an elder in the local Indigenous community, who said the volcanic earth carried the memory of fire and rebirth to help new growth take hold. [387]
She touched the drilled foreign coin on its red thread to the cut for three slow breaths, a small ritual she used to honor both the harm her father had caused and the good she hoped to leave in its place. [428]
She fitted the cut end of the fire-resistant sapling into the notch she’d made, wrapped it tight with waterproof biodegradable tape, and sat back on her heels to watch the clouds drift overhead. [461]
She thought of the thin, silvery scar that ran up her left forearm, from when she’d tried to pull her father away from the match he’d held to their apartment’s curtains when she was small, and how she used to hide it under long sleeves even on the hottest summer days. [513]
Now she rolled up her sleeve without hesitation, letting the warm coastal air brush the scar, because she’d learned that unyielding compassion first meant being kind to the parts of herself she’d once thought were too broken to be seen. [553]
A soft breeze carried the faint, gurgling sound of the lagoon’s outlet meeting the open ocean, and a clump of kelp drifted past the bank, bobbing gently as if keeping time to some quiet, unheard song. [589]
She looked at the grafted mangrove, at the small scar where the two pieces of wood were joined, and knew she’d already proven what she’d come to prove: scars don’t just mark where you’ve been hurt, they mark where new life can take root, if you let it. [637]
She pulled a small notebook from her pack to mark the date of the graft, tucking a single grain of the black volcanic sand between the pages to mark the moment, the same way she tucked stories of fire survivors who’d rebuilt their lives into the margins of every page. [687]
As the last of the migrating clouds drifted over the lagoon, she smiled, tracing the edge of the foreign coin on its red thread, knowing the scar on her arm, the graft on the mangrove, and the song of the coast were all proof that kindness could outlast even the hottest fire. [739]
