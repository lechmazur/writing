Lira, the sea-foam recollectionist, perched on a jagged quartz ledge in the iridescent cave inside a giant crystal geode, running a thumb over the pocked surface of a shard of crater glass she’d fished from the tidewrack three moons prior. [40]
Her trade, which she’d practiced for 72 tide cycles, was that she transliterates emotions from half-forgotten trinkets into tangible, glowing thread that she weaves into tapestries for villagers who’d lost loved ones or favorite places. [75]
For months, she’d been chasing the core concept of missing pieces, specifically the gaps in her own memory where the old cliffside walking paths she’d loved as a child used to be, her core motivation to discover why some paths disappear entirely rather than simply fading from common use. [124]
She knew she had to work quickly, her timeframe the narrow window before memory fades entirely, leaving only blank, unfeeling space where those paths once lived in her mind. [153]
The cave hummed around her, light refracting off the geode walls to paint her sea-foam green hair in streaks of pink and lavender, a soft, whimsical glow that undercut the sharp, sad twist in her chest at the thought of losing the paths forever, the cheerful gloom she’d grown used to wrapping around her like a well-loved shawl. [211]
Today, she planned to conjecture what had become of the paths via the scattered seeds dropped by a migrating bird she’d caught leaving tiny, sunflower-like seeds across the cave mouth two days prior, seeds that glowed the same faint blue as the emotion of remembrance she often pulled from trinkets. [261]
She set the crater glass down on the flat quartz altar in the middle of the cave, then arranged the seeds in a circle around its edge, letting the faint warmth from the glass seep into the seeds to activate the tiny traces of memory stored in their husks. [310]
As she closed her eyes and breathed in the sharp, mineral scent of the geode, she felt the seeds begin to hum, and the crater glass lit up with snippets of memory she could transliterate directly into her own mind, bypassing the thread she usually used for her work. [359]
She saw first that the paths had been washed away in a storm she’d forgotten, the cliffside crumbling under 20-foot waves that had swept the dirt and stone trails into the ocean, leaving no trace behind for anyone to find. [399]
She also saw that she’d blocked the memory because she’d been walking the path with her older sister that day, the sister who’d been swept out to sea in the same storm, the missing piece of her own past that she’d hidden away to avoid the sharp, unyielding pain of loss. [450]
Tears rolled down her cheeks as the full memory flooded in, the sound of her sister’s laugh, the taste of salt on the wind, the way her sister had pushed her to safety just before the wave hit, and she laughed through the tears, the cheerful gloom of the memory wrapping around her instead of cutting her open. [508]
She’d found her answer: paths don’t disappear because they’re destroyed, they disappear when the people who love them hide their memories away to protect themselves from the pain of losing the things those paths are tied to. [545]
She had just enough time before memory fades to weave the full story into a tapestry, one that would hang in the village square so everyone who’d loved the paths and missed her sister could remember them both, the joy and the grief tangled together in glowing thread. [593]
As she picked up the crater glass and brushed the glowing seeds into her pocket to use in the tapestry, she smiled, knowing that even the missing pieces could be found again, if you were brave enough to look for them. [634]
The geode walls glowed brighter around her, as if cheering her on, the soft hum matching the sound of her sister’s laugh that she now carried with her, no longer hidden away in a gap in her memory. [672]
