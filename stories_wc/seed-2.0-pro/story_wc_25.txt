Jessa, a labyrinth maintenance worker who’d spent three years navigating the towering labyrinth of shipping containers stacked along the fog-choked port city coast, leaned against a rusted corrugated wall as the first flickers of the geomagnetic storm bleached the overcast sky pale green. [43]
Her most unusual skill, one she’d honed during hundreds of unaccompanied patrols, was that she identifies people’s walks from fifty meters away even through fog and the rattle of loose container panels. [75]
She’d come out mid-shift tonight to chase a dare, her core motivation to prove that whispers travel farther than shouts after her coworker Marnie had laughed off the claim two nights prior over takeout fries. [110]
Her plan balanced calculated spontaneity: she’d picked the storm on purpose, since geomagnetic interference would mess with the port’s radio systems, forcing any communication to travel through the maze of metal walls instead of over airwaves. [146]
She’d tucked three folded maps of the labyrinth’s current layout in her work vest pocket, each marked with a different drop point where she’d left a small speaker broadcasting a pre-recorded whisper of her name. [181]
The challenge required her to track each whisper’s range without using her radio, and meet Marnie at the far end of the maze in 45 minutes with proof each broadcast had carried at least 200 meters. [217]
She’d left a zinc zodiac coin, its face etched with the Aquarius symbol that matched both her and Marnie’s star sign, taped to the side of each speaker to confirm she’d found each drop point’s edge of reception. [255]
Slung on her lanyard next to her standard issue work keys was a key with no apparent lock, a trinket a retired maintenance worker had given her on her first day, saying it “unlocked paths the maps don’t mark” when she’d asked what it went to. [301]
Halfway to the first drop point’s reception edge, a gust of storm-wind slammed a loose upper container panel loose, sending it crashing down to block the narrow corridor marked on her top folded map. [335]
She was about to crumple the map and backtrack 100 meters when she spotted a faint, rusted indent on the container wall beside her, shaped exactly like the head of the unmarked key on her lanyard. [371]
She twisted the key into the indent, and a thin, previously hidden hatch popped open between two stacked containers, leading to a narrow service corridor that cut 80 meters off her planned route. [404]
She moved through the unmarked corridor quickly, and within five minutes she was standing at the first reception edge, where she plucked the first zodiac coin from the speaker, still able to hear her recorded whisper curling soft through the metal corridors. [446]
She repeated the process for the next two drop points, adjusting her route on the remaining folded maps as small storm shifts rearranged the maze’s looser outer walls, and finished collecting all three coins with 12 minutes left to spare before her meeting with Marnie. [491]
As she rounded the last corner to the meeting point, she picked up the familiar scuff of Marnie’s steel-toe work boots 60 meters away, long before she could see the other woman through the storm’s thickening fog. [528]
Before she could call out, she caught Marnie’s voice, soft and quiet, whispering her name, the sound carrying clear over the howl of wind through the container gaps. [556]
When she stepped into the small open clearing where Marnie waited, her coworker held up her hands, grinning, and said she’d given up shouting through the radio static 10 minutes prior, and found that whispering carried far better through the metal walls. [598]
Jessa dropped the three zodiac coins into Marnie’s outstretched palm, then pulled the dented key with no apparent lock from her lanyard, tapping the folded maps peeking out of her vest pocket to explain how she’d made good time even when the storm blocked her planned paths. [645]
The pair laughed as the storm’s green glow washed over the stacks around them, the quiet win of Jessa’s challenge landing softer than any shouted victory, but sticking far longer, just like she’d known it would. [681]
