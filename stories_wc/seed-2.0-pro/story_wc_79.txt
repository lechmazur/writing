The brass gate separating the shell city of Kaelth from the open dune-sea rusted shut three generations ago, leaving its residents clustered on the ridged backs of half a dozen gargantuan long-dead turtles, their shells worn smooth by centuries of foot traffic and salt wind. [45]
Maren, the spiral-shell cartographer, spent most of her days hunched over a slab of polished mother-of-pearl, etching faint, interlocking lines that mapped not just the shell ridges and tidal pools of the city, but the forgotten routes of the expedition that had left through the gate decades before its hinges seized. [96]
Quietly driven, she rarely spoke of her work to the other residents, who dismissed the lost expedition as a fool’s errand that had deserved the silence that followed its departure. [126]
Her only inherited memento of the expedition, which had been led by her grandmother, was a chipped reed whistle carved with the same interlocking patterns she etched into her maps, its tone thin and reedy when she blew it at dusk. [167]
Every evening, she would climb the highest ridge of the eldest turtle’s shell to stare at the silent watchers on the horizon, tall, hooded figures that stood motionless at the edge of the dune-sea, never approaching, never speaking, and her greatest motivation was to question them about what had happened to her grandmother’s crew. [221]
She had learned from her grandmother’s old journals that the watchers communicated through pattern languages, the same swirling, spiral shapes etched into her maps and the reed whistle, a visual tongue that transcended sound and wind. [257]
Last week, a thin, jagged crack had split through the rusted lower slat of the gate, just wide enough for her to slip through at high tide, when the dune-sea’s shifting sands softened enough to leave a narrow path toward the horizon. [299]
The air that seeped through the crack smelled of sunbaked bone and dried sea grass, and the usual golden glow of the afternoon light took on a sharp, bleached edge that made her skin prickle, the first hint of the sunwashed dread that clung to every story of the land beyond the gate. [352]
She packed her smallest map scroll, the reed whistle tucked into the pocket of her linen tunic, and a skin of fresh water, her steps quiet as she slipped through the empty streets of the city at dawn, when most residents were still asleep in their shell huts. [400]
The metal of the gate scraped her shoulder as she squeezed through the crack, fine rust dust sticking to her sweat-damp skin, and when she turned to look back, the city behind her looked smaller than she had ever seen it, its shell ridges glowing pale under the climbing sun. [450]
She walked for three hours, the sand hot through the soles of her woven sandals, the watchers never seeming to grow closer even as the horizon stretched out endlessly before her, the dread in her chest growing warmer, lighter, as if baked into her bones by the unforgiving sun. [499]
When she finally stood ten paces from the nearest watcher, she pulled the reed whistle from her pocket and blew, the sound carrying across the quiet sand, and the watcher tilted its head, its hood falling back to reveal a face covered in the same spiral patterns she had been mapping her whole life. [553]
She traced the largest spiral on the map scroll she carried, a question she had practiced a hundred times through pattern languages, asking where the members of the lost expedition had gone, and the watcher lifted one long, pale finger to point at the space over her shoulder. [601]
When Maren turned, she saw that the gate was gone, and the city of shelled turtles had vanished, replaced by a line of hooded figures standing where the ridge had once been, their faces turned toward the empty dune-sea where she now stood. [644]
She looked down at her hands, and saw faint spiral patterns blooming across her knuckles, the same mark she had seen on the watcher’s face, and she realized the lost expedition had never disappeared—they had become the watchers, guardians of a gate that only let people cross one way. [693]
She tucked the reed whistle back into her pocket, turned to face the endless dune-sea, and stood still, letting the sun bleach the last of her old life from her bones as she joined the quiet line of watchers, waiting for the next person brave enough to slip through the crack. [744]
