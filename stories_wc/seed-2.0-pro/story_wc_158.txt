The insomniac poet had lived curled in the gargantuan spiral shell of a long-extinct cosmic sea snail for as long as they could remember, their sleep stolen by centuries of longing for the home planet they’d fled when war consumed its cherry blossom groves. [44]
The only memento they’d brought was a frayed galaxy postcard, its edges singed from the escape pod’s reentry burns, printed with an image of their homeworld’s pink canopies tangled in the pale blue arm of the Andromeda spiral. [82]
Their core purpose, honed across 12 generations of sleepless nights scribbling verse on the shell’s pearlescent inner walls, was to photosynthesize pain into beauty, turning the sharp, unyielding grief of displacement into lines that glowed like bioluminescent coral. [120]
They’d spent decades researching the fabled blossom armistice, an ancient spell that could call a permanent truce between warring peoples by coating every weapon in the world in unfurling cherry blossom petals, rendering them too delicate to hold. [158]
They were shyly unstoppable in this work, never sharing their verses with the rare passing interstellar traders, never boasting of their progress, just waking each (sleepless) “morning” to refine the spell’s syllables until they hummed like warm honey on their tongue. [199]
Last cycle, they’d finally found the perfect spot to ensconce the galaxy postcard, tucked into a small natural crevice in the shell’s highest spiral, where the thin, alien wind that drifted through the shell’s opening would catch the ink of the verses they wrote on its back. [246]
The spell was designed to travel via half-sung lullabies carried on an off-key wind, its verses soft enough not to startle even the most skittish of wartime sentries, but persistent enough to seep through concrete bunkers and steel armor alike. [286]
They moved through their days with a quiet reef-shadow joy, the kind of soft, unshakable happiness that lives in the dim, warm crevices of coral reefs, hidden from harsh surface storms but bright enough to light the path for small, lost fish. [328]
One evening, as the faint purple light of the system’s twin suns dipped below the shell’s curved edge, they sang the final verse of the spell into the wind, their voice cracking only slightly when they reached the line referencing their grandmother’s cherry blossom orchard, where they’d learned to write their first poems. [381]
They didn’t expect results for another hundred years, if ever, so they were stunned when three days later, a small scout pod touched down outside the shell, its hull crusted with pink cherry blossom petals that glowed the same pale blue as the spiral on their postcard. [428]
The pilot, a young girl with cherry blossom pollen dusted in her curly brown hair, stepped out holding a copy of the poet’s lullaby, printed on thin homeworld paper, and explained that the blossom armistice had taken effect the day the wind carried the song to their planet, ending a war that had raged for three centuries. [485]
She told them that every rifle, every tank, every missile on the planet had sprouted blossoms overnight, and that soldiers on both sides had dropped their weapons to sit in the new pink groves and share bread and stories for the first time in living memory. [531]
The poet cried when they stepped into the scout pod, their hand resting on the galaxy postcard they’d tucked into their coat pocket, finally ready to return home after centuries of longing spent alone in the shell. [568]
They slept for the first time in 300 years on the journey back, lulled by the sound of their lullaby playing softly on the pod’s radio, their mind quiet of the grief that had kept them awake for so long, their work of turning pain into beauty finally complete. [617]
When they stepped off the pod onto their homeworld’s soil, the first thing they saw was their grandmother’s old orchard, fully regrown, its branches heavy with blossoms that swayed in the same off-key wind that had carried their song across the stars, filling their chest with that same soft, reef-shadow joy they’d carried with them for all those lonely years. [677]
