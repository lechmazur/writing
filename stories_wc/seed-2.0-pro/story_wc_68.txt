The wannabe philosopher who’d made his home in the sewer tunnels branching off the sunken city ruins dug slowly through sedimentary layers, his caked knuckles scraping against silt and crushed ceramic half-buried since the plague ended three cycles prior. [39]
His rationally rebellious streak had kept him from joining the surface collective’s push to erase all pre-plague records, framing them as the source of the sickness that had wiped out 90% of the population. [73]
He spoke to no one, his tone a steady abrasive calm that warded off the few scavengers who stumbled down into his tunnels, convinced he was either a ghost or a madman hoarding useless trinkets. [108]
His core motivation, the thing that drove him to dig for 12 hours every day without rest, was to preserve ancient methods the collective claimed had no value in their new, “pure” society. [141]
The core concept of competing narratives had been his obsession long before the plague, when he’d been a disgraced undergrad kicked out of his philosophy program for arguing that oral histories were no more valid than physical artifacts when constructing accounts of the past. [185]
Today, his trowel hit something small and metallic, half-embedded in a layer of 150-year-old clay, and when he brushed the silt away he recognized it immediately as a seafarer’s chronometer cog, its teeth still sharp enough to catch on the callus of his thumb. [229]
The collective’s official narrative held that pre-plague seafaring had been a frivolous, selfish activity that spread the plague across continents, but the cog was proof that those sailors had developed precise, life-saving navigational methods that the collective was now re-inventing from scratch, wasting decades of work. [275]
He sat back on his haunches, the cog heavy in his palm, and began to agonize over the choice he’d been avoiding for months: whether to hide the cog in his growing cache of artifacts, or carry it to the surface and force the collective to confront the lie they’d built their new world around. [330]
If he hid it, he would keep the artifact safe, preserve the method of chronometer construction for a future generation that might be willing to listen, but he would be letting the collective’s false narrative go unchallenged for longer, letting more people die as they fumbled with inaccurate navigation tools to cross the newly flooded coastal plains. [387]
If he brought it to the surface, the collective would almost certainly seize and destroy it, label him a plague sympathizer, and either execute him or banish him to the flooded outer zones where no one survived more than a few weeks. [429]
He ran a finger along the cog’s teeth, his abrasive calm not wavering for a second, even as his mind raced through every possible outcome of both choices, no emotion clouding his rational assessment of the risks and rewards. [468]
He had spent years digging through sedimentary layers, pulling out everything from medical textbooks to farming tools, each artifact a counterpoint to the collective’s lie that all pre-plague knowledge was inherently corrupt, each one a brick in the competing narrative he was building piece by piece. [514]
His rationally rebellious choice, when he finally made it, was not the grand, dramatic gesture a younger version of himself would have chased, nor was it the cowardly retreat he’d feared he would settle for. [549]
He made a precise, shallow cut in the soft flesh of his upper arm, slid the small cog under the skin, and stitched the wound shut with sterilized fishing line he’d pulled from a lower sediment layer two weeks prior. [589]
This way, he could carry the cog to the surface, share the measurements and design of the chronometer with the small group of underground engineers he’d been quietly in contact with for months, and keep the physical artifact safe even if the collective searched his belongings. [635]
He did not delude himself that this small act would topple the collective’s narrative overnight, but he knew that every piece of preserved knowledge, every competing story that slipped through the cracks, would eventually erode the lie they’d built their society on. [677]
He slung his trowel back onto his belt, wiped the remaining silt from his hands, and began the slow climb up to the surface, his step steady, his abrasive calm unbroken, the cog pressing lightly against the muscle of his arm as a quiet, tangible promise of the truth he was fighting to preserve. [731]
