<story>Mira had been a wistful loom spinner all her life, weaving sorrows into cloth nobody bought. [16] After her son sailed beyond the horizon and never returned, a raveling silence settled over her cottage. [33] She abandoned the loom, certain the world had nothing left to say. [45] One gray morning she wandered into a rummage sale and bought a battered box of jigsaw puzzle pieces for a single coin. [67] The picture on the lid showed a forgotten orchard deep in a bamboo forest, a place she had dreamed of as a girl. [90] That night, fitting the jigsaw puzzle edges together by candlelight, she felt the room dissolve. [105] The completed border became a doorway, and she stepped through into the very orchard on the lid. [122] Bamboo rose like green pillars around gnarled apple trees that had not borne fruit in a hundred quiet years. [141] Here, she understood, a living soul might carry a message across impossible distance, through the hush of time's unraveling tapestry. [161] At the orchard's heart waited an old keeper, his eyes stoically sparkling beneath frost-white brows. [176] "You wish to speak to one who passed," he said, voice soft as falling leaves. [191] She nodded, clutching a single tree sap amber bead her son had given her before he left. [208] "Inside that bead sleeps a drop of his laughter," the keeper murmured. [220] "To carry your message to him, you must feed it to the loom of this place." [236] He led her to a loom woven of bamboo and moonlight, its threads humming with unfinished lives. [253] "But know the cost," he warned; "to recommence weaving here, you must give what you most wish to keep." [272] Mira's hands trembled, for the amber bead was the last warmth of her child she still possessed. [289] Yet a mother's love measures distance differently than grief does. [299] She placed the bead upon the warp, and the silence that had raveled her world began to unspool into song. [319] The loom drank the amber, weaving her words "I forgive you, come home in dreams" into a thread of light. [339] The thread shot upward through the bamboo, crossing the impossible distance between the living and the lost. [356] Far away, in some unmapped dusk, she sensed her son receive it and smile. [370] The keeper bowed, his sparkling eyes dimming as the orchard's apple trees burst at last into late blossom. [388] "The bead was your tether to this gate," he said gently. [399] "You may never return, for the doorway closes with the giving." [410] Mira only nodded, understanding the latent sacrifice woven into every miracle. [421] She stepped back through the jigsaw edges into her cottage, where the box now held only blank, pieceless wood. [440] The orchard was sealed, the amber gone, yet the silence no longer raveled around her like a shroud. [458] She sat at her old loom and let her fingers begin again the work she had once forsaken. [476] Each evening she wove blossoms into cloth, the color of amber, the color of a boy's remembered laughter. [494] Townsfolk who once ignored her now bought her tapestries, swearing the threads glowed faintly in the dark. [511] She never told them of the orchard, nor of the bargain she had struck beyond the reach of ordinary days. [531] But sometimes, in the deep hush before sleep, she felt a warmth settle beside her like a returning son. [550] And she would smile, knowing her message had crossed every impossible distance and come, somehow, full circle home. [568] The wistful spinner was wistful no more, only steady, only sure. [579] She had traded a keepsake for a sentence, and found the sentence worth more than gold. [595] For love, she had learned, is the only thread long enough to stitch the living to the dead.
