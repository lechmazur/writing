<story>The first time Mara knocked on the tutor's door, she only meant to borrow sugar. [15] He answered as if he had expected her for years, which unsettled her with its mysterious familiarity. [32] The borrowed sugar became weekly tea sessions, held on his rooftop garden among pots of climbing jasmine. [49] He was a tutor of dead languages, fluent in tongues no living mouth still spoke. [64] Mara, an apprentice restorer, wanted only one thing: to learn the language of old buildings. [79] She believed brick and beam held grammar, that a cracked lintel was a sentence waiting to be read. [97] He laughed gently and said that buildings speak only to those who have learned to listen patiently. [114] Week after week, his patiently nimble fingers folded paper cranes while he recited verbs older than the city. [132] On the table between them sat an antique desk seal, brass worn smooth, its face carved with a symbol she could not place. [155] Whenever she asked about it, he changed the subject with a smile, and she let him. [171] Their friendship ran on small, agreed upon lies: that the tea was only tea, that she came merely for company. [191] But she had begun to suspect that he knew exactly what she needed, and was teaching her in secret. [210] One night he promised a lesson she would never forget, timed to a meteor shower. [225] They climbed to the rooftop garden as the first streaks tore across the dark. [239] He pressed the antique desk seal into her palm and told her to lay it against the old stone parapet. [259] When the brass touched the wall, she felt the building hum, as if a long-held breath had finally loosened. [278] Words rose through her fingers, names of masons dead three centuries, complaints of settling foundations, prayers pressed into mortar. [297] She gasped, and began to interrogate him, demanding to know how a seal could carry such a flood. [315] He sat among the meteors' falling light and finally answered her plainly. [327] The seal was the last tool of his own teacher, a woman who had taught buildings to confess. [345] The dead languages he tutored were never meant for books, but for stone, glass, and iron. [361] He had waited, he said, for someone who wanted the language not for power but for repair. [378] That was why he had let her keep borrowing sugar, week after patient week. [392] The lies between them, he admitted, had been a kindness, a way to give her time to be ready. [411] Now she was, and the rooftop garden seemed to lean toward them, listening. [424] Above, the meteor shower scrawled bright sentences across the sky, a language even he could not read. [441] Mara held the warm brass and understood that her real lessons were only beginning. [455] She thought of the buildings she had failed to save, the ones whose pleas she had not understood in time. [475] A collapsed library, a demolished chapel, a row of houses bulldozed before she could ask them anything. [492] She vowed never to be that helpless again, and the tutor read the vow in her eyes. [509] He taught her the first true word that night, a verb meaning to hold, to endure, to remain standing. [528] She repeated it until the parapet warmed beneath her hand, answering in kind. [541] When the meteors faded, they sat in a silence that felt less like an ending than a promise. [559] She returned the seal, but he closed her fingers over it and shook his head. [574] It was hers now, he said, and so was the long, patient work of listening. [589] Next week, she would knock again, sugar bowl in hand, and the lie would carry them onward. [606] Both of them knew, by now, that she had never really come for the sugar at all. [623]</story>
