After the carnival left town, the abandoned spaceport passenger terminal returned to its old silence. [15] Mira, a curious civil engineer intern, stepped through the broken turnstiles with a flashlight and a notebook. [32] Her supervisor had called the place a ruin, but she saw a maze of intentions instead. [48] Every corridor, every dry fountain, every dead escalator had once meant something to the engineers who built it. [66] She had come here to puzzle out new equations for hope. [77] The town downstream was rationing water, and the old terminal sat atop a reservoir nobody trusted anymore. [94] Mira believed she could resurrect the terminal's forgotten plumbing and feed the dry homes below. [109] Stoic wonder settled over her as her beam swept the vaulted ceiling, where painted stars still wheeled. [126] On the floor she found something the carnival had abandoned: a brass explorer's sextant piece, half buried in confetti. [145] It was only a fragment, an arc of graduated metal, but its angles were precise. [160] She turned it over, reading the etched degrees like a sentence in a lost language. [175] An explorer had once used such a tool to find direction by the stars; tonight Mira would use it to find slope. [197] The terminal's original drains had been sealed when the spaceport closed, choking the reservoir. [211] But Mira was functionally creative, the kind of engineer who solved problems with whatever lay at hand. [228] She propped the sextant piece against a pillar and sighted along its arc to measure the gentle pitch of the concourse. [249] If she could not reopen the drains, perhaps she could move the water through redirected flows instead. [266] The maze of intentions began to clarify into a single, usable design. [278] She traced the baggage tunnels with chalk, marking where gravity already wanted to carry runoff. [293] By tilting her plan to match the building's hidden grade, she avoided the sealed pipes entirely. [309] The reservoir would empty through the old departure ramps and down into the town's cisterns. [324] Each measurement she took with the sextant piece confirmed the angle she needed. [337] She worked through the night, prying open a service valve and stacking debris into makeshift weirs. [353] The terminal, built to launch travelers toward distant worlds, would now send water home. [367] When dawn grayed the painted stars, Mira turned the valve and waited. [379] A trembling rose from the floor, then a long sigh of moving water. [392] The flow found her redirected channels and rushed down the departure ramps exactly as her equations promised. [409] Mira watched the dead terminal come alive, its veins carrying purpose again. [421] She had resurrected not the spaceport, but the intention buried inside it. [433] Stoic wonder held her still as the sound carried toward the thirsty town below. [447] She pocketed the sextant piece, a relic of one explorer passed to another. [460] In her notebook she had written equations for slope and volume, but the equation she cared about was simpler. [479] Hope, she decided, was just a flow that had been blocked and needed redirecting. [493] The carnival had brought the town one night of cheap light, then taken it away. [508] Mira meant to leave something that stayed. [515] She climbed to a high window and looked down the long valley where the cisterns sat. [531] Within an hour the lowest one would begin to fill. [541] She imagined the townsfolk waking to taps that no longer coughed dust. [553] They would not know about the maze of intentions she had walked all night to read. [569] They would only know the water came back. [577] That was enough. [580] Mira drew one last sighting through the sextant piece, aligning its arc with the climbing sun. [596] The explorer who once owned it had charted oceans; she had charted a way for water to find its people. [616] Below her, the abandoned terminal hummed with redirected flows, alive in a way no carnival could fake. [633] Mira closed her notebook, certain now of her new equation for hope. [645]
