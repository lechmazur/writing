<story>The curator arrived bewildered, blinking at the orchard that should not exist. [12] Trees of pale light blossomed across a lonely asteroid, their petals drifting upward into a starless dark. [29] He had been summoned here by a will, an inheritance written in a hand he half recognized as his own. [49] The estate, the lawyers said, was simply complicated. [57] That phrase had haunted him between the first and last page of the testament, which described an orchard, a riddle, and a single bequeathed object. [82] The object waited on a marble pedestal: a pistachio shell cameo blank, smooth and unworked, no portrait carved into its surface. [103] "Why leave me an empty cameo?" he asked the empty air. [114] No one answered, so he decided to inquire of the orchard itself. [126] He walked between the luminous trees, touching petals that dissolved like cold sugar against his fingers. [142] Each tree, he realized, bore a different version of his late great-aunt's face, younger in the saplings, ancient in the tallest boughs. [164] This was her doing; she had been an astronomer obsessed with what she called nonlinear inheritance. [180] She believed that memory, like light, did not travel in straight lines, that a grandchild might receive a thought before the grandparent ever had it. [205] Such ideas were forbidden, struck from journals, whispered only in locked rooms. [217] And yet here her theories grew, rooted in stone, fed by starlight, undeniable. [230] The curator felt a brightened disquiet, a wonder sharpened by dread. [241] He understood that to claim the inheritance he would have to understand it, and to understand it he would have to pursue the very forbidden knowledge that had exiled her. [271] He retreated to the small habitat dome at the orchard's edge, where the only private room was a cramped lavatory. [291] There, oddly, his thoughts always cleared. [297] His great-aunt's letters had confessed the same quirk: she did her finest reasoning leaning against sinks, having philosophical breakthroughs in bathrooms while the rest of the lab slept. [325] Inheritance, he thought, was stranger than blood. [332] Staring into the mirror, he saw the cameo blank reflected in his hand, and an idea arrived fully formed, as if delivered from somewhere ahead of him. [359] The blank was not empty because it was unfinished; it was empty because its portrait had not yet been chosen. [379] Nonlinear inheritance meant the carving could come last and still have always been there. [393] He carried the blank back into the orchard, certain now and frightened by his certainty. [408] He pressed it against the oldest tree, the one wearing his great-aunt's most ancient face. [423] The petals stilled; the starlight bent. [429] Into the smooth shell a portrait surfaced, not of her, but of him, older, grayer, smiling with a knowledge he had not yet earned. [453] The face on the cameo mouthed words he could not hear, and somehow he answered them anyway. [470] He finally understood the riddle of the will: she had not left him an estate but a question, looping backward through time. [492] The orchard was a single thought blossoming across years, and he was both its gardener and its seed. [510] To inherit was to become the ancestor of his own understanding. [521] The forbidden knowledge was not a fact but a shape, the shape of a mind teaching itself across generations that refused to stand in order. [546] He laughed, the sound bright against the disquiet pooling in his chest. [558] Tomorrow the lawyers would inquire whether he accepted the bequest. [568] He already knew his answer, because the older face in the cameo had already given it. [584] He set the finished cameo on the pedestal where the blank had waited, completing the loop. [600] Petals rose again, carrying his great-aunt's faces into the dark, and one new face among them, his own, blossoming where it had always belonged. [624]</story>
