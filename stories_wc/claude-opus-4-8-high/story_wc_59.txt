<story>The suspended palace trembled where it hung, anchored to giant stalactites that wept mineral light into the dark below. [19] Prince Aelric crossed its quaking halls in a reverie, bedraggled in a coat that had once been royal. [37] Below him gears ground the seasons, pistons breathed the air, and clockwork birds clicked out their joyless songs. [55] His ancestors had built this mechanical world to outlast catastrophe, but in saving life they had murdered its beauty. [74] Aelric had vowed to reawaken that beauty, and he believed the key lay sealed in the vaults beneath the throne. [94] There, among brittle ledgers, he found an extinct bird's egg, pale as moonstone and impossibly intact. [110] A faded note coiled around it claimed the creature had sung the world into color before the engines came. [129] Aelric spent his nights hypothesizing about everything: how warmth might wake it, how song might answer song, how a single hatching might unravel centuries of iron sleep. [156] He had no science, only ruined books and a stubborn ache, so he tested theory after theory. [173] He warmed the egg against the palace's geothermal veins; he hummed forgotten lullabies; he bathed it in the stalactites' glow. [193] Nothing answered. [195] Then he understood that an egg alone was not a bird, and that genesis required more than heat. [213] He resolved to assemble a cradle of the old machines, repurposing the clockwork birds' delicate organs into an incubator of song and flame. [236] For weeks he labored, fitting gear to bellows, voice-box to lamp, until the contraption breathed like a living thing. [255] He set the egg within it and waited, the whole palace shuddering on its tethers as if it too held its breath. [277] On the seventh dawn, a crack split the shell. [286] Through the discovery of that fracture Aelric learned the bitter truth of his hope. [300] What emerged was not the radiant songbird of the legends but a trembling, half-formed creature, its feathers fused with copper filaments, its eye a clouded lens. [326] His incubator had shaped it, and so the genesis was flawed, a beauty born wrong from the very machines he despised. [347] Aelric knelt, bedraggled and weeping, holding the imperfect thing. [356] Then it opened its beak and sang. [363] The note was cracked, metallic, unlike any lullaby, yet it was alive, a sound no gear had ever produced. [382] It was bitterly sweet, the song of a world that could never return to what it had been, only become something new. [404] Aelric realized then that he had been wrong to chase a perfect past. [417] Beauty would not come unspoiled into a mechanical world; it would have to be grafted, hybrid, half-wild and half-made. [436] He carried the strange fledgling out onto a balcony where the stalactites blazed and the suspended palace swayed in its endless reverie. [458] Below, the clockwork birds clicked their hollow chorus. [466] He held his flawed creation aloft and let it sing into the cavern. [479] One by one, the mechanical birds faltered, their gears catching, as though startled by a voice they could not imitate. [499] Aelric began hypothesizing about everything again: how this song might spread, how others might be assembled from old metal and new life. [521] The work would be slow, the results imperfect, every genesis flawed in its own way. [536] But the prince no longer wanted perfection. [543] He wanted the trembling, the cracked note, the quaking wonder of something that should not exist and did. [561] He cupped the bird close, its copper heart ticking against his palm, its breath warm and real. [578] Far below, in the gears that had ruled the world, a single new note began to echo, bitterly sweet, and the long reawakening began. [602]</story>
