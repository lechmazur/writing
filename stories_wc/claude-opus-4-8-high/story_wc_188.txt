<story>Mira was a wandering fireworks designer, and she chanted while walking, as she always had. [15] Her boots knew the road to the blooming fortress at dusk, where ivy and night-roses swallowed old stone. [33] She came during the hush of a night with no stars, because that was when the fortress listened best. [52] Inside, the courtyard floor held a note in mosaic, thousands of glazed tiles arranged into a message no one had read in years. [75] Mira knelt and let her senses settle, learning to read the pattern beneath the dust and shadow. [92] She read the meaning slowly, through ambient awareness, until the tiles confessed their secret. [106] The mosaic was a letter from a fire spinner long dead, who had performed here when the fortress still breathed music. [127] Tucked into a hollow tile she found his fire spinner's kerosene rag knot, hard and brown with age. [145] The note said, with serious playfulness, "Whoever finds this, you are too stubborn to quit, so light something." [163] Mira laughed, because she had walked here for exactly that reason: to defy the gravity of hopelessness. [180] Her workshop had burned three months ago, taking her designs and, she feared, her reason to make anything bright again. [200] Since then she had walked from town to town, chanting old launch-counts under her breath to keep her hands from shaking. [221] The mosaic-note felt like a hand reaching across decades, telling her the work was worth continuing. [237] She unrolled her satchel of powders and shells in the courtyard, the night with no stars pressing close and silent. [257] The blooming fortress at dusk had darkened fully now, its roses closing like small fists. [272] She dipped the old kerosene rag knot in fresh fuel, surprised it still held together after so long. [290] Knotting it to a chain, she spun it slowly, a small orange comet circling her wrist. [306] Then she set her shells along the mosaic's edges, aligning each fuse with a tile of the dead spinner's message. [326] She had to filter her fear from her focus, the way a craftsman strains grit from good powder. [344] She touched the spinning flame to the first fuse and stepped back into the dark. [359] One by one the shells climbed and burst, and their light fell back down onto the mosaic. [376] For a few breaths the dead spinner's words glowed in full color, lit from above by Mira's own fire. [395] The starless sky finally had stars, ones she had made, and the fortress seemed to breathe again. [412] Mira understood the message now in her bones: hope was not found, it was lit. [427] She gathered her satchel, still chanting, and pressed the spinner's knot into a hollow tile for the next wanderer. [446] Beside it she set a new tile of her own, a single bright square added to the mosaic-note. [464] It was small, but it changed the message, the way one new design could change a tired sky. [482] Then she walked out through the closing roses, defying the gravity of hopelessness with every counted step. [499] The road ahead was black and uncertain, but her chant filled it, a small rhythm against the silence. [517] She thought of the next town, where children would gather and ask her to make the dark beautiful. [535] She would say yes, as the dead spinner had taught her without ever speaking a word. [551] The kerosene smell still clung to her fingers, serious and sweet, a promise she meant to keep. [568] Behind her, the fortress held its glowing letter a moment longer, then folded back into ordinary night. [585] Mira did not look back, because a fireworks designer always faces the next empty sky. [600] And she walked on, chanting, lighting the way one bright count at a time. [614]</story>
