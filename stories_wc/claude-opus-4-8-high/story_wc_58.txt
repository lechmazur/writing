<story>The last ship sailed at dawn, and only then did Ilse permit herself to enter the shadowed garden. [18] She was a traveler who wrote obituaries for the living, paid in coins by those who wished to read their own grief before they died. [43] Autumn had stripped the orchard to its bones, and the few remaining leaves burned amber in the slanting, half-hidden light. [63] It was a place of lonesome splendor, where marble fountains stood dry and ivy crept across forgotten thrones. [81] Ilse had come for one commission only: the dying queen, who had requested an obituary while she still breathed. [100] But the queen was not in her chamber, and the servants offered only explained absences, each more elaborate than the last. [121] She walks the cliffs, they said; she prays in the chapel; she sails with the fleet that just departed. [140] Ilse understood the truth beneath the lies, for she had spent her life cataloguing the ways people vanish before their bodies do. [162] In the garden's heart she found the queen at last, seated on a loom-bench, weaving by lamplight. [179] Between her fingers ran a single palace carpet weft thread, crimson and frayed, drawn from the great hall's ancestral rug. [199] I am unraveling it, the queen said, because I wish to know the geography of healing before I am gone. [219] Ilse sat beside her, close enough to be intimate, and listened with the patience her trade demanded. [236] Every knot in this carpet, the queen explained, marks a place I loved and lost. [251] By pulling the thread I retrace each absence, and the wound becomes a map I can finally read. [269] Ilse opened her ledger and began to write, but the queen stopped her hand. [283] Do not record my death, she whispered; record my presence, so that I do not disappear from the living world. [303] And so Ilse changed her craft that night, choosing to keep the queen alive by projecting presence onto every page she filled. [325] She wrote in vividly descriptive lines, summoning the smell of the queen's salt-stung hair and the weight of her ringed hands. [346] She described the gardens as they once were, fountains leaping, hedges trimmed, courtiers laughing beneath lanterns. [362] Each sentence wove the queen back into the air, the way the weft thread bound warp to warp. [380] They worked together through the long hours, queen unraveling and traveler reweaving, until dawn paled the eastern wall. [398] When the last knot loosened, the queen smiled, for she had mapped at last where her grief lived and where it might mend. [421] She handed Ilse the freed crimson thread, now coiled like a tiny river upon her palm. [437] Carry this, she said, and wherever you wander, lay it down to mark a place where someone learned to heal. [457] Ilse left the palace before the morning bells, the obituary unwritten, a living portrait taking its place. [474] She traveled the coast roads, and at every town she explained the queen's absence not with death but with stories. [494] Listeners wept and then laughed, feeling the queen seated beside them, summoned by projecting presence through Ilse's vivid words. [513] In each grieving village she unspooled a length of the crimson weft and pressed it into the soil. [531] Slowly, the geography of healing she had sought began to take shape across the whole grieving country. [548] Years later, when word came that the queen had truly died, Ilse felt no need to write the dreaded page. [568] She returned alone to the shadowed garden, now bright with red threads sprouting like autumn vines along the walls. [587] There, in the lonesome splendor of fallen leaves, she sat where the loom had stood and listened. [604] The queen was there, intimate as breath, woven forever into the living air Ilse carried with her. [621]</story>
