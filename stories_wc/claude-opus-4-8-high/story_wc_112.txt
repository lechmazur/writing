<story>Vael had always been an obliging sky mechanic, the kind who fixed broken thrusters before anyone thought to ask. [19] Now she worked alone aboard Kessrin, a xenobiology research station orbiting a frozen ringed world. [34] The station studied the gliders, vast translucent creatures that drifted through the upper clouds on membranous wings. [51] Every researcher had departed months ago, leaving Vael with the silence and a single stubborn theory. [67] She wanted to prove that time moves sideways. [75] Her colleagues had laughed, but the gliders had taught her otherwise. [86] When the creatures folded their enormous wings to rest, the station's instruments recorded strange ripples in the local fields. [105] Within the hush of beating wings at rest, the clocks of Kessrin disagreed with one another. [121] Vael believed the gliders perceived moments laid out side by side, not strung in a line. [137] To capture this, she had built a magnetic nexus at twilight, when the planet's shadow softened the radiation. [155] The nexus gathered the gliders' field-ripples and projected them through holographic principles into a chamber she could walk inside. [174] Inside, time would not flow forward; it would spread, and she would step from one instant to its neighbor. [193] On the chosen evening, Vael descended to the nexus chamber and rested her hand on the activation lever. [211] The metal was mechanically warm, heated by the humming coils, an icy warmth that matched the station's perpetual chill. [230] She pulled the lever down. [235] The chamber bloomed with overlapping images, each a slice of the same twilight seen from a different angle of time. [255] To her left she saw herself arriving years ago; to her right she saw a quieter, older version of herself. [275] These were subdued awakenings, moments of clarity that arrived softly, without the violence of revelation. [290] Vael understood at last what the gliders felt when they folded their wings. [303] They did not sleep; they rested in every moment of their lives at once. [317] She stepped sideways, and the years parted like curtains. [326] In one panel her old crew laughed at her theory; in another they nodded, convinced. [341] Both were true, because every choice still existed somewhere beside her. [352] The obliging mechanic in her wanted to fix something, to mend the loneliness she had carried since the others left. [372] So she reached sideways into the moment before their departure. [382] There she found herself shaking, certain her theory was a fool's dream. [394] Gently, the present Vael spoke across the gap, telling that frightened self to keep building the nexus. [411] It was the kindest repair she had ever made. [420] Outside the chamber, a glider drifted close to the station's dome and folded its wings against the glass. [438] In that hush, Vael felt the creature acknowledge her, one sideways traveler greeting another. [452] She had proven her theory, not with equations, but with the warmth of standing inside her own scattered life. [471] When she finally raised the lever and let the holograms fade, the chamber returned to ordinary, linear quiet. [489] Yet Vael no longer felt alone. [495] Every version of her remained, resting like wings, awake in every direction at once. [509] She recorded her findings and transmitted them toward the distant fleet, knowing the message would arrive in its own sideways time. [530] The magnetic nexus dimmed at twilight's end, its coils cooling from their humming glow. [544] She pressed her palm once more to the lever, feeling its fading heat like a goodbye. [560] The gliders outside stirred, then settled again into their patient, folded rest. [572] Vael smiled, content to live the way they did, sideways and unhurried, gathering every quiet awakening as it came. [591] The station hummed around her, an icy warmth pressing back the cold of the void beyond the glass. [609] In the hush of beating wings at rest, the obliging sky mechanic had finally repaired herself. [625]</story>
