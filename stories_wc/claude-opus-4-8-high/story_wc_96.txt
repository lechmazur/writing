<story>They called him the penny poet, a vagrant who paid for bread with verses scrawled on torn receipts. [18] I met him sorting books at the prison library, where the law had finally caught his wandering feet. [36] We were both inmates assigned to the same shelves, and our friendship sparked over a debate about whether anarchy could be beautiful. [58] He insisted it could; his whole life, he said, was an attempt to forge beauty from anarchy. [75] The strangest thing about him was that he molted personalities monthly, shedding a self the way a snake sheds skin. [95] In January he was a gentle scholar; by February a snarling cynic; by March a saint who fed his rations to the rats. [118] The guards thought him mad, but I understood it as his resistance, each new mask a refusal to be reduced to a number. [141] He kept everything in an old coffee tin, dented and labeled with a faded brand. [156] One night he showed me its secret false bottom, where he hid the seeds. [170] Not metaphor, but real seeds, marigold and morning glory, smuggled in past every search. [184] Above the cellblock stood a rusted library, an abandoned wing the prison had left to the weather and the stars. [204] Its roof had collapsed years ago, so the shelves stood open to the night sky. [219] We were sent there to rebuild the collection, to sort the swollen, rain-warped books that no one else wanted. [238] The penny poet saw something else in that ruin: soil, sun, and freedom from supervision. [253] Between cracked spines and broken shelving, he began to plant. [263] Each evening, as the moon cleared the skyline, we worked by its pale light, pressing seeds into the gaps where mortar had crumbled. [286] He recited poems to the dirt as if the words were fertilizer. [298] This was his real sentence, he told me, not the years but the silence the system wanted. [315] By planting, he answered that silence with a blooming resistance no warden could confiscate. [329] April came, and with it his molt. [336] The gentle gardener I knew gave way to a brittle, frightened man who wanted to destroy the seedlings before the guards found them. [359] I sat with him through that fracture, reading his own poems back to him until he remembered who he had been. [380] That was the bargain of our friendship: I held the thread of his many selves, and he taught me to see beauty in collapse. [404] By May the marigolds opened, fierce orange suns blooming from the broken architecture of punishment. [419] The morning glories climbed the rusted shelves, threading vines through titles no one would ever read again. [436] It was a luminous fracture, the place split open by light, beauty leaking from every wound in the concrete. [455] Other prisoners came to see, and soon they too smuggled seeds, asking the coffee tin for its quiet inheritance. [474] The poet, in his June self, declared the rusted library a temple and crowned it with paper sonnets. [492] When his release finally came, he left the tin to me, false bottom still cradling a final handful of seeds. [512] Keep them blooming, he said, and keep rebuilding what they tear down. [524] I stayed three more winters, tending the garden that grew from the corpse of the library. [540] Each season I sorted the surviving books, dried them in the sun, and shelved them among the flowers. [558] When my own day of release arrived, I walked out as the moon cleared the skyline once more, the tin tucked under my arm. [582] I had no verses worth a penny, only the lesson he had planted in me. [597] Wherever the world cracks, you forge beauty from anarchy, and you let the resistance bloom. [612]</story>
