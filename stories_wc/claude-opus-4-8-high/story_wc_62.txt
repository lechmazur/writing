<story>Mara was a thoughtful gravestone carver who had learned to listen to stone the way others listened to rivers. [19] Each evening she worked inside an ancient greenhouse at dusk, where forgotten roses pressed against fogged glass and the light turned the color of old honey. [45] She had inherited the place from her grandmother, along with a woven ribbon roll bound tight with the names of everyone the family had buried. [70] The roll was meant to be unwound only once a year, across the butterfly migration, when monarchs streamed south like sparks blown off a slow fire. [96] Her grandmother had died in spring, and Mara now carried a question heavier than any granite slab: she wanted to discover the shape of collective grief. [122] On the first night of the migration, as a butterfly landed on her chisel, the old woman appeared. [140] She was translucent as breath on cold glass, yet her voice arrived with a thundering calm that steadied Mara's hands. [160] This was the spectral mentorship Mara had half-prayed for and half-feared: her grandmother, returned to teach. [176] "You carve names," the ghost said, "but you have not learned to read them." [190] So they began, and each dusk the ghost asked Mara to retrace the letters of a single buried name with her fingertips until she felt the life behind it. [219] Mara grew fiercely meditative, sitting for hours among the ferns while monarchs clustered on the rafters like a living roof. [239] To keep her steady, the ghost revived a family custom of trading favorite poems at dinner. [255] Each evening Mara set two plates, recited a verse she loved, and waited as her grandmother answered with lines older than the greenhouse glass. [279] The poems were not decoration; they were keys. [287] One poem unlocked the memory of a drowned uncle, another the memory of a stillborn cousin whose name had never been spoken aloud. [310] With each name, Mara unwound a little more of the woven ribbon roll, and each strand carried the scent of a different season of mourning. [335] "Grief is not a hole," the ghost told her one violet evening. [347] "It is a shape, and you are carving it whether you know it or not." [362] Mara understood then that the migration would end, and so would her teacher. [375] She worked faster, retracing not just letters now but whole lives, until the ribbon was nearly spent. [392] On the final night, when the last monarchs gathered to leave, Mara unwound the final strand and read the only name left: her grandmother's. [416] She set two plates one more time and offered the poem her grandmother had loved best. [432] The ghost answered, softer now, and Mara finally saw the shape she had been seeking. [447] Collective grief was not a wall of separate stones but a single woven thing, ribbon and root and rafter, each loss tied to the next. [472] It was the shape of the migration itself: countless fragile wings moving as one body across an impossible distance. [491] "Now you can carve me," her grandmother said, and began to dissolve into the dusk-light. [506] Mara did not weep; she lifted her chisel with that same thundering calm her grandmother had given her. [524] Through the night she carved, fiercely meditative, retracing each letter of the name she knew best. [540] By dawn the stone was finished, and the last butterfly lifted from its surface and followed the others south. [559] Mara rolled the spent ribbon back into its woven coil, lighter now, ready to be filled again. [576] She would keep the custom alive, trading poems at a table set for one, until she too became a voice in someone else's greenhouse. [600] Grief, she finally knew, was never meant to be carried alone. [611] It was meant to be woven, name to name, wing to wing, across every dusk that would ever come. [630]</story>
