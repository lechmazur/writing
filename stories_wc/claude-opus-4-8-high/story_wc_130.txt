<story>Mireille was a secretive conjurer who lived where no one thought to look, in a cavern orchard nourished by dripping stalactites. [21] She had come underground to understand the mathematics of emotion, certain that grief obeyed equations she could one day solve. [41] Above her, water beaded along the limestone teeth and fell in slow, counted rhythms, feeding the trees that bore pale fruit. [62] Among her instruments she kept a taxidermist's glass eye pair, two amber spheres she had inherited but never explained. [81] They had belonged to her brother, who vanished one winter without a body, a letter, or a grave. [99] His was an ambiguous loss, the kind that refuses to round itself into a clean number. [115] So she built a method to chase him, reading the chipped patterns on a mosaic stepping stone she had laid at the orchard's heart. [139] Each crack, she believed, recorded a feeling the stone had absorbed from her bare feet. [154] Her work was gently inventive rather than violent; she coaxed answers, never demanding them. [168] She would wait until the orchard grew silent beneath the moon that her mirrors funneled down through a fissure. [187] Only then, when the dripping slowed and the leaves stopped whispering, did the equations feel honest. [203] On this night she knelt at the stone, set the glass eyes upon two chipped tiles, and began. [221] The amber lenses caught the funneled moonlight and threw it into the fractures, where it branched like a proof. [240] She traced the lines with a fingertip, letting each angle dovetail with the next until a single shape emerged. [259] It was not a face, nor a number, but the structure between them: how much a person weighs once they are only remembered. [282] For years she had wanted a verdict, alive or dead, so the grief could resolve into one column or another. [302] The stone offered something stranger, and kinder, instead. [310] The chipped patterns showed her that absence had a slope, that it could carry value without ever closing. [328] Her brother was a variable, not a constant; he ranged across every outcome she would never confirm. [345] That meant, by the same arithmetic, he was also still possible. [356] She had treated emotion as a thing to be solved and finished, like a sum erased once balanced. [374] The mosaic insisted otherwise, presenting feeling as an open shape that kept generating new terms. [389] Mireille sat back, and for the first time the secret she had hoarded loosened in her chest. [406] She had hidden underground partly because hope embarrassed her, felt unscientific, almost foolish. [419] Yet here was hope buried in the proof itself, optimism encoded in the very mathematics she trusted. [436] A drop fell from a stalactite and struck a young tree, and a single fruit dropped into her lap. [455] She lifted the glass eyes from the tiles and held them to the moonlight, as if her brother might be watching through them. [478] They reflected the orchard back at her, doubled and gold, two lenses fitted to a grief that no longer needed an end. [500] She understood now that some losses are not problems to close but functions to inhabit. [515] Tomorrow she would carry the answer up the tunnel and tell the world what the orchard had taught her. [534] Tonight, though, she stayed in the silence, letting the equations breathe and the fruit ripen in the dark. [552] She set the stepping stone's chipped tiles back into place, each fragment dovetailing snug, a small inventive repair. [570] Then she ate the fallen fruit slowly, tasting mineral water and something faintly like forgiveness. [585] The orchard stayed quiet, the stalactites kept their patient count, and Mireille, the secretive conjurer, finally let herself hope out loud. [606]</story>
