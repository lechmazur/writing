<story>After the last echo of laughter fades from the carnival above, Mira descends into the subterranean crystal cave alone. [19] She is a charcoal-fingered portrait sketch artist, and her smudged hands leave dark fingerprints on every glittering wall she touches. [39] Quartz columns refract her lantern into trembling auroras, ribbons of green and violet that drift like grief made visible. [58] Years ago her twin sister Della painted down here, before the sickness silenced her brush forever. [74] Mira has come to rectify a betrayal she can never quite forgive herself for committing. [89] When Della lay dying, she begged Mira to keep painting, to uncork the suppressed creativity their stern father had bottled inside them both. [112] Instead Mira fled into safe commissions, drawing strangers' faces for coins, abandoning every wild color her sister loved. [130] Now, blessed and burdened by being mnemonically gifted, she remembers the exact route Della carved through these tunnels. [148] Every echo, every footfall pattern, every crystal facet stays etched in her flawless memory. [162] At the deepest chamber she finds Della's last work, a painting propped against amethyst, depicting the two of them as laughing children. [184] Mira's throat tightens, for the joy in those painted faces is the very laughter the world has since taken from her. [205] Tucked into the frame is a brass combination lock, broken, its dial frozen mid-number. [219] She recognizes it instantly: the lock from the toolbox where their father once imprisoned their paints. [235] Della must have stolen it, smashed it open, and carried the freed colors down here as a quiet rebellion. [254] Mira lifts the canvas and discovers the method her sister left for her: a painting with something hidden behind it. [274] Behind the joyful portrait, taped to the wood, rests a folded letter and a fan of unused sable brushes. [293] The letter reads: "Sister, our calling is inescapable; you may bury it, but it will surface like these crystals through stone." [314] "When you read this, finish what I could not." [323] Mira understands now that running from art only deferred the ache, never cured it. [337] The auroral light shimmers across the cavern, sorrowful and luminous, painting the walls in shades she has refused to mix for years. [359] She kneels, opens a tin of Della's preserved pigments, and dips a brush for the first time in a decade. [379] Her charcoal-stained fingers tremble, then steady, as if the cave itself were guiding her hand. [394] She begins to rectify the unfinished portrait, layering color over Della's faint pencil ghosts. [408] Each stroke feels like answering a voice she had muffled, a calling too stubborn to die. [424] Hours blur; the lantern gutters; still she works, weeping and smiling at once. [437] She paints the laughter back into Della's mouth, the mischief into her eyes, the warmth they once shared. [455] When at last she sets down the brush, the finished faces glow under the crystals like two suns reunited. [474] The broken lock, she realizes, was never meant to be repaired; its ruin was the gift. [490] Some things must stay broken so that what they confined can finally pour out. [504] Mira tucks the brushes into her coat and pockets the shattered lock as a keepsake of release. [521] She climbs toward the surface, where the carnival lies dark and the last echo of laughter has long since died. [541] Yet within her, a new sound rises, the quiet hum of a calling she will no longer escape. [559] Tomorrow she will buy real paints and rectify the empty years, one canvas after another. [574] The auroral sorrow that filled the crystal cave now travels upward inside her, transmuted into resolve. [590] She steps into the cold night, charcoal fingers itching for color, finally free.
