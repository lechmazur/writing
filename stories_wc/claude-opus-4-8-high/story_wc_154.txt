<story>Mosi had translated surrender and storm warnings across three Pacific conflict zones, but the hospital atrium asked a quieter language of him. [22] He arrived in the mist-clad atrium at twilight, where fog drifted between potted ferns and the glass ceiling blurred into pewter. [43] His niece, Lani, slept in the recovery wing, and he had promised to be there after the anesthetic kicked in. [63] In his pocket sat a brain coral fragment, smooth as a folded brain, the last thing he had salvaged from a reef that shelling had erased. [89] He had carried it for years, a souvenir of guilt, wanting only to graft new meaning onto old wounds. [108] When the nurse waved him in, Lani was groggy, shrewdly innocent in the way children are when they sense more than adults admit. [131] Her broken arm wore a fresh white cast, and across it, in marker already smudged by sweat, ran rows of tiny symbols. [153] "I wrote you a code," she whispered, "so you'd have something to translate that wasn't sad." [169] His throat tightened; she had built him a puzzle, a gift disguised as mischief. [183] So he sat, set the coral fragment on the bedside tray, and began the work he knew best, decrypting the smudged notes on the child's cast. [209] The cipher was simple, a substitution she had learned from a comic, yet the smudges forced him to guess at half-erased letters. [231] Letter by letter, the message surfaced: UNCLE, WE ARE BOTH SCARED OF THE SAME DARK. [246] He read it twice, and something in his chest began to unknot. [258] For years he had believed his fear was a private wreckage, a language no one shared. [274] But here was proof of their shared anxieties, named plainly by a six-year-old half-asleep on morphine. [290] "You decoded it," Lani murmured, smiling at the ceiling. [299] "I did," he said, "and you're right." [306] He thought of the reef, the way bombs had unstitched the water, and how he had fled translation jobs hoping distance would mute the noise. [331] It never had; the dark followed, identical in every harbor. [341] Yet a child had handed him the trick he had forgotten: fear spoken aloud becomes shared, and shared fear weighs less. [362] He lifted the brain coral fragment and pressed it gently into her good hand. [376] "This came from a place that got hurt," he told her, "but it kept its shape, see, all those little rooms." [397] She turned it in the dim light, tracing the maze of channels with a fingertip. [412] "It looks like thinking," she decided. [418] "Then we'll keep it," he said, "and every time we're scared, we'll remember it survived." [433] The mist outside thickened, and the atrium lamps came up like soft moons. [446] Lani drifted back toward sleep, the coral cupped against her chest, the cast rising and falling with her breath. [465] Mosi stayed, copying her cipher into his notebook, choosing to keep this translation forever. [479] The ache in him was real, but it was lotus-hearted now, rooted in muck yet opening upward. [496] He had spent his life carrying other people's words across the wreckage of borders, never trusting that his own could be received. [518] Tonight a smudged sentence had crossed the smallest border of all, the gap between two frightened people, and arrived intact. [538] Outside the dark would still come, as it always did across every Pacific shore he had known. [555] But he no longer faced it as a solitary translator of disasters. [567] He had a partner now, brave and clear-eyed, who had taught him to graft meaning onto old wounds by simply naming them together. [590] In the mist, he whispered her code back to her like a lullaby, and the atrium held them both. [609]</story>
