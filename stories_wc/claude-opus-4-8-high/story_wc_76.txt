<story>Eira built clocks that ran backward, every gear turning counterclockwise against the world's insistence. [14] She was a clockmaker who believed time owed her an exit from the fate stamped into her bones. [32] The mysterious lighthouse on its rocky island had drawn her here under the slow exhale of low tide. [50] Locals said the tower hid a hunger stone, a slab carved by famine-haunted ancestors who marked their dying years upon it. [71] When you see me, weep, the oldest inscription warned, and Eira read it grinning with bleak exhilaration. [88] She had not come to mourn the drought the stone foretold, but to use it. [103] Beside the hunger stone lay an ancient yoyo, its twin discs etched with symbols no scholar had unwound. [121] Eira, neatly inventive, suspected the toy was no toy but a lock. [133] She would open whatever it guarded through reverse-engineering the puzzle of that spinning relic. [147] For three nights she measured its string, timed its sleep, and charted the angles of its return. [164] Each throw, she realized, mirrored a counterclockwise rotation, the same defiant motion her own clocks practiced. [180] The yoyo wanted time spun against its grain. [188] Under the slow exhale of low tide, when the sea drew back from the rocks like a held breath, she began. [209] She looped the string in reverse, threw, and let the discs descend while the lighthouse lamp hung dark above her. [229] At the bottom of its fall the yoyo paused, trembling, and the etched symbols aligned with grooves on the hunger stone. [250] A seam of latent radiance woke inside the slab, light that had slept in the rock since the first starving winter. [271] Eira understood, then, what the lighthouse truly was. [279] Not a warning to ships, but a mechanism to initiate a single, impossible choice. [293] The radiance offered to rewrite one destiny, but the hunger stone demanded the years it had counted be paid in full. [314] To step beyond fate's design, she would have to surrender the long life already written for her. [331] Her future, she saw in the glow, was decades of safe, hollow clockwork in a dim shop, mending other people's time. [352] Predictable, endless, and utterly chosen for her by ancestors who feared the sea. [365] She thought of every backward gear she had ever filed, each a small rebellion against inevitability. [381] Grinning still, that terrible joy rising, Eira pressed her palm into the radiant seam. [395] The hunger stone drank her ordained decades in one cold pull, and the inscription's threatened tears finally made sense. [414] What remained was brief, unwritten, and wholly her own. [423] The lighthouse lamp flared, fed at last by the radiance she had released, sweeping the black water in a wide, defiant arc. [445] She had no map for the days now left to her, no preordained groove to follow. [461] The tide began its slow return, climbing the rocks it had abandoned. [473] Eira wound the ancient yoyo one final time and let it spin counterclockwise into the rising surf. [490] She felt the bleak exhilaration of a clock with no hands, free and falling toward whatever she would invent next. [510] Behind her, the hunger stone went dark, its grim ledger settled and its purpose spent. [525] She descended the spiral stairs, counting the steps backward out of habit, twelve, eleven, ten. [540] Each number felt lighter than the last, unburdened by the long, gray years she had just unspent. [557] The island's gulls cried as if startled to find someone unwritten walking among them. [571] She had stepped beyond fate's design, and the cost glittered behind her like a drowned star. [587] Somewhere ahead waited a future no ancestor had carved, no clock had measured, no stone had counted. [604] Eira walked into it, gladly, while the lighthouse blazed its borrowed radiance across the dark and rising sea. [622]</story>
