<story>Cassiel had been alive for eleven thousand years, and boredom had become his only weather. [15] He felt the solar storm before it arrived, a hushed thunder rolling through the magnetic fields that braided the sky. [35] Such storms were the only events that still interested him, because only during a solar storm did the walls of the prismatic escondite grow thin. [60] The escondite was his hiding place, a refracting sanctuary he had built between dimensions, where every surface split light into trembling color. [82] Cassiel was dimensionally aware, able to feel the geometry of space the way a sailor feels a deck shift underfoot. [102] Lately, that deck had begun to rebel. [109] The rooms refused their old order; corridors swapped places, and distances lengthened when he tried to cross them. [127] It was a spatial mutiny, the architecture itself conspiring against its maker. [139] At the heart of the rebellion lay a cursed amulet fragment, a shard he had stolen from a dying god a thousand years before. [163] The fragment hummed with a will of its own, and it had been whispering to the walls. [180] Cassiel followed its pull during the solar storm, when its voice was loudest. [193] He found the shard suspended in a column of fractured light, turning slowly against the magnetic fields the storm had stirred. [214] "You are restless too," he murmured. [220] The amulet answered not in words but in intrigue, planting a scheme directly into his thoughts. [236] It showed him that the mutiny was not malice but invitation. [247] The walls were not collapsing inward; they were tearing open toward a place beyond his endless rooms. [264] For eleven thousand years Cassiel had watched mortals leave, had spoken a hundred thousand goodbyes at deathbeds and harbors. [283] He alone never followed, never learned what waited on the far side of farewell. [297] That hunger, to discover what happens after goodbye, was the one curiosity his immortality could never satisfy. [314] Now the cursed fragment offered a door. [321] The storm peaked outside, and the hushed thunder swelled into a pressure he felt behind his eyes. [338] The escondite shuddered, its prismatic facets aligning like soldiers choosing a new commander. [351] Cassiel understood the bargain the amulet proposed. [358] If he surrendered to the spatial mutiny, the rooms would fold him through the magnetic fields and out beyond the only world he had ever known. [384] He would cross his own goodbye. [390] But the curse demanded its price: whatever passed through could never return, not even one as deathless as he. [409] For a being who had measured time in empires, the choice should have terrified him. [424] Instead, boredom lifted from him like fog burning off a valley. [435] He reached for the fragment, and the magnetic fields sang against his skin. [448] "I have said farewell to everyone," he whispered, "and never once to myself." [461] The dimensionally aware part of him mapped the opening as it bloomed, a wound of color where the storm and the curse agreed. [484] He gripped the cursed amulet fragment until it cut his palm, and dark immortal blood spiraled along the magnetic lines. [504] The mutiny completed itself; the escondite turned inside out, every prism pointing toward the breach. [519] Hushed thunder became a single held note. [526] Cassiel stepped forward, and for the first time in eleven thousand years he felt the deck of space tilt willingly beneath him, carrying rather than resisting. [552] The intrigue that had unsettled his home was, he realized, simple mercy wearing the mask of rebellion. [569] His sanctuary had grown weary of imprisoning a god who refused to live. [582] As the light closed around him, he finally understood the answer to his oldest question. [597] What happens after goodbye is simply the next room, waiting, patient, and entirely new. [611] And the bored immortal, smiling at last, walked through to find out for himself. [625]</story>
