<story>Mara climbed the infinite staircase the way she did everything: grimly, hopefully, certain that the next landing would change nothing and everything at once. [24] She was a grim optimist, the only species of climber the staircase tolerated for long. [39] The steps rose forever above and plunged forever below, a boundless confinement that mocked every traveler with the illusion of progress. [60] In her pocket she carried a pair of dice, one chipped along its corner, the only objects she had brought from the life she could no longer quite picture. [89] She had rolled them once, years ago, deciding whether to take a chance that she ultimately refused. [106] Now she climbed to capture the texture of that missed opportunity, to feel again the grain of the choice she had let slip. [129] The staircase, she had decided, was the universe's way of letting her relive it forever. [144] On the thousandth landing she met the Architect, a thin man scribbling equations onto the risers themselves. [161] He claimed the staircase was a perpetual motion machine, a structure that fed on its own endlessness and would never wind down. [183] "Nothing perpetual is truly stable," he said, "and I intend to prove it through mathematical proofs." [199] He showed her how the energy of the climb, summed across infinite steps, diverged into contradiction. [215] A perpetual machine, he argued, could not exist without somewhere a rupture, a single flawed point where the equation broke. [235] Find the flaw, and the whole infinite cage would come apart. [246] Mara listened, balancedly daring, weighing the risk against a freedom she was no longer sure she wanted. [263] For the staircase, however cruel, had given her the one thing the living world denied: a chance to dwell inside her regret without it ending. [288] "Where is the flaw?" she asked, surprising herself. [296] The Architect pointed at her pocket. [302] "Every perpetual system needs a seed of imperfection to keep moving," he said. [315] "Your chipped die is the asymmetry the staircase borrowed to run forever." [327] She drew the dice out and understood. [334] The chip was the missed opportunity made solid, the bias that had tipped her old decision toward refusal. [352] To rupture the staircase she had only to roll fairly, to let the chipped die fall and finally accept its weighted truth. [374] She hesitated on the edge of forever. [381] If she rolled, the perpetual motion would stop, the proofs would close, and her endless rehearsal of the missed chance would end. [403] She would lose the texture she had climbed so far to keep. [415] But the Architect's equations were honest, and a grim optimist trusts honesty even when it hurts. [431] Holding the chipped die, she felt the grain of her regret at last, every facet of the chance she had refused. [452] She had wanted to capture its texture, and now, in this instant, she finally did. [467] Holding regret was not the same as living inside it forever. [478] She rolled. [480] The chipped die clattered down the steps, and where it struck, the proofs resolved to a single perfect equality. [499] Balance arrived, and balance, for a perpetual machine, is death. [509] The rupture ran upward and downward at once, the infinite staircase folding into the silence between two numbers. [527] The boundless confinement broke, and Mara fell, not down, but out. [538] She landed in the ordinary world, on the ordinary morning she had abandoned, the surviving die still warm in her hand. [559] The bias was gone, and every choice ahead of her was, at last, fairly weighted. [574] She smiled grimly, hopefully, and chose to take the chance. [584] The opportunity she had missed was no longer missed, only deferred until now. [597] She did not need the staircase to relive the moment; she needed only to live it. [613] And so, balancedly daring as ever, she stepped forward into a morning that finally moved. [628]</story>
