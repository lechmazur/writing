<story>Margit, the lion tamer's widow, climbed the iron stairs to her rain-beaded skylight loft and set the kettle on the burner. [21] She had given herself a small, hauntingly practical ritual: while the kettle boils, she would carve. [37] On the workbench waited an ash wood carving blank, pale and unmarked, smelling faintly of the woods where Tomas had once trained the cats. [61] Tomas was here and gone in the space of one afternoon, taken not by a lion but by a quiet, ordinary heart. [83] The circus had folded its tents after that, and the animals went to other hands, leaving Margit the silence and the tools. [105] She believed, with a conviction that surprised her, that grief could be contained the way a tamer contains a beast: not by killing it, but by learning its pacing. [134] So each evening she set herself to harvest lessons from loss, one carving, one boiling at a time. [152] The knife bit into the ash, and a curl of wood fell like a shaving of time itself. [170] Below the skylight, rain ticked against the glass, each bead catching the rust-colored streetlight until the loft glowed with a tired, rust-hued peace. [193] Margit worked the blank into the shape she always began and never finished: a small lion, mid-stride. [210] Tonight, though, she understood the lesson hiding in the unfinished thing. [221] A memory, she realized, was not a photograph but a method of holding presence through crystallized time. [238] When she pressed the blade just so, she could feel the exact afternoon Tomas taught her to stand still before a charging cat. [261] "Fear is here and gone," he had said, snapping his fingers, "if you let it move through you." [279] The kettle began its low hum, and Margit carved faster, racing the rising whistle. [293] She did not want to finish the lion; she wanted only to keep the motion of him alive in her hands. [314] For months she had treated the carvings as cages, trying to trap her husband inside the grain. [331] But tamers do not own their lions; they share an honest arrangement, present and trusting until the gate opens again. [351] So she let the unfinished lion stay unfinished, a creature forever mid-leap, neither captured nor released. [367] That, she decided, was how to contain love without strangling it. [378] The whistle climbed, and steam fogged the cold underside of the skylight, blurring the rain into soft amber smears. [397] Margit set the blade down exactly when the kettle reached its full cry, as if the two of them had agreed on the hour. [421] She poured the water over dark leaves and let the steam touch her face like a warm, departing hand. [440] Each night's small carving had become a hauntingly practical archive, a shelf of crystallized time she could lift down whenever the loneliness grew loud. [464] She lined the new lion beside the others, a small pride of unfinished animals, all of them forever arriving. [483] The lessons had begun to take, slowly, the way scar tissue grows stronger than the skin it replaces. [501] Tomas was here and gone, yes, but so was every breath, every wisp of steam, every rain bead sliding down the glass. [523] The trick was not to mourn the leaving but to honor the arriving. [536] Margit cupped the mug, feeling its heat seep into fingers stiff from carving and grief alike. [552] Outside, the rain thinned, and the rust-hued peace of the loft settled over her like a familiar coat. [570] Tomorrow she would take a fresh ash wood carving blank and begin another lion she would never complete. [588] That was enough; that was the whole of it. [597] To harvest lessons from loss, she had learned, you did not need to hold the lion forever; you only needed to remember the shape of its leap. [624]</story>
