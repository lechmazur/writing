<story>Mira returned to the island her grandmother had fled, carrying nothing but a trowel and a warmly curious heart. [19] She was a diaspora returnee, the first of her scattered family to come back across three generations of interrupted journeys. [39] Each ancestor had set out to return, and each had been turned aside by war, by famine, by the slow forgetting that drowns a faith. [64] Mira had come to restore that lost faith, though she could not yet name what it required of her. [83] The old shrine lay beneath the hill, a catacomb orchard where tree roots snake between bones. [99] Generations of her people had been buried there, and over them grew fig and almond, fed by what the dead gave back. [121] Mira moved through purposeful wandering, letting the slant of light and the pull of memory choose her path among the graves. [142] She did not dig at random; she followed the orchard the way a pilgrim follows a half-remembered prayer. [160] Her grandmother had spoken of one tomb in particular, where the family's first priest was said to rest. [178] So Mira began excavating a tomb, and across the slow days of that work her story unfolded. [195] On the first day she cleared roots that gripped a skull like patient fingers. [209] She worked gently, asking the bones permission, half-believing they might answer. [220] A cautious enchantment settled over her, as though the orchard were deciding whether to trust the one who had finally come home. [242] On the third day her trowel struck stone. [249] She uncovered a sundial base, its bronze gnomon long gone, its face worn smooth by darkness. [265] It puzzled her, for what use was a sundial buried where no sun could ever reach? [281] She brushed the engraving and read the words her grandmother used to murmur: the faithful keep time even in the dark. [302] Something loosened in Mira's chest, a knot tied by all the journeys her family had never finished. [319] She understood then that the sundial had once stood above ground, marking the hours of a living temple. [337] When the temple fell, the people had carried its base down into the catacomb, refusing to let their faith stop measuring meaning. [359] Their journey had been interrupted, yet they had buried hope where it could wait for a returning hand. [377] Mira decided she would not merely excavate; she would rebuild. [387] Over the following weeks she lifted the sundial base from the tomb and carried it up through the orchard into open air. [409] She set it on the hilltop where light could find it again, and forged a new gnomon from the trowel that had brought her here. [434] Other returnees came, drawn by rumor, each carrying the residue of an unfinished homeward road. [449] Together they cleared the shrine, mending walls with the same warmly curious patience Mira had carried in her hands. [468] The orchard kept its bones, but now the roots reached toward people instead of only the dead. [485] On the first morning the new gnomon threw a shadow, Mira watched it cross the worn face and mark an hour for the first time in centuries. [512] The cautious enchantment that had guarded the place gave way to something steadier, a quiet, shared belief. [529] She had not found her grandmother's lost faith waiting whole in the ground. [542] She had restored it the only way faith is ever restored: by completing the journey others were forced to abandon. [562] Standing among the wandering roots and the patient bones, Mira felt the hours begin again. [577] The light moved, the shadow followed, and for once nothing interrupted it. [589] She had come home, and the orchard, at last, kept time with her. [602]</story>
