<story>Doctor Iven Calloway had aged backwards for eleven years, and tonight his hands looked almost like a boy's. [18] He sat cross-legged on the floating rock meditation platform that hovered above the magnetic grotto, suspended there by forces he had spent his shrinking life studying. [44] Below, the grotto glittered under starlight, its black walls drawing the iron in his blood until he felt rooted to the air itself. [67] A single student knelt across from him, a girl named Wren who had climbed for three days to learn the thing he refused to name. [92] A sycamore hush lay over the platform, the kind of quiet that seemed to hold its own breath. [110] "Teach me to vanish," Wren said. [116] Calloway smiled, for that was exactly backwards, and backwards was his entire science. [129] "I do not teach vanishing to disappear," he answered. [138] "I teach vanishing to appear." [143] She frowned, not understanding, and he did not rush to explain. [154] He had learned long ago to withhold the answer until the student was ready to receive it without breaking. [173] A truth given too early, he believed, was a truth wasted, so he weaved it carefully, thread by patient thread. [193] From a fold of his robe he drew a smooth gray stone and set it in her palm. [211] "There is a still pond at the platform's center, fed by mist from the grotto," he said. [228] "Throw the stone, then watch." [233] Wren cast the stone, and the rings spread outward across the dark water in perfect, widening circles. [250] "Read the patterns," Calloway murmured, and as she stared, the trance took her. [263] Through the trance the rings became years, each circle a season of his strange unwinding life, and she understood that the method of his teaching lived through the patterns in the rings of a pond after a stone is thrown. [303] Each ring weakened as it traveled, growing fainter, vanishing into the still edge. [316] Yet the moment one ring dissolved, the next was already born from the center. [330] Nothing truly left; the disturbance only changed shape, surrendering its old form to make a newer one possible. [348] Wren watched her own face waver on the water, watched it grow old and then young, and felt no fear. [368] When she surfaced from the vision, the sky had wheeled, and the starlight struck the grotto at a sharper angle. [388] "I felt myself disappear," she whispered, "but I was still there." [399] "That is the whole of it," Calloway said, and now he gave the delayed revelation he had withheld since her arrival. [420] "My body runs backwards because, years ago, I learned to do what the rings do." [435] "I let one self vanish so that another could appear, again and again, until time itself reversed in me." [454] "To vanish is not to be erased," he continued, his young voice steady. [467] "It is to release the form you cling to, so a truer one can rise." [482] Wren looked at the pond, still rippling faintly, and understood why he had refused to speak the lesson aloud at first. [503] Words alone would have been a stone tossed at empty ground, making no rings at all. [519] She had needed to feel the vanishing before any meaning could appear. [531] "You are younger than when I climbed here," she said, noticing the smoothness of his cheek. [547] "Soon I will be a child, then less, then a stone in someone else's palm," he admitted without sorrow. [566] "That is why you came; someone must throw the next stone after I am gone." [581] Wren took the cooling stone again, and in the sycamore hush she let it fall. [596] New rings opened beneath the starlit grotto, and somewhere within them a teacher faded so a student could finally appear. [616]</story>
