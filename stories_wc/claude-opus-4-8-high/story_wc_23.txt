<story>They called me the patchwork navigator because my coat was stitched from the maps of every place that had emptied. [20] I lived at the starlit signal station, a tower of dead antennas leaning over the salt flats. [37] The cities below had begun their reverse migration, families flowing back from the coasts they had fled, retracing the roads of their grandparents. [60] Someone had to chart the way home, and the duty fell to me after the hydraulics failed. [77] When the great pumps that drove the station's instruments collapsed, the maps went still, and only the older arts remained. [97] On the dead console I kept one relic: a patina bronze censer foot, green as drowned copper, salvaged from a temple no one returned to. [122] Its strange power fluctuates with the moon phases, glowing faint at the new moon and burning warm beneath the full. [142] By its heat I learned to build a new kind of compass, one that pointed not toward magnetic north but toward absence. [164] My purpose was simple and impossible: to navigate the geography of loss, to give the returning ones a map back to what they had abandoned. [189] I received my bearings through cryptic phone calls at midnight. [199] The station's last working line would ring, and a voice, thin as wind through reeds, would recite coordinates wrapped in riddles. [220] "Where the orchard drowned, count three stones north of the bell," it might say, then fall silent. [237] I never knew the caller, though I suspected she was the station's previous navigator, long dead, calling from some place the maps could not name. [262] Each midnight I translated her riddles against the censer foot's shifting glow, and by dawn another route lay finished. [281] The returning travelers arrived in caravans, their faces hungry for the homes they had only heard about in stories. [300] I handed each of them a stitched scrap of my coat, a fragment of map, and watched them walk into the geography of what they had lost. [327] My coat grew thinner with every departure. [334] One night, beneath a full moon, the censer foot burned so warm it nearly scalded my palm, and the phone rang earlier than ever. [358] The voice did not give coordinates this time. [366] "You have sent everyone home but yourself," she said, gently. [376] "What place are you navigating toward?" [382] I looked at my coat, reduced now to a single patch over my heart, the map of a town I had left as a child. [407] The reverse migration, I understood, was meant for me too. [417] I had been so busy charting the way back for strangers that I had forgotten I, too, had once fled a place that ached. [441] So I built one last route, tracing the censer foot's lunar warmth backward through every coordinate I had ever given away. [462] They all converged, impossibly, on the town stitched above my heart. [473] At the next new moon, when the bronze dimmed to its faintest pulse, I locked the starlit signal station and stepped out onto the salt flats. [499] I carried the censer foot in one hand and the last patch of map in the other. [516] The phone would ring at midnight to an empty room now, and perhaps another wanderer would answer it someday. [535] Walking under the thinning stars, I felt the raw solace of finally moving toward my own absence instead of mapping everyone else's. [557] The grief did not vanish; it simply became terrain I could cross at last. [571] Somewhere ahead, the drowned orchard waited, three stones north of a silent bell. [584] I was no longer the patchwork navigator; I was simply someone going home. [597] The bronze warmed once, as if in farewell, and I followed the moon. [610]</story>
