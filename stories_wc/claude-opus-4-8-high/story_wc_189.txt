<story>Maren had always called herself a tidal-pool chronicler, though no one had ever paid her for it. [17] She mapped the small worlds that filled and emptied with the sea, recording how anemones bloomed and barnacles waited. [36] What fascinated her most were the transition periods, those brief hours when a pool was neither ocean nor air but something between. [58] Her brother Edan thought this was a waste of a life. [69] He had come to the coast to convince her to sell their late mother's house, and the conversation had grown difficult. [90] They argued along the shore until words ran out, and silence fell between them like low tide. [107] It was across that pause in their difficult conversation that Maren led him into the ancient grotto under stars. [126] The cave opened to the sky through a collapsed ceiling, so the constellations hung over a still black pool inside. [146] She did not answer his demand directly; instead, warmly observant as always, she crouched and pointed at the water. [165] Resting on a ledge beneath the surface lay a jade cicada, green and translucent, carved long ago. [182] Their mother had told them that ancient mourners placed such carvings on the tongues of the dead. [199] The cicada was a promise of transition, she had said, because the insect sheds its shell and emerges renewed. [218] Edan recognized it at once as the charm their mother wore until the day she died. [234] I buried it here, Maren admitted, because this grotto is where she taught me to read the tides. [252] Edan opened his mouth to repeat his argument about money, but she lifted her hand gently. [268] Watch the pool, she said, and you will understand why I cannot sell what she left us. [285] Maren explained that she never approached grief head-on, but reached it through a tangential approach. [300] By studying the pools, the tides, the slow renewals of small creatures, she could feel her mother's life sideways. [319] Her true motivation, she confessed, was to discover the texture of time itself. [332] She wanted to know whether time was smooth or rough, whether it folded or simply flowed away. [349] And here, in the transition periods of the tide, she believed she could almost foresee the shape of things to come. [370] Edan said nothing, but he knelt beside her at the edge of the dark water. [385] For a long while they watched the starlight tremble on the pool as water seeped in through hidden channels. [404] The cicada brightened slowly as the rising sea lifted the level around it. [417] I thought selling would let us move on, Edan finally said, his voice low. [431] But sitting here, I feel her in the way you do, sideways, not all at once. [447] Maren smiled, recognizing the small transition happening inside him, a shell loosening. [459] We do not have to decide tonight, she offered, and he nodded with relief. [473] The tide kept climbing until the jade cicada vanished beneath a silver skin of seawater. [488] Maren realized then that time had a texture after all, grained and patient like wet stone. [504] It was not a river but a tide, returning whatever it seemed to take. [518] Their mother had not vanished; she had only entered a longer transition, like the cicada under the rising pool. [537] Edan touched the water where the carving lay hidden, then withdrew his hand. [550] Leave it for the sea, he murmured, surprising himself. [559] They climbed out of the grotto as the stars faded toward the gray hour before dawn. [575] Maren carried no notebook this time, content simply to remember the chronicle they had shared. [590] She understood now that the warmest observations were the ones made beside someone you loved. [605] Below them the pool kept its slow rhythm, filling, emptying, foretelling nothing yet promising everything. [620]</story>
