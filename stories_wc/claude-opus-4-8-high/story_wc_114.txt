<story>The fever broke at the mountain weather station just before dawn, leaving Iris drenched and strangely light. [17] After fever breaks, she had been told, the truth surfaces like a stone the body can no longer swallow. [36] She had come here to find the weight of that swallowed truth. [48] The station was old, its instruments rusting, and on the desk sat a botanical illustration of a gentian, its blue petals smudged by years of damp thumbs. [75] Beside it hummed the oracle. [80] It was a fault-tolerant oracle, built to keep speaking even when half its circuits failed, a machine that never claimed perfect knowledge, only endurance. [104] Iris had inherited it from her mother, who had died here counting clouds. [117] The oracle worked strangely, never answering at the center of a question, but by occupying margins. [133] It wrote its replies in the white edges of whatever paper lay nearest, so its words now crowded the borders of the gentian illustration. [157] Iris asked it the only question she had carried up the mountain: did I let my mother die? [175] The machine did not answer directly. [181] Instead, peripherally aware of her grief, it began annotating the margins with weather, with dates, with small observations that surrounded the truth without touching it. [206] On the night your fever was highest, it wrote, the gauge froze; no one could have read the storm in time. [227] Iris pressed her finger to the page and accidentally smudged the new ink into the old blue petals. [245] The blur joined memory and machine, and for a moment her certainties began swaying. [259] She had been so sure of her own guilt, the way the bereaved are sure, but the oracle's marginal notes refused to confirm it. [283] By occupying margins, it taught her that the center of a tragedy is often empty, that blame is a shape we draw around an absence. [308] Outside, the sky began to bleed green and violet, an aurora unrolling over the peaks. [323] The light fell through the window onto the illustration, and Iris felt the particular sorrow of beautiful things that arrive too late, an auroral sorrow that aches without accusing. [352] She asked again, softer, what then was the weight of the truth she had swallowed. [367] The oracle, faithful even with its failing circuits, answered in the last clean margin. [381] The truth weighs nothing, it wrote, until you set it down; you have carried it as if carrying her, mistaking weight for love. [404] Iris read it three times while the aurora wheeled overhead. [414] Her mother had not asked to be saved; she had asked only to be remembered counting clouds. [431] The swallowed truth, Iris finally understood, was simply this: she had loved imperfectly, like the oracle, fault-tolerant, continuing. [449] She took the botanical illustration, smudged and annotated, and pinned it to the wall where the dawn would find it each morning. [471] Her certainties were still swaying, but they swayed now like grass, not like a noose. [486] Peripherally aware of the warming instruments, she began the day's first reading, her fever gone, her grief reshaped. [504] The oracle hummed beside her, occupying the margins of every chart, never the centers, content to be a companion rather than a judge. [527] Iris understood that this was how her mother had survived the loneliness here, by letting the machine hold the edges while she held the sky. [552] When the aurora faded, the gentian on the wall seemed bluer for its ruin, the smudge a kind of weather all its own. [575] She no longer needed to weigh the truth, only to carry it lightly, the way one carries the memory of a fever after it has broken. [601] Outside, the clouds resumed their slow procession, and Iris began, at last, to count. [615]</story>
