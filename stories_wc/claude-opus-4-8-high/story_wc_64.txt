<story>Sage tended the cemetery with a blithe whistle, clipping ivy where the dead lay tenderly organized beneath marble. [18] He had buried his employer, old Mr. Varga, only a week before the strange letter arrived. [34] The letter named a place Sage had never seen: a cobalt silo standing alone in the eastern fields, beneath a wide spill of stars. [58] Inside, the letter said, he would learn the shape of intentions he had spent years guessing at. [75] So he walked out after dusk, carrying only Varga's antique sovereign case, a small brass box that had always rattled with coins and secrets. [99] The silo's interior held a velveteen gloom, soft and dark as a closed eyelid. [113] At its center stood a chess clock and a loom, and on the loom hung an unfinished tapestry. [131] A note pinned to the loom read: Press the clock; when it runs down, the silo will speak. [149] Sage pressed the lever, and the old mechanism began its patient tick. [161] He opened the sovereign case and found, instead of coins, a single key and a folded diagram of the tapestry's border. [182] The diagram instructed him to untangle the meaning hidden in the weave by decoding the frayed threads in the tapestry's border. [203] Each loose thread, he realized, corresponded to a letter, and the fringe spelled fragments of Varga's last confession. [221] Sage worked in the cobalt dark, fingers tracing crimson and silver, naming each strand as a child names stars. [240] The clock ticked toward silence while he untangled the frayed border thread by patient thread. [255] The threads confessed that Varga had once loved Sage's mother and had quietly paid for the boy's whole life. [274] Every grave Sage had tended, every wage, every kindness, had been Varga shaping a future he never dared to claim aloud. [295] Here at last was the shape of intentions, woven and frayed, waiting to be read. [310] The chess clock's final tick fell, and after the chess clock ran down, the silo answered. [326] From its curved walls rose uncharted echoes, recordings Varga had layered over decades, his voice doubling and tripling in the round chamber. [348] The echoes spoke names Sage half remembered, lullabies, apologies, and a final, unguarded I'm sorry. [363] These were sounds no one had charted, private as breath, and they filled the velveteen gloom until it felt almost warm. [384] Sage understood then why the gardener's hands had always been so blithe, so unburdened, despite the company of the dead. [404] He had been loved invisibly, and the proof had been tenderly organized into thread and brass and stored starlight. [423] The key from the sovereign case fit a hatch in the floor, opening on a small archive of letters Varga had never sent. [446] Sage read until dawn pearled the silo's cobalt skin, and the stars surrendered one by one. [462] He carried the tapestry home and hung it among the headstones, letting its frayed border catch the wind. [480] Now when he gardened, he no longer guessed at the shape of intentions; he had untangled it and found it kind. [501] Still, the work was not finished, for the diagram bore one final instruction folded into the sovereign case's lining. [520] It asked him to record his own voice in the silo, so the uncharted echoes would not end with Varga. [540] On the next clear night, Sage returned to the cobalt silo under stars and pressed the chess clock again. [559] He spoke softly into the round dark, telling Varga what the threads had let him finally untangle. [576] He thanked the man for a love so tenderly organized it had outlasted the chess clock and the grave alike. [596] When the chess clock ran down, his words joined the others, and the velveteen gloom held them gently. [614] Outside, the gardener locked the sovereign case, blithe once more, and walked home beneath the slow, listening stars. [632]</story>
