<story>Marlow had always been a hands-on folklorist, the kind who trusted his fingers more than footnotes. [16] He arrived at the sunken orchard greenhouse ringed by shipwrecks on the morning his oldest habit finally broke. [34] For forty years he had skipped breakfast to chase rumors, but today he ate slowly, because today he meant to remember. [55] The greenhouse sat in a tidal bowl, its iron ribs green with salt, surrounded by the splintered hulls of vessels that had wandered too close. [80] Locals said the orchard had drowned decades ago, and that anyone who walked its rows lost a piece of himself to the water. [103] Marlow believed them, because he was missing pieces too. [112] He had come to recover stolen memories. [119] Years earlier, when he was young and chronically curious, he had spent a single autumn here with a woman whose name he could no longer summon. [145] The orchard had taken her face, her laugh, the shape of their argument under the apple trees. [162] He remembered only that he had loved, and that the loving had been erased. [176] Now he knelt in the wet grass and searched for the one thing the legends promised: a fairy ring mushroom, pale and perfect, growing in a flawless circle. [204] The old tales claimed that such rings were doors, and that what passed through them could be called back. [223] He found the ring near the greenhouse threshold, twelve mushrooms bowed like mourners. [236] But the door, the stories warned, opened only at the proper hour, and that hour could be read in the sky. [257] This was where his real craft mattered. [264] Marlow could detect the turning of forgotten seasons by studying broken constellations, the star-patterns that sailors had abandoned when their charts changed. [286] He lay back among the wrecks, tracing the shattered Net, the half-drowned Plough, the constellation the orchard-folk had once called the Keeper. [308] When the Keeper's broken arm aligned with the masts of the nearest hull, the old hour would arrive. [326] He waited through a crisp, gold afternoon that smelled of cider and rust. [339] Nostalgia moved over him like tide, gentle and exact. [348] He recalled the texture of this air, though not the woman who had breathed it beside him. [365] As dusk thinned, the constellations cracked into their proper places, and the Keeper pointed. [379] Marlow stepped into the fairy ring. [385] The mushrooms glowed faintly, and the greenhouse glass around him brightened with reflected starlight. [399] For a moment the drowned orchard stood whole again. [408] Apple trees rose silver, unbroken, and between them walked a memory wearing a familiar coat. [423] She turned, and her face returned to him all at once, like a word on the tip of the tongue finally spoken. [445] Her name was Elen. [449] He remembered the argument now: she had wanted to leave the coast, and he had refused, and the orchard had swallowed the rest as punishment for his stubbornness. [477] "You kept your habits," she said, not unkindly, "and lost everything that broke them." [491] Marlow understood then why the spell had waited for this morning. [502] The orchard returned memories only to those willing to change. [512] By eating slowly, by choosing to remember instead of to chase, he had finally paid the toll. [529] "I came back," he said. [534] "Decades later," Elen answered, "but you came." [541] The starlight held them inside the ring while the tide rose around the wrecks. [555] She gave him the autumn back, hour by hour, the cider and the quarrel and the reconciliation he had never let happen. [577] When the Keeper's arm slid past the masts, the door began to close. [590] Marlow stepped out of the fairy ring with his arms full of recovered years. [604] The mushrooms dimmed. [607] He was old, and alone, and complete. [614] He pressed one pale mushroom flat between the pages of his field journal, evidence that folklore could be touched, tasted, and trusted. [636] Then he climbed out of the tidal bowl, past the patient shipwrecks, carrying Elen's restored autumn like a lantern. [655] Habits, he had learned, were only worth keeping until the day they cost you your heart. [671] The folklorist walked home, chronically curious still, but no longer afraid to break. [684]</story>
