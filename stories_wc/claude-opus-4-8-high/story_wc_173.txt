<story>Liorah was called the chaste courtesan, beloved for company she gave but never for the body she withheld. [18] Each night she walked the silver courtyard under snow, where lanterns burned without melting a single flake. [35] There she tended the prism sanctuary under starlight, a chapel of glass that split the heavens into trembling colors. [54] The city's masters had built it to perform the harvest of echoes, a quiet ritual that gathered the last words of the dying and stored them as obedient song. [83] Those songs became a lullaby of conformity, sung over every cradle until no child grew capable of an unscripted wish. [103] Liorah had chosen chastity not from cold blood but from a stranger vow: to defy the lullaby of conformity by keeping one part of herself unharvested. [129] She believed the body was bodily poetic, that flesh remembered verses the mind dared not speak. [145] On the longest night, an old composer came to her, dying, his throat full of echoes the masters wished to claim. [166] He had hidden a melody inside his pulse, knotted there so the harvest could not steal it. [183] To free that song, Liorah had to unlock his hidden music by untangling threads of breath and memory that wound around his failing heart. [207] She knelt in the prism sanctuary, laid her palms over his chest, and read the snarled pulse like a stanza. [227] An alarming serenity filled the courtyard; the snow hung mid-fall, the lanterns held their flame, and time narrowed to the slim hour between sigh and smile. [253] In that suspended breath she worked, loosening one knotted vein of sound after another, careful not to spill it into the harvesting glass. [276] The composer's last verse rose, raw and unowned, a melody no cradle had ever heard. [291] It struck the prism, and the split starlight scattered the tune across the snow in colors that refused to repeat. [311] The masters' lullaby faltered, for here at last was a song nothing could harvest into sameness. [327] The old man smiled, then sighed, and in the space between those two gestures his life slipped free. [345] Liorah caught the loosened melody not in glass but in her own body, where it became another verse the harvest could never reach. [368] This was why she stayed chaste: her flesh was a vault, and each unspoken song made the vault deeper. [387] When morning came, the masters found their harvesting glass empty and a courtyard humming with colors they could not name. [407] They sent guards to question the chaste courtesan, but she only smiled, and her smile was a riddle they could not untangle. [429] Night after night she returned to the silver courtyard under snow, gathering the dying who feared erasure. [446] One by one she unlocked their hidden songs by untangling the threads that pain and obedience had wound through them. [466] Each rescued verse she kept within her bodily poetry, until she carried a whole forbidden chorus beneath her ribs. [485] The prism sanctuary under starlight became a quiet rebellion, splitting the masters' single song into a thousand colors of dissent. [505] Children born that winter dreamed in unfamiliar melodies, and no lullaby could lull them back to sameness. [522] The masters grew afraid of the serenity in her, for it never raised a fist, only opened doors. [540] They could harvest grief, harvest praise, harvest every echo of a closing life, but they could not harvest a woman who gave nothing they could take. [566] So Liorah remained, between sigh and smile, the keeper of every song the world had tried to silence. [584] And on the stillest nights, if you stood in the silver courtyard, you could hear her heartbeat untangling threads of melody, defying the lullaby with each unhurried, alarming, serene beat.
