<story>After reality became negotiable, the glass orchard library drifted untethered upon a swirling sea of clouds. [56] He spoke rarely, and when he did, his sentences folded back on themselves like riddles pressed into lead. [74] Each dawn he read the patterns in morning frost that bloomed across the library's glass, treating the crystals as proofs. [94] The frost told him a secret: a single rupture, placed correctly, could compress eternity into a moment. [111] That was his motivation, the ache behind his quiet hands. [144] If eternity could be folded into an instant, he reasoned, he might hold their last morning forever. [161] On the highest shelf sat his only tool, a bottled whisper, stoppered and trembling. [175] Inside it murmured the very first word reality had spoken when it agreed to be unmade and remade. [193] A whisper like that, released against the frost-patterns, would destabilize perfect systems. [205] The library was the most perfect system of all, a lattice of crystalline order that kept eternity stretched thin and endless. [226] To compress it, he would have to break it. [235] For years he had hesitated, fearing the rustling awakenings the old texts foretold. [248] Those texts warned that any rupture would rouse the sleeping books, that the orchard would stir leaf by leaf into wakefulness.
