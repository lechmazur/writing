<story>Mira was a gilded-leaf calligrapher, and her gift was strange: she wilted near dishonesty. [14] Whenever a lie slid past someone's lips, her skin paled and her shoulders drooped like a stem starved of water. [34] She lived in the sprawling greenhouse city of Verdal, where every street was a glass corridor and every roof a fogged dome holding back the toxic sky. [61] After the oasis dried, the city had sealed itself, trading open air for filtered humidity and the dim, recycled light that made the ferns grow pale. [87] People there had learned to speak carefully, because honesty was rare and Mira's trade depended on it. [104] She inked truths onto leaves of beaten gold, letters that nobles paid for because, they said, gold could not lie. [124] One evening, repairing the floor of her studio, she pried up a loose board and found a packet wrapped in oilcloth. [145] Inside lay a shredded treasure map, its pieces curled like dead petals, and a single zinc-etched postal plate fragment. [164] The fragment bore half an address and the worn imprint of a postmark from the years before the domes. [183] Mira had always wondered where lost conversations go, the words spoken and then forgotten, the letters never delivered. [201] Something about the plate promised an answer, so she resolved to follow it. [214] She began by piecing together the shredded map, laying each strip on her workbench beneath a magnifier. [231] It was slow work, and her gift made it slower. [241] The map's faded notes were half lies, exaggerations scrawled by some long-dead courier, and each falsehood made her sway and weaken. [262] Only when she found the honest lines did her strength return. [273] Over three nights she fit the fragments to the plate's broken edge, and the address resolved into a route through the old postal vaults beneath the greenhouse city. [301] There, the map promised, was the place where undelivered letters had been gathered after the oasis dried and the surface mail collapsed. [323] Mira descended through a service hatch into tunnels that smelled of rust and standing water. [338] The vault was a cathedral of pigeonholes, each slot stuffed with envelopes that no one had ever come to claim. [358] She understood, then, where lost conversations went: not into the air, but into patient paper, waiting. [374] An old archivist lived among the slots, tending them by lantern. [385] He told her the letters held everything people had been too afraid to say aloud. [400] Mira felt no wilting near him; the man spoke only truth, and in his presence she stood tall for the first time in years. [424] He handed her one envelope addressed by the same hand as the postal plate, and asked her to reread it with him. [446] She did, slowly. [449] It was a letter from her own grandmother, written before the domes, confessing a small unkindness and begging forgiveness she had never dared request in person. [475] The candor of that small moment struck Mira harder than any grand revelation could. [489] She realized her grandmother had buried the map and plate beneath the floor so that someone honest enough would one day find this room. [513] The treasure was never gold; it was the courage to say true things while there was still time. [531] Mira asked the archivist if she could stay and help. [541] Together they began answering the letters, writing back on gilded leaves so the replies would outlast the toxic years above. [561] To each writer's heirs she delivered a single honest sentence, and with every truth spoken her strength grew. [579] Word spread through the greenhouse city that the calligrapher had found where lost conversations go. [594] People came down into the vault to read and to confess, and the ferns above, it was said, grew a little greener. [616] Mira never wilted again, because she had filled her life with candor in small moments, the only treasure worth piecing together a map to find. [641]</story>
