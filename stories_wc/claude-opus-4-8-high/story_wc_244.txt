<story>Nobody could pin down Marlowe Quist, the elusive disaster-relief logistician, who arrived wherever calamity had just finished and paperwork had not yet begun. [23] He kept his office in a disused railway station overgrown with ivy, where the schedules had rotted into suggestions and the clocks agreed on nothing. [48] I came to him with a fragment of moon rock and a problem I could not name. [65] He examined the rock under a lantern strangled by vines, then grinned the grin of a man one joke away from collapse. [87] "Ah, lunar real estate," he said, desperately jocular, "the only neighborhood quieter than this one." [102] I told him the truth: I wanted to find the cure for loneliness, and I had heard, through improbable rumor chains, that he traded in impossible deliveries. [129] The rumor had passed from a stranded ferry captain to a sleepless baker to a child who sold buttons, each adding a flourish, until the legend reached me whole. [158] Marlowe nodded as though rumors were a freight system he personally maintained. [170] "Everything here is bound by reciprocity," he warned. "You bring a thing, I owe a thing; I give a thing, you owe the world. No charity, only exchange." [198] It was polite anarchy, a kingdom of favors with no king, and he ruled it by refusing to rule. [217] He weighed the moon rock in his palm and explained that loneliness had no cure you could carry, only one you could catalyze. [240] "Give me your rock," he said, "and I will start a chain that ends with you no longer alone." [259] I hesitated, because the fragment was the last thing my brother had touched before the flood took our town. [278] Marlowe softened, his jokes thinning to reveal the grief beneath them. [289] "I am the most alone man alive," he admitted. "I arrive after every disaster precisely so I never have to stay for one." [312] That confession was its own currency, and I felt the reciprocity bind us tighter than any contract. [329] So I surrendered the moon rock, and he set the machinery of whisper into motion. [344] He told the button-child that a stargazer sought company; the child told the baker, who told the captain, who told a hundred strangers along the dead railway line. [372] The rumor mutated, as rumors do, into an invitation: come to the ivy station, bring something you have lost, and trade your solitude away. [396] Within a week they arrived, dozens of them, each clutching a relic and a wound. [411] The platform filled with strangers swapping stories, and the moon rock passed hand to hand as proof that even fallen things travel far. [434] I watched the timeframe shift, the exact hour when the story changes, when a building of departures became a place where people chose to stay. [459] Marlowe moved among them, desperately jocular as ever, but his laughter had gained a floor to stand on. [477] The cure for loneliness, I understood, was not a substance but a circuit; you could only receive it by passing it on. [499] When the others had paired off into conversations, Marlowe handed the moon rock back to me. [515] "Reciprocity," he said. "You gave it to start the chain; the chain gives it back. Now you owe the next lonely fool a rumor." [539] I asked him to stay, and for once the elusive man did not vanish. [553] He sat beneath the ivy, beside a stranger, and told a joke that nobody needed to laugh at. [571] They laughed anyway, and the dead station, for the first time in years, kept a schedule of arrivals. [589] I pocketed the moon rock and walked toward the first stranger I had ever chosen, carrying a debt I was glad to repay forever. [613]</story>
