<story>After the rainforest burned, the whole coast fell into a chronic hush. [12] Ash drifted over the water and settled on the masts of the shipwrecks that ringed the sunken orchard greenhouse. [31] Mira had grown up there, an echo-harbor violinist who learned music inside that glass cathedral of drowned apple trees. [50] She was acoustically perceptive, able to hear the difference between a healthy silence and a wounded one. [67] This silence, she knew, was wounded. [73] Before the fires, her family had gathered every solstice around a bonfire on the greenhouse roof, passing stories like embers from hand to hand. [97] That family tradition of storytelling around a bonfire was how the orchard had always kept itself awake. [114] Now her relatives were scattered, and the trees that once sang with wind through their leaves stood mute beneath the brackish glass. [136] Mira rowed out alone, carrying her violin and an old mendicant's bowl her grandmother had used to beg songs from strangers. [157] The bowl, her grandmother said, never asked for coins, only for unfinished things. [170] Mira tied her boat to a leaning hull and climbed down into the greenhouse, where dim green light wavered through the panes. [192] Inside, the chronic hush pressed against her ears like deep water. [203] She set the mendicant's bowl on a mossy branch and began to play. [216] Her bow found no melody, only fragments, because grief had no finished song. [229] At first the silence swallowed every note, the way the fire had swallowed the forest. [244] But she was acoustically perceptive, and she listened for the room's hidden shape. [257] She heard how each shipwreck around the greenhouse held its own resonance, like a ring of sleeping bells. [275] If she could wake them, she might unbind the hush that bound the orchard. [289] So Mira played to the hulls, not to fill the silence but to converse with it. [305] She offered the unfinished phrases into the mendicant's bowl, as her grandmother had taught her. [320] One by one, the wrecks answered, their old timbers humming in sympathetic vibration. [333] The greenhouse filled with a low, layered chord, ragged and incomplete. [344] Mira realized she did not need to repair the song. [354] Her motivation had never been to perfect it, but to find beauty in the unfinished. [369] The hush, she understood, was not an enemy but a held breath waiting for someone to speak first. [387] When darkness came, she gathered driftwood on the highest hull and lit a bonfire. [401] Smoke rose where the rainforest's smoke had once choked the sky, but this smoke smelled of welcome. [418] Distant boats saw the light, and her scattered family began rowing home. [430] They climbed the hulls one by one, drawn by flame and by the resonance still trembling in the wrecks. [449] Around the bonfire, the family tradition of storytelling resumed, each voice adding a fragment no one tried to finish. [468] An uncle spoke of orchards before the flood. [476] A cousin told half a tale of the burning trees and let the smoke carry the rest. [493] Mira passed the mendicant's bowl around the fire, and each storyteller dropped a word into it like a seed. [512] When the bowl returned to her, it held no coins, only the soft weight of things left open. [530] Mira lifted her violin again and let the unfinished melody rise over the water. [544] The chronic hush did not vanish; it simply learned to listen. [555] In that magnificent simplicity, the harbor breathed once more. [564] Mira understood now what her grandmother had meant about the bowl and its hunger for unfinished things. [581] A finished song closes; an unfinished one keeps making room for everyone still rowing toward the light. [598] So she played on, and the orchard, the wrecks, and the waking family sang the night into a beginning. [617]</story>
