<story>Sir Aldous had not spoken aloud in seven winters, and the silence had grown a skin over him like frost on glass. [22] A withdrawn knight, he kept to the one place where no one expected speech: the alchemical orchard greenhouse, shrouded in endless night. [44] Beneath its black-domed glass, trees of mercury and pear-light bore fruit that never ripened, only waited. [60] Here the world was factually mystical, where every law of metal and root could be measured, yet each measurement bent toward wonder. [82] He had come, as he came each night, to capture the shape of possibility before it dissolved into morning. [101] Tonight, tucked among the roots of the oldest mercury tree, he found a slim, water-stained booklet. [117] It was an abandoned marionette theater program, its cast list faded, its puppets long since rotted to dust elsewhere. [136] Aldous turned the brittle pages and recognized, faintly, the names of his own forgotten boyhood. [151] He had once worked the strings here, in a season before the wars taught him to fold his voice away. [171] The diagrams in the program were not stage directions at all, he realized, but instructions written through forgotten patterns of movement. [192] Each figure traced a path the hands must follow to wake something sleeping in the orchard's enchanted air. [210] His fingers, stiff from disuse, began to mimic the looping gestures, half-remembering, half-inventing. [223] Where his hands passed, the night thinned, and faint silver marionettes assembled themselves from dew and the breath of fruit. [243] They hung from no strings he could see, waiting, as he had once waited to be told who he was. [263] A shivering bliss climbed his spine, the first warmth he had felt since silence claimed him. [279] He understood now what the orchard had been built to do: not to grow fruit, but to grow futures. [298] Each puppet was a possibility, a self he might have been, and his hands could shape them into being. [317] But the program warned, in the smallest forgotten print, that one chosen shape would become real as reality shifts. [336] To choose, he must give a murmured allegiance, naming aloud the future he wished to keep. [352] Speech, the very thing he had buried, was the toll the orchard demanded. [365] The marionettes turned their blank faces toward him, each pleading silently to be the one drawn through into life. [384] Aldous studied them: the warrior he had become, the husband he had never dared be, the boy still laughing among the strings. [406] His throat ached around words he had not used in so long they felt foreign as a stranger's coat. [425] The dome above seemed to unsnap, peeling back its endless night to reveal a darkness even older and listening. [444] He reached toward the laughing boy, then stopped, for that future was already spent. [458] Instead he chose the husband, the gentle, unguarded man, and let the syllables rise. [472] "You," he murmured, his vow no louder than a falling petal, "I pledge myself to you." [488] The orchard drank the murmured allegiance like rain, and the chosen marionette shuddered, warming, taking weight and want. [506] The others sighed and dissolved, returning to dew, and Aldous wept without grief, only release. [521] When dawn finally found the greenhouse, the endless night had lifted, and two sets of footprints led out through the orchard gate. [543] He left the faded program tucked back among the roots, for the next silent soul who might need to remember how to speak. [566] Behind him the trees ripened at last, their fruit glowing with the soft certainty of a chosen life. [584] And the withdrawn knight, withdrawn no longer, walked into morning with his impossible companion, still shivering, still blissful, finally whole. [604]</story>
