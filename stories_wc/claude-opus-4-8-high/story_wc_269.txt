Mara had witnessed the coral bleaching once, years before the fossil fuel ban, and that white graveyard still anchored her thoughts. [21] Now she lived in the sea level monitoring station, a rusted pod moored above the drowned coastline. [38] The waters had risen and stilled, and her days drifted past like the tide that never quite left. [56] Each morning she practiced tapping rhythms on the metal hull, a habit from childhood that calmed her. [73] The taps traveled through the station and, she believed, out into the water where something might answer. [90] On the wall above her bunk she had pinned an atlas page torn out from an old schoolbook. [108] It showed shipping lanes and migration routes, thin paths inked across a blue that no longer existed. [125] She had torn it free to keep one question close: why did some paths disappear while others endured? [143] That question had become her motivation, the thread pulling her through each identical, moored day. [158] The instruments around her recorded the sea, which now seemed periodically eternal, rising and pausing in long, patient breaths. [177] One night her tapping returned to her, faintly, from the dark beyond the hull. [191] She froze, certain she had imagined it, then tapped again, three slow beats. [204] Three beats answered, perfectly matched, traveling up through the steel into her palm. [217] For weeks she and the unseen rhythm spoke, assembling a language from echoes and pauses. [232] She would assemble fragments of meaning each dawn, piecing the taps into something close to sentences. [248] The rhythm spoke of currents that had vanished, of warm dead reefs, of routes erased by the changing water. [267] Mara realized, slowly, that her companion was answering the very question she had pinned to her wall. [284] Paths disappeared, it tapped, because the living things that drew them died, and the map forgot their feet. [302] She thought of the bleached reef she had watched whiten, its fish scattering down corridors that closed behind them. [321] The delayed answer arrived like grief deferred, a truth that had waited years to surface in her hands. [339] She asked who tapped back, fingers trembling against the cold hull. [350] The reply came not as words but as her own childhood rhythm, the one she had drummed before the bleaching. [370] It was the sea itself, or her memory of it, answering through the patient steel. [385] She understood then that she had been tapping to herself all along, mooring her grief to a rhythm. [403] The fossil fuel ban had come too late for the coral, but not too late for remembering. [420] Mara took down the torn atlas page and laid it flat across the table. [434] On its faded blue she began to draw the vanished routes the rhythm had described. [449] She would assemble a new atlas, one that recorded not where things went but where they had been. [467] Each erased path deserved a witness, and she was the one still moored above their graves. [483] The station drifted on its chains, periodically eternal, rising with each tide and settling back. [498] Mara tapped a final rhythm against the hull, a thank-you to whatever had listened. [512] The water held the sound, then released it, carrying her question outward toward all the lost, disappeared paths. [530] She had her answer now, but answers, she learned, were only the beginning of better questions. [546] Why had she survived to remember, when so many corridors of life had simply closed? [561] The tapping had taught her that remembering was its own kind of mooring, a way to stay. [578] Outside, the drowned world stretched silver and patient under a sky scrubbed clean of smoke. [593] The air, at least, had healed; the ban had given the future that much. [607] Mara pressed her palm to the cold steel and waited, listening, for the next rhythm to rise. [624]
