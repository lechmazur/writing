<story>Mira was the quiet shoreline poet whom the village rarely heard but always trusted. [14] Each morning she walked the cobalt lagoon after rain, where the water held a burnished hush. [30] She came to tend a small craft she had inherited from her grandmother: a loom strung beside the shore. [49] On it she practiced what her grandmother had called weaving woven dawns, threading colors that mimicked sunrise into cloth. [68] Yet every tapestry she finished felt wrong, too perfect, too closed, and she would unravel it before dusk. [86] Her motivation had narrowed to a single ache: to discover beauty in the incomplete. [100] One bright afternoon a boy flew a kite above the lagoon, laughing as it climbed. [115] Mira watched, warmly attentive, the way she watched all small struggles against the sky. [129] Then, as a kite string snaps, the bright diamond tore loose and tumbled toward the water. [145] Something fell with it, spiraling down to land at her feet on the wet sand. [160] It was a drake's tail feather, iridescent green and copper, half-broken at the quill. [174] The boy ran up, breathless, and explained the feather had been his good-luck charm tied to the line. [192] Now both kite and luck were ruined, he said, and his eyes filled. [205] Mira knelt and turned the feather, studying the broken vane that no longer lay smooth. [220] Its barbs had separated into a small chaos of green, a discord of torn threads. [235] She thought of her loom, and an idea moved through her like the after-rain light. [250] Take heart, she told the boy, and bring me your broken kite as well. [264] Together they sat at the loom beside the cobalt water, which now mirrored a torn sky. [280] Mira began to weave, but this time she worked by harmonizing discord rather than hiding it. [296] She set the kite's ripped silk against rough lagoon reeds, letting their colors argue and then agree. [313] Into the very center she wove the drake's tail feather, broken barbs and all. [327] She did not mend its fracture; she framed it, so the gap caught the light. [342] As she worked, she explained to the boy what her grandmother never quite had. [356] A finished thing closes a door, she murmured, but an unfinished thing keeps inviting you in. [372] The cloth that grew was not symmetrical, and that was exactly its strength. [385] Its torn edges and bright feather suggested a sunrise still arriving, never wholly arrived. [399] These, at last, were the woven dawns her hands had searched for all along. [413] The boy touched the feather and smiled, his ruined luck remade into something he could keep. [429] Word spread quietly, and soon others came to the lagoon to tend their own broken things. [445] They brought cracked oars, faded ribbons, a single shoe, the small wreckage of ordinary days. [460] Mira received each one warmly, attentive to the particular sorrow behind every flaw. [473] She wove their fragments into a long banner, harmonizing discord into a single rippling chord. [488] When the banner finally hung between two posts above the cobalt lagoon after rain, the village fell silent. [506] It was full of holes, and through each hole the morning poured. [518] No one had ever seen anything so unfinished, and no one had ever seen anything so whole. [535] Mira stood in the burnished hush, the drake's tail feather catching the new sun at the banner's heart. [553] She understood now that she had not been failing all those years. [565] She had simply refused to finish what was more beautiful left open. [577] The quiet shoreline poet wrote no poem that day. [586] She had finally woven one instead, and left the last thread loose. [598] It swayed in the wind, still reaching, beautifully incomplete. [607]</story>
