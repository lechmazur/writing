<story>Through the hush of an overlooked era, the shooting star tracking station hummed alone at the edge of a forgotten galaxy. [21] Vael, the cosmic janitor, swept its silent corridors with a broom of woven starlight. [35] He was spiritually curious, the kind of soul who paused to wonder why the dust still settled when no one remained to see it. [59] The astronomers had gone ages ago, leaving their instruments to gather quiet. [71] Only one relic kept its color: a curved pane of yellow lantern glass, salvaged from an ancient lighthouse and bolted above the main console. [95] Each night, Vael pressed his face to it and watched the heavens. [107] His task was simple and endless: to keep the station clean for visitors who never came. [123] Yet loneliness had begun to hollow him, and he feared the ache more than he feared the dark. [141] He longed to find strength in vulnerability, to let the emptiness teach him rather than break him. [158] So he turned to the only ritual the station offered. [168] He learned to read the sky by tracing the path of falling stars, marking where each one died against the black. [189] Through the yellow lantern glass, every streak became a thread of warm gold, gentle as a held breath. [207] He began to empathize with the dying stars, sensing in their plunge a courage he had never owned. [225] Each star fell without complaint, surrendering its fire so that the darkness might briefly remember light. [241] They were not destroyed, he realized, but offered. [249] Vael charted their arcs night after night, filling old logbooks with delicate maps of endings. [264] In their geometry he found a strange instruction, written in the oldest language the universe knew. [280] To fall openly was not weakness; it was a primal elegance, the grace of giving everything away. [297] One evening, a particularly bright meteor crossed the glass and lingered, as if waiting for him. [313] Vael traced its descent with his fingertip against the warm pane and felt his own fear soften. [330] If a star could spend itself so beautifully, then perhaps his hollow places were not wounds but openings. [348] He set down his broom and simply sat, allowing the silence to enter him completely. [363] There, in that surrender, something unexpected bloomed. [370] He discovered a flourishing stillness, a quiet so full that it no longer felt like absence. [386] The station, empty for an age, now seemed brimming with company he had failed to notice. [402] Every fallen star he had charted was a friend who had spoken to him in light. [418] Vael understood at last why the dust still settled, why he still swept. [431] Tending an overlooked place was itself a kind of devotion, a way of honoring what others abandoned. [448] He no longer waited for visitors to give his work meaning. [459] The meaning had been here all along, glowing through yellow lantern glass. [471] From that night on, the cosmic janitor kept two ledgers. [481] One recorded the corridors he cleaned; the other recorded the stars he mourned and celebrated. [496] His spiritually curious heart, once frightened by its own tenderness, now welcomed each ache as proof of his aliveness. [515] He had wanted strength, and found it not in armor but in opening. [528] When the next era finally arrived and wanderers stumbled upon the station, they expected ruin. [543] Instead they found a luminous keeper and a wall of golden charts mapping the paths of falling stars. [561] Vael greeted them gently, ready at last to teach what the quiet had taught him. [576] He pointed to the yellow lantern glass and to the heavens beyond. [588] Watch how they fall, he said, and learn how to live. [599] In the flourishing stillness, the overlooked station shone, and was no longer alone. [612]</story>
