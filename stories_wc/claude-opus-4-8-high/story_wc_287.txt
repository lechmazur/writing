<story>Mira tended her garden on the fire escape, where rust and roses had learned to coexist. [16] She was the building's fire escape gardener, a title she had given herself, wistfully pragmatic about beauty grown in narrow places. [37] For years the old rosebush had anchored her mornings, but tonight she watched the final petal drop from it, spiraling into the dark stairwell below. [62] After the final petal fell, she understood the season was ending, and so was something else she had not named. [82] Her father, the harbor's old lockmaster, had died that spring, leaving her a single keepsake: his bitting gauge tooth, a small brass tool for measuring the cuts of keys. [111] He had spent his life ensuring locks opened only for the right hands, and he had passed that same careful trust to her. [134] Now she carried the brass tooth down the fire escape, descending each step with the rhythm of oars in water, steady and unhurried. [157] At the bottom waited the luminous harbor by moonlight, silver water trembling against the docks her father once guarded. [176] A quivering hush hung over the piers, the kind of silence that feels alive, as if the night were holding its breath with her. [200] She had come to do what her father had asked in his last lucid hour: to open the harbor's oldest lamp-house. [221] Its lock had jammed decades ago, and the great mirror inside had gone dark, leaving the eastern coves blind to ships. [242] He had wanted, before he died, to reflect light into dark corners again, and he had trusted her to finish it. [263] Mira knelt at the lamp-house door, fitting the bitting gauge tooth against the worn pins, reading the old cuts by touch. [284] Her hands shook, but the tool steadied them, measuring depths the way he had taught her years before. [302] She felt the strange, protected vulnerability of grief given a task, of sorrow held safely inside purpose. [319] One by one the pins aligned, and with a soft click the ancient lock surrendered. [334] Inside, dust hung thick, but the brass mirror still curved against the wall, waiting. [348] She cleaned its surface slowly, wiping decades of salt away until the glass remembered how to shine. [365] Then she angled it toward the moon, catching the cold light and bending it outward across the water. [383] A beam swept the eastern coves, and the quivering hush broke into the gentle slap of waves rediscovering the shore. [403] Far out, a fishing boat blinked its lantern twice, answering the light it had not seen in a generation. [422] Mira sat on the lamp-house steps, the bitting gauge tooth warm in her palm, and let herself cry without shame. [442] Her father had not left her flowers or fortunes, only a tool and a duty, and she found that fitting. [462] The rosebush above might be bare now, but here was a different kind of bloom, one made of light. [481] She would tend this lamp as she tended her garden, faithfully, with the rhythm of oars in water carrying her up and down the long climb each night. [509] The work would instill in the harbor a memory of safety, and in her a reason to keep rising. [528] Above the harbor, the moon held steady, and the beam she had freed reached every cove that had waited so long in shadow. [551] Mira tucked the brass tooth into her pocket and began the climb home, counting steps like strokes through dark water. [571] Tomorrow she would plant something new beside the spent rosebush, something hardy enough for rust and wind. [588] But first she paused, looked back once at the glowing lamp-house, and let herself smile at what endured. [606]</story>
