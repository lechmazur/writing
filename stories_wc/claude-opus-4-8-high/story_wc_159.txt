<story>The glass-orchid caretaker climbed the last stair into the opal library above clouds, where every shelf glowed like frozen dawn. [20] She had tended this place alone for forty years, a gently systematic keeper of deserted marvels. [36] Her name was Liora, though names were exactly the thing she feared losing. [49] The library was not made of books but of feelings, each one extinct, each one catalogued across the museum of extinct feelings that filled its endless wings. [76] Visitors had stopped coming long ago. [82] Now only Liora walked the aisles, lighting the lamps so the marvels would not forget themselves in the dark. [101] Among her charges hung a wind chime, its bamboo tube cracked down one side, silent for as long as she could remember. [123] The placard beneath it read: Tenderness for Strangers, Last Heard 1894. [134] Liora's true purpose, the one she never spoke aloud, was to preserve the sound of forgotten names. [151] Every feeling in the museum had once belonged to a person, and when the feeling died, the person's name dimmed too. [172] She believed that if she could make even one extinct feeling sing again, the names tethered to it would return to light. [194] The instructions, written in the library's oldest ledger, were precise: a dead marvel could be revived only by the echo of a long-forgotten song. [218] But Liora had forgotten every song she ever knew, worn smooth by decades of solitude. [233] That evening, dusting the cracked bamboo tube, she felt a familiar ache rise in her chest, the foxglove regret of having outlived her own memory. [258] She pressed her lips together and, without thinking, began to hum. [269] It was nothing, three notes, a fragment surfacing from some childhood she had filed away and lost. [286] The wind chime trembled. [290] Through the crack in its tube, a thin sound slipped out, fragile as breath on glass. [306] Liora froze, then hummed the three notes again, more carefully this time. [318] The fragment widened into melody, an old song she had not known she still carried, and the chime answered. [337] Note by note, the echo of that long-forgotten song poured through the cracked bamboo, and Tenderness for Strangers woke. [356] Warmth spread through the opal library above clouds, and the shelves flushed with returning color. [371] Then, faintly, names began to glow along the placards, names that had been dark for a century. [388] Liora read them aloud, one after another, her voice the only witness they had left. [403] She understood now why she had been kept here, gently systematic, faithful, alone. [416] The deserted marvels were not exhibits to mourn but seeds, waiting for someone whose memory could still bloom. [434] All night she sang the song through the chime, and feeling after feeling stirred awake across the museum of extinct feelings. [455] Each one returned a cluster of names to the light, and Liora spoke every single one. [471] By dawn her throat was raw, her hands trembling, but the foxglove regret had thinned into something close to peace. [491] She no longer feared losing her own name. [499] She had given it freely to the song, and the song would carry it as it carried all the others. [519] The cracked bamboo tube hung still now, its work done, faintly warm to the touch. [534] Liora the glass-orchid caretaker sat among her revived marvels and listened to the names breathe. [549] Somewhere far below the clouds, she hoped, the people who once owned these feelings felt a flicker they could not explain. [570] A sudden kindness toward a stranger, a name on the tip of the tongue, a warmth without a source. [589] That, she decided, was enough to keep the lamps lit for another forty years. [603]</story>
