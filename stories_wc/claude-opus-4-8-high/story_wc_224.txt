<story>The bookstore filled the whole mountain, shelf upon shelf winding through hollowed stone like veins through a sleeping body. [19] I came here because remembering had finally become painful, and I needed somewhere vast enough to lose the ache. [38] They told me a seer lived in the deepest gallery, a woman made of mercury glass who answered only the truly lost. [60] I climbed for hours, my lamp throwing my shadow against spines in forgotten tongues. [74] When I found her, she sat motionless, her silvered skin reflecting a thousand titles at once. [90] 'You want to understand the shape of home,' she said, before I spoke a word. [105] I nodded, throat tight, because that was exactly the hollow I had carried up the mountain. [121] She lifted a smooth gray object from her lap, worn with carved marks no living mouth could pronounce. [139] 'This is the lost language stone,' she murmured, 'and home is a word written in it.' [155] She explained that she substitutes reality nightly, dreaming new worlds over the old until the sleeper forgets which is true. [175] 'That is my buried talent,' she admitted, 'and it is also my prison.' [188] Long ago she had replaced her own past so often that she no longer knew where she had been born. [208] Now she wanted what I wanted, and so we were two lost people bargaining in the dark. [225] She pressed a single white feather into my palm and told me to read the stone via the texture of a feather. [247] 'The marks resist the eye,' she said, 'but the barbs of a feather catch their meaning.' [263] I dragged the feather across the carvings, and where the vane snagged, sounds rose in me like distant weather. [282] It was hidden thunder, a pressure under the silence, the kind you feel before any storm announces itself. [300] Letter by letter the lost language opened, and I understood that home was never a place I had left. [319] Home was a thing I could seed, planted forward rather than mourned backward. [332] The stone said: build it, do not search for it. [342] When I looked up, the seer was trembling, her mercury face rippling like disturbed water. [357] 'Read it for me,' she begged, 'I have substituted so many nights that I cannot feel the texture anymore.' [376] So I took her cold glass hand and guided the feather under her fingers. [390] Together we drew it across the stone, and the buried talent I had never named in myself woke up. [409] I could teach the feather to speak; I could give meaning back to the marks she had forgotten. [427] The hidden thunder grew louder, no longer a threat but a heartbeat under the mountain. [442] Her reflection stilled, and for the first time the thousand titles in her skin resolved into one image. [460] It was a small house, lamplit, with two chairs, the shape of home she had buried under endless substitutions. [479] 'I remember now,' she whispered, and remembering no longer seemed to hurt her. [492] We sat in the deepest gallery and decided to seed a real home from the dream she had perfected. [511] She would substitute reality one final night, not to escape it, but to plant something that could stay. [529] When dawn climbed the mountain, the bookstore had grown a doorway that had never existed before. [545] Beyond it stood the lamplit house, real now, seeded by two people who had finally read themselves correctly. [563] The seer stepped through, her glass skin warming to something almost human in the light. [578] I kept the feather, because I would need it to read the language of every home I built afterward. [597] And the thunder, no longer hidden, sang quietly through the stone, welcoming us in. [611]</story>
