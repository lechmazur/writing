<story>Halloran was the reticent lighthouse supply skipper, a man who spoke to the sea more than to people. [18] Every fortnight he hauled oil, bread, and mail to the keepers stranded on the rock at Vane's Point. [36] He rarely lingered, but he was warmly observant, noticing how a keeper's hands shook or how long the lamp had burned. [57] On the morning the lamp flickered out for good, he found the tower silent. [71] The old keeper, Maren, had died in her sleep, and the great light above had gone dark with her. [90] Halloran climbed to the haunted lantern room, where the air felt thick with all the watches she had kept. [109] On the brass rail sat a piece of sea glass, frosted green, smoothed by decades of tide. [126] Beneath it lay a letter, unsigned, addressed only to 'the next pair of hands.' [140] Maren had written that she, too, had once found such a letter, left by the keeper before her. [158] It was an anonymous letter chain, passed for a century from one lonely soul to the next. [175] Each writer confessed a single fear and a single comfort, then sealed the page for whoever came after. [193] Maren's comfort was the sea glass, which she swore could bottle the feeling of safety if you held it to the lamplight. [215] Halloran, who had buried a brother to these same waters, understood the wish to keep one warm thing safe. [234] But the lamp was dead, and there was no light left to hold the glass against. [250] He stayed three nights, unwilling to leave her unburied and the chain unanswered. [263] The lantern room creaked with footsteps that were not his, and lamps he had not lit glowed faintly at the edges of sleep. [286] He was not frightened; the presence felt like Maren still keeping watch, reluctant to abandon her post. [303] On the shore below, Halloran remembered the keepers' old custom: a way of stacking stones as messages. [320] Three flat stones meant 'all is well'; a leaning cairn warned of danger to passing boats. [336] With the great lamp out, the village fishermen would have no signal to protect them from the reef. [354] So Halloran climbed the rocks each dusk and built the old stone messages, tall enough to be seen from the channel. [375] By day he scoured the lens and refilled the reservoir, coaxing the dead mechanism back toward life. [392] On the fourth evening the wick caught, and the lantern room flooded with a luminescent ache of gold. [410] He lifted the sea glass to the beam, and for one moment the green held the light like a captured breath. [431] In that glow he finally felt what Maren meant: safety made visible, briefly bottled, achingly bright. [447] The footsteps in the room went still, as if satisfied, and never returned. [460] Halloran buried Maren beneath a cairn of three flat stones, telling the sea that all was well. [477] Then he took up the pen and added his own page to the anonymous letter chain. [493] His fear, he wrote, was being forgotten; his comfort was the stones, which spoke even when men could not. [512] He placed the sea glass atop the letter, the chain unbroken for the next pair of hands. [529] Halloran chose to stay and keep the light himself, the reticent skipper become a keeper at last. [546] Each night the lamp turned its slow gold over the reef, and each night he stacked his stones to protect the boats below. [569] He spoke little, as always, but the warmly observant keeper had learned the language of stone and glass and light. [589] And on the clearest nights, when the beam swept far, he believed Maren watched the channel beside him, finally at peace. [610]</story>
