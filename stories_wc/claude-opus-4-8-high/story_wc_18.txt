<story>Liora was the last mirror maker in the ruined orchard district, a place lost in centuries of thick fog. [19] Among the collapsed cider barns and skeletal apple trees, she polished glass until it sang like distant, breakable mountains. [38] She was a firmly grounded woman, suspicious of anything she could not weigh in her calloused hands. [55] Yet one duty pulled her beyond reason: to capture a runaway dream. [67] The dream belonged to her dying brother, an astronomer whose mind had wandered into the fog and never returned. [86] Each night he murmured of a vision he could no longer hold, and each dawn it slipped further from him. [106] He had left her only one tool, his astronomer's broken sextant, its mirror cracked, its arc bent like a wounded wing. [127] A mirror maker, she believed, could recreate any reflection, even one stolen by sleep. [141] So she carried the sextant out upon the shimmer of twilight frost, when the orchard glittered like a field of fallen stars. [163] The fog here was not empty; it pressed close, erasing boundaries between earth and sky, between the living and the half-remembered. [184] Liora knelt among the frozen roots and set the broken sextant before her like an altar. [200] Her brother had once taught her that dreams could be measured the way sailors measured stars, by aligning two breaths into one rhythm. [223] So she would do this through synchronized breathing, matching her lungs to his faint pulse miles away in their cold cottage. [244] She inhaled as the frost thickened, exhaled as the fog curled, until the boundary between her body and the night grew thin. [266] Slowly, the cracked mirror of the sextant began to glow, gathering loose light from the air. [282] In its splintered surface she saw it: her brother's runaway dream, drifting like smoke through the ruined trees. [300] It was a vision of glass mountains rising from the orchard, ranges so clear and sharp they seemed to hum with cold music. [323] The dream had fled because no one would hold it steady, and a mirror maker, firmly grounded, was exactly the anchor it needed. [346] Liora breathed slower, deeper, coaxing the drifting image toward the waiting glass. [358] The dream resisted, swirling, threatening to dissolve into the eternal fog that had swallowed everything else here. [375] But she did not chase it; she simply matched its trembling, letting her breath become its breath. [392] As their rhythms aligned, the boundary between dream and glass collapsed entirely. [404] The runaway vision poured into the cracked mirror, and the fracture sealed itself with a sound like ringing crystal. [423] Now the sextant held not a broken arc but a perfect window onto those impossible glass mountains. [440] Liora wept, for she had managed to recreate the very thing her brother feared losing forever. [456] She rose, knees stiff with frost, and pressed the warm sextant to her chest. [470] The fog parted just enough to let her find the path back through the dead orchard. [486] When she reached the cottage, she knelt beside her brother and held the mended sextant before his fading eyes. [505] He looked into the glass mountains and breathed, once, in time with her. [518] For a moment their two breaths were one, erasing the last boundary between memory and morning. [534] He smiled, no longer afraid, because his dream would outlive him in unbreakable glass. [548] Liora stayed firmly grounded at his side until twilight returned and the frost shimmered once more. [564] She would remain the mirror maker of the lost orchard, keeper of a dream that fog could never again erase. [584] Each evening she breathed with the sextant, holding the glass mountains steady against the encroaching dark. [600] And in that quiet ritual, brother and sister remained joined, two souls measuring one boundless sky together. [617]</story>
