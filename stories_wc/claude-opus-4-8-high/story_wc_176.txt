<story>Mira had been a lost wax caster for thirty winters, pouring bronze into hollows where wax once held a shape. [20] Her hands were technically gentle, schooled to coax molten metal without scarring the mold. [34] She had come to the icebound sanctum beneath the aurora because the dead had asked her to. [51] Not in words, but in a single artifact: a street organ punched card scrap, brittle as a dried leaf. [70] Her grandmother had played a barrel organ in the harbor town, and each holes-and-bridges card was a transcribed memory of a song. [92] When the town drowned, the songs scattered, and the names of the drowned scattered with them. [108] Mira's motivation was simple and immense: to preserve the sound of forgotten names. [121] The sanctum held one instrument capable of the task, a centuries-old ceremonial rope strung between two pillars of blue ice. [141] Legend said the rope sang only when its braids were rearranged into the shape of a true name. [159] To wake it, Mira would have to work by reconfiguring the braids of that centuries-old ceremonial rope, thread by frozen thread. [180] But for years she had not dared, because guilt sealed her fingers shut. [193] She had been on the last boat out, and she had let go of her grandmother's hand in the dark water. [214] Only after forgiving yourself, the old keeper of the sanctum had told her, will the braids answer. [231] So she sat in the cold for three nights, holding the punched card scrap to her chest. [248] Above her, the aurora unrolled in slow green rivers, and the ice ticked like a cooling crucible. [265] She remembered her grandmother's laugh, the cinnamon of her coat, the way she had said Mira's name as if it were music. [287] She remembered, too, that a child's grip cannot hold a drowning woman, and that survival is not a sin. [306] On the fourth dawn, with no tears left, she finally laid the guilt down beside her like a tool she no longer needed. [329] Then her technically gentle hands rose to the rope. [338] She read the punched card scrap like a score, each hole a note, each gap a silence. [355] Following its pattern, she loosened the ancient braids and rewove them, over, under, around the icy core. [372] Her fingers bled, then numbed, then warmed with a strange snowmelt ardor, as if spring were rising inside her wrists. [392] Each new braid matched a hole in the card, and each hole was a name. [407] When the last twist locked into place, the rope shivered. [417] A low hum climbed it, gathering into the melody her grandmother had punched into wax and card a lifetime ago. [437] And the song was not only notes. [444] Woven through it, clear as struck bronze, came voices speaking the forgotten names, one after another, each a transcribed memory released from the rope. [468] Eluned. Tomas. Sefa. Old Bran the netmaker. [475] Mira spoke them back into the freezing air, and the aurora seemed to brighten, leaning down to illuminate the sanctum in trembling green. [498] The light pooled along each rewoven braid until the rope glowed like a vein of poured metal. [515] Mira understood now why a caster had been called: the rope had to be set, like bronze, so the names would never melt away again. [540] She pressed the punched card scrap into the braids' heart, where the cold would hold its shape forever. [558] The hum settled into a steady, patient resonance, a sanctum that would sing the drowned town's names to any who came. [579] Mira wept, but warmly, the snowmelt ardor still loosening something frozen in her chest. [593] She had forgiven herself, and the forgiveness had hands enough to weave a whole town back into sound. [611] Outside, the aurora kept its slow fire, and inside, the names burned gently on, unforgotten. [626]</story>
