<story>On the orchard planet of Caldra, where floating rock arches drifted above the apple rows like patient gray whales, the survivors had finally stopped calling each other strangers. [28] After strangers become family, the work changes shape, and so does the grief. [41] It was Bramwell, our altruistic ghost, who taught us that. [51] He had died tending these orchards long before our colony ship limped down through the arches, and he refused to leave the living untended. [75] We never saw him whole, only the cool blue shimmer of him at the edge of lantern light, yet he was strangely trustworthy. [98] A ghost cannot lift a crate or seal a leaking dome, so Bramwell chose a quieter method: he spoke through messages chalked onto deserted sidewalks at dawn. [125] Each morning we found instructions in his looping hand, and each morning we obeyed, because his guidance kept us alive. [145] His true motivation, we learned, was to orchestrate collective effort among people too frightened to trust one another. [163] Chalk by chalk, he stitched a hundred lonely refugees into a crew. [175] Mira mended the irrigation lines; Tomas grafted starveling saplings; old Devi catalogued which floating arches blocked the killing winds. [194] Still, surviving is not the same as living, and Bramwell knew the difference. [207] One dawn his chalk asked us to reexamine an abandoned greenhouse none of us had dared enter. [224] Inside, beneath shattered glass and a rope of dead vines, we found a butterfly garden watering bell, its brass tarnished but its tongue still bright. [249] The next message explained everything in three short lines. [258] Before the famine, Bramwell had rung that bell each evening, and the colony's first families had gathered among the milkweed to rest beneath what they called the nebula of respite. [288] It was the violet smear of stars visible only through the largest arch, a sky-window where work stopped and breath returned. [309] We had forgotten how to stop. [315] So we restored the greenhouse together, and on the appointed evening Mira rang the watering bell beneath the great arch. [335] Its small, clear note rolled out across the apple rows and up into the floating stones. [351] One by one we came, carrying lanterns, and lay in the new milkweed to watch the nebula of respite bloom violet overhead. [373] Tomas wept; Devi laughed; children chased the first butterflies hatched on Caldra in years. [387] For the length of that ringing, we were not laborers or survivors but simply a family resting under a kind sky. [408] That, I think, was the gift the ghost had been building toward all along. [422] He had never wanted only to keep us breathing; he wanted to give us a reason to. [439] The morning after that first gathering, the chalk message was shorter than ever. [452] It read only: Now you know the way; ring it without me. [464] We understood, with a grief like a thorn closing over a seed, that he was telling us goodbye. [482] A ghost who orchestrates a family no longer needs to haunt it. [494] The blue shimmer thinned at the orchard's edge, lingered once on the watering bell, and was gone. [511] We did not chalk a reply, because the deserted sidewalks were no longer deserted. [525] Now we walk them together at dawn, on our way to the orchards, then back at dusk to the milkweed. [545] Every evening someone rings the butterfly garden watering bell, and we gather beneath the floating arches to reexamine our small, mended lives. [567] The nebula of respite still blooms violet through the great arch, and in its light the brambled hope Bramwell planted keeps fruiting. [589] Strangers built this; family keeps it; and a generous ghost, somewhere among the stars, is finally at rest. [607]</story>
