<story>After the great fire, the ancient plaza lay beneath a shattered sky. [12] Embers had cooled into a grey crust, and the heavens themselves seemed cracked, as if the firmament had been struck and never mended. [35] Through this ruin walked Saira, whom the surviving councils called a gentle radical. [48] She wanted nothing less than to cradle empathy in arms of steel, to build mercy into the bones of the rebuilt city. [70] The old men feared her warmth, for it came wrapped in a cold certainty that frightened them. [87] At the plaza's heart stood a mosaic stepping stone, its tiny tiles arranged by hands dead for centuries. [105] Saira knelt before it, because she had learned a secret: via the chipped patterns on the mosaic stepping stone, one could read the past. [129] She pressed her palm to a single loose tessera, a fragment that had once crowned the minaret before the flames brought it down. [152] The minaret mosaic tessera was warm, though the day was cold. [163] When she touched it, the patterns took her, and she became retrocognitively trapped inside the moments before the fire. [182] She saw the city alive, lanterns swinging, children chasing pigeons across these very stones. [196] She saw the man who set the blaze, a frightened boy clutching an oil lamp, certain that burning the old order would free the poor. [221] Saira recognized him, for he had once been her student, a radical like herself but without her gentleness. [239] Now she was trapped in the recollection, unable to change a single ember, only to watch. [255] She felt the boy's terror as his own hands shook, and she understood the cruelty that had shaped him. [274] The vision released her, and she returned to the shattered sky gasping, the tessera cooling in her hand. [292] The councils wanted the arsonist hanged, his name struck from every record, erased into nothing. [307] Saira proposed something stranger, an aberrant mercy that no court had ever offered. [320] She would not kill the boy, nor forgive him cleanly; instead she would rename him. [335] Before the assembled survivors, she took the boy's hand and pressed it to the same stepping stone. [352] Let him be retrocognitively trapped too, she said, let him live every death he caused. [367] The boy convulsed as the chipped patterns poured the burned city into him. [380] When it ended, Saira spoke his new name aloud, calling him Keeper, no longer Destroyer. [395] The crowd murmured, unsettled, for this mercy was a sentence heavier than death. [408] He would carry every burned face inside him forever, and in carrying them, he would protect them. [425] This was Saira's steel arms cradling empathy, a love so hard it looked like punishment. [440] The warm coldness of it settled over the plaza like ash. [451] Keeper rose, weeping, and took the loose minaret mosaic tessera from Saira's palm. [464] He pressed it back into its socket on the stepping stone, mending one chip among thousands. [480] It was the first act of the rebuilding, a fragment returned, a pattern restored. [494] Saira watched him work and felt the strange satisfaction of her own creed. [507] Mercy need not be soft; it could be an architecture, a discipline, a cage built lovingly. [523] Under the shattered sky, the gentle radical began to teach the survivors her method. [537] Each of them would touch the chipped patterns and become briefly trapped in the memories of those they had wronged. [557] None would be killed, and none would be free; all would be renamed for what they must now become. [576] It was an aberrant mercy, neither vengeance nor pardon, but a third and colder grace. [591] And slowly, tessera by tessera, the broken plaza learned to hold its dead with steady, unsentimental arms. [608]</story>
