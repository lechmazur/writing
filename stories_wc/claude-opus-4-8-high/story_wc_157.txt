<story>After thoughts became taxable, the world grew quiet. [8] Every idea you let bloom in your skull was metered, assessed, and billed by the Ministry of Cognition. [26] Silas Vane, a charming rogue scholar with ink-stained cuffs and a smile he never paid taxes on, refused to surrender his spark of defiance. [50] He had founded a secret language club, a circle of dissidents who spoke in metaphors too slippery for the auditors to tally. [72] They met where the Ministry never thought to look: in Dr. Marrow's entomologist's greenhouse, among glass cases of beetles and the slow hum of pollinators. [97] The trick, Silas explained, was to be candidly cryptic. [106] Speak openly, but in a code only the club could parse, and the taxmen would find nothing taxable in your honest gibberish. [128] Their masterpiece was a method born of desperation: joining community gardens to plant message patterns into the soil itself. [147] Rows of beans, marigolds, and squash, arranged as letters readable only from the greenhouse's high catwalk, spelled defiance no ledger could itemize. [169] The keystone of the scheme was a single gourd rattle seed, the last of an old cultivar whose dried fruit shook like a coded drum. [194] Whoever held it could rattle out the club's grammar, a rhythm that told members how to read the planted rows. [214] Silas guarded that seed as one guards a flame in a windstorm. [226] Then the auditors came, and the chaos began to converge. [236] An informant inside the gardens had whispered, and three Ministry warp-wardens descended on the greenhouse at dusk, their badges glowing. [256] Warp-wardens carried devices that could warp a man's perception of time, stretching a single guilty thought into hours of billable rumination. [277] Dr. Marrow met them at the door with trembling hands, beetles scattering across her workbench. [292] Inside, Silas felt the warp-field bite, the seconds smearing like wet paint as his own mind threatened to betray him to the meter. [315] He clutched the gourd rattle seed and made a choice. [325] If they took him, the club lived only if the grammar survived, so he had to stand guard over his own spark of defiance even as the world bent around him. [356] He pressed the seed into the nearest trough of dark soil. [371] Hidden, alive, it could sprout long after his arrest and rattle again for whoever came next. [387] The wardens seized him, demanding he confess what the gardens spelled. [398] Silas grinned and gave them the truth, candidly cryptic to the end. [410] "The beans say bread, the marigolds say morning, and the squash says we are not afraid of accountants," he announced. [430] It was, of course, the literal truth, and also the unbreakable code. [442] The wardens taxed every word, but honest nonsense bills at zero. [453] Furious, they dragged him into the warping dusk, leaving Dr. Marrow alone among her beetles. [468] Months passed, and the Ministry congratulated itself on silencing the secret language club. [481] But community gardens are stubborn things, tended by hands the auditors never counted. [494] In spring, a strange vine climbed from Marrow's trough, heavy with hard-shelled gourds. [507] When the wind moved through the greenhouse, the dried fruit rattled with the old grammar, teaching the rhythm to anyone who paused to listen. [531] New gardeners learned to read the rows again, planting fresh patterns across a dozen plots that the Ministry could never wholly survey. [553] Silas never returned, but his spark had taken root, guarded now by soil and seed and seasons. [570] The chaos he had set converging did not end in arrest; it ended in harvest. [585] And every quiet rattle in the entomologist's greenhouse reminded the world that some thoughts grow cheaper, and braver, the more they are taxed. [608]</story>
