<story>After the final grain slips through the hourglass, the town of Wend goes silent, and only then does Oren lift his baton. [22] He is the leaf blower symphony conductor, a man who once led twenty roaring machines through autumn streets for applause that no longer comes. [46] Now his orchestra is one dented blower, and he wheels it nightly into the echo chamber tunnel beneath the river. [66] There the curved walls swallow sound and return it changed, softer, stranger, almost like the voices he has lost. [85] His wife laughed here once; his daughter giggled until the tunnel rang. [97] Both are gone now, taken by the same gray winter, and the house above the river has forgotten how to smile. [118] Oren's motivation is simple and impossible: to restore lost laughter to a household where only echoes remain. [135] He grips the pull cord handle, mournfully eager, and yanks. [145] The engine coughs, sputters, and dies. [151] He pulls again, and again, until the motor catches and a low wind pours from the nozzle. [168] But Oren no longer scatters leaves. [174] He has learned to play it like a reed, throttling the airflow with his thumb, shaping breath into melody. [193] Tonight he conducts himself, the lone musician, and he begins via half-sung lullabies carried on an off-key wind. [211] These were the songs his daughter loved, the ones his wife hummed while cradling her. [226] Now they leak from the machine cracked and crooked, and the tunnel drinks them down. [241] He understands what the living rarely admit: grief is the unspoken thing that must be tended, watered, and fed, or it will rot the heart from within. [268] So night after night he comes to tend to the unspoken, letting the lullabies soak the cold stone. [286] The echoes return softened, as though the tunnel chews the music, lets it macerate in darkness, and gives it back tender enough to swallow. [310] On the seventh night, the echo answers wrong. [318] His off-key wind goes out, and a tune comes back in tune. [330] It is his daughter's pitch, perfect and small, threading through the dark behind him. [344] Oren's hands shake on the handle, but he does not stop. [355] This is the true haunting he has prayed for and dreaded equally: not a ghost to fear, but a voice to finish a song with. [380] He plays the next verse; the unseen child sings the response. [391] A second voice joins, a woman's, humming the harmony she always knew. [403] The tunnel fills, and for one trembling moment the three of them make music together again. [419] Then, beneath the melody, comes a sound he has not heard in two years. [433] Laughter. [434] Small and bright, his daughter's giggle, ringing off the curved walls and rolling up the tunnel toward the river. [453] It climbs the stone steps, slips beneath the door of the silent house, and settles into the rooms like warmth returning to ash. [476] Oren weeps, still conducting, knowing the laughter is borrowed and brief. [487] When the engine finally starves and the wind dies, the voices thin, the echoes fold inward, and the silence creeps back. [508] But the house above is not the same. [516] A little of that giggle stays caught in the curtains, in the kettle's whistle, in the creak of the empty stairs. [537] Oren coils the cord, sets the pull cord handle gently against the blower, and rests his hand upon it like a man closing a beloved's eyes. [563] He will return tomorrow, after the final grain slips through the hourglass, mournfully eager to coax one more verse from the dark. [585] For the unspoken cannot be buried, only tended; and grief, when sung, becomes a kind of company. [602]</story>
