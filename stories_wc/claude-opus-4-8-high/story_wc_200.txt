<story>In the hush of a hidden sunrise, Mara descended into Echo Gorge with a crocodile's tooth strung at her throat. [20] She was the lost soul collector, the woman villagers summoned when grief refused to settle. [35] Modestly fearless, she did not boast of her gift; she simply walked toward the places others fled. [52] The gorge held a misaligned harp wedged between two stones, its strings warped by centuries of wind. [69] When she plucked it, the distorted notes did not make music; they made memory. [83] Through those crooked tones, the dead arrived, offering spectral confessions they had carried past death. [98] Mara had learned long ago that to free a soul, she must pursue its last unspoken truth. [115] The first voice belonged to a fisherman who had drowned blaming himself for a child he failed to save. [134] He wept through the harp until Mara reminded him that the child had lived, grown, and laughed. [151] At that word, laughter, the warped strings brightened, and his guilt loosened into light. [165] This was her secret method: to integrate laughter into healing, so sorrow could finally exhale. [180] The crocodile's tooth had taught her this, years before, when she was only a frightened girl. [196] A river beast had nearly taken her, yet she escaped, keeping the tooth as proof that fear could be survived. [216] Now the tooth hummed against her chest whenever a soul stood close, guiding her through the gorge's gloom. [234] One after another the confessions came, each a wound she met with patience and, gently, with humor. [251] A miser confessed he had hoarded coins yet died craving company; she laughed softly and asked who would inherit his loneliness. [272] The question startled him, and his bitterness cracked into a thin, surprised chuckle. [285] With that chuckle he dissolved, lighter than the dust he left behind. [297] But the deepest voice waited until the sky paled, a girl who had died ashamed of being afraid. [315] She confessed she had run from a flood instead of helping her brother, and the shame had chained her here for decades. [337] Mara recognized the gorge's oldest sorrow, and she pressed the crocodile's tooth into the girl's spectral palm. [354] Feel this, she said; fear is not failure, it is the body begging you to live. [370] I survived a monster by being afraid and clever both, and I have laughed every day since. [387] The girl trembled, then asked, haltingly, whether her brother had hated her for fleeing. [401] Mara plucked the misaligned harp, and through its distorted notes the brother's own voice answered from the far side of death. [422] He said he had always remembered her laugh, the one bright thing in a drowning world. [438] The girl let out a sound that was half sob, half giggle, and the chains of decades simply unwound. [457] In that luminous ache, sorrow and joy braided into something whole, and she rose like morning mist. [474] The hidden sunrise finally breached the gorge's lip, gilding the warped strings into gold. [488] Mara gathered the tooth back to her throat, its hum gone quiet now that the gorge held no captive souls. [508] She had not banished the dead so much as reminded them that healing requires both the wound and the smile. [528] As a lost soul collector, she carried no jar, only the patience to pursue every silenced truth until it spoke. [548] Climbing toward the light, she hummed the harp's crooked melody, no longer distorted but simply honest. [564] Echo Gorge answered with her own voice, layered into a chorus of the freed, and she let herself laugh too. [584] The modestly fearless woman knew tomorrow would bring another gorge, another grief, another tooth-hum at her chest. [601] For now, in the hush of the risen sun, she rested in the certainty that even the dead could be taught to smile. [624]</story>
