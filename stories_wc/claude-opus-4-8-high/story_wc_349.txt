<story>The clean slate vendor opened her shop during the lull on its very first opening day. [16] Her store hung inside the glass orchard library, a place floating upon a swirling sea of clouds. [33] Here, trees grew transparent fruit, and each fruit held a book that had never been read. [49] She sold erasure, the gentle removal of regrets, and her name suited her trade perfectly. [64] Customers came carrying their guilt, and she wiped them blank, leaving them lighter but oddly hollow. [80] During the morning lull, no one arrived, and she felt the silence sharpen into longing. [95] On her counter sat a single object she had never been able to sell: bottled moonlight. [111] The light inside moved like liquid memory, mindfully scattered across the curved glass. [124] She had purchased it from a sailor who claimed it was distilled from the first night ever told. [142] That phrase haunted her, because she wanted, above everything, to find the place where stories begin. [158] Her whole craft was an inversion of that wish, for she ended stories rather than starting them. [175] Erasing others, she had grown fragmented herself, a woman stitched from the blanks she gave away. [191] She uncorked the bottle, hoping the moonlight might show her the origin she craved. [205] Instead, it whispered a riddle: the beginning could be located only by quantifying absence. [219] Count what is missing, the voice said, and the negative shape will draw the door. [234] So she began measuring all the erasures she had performed, every regret subtracted from the world. [250] The vendor tallied each blank slate, each hollowed customer, summing absence into a glowing total. [265] As the number grew, a seam appeared in the air, faint and trembling with light. [280] She raised a glass blade and prepared to incise the seam, to cut open the threshold. [296] The cut released a glimmering parole, a luminous promise that the imprisoned beginnings could go free. [312] Through the opening she saw the place where stories begin, raw and unwritten. [325] It was not a single room but countless fragments, drifting yet somehow whole. [338] Every shard reflected another, broken pieces that fit together into one breathing tale. [351] She understood then that her erasures had not destroyed stories but returned them here, to be reborn. [368] The regrets she had taken were the raw material of every new beginning. [381] Her hollow customers would, somewhere, become protagonists again, fuller for having been emptied. [394] The bottled moonlight dimmed, its purpose spent, its riddle answered. [404] She stepped toward the threshold, intending to dwell among origins forever. [415] But the glimmering parole carried a condition, as all paroles do. [426] To remain there, she would have to surrender her own story, becoming a beginning for someone else. [443] She paused on the orchard's glass floor, clouds churning silver beneath her feet. [456] The first opening day had brought her not a customer but a choice. [469] She thought of her own buried regrets, the ones no vendor had ever erased. [483] Perhaps, she realized, she had become a clean slate vendor precisely to avoid her own beginning. [499] Standing at the seam she had cut open, she finally faced it. [511] She would not erase herself; instead she would write. [520] Closing the bottle, she let the seam seal, the door shrinking to a freckle of light. [536] She kept her absences as inventory, not as wounds, ready to sell them as new chapters. [552] When the lull ended, a customer climbed the orchard stairs, carrying a heavy regret. [566] The vendor smiled, no longer erasing but transplanting, sending each sorrow toward its rebirth. [580] Her shop, glass and floating, had found its true trade at last. [592] She had located the place where stories begin not by leaving, but by becoming its keeper. [608]</story>
