<story>After the substitute body was found beneath the orchard, the village finally believed what I had spent my whole life denying. [21] I am a forgiving skeptic, a bone-setter by trade, and I had always insisted that changelings belonged only in the stories told to frighten careless mothers. [47] My hands knew bone setting the way other men knew prayer, easing fractured wrists and crooked fingers back toward wholeness. [67] So when the Aldous child died and a colder, stranger infant was left in his cradle, I called it grief, not magic. [89] The substitute body changed everything; it was carved of root and wax, a perfect mimic that finally crumbled in the autumn damp. [111] Standing over it, I felt a cleft in certainty open inside me, a crack where every confident assumption I owned drained away. [133] The real boy, if there was one, had been taken, and the woman who knew where lived behind the manor's old hedge maze. [156] I walked its green corridors at dusk, turning blind corners until I reached a secret garden hidden behind the maze's final wall. [178] There an old woman sat among silvered herbs, her face shrewdly innocent, the look of someone who has lied kindly for centuries. [200] "You've come for the Aldous boy," she said, before I spoke a word. [213] From a pouch she drew a changeling's baby tooth, small and pearl-bright, humming faintly like a struck bell. [231] "This is the toll," she explained, "a tooth for a tooth, a child for a child; the fair folk trade in symmetry." [253] I, the man who mended bones, asked how a body could be unbroken when so much was missing. [271] She placed the tooth in my palm and told me the boy lived, but the parents must choose to want him still. [293] "Most fear the changed thing they raised," she murmured; "few can yearn for the one they barely knew." [311] I carried the tooth back through the maze, my forgiving nature warring with the skeptic who still demanded proof. [330] At the Aldous cottage I laid the tooth on the table and watched the mother's grief shift into a terrible, fragile hope. [352] We made our choice together, speaking the boy's name aloud until the tooth cracked and a warm wind answered. [371] By dawn the real child slept in the cradle, thin and pale, his small arm bent at a wrong angle. [391] This, at last, was something I understood, and I knelt to the patient work of bone setting his fragile wrist. [411] As I worked I understood the old woman's true craft: she did not erase mistakes but rearranged them into something one could bear to look upon. [437] She had taught me, without a single lecture, to turn mistakes into constellations. [450] My years of stubborn denial were not failures to be ashamed of, but dim points of light waiting to be connected into meaning. [473] The boy healed slowly, and I returned often to that secret garden, no longer to demand answers but to learn. [493] There the old woman and I traded older remedies for newer ideas, a nostalgic innovation blooming between us like the herbs at our feet. [517] We grafted forgotten lore onto my mending splints, and bound broken things with both bandage and belief. [534] The cleft in certainty never fully closed, and I learned to be grateful for it. [549] A man too sure of the world cannot set what he refuses to see is broken. [565] On clear nights I lift the boy to the window and show him the stars, naming each one for an error survived.
