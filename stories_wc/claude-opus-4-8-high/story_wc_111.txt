<story>After luck became measurable, the kingdom traded prayers for ledgers. [10] Each citizen carried a number, and mine had withered to nothing. [21] I was the withered vine dresser of the royal garden maze, hired in greener years to coax leaves along its endless walls. [43] Now my hands trembled, and the auditors said a man's luck should match his garden's bloom. [59] Mine did not. [62] The hedges browned where I touched them, and the measurers wrote my luck at zero. [77] A dresser with zero luck would be dismissed, and a dismissed man with zero luck would simply vanish from the ledgers. [98] So I did the only wisely pragmatic thing I could imagine. [109] I cheated. [111] I owned a collection of marbles, cracked and clouded, left by my father who once tended these same paths. [130] Luck, the auditors taught, could be stored in glass and counted by its shine. [144] So each night I would polish those broken spheres until their fractures caught the moon and glowed like fortune itself. [164] Then I buried them along the maze's roots, hoping the inspectors would read their false glimmer as luck risen from the soil. [186] It was a deception, but a small one, planted to buy a withering man a little more spring. [204] The strange thing was that the vines began to climb again. [215] Where the cracked marbles lay, green crept upward, blooming over the very places that had withered under my doubt. [234] I told myself the glass warmed the roots, nothing more. [244] But I began commemorating small victories elaborately, the way a frightened man builds rituals against the dark. [261] For every new leaf I hung a ribbon, lit a candle, and recited the names of gardeners long buried beneath these hedges. [283] The maze, I swear, leaned toward the sound. [291] When the chief inspector arrived, she measured the soil and frowned at her glowing instruments. [306] Your luck reads the highest in the kingdom, she said, and yet you look like a man the frost forgot to finish. [328] I almost confessed the buried marbles then, the whole mended deception ready on my tongue. [343] But she knelt, dug, and lifted one cracked sphere into the light. [355] This is dead glass, she murmured; it holds no luck at all. [367] Then the garden did this on its own. [375] And there it was, the proof I had secretly hungered for: that gardens know their gardeners. [391] My deception had not made the luck; it had only mended my faith long enough for the maze to answer. [411] The hedges had recognized the hands that once polished marbles at a father's knee, and they rose to vouch for me. [432] The inspector studied my withered face and her measurable numbers, weighing which to believe. [446] Numbers can lie when the soil tells truth, she finally said, closing her ledger. [460] She let the high reading stand, choosing the garden's verdict over the glass. [473] That night I performed my grandest ceremony yet, commemorating this small victory with a hundred candles winding through the green corridors. [494] I dug up every cracked marble, washed each one, and gave them not to the soil but to the inspector as a gift. [517] Honest luck, I told her, needs no buried glass. [526] She smiled, and the maze rustled as though smiling too. [536] I am still the withered vine dresser, blooming and withering in the same breath, as all gardeners must. [554] But I no longer fear the ledgers, for I have proof that cannot be measured in shine. [571] The maze knows my hands. [576] When I am gone, it will brown for a season, then send up green for whoever comes next, remembering. [595] That, more than any number, is the only luck I ever needed. [607]</story>
