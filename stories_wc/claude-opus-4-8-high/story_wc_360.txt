<story>Lio ran the only letterpress in the topaz workshop, a glass-roofed room that glowed amber under stars. [17] He spoke in riddles, a cryptic man who answered questions with questions, smiling through deliberate ambiguity. [33] Customers came to him to disorient themselves, to lose the hard edges of their own minds. [49] Lio habitually borrowed personalities, slipping into a printed voice the way other men slipped into coats. [65] Tonight he wore the temper of a grieving widow he had once set into type, and his hands trembled with her borrowed sorrow. [88] On the press bed lay a halo fragment, a sliver of luminous metal he had bought from a falling-star peddler. [108] He believed it could print emotion directly, ink made of feeling pressed cleanly onto paper. [123] His true motivation was older than any commission: to understand the mathematics of emotion, the equations beneath every ache. [142] He worked only in the timeframe between vanishing stars, those few minutes when one constellation dimmed and the next had not yet risen. [165] In that gap, he said, feelings stood still enough to be measured. [177] A girl named Sera arrived that night carrying grief she could not name. [190] Her brother had died, and she felt nothing, a numbness that frightened her more than any wound. [207] She asked Lio to make her grieve, to print sorrow into her hollow chest. [221] Lio set the halo fragment against the platen and inked it with the widow's borrowed grief. [237] He explained, in his maddening way, that the press did not give feeling but exchanged it. [253] This was transposed empathy: he would take her numbness and she would take his borrowed sorrow, the two swapped across the threshold of the page. [278] Sera hesitated, unsure whether such a trade could be honest. [288] Lio answered only with a shrug and a phrase that meant both yes and no. [303] His deliberate ambiguity was meant to disorient her, to loosen the certainty that kept her feelings frozen. [320] When she finally nodded, he pulled the lever. [328] The halo fragment flared, and the workshop tilted into lucid turbulence, every lamp swaying though no wind moved. [346] Sera felt the numbness lift from her like fog peeled off a lake. [359] In its place came grief, sudden and clean, and she wept for her brother at last. [375] But Lio staggered, for the numbness he received was not empty after all. [388] It carried the precise shape of Sera's loss, a number he had hunted his whole life. [404] For one suspended moment, between two vanishing stars, he saw the mathematics of emotion entire. [419] Grief, he understood, was not subtraction but transfer, conserved like heat, never destroyed, only moved. [434] Every borrowed personality he had ever worn had been a clumsy proof of this single equation. [450] He had spent years disguising himself in others because he had no feelings he could call his own. [468] Now, holding Sera's borrowed numbness, he finally felt something unmistakably his. [479] The cruel arithmetic was plain: to feel his own emptiness, he had to take hers. [494] Sera wiped her eyes and asked if he was hurt. [504] He gave his usual ambiguous smile, but for the first time it trembled with real meaning. [520] The stars returned overhead, and the topaz light steadied into ordinary gold. [532] Sera left with her sorrow held like a gift, grateful to feel anything at all. [547] Lio stayed behind with the cooling halo fragment, studying the new void inside him. [561] He no longer needed to borrow a personality, because the numbness was authentically, painfully his. [576] He set new type in the dark, spelling a single equation no one else could read. [592] It said, in his cryptic shorthand, that empathy is only borrowing made permanent. [605] And under the returning stars, he learned to carry it alone. [616]</story>
