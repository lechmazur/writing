<story>Mira had pruned orchards for sixty seasons, and every tree called her wise. [13] She worked upon the hush of budding hope, when the first blossoms trembled like a held breath. [30] Her ladder leaned against a verdant parapet beside a waterfall, where the mist rose and the two worlds pressed thin against each other. [53] On this side bloomed the orchard of waking; on the other, the orchard of dream, its colors deeper than any waking eye could hold. [77] For years Mira had practiced the quiet art of weaving between worlds, slipping her shears through the seam the falling water made. [99] She did this for one reason: to preserve the color of wonder, which was fading from the waking trees. [118] Each spring the blossoms grew paler, as though the world were forgetting how to be astonished. [134] Mira, insatiably creative, refused to let that gray come. [143] She believed that wonder could be carried, like pollen, if only one knew the method. [158] The method was simple and impossible: by capturing essence. [167] She would stand at the parapet, breathe the radiant hush of the dream orchard, and gather its impossible blues and golds into a single thing she could plant. [195] That thing was an heirloom seed, passed to her by the pruner before her, who had received it from the pruner before that. [218] It was the only seed that could grow in both worlds at once, its roots drinking dream and its branches breathing waking air. [241] But the seed had never sprouted, for it required wonder pure enough to wake it. [256] This spring, the waking orchard was dying. [263] The children below no longer looked up; the blossoms had turned the color of old paper. [279] Mira understood that the world needed succor, and that she was the last who could offer it. [296] So she climbed the parapet as the waterfall roared its silver hymn. [308] She leaned into the seam between worlds, and the mist parted like a curtain. [322] Beyond it, the dream orchard burned with colors that had no names, hues that made her old heart ache with joy. [343] She did not pluck a blossom, for plucked things die. [353] Instead she opened her hands and simply watched, letting the wonder pour into her until she brimmed. [370] This was capturing essence: not taking, but receiving so deeply that the thing left its imprint upon you. [388] When she could hold no more, Mira pressed the heirloom seed against her breastbone, where the gathered color glowed. [407] She felt the seed drink, felt it shudder, felt a small green wish uncurl within its shell. [424] Then the radiant hush broke, gently, like dawn arriving. [433] She drew back through the seam, trembling, and the waterfall closed behind her. [446] On the verdant parapet she knelt and pushed the seed into the warm earth between the two orchards. [464] For a long moment nothing happened, and the old pruner feared she had given too little. [480] Then a single shoot rose, and upon its tip swelled a bud the color of every wonder she had ever known. [501] It opened. [503] Light spilled from it, not blinding but kind, the way a grandmother's story spills warmth. [518] The pale orchard drank that light, and one by one its tired blossoms blushed back into brilliance. [535] Below, a child looked up at last and gasped. [544] Others followed, their faces lifting like flowers turning to sun. [554] Mira leaned on her shears and wept, for the color of wonder had been preserved, and the waking world would dream again. [576] The tree she had planted grew tall between the worlds, its roots a bridge, its branches a loom. [594] Each season it would weave a little dream into every waking thing, so no one need climb the dangerous seam again. [615] Mira pruned it once, tenderly, and named it Succor. [624] When the next pruner came, she pressed a new seed, born of the first, into those young and trembling hands. [644] "Wonder is not kept by holding," she said, "but by giving it somewhere to grow." [659] Then she climbed down for the last time, content, into the radiant hush of evening. [674]</story>
