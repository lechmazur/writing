<story>Mara called herself an esoteric realist, a woman who believed the unseen still obeyed rules. [15] She lived in a verdant grotto above the clouds, where ferns dripped light and the air held a blushing stillness. [35] Here she had come to honor overlooked histories, the names erased from ledgers and the songs no one finished singing. [55] Her method was simple and strange: she built temporary structures as signals, lattices of reed and stone meant to be read once and then surrendered. [80] Each was transiently rooted, anchored only long enough to catch a traveler's eye before the wind unmade it. [98] The travelers she awaited were butterflies. [104] It was the season of the migration of the last monarch butterfly, a single orange ember threading the sky where millions once flew. [127] Mara wanted to mark its passage, because a story that ends without witness is the loneliest kind. [144] She kept one fragile relic in her palm: a moth wing, grey and powdered, salvaged from an earlier extinction no almanac recorded. [166] To her the wing was proof that vanished things deserved a cadence of remembrance, even if the world had moved on. [187] For three dawns she labored, lashing reeds into a slender spire above the grotto's mouth. [202] At the spire's crown she tied a careful knot, binding the moth wing so it would flicker like a tiny grey flag. [224] The signal said, in the only language she trusted, here someone remembers. [236] Then she waited inside the blushing stillness, counting her own breath like a metronome. [250] On the fourth morning the monarch came. [257] It rose through the cloud floor, wings beating a steady rhythm, an ancient measure repeated across a thousand generations. [276] Mara held still, afraid that any motion might frighten the last of its line. [290] The butterfly saw the fluttering moth wing and paused mid-flight, an interrupted cadence in the long song of its journey. [310] For one suspended moment the two relics regarded each other, one grey and gone, one orange and going. [328] Mara understood, in that hush, that her structures were never meant to last. [341] They were rests in a melody, deliberate silences that made the surrounding notes audible. [355] An esoteric realist accepts that meaning often lives in the gap, not the gesture. [369] The monarch resumed its beat, lifting past the spire, and Mara felt the interruption become a kind of blessing. [388] She had given the creature a moment of recognition, and the creature had given her proof that her vigil mattered. [408] The wind, faithful to its nature, began at once to loosen the reeds. [421] Mara did not mend the knot or shore the spire. [431] Permanence was never the point; the point was to be transiently rooted long enough to say a true thing. [450] By dusk the structure had collapsed into the grotto's moss, and the moth wing drifted free. [466] She let it go, releasing one overlooked history back to the air it came from. [481] The grotto held its blushing stillness, greener now for having hosted a farewell. [494] Mara sat among the fallen reeds and let herself remember everything that had ever ended unwitnessed. [510] She thought of forgotten languages, drowned villages, and species whose only monument was her stubborn attention. [526] Tomorrow she would gather new reeds and tie a new knot, building another signal for whatever passed next. [544] The work would vanish again, as all her work did, and that was its quiet honesty. [560] Above the clouds, where few would ever climb, an esoteric realist kept the oldest promise of all. [577] She promised that nothing would pass entirely unseen, not while she could still build, watch, and let go. [595] And so the migration ended, but the remembering did not. [605]</story>
