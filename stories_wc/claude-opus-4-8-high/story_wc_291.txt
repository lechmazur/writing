<story>Mara could not recognize faces, but she remembered everything else about a person. [13] She was a portrait artist who worked for the police, sketching the dead from cold cases nobody else would touch. [33] Her face blindness made her very methodical, for she could trust no glance, only evidence. [48] This morning the detective called her about a girl who vanished from a masquerade twenty years ago. [65] All that remained was a single masquerade mask ribbon fragment, frayed silk the color of bruised plum. [82] They met at the old place where the trail had gone silent: a shadowed quarry in mist. [99] The detective spoke in a furrowed hush, as if the fog itself might overhear and flee. [115] He confessed the worst part: after the evidence disappeared from the locked archive, the case was officially dead. [133] Only the ribbon survived, smuggled out in his coat the night before the theft. [147] Mara understood why he chose her now: she alone reconstructed people without faces. [160] She knelt on the wet stone and laid the fragment across her palm like a relic. [176] Methodical as always, she catalogued each thread, each knot, each faded thread of dye. [190] The ribbon had been tied by two different hands, one left, one right, knotted together. [205] A mask, she realized, joined by unifying opposites, sewn so the wearer became two souls at once. [222] The girl had not worn it alone; she had shared it with the one who took her. [239] Mara began to sketch, not a face, but two pairs of hands meeting at a single ribbon. [256] The detective watched, and his breath caught when he saw his own knuckles in the drawing. [272] She did not know his face, yet she had drawn his hands a hundred times in old reports. [290] He was the one who had made the evidence disappear, because the ribbon named him. [305] The quarry held its breath, the mist pressing close like a witness afraid to speak. [320] He had brought her here to confess, not to silence her, for his guilt had grown teeth. [337] Twenty years he had felt nothing; now he wished only to awaken dormant empathy long buried. [353] He told her the girl had been his sister's friend, and the masquerade a dare gone cruel. [370] They had tied the ribbon to seal a secret, then he had let the secret rot. [386] Mara felt the old anger lance through her, sharp and bright as a surgeon's blade. [401] Yet she stayed methodical, asking where the girl now lay beneath this drowned and hollow ground. [417] He pointed to the flooded pit, where the water held its secret in black stillness. [432] She drew the girl at last, not her face, which Mara could never hold, but her hands. [449] Small hands, half a ribbon clutched, the matching half still warm in the detective's coat. [464] Two fragments, two people, rejoined on her page into one whole and undeniable truth. [478] The drawing was new evidence, born after the old evidence vanished, and it could not be stolen. [495] It lived in her hands, and her hands forgot nothing, even what her eyes could not see. [512] The detective wept then, the first true tears in two decades of practiced numbness. [526] His dormant empathy had finally stirred, awakened by the sight of hands he had failed. [541] He placed his ribbon fragment beside hers, completing the mask that had hidden a murder. [556] Mara photographed everything, methodical to the end, before the mist could swallow proof again. [570] Then she led him up from the shadowed quarry toward the waiting lights of the law. [586] She could not picture his face for the judge, but she would never need to. [601] The hands had confessed, and the cold case, at last, had grown warm. [614]</story>
