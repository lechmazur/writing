<story>They called Veld a lukewarm mage, and he never argued the point. [12] His spells warmed soup, dried laundry, and occasionally nudged a stubborn door, but they had never once bent the world the way real sorcerers bent it. [38] Still, he believed, earnestly and naively, that small magics could mend large wounds. [51] The wound he meant was the sky. [58] For a hundred years the river had thinned, the harbor had emptied, and the priests had warned that a cosmic imbalance was draining the heavens like a cracked cup. [87] When the last fish was caught from the dying estuary, hauled up silver and gasping, Veld decided someone earnest had to act. [109] He carried with him a broken ship's bell, dredged from the silt, its bronze split clean down one side. [128] An old fisherwoman had told him the bell once rang the tides into balance, summoning water as faithfully as a hound. [149] Its devotion, she said, had grown brittle, and brittle devotion always breaks at the worst hour. [165] So Veld set out to rebuild it. [172] He climbed to the sapphire viaduct at dawn, where the great aqueduct arched blue against a paling sky, its stones humming with the last trickle of the world's water. [201] Here, he reasoned, magic and water met, and here a mended bell might ring loud enough to be heard above. [221] But Veld could not forge bronze; his fire was too gentle, his hands too cold. [236] What he could do was understand dripping rates. [244] He had spent his lukewarm years measuring how water fell from leaky roofs and slow taps, learning the exact rhythm at which a drop gathers, swells, and lets go. [273] Now he set the cracked bell beneath a single seam in the viaduct, where one bead of water still wept through the sapphire stone. [297] If a drop fell too fast, it shattered against the crack; too slow, and it dried before its work was done. [318] But at the perfect rate, Veld knew, mineral water would leave behind a thread of stone, layer upon patient layer, until the split sealed itself. [343] It was, he admitted, an impossible plan, the kind that should not work. [356] Yet he tuned the drip with whispered spells, slowing each bead, warming it just enough to carry its dissolved stone, and the impossible began to look merely difficult. [384] Drop by drop, dawn by dawn, he returned to the viaduct. [395] Other mages laughed at the lukewarm man kneeling beside a broken bell, counting water like a miser counting coins. [414] He let them laugh, for his devotion was not brittle; it was slow, and slow things do not crack. [433] Months passed, and the seam in the bronze narrowed to a line, then to a scar, then to nothing at all. [454] On the morning the last drop closed the wound, the bell trembled, full and whole. [469] Veld struck it once with a knuckle, and a clear note rolled out across the sapphire viaduct, climbing the dawn like a returning tide. [493] The sky shivered. [496] Far off, clouds that had not gathered in a hundred years began to thicken, drawn back toward the balance the bell had once kept. [520] Rain fell that evening, thin at first, then steady, filling the cracked cup of the heavens one patient drop at a time. [542] Veld did not feel powerful, only useful, which he had always thought were the same thing. [558] He left the mended bell hanging on the viaduct, where it would ring with every dawn, righting the cosmic imbalance one tide at a time. [583] And the rivers, in their own slow and earnest way, began to remember how to be full. [600] Somewhere downstream, in the empty estuary, the water rose to meet a fisherman's net once more.
