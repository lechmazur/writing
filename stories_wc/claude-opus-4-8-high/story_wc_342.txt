<story>Mara had painted a thousand faces and remembered none. [9] Faces dissolved for her the instant she looked away, smearing into fog, which was why the guild had summoned a face blind portrait artist for this final, impossible commission. [38] The commission stood inside a tree trunk library hollowed by a scholar's guild, an ancient oak whose chambers spiraled upward like the whorls of a fingerprint. [64] Tomorrow the dead oak would fall. [70] City engineers had condemned the rotting trunk, and Mara worked at the hush just before a condemned building is demolished, that suspended breath when everything waits to become rubble. [99] A heightened stillness pressed against the bark walls, so complete that she could hear sawdust settling. [115] Her task was strange: to craft the portrait of the guild's founder, a woman dead three centuries, whose only likeness survived through faint indentations on a centuries-old coin. [143] The coin lay in a secret book safe carved into the oak's heart, a hollow disguised as a leather volume, its pages fused into a lid. [169] Mara opened it and lifted the coin to the lantern. [179] Because faces meant nothing to her, she had learned to read by touch instead, tracing edges the way others read words. [200] Her fingertips moved across the metal, and the worn profile resolved not as a face but as a landscape of ridges, each one fractally precise. [225] A brow became a ridgeline; a cheek became a valley repeated in smaller valleys within. [240] The guild elders had warned her of the founder's last wish, a vow that the artist must honor. [258] The founder had wanted, above all, to mirror the sky inside walls, to bring the open heavens into the choked dark of a hollow tree. [283] Mara understood the wish only as instruction, never as feeling, for she had never been able to hold a face long enough to love it. [308] She began to work. [312] She mixed pigment from oak gall and lapis, and she painted what her fingers had told her, ridge by ridge, valley by valley. [335] Hours folded into the stillness. [340] As the portrait grew, Mara confronted the core concept she had spent her life avoiding: she was discovering limits, the hard boundary of what touch could and could not give. [370] She could map every contour, yet she could not feel whether the woman had been kind or cruel. [388] The face would always remain, for Mara, a country she could survey but never inhabit. [403] Then she noticed something the coin had hidden in plain sight. [414] The indentations did not form a portrait at all. [423] Read by touch, ordered by depth, the ridges were a map of constellations, the founder's own night sky pressed into silver. [444] The founder had encoded heaven onto her own coin, knowing that one day someone who saw with hands instead of eyes would find it. [468] Here, finally, was how to mirror the sky inside walls. [478] Mara abandoned the portrait. [482] Working swiftly against the dawn, she pierced the inner bark with an awl, placing each star where the coin commanded, fractally precise, the small holes echoing larger holes in a pattern that deepened the longer one looked. [519] When she set the lantern at the chamber's center, light spilled through the punctures and threw a galaxy across the curved walls. [541] The dead tree held the heavens at last. [549] Outside, the demolition crew waited in the gray light, their machines silent for one more held breath. [566] Mara could not see their faces, and she knew she never would, but she no longer mourned that limit. [585] She had spent her life believing her blindness was a wall. [596] Now she understood it had only ever been a window. [606] She slipped the coin back into the secret book safe and closed the lid, leaving the founder's true portrait blazing on the walls. [629] Then, in the hush just before the condemned building is demolished, Mara stepped out into the morning, carrying the only face she would ever truly know: a sky. [657]</story>
