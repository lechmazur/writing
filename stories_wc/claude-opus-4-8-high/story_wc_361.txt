<story>Mira had carried relics along the aurora-lit ridgeway for nine years, and her hands still shook with every delivery. [19] The jittery relic courier blamed the cold, but she knew it was something older than cold. [35] Her mentor, Oren, had vanished a year ago, leaving only a single instruction scratched into a delivery ledger: coax wonder from the routine. [58] She had read those words so often that they had worn smooth in her mind. [73] Tonight her cargo was a dragonfly-wing pendant, sealed in a velvet pouch and bound for a collector at the ridge's far end. [95] The aurora rolled overhead in green and violet ribbons, painting the snow with shifting light. [110] Mira walked, counting her steps to steady her nerves, until a traveler appeared from a fold in the rock. [129] He was old, wrapped in furs, and he watched the lights with an open hunger. [144] What is that you carry, he asked, that hums against the sky? [156] Mira had not noticed any sound, but when she lifted the pouch, the pendant was indeed trembling. [173] It was answering the aurora by vibrating sympathetically, matching the silent rhythm of the curtains of light. [190] She drew it out, and the wing-thin glass shivered like a living thing in her palm. [206] The old man stepped closer, and Mira felt the familiar urge to rush onward, to keep the encounter short. [225] Instead she remembered Oren, who had a gift for making strangers linger. [237] He could extend brief encounters into whole evenings simply by asking the right question. [251] So Mira asked, where are you walking, on a night like this? [263] The traveler smiled and told her of a village that no longer appeared on any map. [279] He spoke of a craftsman there who had once shaped wings of glass, hoping each one would germinate into a story. [300] Mira's breath caught, for that was precisely how Oren had spoken of his own craft. [315] The pendant in her hand grew warmer, its vibration deepening as the old man's voice rose and fell. [333] She realized the encounter was stretching, minute by minute, the way her mentor's always had. [348] Tell me about the craftsman, she said, and the old man's eyes shone. [361] He vanished into stories of vanished places, he said, until people forgot whether he was a man or a rumor. [381] That is the fate of every maker, he added, to travel across the fade from memory to myth. [399] Mira understood then that Oren had not abandoned her, but had simply walked into that fade himself. [416] His instruction had never been about the relics, but about the people who held them for a moment along the way. [437] She had spent nine years delivering wonder without ever pausing to feel it. [450] The aurora pulsed, and the pendant answered, and for once Mira did not feel jittery at all. [467] She and the old man stood together for an hour, watching the glass shiver in time with the heavens. [486] When at last he turned to leave, Mira asked his name, half-afraid of the answer. [501] He only smiled and said that names were the first thing to fade. [514] Then he was gone, swallowed by the dark between the curtains of light. [527] Mira finished her route as dawn bleached the aurora from the sky. [539] She delivered the dragonfly-wing pendant to the collector, who paid her and asked if anything interesting had happened on the ridge. [560] Mira smiled, and instead of hurrying away, she stayed to tell him the whole story. [575] She had learned, at last, how to coax wonder from the routine, and how to let a brief encounter germinate into something that would outlast her. [601]</story>
