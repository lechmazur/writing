<story>After the plague ended, the city of Velmaris still lay drowned, its towers slumped beneath green water like the spines of dead leviathans. [23] I lived below it, in the sewer, where the philosophers had once kept nothing and I now kept everything. [42] They called me a wannabe philosopher, and they were not wrong, though I preferred the word student. [59] My teacher had died of the sickness, leaving me his abrasive calm and one instruction: preserve the ancient methods. [78] So I worked, daily, through sedimentary layers of silt and ash that the floods had pressed over the sunken city ruins. [99] Each layer told a different story, and that was the trouble. [110] Competing narratives lay stacked like pages: one stratum insisted the drowning was punishment, another swore it was accident, and a third said nothing at all, which was its own argument. [140] I trusted none of them, because I had been taught to be rationally rebellious, to doubt the loudest voice precisely because it was loud. [164] On the day everything changed, I dug deeper than usual and my trowel struck metal. [179] It was a seafarer's chronometer cog, small as a coin, its teeth still bright beneath the grime. [196] I cleaned it in the brown trickle of the sewer and held it to the lamp. [212] Such a cog belonged to the great clocks the old navigators used to find their place at sea, and that meant Velmaris had not been a city of the doomed, but a city of measurers. [247] They had kept time, charted stars, trusted method over prophecy. [257] The punishment narrative crumbled in my hand. [264] I began to agonize over what I had found. [273] If the drowned had been rational people who measured the world honestly, then their methods had not saved them, and method itself seemed mocked by the water. [300] Why preserve ancient methods that had failed their own makers? [310] I sat with the cog for three nights, the abrasive calm of my teacher arguing inside me against my grief. [330] Then I understood the difference between a method and a promise. [341] A promise says you will be spared; a method only says how to look, how to measure, how to ask again when the answer fails. [366] The navigators had not been promised survival, only the means to know where they stood, and that knowledge was worth keeping even after the ship went down. [393] After the plague ended, the survivors above had begun building new myths, and they came down sometimes to ask which story I believed. [416] They wanted a verdict, a single clean narrative to comfort them. [427] I gave them none. [431] Instead I showed them the cog and taught them to read the sedimentary layers themselves, how each band of silt held a competing claim and how to weigh them against one another. [463] This was my rebellion, calm and reasoned: not to hand them a faith, but a way of doubting faiths. [482] Some left angry, preferring the comfort of punishment or the comfort of accident. [495] A few stayed, and those few began to measure. [504] I kept the chronometer cog on a string around my neck, a small wheel that had once helped someone find north. [525] It could no longer turn any clock, but it could still turn a mind. [539] I was no philosopher yet, only a wannabe in a sewer beneath a drowned world. [554] But I had learned what my teacher meant by preserving the ancient methods. [567] You do not save the answers, which rot like everything else. [578] You save the patient, rebellious habit of asking, of digging through one stubborn layer after another until the loud story falls silent and the quiet evidence speaks. [605] That is the only city worth raising from the water. [615]</story>
