<story>On the island of Semaya, my title was Reality Checker, and I wore it like an apology. [17] My duty was empirically whimsical: to measure marvels, weigh rumors, and decide which of our wonders were true enough to keep. [38] I tested floating lanterns with string and skepticism, and I cataloged ghosts by their failure to chill my tea. [57] Then the elders summoned me to the ancient ridge in bloom, where a single old tree had begun to die. [77] It was the Remembering Tree, and within its last fruit lay a knowledge fruit seed that held our collective memory. [97] Every story Semaya had ever told slept inside that seed, and if it withered, our past would vanish before the monsoon arrived. [119] The eldest woman pressed the seed into my palm and said, 'You must waken it.' [134] I almost laughed, for a seed cannot be argued awake. [144] But she explained the old method: the seed could only waken through interpreted dreams, sung to it by someone who believed. [165] That, of course, was the cruelty of choosing me, the one islander trained to doubt. [180] My motivation, though, was simpler than belief: I wanted to protect our cultural memory, even the parts I could not prove. [201] So I slept on the ridge among the blossoms, the seed cupped against my heart. [216] The first night, I dreamed of a fisherman who counted waves until they counted him. [231] I woke and wrote what it meant: that measuring the sea does not make you its master. [248] The seed grew warm, as if listening. [255] The second night, I dreamed of twin moons quarreling over which was the reflection. [269] I interpreted it as the truce between fact and faith, and the seed split its husk a finger's width. [288] Each interpretation I dared to offer felt like a small, luminous doubt, a candle I was not certain I had lit. [309] On the third night, the clouds thickened, and I could smell the monsoon leaning against the horizon. [326] I dreamed of a child who asked every elder the same question and stitched their answers into a single quilt. [346] When I woke, I understood: collective memory is not one truth but many remembered together. [361] I had spent my life dividing the real from the imagined, but the seed needed both braided into one cord. [381] Yet doubt clutched me, for what if my interpretations were merely my own inventions? [395] What if I was not reading the dreams but writing them? [406] Then the first raindrop struck the seed, and I had no time left to be sure. [422] I knelt and pressed it into the ridge soil and spoke aloud the three meanings I had gathered. [440] I admitted I did not know if any of them were correct. [452] Perhaps the seed only needed someone honest enough to confess the uncertainty and tend it anyway. [468] The monsoon broke fully then, warm and roaring, and the blossoms shivered around me. [482] From the wet ground a green shoot rose, faster than any reality I had ever checked. [498] Its leaves opened like pages, and on each one I saw a face I recognized from the dreams. [516] The fisherman, the moons, the child with her quilt, all of them wakened into the new tree. [533] The elders climbed the ridge through the rain and wept, because the memory had survived. [548] I never learned whether my interpretations had been accurate or simply sincere. [560] Maybe that was always the secret the title hid from me. [571] A culture does not endure because every story is verified, but because someone keeps telling them with care. [589] I am still the Reality Checker of Semaya, but now I check reality gently, leaving room for the marvels to grow. [610]</story>
