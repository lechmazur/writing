<story>Mireille was a sea-foam recollectionist, which meant she gathered the memories the tide forgot and pressed them into salt. [19] Today her work led her below the cliffs, into the iridescent cave inside a giant crystal geode. [36] The walls there breathed color, and every facet hummed a small, cheerful gloom, as if the rock were happy to be sad. [58] She had come to find the missing pieces of one particular life: her own. [72] For years Mireille had noticed that some paths in her past simply disappeared, leaving gaps where roads once ran. [91] Her motivation was simple and stubborn: to discover why some paths disappear before memory fades entirely. [107] In her palm she carried a shard of crater glass, dark and pocked, formed where a star had once struck the shore. [129] When she held it to the geode's light, the crater glass transliterates emotions, turning feeling into letters she could read. [149] Grief spelled itself in slow blue vowels; joy crackled in quick gold consonants. [162] But the glass alone could not show her where the lost roads went. [175] For that, she needed a guide, and tradition offered only one: the trail laid via the scattered seeds dropped by a migrating bird. [198] Each autumn a single grey heron crossed the cliffs, and wherever it had passed over a forgotten place, a seed fell. [219] Mireille followed these seeds into the geode now, one glinting kernel at a time. [233] The first seed lay beside a pool, and when she touched it the crater glass wrote a child's laughter onto her wrist. [255] She remembered a sister, then, a small face she had somehow misplaced among the years. [270] The second seed glowed near a curtain of violet crystal, and its emotion transliterated into a single word: leaving. [289] Here Mireille paused to conjecture, weighing the letters against the gaps. [300] Perhaps, she reasoned, a path disappears only when someone chooses to stop walking it. [314] The seeds led deeper, each one a missing piece returned: a quarrel, a harbor, a door left open in the rain. [335] The final seed rested in the deepest chamber, where the iridescent walls dimmed to a tender, cheerful gloom. [353] When Mireille lifted it, the crater glass spelled the whole sentence at last. [366] Her sister had drowned, and Mireille, unable to bear the road that led to that grief, had erased it herself. [386] She was the recollectionist who had refused to recollect. [395] Now the conjecture proved true: paths vanish because we cannot carry every weight at once. [410] The migrating bird, she understood, dropped seeds only where mercy had buried something too heavy to hold. [427] Mireille sat among the glowing facets and let the salt gather on her cheeks. [441] She could press this memory back into the foam, and forget once more, before memory fades on its own. [460] Instead she gathered every missing piece and held them, a mosaic of laughter and leaving. [475] The geode brightened, as if approving, painting her in gentle, contradictory light. [487] She tucked the crater glass away, no longer needing it to read what she finally felt. [503] Some paths disappear, she now knew, so that we may survive long enough to walk them again. [520] Outside, the heron circled once and flew on, scattering one last seed toward the sea. [535] Mireille rose, cheerful in her gloom, and followed it home. [545] Behind her, the cave kept her recovered roads safe in its many-colored hush. [558] She thought of all the other recollectionists who had walked in before her and turned back at the second seed. [578] They had feared what the crater glass might transliterate, and so their gaps had stayed gaps forever. [595] Mireille would not be one of them. [602] The sea-foam waited, patient as ever, ready to remember alongside her. [613]</story>
