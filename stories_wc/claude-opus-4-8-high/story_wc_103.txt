<story>Mira had spent her life shaping green walls, but the living bridge garden was the first maze that shaped her back. [21] As a hedge maze designer, she had been summoned to repair a labyrinth grown across an aqueduct of braided roots. [41] The owners had vanished, leaving only fading photographs pinned along the entrance arch. [54] Each picture showed the garden younger, greener, until the oldest dissolved into pale fog. [68] Mira studied them with a quaking reverie, as though the images might breathe. [81] Among the hedges she found a halo fragment, a curved shard of luminous metal half-buried in moss. [98] When she lifted it, the corridors rearranged, folding inward through hyperbolic geometry that no living plant should obey. [116] Walls multiplied impossibly, every turn opening more turns, the maze widening faster than she could walk. [132] She understood then why the designers before her had disappeared. [142] The halo fragment was a key, and the maze was a memory engine. [155] Mira's true motivation surfaced, the hunger she had hidden from every client: to discover the texture of time. [173] She wanted to feel its grain beneath her fingers, the way she felt the rough bark of a yew. [192] So she pressed deeper, and the fragment warmed against her palm. [203] With each step she became retrocognitively trapped, sliding backward into moments that were not her own. [219] She saw the first gardener plant the bridge, weaving roots between two cliffs while clutching the same shard. [237] She watched seasons reel past in reverse, blossoms folding into buds, rivers running uphill. [251] The fading photographs, she realized, were not decaying but rewinding, each one a window the maze had opened before. [270] Trapped in borrowed pasts, Mira finally touched what she had always craved. [282] Time had a texture after all, ridged like a leaf's underside, cool and trembling. [296] But the deeper she fell, the more her present began to disintegrate. [308] Her hands grew translucent, her memories thinning into the same pale fog that swallowed the oldest image. [325] She was becoming a photograph, a moment the maze would pin upon its arch for the next designer. [343] Quaking, she forced herself to remember the shape of the exit, the only geometry that ran straight. [360] She hurled the halo fragment away from her, breaking the curved logic of the hedges. [375] The hyperbolic corridors collapsed back into ordinary right angles, releasing her into the green present. [390] Mira stumbled across the living bridge garden, solid again, her hands opaque, her heart still echoing the texture she had stolen. [411] Behind her, the fragment gleamed in the moss, patient, waiting for the next curious mind. [426] She did not repair the maze. [432] Instead she pinned one new photograph to the arch, an image of herself mid-stride, already beginning to fade. [450] It was a warning, and a confession. [457] She had learned that time's grain cuts both ways, that to feel it is to be unmade by it. [476] Yet some hungers never disintegrate, no matter how the body remembers its terror. [489] Years later, Mira still walked with a quaking reverie, flinching at every photograph that yellowed on a wall. [507] She designed no more mazes, fearing what straight paths might secretly curve. [519] But in dreams she returned to the borrowed pasts, retrocognitively trapped within the first gardener's trembling hands. [536] There she would feel the texture of time once more, ridged and cool, almost worth the price of nearly dissolving. [556] She had wanted to understand how moments held together, what fibers bound one breath to the next. [573] Now she knew, and the knowing followed her like the scent of cut yew. [587] The living bridge garden, meanwhile, kept growing, weaving its impossible corridors out of patience and root. [603] And on its arch her photograph faded, one slow shade per season, until the day it too would open as a window for someone braver, or hungrier, than she. [632]</story>
