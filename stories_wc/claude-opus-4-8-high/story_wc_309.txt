Berran had always been the dimmest light in the academy's brilliant hall. [12] In the age when creativity got automated, when humming engines dreamed murals and spun symphonies before breakfast, an underachieving mage was a relic nobody needed. [37] So he climbed where no engine bothered to go: the icebound spire above the clouds, a tower so old its stones accommodated impossibilities the way kind hosts accommodate strange guests. [67] Legend said a relic slept at its summit, and Berran wanted, more than any commission or accolade, to find the sacred in the simple. [91] He had no machine for that yearning, and machines, he suspected, never would. [104] The stair gave out halfway, swallowed by frost, so Berran practiced the one art he had nearly mastered: mist walking. [124] He stepped onto the curling clouds, breathing them solid beneath his boots, drifting upward through a brilliant dimness where the sun glowed without ever quite shining. [150] Each footfall held only because he believed it might, and belief was the last thing the engines could not draft for him. [172] At the summit waited not a throne nor a sword, but a small ruined garden, frozen mid-bloom under glass-clear ice. [192] In its center stood a folly, a tiny ornamental arch built for no purpose but delight, and at its peak sat the garden folly keystone. [217] The stone shone with lingering luminance, the soft afterglow of every gardener who had ever paused here to admire ordinary roses. [238] It was light left behind by love, not made by labor, and it pulsed gently as Berran approached. [256] A voice rose from the keystone, dry and patient as old mortar. [268] "You came for greatness," it said, "and found a garden ornament." [279] "I came to feel something the engines cannot copy," Berran answered. [290] The keystone warmed. [293] "Then unstrap your satchel of borrowed spells," it told him, "for you will not need them here." [310] Berran obeyed, loosening the buckles and letting the heavy bag of automated incantations slide into the snow. [327] Without it he felt lighter, smaller, suddenly able to notice the frost-flowers' fragile geometry. [341] "The engines render every wonder," the keystone murmured, "but they cannot kneel." [353] "They produce; they do not cherish." [359] "A folly is useless, and that is its holiness; it exists only because someone loved making it." [376] Berran understood then why he had failed every grand examination. [386] He had never wanted to be useful; he had wanted to be moved. [399] He pressed his palm to the keystone, and the lingering luminance flowed into him, warm and unhurried. [416] It carried the memory of a thousand small devotions: pruning, watering, simply standing in the dim brilliant dawn. [434] The ice around the garden cracked, not melting but softening, accommodating the impossibility of spring atop an eternal winter. [453] One frozen rose opened, slow as a held breath. [462] Berran laughed, and it was the first honest spell he had ever cast. [475] When he descended by mist walking, he left his satchel behind on the spire. [489] Back in the automated city, he opened no grand studio and challenged no humming engine. [504] Instead he planted a small, useless garden in a forgotten courtyard. [515] Each evening he sat among the ordinary flowers, watching their colors hold a brilliant dimness as the sun withdrew. [534] People passed and called him an underachieving mage, and he agreed with a smile. [548] The machines could outproduce him in everything but one quiet thing. [559] They could not love a thing for being small. [568] And so, in the simplest of acts, Berran had finally found the sacred. [581] Somewhere above the clouds, his abandoned spells gathered frost, while a single rose kept blooming in the lingering light. [600]
