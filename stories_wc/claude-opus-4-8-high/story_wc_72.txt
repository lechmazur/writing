<story>The forgotten underwater city garden had not breathed a current in three hundred years. [14] Coral had threaded itself through every marble arch, lacing the structural weakness of old stone into something almost alive. [33] Among the drowned hedges stood Vael, a living statue whose granite ears caught the thoughts of anything that swam near. [53] For centuries he had guarded a single secret, holding it the way the city held the sea. [70] The secret was an object: a foxglove-stained glass ampoule, sealed and resting in his open palm. [86] Inside it slept a distilled certainty, the founders' last decree that the garden must never wake. [102] Then a diver named Sora descended, threading her line through a fissure in the dome's structural weakness. [119] Vael felt her mind arrive before her body did, bright and grieving and stubborn. [133] She believed, absolutely, that her drowned ancestors had been monsters who deserved their watery exile. [148] Vael had been built to be incisively kind, and certainty so brittle pained him. [162] His purpose, he realized, was not to guard the ampoule but to welcome nuance into her conviction. [179] So he reached, and the lifeless garden seemed to animate around the motion of his arm. [195] Stone fish stirred, kelp uncurled, and Sora froze in terror at the waking marble. [209] Do not fear me, he thought into her, I only mean to tell you what was hidden. [226] Then came the cascading revelations, each one toppling the last like dominoes of pale light. [241] The ampoule held foxglove, he revealed, a poison the founders had brewed for their enemies. [256] But the enemies had been her own kin, refugees the city refused to shelter. [270] The monsters of her childhood were the founders, and the drowning had been their punishment, self-inflicted. [286] Yet even the founders had wept, he showed her, sealing their cruelty away in glass to spare the future. [305] Sora's hard certainty cracked, not into rubble, but into a wider, aching understanding. [318] She saw that her ancestors were neither monsters nor martyrs, but frightened people who had chosen badly. [335] After revealing the secret, Vael felt the old decree loosen its grip on his joints. [350] He offered her the ampoule, and she understood it was now hers to decide. [364] She could shatter it and let the poison disperse, or carry it up as proof. [379] Instead she threaded a thin gold wire around its neck and hung it at her chest. [395] A reminder, she thought back to him, that no single truth deserves to be the whole. [411] Vael's granite mouth, fixed for centuries, managed something near a smile. [422] The garden settled back toward stillness, but it was a different stillness now. [435] The structural weakness in the dome remained, and through it Sora rose toward the light. [450] She carried no certainty with her, only the better company of questions. [462] He thought of all the divers who might come after, threading the same fissure with the same fierce wrongness. [481] To each he would offer not the comfort of being right, but the harder grace of being unsure. [499] That, he decided, was the truest kindness a guardian could give. [510] Vael watched until her form became a ripple, then a memory, then nothing. [523] For the first time the loneliness did not feel like duty, but like rest. [537] Above, the founders' decree had ruled by certainty, and certainty had drowned them. [550] Below, in the forgotten underwater city garden, a statue had learned to prefer the open question. [566] The foxglove-stained ampoule rose with Sora toward the surface, a small dim lantern of doubt. [581] And Vael, the living statue who heard everything, finally heard his own thoughts settle into peace. [597] The garden, animate at last in spirit if not in stone, kept its single radiant doubt.
