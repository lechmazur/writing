<story>Mira kept her post at the celestial mapping observatory long after the other astronomers had drifted to sleep. [18] She was a lucid dream chronicler, hired to record the visions of sleepers and trace their meanings. [35] Tonight her task was stranger: to decode temple inscriptions that no living linguist could read. [50] The inscriptions had been copied from a ruin that vanished beneath a reservoir decades ago. [65] Only one clue had survived: a daylily-pressed telegram, its petals flattened against yellowing paper, sent by her grandmother the year she died. [87] The message read only: "Read the temple through variable stars." [97] For years Mira had not understood, but the observatory existed precisely to catalog such stars, whose brightness swelled and faded like breathing. [119] She lay back on the cot beside the great telescope and let herself fall, deliberately, into a lucid dream. [138] In the dream the temple stood whole again, its walls crawling with symbols she had memorized but never solved. [157] Above the temple, the night sky pulsed, and she realized each carving aligned with a particular star. [174] The variable stars dimmed and brightened in sequence, and the inscriptions were not words at all but a rhythm of light. [195] As she watched, a woman appeared among the columns, translucent and patient. [207] It was her grandmother, who had spent her own life trying to read these same stones. [223] "I could not finish," the old woman said, "so I asked you to witness what I began." [240] Mira understood then that the work had always been a shared burden, passed gently from one sleeper to the next. [260] Together they read the temple, grandmother naming the steady stars, Mira tracking the flickering ones. [275] Each pulse spelled a syllable, and the syllables formed a prayer meant to comfort the grieving. [291] The temple, she realized, was a place that healed with words, a sanctuary built so the living could speak to the lost. [313] Sentence by sentence the prayer assembled itself, and Mira felt the old grief she carried for her grandmother loosen. [332] "You carried this alone too long," Mira whispered, and her grandmother smiled. [344] "Now we set it down together," the old woman answered, fading into the brightening east. [359] Mira woke when understanding dawned, both in her mind and across the real horizon outside the dome. [376] Pale light spilled over the observatory floor, settling on the daylily telegram still clutched in her hand. [393] She reached for her notebook and began to transcribe the prayer before the dream could blur. [409] The words came in the calm of midnight solace, each line a small light against the dark. [426] For the first time the inscriptions made sense, not as a riddle to conquer but as a gift to pass on. [447] Mira understood her role now: not merely to decode temple inscriptions, but to deliver their comfort to others who mourned. [467] She would teach the prayer at the observatory's open nights, letting strangers read their losses through the variable stars. [486] A burden split among many hands grew light, and a sky once silent began, at last, to speak. [504] Mira pressed the telegram flat inside her journal, beside the finished translation, and watched the daylily petals catch the first gold of morning. [527] Outside, the stars surrendered to dawn one by one, their last flickers spelling the same syllables she had learned. [546] She whispered the prayer aloud to the empty dome, and the room seemed to listen. [561] Somewhere in that quiet, she felt her grandmother set down her share of the labor for good. [578] Grief, Mira thought, was only a story carried alone until someone agreed to witness it. [593] She closed the journal, switched off the lamp, and let the dawning light finish what the long night had begun. [613]</story>
