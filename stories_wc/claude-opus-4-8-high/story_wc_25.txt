<story>Mara kept the labyrinth, and the labyrinth kept her secrets. [10] She was the maintenance worker for the port's towering maze of shipping containers, a city of rusted steel stacked against the gray harbor sky. [56] A whisper, she said, followed the metal seams and slipped under doors, while a shout only bounced and died. [96] Mara worked by feel, her boots reading the corrugated floor, her ears tracking footsteps three rows away. [113] She had a gift: she identified people by their walks, the way a stranger's heel struck or a friend's stride hesitated. [134] Tonight an unfamiliar gait approached, light and deliberate, a step that paused to listen between each footfall. [151] The visitor was an old cartographer named Edwin, who carried folded maps tucked into every pocket of his coat. [170] He unfolded one against a container wall, its creases mapping the labyrinth as it had stood a decade ago. [263] The folded maps show every wall, Edwin said, but not the gaps, and only your ears know the gaps.
