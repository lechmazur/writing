<story>Nabu-Esagil counted his lamp oil by the drop, for he was a frugal astronomer and the palace stores had thinned between regimes. [22] The old king had fallen, the new one had not yet been crowned, and in that hush the reimagined Babylonian palace seemed to hold its breath. [48] Its terraces, once gilded, now wore a dusky harmony of shadow and faint star, and Nabu-Esagil loved them more for their poverty. [70] He had served the dead king as a reader of heavens, but his true labor was secret. [87] Years ago his wife, Sarpani, had died, and he had buried his grief not in stone but in a single magic square tile. [110] The tile bore numbers that summed alike in every direction, and within those sums he had hidden an encrypted devotion to her. [132] Each numeral mapped to a star, each star to a word, so that the night itself recited his love through schematic representations no censor could read. [158] He found it empirically ironic that the same mathematics priests used to flatter dead gods now carried a widower's whisper. [178] Tonight, though, he meant to do more than mourn. [187] He climbed to the highest terrace where the tile lay set into an altar, and he prepared to uncloak it before the rising constellation he had named for her. [216] For thirty years the tile had been wrapped in dark linen, shown to no one, its meaning known only to him. [237] His motivation was simple and vast: to seal a covenant with the unknown. [250] If death was a country without maps, he would send it a cipher only love could solve. [267] He pulled away the linen, and starlight struck the glazed numerals. [278] The frugal man who hoarded oil spent freely now, lighting every lamp he owned in a single reckless arc. [297] The altar glowed, and the magic square seemed to lift its sums into the dark like answered prayer. [315] He read the schematic aloud, tracing line to line, mapping each glaze to the constellation overhead. [331] Fifteen along the rows, fifteen down the columns, fifteen on the slants, the same balance everywhere, the way her patience had once steadied his fevered nights. [357] Footsteps interrupted him. [360] A young scribe from the incoming regime had followed the unusual light, sent to inventory what the palace still owned. [380] He stared at the blazing altar, then at the old man, and demanded to know what treasure was being concealed. [400] Nabu-Esagil did not hide the tile. [406] Instead he explained the cipher, how the numbers became stars and the stars became a woman's name. [423] The scribe expected gold and found only grief made geometric, and something in his official hardness loosened. [440] Regimes rise and fall, the old man said, but a covenant with the unknown outlasts every throne. [457] I do not know if she hears, he admitted, but I have built a door of numbers in case she might. [478] The scribe, who had lost his own mother in the turmoil between kings, asked quietly to be taught the method. [498] So the astronomer showed him through schematic representations how to encode a name no decree could erase. [515] They worked until the lamps guttered, two men of different ages drawing squares of grief and devotion. [532] Dawn arrived in the same dusky harmony that had opened the night, neither triumphant nor mournful, simply continuous. [550] The frugal astronomer wrapped his tile again, no longer needing it shown, for the secret was now shared and therefore safe. [571] It struck him as empirically ironic, one last time, that he had guarded his devotion in plain numerals and only secrecy had made them precious. [596] When the new king was crowned, the palace counted its remaining treasures and found one missing, an old glazed tile of no recorded value. [620] But high above, between the constellation and the dark, a covenant had been sealed, and the unknown, if it listened, now knew her name. [644]</story>
