<story>Maren never asked to mind the cursed orchard, but the deed came with her grandmother's debts, so she pruned what she hated. [22] The trees grew on a cliffside labyrinth, hedges of blackthorn winding above a drop that swallowed sound. [39] Mechanical birds patrolled the corridors, copper wings clicking, their painted eyes recording every wanderer who lost the path. [57] Travelers came at dusk, drawn by rumors of a shortcut, and the labyrinth kept most of them. [74] Maren's curse was simple and cruel: she could guide the lost, but only before the night frost formed, for frost froze the birds into judges who reported any soul still trapped. [105] She hated the duty, yet she did it, because letting strangers freeze was a debt heavier than her grandmother's. [124] Tonight a family of three had stumbled in, a father and two children, calling names the hedges ate. [142] Maren could not touch the walls without waking the birds, so she worked her grandmother's old trick: leaving impressions without touching. [163] She breathed on the cold thorns and her warmth left ghost-prints, faint arrows that pointed the way without bending a single branch. [185] But the labyrinth had rearranged itself again, and even she had forgotten its shape. [199] Then she remembered the forgotten blueprint, rolled inside the hollow of the oldest apple tree. [214] Her grandmother had drawn it offhandedly exact, every corridor labeled in a careless hand that somehow never erred. [232] Maren pried the bark loose and found the brittle paper, along with a small nut wedged beside it. [250] The nut was iron, etched with gear-teeth, the master winder her grandmother had whittled to command the birds. [268] With blueprint and nut, she could assemble a safe corridor, switching the birds from wardens into guides. [285] She ran the hedgerows, comparing the drawing's lines to the shifting walls, breathing arrows as she went. [302] The first frost had begun to silver the upper thorns, and her breath came shorter with each turn. [320] At the labyrinth's heart stood a clockwork bird larger than the rest, its breastplate hiding a socket. [337] Maren fitted the small nut into the socket and turned it, and the great bird shuddered awake. [354] Down the corridors the smaller birds answered, their clicking softening into a chiming song. [368] The lanterns in their beaks flared, and the whole maze became a map of light. [383] It was a graceless beauty, all gears and grinding, yet the glow led the father and his children toward the gate. [404] Maren followed at a distance, never touching them, only breathing her arrows ahead so they would not stray. [422] The frost climbed faster now, biting the lower branches, racing her toward the edge of the curse. [439] She reached the gate as the family stepped through, the youngest child clutching a fallen apple like treasure. [457] They turned to thank her, but she only nodded, because gratitude felt strange to a woman who resented the task. [477] When the last frost settled, the birds folded their wings, silent, with no trapped soul to report. [494] Maren slumped against the oldest apple tree, the blueprint still warm in her hand. [508] She had hated every step, yet she had moved with the offhandedly exact certainty her grandmother once owned. [526] Perhaps the curse was not the orchard, but the inheritance of caring against her will. [541] She tucked the small nut back into the bark, saving it for the next lost traveler. [557] Below, the cliff exhaled mist, and the labyrinth rearranged itself for morning, indifferent to her mercy. [573] Maren wiped frost from her sleeve and counted the corridors she would have to memorize again before the next dusk.
