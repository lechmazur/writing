<story>Vela had been a broken mask restorer for nineteen years, mending faces that other people preferred to forget. [18] Tonight she worked inside the prism sanctuary, where stained glass split the starlight into trembling ribbons of color. [36] The sanctuary, she had learned, accumulates silence the way other rooms accumulate dust. [49] Each broken mask brought there carried the last held breath of someone who had died mid-sentence, mid-secret. [66] Her task was not merely cosmetic; she came to ground restless spirits, to give them rest. [82] The dead, she believed, lingered because their stories had been cut off, knotted, tangled. [96] To free them she used an old method passed down from her grandmother: by unraveling knots in a centuries-old fisherman's net. [117] The net hung across the sanctuary like a gray cobweb, each knot a soul caught at the moment of drowning. [137] Tonight's spirit belonged to her own mother, whose mask lay cracked upon the workbench. [151] Vela had agreed to do this only across sharing a secret, the bargain her grandmother had set: speak one truth for every knot loosed. [175] She lifted her tapestry needle, the slender bone tool she used to coax threads apart. [190] The needle slid beneath the first knot, and Vela whispered her opening confession. [203] "I was glad when you stopped speaking to me," she told the empty mask. [217] The knot loosened, and a thin sound, half a sigh, escaped into the colored light. [232] She felt the interlaced hush settle around her, the woven quiet of stories meeting at last. [248] It was not peace exactly, but a lucid unrest, a clarity sharpened by everything still unsaid. [264] She worked the second knot, and offered another truth. [273] "I kept the letters you wrote to my father, and I never sent them." [287] The net trembled, releasing the smell of salt and old rope. [298] Vela understood now why the dead clung; they wanted their secrets exhumed, lifted into the light. [314] To exhume a buried truth was the only way to let a spirit finally rest in the ground. [332] The third knot was the tightest, knotted by years of mutual quiet. [344] Vela's hands shook as she pressed the tapestry needle into its core. [356] "I blamed you for leaving," she said, "but I learned to disappear from you first." [371] The knot gave way, and the starlight through the prism flared into a single white seam. [387] Within that seam Vela glimpsed her mother's face, restored as no mask could manage. [401] The accumulated silence of the sanctuary thinned, exhaling toward the open dome. [413] Only one knot remained, and Vela knew the final secret it required. [425] It was the one she had carried since childhood, the heaviest of them all. [439] "I am the reason you went out on the water that night," she whispered. [453] The fisherman's net had caught her mother long ago, and Vela had inherited its tangles. [468] She slid the needle under the last knot, and pulled gently, breath by breath. [482] The net came apart, drifting to the floor like loosed gray hair. [494] The interlaced hush deepened into something almost musical, a chord of many quiets resolving. [508] Vela placed the mended mask over her mother's vanished face and felt the spirit settle. [523] Grounded at last, the restless presence sank into the stone floor without protest. [536] The starlight returned to ordinary color, prismatic and calm above her. [547] Vela sat among the threads, holding the tapestry needle, no longer trembling. [559] She had ground a spirit, yes, but she had also unburied herself. [571] Tomorrow another broken mask would arrive, another net of grief to undo. [583] But for tonight, the sanctuary held only the lucid unrest of secrets finally spoken aloud. [598] Vela rose, and walked out beneath the stars, lighter than she had ever known. [612]</story>
