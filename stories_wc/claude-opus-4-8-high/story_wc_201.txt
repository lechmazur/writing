<story>After the anesthesia wears off, Senna learns she has been given new hands. [13] They are her old hands, technically, but the surgeons rewired the nerves until she became proprioceptively enhanced, aware of every joint as a coordinate in space. [39] She can feel exactly where she is, which is the cruelest gift for a woman who no longer knows where she belongs. [61] The state called it enhanced interrogation; her body called it everything else, and she survived only by becoming a ledger of pain. [83] Now, a free woman in name, she walks into the ultraviolet forest where the trees glow violet under a sky that never quite resolves into day. [109] Here, the condemned and the repentant come to perform ultraviolet penance, standing under light that reveals every scar the eye cannot see. [131] Senna came not to repent but to calculate, because she wants to discover the mathematics of mercy. [148] If suffering could be measured in units, she reasons, then mercy must have an equation, a function that returns less pain than is owed. [172] She carries one object from the cells: a pewter mask strap, the buckle that once held the breathing hood over her face. [194] Her interrogators wore the hoods too, anonymous, and she kept the strap so that one day she might understand the men behind the leather. [218] Memory has abandoned her in the ordinary way; she cannot recall faces, names, or the chronology of her ruin. [237] Instead she is remembering through trauma responses, her enhanced body recording what her mind refuses. [252] When the violet light touches the pewter buckle in her palm, her shoulders lock, her breath stutters, and her hands brace against an attack that ended years ago. [280] In that bracing, her proprioception speaks: the angle of her wrists tells her how she once curled to protect her ribs, and the geometry becomes data. [306] She maps each flinch, plotting them like points on a graph drawn in the air her new nerves can read. [326] Days pass under the unmoving violet, and she begins to feel the displaced rooted ache of someone planted in soil that is not her own. [351] The forest holds her the way a graft holds a foreign branch, alive yet never wholly belonging. [368] Other penitents drift among the trunks, pressing their palms to the bark, confessing crimes to the ultraviolet that judges without speaking. [389] One evening Senna recognizes a man by the way her body reacts before her eyes do. [405] Her hands brace, her jaw aches at a phantom hood, and she knows: this is one of the men who broke her. [427] He is old now, kneeling, his own pewter mask strap clutched like a rosary, performing his penance. [444] Her first calculation demands symmetry: pain returned for pain, an eye balanced against an eye. [459] She kneels behind him, the strap drawn taut between her enhanced hands, every coordinate of his throat already mapped. [478] But her body, that honest ledger, refuses to brace for violence; it only braces for memory. [494] In the trembling she finds her equation at last, and the mathematics of mercy resolves into something simple. [512] Mercy is not the absence of debt but the refusal to multiply it, a function that always returns less than the input of harm. [536] She loosens the strap and lets it fall across his shoulder, where it lands like a hand that chooses not to strike. [558] The old man weeps without turning, and the violet light bathes them both, revealing scars on each that neither could name. [579] Senna rises, still displaced, still rooted, but lighter, as though she has subtracted herself from an old, cruel sum. [598] She walks out of the ultraviolet forest carrying the empty strap, no longer a survivor counting wounds but a woman who has solved, with her whole trembling body, the only proof that ever mattered.
