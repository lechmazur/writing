<story>Mira painted in grays the world insisted were colors. [9] Born colorblind, she had learned to trust texture over hue, the rasp of dried pigment, the way light fractured against rough canvas. [31] She worked in the glassblower's courtyard, where the furnace breathed and molten beads cooled into accidental jewels. [48] This was after the phoenix rose, when the old town had burned and risen from its own ashes, scarred but stubbornly alive. [70] Mira had come here for one reason: to find beauty in imperfection, in the bubbles and warps the glassblower cursed as flaws. [92] The glassblower, old Tomas, no longer spoke. [99] Since the fire stole his voice, he communicated through plant arrangements, setting clay pots along the courtyard wall. [117] Rosemary meant welcome; a single thorned rose meant grief; bound reeds meant he wished to bargain. [133] One morning Mira found the reeds laid out, and beside them an object she did not recognize: a concertina reed tongue, a thin brass blade pried from some broken instrument. [163] Tomas pressed it into her palm and arranged a new bouquet: lavender beneath a sealed bud, the symbol, she had learned, for sleep waiting to awaken. [189] She understood at last what the town whispered about Tomas: he dealt in bartered awakenings. [204] For a price, he could rouse a dormant gift sleeping inside a person, but the gift demanded an equal sacrifice. [224] The reed tongue, he indicated through trembling sage stems, would let her hear color as sound. [240] The cost was her steady painter's hand. [247] Mira, who had spent her life feeling cheated by a flat gray world, felt her pulse leap. [264] Yet she hesitated, because her hand was the one true thing she trusted. [277] She was innocently cunning, this painter, the sort who smiled while quietly rearranging the world to her advantage. [295] So she answered Tomas in his own language, gathering plants from the courtyard's neglected beds. [310] She set a cracked pot of blossoming weeds beside a perfect, sterile lily, then snapped the lily's stem. [328] It was an argument, made in petals: imperfection lives, perfection is already dead. [341] Tomas tilted his head, intrigued by the riddle she had planted. [352] Mira proposed a different bargain. [357] She would not trade her hand for the reed tongue. [367] Instead, she would awaken his silenced voice, and let him keep the brass blade. [381] She had noticed that the reed tongue matched the broken concertina Tomas hid beneath his bench. [397] His voice had not truly burned away; it had simply lost its instrument. [410] She fitted the tongue back into its frame, and pressed the bellows into his scarred hands. [426] Tomas drew a breath, and the concertina sang one wavering, imperfect note. [438] It cracked, it wheezed, and it was the most beautiful sound the courtyard had heard since the phoenix rose. [457] Tomas wept and hummed along, his throat remembering words it thought were ash. [470] Mira kept her steady hand, and the world stayed gray. [480] But she had bartered an awakening of her own kind. [490] She finally understood her motivation completely: beauty did not live in flawless color, but in the cracked note, the warped glass, the gray that held a thousand textures. [518] That evening she painted Tomas playing, his face built from rough strokes and deliberate smudges. [533] She could not see its colors, but she could hear the music inside it, and that was enough. [551] On the wall, she left him an answer in plants: a flawed wildflower, blooming sideways, reaching anyway toward the furnace light. [572] Tomas read it, and laughed a creaking, glorious laugh. [581] Together they had proven that the awakened world need not be perfect to be worth waking for. [598] And in the glassblower's courtyard, two imperfect people made something whole. [609]</story>
