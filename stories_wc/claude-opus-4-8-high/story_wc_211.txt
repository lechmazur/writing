<story>After the foghorn falls silent, the city below holds its breath. [11] Elowen, the venerable oath scribe, ascends to the ancient orchard garden atop the skyscraper. [25] Here, between gnarled apple trees that should not survive the wind, she comes each night to archive emotional patterns. [44] Her tools are old: a wax cylinder phonograph and a thin blade for trimming its surface. [60] Each shaving she peels away carries a recorded feeling, a sliver of someone's grief or hope, ready to be reread. [80] She speaks dead languages, and the old machines answer her in tongues no living mouth still shapes. [97] Tonight, a single wax cylinder phonograph shaving lies curled on her workbench, glistening like a ribbon of dark honey. [116] It came from a recording so corroded that no one had ever managed to play it. [132] To read these fragile remnants, Elowen does not use her eyes; she must immerse herself. [147] She lowers the shaving onto her tongue and lets memory rise to meet it. [161] The taste opens a door she has not walked through in decades, reaching back via a curious third-grade field trip memory. [182] She is eight again, pressed against a cold museum case, watching a guide crank an identical phonograph. [199] That afternoon, a recording of a man's last words had played, and Elowen had wept without knowing why. [217] Decades later, she understands: that grief was the seed of her whole vocation. [230] Now the old memory becomes a key, and the corroded recording unlocks at last. [244] A voice swells inside her skull, speaking one of the dead languages she alone still keeps. [260] It belongs to a long-dead orchard keeper, recorded a century ago in this very garden. [275] He was only describing his trees, listing which branches to prune before the frost. [289] Yet his weary words, half-sung in mourning for a dying wife, arrange themselves into something else. [305] The names of the apple varieties, in order, spell out the precise date the foghorn would fall silent forever. [324] It is an accidental prophecy, made by a grieving man who only meant to save his fruit. [341] Elowen's blood cools, for the foghorn fell silent only this very evening. [353] The dead man had named the hour of his own forgetting, and hers. [366] She surfaces from the trance, trembling among the moonlit branches, the shaving dissolved on her tongue. [382] A velvet disquiet settles over the garden, soft and suffocating at once. [394] If a man could foretell the future while merely cataloguing his orchard, then her own archive might be brimming with prophecies she has never heard. [419] Every emotional pattern she had so carefully preserved might be a sentence in some vast, unspoken oracle. [436] As the venerable oath scribe, she had sworn to keep feelings, not to predict fates. [451] Now those two duties twist around each other like roots beneath the apple trees. [465] She gathers the remaining cylinders, suddenly afraid of what other accidents they might confess. [479] One bears the orchard keeper's name, and she has never dared to immerse herself in it. [495] Tonight she finally does, returning once more via that curious third-grade field trip memory, that first taste of borrowed sorrow. [515] The keeper speaks again, gentler now, as though he had always known a listener would come. [531] He names a final variety, one no catalogue records, and tells her to plant it where the foghorn once stood. [551] It is, she realizes, another accidental prophecy, but this one offers a task instead of an ending. [568] So she descends with a single seed of meaning, resolved to plant living feeling where dead sound has gone quiet. [588] The skyscraper hums, the orchard breathes, and the silenced harbor waits to be answered. [602]</story>
