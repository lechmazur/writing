<story>In the canyon of endless echoes, towers of bone rose like the ribs of some buried god. [17] Among them walked Saelu, the copper-veined storyteller, whose skin shone with thin metallic threads that warmed when she spoke. [36] She had come across the death of a language, the last tongue of the Veil people, which no living mouth could still pronounce. [59] Saelu was quietly articulate, the kind of speaker who let silence do half the work. [74] Her purpose was strange and singular: to translate texture into sound, to give the dying words a body of vibration before they vanished forever. [98] In her palm she carried a flawed diamond rumored to be cursed, its single crack splitting light into trembling colors. [118] The Veil elders had once pressed their grammar into that stone, etching its facets with grooves too fine for any eye. [139] Touch alone could read them, and Saelu meant to harness that texture, to drag it out of stillness and into living sound. [161] But the diamond would only sing in one place, where the canyon narrowed into a tunnel of echoes carved beneath the tallest tower of bone. [186] There, the elders had said, sound did not fade but folded, returning richer each time it struck the walls. [205] Saelu descended at dawn, when the wind carried a flock of birds spiraling above the canyon's mouth. [222] She had learned the elders' final secret: their language could be read through the patterns in a flock of birds in flight. [244] Each swerve of wings spelled a phoneme, each gathering and scattering a clause, and Saelu watched the sky like a page. [265] She traced their arcs with the diamond, matching the cracks beneath her thumb to the shapes overhead. [282] When bird and groove aligned, she stepped into the tunnel of echoes and began to speak. [298] Her copper veins glowed, drawing heat from the friction of texture against breath. [311] The diamond's flaw caught her words and broke them into many, and the tunnel answered, folding each syllable upon itself. [331] What had been mere grooves became a chorus, the dead Veil tongue rising in overlapping waves. [347] Saelu felt the rumored curse stir, the old warning that whoever woke the stone would lose their own voice to it. [368] Her throat tightened, her syllables thinning, but she did not stop. [379] She understood now that the curse was no malice but a trade: a living voice given so a dead one might speak. [401] Above, the flock kept turning, feeding her the last lines she could no longer say aloud. [417] Saelu read the birds and let the diamond translate the texture for her, her own words surrendered to the echoes. [437] The towers of bone hummed, resonant as flutes, carrying the resurrected language across the canyon. [452] For one long morning, the Veil people spoke again, not from any throat but from stone and wing and wind. [472] When the sun stood high, the diamond went dark, its grooves smoothed, its work complete. [487] Saelu emerged voiceless, but her copper veins still glowed, warm with everything she had carried. [502] She had begun this journey believing that languages were merely words, strings of meaning passed mouth to mouth. [520] Now she knew the truth the Veil elders had guarded: a language is a texture, a grain you feel before you ever hear it. [544] She could no longer be a storyteller in the old way, yet the canyon now held her final story forever. [564] Saelu walked on, quietly articulate even in silence, leaving the birds to tell the rest. [579] Travelers who pass the towers of bone still hear it, a flock of sound circling endlessly through the tunnel. [598] And though no one alive remembers the words, the texture of them survives, translated at last into pure, unfading sound. [618]</story>
