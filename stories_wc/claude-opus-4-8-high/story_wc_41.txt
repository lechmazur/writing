<story>The junkyard prophet lived where the fog sculpture garden met the sea. [12] He was called a prophet because he remembered the future and forgot the past, a condition he named being precognitively forgetful. [33] Each morning he woke knowing what tomorrow would bring, yet unable to recall his own name. [49] The garden was his only anchor, a field of figures carved from mist that the cold air held in place until noon. [71] He had one possession he trusted: a piece of plain chalk he kept against his heart. [87] With it he wrote messages on the rusted hulls of the junkyard, notes from his future self to the man who would forget. [110] The chalk obeyed his hand even when his memory would not. [121] His one ambition, written everywhere in white letters, was to capture the sound of growing older. [137] He believed that sound lived inside a stack of old videotapes left unwatched in a sunken cabinet near the shore. [157] The tapes held recordings of his family, voices aging across decades, and amid their hush he hoped to hear time itself. [178] But the precognitive curse worked cruelly: he knew he would find the tapes tomorrow, yet each tomorrow he forgot where he had hidden the player. [203] So he devised a method that did not depend on remembering. [214] Along the tideline he began arranging driftwood as directional markers, pale arrows pointing through the fog toward the cabinet's grave. [234] Every dawn the tide rearranged them, so every dusk he rebuilt the trail, clinging to echoes of a plan he could not consciously hold. [258] The driftwood spoke to him in a language older than memory, and his hands followed it like water follows the moon. [279] One grey morning he woke already knowing today was the day he would locate the player at last. [297] On a tin door he read chalk words from his unremembered hand: follow the arrows, do not stop, the sea is patient and so are you. [323] He walked into the fog sculpture garden, where carved swans and mothers dissolved and reformed in the moving mist. [342] Each shape was a memory he could not own, so he let himself feel the tidal longing instead, the ache of nearness without grasp. [366] The driftwood arrows led him past the sculptures, down the slick rocks, to the cabinet swollen with brine. [384] Inside, wrapped in oilcloth, lay the videotapes and a battered player powered by a small dynamo. [400] His hands trembled, knowing the future of this moment but not the faces it would show. [416] He fed a tape into the slot and turned the crank until light flickered against the fog. [433] A voice rose from the speaker, his mother calling his name across years he had lost. [449] She said it young, then older, then frail, the same syllables thinning like rope worn by tide. [466] And there it was, the thing he had chased: the sound of growing older, hidden in one repeated word. [485] He learned, at last, his own name, spoken by the woman who had given it. [500] He wept, and with the chalk he wrote the name on the cabinet, an echo for tomorrow's stranger to keep. [520] He knew already that by sunrise he would forget the voice, the tape, the tears. [535] But the driftwood would still point, and the chalk would still speak, and the sea would bring him back. [554] This, he understood, was how a forgetful prophet loved: not by holding, but by leaving signs. [570] Every night he would rebuild the trail, clinging to echoes that the tide kept washing clean. [586] And each return, the voice would age again, and he would meet his mother growing older, forever, for the first time. [607]</story>
