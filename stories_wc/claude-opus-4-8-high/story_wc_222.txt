<story>They said the sky would turn wrong on the night I climbed the icebound spire above the clouds. [18] I am a thief, but a chivalrous one, which means I steal only what is offered badly and return it better. [39] My master called me the apprentice of wonder, for I learned my trade not from greed but from a hunger to embrace the unknown with open arms. [66] That hunger drove me upward when the stars rearranged themselves into shapes no chart remembered. [81] The sky was wrong, and wrong skies, my master taught, are doorways. [93] Inside the spire lived the Hermit of Hours, who guarded a chessboard of hand-carved pieces, each hollowed to hold a secret. [114] Legend claimed that whoever read all the secrets could amend a single mistake in their life. [130] I wanted to amend nothing; I wanted only to know, to open every hollow and gasp at what lived there. [150] The Hermit met me at the frost-locked door, ancient and sacredly playful, his eyes bright with riddles. [167] "A thief," he said, "who climbs through a broken sky must be either desperate or delighted." [183] "Delighted," I admitted, brushing ice from my coat. [191] He laughed and let me in, for delight, he believed, was the only honest reason to do anything dangerous. [210] The chamber held the board on a pillar of blue ice, and the pieces glinted like teeth. [227] "You may take their secrets," the Hermit said, "but the board must stay full, or the spire forgets how to stand." [248] So I worked with cautious abandon, swapping the hand-carved chess pieces with secrets inside for blank replicas I carried in my satchel. [270] Each pawn I lifted, I set its twin down, so the board never noticed the theft. [286] The first knight held a folded map of a sea that no longer existed. [300] A bishop cradled a lock of hair from someone the Hermit had loved before the ice took the world. [319] The queen hid a pocket sundial lid, its little hinge engraved with a sun that pointed at no hour at all. [340] I held the lid to the broken sky and understood: it had been carved on a night exactly like this one. [361] The wrong sky was not a wound; it was a recurrence, a season that returned to anyone brave enough to climb. [382] The Hermit watched me cradle the sundial lid and smiled. [392] "That one is mine," he said softly. "I left it for the next apprentice of wonder." [408] I realized then that he had once climbed this spire too, hungry to know, and had stayed to guard the wonder he found. [431] The amend the legend offered was not a chance to fix the past, but a chance to forgive it. [450] He had been waiting decades for someone to take the secrets down to the warm world below. [467] I could amend his long solitude simply by carrying his story onward. [479] So I left the blank pieces in their squares and tucked the secrets against my chest. [495] But the chivalrous part of me could not simply rob a lonely man of his only company. [512] So I offered him the one secret I owned: my own name, and the road back down. [529] "Come with me," I said, "and let the spire forget how to stand." [542] He looked at the wrong sky, that sacredly playful grin returning, and for the first time in years he chose to be reckless. [565] We descended as the ice began to sigh and loosen behind us. [577] He carried the pocket sundial lid, and I carried him when his old legs faltered. [592] By dawn the sky had righted itself, and the spire was gone, a cloud where a tower had stood. [611] I had come to steal wonder and learned instead to share it, embracing the unknown with open arms and a friend at my side. [635]</story>
