<story>After the happiness mandate, sound itself was rationed. [8] The state had remade hush into law, declaring that grief, spoken aloud, infected the contented. [23] So I came to the farmhouse overshadowed by giant wind turbines, where silence was easy to keep. [40] The blades turned slow above the roof, swallowing whatever a person might dare to say. [55] My sister had lived here once, and now her dried flower bouquet hung from a nail by the window, brittle and brown. [77] She had died grieving, and grieving was forbidden, so no one had come to mourn her but me. [95] I am a performer, or I was, before performances were required to end in approved laughter. [111] My face had grown blank from the work of pretending; the mask no longer lifted when the show was done. [131] Here, alone, I found a new audience: the turbines, the dust, the cold hearth. [145] I began to perform the only way left to me, dramatically narrating mundane tasks until they swelled into ceremony. [164] "Behold," I told the empty kitchen, "the sweeping of the floor, the great migration of dust toward the door." [183] My voice felt like nothing, an emotional void poured into the room, but the words still moved. [200] Each evening I would whittle at a length of pine, narrating every shaving as though it were a falling kingdom. [220] I told myself this was therapy, a way to cultivate lightness in grief, to make sorrow small enough to carry. [240] But there was a second purpose, one I admitted only to the wind. [253] The whittled pieces were not idle; they were keys, slender hooks I was shaping to break the silencing device buried beneath the farmhouse. [276] Every farm hid one, a regulator that fed the remade hush back into the grid. [291] If I freed this one, the turbines would no longer eat my sister's name. [305] Yet I knew the cost: breaking it would unleash grief across forty contented households, who had never asked to remember pain. [326] Was that mercy or cruelty? [331] I could not tell, and so my purpose remained morally ambiguous, even to myself. [345] On the last night I knelt by the cellar hatch and lifted the dried flower bouquet down from its nail. [365] "Witness," I announced to no one, "the descent of the mourner, bearing the last flowers of the world." [383] The cellar held the device, a humming box ringed with locks shaped to no ordinary key. [399] My whittled pine slid in perfectly, the way grief slides into a quiet heart. [413] I narrated the turning, the click, the long exhale of the machine surrendering. [426] Then the hush broke, and the turbines above slowed as if listening. [438] Sound rushed back into the valley like blood into a sleeping limb. [450] I waited for the void inside me to fill, for the lightness I had labored toward. [466] Instead I heard the first cry drift across the fields, then another, then a chorus of forty households learning to weep. [487] Some of those tears would be rage; some would be relief at last permitted. [501] I had given them my sister's inheritance, and I did not know if they would thank me or hunt me. [521] Standing in the unlocked dark, I lifted the bouquet and finally spoke her name aloud. [536] It came out flat, performed, a blank-faced man's offering to a room that could now answer. [552] And in the spaces between the dying gusts, I narrated my own grief at last, slowly, until the heaviness thinned into something I could almost call lightness. [579] The petals crumbled in my fingers as I spoke, scattering over the silent machine. [593] I told the cellar the small history of her: how she laughed, how she sewed, how she hid her sadness so the mandate would not take her. [620] Footsteps gathered above me, drawn by sound they had forgotten how to fear. [633] I climbed the stairs to meet them, the dried stems still in my hand, my blank face ready for whatever verdict the loud new world would speak. [660] Whatever came, the hush was remade no longer, and grief, at least, had a voice again. [676]</story>
