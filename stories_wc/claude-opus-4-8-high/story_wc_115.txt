<story>Mara called herself a reckless abandoned-mill explorer, but tonight the dead mill had spat her out into an abandoned train tunnel. [21] She walked in that state she loved best, a drowsy alertness, half-asleep yet wired to every drip and echo. [40] Her brother Theo had confessed something an hour ago, and the hush that followed had driven her underground. [58] He'd admitted he was the one who'd let their mother's old factory burn, the night the last shift never clocked out. [79] Mara wanted to weigh that swallowed truth, to find its real heft before she decided whether to forgive him. [98] She carried a battered radio, because she appraised the world's mysteries the only honest way she knew. [115] Being religiously skeptical, she trusted no scripture, only the frantic calls to late-night talk-radio psychics that bled through the tunnel's stale air. [137] A woman's voice crackled out, begging a psychic to explain why her dead husband kept turning a wheel in her dreams. [158] Mara froze, because there, bolted to a corroded pipe ahead, sat a beaded valve wheel. [173] The pipe ran from the factory above straight into this tunnel, an artery the workers had once used to vent steam. [194] She listened harder, interpreting the next caller's panic as if it were a map. [208] A man insisted his late coworkers still reported for a ghost shift, machines humming with no living hands to feed them. [229] The skeptic in her wanted to laugh, yet the valve wheel had begun, slowly, to spin. [245] Its beads clicked like rosary stones she had always refused to count. [257] Steam she could not explain hissed from the pipe, warm against the tunnel's cold throat. [272] On the radio, the psychic told the frightened man that the dead keep working a debt until someone names it aloud. [293] Mara understood the message was meant, somehow, for her. [302] The factory's ghost shift was not haunting the building; it was haunting the guilt that had leaked down here through the pipe. [324] She gripped the beaded valve wheel and felt how heavy it truly was, far heavier than rusted metal should be. [344] This, she realized, was the weight she had come to measure. [355] The swallowed truth had mass; Theo's confession had not lightened him, only moved the burden onto her hands. [373] The radio sputtered, another caller weeping about footsteps pacing an empty factory floor at three in the morning. [391] Mara closed her eyes and did the thing she had sworn no skeptic ever should. [406] She spoke into the steam, naming the fire, naming her brother, naming the workers who had died unpaid. [424] She said the truth aloud so it could no longer sit swallowed inside anyone. [438] The valve wheel slowed under her palms, beads settling like a held breath released. [452] The steam thinned, the humming faded, and the ghost shift, debt finally named, clocked out for good. [469] On the radio the psychic went quiet, as if her line had finally connected to something real. [486] Mara stayed in that drowsy alertness a while longer, listening to her own pulse fill the tunnel. [503] She had appraised the weight at last, and found it was meant to be carried, not buried. [520] When she finally climbed back toward the world, she carried the beaded valve wheel under her arm. [537] It weighed less now, the way any truth does once it is spoken instead of swallowed. [553] Theo waited at the tunnel mouth, having followed the same hush that had pulled her down. [569] She handed him the wheel and told him plainly that the dead were no longer working his guilt. [587] He took it, surprised by how ordinary it felt, just beaded metal warmed by her hands. [603] They walked home together, two skeptics who had learned that some weights must be named before they can ever be set down. [625]</story>
