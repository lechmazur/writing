<story>After the shutdown, the station kept only its echoed silence. [10] I drifted onto the time dilation observation deck, where the great windows still framed a sky no longer ours. [29] They called me the blessed pariah, the one the engines spared and the people could not forgive. [46] Blessed, because I survived; pariah, because survival felt like theft. [56] On the deck, time had always run strangely, every hour here temporally stretched against the years racing past below. [75] My mother had loved this place. [81] Every anniversary of her wedding, of my birth, of the day she first left the homeworld, she kept a family custom of sketching the night sky. [107] She would press a gilded compass rose applique onto each finished page, its stitched points catching the starlight. [125] The longest point, she said, always marked true north, a direction that meant home no matter how far we traveled. [145] When the reactor failed, she stayed behind to seal the breach, and I was the only one she pushed through the closing door. [168] Now the crew avoided me, certain I had bought my life with hers. [181] So I came to the deck to be alone with the only sky that still held her. [198] I carried her last sketchbook, its final page blank, the applique already pressed into the corner but the stars unmapped. [218] She had died before the anniversary she meant to draw. [228] For weeks I had felt only confusion, a question I could not voice: why me, and what now? [246] On the deck, that confusion finally found something to push against. [257] Through the window I caught a glimpse of a star I did not recognize, a faint blue point trembling at the edge of the dark. [282] It had no place on any chart we owned, born from light that had traveled while our clocks crawled. [301] Something in me shifted, the grief turning slowly toward wonder. [311] I opened the sketchbook and began to draw. [319] I marked the familiar constellations first, the ones my mother had taught my hand to trace. [335] Then I added the blue stranger, and beside it I wrote a single word: explore. [350] The applique's longest point still aimed at true north, toward the home she had left behind. [366] But I turned the page in my mind and let true north become a beginning instead of a return. [385] If the deck stretched my hours so long, then I would use them to find where that blue star led. [405] The silence around me no longer felt like accusation; it felt like patience. [418] I imagined my mother pressing the applique one last time, blessing the page she would never see finished. [436] Perhaps that was why I had been spared, not as a thief, but as a witness. [452] Someone had to carry the custom forward, to keep sketching skies no one else would pause to see. [470] After the shutdown, with the engines cold and the corridors hollow, I had assumed our journey was over. [488] Now I understood the journey had merely lost its old direction and needed a new one drawn by hand. [507] I pressed my thumb to the gilded compass rose, feeling its raised threads like a vow. [523] Tomorrow I would bring the others here, one by one, and show them the stranger in the dark. [541] Let them hate me if they must, but let them also glimpse what waited beyond their grief. [558] Confusion could become exploration; that was the only inheritance worth keeping. [569] On the temporally stretched deck, an hour was enough to chart a future. [582] I finished the sketch as the blue star climbed higher, and for the first time the echoed silence sounded like the hush before a launch. [607] Below us the years would keep racing, but here, in stretched time, I had room to decide who we would become. [628] I closed the book over the gilded rose and turned toward true north, ready at last to follow it forward. [648]</story>
