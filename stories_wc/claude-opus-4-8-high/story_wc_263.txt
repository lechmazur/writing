<story>Elias had always been a moonstruck dreamer, the kind of boy who forgot to eat when the night sky opened above him. [22] Now grown, he kept watch beneath an aurora-lit archway at the edge of the world, where the stone curved like a question no one had answered. [48] The archway hummed with green and violet fire, and Elias believed it marked the place where dreams escaped into the heavens. [69] He had come here for one purpose: to free the trapped creativity that had hardened inside him like a seed refusing to bloom. [92] For too long his imagination had been a caged thing, mute and pacing, and the silence had nearly broken him. [112] In his hands he carried an astronomical instrument, a brass astrolabe inherited from his grandmother, who had whispered that it could measure more than stars. [137] She had told him the device read the sky's hidden grammar, and that a true dreamer could use it to chart the route back to wonder. [163] The method was simple to say and difficult to do: he must reclaim his lost art by tracing the path of falling stars. [186] So he lifted the astrolabe, aligned its rings against the shivering aurora, and waited for the heavens to fall. [205] The first meteor came at midnight, a thin white scratch, and Elias followed its arc with the instrument's sight. [224] As the streak burned, he felt something stir, and he realized he could hear the unspoken words the star left behind. [245] They were not language but pure intention, the things the universe had meant but never managed to say. [263] Each falling star carried a syllable of remembered stardust, the ancient material from which both planets and poets were made. [283] Elias understood then that his creativity had not died; it had simply forgotten that it once belonged to the sky. [303] Night after night he returned, working across an unwritten calendar, refusing to count days because the heavens kept no such ledger. [324] He measured each meteor, transcribed its whispered intention, and stitched the syllables into verses that glowed faintly on his page. [344] A shadowed rapture filled him, a joy edged with grief, for every gift the stars gave was also a death he witnessed. [366] He loved them as they died, and that contradiction was the very engine of his returning art. [383] The caged thing inside him began to loosen its bars, syllable by glowing syllable. [397] Then, on a night without number, the aurora flared so brightly that the archway seemed to breathe. [414] A great shower of stars descended at once, and the unspoken words arrived in a flood Elias could barely hold. [434] He wrote until his hand cramped, until the astrolabe grew warm, until the page could hold no more light. [453] When the last meteor faded, the cage within him was empty, and his creativity stood free at last. [471] Exhausted and luminous, Elias finally lay down beneath the archway to rest. [483] He pressed the astrolabe to his chest and closed his eyes, listening to the quiet hum of green fire. [502] In his dream he saw his grandmother smiling, and he heard, without words, that she had always known he would return to wonder. [525] When he woke, the verses on his page had hardened into something permanent, gleaming faintly with remembered stardust. [543] He understood that the dreamer's task was never to invent beauty, only to remember where it had fallen. [561] And so the moonstruck dreamer kept his vigil beneath the aurora, tracing each falling star, freeing one trapped wonder after another, for as long as the sky kept no calendar at all. [593] Each night the stars fell, and each night a little more of him came alive again. [609]</story>
