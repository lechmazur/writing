Mira had always been called the vigilant horizon mapper, the one who charted where the sky met the world's edge. [19]

She kept her instruments inside a basalt sanctuary by a frozen lake, where the black stone held the cold like a held breath. [44]

The villagers had built the sanctuary generations ago, after the lake freezes over each winter, so a watcher could read the seasons from its windows. [69]

Mira's task this year was different, though no one yet understood it. [81]

She had come to sculpt patience from impatience, to take the restless hunger of her people and shape it into something that could endure. [105]

For the lowland folk were impatient, yoked to a tyrant who measured their lives in quotas and fear. [123]

They wanted to revolt at once, with torches and broken gates. [134]

Mira knew that a fire lit too early burns only those who light it. [148]

So she had chosen another way, a patient revolution, slow as ice thickening over deep water. [164]

Each night she climbed the sanctuary's stair and watched the sky, tracking moon paths across the frozen surface below. [183]

The moon's reflection slid over the lake like a coin across a table, and from its angle she learned the exact night the ice would bear a marching weight. [211]

This was her method: by tracking moon paths she would know when the people could cross unseen, when the frozen lake became a silent road around the tyrant's walls. [240]

For now, she taught them to wait. [247]

She was softly purposeful in her teaching, never raising her voice, only repeating that the lake must thicken, that haste would crack the surface and drown them all. [275]

The young ones chafed. [278]

A boy named Tarl came each evening with questions sharp as flint. [290]

"Why do we sit while he taxes our grain?" he demanded, his breath clouding the basalt air. [307]

"That is our second road," she said, "but only if we move when the storms confuse his sentries, not before." [368]

Tarl frowned, but he listened, and listening, he began to change. [380]

There was no great battle. [532]

"You taught us to wait," he said, "and waiting made us strong." [670]

"Impatience wants the harvest before the seed," she answered. [685]
