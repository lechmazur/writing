<story>Mordecai had buried the whole coast and still the dead kept arriving. [12] He was a weary undertaker, a man whose hands knew the weight of stillness better than the warmth of any living thing. [34] After the mysterious package arrived, that stillness began to chase him. [45] It came wrapped in oilcloth, smelling of brine, with no sender and no postmark. [59] Inside lay a broken lighthouse lens, its prisms cracked like a frozen tear. [72] When he lifted it, light bent wrong, and the room folded around a doorway that had not been there before. [92] The lens, it seemed, had a habit of occupying impossible spaces, opening passages where geometry refused to agree. [110] Mordecai stepped through and found himself walking, dry and breathing, along the flooded avenues of a forgotten underwater city library. [130] Shelves rose like coral cliffs, their books swollen and silent, their ink dissolved into the green dark. [147] Something here had been calling, he realized; the lens had carried distress signals across the years. [163] Faint pulses of light still throbbed in the lens whenever he neared the library's drowned heart. [179] There, among toppled catalog drawers, he met the keeper, a translucent woman woven from old lamplight. [195] She had been the lighthouse spirit once, she explained, the eye that warned ships from the rocks. [212] When the sea swallowed the city, it swallowed her too, and her broken lens drifted for a century, blinking for help. [233] No one ever answered, until an undertaker who collected the unclaimed dead finally listened. [247] She wanted only one thing: to revitalize the library so its stories could rest somewhere real instead of dissolving forever. [267] Mordecai understood the wish better than most. [274] His whole life had been spent trying to give rest a permanent address. [287] He had grown inadvertently stubborn about it, refusing to leave any soul unmarked, any grave untended. [303] So he set to work without being asked. [311] Using the lens, he opened the impossible spaces wider, threading dry air through drowned halls. [326] He gathered the swollen books and laid them out like bodies he intended to honor. [341] Where ink had fled, the keeper's lamplight poured back into the pages, and faded words rose again. [358] Sentence by sentence, the forgotten city library remembered how to speak. [369] But the work demanded a price the lens had been hiding. [380] To anchor the stories permanently, the broken lens needed a steady keeper, a still center that would never drift again. [400] The spirit could maintain the light, but she could not also hold the doorway shut against the hungry sea. [419] Someone living had to stay and become the place where rest was filed and kept. [434] Mordecai thought of his cold cottage, his ledgers of the dead, the stillness that had chased him for forty years. [454] He had spent that whole life seeking a permanent address for other people's rest. [468] Here, at last, was one he could inhabit himself. [477] He pressed his palm to the cracked prisms and let the light claim him. [491] The keeper wept lamplight tears and thanked him, then dimmed gratefully into rest of her own. [507] Now Mordecai is the lens and the lens is Mordecai. [517] He occupies the impossible space between the drowned shelves and the dry, waiting air. [531] The distress signals have finally stopped, for nothing here is calling for rescue anymore. [545] Instead, his light turns slow and patient through the forgotten city library, cataloging every story that washes down to him. [565] Drowned sailors, lost letters, the unread last words of the sea's countless dead all find a shelf. [582] The weary undertaker is weary no longer, because the stillness he fled has become the stillness he tends. [600] He has given rest a permanent address, and the address, at last, is also his home. [616]</story>
