<story>Mireille descended into the catacomb orchard, where tree roots snaked between bones like patient fingers. [15] She was a reckless idealist, and the dead had always seemed to her merely waiting. [30] Within the glow of half-lit corridors, lanterns guttered against damp limestone, and pale apples hung from branches fed by the buried. [51] She had come to barter a destiny. [58] In her coat pocket lay a burned tarot card, its edges charred to lace, the painted figure on it scorched beyond reading. [80] It had been her brother's card, the only thing salvaged from the fire that took him. [96] The orchard's keeper waited beneath the largest tree, a woman whose hands moved as if inlaying meaning everywhere she touched. [116] She pressed symbols into bark, set stones among the roots, knotted ribbons to bones, until the whole orchard read like a sentence no one could finish. [142] "You want him back," the keeper said, fiercely gentle, her voice both blade and balm. [157] "I want to patch the holes in my soul," Mireille answered. [168] Since the fire, she had felt herself a sieve, every joy draining through gaps grief had torn. [185] The keeper explained the trade with dusky resolve. [193] Here destinies were bartered: a life's untaken paths planted as seeds, grown into fruit that strangers ate to mend their own. [214] To pull her brother's future from the burned card, Mireille would have to surrender a future of her own. [233] "Lay the card among the roots," the keeper instructed. [242] Mireille knelt, and the cold bones gave beneath her knees. [252] She set the charred card against a thick root, and the wood seemed to drink its ashes. [269] A sapling rose at once, fragile and silver, bearing a single fruit shaped like a sleeping face. [286] "Eat," said the keeper, "and his destiny continues in you, but the trammel binds." [300] Mireille hesitated, for she finally understood the trammel: a net of obligation, her years yoked to carrying a life not hers. [321] His ambitions would crowd her own; his unspent days would spend hers. [333] The reckless part of her reached for the fruit anyway, certain love could outrun any cost. [349] But the keeper caught her wrist, fiercely gentle once more. [359] "The holes in your soul cannot be patched with someone else's cloth," she warned. [373] "You would only trade one emptiness for a heavier one." [383] Mireille looked at the sleeping fruit, at her brother's face caught between waking and root. [398] She thought of him laughing, fiercely alive, and knew he would refuse to live inside her like a debt. [417] Within the glow of those half-lit corridors, her recklessness cooled into resolve. [429] She would not barter his destiny back, nor trammel herself to a borrowed tomorrow. [443] Instead, she plucked the silver fruit, and she planted it whole among the bones. [457] "Let his paths feed the orchard," she whispered, "so strangers might be mended by his kindness." [473] It was, she realized, the most idealistic thing she had ever done, and the most honest. [489] The keeper smiled and resumed her work, inlaying meaning everywhere, knotting one new ribbon for the sapling's roots. [507] Mireille rose, dusting bone-dust from her knees. [514] The card was gone, given fully to the soil, and somehow the gaps in her felt smaller. [531] Grief had not left, but it had become a thing that grew rather than only burned. [547] She walked back through the half-lit corridors, and the apples glowed faintly, fed by everyone who had ever chosen to let go. [569] At the threshold she paused, fiercely gentle now with herself. [579] Some holes, she understood, were not meant to be patched but tended. [591] And carrying that dusky resolve, she climbed toward the living light above. [603]</story>
