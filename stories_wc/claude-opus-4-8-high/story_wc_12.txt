<story>Father Ansel was called the loop pedal priest because he preached through layered echoes, stacking his own voice into walls of sound. [22] His chapel was no chapel at all, but a dimensional anchor, a stone room that held the seams of reality together where they had begun to fray. [49] Something was trying to enter the world, and the priest had perhaps an hour before the possession completes. [67] The thing had chosen a vessel: a young painter named Lira, who sat motionless at the center of the room. [87] Her stillness was unnatural, an unraveled stillness, as if the calm itself were coming apart thread by thread to make room for another tenant. [111] In her lap rested an artist's palette with one color, a single smear of deep blue that she had not stopped staring at for days. [136] Ansel understood his enemy: it fed on certainty, on the assumptions people never questioned, and it could only be banished if those assumptions were dragged into the light. [164] His true weapon was not holy water but language; he fought through rhetorical devices, sharpening questions until they cut. [183] He pressed his loop pedal and let his first words repeat beneath him like a tide. [199] "Why blue?" he asked, and the question circled the room, doubling, tripling, until it became a chorus. [216] Lira's lips moved without her permission. [222] "Because," the borrowed voice murmured, "blue is sorrow, and sorrow is true." [234] There it was, the hidden assumption, presented as if it were stone. [246] Ansel's mission was to illuminate hidden assumptions, to plumb the dark well beneath them until he struck the false floor that the creature stood upon. [271] "Is blue sorrow," he sang, layering the line, "or is it the sky, or the sea, or the cold heart of a flame?" [294] He used the loop to surround her with contradictions, each repetition a mirror angled at her certainty. [311] The room, which the entity had decorated with silence, began to crack with the weight of his stacked voices. [330] For the creature, silence was a garment, a stillness it draped over thought so that no one would examine what they believed. [352] But Ansel's looped questions filled every quiet corner, and the quiet had nowhere left to hide. [368] A strange muffled clarity settled over the chamber, the way truth feels when heard underwater, distorted yet undeniable. [386] "You assume she is empty enough to fill," Ansel said, and looped it. [399] "You assume her sorrow is yours to claim." [407] Lira's hand twitched toward the palette, toward that single, stubborn blue. [418] He pressed harder, his voice a question made of questions: "If sorrow were truly hers, why would she need you to feel it?" [441] The creature faltered, its borrowed certainty exposed as scaffolding around nothing. [452] Lira blinked, and for the first time in days the stillness in her eyes broke into motion. [469] She looked at the one blue color and, slowly, dipped a finger into it. [483] On the bare wall she pressed her blue into a shape of her own choosing, and choosing was the one thing the creature could not survive. [509] The entity unraveled, not with a scream but with a sigh, the stillness it wore dissolving thread by thread. [528] The dimensional anchor groaned, its frayed seams knitting shut as the pressure released. [541] Ansel lifted his foot from the pedal, and the looped voices faded one by one, leaving only his own breath. [561] Lira wept, and the priest let her, for grief examined freely is no longer a door for monsters. [579] He gathered his pedal and her palette, the single blue now smeared into something shaped by a living hand. [598] Outside, the world held, quietly, the way a question holds an answer it has not yet spoken. [615]</story>
