<story>Mira had been the beacon-tower custodian since the collapse of civilization, tending a light no ships remained to read. [19] Each dusk she climbed the spiral stairs of the glass humming pavilion, where wind through fluted panes sang a low, endless chord. [41] The world below had gone quiet, but the pavilion never did. [52] Mira was retrocognitively blessed, a gift that let her touch any object and witness the moments that had shaped it. [72] Most people had called such talents coincidence, mere tricks of a desperate mind. [85] She lived now to prove that coincidences have meaning, that nothing aligned by accident. [99] Her evidence sat on the windowsill: a broken crystal prism she had found buried in the ash of the old observatory. [120] When she held it, visions arrived only in fragments, for the prism was cracked through its heart. [137] Yet within those fragments she had glimpsed something the histories never named: a shard of hush. [153] It was a sliver of perfect silence, sealed inside the prism, older than the noise that had drowned the human world. [174] Mira believed that if she could free that hush and join it to the beacon, she could rekindle the old light into something true again. [199] But a broken prism cannot bend light, and a shattered thing cannot be mended by force. [215] The visions had taught her another way: the prism had originally been ground and aimed by tracking star paths. [234] Each facet matched a constellation, and only when starlight struck the correct angle would the crack seal itself in resonance. [254] So night after night she lay on the pavilion floor, charting which stars rose where, listening to the glass hum guide her. [276] The humming, she realized, was the pavilion remembering the old sky, singing the very paths the builders had once followed. [296] That was no coincidence; that was meaning, waiting to be heard. [307] On the equinox the constellation she had been waiting for crested the eastern panes. [321] Mira lifted the broken prism into the precise beam, her hands steady with thistle-blown hope, light as seeds carried on a doubtful wind. [344] Starlight entered the fracture, and the pavilion's hum climbed to meet it. [356] For one held breath the crack glowed, then drew itself closed, and the shard of hush slipped loose into the air. [377] Silence fell so complete that even the humming stopped, and Mira understood that the hush had been the pavilion's missing note all along. [400] Into that stillness she carried the mended prism up to the dark beacon and set it within the cold lamp. [420] The prism caught the same starlight and threw it outward, and the beacon began to rekindle, not with fire but with gathered starlight. [443] A clean beam swept across the ruined valley, touching the broken towns where a few survivors still huddled. [461] Within nights, lanterns answered from distant ridges, strangers reading the light and walking toward it. [476] They arrived at the glass humming pavilion expecting a custodian's tomb and found instead a gathering place. [493] Mira touched each newcomer's hand and, retrocognitively blessed, saw the long, tangled roads that had braided their paths to hers. [513] None of it was accident; every wrong turn had bent them, eventually, toward the light. [528] She had proven what she set out to prove: that coincidences have meaning, that scattered seeds still find their soil. [548] After the collapse of civilization, no one had believed a single quiet woman could call a broken world back to itself. [569] Yet the hush remained inside the pavilion, a held breath, and the beacon turned through the night like a slow patient promise. [591] And every star Mira had tracked still wheeled overhead, lighting the path home for whoever was brave enough to follow. [611]</story>
