<story>Mira sold feelings she did not have. [7] As a counterfeit emotion dealer, she bottled longing for the lonely and courage for the timid, each vial a half truth that worked just well enough to keep her customers loyal. [38] She was professionally thorough, labeling every counterfeit with the exact percentage of genuine sentiment it contained. [54] Her workshop sat inside an amber grotto, where fossilized resin glowed like trapped honey, and at dusk the whole cavern turned the color of slow regret. [80] There she sensed her clients through static electricity, brushing a glass rod against their sleeves until the tiny crackle told her what emotion they lacked. [105] Mira liked her customers the way one likes distant weather: with affectionate detachment, never close enough to be soaked. [124] One evening a fragrance chemist arrived, carrying a blotter petal he had cut into the shape of a real bloom, soaked in a scent he could not name. [152] He wanted to buy empathy. [157] I make scents that move people, he said, but I feel nothing while I do it. [173] I understand emotion completely, yet I cannot share it. [182] Mira ran her glass rod near his coat, and the static snapped sharp and bright, confirming his confession was a half truth. [204] He had insight in abundance but no empathy, like a man who could read every map yet had never walked a road. [226] His motivation, he admitted, was to fuse insight with empathy, so that the perfumes he built might finally mean what they promised. [248] Mira had no genuine empathy to sell, only counterfeits, and she told him so, mostly. [263] What she withheld was the older method, the dangerous one. [273] A true emotion could be grown only by letting one feeling subsume another, the way amber subsumes an insect, preserving it forever inside something larger. [298] To make him whole, his cold insight would have to be subsumed by borrowed warmth until the two became a single living thing. [321] Such a fusion required a real donor, and Mira, professionally thorough, knew exactly whose feeling she would lend. [339] Her own. [341] She pressed the blotter petal to his wrist and to hers, joining them, then drew the glass rod between, coaxing the static into a steady, humming current. [368] The grotto's amber light deepened to ember as dusk thickened. [378] Through the petal, his understanding flowed into her, cataloguing her every guarded distance; through the rod, her warmth flowed into him, soft and unguarded for the first time in years. [408] She felt his insight name the lie she lived: that detachment had ever protected her. [423] He felt her warmth, and understood, at last, what his maps had always missed. [437] As the transformation completed, the current fell silent, and the petal crumbled to fragrant ash between their hands. [455] He stood changed, insight and empathy fused into one quiet certainty, and he wept without quite knowing why. [473] Mira watched him, and found the old detachment thinner now, worn through in one tender spot she could not seal. [493] She had given away real warmth, and the counterfeits on her shelves looked suddenly cheap. [508] Yet she smiled, affectionate and a little distant, as if observing her own loss from a comfortable balcony. [526] Thank you, the chemist said, and offered to pay. [535] Keep it, she answered; you only bought back what was already yours. [547] Another half truth, for she had kept a fraction of his insight for herself, the better to read the next lonely customer. [569] She was, after all, professionally thorough. [575] When he left, the grotto dimmed to its last amber spark, and Mira sat among her bottled half truths, finally feeling one true thing she had no intention of selling. [605]</story>
