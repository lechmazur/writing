<story>High in the ashen skyborne arbor, where roots clutched cloud instead of soil, lived Marn the orderly seed sorter. [19] Each day he arranged seeds by weight, color, and the soft songs they hummed when shaken. [35] But Marn's true labor was older than order itself: he was building tiny monuments to forgotten things. [52] From bark splinters and dew he raised little shrines to lost names, vanished birds, and rivers nobody remembered. [70] The arbor's regional folklore promised that during the hush where past meets prophecy, the great tree would speak. [88] It would speak not in words but in falling leaves, and only a patient sorter could read them. [106] Marn's single motivation, mossy and quiet as a thought, was to decode the message in those leaves. [123] So he waited, sorting, sorting, until the sky turned the gray of cooled ash. [137] The hush arrived without warning, a silence so complete it had weight. [149] One by one, leaves loosened and spiraled down, each carrying a single shimmering speck. [163] Marn cupped his palms and caught a moonstone cabochon dust speck, smaller than a heartbeat. [178] It glowed with the blue-white milk of buried moons, and it pulsed as if alive. [193] He laid the leaves in rows, sorting them by the patterns of their veins. [207] Slowly, an order emerged, and within the order, a sentence shaped itself. [219] The message read: remember us, and we will remember you. [229] Marn understood at last why his monuments mattered. [237] The arbor itself was a forgotten thing, fading, ashen, its name unspoken for centuries. [251] It had grown gray because no one remembered it. [260] With one careful finger, Marn moved to prod the glowing speck into the heart of his largest shrine. [278] He whispered the tree's true name, the one the folklore had buried in riddles. [292] The dust speck flared, and the shrine drank its light like rain after drought. [306] Then the whole arbor shivered, and Marn laughed with a mossy delight he had not felt in years. [324] Color crept back into the branches, green threading through the ash like returning blood. [338] For in this place, what is remembered emerges transformed always. [348] The forgotten do not merely return; they bloom anew, brighter than before. [360] Marn the seed sorter looked at his shelves of tiny monuments and saw them in a new light. [378] Each shrine was a seed of memory, and memory, he realized, was simply another thing to be planted. [396] He spent the long hush carrying speck after speck to monument after monument. [409] To the lost river he gave a shrine of silver pebbles, and water trickled from nowhere. [425] To the vanished birds he gave a shrine of hollow reeds, and the air filled with forgotten songs. [443] Each name he honored returned, changed, gentler, as though grief had been polished into wonder. [458] The folklore had not lied; the tree spoke, and Marn had answered. [470] When the hush finally lifted, the arbor was no longer ashen but luminous. [483] Its leaves no longer fell as messages, for the message had been received and lived. [498] Marn returned to his sorting table, but he sorted differently now. [509] Beside every seed he placed a small monument, a pebble, a feather, a word. [523] He had learned that nothing truly grows unless it is also remembered. [535] The single moonstone cabochon dust speck he kept, tucked in a hollow above his heart. [550] On quiet evenings he would prod it gently, and it would glow, reminding him of the hush. [567] Travelers who climbed the skyborne arbor would find an old sorter humming among his shrines. [582] He would tell them the folklore, then hand them a seed and a stone. [596] Plant one, he said, and honor the other, for what is loved emerges transformed always. [611] And the arbor, no longer forgotten, kept on growing greener with every grateful breath. [625]</story>
