<story>Beyond the murmur of lost languages, where forgotten syllables dissolved into dust, Vael the marsh-reed sculptor climbed toward the crystalline orchard. [21] It perched at the edge of a shimmering crater, its glassy branches faceted like frozen tears, ringing softly whenever the wind passed through. [44] All his life he had woven reeds into figures, yet the marsh had emptied of people, and he was tired of making likenesses no one else would ever see. [73] He had come chasing rumor of a settlement among the crystal trees, hoping to graft himself onto some living community before his own voice joined the lost tongues. [101] At the orchard's heart stood a glimmering obsidian arbor, black as a sealed eye, its surface scrawled with painted glyphs. [121] A young woman crouched beneath it, tracing one bright mark with her thumb. [134] "You're looking for it too," she said, not turning around. [144] She introduced herself as Senna, keeper of this place, and explained that an elusive street artist had once tagged every village from here to the dead delta. [171] "Find the true tag," she said, "and you find the thread that ties all our scattered houses together." [189] So began their shared search for the artist's signature, a hunt that pulled Vael from his solitude. [206] They walked the rim of the shimmering crater, where light pooled like spilled honey and every facet of fruit threw back a different face. [230] Senna proved boldly sympathetic, coaxing out his griefs as though they were her own, and Vael felt the strange warmth of being known. [253] They compared the glyphs on the obsidian arbor to faded marks on cellar doors and bridge stones, mapping a hidden web of kinship the artist had drawn across the land. [283] Each tag, they realized, named a family, and the lines between them traced who had loved, married, or mourned whom. [303] The luminescent ache of those records moved through Vael like a tide, beautiful and unbearable at once. [320] Here was the community he craved, yet it had been written by a stranger he could not find. [338] For days they searched, until Senna led him to the crater's lowest terrace, where the oldest crystal tree bore a single unsigned mark. [361] "This was the first," she whispered, "and no one ever knew whose hand it was." [376] Vael understood then that the elusive artist had never wanted a name; the tags were gifts, not signatures. [394] The whole web of kinship had been an invitation, waiting for someone willing to add their own line. [412] So Vael unrolled his bundle of marsh reeds and began to weave. [424] He shaped figures of every villager whose tag they had found, binding their woven hands into a single chain that circled the obsidian arbor. [448] He added himself and Senna at the chain's open end, leaving room for hands yet to come. [465] When the work was done, he chose to dedicate it not to the vanished artist but to everyone the tags remembered. [486] Senna pressed her palm to a fresh reed, and Vael guided it into the weave, giving her a place beside his own. [508] For the first time the sculptor had not made a likeness to be forgotten, but a thread to be continued. [528] Word traveled, and others came up from the delta to find their tags glimmering on the black arbor. [546] Each newcomer added a reed, until the crystalline orchard hummed with more voices than the crater had heard in lifetimes. [566] Vael had finally grafted himself onto something larger than his solitary craft, and the ache in him turned to light. [586] Beyond the murmur of lost languages, a new web of kinship had taken root, woven of reed and obsidian and the simple courage to belong. [611]</story>
