<story>After the climate shifted, the old mansions of the drowned coast were abandoned to ivy and silence. [17] Mira walked among them as a corridor echo cartographer, mapping how sound traveled through forgotten halls. [33] Her work was strange but necessary, for in a world reshaped by rising seas, the surviving houses were the last archives of human memory. [57] She was sensitively perceptive, able to feel the grief held in plaster and the laughter trapped in stairwells. [75] Most cartographers measured echoes for salvage value, but Mira had a different motivation: to discover what walls dream about. [94] She believed buildings remembered, and that their memories surfaced as faint resonances only she could read. [110] The house at the edge of the marsh drew her through an ivy-veiled passage that breathed cold against her face. [130] Inside, her instruments trembled, registering echoes older and sadder than any she had charted before. [145] She followed the resonance to a parlor where a fireplace held a heap of half-burned diaries. [161] Someone had tried to destroy them, yet the flames had only nibbled their edges, leaving the secrets mostly intact. [180] Through the half-burned diaries found in the fireplace, Mira learned the house had belonged to her grandmother. [197] The pages spoke of a family gift passed down like a curse, the ability to hear what stone confessed in the dark. [219] Her grandmother had named it legacy's burden, a duty no descendant could refuse without the walls falling silent forever. [238] Pressed between two scorched pages lay a film negative strip, its tiny frames clouded with age. [254] Mira lifted it toward the gray window and caught a glimpse of figures standing in this very parlor, long before the floods. [276] Among them she recognized a small child holding her grandmother's hand, and she understood the child was her own mother. [296] The diaries explained that her mother had fled this gift, burning the records to spare Mira the same weight. [315] Yet the gift had found her anyway, drawing her across the ruined coast to the house that dreamed of being remembered. [336] Mira pressed her palm to the cold wall and finally let herself listen without her instruments. [352] The walls dreamed, she discovered, not of grand events but of ordinary tenderness. [365] They dreamed of her grandmother humming at dawn, of her mother's first uncertain steps across the floorboards. [382] Mira wept, for she felt the love that the fire had failed to erase. [396] She realized her grandmother had not meant the gift as punishment but as a way to keep the dead from vanishing entirely. [418] The burden, she now saw, was also a tenderness, a promise that no one need be forgotten. [435] Mira gathered the surviving diaries and the fragile film negative strip into her satchel. [449] She would carry them forward, the last cartographer of a family that mapped love instead of land. [466] Outside, the tide gnawed at the foundations, patient and rising, but the house would not fall unremembered. [483] Mira stepped back through the ivy-veiled passage, listening to the corridor echo her grandmother's forgotten lullaby. [499] She had accepted the inheritance, not as a chain, but as a gift she would finally answer. [516] Walking the drowned coast, she promised to record each abandoned house before the water claimed it. [532] Every wall held dreams, and every dream deserved a witness who could feel the tenderness inside. [548] The climate had taken the old world, but memory, Mira decided, could still be salvaged. [563] She paused at the marsh's edge and looked back one last time. [575] In the dim windows she imagined her grandmother smiling, relieved that the listening had not died with her. [593] Mira turned toward the next ruin, her satchel heavy with light and ash, carrying the dreams of walls into whatever world the waters left behind. [618]</story>
