<story>In the Vanishing Point Gallery, where every hallway narrowed toward a single dot of dark, Mireille scattered the ashes of portraits she had burned. [24] She was the charcoal portrait ash scatterer, a title earned by her strange craft: drawing faces in soot, then releasing them to powder into the air. [50] Each portrait captured someone she had loved, and each scattering was meant to crystallize a fleeting emotion before grief could blur it forever. [73] Visitors came believing the gallery offered sanctuary, a hushed refuge from the world outside. [87] Mireille knew better, for she understood that such safety was only a mirage, a comforting trick of the narrowing walls. [107] At the gallery's heart sat an old rocking chair, and in it her grandmother had once rocked, telling stories that wove truth carefully through incomplete information. [133] Her grandmother never lied, yet never told the whole story; she gave just enough for the listener to feel safe. [153] Now the chair held something else: a mask made from shattered porcelain shards, the only object Mireille had refused to burn. [174] It had been her grandmother's face, reassembled after the old woman dropped a teacup the day her memory began to vanish. [195] Mireille had glued each fragment, weaving the broken pieces into a face that could almost smile again. [212] Tonight she had drawn her grandmother one final time in charcoal, the last portrait she would ever make. [230] The old woman had died at dawn, in that very chair, while the gallery pretended nothing had changed. [248] Mireille set the charcoal portrait against the porcelain mask and watched both catch the dim light, a somber marvel of memory and ruin. [271] She knew the ritual now: to scatter the ash was to admit the face was gone, yet also to keep one feeling intact. [294] The chair was still rocking, pushed by her grandmother's last breath, swinging slower with each pass. [310] Mireille understood her own quiet deception: she had told the mourners that the gallery would protect them from sorrow, the same kind half-truth her grandmother once offered. [337] Safety here was the mirage, the gentlest lie, and she had inherited the art of giving people only the truths they could bear. [360] She lifted the charcoal portrait and crushed its edge between her fingers, letting the first powder drift down. [378] Each black grain settled on the porcelain mask, filling the hairline cracks like ink poured into a riverbed. [396] In that union, the fleeting tenderness she feared losing seemed to crystallize at last, fixed in soot and glaze. [415] The hallway behind her dwindled toward its vanishing point, swallowing the light as if the world itself agreed to forget. [435] Mireille scattered the rest of the ash, watching it powder the air and drift toward the slowing chair. [453] She would tell visitors tomorrow that her grandmother had simply gone home, a truth woven carefully through what she chose to leave unsaid. [476] It was not a falsehood; her grandmother had gone home, into ash, into memory, into the cracked mask now darkened with love. [498] The gallery stayed silent, offering its illusion of refuge to anyone who needed it more than the truth. [516] Mireille knelt before the chair, and the porcelain mask gazed up at her with a thousand mended seams. [534] She pressed her palm to its cool surface, feeling, beneath the cracks, the warmth she had managed to preserve. [553] Then, as the old rocking chair finally slows to a stop, Mireille closes her eyes and lets the last grain settle. [574] The emotion she sought is captured, not in any image, but in the stillness that follows the final sway. [593] And in that hush, the mirage of safety becomes, for one breath, almost true.
