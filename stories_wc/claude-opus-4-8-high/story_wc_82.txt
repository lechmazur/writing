<story>After the shadow detaches from my heel, I know my daughter is truly gone. [14] I am Veil, the craftiest lantern maker on the floating island tea garden, and grief has finally caught me. [33] Here, between teapots steaming on iron tables, the lanterns I hang glow with a luminous torment that customers mistake for beauty. [54] Each lantern holds a captured dream, for my true craft is dream recording. [67] When sleepers drift beneath the tea boughs, my gem-lined lamps drink their visions and store them like honey. [85] Above us floats the glass basilica on a floating isle, a cathedral of panes where the richest dreams are auctioned. [105] My daughter, Pell, polished the gems that made my lanterns sing. [116] Her gem polishing was patient art; she turned dull stones into clear windows for sleeping minds. [132] Fever took her in spring, and I let her last dream be recorded, sealed inside my brightest lantern. [150] Now the shadow that mirrored my steps has peeled away, leaving me weightless and hollow. [165] I climb the spiral stair toward the basilica, and the strangest thing happens: I descend while ascending. [182] Each upward step sinks me deeper into memory, so that height and grief become the same direction. [199] The auctioneers want Pell's final dream, and they will pay enough to keep the tea garden afloat for a decade. [219] But I am crafty, and I have a different plan for that light. [232] Inside the glass basilica, sound is hushed and the floor reflects clouds drifting far below the floating isle. [250] I set Pell's lantern on the altar, but I do not let the buyers open it. [266] Instead, I begin to fold. [271] My motivation is simple and unbearable: to fold grief into origami cranes. [283] I had learned, through years of dream recording, that a captured vision can be poured onto paper if the paper is thin enough. [306] So I crack the lantern's seal and let Pell's last dream spill across translucent sheets. [321] The dream is small: she is six again, chasing paper birds through the tea boughs, laughing. [337] Each sheet I fold begins to transform, becoming a crane that glows with her remembered laughter. [353] The auctioneers gasp, for the cranes are worth more than any sealed dream, yet they cannot be sold. [371] Folded grief belongs to no buyer; it belongs to the one who folds it. [385] One by one, I fold seven cranes, and with each, a little weight returns to my chest. [402] This is the meaning of descending while ascending: by emptying the lantern, I climb back toward the living. [420] The luminous torment does not vanish, but it changes shape, the way light changes when it passes through polished gems. [440] I think of Pell's gem polishing, how she always said a stone only shines once you stop fighting its shape. [460] When the last crane settles on the altar, the empty lantern dims, and I feel her dream finally rest. [479] The buyers leave the glass basilica disappointed, their coins useless against paper birds. [492] I carry the seven cranes back down to the floating island tea garden, where lanterns still burn. [509] I hang them among the boughs, and they sway over the steaming pots like small bright ghosts. [526] Customers come to sit beneath them, and they say their tea tastes faintly of childhood. [541] Some fall asleep, and I let those dreams stay their own, recording nothing. [554] I am still the craftiest lantern maker on the isle, but my craft has changed. [569] I no longer trap dreams to sell; I fold sorrows until they learn to fly. [584] At dusk, when the shadow tries to return to my heel, I let it find me lighter than it remembers. [604] The cranes glow, the tea steams, and grief, transformed, finally holds still enough to love. [619]</story>
