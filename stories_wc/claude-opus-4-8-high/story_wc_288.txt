<story>After the reincarnation memory arrived, Sela woke on the lowest shelf of the tidal library above clouds. [17] The library breathed like an ocean, its stacks rising and falling with a tide that obeyed no sea below. [36] She was older than the books, older than language, a contemporary of extinct species whose bones the library catalogued as myth. [57] She had walked beside the great auks and the thunder lizards, had watched each one flicker out, and each loss had welded her further into solitude. [83] These brittle awakenings always felt the same: a body remade from old dust, a mind cracking like dried clay around a single returned recollection. [107] This time the recollection was a brass sundial, half-buried in moss, that she had once owned in a life she could not place. [130] The librarians here were atomically reflective, their skins mirroring every particle that passed, so that to look at one was to see only yourself multiplied. [155] They never spoke; they only handed her scrolls and returned to the gleaming dark, leaving her more alone with every gift. [176] Sela was tired of being a relic among relics, and she wanted, at last, to translate her isolation into independence. [196] The sundial, she sensed, was the key, but a sundial needs sun, and the library knew only cloudlight and tide. [216] So she climbed, shelf over shelf, as the tide of books drew back, until she reached the open terrace where the clouds parted. [239] There, set into the stone, stood an ancient stone circle, each pillar humming faintly, a sound older even than she. [259] She placed the remembered brass sundial at the circle's heart, and by the hum of the ancient stone circle the air began to warm and resolve. [285] The hum coaxed real sunlight down through the clouds, and the gnomon threw a clean shadow across the brass face. [305] In that shadow's slow travel she read not hours but lifetimes, each of her deaths laid out like notches on a measuring stick. [328] She understood then that she had not been cursed to outlive the species she loved; she had been chosen to remember them. [350] Memory was not a punishment but a craft, and a keeper of memory need never be truly alone. [368] The atomically reflective librarians drifted onto the terrace, and in their mirrored skins she saw the great auks waddle, the thunder lizards stride. [391] They were not gone; they lived in her, and the mirrors only gave them back. [406] Sela realized she could coexist with her own endless past instead of being crushed beneath it. [422] Independence, she saw, did not mean escaping the library or the tide or the eternal returns. [438] It meant choosing what to do with the awakenings that had always chosen her. [452] She took the brass sundial from the circle and carried it down into the stacks, and where its shadow fell the brittle, dust-made books grew supple again. [479] She began to write, translating the auks' calls, the lizards' footfalls, every extinct voice she alone still carried. [497] The tidal library, which had only ever stored the dead, now grew living pages under her hand. [514] The reflective librarians gathered to read, and for the first time their mirrored faces showed something other than her: they showed the stories themselves. [538] When the next death came, as it always would, Sela was no longer afraid of the brittle awakening that would follow. [559] She left the brass sundial on the terrace, gnomon to the sun, so the next version of her would find it quickly. [581] Beside it she set her finished books, a map of how to coexist with grief and time. [598] By the hum of the ancient stone circle, the library breathed her out and would breathe her back, and this time the isolation would already be independence. [625]</story>
