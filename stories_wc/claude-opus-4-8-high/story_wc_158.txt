<story>Mira had not slept in three hundred years, and she had stopped apologizing for it. [15] An insomniac poet by trade and torment, she lived inside a gargantuan shell that the tide had abandoned on the edge of a drowned city. [40] Its spiral walls glowed with reef-shadow joy, that strange happiness that only thrives where light and dark trade places. [59] She had come here to ensconce herself away from the world that had broken her heart. [75] Long ago, two nations had warred over the reef, until a strange truce called the blossom armistice ended the bloodshed. [95] The terms were simple: every soldier planted a flower instead of raising a blade, and Mira, then young, had planted the first. [117] Her lover had not survived to see the petals open. [127] He had left her only one keepsake, a galaxy postcard, its inked spiral of distant stars eerily echoing the shell she now called home. [151] Across centuries of longing she had read its faded message a thousand times: Grow something from the grief. [169] So that became her vow, to photosynthesize pain into beauty, to turn the darkness in her chest into something that could feed on light and bloom. [195] Each sleepless night she wrote poems on the curving walls of the shell. [208] She delivered them the only way the drowned could hear, via half-sung lullabies carried on an off-key wind. [226] The wind was a clumsy courier, always cracking on the high notes, yet it never failed to reach the sleepers beneath the waves. [249] Her grief, she discovered, was shyly unstoppable. [256] It hesitated at every doorway, duck-headed and apologetic, and then it walked through anyway, year after year, refusing to be small. [277] From that quiet, relentless ache, gardens grew. [284] Where her lullabies settled, anemones unfurled into verses, and coral spelled out the names of everyone the war had taken. [304] Travelers came to the shell to read the living poems, and many wept, and many, oddly, laughed. [321] That was the reef-shadow joy again, sorrow and delight braided so tightly that neither could exist alone. [338] One night a boy arrived, clutching a postcard identical to hers. [349] He was the great-grandson of the soldier who had stood across the field from her lover, the one who had planted the second blossom of the armistice. [376] He had inherited the same insomnia, the same restless need to make meaning out of mourning. [392] I cannot sleep either, he confessed, and I do not know what to do with all this longing. [410] Mira pressed her galaxy postcard into his palm beside his own. [421] Two spirals, two griefs, one instruction shared across the broken centuries. [432] Grow something from the grief, she said, and taught him the half-sung lullabies, off-key and all. [448] They sang together until the wind picked up their cracked harmony and carried it out over the reef. [466] By dawn a new blossom opened on the seabed, white and impossible, the first one to bloom from two strangers' shared pain. [488] Mira felt, at last, the weight of three hundred sleepless years loosen its grip. [502] She would not sleep tonight, nor likely ever, but she no longer needed to. [516] Her longing had finally found its shape, ensconced safely in petals and verse and a stranger's grateful eyes. [534] The boy stayed a season, then carried the lullabies inland, to deserts that had never known the sea. [552] Wherever he sang, the off-key wind followed, and gardens rose from the cracked ground like answered prayers. [569] He understood now what Mira had learned across her long vigil: that pain, given light and patience, could photosynthesize into beauty. [590] And so the blossom armistice, declared once to end a war, became a quieter promise renewed each night against despair. [610] Inside her gargantuan shell, the insomniac poet kept writing, shyly unstoppable, her sorrow blooming into joy that no shadow could drown. [631]</story>
