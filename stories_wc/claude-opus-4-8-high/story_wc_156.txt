Mara restored murals for a living, coaxing faded gods and rivers back into brilliance with brushes finer than eyelashes. [19]

She was methodically playful, labeling each pigment jar with a pun, then arranging them by wavelength like a tidy rainbow nobody asked for. [42]

Her latest commission led her past the city's edge into the sound wave garden, a place she had assumed was only a rumor. [65]

Here, hedges were trimmed into the shapes of frozen vibrations, and stone basins held water that rippled even in stillness. [85]

A curator named Oswin met her at the gate, pressing a worn coin into her palm. [101]

"A hobo nickel," he explained, "carved by some wanderer a century ago; the garden likes it, and the garden's likes are commands." [125]

The nickel showed a hobo's face that, tilted, became a tiny owl, two pictures sharing one surface. [143]

"The mural you'll restore is in the listening grotto," Oswin said, "but be warned, the place works through reflected sound." [164]

Mara laughed, certain he was being theatrical, and certainty was exactly the thing she had come to abandon. [183]

For years she had repaired other people's visions while losing her own appetite for wonder, and she wanted, badly, to exchange certainty for curiosity. [208]

The grotto's walls curved like a cupped ear, and upon them sprawled a faded mural of sleepers stacked inside sleepers, dreamers within dreamers. [232]

Nested awakenings, the title plate read, and Mara felt a small chill of recognition she could not explain. [251]

She set to work, and as her brush whispered across the plaster, the curved walls caught the sound and threw it back, magnified. [275]

The reflected hiss of bristles became a voice, then many, and the painted sleepers seemed to breathe with the rhythm of her strokes. [298]

"Why do you wake them?" the garden asked, the question rolling around the basin and arriving from every direction at once. [319]

In the pause between question and reply, something extraordinary happened. [329]

That silent gap stretched open like a held breath, and Mara found herself inside it, standing in a second grotto identical to the first. [353]

There a second Mara restored the same mural, and when she asked the painted sleepers a question, the pause swallowed her into a third grotto, deeper still. [380]

Each awakening revealed another sleeper waking, layer beneath layer, until she could no longer count which Mara was the dreaming one. [402]

She understood then that the garden meant to bewitch her, not cruelly, but the way a riddle enchants until you forget there was ever an answer. [428]

The reflected sound had folded her certainties into a maze, and the only thread out was the nickel cooling in her fist. [451]

She tilted it, and the hobo became an owl, the owl became a hobo, two truths refusing to settle. [471]

That refusal was the lesson; the coin itself lived in the pause between one reading and the next, never resolving, always inviting. [494]

So when the garden asked again, "Why do you wake them?", Mara did not rush to reply. [512]

She lingered inside the question, letting the gap widen, and instead of fearing the nested grottos she walked them like rooms in a beloved house. [537]

In each layer she added a single bright stroke to the mural, a color the previous Mara had been too sure to try. [560]

Crimson where she had always chosen rust, gold where she had defaulted to brown, choices made from curiosity rather than rule. [581]

The sleepers, brightening, began to wake in earnest, and their waking woke the next, until the whole spiral of grottos blazed with revised dreamers. [605]

When the last sleeper opened painted eyes, every nested Mara turned and met her own gaze, and they all laughed the same surprised laugh. [629]

The layers collapsed gently inward, like a telescope folding, and she stood once more in the single listening grotto, brush in hand, nickel warm. [654]

The mural before her was finished, more vivid than the commission had ever demanded, alive with colors no one had instructed her to use. [678]

Oswin entered, studied it, and asked softly, "Did the garden bewitch you?" [690]

Mara held the question in her mouth, savoring the pause, no longer hungry to close it. [706]

"It taught me to stay here," she said at last, tapping the gap between his words and hers, "in the not-knowing." [728]

She handed back the hobo nickel, owl-side up, hobo-side up, depending on how the light fell. [745]

Then she gathered her brushes, leaving behind her old certainty and carrying out, like a lantern, the curiosity she had traded for it. [768]
