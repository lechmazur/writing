<story>When the anesthesia wore off, Mira woke in the quiet tower at dawn, her tongue thick and her thoughts slow. [20] A meteor hush lay over everything, that breathless stillness the sky keeps just before light. [35] On the sill sat a withered fern, its fronds curled as if it too had been put under anesthesia. [54] Mira had come here to learn to read the stories in scars. [66] She was a closure seeker, though everyone who knew her said she only ever opened wounds. [82] Her sister's death had left a question that healing alone could not answer. [95] The surgeon at the tower's clinic had promised something stranger than medicine: a method for reading the past. [113] You examine the coffee rings, he had told her before the drip took hold. [127] Every cup the dead once held leaves a ledger of mornings stained into the wood. [142] Now, steadying herself, Mira limped to the round table where her sister's old mugs had stood for years. [160] The tabletop was a map of overlapping brown circles, faint as old constellations. [173] She traced them, and the rings answered, opening like doors onto small recovered afternoons. [187] But the surgery had done more than teach her to read. [198] Some nerve, rewired or simply jostled, had left her accidentally telepathic with plants. [211] The ivy along the tower wall murmured of rain it remembered. [222] The withered fern whispered that her sister had watered it the morning she vanished. [236] Listen, the fern seemed to say, and Mira bent close, breath held in the meteor hush. [252] The coffee rings nearest the window pulled her into the last dawn her sister had known. [268] She saw a tunnel beneath the tower, one her sister had dug toward the old aquifer. [284] The fern confirmed it, having felt roots tremble as someone burrowed downward in secret. [298] Mira followed the memory and her own aching feet into that earthen passage. [311] The walls wept groundwater, and small pale roots reached through the dirt like patient fingers, guiding her. [328] There, at the tunnel's end, she found a frosted hourglass buried in cool clay. [342] The hourglass held no sand but a slow, silver light her sister had called a displaced miracle. [359] It was a cure meant for another time, another person, smuggled here and frozen mid-pour. [374] Her sister, terminally ill, had tunneled down to hide it, unwilling to steal a stranger's salvation. [390] That was the wound Mira had circled for a year: why save the world's grace yet refuse it yourself. [409] She had her own fresh scar now, surgical and tender, and she pressed it, reading her sister's choice in her own healing flesh. [432] Now the answer pressed against her palms like cold glass. [442] The fern's roots had cradled the hourglass, keeping its frost intact, and they offered the memory whole. [459] Her sister had whispered to these very roots, trusting plants to carry what people would not hear. [476] Mira understood at last that closure was not a wound sealed but a wound finally read aloud. [493] She lifted the frosted hourglass and climbed back up the tunnel into the brightening tower. [508] Dawn had ripened, and the meteor hush was breaking into the ordinary clatter of birds. [523] She set the hourglass among the coffee rings, where memory and miracle could rest together. [538] You taught me to read scars, she told the silence, and now I can read yours. [554] The displaced miracle would go where it belonged, carried by a stranger's need rather than hoarded by grief. [572] Mira would mail it onward, an anonymous gift, the way her sister had always intended. [587] The fern, watered now by her careful hand, hummed something like relief. [599] And in that quiet tower, with the anesthesia long gone and the scars finally legible, she let the old wound close. [620]</story>
