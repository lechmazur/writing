The gloom-hill coachman arrived at the jetty after the reef died, when the tide had grown quiet and strange. [19] He had driven his black coach down from the gloom-hills for thirty years, but no one rode with him now. [39] In his lap he carried a collection of dried seed pods, each rattling with a sound like small bones. [58] Twilight settled over the blooming jetty, where salt-roses and pale anemone-flowers had taken root in the cracks of old stone. [78] This place had once been a battlefield, where two drowned navies fought until the reef itself was broken. [96] The coachman remembered, for he spoke dead languages, and the old wars whispered to him in tongues no living mouth still shaped. [118] He had come to fertilize this ruined shore, to let a garden reclaim the battlefield at last. [135] But he did not know where to plant the seeds, for the jetty was vast and narrow at once. [154] It stretched only a single arm into the sea, yet beneath it lay an entire sunken world. [171] So he listened, learning where to sow by understanding bird calls. [182] The gulls cried over the warm shallows, and the terns marked the cold, and the small dark sandpipers named the places where blood had soaked deepest. [208] He followed their voices, pressing each dried pod into the crevices they sang of. [222] As he worked, he murmured to the seeds in those dead languages, the way a man comforts the dying. [241] The reef below was dead, bleached white as the bones of the men it had buried. [257] Yet the coachman believed in a submerged concord, an agreement made beneath the water that the living and the drowned would no longer war. [281] That was why he had come: not to conquer the dead place, but to make peace with it through roots. [301] The birds knew the terms of that peace better than any treaty, and so he trusted them entirely. [319] By the time the last light bled orange across the water, every pod had found its home. [336] He stood at the end of the jetty, an old man on a narrow finger of stone above a vast grave. [357] A heron landed beside him and called once, low and certain. [368] He understood it as a blessing, the first word of the new accord. [381] He answered in a language ten centuries dead, thanking the bird for its counsel. [395] Through the night the tide rose and fed the seeds, and the dead reef gave up its old minerals to fertilize the green ambition above. [420] When the next twilight came, thin shoots had risen between the stones, trembling and impossibly soon. [436] The coachman smiled, for the garden had begun to reclaim the battlefield, just as he had hoped. [453] Each dawn the birds returned to inspect his work, and each dusk he came back with his coach to listen again. [474] He learned that the sandpipers wanted lavender near the sunken cannons, where men had fallen thickest. [490] He learned that the gulls asked for vines to drape the broken masts that still pierced the surface. [508] And he learned, slowly, that the dead languages he spoke were not truly dead while something still answered them. [527] The submerged concord held, season after season, signed in pollen rather than ink. [540] Where there had been a battlefield there was now a blooming jetty, narrow as a path yet vast as a promise kept. [562] In time the gloom-hill coachman grew too old to climb down from his seat, and he asked the heron what he should do. [585] It called him toward the water, and he understood. [594] He scattered his last dried seed pods across the waves and let the garden take him too. [611] The birds sang his name in a dead tongue, and the flowers nodded, and the long war was finally over. [631]
