<story>Mira had always been a luminous skeptic, the kind of woman whose face glowed faintly when she doubted miracles. [19] She arrived in the city of forgotten fountains during the pause of migrating geese, that single hushed breath when the flock hangs motionless above the rooftops. [45] The fountains here had quit speaking long ago, their basins dry, their stone mouths clogged with leaves and silence. [64] Yet Mira had come for one thing only: to liberate trapped memories. [76] Her grandmother's recollections, she believed, lay sealed inside a stained glass box that the old woman had clutched until her final hour. [98] The box now sat in the city archive, behind glass, waiting with what the curators called sidereal patience. [116] They said it kept time the way stars do, indifferent to centuries, certain that someone would eventually come. [134] Mira did not believe in such things, and that was precisely why she was perfect for the task. [152] A believer would have prayed; a skeptic would simply work. [162] The grandmother had left a trail, and Mira intended to find the answer by following breadcrumbs. [178] Literal ones, almost: scraps of paper tucked into the lips of each dead fountain, each bearing a riddle in faded ink. [199] She moved through the streets in a defiant hush, refusing to whisper out of fear but unwilling to disturb whatever still slept. [221] The first note read: "What pours when the source is gone?" [232] Memory, she decided, and the second fountain confirmed it with another slip of paper. [246] Mira was realistically imaginative, a rare balance: she could picture wonders without ever mistaking them for facts. [263] So when each riddle pointed her toward the archive, she imagined the box opening, then checked her imagining against the cold logic of locks and hinges. [289] By the seventh fountain she understood the pattern. [297] The breadcrumbs were not leading her to the box at all, but teaching her how to unstick its lid. [316] Each riddle named a memory her grandmother had loved, and the answers, spoken in order, formed a kind of key. [336] At dusk Mira reached the archive, where the stained glass box waited beneath a dome of dust. [353] The geese still hung in their pause outside, the whole sky holding its breath with her. [369] She laid out the slips and recited the answers, one by one, in that same defiant hush. [386] "A house by the harbor. A blue coat. A song with no second verse." [400] The lid trembled, then released with a sigh, as though it had only ever needed to be remembered correctly. [419] Inside lay no glowing spirits, no captured ghosts, only colored light spilling through the panes onto the floor. [437] Mira laughed softly, the luminous skeptic vindicated; there was nothing supernatural here at all. [451] And yet the patterns of light fell across the wall in shapes she knew: the harbor, the coat, the unfinished song. [472] The memories had not been imprisoned by magic but by neglect, by no one bothering to look. [489] To liberate trapped memories, it turned out, required only attention and the willingness to follow a trail to its end. [509] She understood then why the box had practiced its sidereal patience. [520] It was never waiting for a believer; it was waiting for someone stubborn enough to do the work. [538] Outside, the geese resumed their flight, and the city seemed to exhale. [550] One by one, water began to stir in the forgotten fountains, fed by some long-buried spring her recitation had somehow unstuck. [571] Mira, realistically imaginative to the last, told herself it was groundwater and broken valves. [585] But she let the wonder stand beside the explanation, neither cancelling the other. [598] She carried the stained glass box into the square and set it where the colored light could touch the rising water. [619] Her grandmother's memories were free now, scattered across reborn fountains, and that was enough. [633] In the defiant hush before the geese vanished north, Mira finally let herself glow. [647]</story>
