<story>After the birth, I climbed past the last ridge of stars to find the cartomancer who lived where the air thinned to silver. [23] She kept a midnight glade above the clouds, a meadow of pale grass that swayed though no wind reached it. [43] They called her the onyx-ink cartomancer, for her cards were drawn in black so deep they seemed to drink the moon. [64] I had come to find where lost time accumulates, because somewhere among the years I had misplaced whole seasons of a life not yet mine to keep. [91] My wife had died as our son drew his first breath, and I needed to know where those traded hours had gone. [113] The cartomancer met me at a low table set with a child's dolls' tea set, each cup no larger than a thimble. [135] One saucer bore a crack, a thin black river splitting the porcelain, and she traced it as though reading a map. [156] "This crack," she said, "is where your lost time has been leaking, drop by drop, into the place beneath the world." [177] I was willingly perplexed, content to let her riddles wash over me, for understanding had become a burden I gladly set down. [199] She dealt three cards across the cracked saucer and asked me to name the custom by which my family marked time. [220] I told her of our birthday tablecloths, how each year we traced our hands in ink upon the linen, layering palm over palm. [243] Generations of hands grew small to large across those cloths, a forest of fingers proving we had lived, that we had been measured and kept. [268] But my wife's hand stopped the year our son arrived, and the last tablecloth held only a single new tracing, impossibly small. [290] The cartomancer nodded and bade me unfasten the clasp of a silver locket hanging at the table's edge. [308] Inside lay no portrait, only a folded square of birthday linen, soft as breath and stained with palms I half remembered. [329] "Cloudbound repentance," she murmured, "is the only fare this glade accepts; you must forgive yourself for the hours you spent grieving instead of holding him." [354] The beauty of that meadow was a choking thing, every silver blade trembling with the weight of what I had refused to feel. [377] I pressed my palm to the cracked saucer, and through that hairline river the lost time began to rise like steam. [398] There they were, the accumulated months, pooling in the dolls' cups: first smiles, midnight feedings, the soft animal weight of a sleeping infant. [421] I had not lost the time; I had hidden it from myself, afraid that to love him was to forgive his arrival. [443] The cartomancer gathered her onyx cards and stood; her work, she said, was only to show me the leak, the mending was mine. [466] So above the clouds, in that glade where lost hours gather like dew, I drank from the cracked saucer until the months returned to me whole. [492] When I descended, I carried the locket's linen home and spread a fresh tablecloth across our table. [509] I dipped my son's tiny fist in ink and pressed it down beside the ghost of his mother's hand. [528] The two tracings overlapped, small palm within large, and the crack in me sealed shut like cooling porcelain. [546] Some nights I still climb to the midnight glade, not to recover what I lost, but to thank the silence that taught me where time waits for the brave enough to claim it. [579] My son traces his own hand now, each birthday, layering the years I once tried to lose. [596] And the dolls' saucer sits whole upon his shelf, a small reminder that even broken things can hold a tide of returning love. [619]</story>
