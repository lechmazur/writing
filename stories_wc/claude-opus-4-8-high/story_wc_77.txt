<story>After the resuscitation, I woke inside the abandoned carnival funhouse, my chest still aching where the paddles had bitten. [19] They said I had died for ninety seconds, and in those seconds something followed me back. [35] It calls itself my whispering advisor, though it sounds like wet velvet dragged across teeth. [50] The funhouse had been shuttered for decades, yet its warped mirrors held a lingering luminance, a sickly glow that refused to die when the carnival did. [76] I had come here on purpose, dragging my fear with me, because the advisor promised a single, repulsive bargain. [95] My intention was simple and cruel: to trick fear into revealing its weakness. [108] Ever since the flatline, I had become photographically cursed; every photo of me developed with a second face peering over my shoulder. [130] That face was the advisor, and it grew bolder with each exposure. [142] The whispering advisor told me the only way to learn fear's weakness was through paradoxical notes in a dream dictionary it had buried beneath the funhouse floor. [169] I pried up the rotten boards and found the book, its pages swollen with damp and contradiction. [186] Each entry undid the last. [191] "To flee is to arrive," read one note; "the locked door is the open road," read another. [208] The advisor explained that fear, like the dictionary, lives on paradox, and that a creature built of contradiction can be unmade by its own logic. [233] So I read the paradoxes aloud in the glowing dark, and the funhouse mirrors caught my voice, multiplying it. [252] Something in the corridor began to gather, drawn by that repulsive allure of meaning that cannot mean. [269] Fear took shape from the lingering luminance, a figure stitched from leftover light, beautiful and nauseating at once. [287] At its throat gleamed an opera cloak clasp, the same one I had worn the night my heart stopped. [306] I realized then that the advisor and the fear were one, and that the clasp had pinned them to me all along. [328] The advisor whispered for me to unbosom myself, to confess the terror I had carried since the table, since the paddles, since the cold. [352] It wanted my confession because that was how it fed, how it grew its second face on every photograph. [371] But I had read the dictionary, and I knew the trick now. [383] So I unbosomed the one paradox fear could not survive. [393] "I am most alive," I told it, "because I have already died." [405] The figure flinched, its borrowed luminance flickering, because fear cannot threaten one who has met the end and returned indifferent. [425] That was its weakness, the contradiction I had tricked it into facing. [437] The whispering advisor screamed without sound, and the opera cloak clasp clattered to the floorboards, cold and ordinary. [455] The lingering luminance drained out of the mirrors, leaving only honest darkness behind. [468] I picked up the clasp, no longer afraid of the second face, because there was no second face to fear. [488] When I walked out of the abandoned carnival funhouse, dawn was rising gray and clean. [503] I took a photograph of myself in that first light, hands trembling, and waited for the print to develop. [522] Only my own face looked back, alone at last, the curse spent. [534] Still, I kept the dream dictionary, its paradoxical notes now silent, because some lessons are too dangerous to discard. [553] Sometimes, late at night, I open it and read a single line, just to remember the texture of that repulsive allure. [574] The advisor had been right about one thing: fear is only a mirror, and mirrors hold whatever light you refuse to let die. [597] I keep mine, now, turned firmly toward the morning. [606]</story>
