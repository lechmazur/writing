<story>After the universe contracted, only the ambered observatory deck remained lit, a bubble of fossil-gold suspended where space used to breathe. [21] Vesh kept the watch there, the last cast lots decision maker of a guild that had once governed worlds by chance. [42] He owned nothing now except a collection of foreign coins, scuffed disks from nations the contraction had folded into nothing. [62] Each morning, if mornings still meant anything, he tossed them to decide what to hope for. [78] The coins were his oracle, and lately they had begun answering questions he had not yet asked. [95] This was the backwards prophecy: the future arrived first, and only afterward did the cause stumble in to explain it. [115] Vesh first noticed it when the coins spelled a word he would invent three days later. [131] Such certainty unsettled him, for he had been raised on stellar doubt, the creed that no light could be trusted until its source was confirmed. [156] And there were no sources left, only echoes pressed against the amber glass. [169] Still, Vesh remained quietly enthusiastic, humming to himself as he arranged the coins into spirals and read their verdicts. [188] He believed, against all evidence, that meaning could still be found in the collapse. [202] The dead instruments on the deck offered only static bursts, ragged hisses that leaked through the dark. [219] Most listeners would have dismissed them, but Vesh had spent a lifetime understanding static bursts, hearing the grammar buried in the noise. [241] He realized the bursts were the prophecy's voice, speaking the ending before the beginning. [255] If he could distill those hisses into a single tone, he might learn what the new universe wanted of him. [275] For that was his deepest motivation now: to wavelength align with purpose, to vibrate at whatever frequency the reborn cosmos would require. [297] He laid the foreign coins across the consoles, one for each broken nation, and let their weights tune the receivers. [317] Slowly the static began to braid itself into pattern, the backwards prophecy unspooling toward its origin. [333] The message, he understood, described his own future words, the command he was destined to speak to ignite a second creation. [354] Stellar doubt seized him at the threshold. [361] Who was he, a gambler with pocketed relics, to author the dawn of everything? [375] So he did the only thing he trusted, and cast lots one final time. [389] The coins fell on the ambered observatory deck and froze mid-spin, refusing gravity, hanging like tiny suspended planets. [407] In their stillness he read the same verdict the static had distilled: speak. [420] Past and future had agreed, and the prophecy was only waiting for him to catch up to it. [438] He drew a breath that tasted of cold copper and ancient nations. [450] Quietly enthusiastic even at the edge of unmaking, he let his voice climb until it matched the tone he had distilled from the noise. [474] When he finally wavelength aligned with purpose, the coins sang back, ringing each foreign currency into one chord. [492] The amber glass shivered, and beyond it the contracted dark flinched, then bulged. [505] Light returned, not as certainty but as a question, exactly the way stellar doubt taught it should. [522] Galaxies unfolded outward from the deck like a hand reopening after the long clench. [536] Vesh watched his single spoken word race ahead of its own meaning, becoming the prophecy that had warned him of itself. [557] He laughed, gathering the cooling coins, each now stamped with constellations no nation had yet minted. [573] They were foreign again, but to a future rather than a past. [585] On the ambered observatory deck, the last decision maker became the first witness. [598] He cast the coins one more time, simply to hear them laugh along. [611]</story>
