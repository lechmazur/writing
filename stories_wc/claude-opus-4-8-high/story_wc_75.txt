<story>The grotto city of Velmarra hid behind a monumental waterfall, its limestone halls glowing like wet pearl. [17] I came here after the beekeeper's last visit, when the cisterns of honey ran dry and the candles guttered. [36] As the city's only museum guide, I am historically oriented to a fault, cataloguing every ruin while the living forget how to live. [59] The displaced persons arrive each week, refugees pushed from drowned valleys above, carrying nothing but grief and recipes. [77] I greet them in the lantern-hall, my voice cultured and calm, though a rosewater dread perfumes every welcome. [95] Their old crafts are dying arts, and I have sworn to rescue them before the waterfall's roar erases their names entirely. [116] My most treasured exhibit is an emerald-tinted opera glass lens, salvaged from a flooded theater that no longer exists. [135] Through its green glass, the refugees see their lost homes as they were, and something frozen inside them begins to thaw. [156] Last evening an old woman named Tova pressed the lens to her eye and wept. [171] Through it she glimpsed her childhood kitchen, where her mother once kept a family custom of baking bread shaped like initials. [192] Each loaf bore the curve of a name, she explained, so that no one ate a stranger's portion and no one was forgotten. [215] I realized then how to rescue these dying arts: not as relics under glass, but as living habits returned to hungry hands. [237] We lit the cold ovens that the beekeeper had once warmed with his waxen trade. [252] Tova kneaded dough with trembling fingers, looping it into the initials of every displaced family she could remember. [270] The grotto filled with the smell of rosewater and crust, and the dread that had perfumed my welcomes softened. [289] Word spread along the wet corridors, and refugees came carrying their own half-remembered arts. [303] A weaver dyed thread the exact emerald of the opera lens; a luthier strung an instrument from waterfall reeds. [322] I, the historically oriented guide, learned at last that history must be eaten, played, and worn to survive. [340] Yet the rosewater dread never wholly left me, for I knew the valleys above were still drowning. [357] Each new refugee was another name lost upstream, another loaf I would have to teach Tova to bake. [375] Still, the museum changed; its silent cases opened, and the displaced persons became its keepers rather than its exhibits. [394] On the night the last oven cooled, Tova handed me a loaf shaped like the letter of my own name. [414] I, who had only ever guided others through ruins, was for the first time included among the living. [432] The emerald lens, I decided, would no longer hang behind glass. [443] Instead I set it at the grotto's mouth, where the monumental waterfall thundered and split the light into a thousand greens. [464] Through it, each arriving family could look back and see the home they had been forced to leave, and grieve, and then turn forward to bake. [490] The beekeeper never returned, but his ovens kept their warmth through borrowed fire and stubborn memory. [506] I, the cultured guide, finally understood my true exhibit was never the lens nor the bread. [522] It was the slow, deliberate thaw of frightened people relearning how to belong. [535] Every initialed loaf was a small monument, more durable than limestone, because it could be made again. [552] And so the dying arts did not die; they migrated, the way people do, finding new ovens and new names to fill. [574] Some nights I still feel the rosewater dread rise with the spray, whispering that the water will always take more than we can save. [598] But then I smell baking crust drifting through the grotto, and I answer it the only honest way I know. [618] I roll up my sleeves, dust my hands with flour, and shape another name. [632]</story>
