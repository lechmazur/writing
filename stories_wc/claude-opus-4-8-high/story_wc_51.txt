<story>After the first snowmelt, the Fractal Weaver descended into the bronze grotto, where starlight pooled like spilled milk across the metal walls. [22] She had come, as she always did, to collect the weights of different burdens. [36] The grotto breathed a shingled hush, layer over layer of silence, each overlapping the next like roof tiles laid by patient hands. [58] In her hands she carried a single kerosene lamp burner, brass and ancient, its wick long gone. [75] It was not for light. [80] It was a vessel, the only thing that could hold what she had come to gather. [96] The weight of grief, the weight of regret, the weight of love that had nowhere left to go. [114] Travelers left their burdens here, whispering them into the bronze, and she alone knew how to measure them. [132] Yet measuring required a method, and the method was simple and terrible: by orienting properly. [147] Each burden had a true direction, a way it wished to fall, and only when she tilted the burner to align with that fall could the weight pour in. [176] The Fractal Weaver worked the way her name promised, drawing the same gesture at every scale, repeating the small motion inside the large one until the pattern folded into itself. [206] She turned the brass burner this way and that, listening for the moment of true orientation, when a burden would slide home with a sigh. [231] It was a whalebone reverie, this labor: slow, vast, and pale, like dreaming inside the ribs of some great drowned thing. [252] The starlight moved across the bronze, and the hush layered itself thicker, tile upon tile. [267] But this night something went wrong. [273] She lifted a burden too heavy to name, an old sorrow that had waited beneath the snow all winter for the melt to free it. [298] When she tried to orient the burner, the sorrow refused any single direction. [311] It wished to fall every way at once, and so it was boundary-lessly trapped within itself, a thing without edges and therefore without escape. [335] The Weaver felt the weight enter not the burner but her own hands. [348] It climbed her wrists, and she understood, with the calm of deep dreaming, that she was beginning to calcify. [367] Her fingers hardened to pale stone, then her arms, the burdens she had spent years collecting hardening her from the inside. [388] This, she realized, was always the ending: the collector becomes the vessel, the weigher becomes the weight. [405] She did not panic. [409] In the whalebone hush she remembered her own method and turned it upon herself. [423] If a thing without edges could not be oriented, then perhaps she could give the sorrow her edges instead. [442] With her last soft motion she pressed the kerosene lamp burner against her calcifying chest. [457] She became the orientation. [461] Her body, half stone, was a direction now, a single true fall, and the boundary-less sorrow finally found a shape to pour into. [484] It rushed from her into the brass, and the weight of it rang the burner like a bell. [502] The hush broke, shingle by shingle, each layer lifting away into the starlight above the bronze. [518] When the last tile of silence rose, the Weaver's stone receded from her skin, leaving her whole and trembling. [537] She had learned the secret she had collected burdens for years without grasping. [550] A weight cannot be measured from outside; one must offer it a direction made of oneself. [566] The burner, full now, glowed faintly, as if a wick had returned to it. [580] She set it among the others on the bronze shelf, a row of small brass lamps, each holding the precise weight of a different burden. [605] Outside, the snowmelt ran in bright threads toward the sea. [615] The Fractal Weaver climbed back into the night, lighter by one sorrow, heavier by one truth. [631] And the grotto, beneath the patient stars, began once more to gather its shingled hush. [646]</story>
