<story>After the empathy virus burned through us, I learned to trust only mushrooms. [13] They asked nothing, demanded no feeling, and rewarded the rational truffle forager who read soil like scripture. [30] My name was Wren, and I worked the hanging orchard in a ravine battered by storms, where apple trees grew sideways from the cliff and roots netted the rock. [59] The virus had not killed bodies; it had erased the buffer between minds, so a stranger's grief could drown you on contact. [81] Survivors lived apart, and I had come to the ravine to cultivate psychological safety the only way left: distance, routine, and the quiet companionship of fungus. [107] Then the courier arrived, half-dead, having climbed the storm-slick ropes with a smuggled relic wrapped in oilcloth. [124] She pressed it into my hands and named it: an antique glass eye, milky and cold, supposedly carved from a saint who never lied. [148] The relic, she swore, could heal the virus through rumored cures in discredited textbooks she had stolen from a burned clinic. [169] I told her, flatly, that I did not believe in saints or stolen pages. [183] She laughed, blood on her teeth, and explained the relic's curse: whoever held it became involuntarily truthful. [200] I scoffed, and then she asked why I really lived alone. [211] The answer left me before I could shape it: because I had infected my own brother, and watched his mind flood with mine until he stopped being himself. [239] I had never said that aloud. [245] The glass eye was warm now, and the truth kept coming, raw and ungoverned. [259] She wanted me to study the textbooks, to test whether the relic's compelled honesty could be the cure itself. [278] The theory in those discredited pages was simple and mad: the virus spread because we hid from one another, and hiding made every shared feeling a violation. [305] If no one could lie, the authors argued, no one would need to occlude their inner weather, and the contagious panic would have nowhere to hide. [331] I called it nonsense, even as the relic forced me to admit I wanted it to be true. [349] We stayed through three storms, the orchard groaning on its ropes above the black ravine. [364] She was dying from the climb, and I could not lie to comfort her, so I gave her something better. [384] I told her exactly what I felt, every fear and every flicker of hope, and held the glass eye between us so neither of us could pretend. [411] Something strange happened: her grief touched mine and did not drown me. [423] Without lies stacked between us, there was no pressure behind the feeling, no flood, only weather passing through two open windows. [444] The discredited textbooks had been right by accident; the cure was not the saint's bone, but the surrender it demanded. [464] She died at dawn, unafraid, and I buried her among the sideways trees. [477] Then I did the rational thing, which had finally stopped meaning the safe thing. [491] I left the ravine carrying the smuggled relic toward the nearest settlement, where survivors still cowered behind walls of careful silence. [512] I no longer wanted only my own safety; I wanted to cultivate psychological safety wide enough to hold a whole frightened town. [534] The forager in me knew the method: you do not force open a closed thing, you teach it that opening is survivable. [556] I would pass the glass eye hand to hand, and let the truth do its slow, terrible mercy. [574] Some would refuse, preferring to occlude their wounds behind comfortable lies, and the virus would keep them apart forever. [593] But some would hold the cold glass and finally tell each other what they were, and in that fractured grace, begin again. [615]</story>
