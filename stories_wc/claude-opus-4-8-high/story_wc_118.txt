<story>The reclaimed silo library held a bronze quiet, the kind that settles only after the last human dream is dreamt. [20] Esmer was the odd historian who lived there now, alone among shelves curved to the tower's round wall. [38] She had a single, peculiar purpose: to collect the stories walls have heard. [51] Buildings remember, she believed, and she was sensitively perceptive enough to feel their memories pressing like warm coins against her palms. [72] The silo had once stored grain, then ammunition, then nothing, and finally books carried up by the last people before they slept. [94] One winter morning she found, tucked behind a loose brick in the great fireplace, a bundle of half-burned diaries. [113] Their edges were charred to lace, but the inner pages survived, and Esmer read them the way one reads a pulse. [134] Through the half-burned diaries she began to probe the silo's oldest secret. [146] The earliest writer, a watchman named Corin, described a fragment of a medieval tapestry showing dragons, nailed above the hearth for luck. [168] He wrote that the dragons faced east, toward a window where the morning star should rise. [184] But the star, Corin insisted, rose in the wrong place, as if the whole sky had a displaced axis. [203] Later diarists copied his fear; each generation noted the same drift, the heavens leaning a little further off true. [222] Esmer pressed her hand to the fireplace stone and let the wall tell its version. [237] What rose in her was not a memory of stars but of fear, layered like soot. [253] The watchmen had not measured the sky. [260] They had measured themselves, and found the displacement was within. [270] Generation by generation, the people of the silo had tilted away from one another, their inner compass swinging off center, until no one stood at the true heart of anything. [300] The tapestry dragons, Esmer realized, had not guarded the door against beasts. [312] They had been hung as a promise that some things still faced east, still pointed toward dawn. [329] She lifted the surviving fragment from the bricks where it had fallen, the woven scales still glinting copper in the dim. [350] Esmer understood her own oddness then. [356] She, too, had a displaced axis; she had turned from living people toward listening walls because the living had gone quiet. [377] The last human dream had been dreamt seasons ago, and she had stayed to be the one who remembered. [396] To collect the stories walls have heard was, she saw now, a way to keep facing east herself. [414] She copied Corin's words into her own ledger, then the next writer's, then the next, mending the broken chain. [433] The half-burned diaries gave up their fragments willingly, as if grateful to be read before the last ash claimed them. [453] By dusk she had nailed the tapestry fragment back above the hearth, dragons aimed at the eastern window. [471] It corrected nothing in the sky. [477] But within the bronze quiet of the silo, one small axis stood true again. [491] Esmer sat with her palm on the warm stone and continued to probe, gently, for every voice the walls still carried. [512] The silo answered slowly, the way old metal warms, releasing names she had never been told. [528] A girl who had carved a dragon into the doorframe; a baker who wept at the eastern window; a watchman who finally slept. [551] Each story arrived faint and exact, like the smell of rain on grain dust. [565] Being sensitively perceptive was a burden and a gift; she felt each grief as if it were her own newly remembered childhood. [587] Yet she did not flinch, because someone had to hold the displaced axis steady while the world decided whether to wake. [608] When the morning star finally rose, off true as ever, Esmer simply nodded and wrote it down. [625] The walls, at last, had a listener, and that was its own quiet kind of dawn. [641]</story>
