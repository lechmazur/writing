<story>The ancient temple library lay lost in time, its shelves bowing like old monks beneath the weight of centuries. [19] Here lived Bormo, a diminutive titan, broad as a doorway yet shorter than the candle stands he tended. [37] He was regally unpretentious, the last of a giant lineage that had once worn crowns but now wore dust. [56] A pretender now sat the high throne above, a man who claimed the titan blood Bormo alone still carried. [75] Bormo had vowed to unmask him, yet a titan in exile cannot march upon a palace with fists alone. [94] So he chose patience, working through cryptic messages left in library books that scholars from the capital still borrowed. [113] Each night he crushed an iron gall ink pellet against a saucer, dissolving the hard bead into bitter black fluid. [133] With it he inked the margins of borrowed volumes, hiding truths between psalms and ledgers. [148] The messages spoke of the false king's birth, his stolen seal, the night the real heir vanished. [165] Yet ink alone could be burned, so Bormo bound each note with a rune of drift. [181] The rune let his words wander from page to page, slipping into new books whenever a reader closed the cover. [201] No censor could catch a sentence that refused to stay still. [212] He worked across the pause of shifting constellations, that long astronomical hush when the stars seem to hold their breath before realigning. [234] Old prophecies promised that during this pause, a buried truth would rise and a usurper's lie would sunder. [252] Season by season, the drifting words gathered readers like a slow tide. [264] A young archivist named Sela first noticed the migrating ink, a phrase she had read in a hymnal reappearing inside a tax record. [287] She followed the trail back to the forgotten temple, climbing stairs no map remembered. [301] There she found Bormo bent over his saucer, enormous hands cradling a pellet smaller than a peppercorn. [318] He did not threaten her; he simply offered tea and the whole quiet history of a kingdom's theft. [336] Sela believed him, for the drifting runes had already half-convinced the capital's clerks. [349] She carried his final message herself, a single page that drifted from a prayer book into the pretender's own hands during court. [371] The words named his crimes aloud as he read, and the assembled lords heard the truth in his stammer. [390] His authority began its graceful collapse, not in a single blow but in a slow, dignified crumble. [407] Crowns slid from him as gently as autumn shedding leaves, and the false reign quietly sundered. [423] Bormo never claimed the empty throne. [429] A titan who tends candles knows that thrones are merely furniture, and dust outlasts every king. [445] He returned to his library as the constellations resumed their slow march overhead. [458] Sela stayed, learning to crush ink pellets and to whisper runes into the spines of books. [474] Together they let truth wander where soldiers could not follow. [484] The temple, lost in time, kept its small regent of words, content among the dust. [499] Years later, scholars still found stray sentences blooming in unexpected margins, accusations and confessions migrating from book to book. [518] Some called it haunting; Sela called it justice, refusing to settle. [529] When Bormo finally lay down among the shelves for the last time, his great chest stilling like a closing book, the rune of drift carried his story onward without him. [559] His was a gentle ending, the settling of a giant who had outlived his grievance and his crown. [577] And across the pause of shifting constellations, the truth he had set adrift kept drifting, unkillable, page after page. [596] No throne could sunder a sentence that had learned, at last, how to walk. [610]</story>
