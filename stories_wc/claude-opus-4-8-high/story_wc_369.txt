<story>Mira was the labyrinth's maintenance worker, and she knew every loose stone by name. [14] Her job sounded grand, but it was mostly oiling hinges and sweeping leaves that drifted from the old trees overhead. [34] She had taken the work for one private reason: to discover what trees witness. [48] The labyrinth's heart held a stormlit tower, and on clear nights its top room filled with moonlight. [65] There lived Oren, an old astronomer whose hands had grown too unsteady to read the heavens. [81] On his desk sat his most treasured tool, a broken sextant, its mirror cracked and its arm bent. [99] Oren claimed the instrument could measure something stranger than stars: quantified sorrow. [111] He insisted grief had a number, an angle, a weight you could read like latitude. [126] Mira thought it nonsense, but she liked the old man, so she climbed the tower each night to share weak tea. [147] One evening Oren told her the sextant only worked at a precise moment: at the moment a pin is heard in a silent corridor. [171] Only then, he said, was the world quiet enough to deconstruct a person's grief into its parts. [188] The method was odd: he claimed sorrow could be charted by mapping scattered dreams. [202] Each night people dreamed of small lost things, and those fragments hung in the air like faint stars. [220] The sextant, if mended, would sight them and give each sorrow a measurable place. [234] But the sextant was broken, and Oren had no way to fix its cracked mirror. [249] Then Mira noticed the labyrinth's smallest, dullest miracle. [257] The old trees overhead carried sap that, the gardeners swore, heals in moonlight. [270] She had seen split bark close like skin under a full moon, an ordinary wonder no one questioned. [288] So Mira gathered the silver sap, painted it across the sextant's fractured glass, and carried it up the stormlit tower. [308] Lightning flickered beyond the windows, but the moon held steady, and slowly the cracks sealed. [323] The mirror cleared; the bent arm straightened as if waking. [333] Oren wept, and through the healed glass his tears refracted into countless tiny lights. [347] They waited together, breathless, listening for the appointed instant. [356] Far below, in the labyrinth's longest corridor, every echo faded to perfect stillness. [369] Then a single pin, loosed from Mira's sleeve, fell and rang against the stone. [383] At that sound the sextant blazed to life. [391] Oren raised it, and the room filled with the scattered dreams of the sleeping town. [406] He began to deconstruct each one, reading the angle of every loss. [418] A widow's sorrow measured forty degrees; a child's, only six. [428] By mapping the scattered dreams, Oren charted the whole town's grief onto a single quiet page. [444] Mira leaned close, and there, among the dreams, she found the trees. [456] Their sorrow was the smallest number of all, a faint half-degree. [467] And in that fraction Mira finally saw what trees witness. [477] They held the dreams of everyone who had ever passed beneath them, lovers, mourners, lost children, all remembered without complaint. [497] That was why their grief was so light: they carried it together, season after season. [512] Mira understood then that her labyrinth was not a puzzle but a vessel of patient memory. [528] She kept the chart, not to measure pain, but to remember that sorrow shared grows smaller. [544] Oren died that winter, peaceful, the sextant resting in his folded hands. [556] Mira buried him among the old trees, so they might witness him too. [569] Each dusk she still oils the hinges and sweeps the leaves. [580] And each clear night she climbs the stormlit tower, raises the mended sextant by moonlight, and listens for a pin. [600]</story>
