<story>Mira had always been a between-worlds translator, fluent in the languages spoken on either side of a closed door. [19] The alchemist's market hung high above the city rooftops, its stalls strung on cables that swayed like rigging in a wind that smelled of copper and rain. [46] She came here often, because the merchants traded in things that were elusively tangible: bottled echoes, folded shadows, and the last warmth of vanished hearths. [71] Her task tonight was to honor overlooked histories, the small lives that maps forgot and chronicles never bothered to name. [91] At a stall draped in old canvases, she found what she had been hunting since the gap between childhood and now: a lighthouse lens fragment, faceted and cold. [119] When she was a girl, a keeper had told her that such glass remembered every ship it guided home. [138] The seller, an alchemist with paint beneath his nails, watched her with patient eyes. [152] He explained that the fragment's true memory was hidden, accessible only through hidden details in paintings. [168] He pressed the glass to a dim seascape, and where the lens touched the canvas, a drowned village rose from beneath the painted waves. [192] Mira understood at once: these canvases were ledgers, and the fragment was the key that could unfasten their concealed truths. [212] She bought the fragment with a memory of her own, the only currency the market accepted. [228] Then she moved from stall to stall, holding the glass against every painting she could find. [244] Beneath a portrait of a nobleman, the lens revealed the servant who had truly built his house. [261] Behind a triumphant battle scene glimmered the faces of those who had simply wanted to go home. [278] Each revelation felt like an eternal instant, time folding so that the forgotten dead stood breathing beside her. [296] As a translator, she had always trusted what she called the anchors of intuition, the quiet certainties that told her which silence begged to be spoken. [322] Those anchors had pulled her here, between childhood wonder and adult grief, to give the nameless their words. [340] But the alchemist warned that every truth unfastened demanded payment in remembered pain. [353] With each hidden figure she freed, a piece of her own past dimmed, as if she traded her history for theirs. [374] She remembered the keeper's face, then she did not. [383] She remembered the smell of the sea from childhood, then only the word remained. [397] The market swayed, cables groaning, and far below the city slept beneath its smug official monuments. [413] Mira raised the lens fragment to the night itself, to the dark canvas of the sky. [429] Through the glass the stars rearranged into the faces of everyone history had refused to keep. [445] She spoke their names aloud, translating their existence into the world of the living. [459] The anchors of intuition held steady, even as her own name grew faint upon her tongue. [475] The alchemist asked if she understood the cost. [483] Mira nodded, because a translator's purpose was never to be remembered, only to carry meaning across. [499] She would become elusively tangible herself, a rumor in the high market, a hand that turned glass toward forgotten light. [519] When dawn unfastened the seams of the dark, the stalls began to fold themselves away. [534] Mira set the lens fragment into a small seascape she had painted as a child, hidden in its lower corner. [554] Someday another translator would press glass to canvas and find her there, an overlooked history waiting to be honored. [573] In that eternal instant between forgetting and being found, Mira smiled, and let the morning carry her across.
