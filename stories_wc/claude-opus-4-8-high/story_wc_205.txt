<story>On the baroque asteroid called Velleity, after time became visible, I learned to read the air like a manuscript. [19] Time hung in slow amber ribbons between the gilded spires, and you could watch an hour ripen and fall. [38] I was once a revolutionary, now defunct, my cause long dissolved into the museums I had sworn to burn. [57] They kept me as a curator instead, which is the cruelest exile, tending the relics of the people I failed. [77] My only companion was Doran, a poetically inclined archaeologist who spoke of dust as though it were scripture. [95] Between us lay a single crystal vase shard, no larger than a thumb, glittering with impossible blue veins. [113] It was the last fragment of the Velleians, the vanished civilization whose asteroid we now haunted. [129] From that shard arose our archaeological dispute, the quarrel that consumed our final years together. [144] Doran believed the Velleians were gentle dreamers, artists who carved the spires for beauty alone. [159] I argued they were warriors, rebels like me, whose monuments were fortresses dressed in ornament. [174] Each of us wanted to devise a complete history from this one blue sliver, to reimagine an entire civilization in our own image. [197] It was vanity, I see now, two lonely exiles painting strangers in the colors of their own regret. [215] The asteroid suffered storms, when visible time thrashed into jagged knots and the past leaked sideways into the present. [234] During such storms we practiced the Velleian custom we had reconstructed: covering mirrors with heavy cloth. [250] Doran insisted the Velleians did this so that their reflections would not be dragged into yesterday and lost. [268] I claimed they covered the glass to hide from their own dead faces during sieges. [283] Either way, the shrouded mirrors lent the halls a burnished sorrow that neither of us could name. [300] On the night Doran died, the worst storm in living memory broke across Velleity. [314] I covered every mirror but one, and through that pane I watched a younger Doran arrive, decades ago, full of hope. [335] He had come to this dead place believing the past could be made kind again. [350] Beside his deathbed I finally held the crystal vase shard to the storm-light and truly looked. [366] Within its blue veins I saw not warriors, not dreamers, but ordinary hands that had simply made a vase. [385] People who loved color, who feared storms, who covered their mirrors and grieved their dead like anyone. [402] Our dispute had never been about them at all; it had been about us. [416] Doran, dying, whispered a final line of verse, for he remained poetically inclined to the end. [432] He said that every ruin is a mirror we forget to cover, and so we drown in it. [450] Then visible time slowed around his last breath, and I watched the moment hang, golden, refusing to fall. [468] I buried him beneath the tallest baroque spire, where amber hours pooled like honey. [482] Now I tend the museum alone, a defunct revolutionary guarding a quarrel that has lost its second voice. [500] I have stopped trying to reimagine an entire civilization, for I cannot even reimagine my own. [516] Instead I devise something humbler: a single honest label beside the crystal vase shard. [530] It reads only that someone here once made a beautiful thing, and that it broke. [545] When the storms come now, I cover every mirror without fail, including the one I once left bare. [563] I do it not for fear of the past, but out of mercy toward it. [578] Some civilizations deserve to be remembered as they were, not as their lonely curators need them to be. [596] So I keep the mirrors veiled, the shard lit, and time visible, falling slowly like everything we love. [614]</story>
