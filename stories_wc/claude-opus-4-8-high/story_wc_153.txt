<story>The mangrove labyrinth breathed around me, its roots tangled like the unsent letters I still carried in my coat. [19] Spirits sang in the branches, voices braided from saltwater and grief. [30] I had come to find the cryptic medium who lived where the channels narrowed. [44] Her stilt-house leaned over black water, windows fogged with breath that was not entirely her own. [60] I climbed the ladder, clutching a gargoyle's rainspout shard, the only relic I had kept of my mother's broken church. [80] The medium did not turn as I entered. [88] She spoke to the glass, where words were forming in the condensation, letters traced by an invisible finger. [106] "You are between things," she said, "in the space where an apology waits unspoken." [120] I nodded, because that was exactly the country I was lost in. [132] My sister and I had not spoken in three years, ever since words turned to weapons over our mother's grave. [152] I had written her a dozen letters and sent none. [162] "I want to find the moment before deciding," I told the medium, "the breath before I choose to forgive or to harden." [184] She smiled, and the spirits outside sang louder, fierce and tender at once. [197] "Then give me the shard," she said, "for the dead speak through what they once guarded." [213] I laid the gargoyle's piece on her table, its carved mouth still shaped to swallow rain. [229] She pressed her palm to it and began to evoke my mother's presence from the salt-thick air. [246] The condensation on the window thickened, and new words bloomed there, letter by letter. [260] Forgive her slowly, it read, the way moonlight heals what daylight cannot. [272] I understood then that my mother's grief, like mine, had always mended only in the dark hours, in the silver patience of the moon. [296] "She left letters too," the medium whispered, "unsent, like yours, both of you waiting in the same silence." [314] The revelation struck me like cold water. [321] My mother had wanted to reconcile my sister and me, and never found the words. [336] We had inherited her silence and called it pride. [345] Outside, the singing spirits drifted closer to the glass, their faces forming in the fog like a chorus of the unfinished. [366] "They are everyone who waited too long," the medium said, "who died in the space before the apology." [384] I did not want to join them. [391] The medium handed me back the shard and gestured to the window. [403] "Write," she said, "while the moon is high and the labyrinth listens." [415] I breathed onto the cold glass and raised my finger to the fog. [428] I traced my sister's name first, then the apology I had carried like a stone. [443] I am sorry I let our mother's silence become ours, I wrote in the condensation. [458] The words held for a moment, glowing in the moonlight, before they ran down the glass like tears finally permitted. [478] Outside, one spirit unbraided itself from the chorus and rose toward the moon, freed. [492] "That is one of your mother's letters," the medium said, "answered at last." [505] I wept, but it was the fierce kind of weeping, tender and unashamed. [518] When dawn came, I left the labyrinth with the shard in my pocket and a true letter in my hand. [538] This one I would send. [543] The medium watched from her window, her own message blooming in the glass behind her: go, before the moment closes. [563] I had found the moment before deciding, and inside it, I had chosen tenderness over the long, comfortable cruelty of silence. [584] Behind me the spirits sang on, fewer now, waiting for the next traveler to learn what I had: that some apologies heal only when finally spoken aloud, beneath the moon. [614]</story>
