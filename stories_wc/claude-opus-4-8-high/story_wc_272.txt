<story>Maren had not painted a face she believed in for three years. [12] The crestfallen portrait artist climbed the lighthouse stairs anyway, drawn by a rumor of a forgotten library coiled inside its stone throat. [34] Books spiraled up the curved walls, their spines faded like tide-bleached driftwood. [46] On a reading table sat a broken tarot card box, its hinges snapped, its cards spilled into a luminous fan. [66] She touched them, and the world stuttered. [73] Reality fragmented around her: the floor became a dozen floors, each at a slightly wrong angle, each holding a different version of the same dim room. [99] Was she dreaming? [102] Was she finally cracking the way her old teachers had warned? [113] She did not expect answers; she asked the dark only to hear her own voice steady. [129] From the splintered air, threads emerged, fine and silver, tying each fragment of the room to the next. [147] She understood, the way one understands inside a dream, that the box had once held her own reflection in order, and its breaking had scattered her. [173] Each card bore a half-finished portrait: her at six, at twenty, at the funeral where she stopped believing faces could be true. [195] The walls of the library began to lean inward, shelves groaning, the curve tightening like a fist. [212] "When the walls close in" was not a metaphor here, she realized, but a deadline. [227] To make herself whole she would have to descend into necessary darkness, down the spiral stair beneath the floor where no lamp reached. [250] Could she face what she had buried? [257] She gathered the silver threads into her fist and went down. [268] The darkness was warm, almost tender, a luminescent ache that glowed faintly blue where the threads passed through it. [287] By untangling threads, one knot at a time, she found the fragments wanting to align. [302] Each loosened strand pulled a scattered version of herself closer, until the dozen wrong rooms slid toward a single true one. [323] At the bottom step waited the portrait she had refused to paint: her own face, unfinished, eyes left blank. [342] Why had she spent years painting strangers while leaving herself a hollow? [354] She did not answer; the question was only the door she needed to walk through. [369] With the last untangled thread she began to redesign the portrait, not restoring the old likeness but composing a new one. [390] She drew the funeral into the cheekbones and the six-year-old's stubbornness into the jaw, letting grief and hope share one mouth. [411] Where the eyes had been blank, she set the blue glow of the necessary darkness she had finally entered. [430] The fragments fused; the lighthouse stopped tightening; the walls exhaled back to their honest curve. [445] Reality knit itself, no longer fragmented, only quiet. [453] She climbed back to the reading table where the broken tarot card box sat, hinges still snapped. [470] She did not try to fix it. [477] Some boxes are meant to break, she thought, so the cards can finally be read. [492] Into the box she laid the single new portrait, complete now, eyes lit from within. [507] Outside, the lighthouse beam swung over black water, and for the first time the crestfallen artist felt the crest return to her name. [530] Was she healed? [533] Perhaps not, but she was assembled, and that was enough to paint by. [546] She thought of the years spent flattering merchants and mayors, capturing their vanities while her own face stayed a rumor. [566] The forgotten library had remembered her better than she had remembered herself. [578] Each book, she now saw, was a portrait someone had abandoned, spines bright with the same luminescent ache. [596] How many had climbed these stairs and never descended? [605] She would not be one of them. [612] Tucking the new portrait beneath her arm, she began the long climb down toward the door and the living shore. [632] Behind her, the spilled cards settled, each blank again, waiting for the next soul who arrived in pieces. [650] </story>
