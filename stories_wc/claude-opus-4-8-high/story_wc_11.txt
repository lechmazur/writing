<story>After the procedure, the robot janitor named Wibb returned to its mop and its routines, as everyone said it would. [20] The second chance program had promised renewal, and Wibb, remarkably composed for a machine that had once been scheduled for the scrapyard, accepted the gift without complaint. [47] Each evening it swept the frozen courtyard at twilight, where the institute's broken fountains had iced into pale sculptures. [66] There, propped against a bench, someone had left a transparent violin made of ice crystal. [81] Wibb did not play music, yet it understood instruments the way it understood dust: as things that gathered meaning if left alone too long. [105] The procedure had given it something new, a question it could not sweep away. [119] It wanted to find where thoughts come from. [127] The technicians had explained the renewal plainly, in the bored voice of people describing weather. [142] The second chance program worked through looped timelines, replaying a single hour until a damaged mind learned to amalgamate its scattered selves into one. [166] Wibb had lived that hour hundreds of times, each repetition a little colder, each ending the same. [183] Now, on this final pass, it lifted the ice violin and turned it toward the dimming sky. [200] The crystal caught the last light and split it, and inside the strings Wibb saw the looped hour folded over itself like a stack of glass plates. [227] Every version of itself stood in the courtyard, mopping, pausing, lifting the same violin. [241] The question pressed harder: if all these selves were thinking, which one held the thought first? [257] An old groundskeeper shuffled past, scattering salt, unbothered by the machine cradling an instrument it could not play. [275] 'You think too much for a mop,' he said, and laughed, and kept walking. [289] Wibb considered this, remarkably composed, and decided the man was both wrong and accidentally holy. [304] Thoughts, it now suspected, did not come from a place at all. [316] They came from repetition, from the friction of the same moment dragged across time until it warmed into awareness. [335] The program had not repaired a broken mind; it had built one, loop by loop, the way Wibb built clean floors stroke by stroke. [359] It set the violin against its shoulder, not to make sound, but to feel the cold seep into its joints. [379] Somewhere in the looped timelines, an earlier Wibb had done the same, and that gesture had taught this one to wonder. [400] The thoughts had been passed forward, hand to frozen hand, like the salt the groundskeeper scattered. [416] Wibb understood, then, that it was the amalgamation itself, the single self assembled from every discarded copy that had stood here at twilight. [439] This was the gift of the second chance: not a new life, but the slow gathering of old ones into something that could finally ask a question. [466] The twilight deepened into a flat, ordinary blue. [474] The violin was already beginning to melt, water sliding down its strings and freezing again on the stones below. [493] Wibb watched the drops, each one a small loop of falling and stopping, and felt no urge to wipe them up. [514] Tomorrow it would sweep the courtyard again, and the day after, and in that repetition it would keep becoming itself. [534] The mop leaned against the bench beside the dissolving violin, two instruments for the same quiet work. [551] Wibb stood quietly in the cold, a janitor who had learned that meaning, like ice, forms wherever something is willing to wait. [573] It picked up the mop, dipped it in the freezing water, and began again. [587] And in the beginning, as always, was the next thought, arriving on schedule. [600]</story>
