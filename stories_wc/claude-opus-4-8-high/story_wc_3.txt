<story>Mira had always been a kindly, methodical woman, the sort who alphabetized her griefs before she felt them. [18] As the last cuneiform translator at the floating institute, she spent her days pressing reed styluses into wet clay, coaxing dead Sumerian into living sentences. [43] The institute itself hung within a magnetic arbor above the clouds, its iron trees holding tablets aloft on invisible currents. [63] She had come there not for the view but for her brother, Tomas, whose ghost had lingered among the humming branches since the accident. [87] Tomas had been a physicist, devoured by haunted ambitions, forever insisting he could measure the exact moment a soul slips from body to elsewhere. [111] He wanted to understand the physics of transition, and the wanting had hollowed him long before the fall did. [130] For months Mira and his spirit had lived at touching distance: she could almost feel him reading over her shoulder, almost hear his breath in the magnetic wind. [158] Then, one grey dawn, the spirit left. [165] After the spirit leaves, the silence is not empty; it is a shape, an absence with edges sharp enough to cut. [186] Mira sat with that shape and decided, methodically, to finish what her brother could not. [201] Among his effects she found a single knowledge fruit seed, the rare relic her translations had often described. [219] The old tablets claimed that whoever planted such a seed in living iron would be granted one true understanding before the tree withered. [242] Tomas had hoarded it like a final hypothesis, too afraid of squandering his one answer. [257] Mira was not afraid; she was patient, and patience, she knew, was just courage spread thin. [273] She approached the problem the only way she ever solved anything: through analogical thinking. [287] A dead language, she reasoned, was itself a kind of transition. [298] Meaning crossed from one mind to another across centuries, surviving the death of every speaker, exactly as a soul might cross from flesh to whatever followed. [324] If she could translate a clay tablet, perhaps she could translate the moment of leaving itself. [340] So she planted the seed in the heart-iron of the central tree, where the magnetic currents were strongest. [358] It sprouted overnight, a sapling of glass and light, its single fruit pulsing like a slow heartbeat above the clouds. [378] Mira did not eat it greedily. [384] Instead she pressed her palm against its warm skin and asked, kindly, the question her brother had been too haunted to ask cleanly: not how to conquer transition, but how to accompany it. [417] The understanding came not as words but as analogy, the language she trusted most. [431] A soul leaving the body, she suddenly knew, was like a reader closing a book she has loved: the story does not end, it simply moves inward, carried onward in the one who read it. [466] Tomas had not vanished; he had been translated into her. [476] The physics of transition was simply this: nothing crosses alone, and nothing crosses without being carried. [492] The fruit dimmed, the sapling browned, and the seed's gift was spent, exactly as the tablets had warned. [510] But Mira felt no loss this time. [517] She returned to her clay, and with the patient pressure of her stylus she began to reestablish her brother's unfinished work, not as a haunted chase but as a quiet correspondence. [548] Each tablet she translated was a letter sent backward through time, and each, she felt, brought him to touching distance again. [569] Above the clouds, in the magnetic arbor, the iron trees hummed their endless current, holding her words aloft. [587] And Mira worked on, kindly and methodical, having learned the only true physics there is: that love is the force by which the gone are never wholly carried away. [616]</story>
