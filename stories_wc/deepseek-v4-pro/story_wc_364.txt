The orchard planet Idris was ribbed with floating rock arches that drifted between the treetops like silent blue-gray leviathans. [19] Now, after the Shattering, dead arches sprawled across the terraces, their shadows smothering the orchards they once fed. [37] Lio, the village blacksmith, was hardly a figure of rescue. [47] He barely reached a grown man’s shoulder, his voice a ghost of a whisper, and his forge had gone cold since the first arch fell. [72] Yet Lio was quietly determined, a trait buried under meekness like the root-threads the elders murmured about. [89]

He kept a folded square of magician’s silk tucked inside his apron, a fabric dyed the exact blue of a defiant sky. [111] The silk came as barter from a traveling conjurer whose clockwork familiar had broken mid-air. [126] Lio spent three deliberate days repairing the brass wing-hinges, and in return the magician pressed the azure silk into his palm. [147] “This cloth wakes stone,” the conjurer said, “but only if you barter a fragment of your own awakening.” [165] Lio nodded without fully understanding, and now he lived in the narrow space between understanding and confusion. [182]

The arches were not dead rock; each held a root-thread bound deep into the planet’s mineral lattice, a web that sang resilience into the orchards. [207] When an arch shattered, the thread snapped, and the soil forgot how to cradle tree roots. [223] Rubble buried the terraces, and hunger drifted through the dust. [233] Lio resolved to coax resilience from rubble, using the tool nobody else trusted. [246]

He descended into the abandoned mine shafts that honeycombed the ridge beneath the fallen orchards. [261] Generations ago, miners had dug for resonance crystals along the arch roots; now the shafts lay dark and choked with debris. [282] Lio chose the narrowest tunnel that dropped under the oldest terrace, where his daughter’s precious star-plum tree lay crushed beneath a collapsed arch. [305] He carried no light, only the silk, and his every movement was deliberate—each footfall testing the rotting timber, each breath a careful treaty with the cold mineral dark. [334]

Far down, he reached the buried keystone—a pale slab still faintly warm with captive energy. [350] He knelt and unfolded the magician’s silk, letting its azure shimmer fill the shaft. [364] The bartered awakening required a toll. [370] Lio pressed his palm over the silk onto the stone and deliberately offered a memory: his daughter’s laughter under a flowering star-plum, the perfect small weight of her hand in his. [401] The silk flared, and the keystone drank the memory whole. [411] A grinding shudder rippled through the rubble; the slab lifted a finger’s width, and a hair-fine thread of crystallized light began reattaching to the deeper lattice. [437] Lio’s mind flooded with the arch’s last moment—a searing rupture-song, a name he almost understood—then crashed into confusion as the memory dissolved, leaving a hollow where laughter had been. [468] He knelt there, caught between understanding the stone’s agony and not knowing which recollections were still his own. [486]

He climbed out at dusk, hollowed but anchored by quiet resolve. [497] Above, the orchard did not magically mend, but a single sapling pushed through the rubble where the star-plum fell, its leaves tipped with that same azure hue. [524] Lio saw it and felt a defiance rise, not loud, but the color of a winter sky that refused to grey. [545] That defiance turned into ritual: each dawn he chose a different mine shaft, knelt by a broken keystone, and bartered another cherished fragment—the warmth of his forge, the taste of honey, the ring of his first anvil—until his mind became a patchwork of erased warmth and borrowed stone-song. [595] The arches did not fly again, but their root-threads rewove, sending enough resilience back that green shoots fingered through the rubble. [616]

The villagers credited the seasons; they never noticed the blacksmith’s deepening silence or the deliberate way he moved as if listening to a buried thread. [641] Only the conjurer, passing through again, recognized the dimming in Lio’s eyes and said, “You’ve bartered away your awakenings, friend.” [661] Lio nodded, meek even in truth. [667] “But the trees are coaxed,” he whispered. [674] The magician touched the fading silk and said, “Then finish it before you forget why you started.” [691]

Lio chose the deepest mine shaft, the one that wormed under the central orchard where the Great Arch—the oldest, the heart—had shattered and left the land’s pulse unsteady. [721] He had traded so many memories that the descent felt like a long forgetting. [735] At the keystone he pressed the last silk and gave his final deliberate awakening: his daughter’s face, no longer clear but fiercely loved. [758] The barter completed with a low chime; the Great Arch split, and a fresh lattice root shot upward, threading rubble to lift a new arch, small but steady, into the azure sky. [789] Lio crumpled, emptied, suspended entirely between understanding and confusion. [798]
