Lior balanced on a scaffold plank inside the condemned hall, his lantern throwing a trembling circle across the hollowed sequoia library. [21] The tree trunk had been carved four centuries ago by a guild of star-obsessed scholars, its spiral shelves now holding only dust. [43] Tonight the building waited in the hush that comes just before a demolition crew triggers the charges. [60] Lior had been given the final night to finish his work. [71] He was painting a sky. [75] He was a portrait artist who could not recognize faces. [85] Face blindness had erased the whole from his sight; he knew others by the pitch of a breath, the twitch of a shoulder, the faint seam of a collar. [114] As a painter, he compensated by making each square inch of a portrait fractally precise, building identity from a million tiny, self-similar details. [137] His brushstrokes repeated like the fronds of a fern, each smaller curve echoing the larger until a face emerged from the accumulation of miniature truths. [162] That same fractal method guided him now as he turned the vaulted ceiling into a night sky. [179] The commission had come from the last archivist, who whispered, “Mirror the sky inside these walls before they fall.” [198] Lior took it as a summons to do what he could never do with a face: capture the infinite within a closed curve. [221] On the third night, while searching for a pigment recipe, his fingers found a hollowed book safe. [238] It was a copy of Ptolemy’s Almagest, gutted and lined with faded indigo velvet. [252] Inside rested a single silver coin, worn almost blank. [261] He angled it under the lantern and discovered not blankness but a geography of faint indentations. [277] The grooves were the remains of centuries of thumbs and the ghost of a stamped nebula, so shallow they whispered rather than declared. [300] Rotated slowly, the indentations formed a continuous spiral of linked arcs that reminded him of jet streams and the branching arms of a galaxy. [324] He realized the coin was not a portrait of a ruler but an accidental map of the sky. [342] He began to craft the mural using a method rooted in those faint indentations. [356] He would press the coin into a patch of fresh plaster just enough to leave a groove, rotate it a hair’s breadth, and press again, building an armature of hairline channels across the wooden dome. [391] Those channels became the fractal skeleton for his brush, guiding every stroke of diluted indigo and white. [408] Over days, the lines multiplied into a lacework of spirals, each one spawning smaller echoes until the wood grain itself seemed to dissolve into atmosphere. [433] At the hush just before dawn, Lior painted the final thread, a curl of white that closed the summit of the tallest arc. [456] He held his breath, and the tree trunk library filled with a heightened stillness so absolute that the lantern flame stopped flickering. [478] Above him, the painted dome breathed a blue that receded without end, crisscrossed by silver streaks of cirrus that split into finer and finer fractals until they vanished at the edge of sight. [511] He had mirrored the sky inside walls, and it was perfect. [522] That was the core concept the whole project had demanded from him: discovering limits. [536] All his life, face blindness had forced him to navigate the boundary between fragment and unity, to build meaning through infinite precision. [558] Now he faced the limit of impermanence—the most complete creation of his life would have no future. [576] The stillness thickened, and he understood that the mural was not less real for its brevity, but more real, because it existed only inside this suspended hush. [603] He climbed down, retrieved the coin from the book safe, and felt the faint indentations warm against his fingertips. [622] The coin was also a portrait of the sky, face-blind in its worn smoothness, speaking only through texture. [640] He set the coin back inside the hollowed Ptolemy and placed the book safe on a low shelf where it would be buried or found. [665] Then he lifted the lantern and looked up one last time, not as an artist dissecting a structure, but as a witness accepting a spell. [690] The fractal sky held no center, yet it embraced the whole hollow like a hemisphere of memory. [707] He discovered the final limit: the moment when the hunger to comprehend falls silent, and only the looking remains. [726] Lior walked out of the condemned building into the grey first light. [738] The demolition foreman stood across the street, a blur of features that would never assemble into a face. [756] High above, the real sky was losing its last stars. [766] He did not look back, because the sky he had mirrored inside the trunk would collapse, but the coin’s indentations still hummed in his hand, a map of limits that had become a beginning. [800]
