The ruined orchard district had not seen sunlight in centuries, smothered by fog so thick it turned day into perpetual grey wool. [22]
Kael was the last mirror maker left, and his workshop leaned against a crumbling cider press at the heart of the drowned grove. [44]
He spent his days polishing the backs of old mirrors with tin and mercury, crafting reflections that held only what stood directly in front of them—no past, no dreams, no lies. [74]
That made him, by the reckoning of the few remaining fog-walkers, firmly grounded, a man who refused to chase phantoms. [88]
Yet he kept, wrapped in oilcloth under his cot, an astronomer's broken sextant, its index mirror shattered into three slivers that still caught the occasional sliver of ghost-light. [114]
It had belonged to his daughter Lirien, who had vanished into the fog ten years before, chasing the dream of a star that she swore winked at her through the mist. [142]
That dream had run away with her, leaving only the sextant and a crack across its heart. [158]
Now, upon the shimmer of twilight frost, when the first ice crystals beaded the rotting apple branches and the fog turned to diamond dust, Kael felt the old pull. [183]
He unwrapped the sextant and carried it up the sagging terrace steps to the highest ledge of the orchard, where the bones of dead trees rose like glass mountains against the silent white. [213]
The frost had not yet melted; each blade of dead grass wore a crystalline peak, and the world seemed balanced on a razor's edge between freezing and dissolving. [243]
Kael knelt and laid the broken sextant before him, its brass frame cold as a dead star. [263]
He pressed his palms flat on the frozen earth to stay grounded, to remind himself he was not a dreamer. [282]
The runaway dream was out there, somewhere in the fog, and he intended to capture it—not to keep it, but to give it a frame, a mirror, a single instant of clear seeing. [314]
He believed that if he could recreate the moment of Lirien's last sighting, he might finally know if she was still somewhere behind the boundary that fog had drawn between the world and whatever lay beyond. [349]
The fog did not simply hide; it erased boundaries, blending lane and orchard, breath and vapour, memory and present ache. [369]
To see through such erasure required a method that no mirror alone could grant. [385]
Lirien's old notes, scrawled on the sextant's case, spoke of synchronized breathing: a discipline she had learned from the fog itself, exhaling when the mist exhaled, inhaling when the frost gave up its moisture to the rising sun. [423]
Kael had dismissed it as a fever dream, but now, at the cusp of twilight frost when the boundary between night and day dissolved, he understood. [449]
He brought the broken sextant to his lips and breathed onto the largest sliver of mirror, a slow, steady plume that matched the rhythm of the fog's slow churn below. [482]
The frost on the brass frame crackled, then began to melt in exact time with his breath. [500]
In the mirror sliver, a ghost-image bloomed: not his reflection, but a pattern of condensation that traced the arc of an unfamiliar constellation, one that Lirien had drawn on the back of a tide chart. [539]
He breathed again, and this time he felt the fog's breath answering from the valley, a low exhalation that stirred the dead leaves. [565]
Boundary erased: his lungs and the world's lungs became one motion. [581]
The mirror cleared, and within its cracked triangle stood Lirien, her hair tangled with frost, her sextant whole in her hands, her eyes alight with the star she had finally reached. [610]
She was standing on what looked like a vast plain of glass mountains, peaks sharp and translucent, refracting light that had no source. [633]
She smiled and raised the sextant, and Kael saw that the dream he sought to capture was not a lost daughter but the vision she had held—a universe where all boundaries were erased, where fog and frost and breath could build new worlds. [674]
Kael exhaled one last time, and the condensation evaporated, taking the image with it. [694]
The shattered sextant tumbled from his hands, but he had already recreated the moment, captured the dream in the silver behind his eyes. [718]
When he rose, the twilight frost was gone, and the ruined orchard was merely fog and rot again. [737]
But something had shifted; the glass mountains were now inside him, a firm grounding in what could be, a dream no longer running but held. [761]
He walked back down the terrace, the mirror maker, and for the first time his mirrors would hold a little of the fog's luminous boundary. [785]
