The ban lifted at moonrise, and the cloud-shadow poet crossed the frozen lake alone. [14] The cobalt atrium waited on the far shore, its vast dome a bruise against the twilight. [30] No path remained, only shards of old ice and the memory of prohibition. [43] The poet stepped inside, where a single lantern dangled from the keystone, its flame a trembling moth-wing of gold. [62] The humming quartz colonnade rose from the cobalt floor, each pillar frosted with ancient light. [77] The poet had waited years to stride through boundaries once thought sacred, and now the columns’ low resonance pulled like a tide. [99] A tremor of certainty passed through the poet’s chest, a wordless knowing that the bound and the boundless were about to blur. [121] The poet was drawn by something deeper than doctrine—an aesthetic ache for the way the quartz turned lamplight into cool, breathing sapphire. [144] That ache had survived the ban, and now it guided the poet’s steps into the humming colonnade. [161] On the nearest pillar old script coiled like frozen breath, words the ban had tried to erase. [178] They were a fragment of a verse that once named the lake and sky as a single blue throat. [197] The cloud-shadow poet did not repeat the forbidden line. [206] Instead, with a voice softer than the hum, the poet began to paraphrase, dissolving the boundary between original and echo, between self and stone. [230] The quartz pillars drank the new words, and their humming deepened into a chord that seemed to answer. [248] This was the method, the poet realized—through dissolving boundaries, not breaching them with force. [263] That tremor of certainty settled into bone, not as a shout but as a quiet, irrevocable alignment. [280] The lantern flame pulsed once, and a moth that had clung to the dome drifted down like a fallen syllable. [300] The poet’s paraphrase had restrung the original line, so that the lake’s ice now answered the sky with a low identical hum. [322] Through dissolving boundaries the poet had become a conduit, not a transgressor. [334] The colonnade’s quartz pillars flared with an inner light, and the poet saw their own reflection branching into a thousand shadow-branches. [355] The boundary that had once divided the sacred colonnade from the frozen world was no longer a wall but a shimmering membrane. [377] The poet stepped through it, carrying the tremor of certainty like a lantern that needed no flame. [394] The moth had followed, a flake of living night, settling on the poet’s shoulder. [408] Outside, the frozen lake lay under a sky rinsed of all noise. [420] The poet, a cloud-shadow thing now, watched as the lantern-moth hush deepened into something older than silence. [437] They had stridden through boundaries once thought sacred not by defiance but by folding the old verse into a paraphrase that dissolved the distance between the lake’s frozen throat and the sky’s blue one. [471] The colonnade behind them hummed the paraphrase back, and the lake’s ice began, for the first time in centuries, to melt into a mirror. [495] The cloud-shadow poet stood at the edge, seeing their own reflection not as a silhouette but as a breathing light. [515] That was the paraphrase—the self re-sung until it became inseparable from the world. [529] The flame in the atrium guttered but did not die; the moth circled it now in a slow spiral, its wings writing brevities on the dark. [555] The poet walked on, a cloud-shadow still, carrying the tremor as a gift that would unmake old divisions among those who still believed in walls. [580] And the lantern-moth hush followed, a quiet so full it became the only law. [594] The poet understood then that certainty was never a shout but a tremor shared between what trembles and what listens. [614]
