Kael, a conspiracy debunker by trade and temperament, arrived at the wasteland orchard on a morning when the sky was the color of a healing bruise. [26] He was sharply observant, a man who had learned to read the landscape as fiercely as he read human faces. [20] The orchard was an impossibility: dozens of apple trees thriving amid radioactive dust storms that had scoured the rest of the territory down to bedrock. [25] According to the survivor encampments, a flawed diamond buried somewhere beneath the roots radiated a curse that kept the radiation at bay. [22] People left offerings of water and fuel at the fence, terrified to step inside. [14] The comfortable lie was that a supernatural force protected the fruit, and that the price for taking it was a slow, soul-deep rot. [23] Kael’s motivation was to excavate truth from comfortable lies—a compulsion that had cost him friends, safety, and almost his sanity after the liquidation. [24]

The liquidation had been the great unmaking. [7] When the corporate councils dissolved their charters and liquidated their holdings, they unleashed a cascade of resource wars and reactor breaches that turned half the continent into a dust-choked graveyard. [30] In the desperate decades that followed, any scrap of hope became legend, and any strange survival became a curse or a miracle. [22] Kael believed that those legends were a poison, not a medicine. [11] He trekked through the storms to this particular orchard because he had heard the myth of the cursed diamond and intuited that it held the skeleton of a pre-liquidation truth. [29]

[18] The trees bent low with fruit so unblemished it looked artificial, crimson apples gleaming through the amber haze. [18] Not a single leaf showed the black mottle of radiation sickness. [11] He walked deeper, and then he noticed something that halted his breath: the dust was not falling everywhere. [18] Ahead, a perfect circle of still air hung like an invisible lens. [23] The flaw was not a curse but a crystalline anomaly, humming against his gloved palm. [15] Etched on the lid were the words: “Project Stillpoint—Solix AgriCorp.
