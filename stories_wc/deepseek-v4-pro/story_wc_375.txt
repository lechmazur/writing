The last echo of laughter had long since faded into the hum of the subterranean crystal cave, leaving only the drip of mineral water and the scrape of charcoal on canvas. [31]
Mael, a portrait sketch artist whose charcoal-blackened fingers were a permanent testament to his trade, did not pause. [49]
He was mnemonically gifted, able to recall every detail of every face he had ever seen, but that gift had become a curse. [72]
For three years his creativity had been corked tight since the exhibition where his final portrait---a raw self-portrait baring his own fractures---drew cruel laughter from the critics, an echo that haunted him long after the gallery emptied. [111]
Now he sat on a cold crystal bench in the glowing chamber, a subterranean cave filled with amethyst and quartz that refracted the faint light into a mist of auroral sorrow. [142]
His intention was to rectify the paralysis, to uncork the suppressed creativity that he believed still throbbed beneath his ribs like a second heart. [166]
Beside him on the crystal floor lay a small wooden box bound by a broken combination lock, its dials frozen with rust. [188]
The lock had been his mother's, and he had carried it unopened for a decade. [203]
Because of his mnemonic gift, he knew the combination instantly---zero-seven-twenty-four-eleven---the date of their last descent into this very cave when he was twelve and she had laughed in pure delight as a crystal prism spun a rainbow across her face. [248]
The echo of that laughter had been the last he ever heard from her before the illness stole her voice. [268]
Now, after all laughter had faded from the world above, he needed to open what she had left. [286]
He took the broken lock in his charcoal-smeared hands and attempted the combination, twisting the frozen dials by force. [305]
The mechanism did not click; the shackle remained jammed, the lock broken beyond mere memory. [320]
Frustration flared, but his mnemonic mind replayed a different memory: his mother's fingers prying a rusty lock with a quartz sliver, laughing at the stubbornness of metal. [347]
He picked up a sharp crystal shard from the cave floor and worked it into the shackle's gap, leveraging until the broken combination lock snapped open, its shattered pieces falling like guilt-ridden tears. [380]
Inside the box lay a single object: a small canvas, no bigger than his palm, bearing a charcoal portrait of himself as a child, drawn with the tender precision that only a mother could muster. [415]
Tucked behind the canvas was a note in faded ink: 'Paint what hides behind the light.' [431]
Mael understood that his calling was not to manufacture new images but to reveal what already existed beneath the surface. [451]
He turned to face the cavern's wall, a smooth plane of translucent crystal backed by a hollow pocket of darkness. [471]
There he stretched a fresh canvas, the same dimensions as the small portrait, and began to draw with charcoal, his black fingers swift and certain for the first time in years. [502]
Using his mnemonic gift, he recreated every detail of the cave's crystal formations, the exact angles of refraction, the bruised-purple shadows, and the faint auroral glow that bled through the stone. [533]
But into the composition he wove the outline of his own face, gaunt and older, overlapping the cave like a ghost waiting to be seen. [558]
When the portrait was complete, he took his mother's tiny canvas and affixed it to the back of his own, so that the child-portrait sat hidden behind the sheet of his new work. [591]
Then he hung the painting against the crystal wall where the hollow behind it caught the faint phosphorescence of the cave, and as the light filtered through, the hidden image emerged---a soft, sad sketch of a boy who still believed in laughter. [633]
In that moment, the cork of his creativity finally loosened; the suppressed wellspring surged back, no longer a torment but a calling as inescapable as the crystals' slow growth. [662]
He did not need to take the painting with him; he left it there in the subterranean cave, a rectified testament that some things must be placed behind other things to be truly seen. [696]
And as he walked out through the winding passages, his charcoal-stained fingers trailing along the cool stone, he understood that the laughter had not been a judgment but a key, and the broken combination lock had been the only door he needed to break. [740]
