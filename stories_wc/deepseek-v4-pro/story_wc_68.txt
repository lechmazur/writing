Orin had chosen the sewer because the water still whispered there, a sound he could shape into syllogisms. [18]
The sunken city lay above him, its shattered domes and brine-crusted spires a mottled grey beneath a lid of fog. [38]

Orin called himself a philosopher, though no academy would have recognized the soot-streaked youth who dredged refuse for lost theorems. [78]
He was rationally rebellious, measuring every dogma against what his hands could catch and his eyes could repeat. [96]

He drove a prybar through strata of harbour silt and shattered amphorae, working steadily downward, each layer a century of drowned commerce. [147]

Orin’s heart punched against his ribs; here was proof that the old methods were not superstition but a lattice of reason. [219]
Yet the competing narratives gnawed at him immediately. [227]

Orin’s narrative clung to the chronometer cog the way a drowning man clings to a spar: a world that could be measured, corrected, navigated. [297]
He agonized over the gap between the two, turning the cog until its tiny teeth nicked his thumb. [315]

She eyed the cog without affection, her tone an abrasive calm he had learned to dread. [346]
‘You dig your philosophies out of the mud while the Wardens string up anyone who quotes the old texts,’ she said. [367]

‘This is a tooth from the body of time,’ he said, his own voice flat as a prayer spoke backwards. [399]
‘They want us to believe the old methods were vermin, but the men who built this cog navigated by mathematics, not by pleading with storms.’ [424]

‘And what will you preserve it for? The children who spit when they hear the word “longitude”?’ [455]
Orin worked his jaw, agonizing because her logic was as clean as his own. [469]
He had no answer that could fit inside a stomach grown tight on kelp and suspicion. [485]
He said, ‘I will chart the sediment itself.’ [493]

His motivation was not hope but a rational rebellion against the narrative that insisted the past was poison. [592]

‘You intend to teach them through rust.’ [663]
