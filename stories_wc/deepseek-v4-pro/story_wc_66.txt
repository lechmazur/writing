After the story famine, the world lay under a permanent hush of damp wool and settled dust. [17] Lian was a diligent orchestra copyist who spent his days in a musty conservatory, inking notes onto vellum with a steady hand. [39] But the symphonies he copied felt hollow, as if the notes had forgotten how to weep or soar. [57] At night he retreated to a narrow ember alcove carved into the city’s old stone wall, where a tiny iron stove glowed and mist crept through the shuttered window like a slow breath. [90] There, on a shelf beside his inkpots, sat a shortwave radio, its dial a secretly luminous eye of amber glass. [110] He kept the radio hidden beneath a wool cloak, for listening to unsanctioned frequencies was a crime punishable by the narrative police. [132] After midnight, when the mist thickened to a whisper, Lian would unveil the radio and spin the dial until a hiss of static sharpened into music. [158] This was the pirate broadcast, a phantom station that transmitted no call sign, only ghost-threaded songs whose lyrics slipped through memory like water. [181] The lyrics were elusive, fragments of lost ballads and half-remembered lullabies that seemed to rearrange themselves with each transmission. [200] Lian began to write them down in the margins of his sheet music, his quill scratching a secret code beneath the printed notes. [223] He suspected the broadcast was a method, a way to smuggle the last stories back into a world starved of narrative. [244] His motivation crystallised slowly, like frost on a windowpane: he wanted to teach dust to tell stories. [261] The dust of the old libraries, now ground to fine grey powder, had once cradled a million tales. [279] If he could find the right words, the right sequence of elusive song lyrics, maybe the dust would remember and speak. [300] Every night he deciphered the broadcast, his fingers trembling as he pieced together refrains. [314] The radio’s secretly luminous dial was his only light, a tiny moon that seemed to pulse with the rhythm of the music. [336] He became fluent in the pirate’s grammar, a syntax of yearning and code, where a lark’s song stood for a forgotten queen and a sailor’s shanty mapped an underground river of story. [368] He worked in a thistled calm, the sort of quiet that pricked if he made a sudden move, knowing that the narrative police hovered just beyond the mist. [396] The cloak over the radio was always at the ready. [406] One storm-wracked night, the broadcast delivered a complete set of lyrics, a long, haunting melody that told of a weaver who spun clouds into memory. [431] Lian’s hand flew, capturing every syllable, and when the transmission ended with a chime, he knew he held a full story. [452] He had never heard a complete story before; the famine had erased all but crumbs. [467] He placed a bowl of ancient library dust on his worktable, a fine silt the colour of forgotten pages. [486] He smoothed it with a feather and knelt before it as if at an altar. [501] Then he began to chant the song he had transcribed, his voice low and steady, infusing the dust with the elusive lyrics note by note. [526] For a long moment nothing happened. [532] The mist pressed close, the embers crackled, and the thistled calm stretched taut. [545] Then the dust stirred. [549] A faint luminescence, secretly luminous like the radio dial, rose from the grey powder, and tiny spirals of dust lifted and swirled into the shape of letters. [576] The letters curled into words, suspended in the air, telling the tale of the cloud-weaver in a whisper that filled the alcove. [598] Lian wept silently, for the dust had learned to tell stories. [609] After that first success, he became a conduit, teaching handfuls of dust across the city, always under the cloak of night. [630] The pirate broadcast continued its gift of elusive song lyrics, and each became a seed that, when planted in dust, bloomed into a narrative. [654] The alcove remained his sanctuary, the ember glow a warm heart in the mist, the shortwave radio dial his guiding star. [675] The story famine slowly loosened its grip on the people’s tongues, as the dust in their homes began to murmur old legends. [697] Lian never met the broadcaster, but he heard in the music a kindred soul, a diligent presence stitching the world back together note by note. [722] One evening, he turned the dial and found only static. [732] The pirate broadcast had gone silent. [738] But the dust already carried its legacy, a library of whispered tales that needed no radio. [754] Lian put away the cloak and let the radio’s secretly luminous dial burn unshrouded for the first time. [772] In that thistled calm, with the mist dissipating at dawn, he set down his pen and listened to the dust telling stories all on its own. [798]
