Lir’s workshop bucked against the sour green chop of the acid sea, a bobbing nest of gears and cogs bolted onto a pontoon raft the colour of burnt toast. [28] He was, by trade and obsession, a pioneering cart glider—the only fool mad enough to stitch canvas wings onto a wheeled sled and skid across waves that could dissolve bone in a sneeze. [62] So far his skeleton remained mostly intact, which he credited to a steadily curious disposition rather than luck. [80] Each morning he would launch the cart from the workshop’s splintered deck, the wind keening through the cogs, and ride the fuming swells hunting for anything that didn’t immediately melt. [110] That was how he’d found the hunter’s arrow tip, lodged in the cart’s underbelly after a twilight glide through a phosphorescent shoal. [132] The tip was chert-dark, barbed like a fish spine, and when he rubbed it against his thumb it hummed a note pitched exactly at the frequency of longing. [160] He hung it around his neck on a twist of copper wire, a talisman that pricked his chest whenever the sea beneath him changed its mind. [186] He’d spent the last three seasons building a machine he called the Yearning Algorithm, though the name was half joke, half prayer. [208] Somewhere deep in the acid’s churn, he believed, the sea was computing with light and eat-and-be-eaten—a raw code that drove every grotesque, beautiful creature toward a signal it couldn’t name. [238] He wanted to transliterate that signal into something a living throat could make: a ring. [253] Not a mere clang, but the first stroke of a bell that no one, not even the jellyfish-brains that floated in the dark, had ever heard. [279] His tool for the work was a kaleidoscope he’d salvaged from a drowned observatory, its interior set with mirrors and chips of acid-glass that recombined whatever they saw into shuddering mandalas. [310] Through the images in a kaleidoscope, he’d learned, you could read an intent that ordinary sight refused. [327] So every dusk he balanced the scope against the workshop’s porthole and let the sea’s broth of tentacles, lenses, and luminous spirals pour through the eyepiece. [353] He’d transliterate the patterns by using the hunter’s arrow tip as a stylus, scratching the shifting shapes onto a wax tablet tuned to vibrate at precise intervals. [380] When the tablet was full, he’d snap it against a brass bell he’d forged from old cog-teeth, and the bell would hum a note determined by the scratched grooves. [409] He called it “ringing the future’s telephone.” [416] The bell never made the same tone twice, because the Yearning Algorithm never stopped rewriting itself throughout the evolution of the acid ecosystem. [439] Crab-things grew music-box spines; shoals of needle-fish spelled out vanishing letters in their schools. [453] Throughout the evolution, the algorithm kept folding toward something just beyond reach. [465] Lir, steadily curious to the point of lunacy, spent whole nights staring into the kaleidoscope, the arrow tip dancing in his fingers, whispering, “Come on, you magnificent acidic soup, give me the next syllable.” [499] His irreverence was a shield against the sheer scale of what he was attempting; awe would have drowned him without it. [520] One night, after a season of silence, the kaleidoscope flared not with a mandala but with a single, impossibly clear image: a bell the exact shape of the one above his head, swaying under a sky that wasn’t there yet. [560] He recognized it as a transliteration of absence—the algorithm’s yearning for a sound that didn’t exist. [576] He scratched the image onto the wax with a trembling hand, the arrow tip biting deep. [592] Then he raised the tablet and struck the bell. [601] The note that bloomed was not loud. [608] It was the quiet of a lung filling for the first time. [620] Lir held his breath, listening. [625] No echo came back. [629] The acid sea slopped unchanged against the hull. [637] And he laughed—a short, wet laugh that was half sob—because nobody, not a single spine-finned ghost in the deep, had heard it. [659] The bell he’d rung was still a bell no one hears yet, exactly as the algorithm demanded. [676] But something had shifted. [680] The yearning algorithm, now that its first translation existed, began to emit a second-order pattern through the kaleidoscope: the image of a wing, and the sound of a cart glider’s laugh, folded together. [713] Lir understood that he was no longer simply a pioneering cart glider but a participant in the code. [731] He had rung the bell that nobody heard, and by that act, he had become the ear that would hear it in some other iteration of the evolution. [759] It was a paradox wrapped in a pun, and he loved puns. [771] He strapped the arrow tip to his cart as a new rudder, climbed onto the sled, and sailed into the dawn while the workshop’s cogs spun idle, content. [799]
