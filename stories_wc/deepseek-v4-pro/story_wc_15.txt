The tide pools had emptied three moons ago, leaving the red canyon labyrinth dry and silent. [16]
In a salt-bleached hut at the canyon mouth, Elara the wharf weaver sat winding threads. [31]
She was a poet by dusk, reciting verses to the gulls, and a weaver by day, mending nets that no longer tasted brine. [54]
Her poems sang of broken precedents—of ancient promises the sea had broken and left smeared in salt on the cliffs. [75]

The plate was a relic of broken precedents, a memory of a time before the sea receded and the tide pools vanished. [121]

Now the precedent lay shattered, but Elara’s voice remained hauntingly optimistic. [161]
“Once, water obeyed an ancient pact,” she whispered, “and I will stitch a new reverence from the wreckage.” [179]
Her motivation carved itself into the canyon walls before she ever lifted a needle: to carve a path from ruin to reverence. [201]
Each night, after the tide pools lay empty under starlight, Elara rose without waking and walked the labyrinth. [219]

Those paths were her method, a cartography of longing that crossed and recrossed the dry basins. [259]

The copper plate rested beside her bed, its labyrinth a promise she intended to sew into being. [295]
Then she would sew. [299]

The illustration showed her a pattern: branching corridors, spiral chambers, and a central void where water once pooled. [340]
Using the sleepwalking trails she could never consciously retrace, she stitched cordage into the canyon floor, sewing a fabric of reverence between rocks. [363]
Her action was not repair but reinvention—a broken precedent turned into a loom. [377]

She incorporated fragments of her poems, knotting verses into the cord so the canyon walls whispered her words when wind moved. [417]
