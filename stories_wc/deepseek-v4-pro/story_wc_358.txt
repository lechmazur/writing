The port city had fallen silent three days before the accident investigator arrived, the evacuation leaving behind a hollowed grid of cranes and stacked containers that breathed only through the lapping tide. [32] She stepped off the last train at dawn, when a sea mist blurred the towering labyrinth of shipping containers into a monochrome reef. [54] She carried an ultrasonic anemometer, a field notebook, and a worn flight engineer's patch sealed in a plastic sleeve, its winged gear a comfort against the silence. [80] Her drive to cultivate foresight in action had been born from her father’s death—a flight engineer lost when undetected wind shear converged with a single missed warning light. [108] Now she was tasked to survey the crane collapse that had turned the container maze into a tomb, and she suspected the true culprit was wind funnelled between the stacks. [137] Documenting wind patterns between buildings—even when the buildings were corrugated boxes of cargo—required walking every alley with an anemometer and a stubborn belief that chaos left footprints. [165] She began at the eastern gate, where a narrow corridor between tank containers squeezed the incoming sea breeze into a cold, accelerating jet. [187] The fog clung to the metal, muting every colour into a hazy precision of grey shapes and sharp numbers on her display. [208] She logged 3.8 metres per second at head height, then mounted the sensor onto a tripod for a ten-minute sampling while she rapidly adapted her survey route to the shifting gusts. [238] The attribute of rapidly adapting was essential; the wind could change direction by forty degrees in the space of three container lengths, creating micro-eddies that a static measurement would miss. [267] At a junction where the container walls closed to a three-metre gap, she spotted a scrap of dark fabric snagged on a bent rail—a flight engineer's patch, identical in design to the one she carried. [302] It belonged to the crane operator, Anton Vekic, a former flight engineer who had kept his old insignia as a lucky charm. [323] The patch marked the exact location where, according to her initial reconstruction, the crane's jib had first struck the container stack before buckling. [345] She left it in place as a fiduciary point and resumed mapping the wind corridors, her curiosity now sharpened. [363] For an hour, she documented how the southern alley delivered a warm, lifting breeze that crossed the cold jet from the east, creating a turbulent convergence zone thirty metres from the patch. [394] She released a smoke pellet and observed the plume twisting into a tight helix, a standing vortex invisible from the crane's cab but strong enough to side-load a suspended transformer. [423] That was the singular convergence she had come to find—the collision of two air streams, a temperature gradient, and a geometry that concentrated all forces into a single rogue gust. [453] In that instant, the crane’s safety system, sluggish and blindly trusting the cab-top anemometer, could not rapidly adapt, and the human operator, trained as a flight engineer, must have recognised the feeling of lift betrayed by unseen shear. [490] She collected the patch from the rail, aware that it was now both artefact and evidence. [505] Tucking it beside her father’s patch in the evidence kit, she felt the weight of a bitter symmetry—two flight engineers, two patches, and a single overlooked convergence that had stolen them twenty years apart. [539] Yet the survey was not a memorial; it was a manual for foresight. [551] She continued documenting wind patterns between the container buildings until the afternoon, mapping the potential for future convergences, drawing a new network of sensors onto a port schematic. [578] The fog thinned into a silver haze, and the port town remained silent, as if holding its breath for her conclusions. [598] The labyrinth no longer felt like a tomb; it was a classroom where the lesson was written in wind. [616] She stepped out of the container canyon, the patch of a flight engineer secure in her pocket, and began composing the recommendations that would teach the port to read the air. [646]
