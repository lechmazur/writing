The mist clung to the storm-worn parapet like a whispered secret, and Kael Orin stood motionless at its edge, his fingers tracing the cool brass of an antique drawer pull hidden in his coat pocket. [35] He had been a prisoner exchange negotiator for seventeen years, and every transaction carved new lines into his face. [54] Tonight’s meeting was set on this windblown stretch of neutral territory, a crumbling watchtower high above a fjord that neither kingdom claimed. [76] That neutrality was a fragile membrane, sustained only by the moon’s pale witness. [89] He was an uncommonly thoughtful man, the kind who replayed every syllable of a conversation before speaking the next, and now his mind turned over the parameters of the trade. [119] The captors from the Western Reach had agreed to release the linguist Alessa Maran in exchange for three of their own, provided the transfer occurred exactly as the moon cleared the cloud bank. [152] That was the method they had demanded—synchronizing through moon phases, a primitive but unmanipulable clock. [168] No wristwatches, no electronic signals, just the ancient orb that tugged at tides and human blood alike. [185] Kael had accepted because it reduced the chance of ambush; both sides were slaves to the same celestial rhythm. [204]

He had arrived an hour early, as he always did, to let the terrain teach him its hidden languages. [223] The parapet’s stone railing was scalloped by centuries of salt wind, and the gaps between merlons framed shifting curtains of mist. [244] He pressed the drawer pull harder until its floral embossing bit into his palm. [258] The object was from the desk where, twelve years ago, he had signed a treaty that fell apart because he missed a single clause about water rights. [285] A friend and mentor had been executed as a result, and the drawer pull was all he salvaged from the burned-out consulate. [307] Its presence was a tether to that failure—a deliberate weight he carried to recycle mistakes into wisdom. [325] Now, as the furrowed hush of the waiting hour thickened, he rehearsed the apology he would deliver to the Reach’s captain on behalf of his own government. [352] The apology had been drafted by diplomats but lacked the marrow of genuine regret, and Kael knew it would buy nothing until forgiveness was earned through action. [379] He stood exactly between apology and forgiveness, a precipice as sheer as the drop beneath his boots. [396]

A distant horn moaned, muffled by the mist, signaling the Reach’s party had begun their ascent. [412] Kael’s eyes narrowed; he would not move from the parapet until they were visible. [426] The prisoner he held, a young reconnaissance officer named Torin, was tethered by a rope to an iron ring set in the tower’s floor. [450] The boy had been uncomplaining but watchful, and now he shifted his weight, making the rope creak. [467] “Stay still,” Kael murmured, not unkindly. [473] He had lost count of the exchanges he had conducted, but each one now felt like a strand in a fraying cord that connected two peoples who had forgotten why they fought. [505] The wind scraped the parapet, pulling at his coat, and he turned up his collar, feeling the drawer pull grow warm against his thigh. [529] Shapes coalesced in the mist: four figures, one hooded and slight. [540] Alessa Maran was being brought forward. [546] The Reach captain, a stocky woman with a lantern unlit in her hand, halted twenty paces away. [563] This was the ordained neutral ground—no weapons drawn, no sudden moves. [575] She raised the lantern skyward as the moon began to burn through the clouds. [589] Kael watched the lunar crescent sharpen, his uncommonly thoughtful mind calculating the exact moment it would be fully unveiled. [608] He noticed the captain’s jaw tighten, her own grief or doubt pressing against her composure. [623] The apology was coming; he could taste it in the salt air. [635]

When the moon finally ripped free of the mist, its light flooded the parapet, and the captain lit the lantern with a single strike. [659] Kael lifted his hand, then knelt to untether Torin. [668] The action was simple—a quick unknotting of the rope—but it held the weight of every lesson he had ever learned. [690] He whispered, “Walk straight, don’t look back,” and the boy stepped forward into the silver light. [706] Simultaneously, the Reach released Alessa, who stumbled slightly before gaining her stride. [718] The two prisoners crossed the midpoint of the parapet, a choreography measured by moonbeams. [732] As they passed, Kael saw Torin’s eyes glint with relief, and Alessa’s with exhaustion. [746] The exchange itself took thirty seconds, but he felt the long pendulum of history swinging beneath it. [763] When Alessa reached him, she did not speak; she simply pressed something into his hand—a folded note, damp with sea spray. [785] Kael read the note: “We regret the harm. This is only a beginning.” [798]
