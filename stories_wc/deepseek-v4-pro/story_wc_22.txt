The gloom-hill coachman stepped onto the jetty's salt-bleached planks as twilight seeped across the water. [15] He carried a leather pouch heavy with dried seed pods, each one rattling like a tiny bone. [31] Behind him, the dead reef sprawled beneath the shallows, a battlefield of coral skeletons furred with decay. [47] The reef had died two seasons ago, poisoned by runoff from the war machines that still rusted in the hills. [67] No fish swam there now, only the memory of colour. [77] The jetty, however, bloomed—not with flowers, but with a stubborn mat of saltwort and sea purslane that had crawled up from the last pockets of life. [104] It stretched into the narrow vastness of the bay, a strip of wood barely wide enough for a single cart, yet extending far enough to lose itself in the purple-hazed horizon. [135] The coachman listened. [138] A tern cried overhead, its call a jagged slash through the quiet. [151] He understood. [153] He had always understood dead languages, and the tern spoke in the tongue of the vanished reef herons, a dialect extinct since the last bomb fell. [180] He had been a coachman on the gloom-hill road, ferrying passengers between the mourning villages, but no one rode with him now. [202] His coach was abandoned in a thicket of ironweed, and he walked instead, following the birds. [219]

He knelt at the jetty's edge and loosened the pouch's drawstring. [231] The seed pods were from the garden his grandmother had planted on a battlefield long before the reef war. [251] She had whispered to him in Latvian, a language dead to his own generation, and told him that a garden could reclaim any ground where blood had been shed. [280] He was here to fulfil that claim. [286] The reef was a battlefield too, its cratered heads and shattered tables marking where depth charges had landed. [305] He meant to let a garden reclaim it, not with coral—coral took centuries—but with the seeds of the marsh elders and sea daisies that could bind the rubble and nurse the water back to something clear. [342] The bird calls would guide him. [347] He had learned as a boy that understanding bird calls was not mimicry but translation, a method rooted in the dead languages he carried in his throat. [374] Every chirp, every warble held the grammar of an older world. [385] The tern, still circling, dipped its wing and gave a three-note sequence that meant, in the heron tongue, *submerged concord*. [407] The coachman lifted his head. [411] Submerged concord was the peace treaty signed between the land and the sea in the time before records, an agreement that freshwater would meet salt in a balanced exchange, that the mangroves would hold the silt, that the birds would carry seeds between the realms. [454] It had been broken by the war, the reef dying as a consequence of that broken pact. [472] Now the tern was naming a place where the concord still whispered beneath the water, a hidden seam of fertile sediment. [493]

He took a single seed pod, rough and shaped like a small urn, and crushed it between his fingers. [513] The chaff drifted onto the water. [519] Holding the dark grains, he waded from the jetty into the shallows, the cold climbing his thighs. [537] He stopped when the tern shrieked twice—the heron phrase for *here, here*. [550] The water was murky, yet his feet touched something that gave slightly, not dead coral but a patch of mud that had gathered in a hollow. [576] He opened his hand and let the seeds fall, then gently stirred the mud to cover them. [594] Fertilize, the tern seemed to call, though the word in the dead language meant *feed the sleeping*. [613] He repeated the action along a line that only the birds could map, wading back and forth until the pouch was empty and the twilight had deepened to a blue so dark the stars began to blink. [651] The narrow jetty behind him now seemed a thread stitching the horizon to the shore, a narrow vastness where the world both closed in and opened out. [678] He climbed back onto the planks, shivering, and watched the water. [689] The surface was still. [692] He knew the seeds would wait until the next high tide, when the moon would pull the sea into a gentle embrace over the mud, and the submerged concord would remember itself. [725] In time, shoots would rise, flowers would bloom, and the battlefield would become a garden that fed the birds that spoke the language of the dead. [752] The coachman sat on the edge of the blooming jetty, listening to the terns, and felt the narrow vastness hold him between ruin and renewal. [776] The reef's silence was not empty; it hummed with the faint clicks of pistol shrimp beginning to recolonize the rubble. [796]
