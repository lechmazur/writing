Lira’s lungs still heaved with the memory of water when she came to in the tilting hall of the abandoned carnival funhouse, a stranger’s rough resuscitation still ringing in her ears. [31]
The only light was a repulsive, fungal luminance that clung to a tarnished opera cloak clasp someone had pressed into her palm during the chaos. [56]
This lingering luminance pulsed like a dying heart, beautiful and nauseating all at once, painting her skin with the jaundice of old photographs. [79]
A whispering voice coiled from the clasp, a confidante too close to ignore, its tone oily and sweet like cough syrup laced with rot. [103]
“I am the Promptress,” it breathed, “the advisor you never asked for, and you’ve already paid the price by surviving.” [123]
It explained that it was the ghost of the carnival’s last coloratura, photographically cursed so that every snapshot taken of her erased a season of her human memory until only this faint luminosity remained, trapped in the clasp she had worn on her velvet cloak the night she vanished. [172]
The Promptress’s curse could only be broken, she whispered, if Lira would unbosom her deepest dread into the heart of the funhouse, because her true motivation was to trick fear into revealing its weakness, and fear, like any predator, shies from the scent of its own blood. [219]
But Lira couldn’t simply wander the crumbling Mirror Labyrinth; she needed a map built from contradictions. [235]
The Promptress instructed her to search the collapsed ticket booth for the Dreamer’s Paradoxicon, a dream dictionary whose every entry had been annotated with paradoxical notes by a long-dead mystic. [265]
Lira forced her waterlogged legs to move, her camera bag still slung across her chest, though she dreaded what her lens might capture now. [289]
She found the booth behind a moldering clown head, its grin repulsively alluring, and retrieved the mildewed book. [307]
Opening it, she saw entries like Mirror: to see your reflection means you are awake; to see your reflection means you are already dream-dead and cannot be photographed. [335]
Another: Drowning—salvation; salvation—the final forgetting. [342]
The paradoxical notes were a blueprint for moving through the distorted halls: to go forward, she had to step backward into the most nauseating reflection; to find Fear, she had to call it beautiful. [376]
Guided by the Promptress’s whispers and the dictionary’s riddles, Lira navigated the hall of warped funhouse mirrors, each step a negotiation with the repulsive allure of her own warped image—she saw herself as a drowned photographer, a forgotten negative, a woman whose name would flake off gravestones. [424]
All the while, the opera cloak clasp she clutched hummed with lingering luminance, projecting faint, vanishing images of the Promptress mid-aria, but each flash of memory cost her another moment of solidity. [456]
Lira understood that the Promptress’s photographically cursed nature meant that even this remembered light would eventually dissolve if fear wasn’t confronted. [477]
At the labyrinth’s core, she found a chamber where a massive, tarnished mirror hung like a guillotine blade, its surface not reflecting but absorbing. [501]
The Promptress’s voice became a frantic insect. [508]
“Here,” she urged, “fear will drink your confession if you give it.” [520]
Unbosom yourself now. [523]
Tell it you are terrified of being forgotten, that your camera was a shield against erasure, that dying in this carnival with no one to claim your name is the horror that has trailed you since childhood. [560]
Lira’s throat tightened, but she spoke the words, her voice cracking as she admitted that every photograph she took was a futile attempt to trap luminance and memory before they could rot. [592]
The moment the confession left her lips, the mirror shuddered and pulled her reflection forward, but instead of fear, the image wore a stunned, boyish terror. [618]
Fear itself had been tricked: by hearing its own weakness—the dread of being acknowledged as something frail and pitiable—it manifested not as a monster but as a scrawny child clutching a broken camera. [653]
The lingering luminance of the opera cloak clasp exploded in a pure, terrible radiance, a repulsive light that made Lira gag even as she could not look away. [681]
The photographically cursed Promptress, freed at last, blazed one final, perfect note that cracked every mirror in the labyrinth, each shard reflecting a season of memory returned to her phantom form before she sublimated into the morning light seeping through the collapsed roof. [724]
Lira collapsed beside the dream dictionary, its paradoxical notes now blank, the clasp a cold, inert piece of silver. [743]
She was alone, her camera ruined, but for the first time she felt no need to capture anything; the fear of being forgotten had revealed its weakness—it only existed as long as she refused to be seen without her armor. [784]
