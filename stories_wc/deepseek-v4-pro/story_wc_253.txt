The farmhouse crouched in the permanent dusk cast by the colossal wind turbines that had replaced the sun. [18] Inside, a blank-faced performer named Elias sat at a splintered table, arranging a bouquet of dried flowers in a mason jar. [39] His face was a smooth, unreadable plane, the tool of his trade after the Happiness Mandate had scrubbed all outward grief. [60] Each morning he whittled small wooden birds from basswood blocks, his knife moving in precise, rhythmic strokes. [77] He would hold up a half-formed wing and, with a dramatic voice that filled the empty kitchen, narrate the act as if to a vast audience. [103] "Observe the craftsperson's hand," he intoned, "shaving away everything that is not bird, not flight, not song." [119] The dried bouquet before him—larkspur, statice, baby's breath—rattled in a draft, a brittle audience. [135] The Mandate, enacted eighteen months prior, had forbidden public lamentation; sorrow became a contraband emotion, to be dismantled in private. [155] In response, Elias had developed his morally ambiguous practice: he performed grief through dramatic narration of the most mundane tasks, so that the performance became a vessel for feelings his face refused to show. [189] To an outsider, he was simply a man talking to himself while carving wood. [203] The moral ambiguity lay in the way he used his daughter's memory as fuel for the performance, preserving her absence in a remade hush he could curate. [230] That hush was the core concept he had stumbled upon: a silence not of emptiness, but of deliberate composition, like a held breath between mourners. [255] He cultivated lightness in grief by whittling away what was heavy, turning memories into objects that could be held without bleeding. [276] Each whittled bird was a fragment of the daughter he could not mourn openly. [290] The turbines outside beat a steady, neutral rhythm, a government-sanctioned metronome intended to soothe the populace. [306] Elias had once been a stage actor, known for his expressive range, but now his face was a blank slate, all emotion channeled into voice and gesture. [333] This was his compliance and his rebellion: he offered the Mandate his expressionless mask while smuggling sorrow through his words. [352] One afternoon, as he carved a particularly delicate tail feather, he began narrating the act of making tea. [370] "The kettle, a battered aluminum vessel, accepts the water with a hiss of protest," he announced, though the kettle sat cold on the stove. [394] He mimed pouring, his hand steady, while the dried flowers watched. [405] "The steep time is exactly three minutes, a sacred interval in which the host contemplates the geometry of the cup." [425] This dramatic narration of mundane tasks had become his daily liturgy, the way he remade the hush into something bearable. [445] The bouquet was the key; without it, the performance felt hollow, a theft with no victim. [461] With its brittle witness, he could cultivate that lightness, as if the flowers absolved him of the crime of surviving. [481] A neighbor child sometimes appeared at the window, drawn by the sound of his rolling, theatrical voice. [498] She never spoke, just watched with large, solemn eyes that reminded him of his daughter before the accident. [516] One day, he finished a bird and, instead of adding it to the flock on the windowsill, he held it out toward the glass. [540] The child hesitated, then pressed her palm to the other side, and he narrated the gesture as if it were a scene change. [563] "The audience member reaches out," he said, his voice softening, "and the performer understands that the hush can be shared." [579] He opened the door and gave her the wooden bird, his blank face betraying nothing. [594] She took it, and for a moment the emotional void between them filled with something unspoken—a lightness, a remade hush. [615] Then she ran away, and the turbines swallowed the silence. [625] Elias returned to his chair, picked up the knife, and resumed whittling, the blade peeling away thin curls of basswood. [645] "The artisan cleans his tool," he said to the empty room, "and prepares for the next session, in which nothing is created and nothing is lost." [671] Outside, the light had shifted to the dull orange of a happiness-mandated sunset, and the wind farm's red lights blinked in unison. [693] He looked at the dried flower bouquet, its colors leached to sepia, and he felt the familiar void, but it was a void he had learned to sculpt. [721] Morally ambiguous or not, he had found a way to keep her close without drowning. [736] He set the half-finished bird down and, with a quiet, dramatic finality, extinguished the lamp. [751] The remade hush settled over the farmhouse, deeper than the turbine hum, and Elias sat motionless in the dark, a blank-faced performer at rest. [775] Tomorrow, he would whittle again. [780]
