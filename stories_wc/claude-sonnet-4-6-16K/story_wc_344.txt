Sera had not slept since the last eclipse. [8] She called herself a lunar-eclipse locksmith because she only worked when the moon turned to ash, when ordinary light abandoned the world and left only the cold mineral glow of shadow. [39] In that dimness, she believed, every lock revealed its truest mechanism. [50] But three eclipses ago, she had broken something she could not name, and her hands had not been steady since. [70]

The library above the clouds was her last recourse. [79]

She climbed the stairwell carved into storm-frozen stone, ice bearding every shelf, each breath hanging like a small ghost. [98] The place smelled of cedar and arrested time, the way a childhood drawer smells when you open it decades later -- familiar and foreign at once. [123] Under the soft hush of a distant morning, the frozen library held its silence the way driftwood holds the memory of a living tree: patiently, and without complaint. [151]

She did not know what she was looking for until she found it. [164]

In the library's far corner, behind a shelf that leaned like a tired shoulder, she discovered loose pages wedged between plaster and stone. [188] They were scraps of prophecy, brittle as moth wings, inked in a hand that had trembled with urgency rather than age. [210] She pieced her path forward through scraps of prophecy hidden in a library's corner, reading their instructions the way you read a fading dream -- imperfectly, but with growing conviction. [241] The scraps spoke of a hall of mirrors at the library's heart, and of a clockmaker's gear assortment left for whoever arrived after the breaking. [266]

Sera felt the recognition move through her like a current. [276]

She found the gear assortment inside a brass-latched case beneath the central reading table. [290] The gears were beautiful in the way old tools are beautiful -- worn smooth at the teeth, each one sized for a specific revolution she had not yet understood. [319] She carried the case through a narrow passage the scraps had described, and emerged into the hall of mirrors. [338]

The room was hauntingly bright. [343]

She had expected darkness, or at least the dusty dimness of abandoned spaces. [356] Instead, every mirror threw light at every other mirror, building a luminescence with no single source, only itself feeding on itself in an endless corridor of reflection. [382] She saw herself repeated until she became a stranger: the locksmith, the eclipsed thing, the woman who had broken something and could not forget it. [408]

The scraps had told her what to do. [416]

She set a gear against the frame of the nearest mirror, where a small notch had been cut into the silver border -- deliberately, she now understood, by the clockmaker who had come before her and had also broken something. [455] One by one she placed each gear into its corresponding notch along the mirrored walls, and as she did, the reflections began to change. [480] The images of herself multiplied, then synchronized, all moving in the same quiet rhythm, like a single mechanism finally running true. [500]

At the center of the hall, she found a small brass cap fitted over a pin in the floor. [519] She knelt and used her thumbnail to uncap it, and beneath lay a keyhole she recognized the way you recognize a word in a language you thought you had forgotten. [551]

She had no key, but she was a locksmith. [560]

She worked with her instruments until the lock gave way with a sound like a soft exhale -- not surrender, but relief. [582] What rose from below was not treasure or light or revelation, but a sound: a low, patient ticking, steady as a heart that had been waiting for someone to remember it. [614]

Sera sat back on her heels and listened. [622]

This was what the scraps had promised. [629] Not answers, but rhythm -- the rhythm of repair, which was the willingness to sit with broken things until you understood their original intention. [653] She had thought that to learn the rhythm of repair meant acquiring a technique. [667] She understood now that it meant acquiring patience, which is the longest and most weathered lesson of all. [685]

The gears turned softly in the mirrored hall. [693] The morning outside the frozen library grew no warmer, but Sera did not mind. [707] She had broken something three eclipses ago, and she could not yet say what it was. [723] But she could hear now what whole things sounded like, and that, she decided, was where every repair began. [742]
