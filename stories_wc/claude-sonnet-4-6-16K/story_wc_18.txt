The mirror maker, Sela, had been walking the ruined orchard district for three days before she found the sextant half-buried in frost-blackened roots. [23] The fog here was ancient, centuries old, so dense it had its own weight, pressing against her cheeks like cold palms. [44] The gnarled apple trees stood as dark monuments, their branches stripped bare, rising into nothing. [59] She had heard the stories -- that the fog had swallowed this place so completely that its borders with the living world had simply ceased to exist. [85]

She carried her tools in a leather satchel: polished grinding stones, a vial of silver backing compound, a single uncut pane of glass wrapped in cloth. [111] She had come here not because someone hired her, but because of the dream. [125]

It had visited her eleven nights running -- a figure standing at the edge of a vast glass mountain, reaching toward something just beyond the horizon. [150] Each morning she woke with the sense of having lost something irretrievable. [162] A mirror maker deals in reflections; she understood that the dream was showing her a boundary, and that the boundary was wrong. [184]

The sextant was the astronomer Draven's -- she recognized the maker's stamp, the distinctive ivory grip now cracked and yellowed. [203] Draven had vanished into this orchard district forty years ago, chasing coordinates that no living navigator could verify. [221] The instrument's arc was snapped at the joint, the index mirror shattered, its pieces scattered across the frozen soil like broken promises. [243] She gathered every shard. [247]

Upon the shimmer of twilight frost, when the fog turned gold and the air held the particular stillness that precedes full dark, Sela sat cross-legged on the orchard floor. [276] She arranged the sextant's broken mirror pieces in a rough circle around her uncut pane of glass. [293] The instrument had been designed to measure the angle between a celestial body and the horizon -- to locate a fixed self within a vast, indifferent universe. [319] She intended to use its pieces for something opposite: to erase the boundary between where she stood and where the dream lived. [341]

She was firmly grounded in her craft. [348] She did not believe in magic, only in surfaces and angles and the physics of light. [364] But she also understood that a perfectly silvered mirror could make a room feel twice as large, could make a wall disappear, could convince the eye that depth existed where there was only flatness. [398] Boundaries, she had always known, were mostly a matter of reflection. [409]

She closed her eyes and began to breathe slowly. [418] She had worked with apprentices long enough to know the technique -- the synchronized breathing she used when two people needed to grind a large lens together, matching rhythm so that pressure stayed even, so that neither hand trembled independently. [457] Now she used it alone, letting her own inhale answer her exhale, building a steady reciprocal current between her body and the frozen air. [481]

The fog thinned. [484] Not dramatically, but perceptibly -- the way a sheet of ice clears when breath touches it. [499]

She opened her eyes and began to work. [507] The sextant's shattered index mirror, piece by piece, she fitted into the backing compound along the face of her uncut pane, each shard placed at a slightly different angle, so that the finished surface would fragment light into a hundred distinct directions simultaneously. [550] She was not recreating the sextant. [556] She was using its broken geometry to recreate the glass mountain from the dream -- to build a surface that measured not just what was above the horizon, but what lived below it, behind it, beyond the fog's centuries of accumulated certainty. [597]

The first true darkness came, and the twilight frost shimmered upward from the ground like something returning home. [615]

The mirror was finished. [619] She held it up. [623]

It showed her the orchard, but also the orchard from inside the dream -- both at once, the fog erased between them, the glass mountain neither distant nor fantastical but simply there, a facet of the same world. [660] The runaway dream had no further horizon to flee toward. [670] It had been caught, not trapped, but held the way a reflection is held: acknowledged, witnessed, made permanent. [688]

Sela wrapped the mirror carefully. [693] Tomorrow she would walk out of the fog. [701] Tonight she breathed, and the boundary was gone. [709]
