The rusted library sat at the edge of the old correctional grounds, its corrugated roof open in two places where the stars looked in without permission. [27] Eli had been called many things over the years—vagrant, poet, fool—but tonight the guards called him volunteer, which was close enough to invisible, and invisible suited him fine. [55]

He came monthly, always carrying a different face. [63] Not costume, not surgery, something deeper—a shedding. [71] His companions under the bridge said he molted personalities the way a snake molts skin: March made him quiet and ecclesiastical, April made him loud and half-drunk on sunlight. [101] May was arriving fully now, and May made him want to forge beauty from anarchy, to press a crushed aluminum can into a sonnet, to find the vein of gold running through wreckage. [133]

He carried his coffee tin under one arm. [141] To anyone who shook it, the tin rattled with coins—pennies mostly, earned reading verse aloud at traffic lights while commuters avoided eye contact. [167] But the bottom was false. [172] Beneath the coins, beneath a soldered sheet of thin tin, lay the folded manuscripts he could not risk losing to rain or to the men who sometimes shook him down near the overpass. [202]

The prison library smelled of mildew and something older, something like patience. [214] He began sorting the way he always did, placing broken-spined paperbacks into genre piles while the single fluorescent tube above him flickered its indecision. [239] That was the night he noticed Marcus sitting in the corner, reading the same page of a Neruda collection over and over, lips barely moving. [264]

"That one giving you trouble?" Eli asked. [271]

Marcus looked up. [274] He was perhaps thirty, with hands that had clearly once built things. [287] "Every word makes sense," he said. [293] "It's what they add up to that I can't hold." [303]

That was how it started. [308] A friendship sparked sorting books at a prison library, which sounds improbable until you understand that improbable places are the only honest ones left. [335] They worked side by side for two hours, talking between stacks. [346] Marcus had been an architect before his trial, and he spoke about structure the way Eli spoke about metre—as though the skeleton of a thing was also its soul. [376]

Eli eventually set the coffee tin on the table and pried open the false bottom. [391]

Not the coins. [394] The poems. [396]

Marcus read them slowly, his mouth shaping the words without sound. [407] Outside, the clouds that had held the moon captive all evening began to loosen. [421] As the moon cleared the skyline, its light came through the open seams in the roof in broken silver lines, cross-hatching the concrete floor like a rough draft of something luminous. [452] The room cracked open without falling. [458] That was the tone of the whole evening—luminous fracture—everything broken just enough to let something through. [474]

"What are these for?" Marcus asked, holding a page with both hands as though it might leave. [491]

"Rebuilding," Eli said. [494] It was the simplest answer and also the truest. [503] He had started writing the poems during a winter that nearly killed him, when homelessness had stripped every comfortable story away and left only raw material. [528] He had decided then to forge beauty from that anarchy—not as escape but as architecture, to build something habitable inside the ruin. [550]

Marcus set the page down carefully. [556] "I used to design libraries," he said. [563] "Real ones. [565] Glass and timber." [568]

"This one's better," Eli said, meaning it. [575]

Marcus laughed—a short, surprised sound, like a lock giving way. [586]

They sorted books until the guard came to close the room. [597] Before Eli left, Marcus pressed a folded napkin into his hand. [608] On it was a drawing—precise and miniature—of the rusted library as it could be: roof sealed, shelves crowded, stars painted on the ceiling in their actual positions for this latitude. [638] At the center of the drawing, Marcus had placed a coffee tin on a table, its false bottom open, blooming resistance outward like a paper flower refusing the dark. [666]

Eli walked back to the encampment beneath the wide and clarified moon. [678] May was settling fully into him now, warm and combustible. [688] He pressed the drawing into the tin beneath the coins, sealed the false bottom, and held the rattling weight against his chest. [711] For the first time in a long, stripped winter, the rebuilding felt less like metaphor and more like mortar, less like hope and more like the first wall going up. [741]
