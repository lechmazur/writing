Mara carried the chip of foundation stone in her left palm wherever she walked the carnival boards. [16] The fragment had been pressed into her hand by Old Decker the night the raft city's original council dissolved, rough-edged and smelling of brine, no larger than a thumbnail. [44] It meant she remembered what the floating city had been before the investors started circling. [58]

The raft city had no formal name, though outsiders called it the Float. [71] It drifted in slow arcs off the harbor mouth, a lashed constellation of platforms and pontoons draped in colored lights, perpetual music leaking from every seam. [95] Carousel horses jutted from second-story balconies. [101] Fire-breathers shared narrow gangways with spice merchants and portrait painters. [111] Before the privatization vote, still six weeks away and still technically stoppable, it was the last place on the water where someone could live without a contract. [137]

Mara's official title was Culture Shock Absorber, a position the council had invented because they needed one. [154] She was the person new arrivals were handed to at the dock-gate, the one who explained which platforms flooded at high tide and which vendors would trade credit on a bad week. [188] She was orderly by training and intuitive by nature, which made her good at reading what people needed before they knew how to ask. [213] It also made her good at reading what people feared before they admitted it to themselves. [229]

She had feared, for months, that she was serving two masters. [240]

The development firm's liaison, a smooth man named Theis, paid her a quiet stipend to report on community sentiment. [259] The council paid her nothing but gave her purpose. [268] Dual allegiance had felt manageable in the abstract. [276] Now, watching the newer platforms fill with pop-up wine bars and boutique lodgings, she understood that the word for what she had been doing was corrosion. [303]

She found the first graffiti message near the carousel anchor post on a Tuesday morning. [318] Someone had painted a looping mural of fish bones and carnival ribbon, beautiful enough that tourists photographed it, but threaded through the pattern were small directional arrows and numbers that matched berth addresses on the Float. [355] Mara stood before it for a long time, her orderly mind mapping the code, her intuition already ahead of the analysis. [376] Someone was using the art to reorganize the displaced. [385]

She began to look for more. [391]

The messages encoded in graffiti art had appeared on seven platforms within four days, each mural vivid enough to seem decorative, each one a quiet instruction. [418] Gather here at low tide. [423] Bring your neighbor from berth twelve. [429] Trust the person who carries stone. [435]

That last line stopped her breathing. [441]

She pressed her thumb against the chip in her palm and understood that Old Decker had known, when he gave it to her, that the moment would come when she would have to choose which ledger to put her name in permanently. [482]

Mara spent that night in her bunk making lists, which was how her orderly mind processed grief. [498] Then she burned the lists, which was how she processed commitment. [509]

She met the graffiti artist the next morning, a young woman named Solene who had grown up on the Float and painted carnival scenes so truthfully that even Theis's colleagues had bought prints of them. [542] Solene looked at Mara with careful eyes and said nothing. [552] Mara placed the foundation stone chip on the gangway plank between them. [563]

"Tell me how to help you reorganize," Mara said. [572]

Solene picked up the chip and turned it over. [582] "You would be giving up the stipend." [589]

"Before the privatization vote, there is still something worth saving," Mara said. [601] "I would rather absorb the shock of choosing than spend the rest of my life softening the blow for people who never got to choose at all." [627]

Solene handed the stone back and unrolled a new canvas. [637] The next mural would go on the Float's largest platform, the one the development firm's brochures featured most prominently. [657] It would be gorgeous, full of carnival color and sweeping line, and buried inside it would be everything the old community needed to reorganize itself before the vote closed the window. [687]

Mara held the stone chip and watched Solene sketch the first figures, already reading the code taking shape in the curves. [708] For the first time in months, the desperation in her chest felt like it had somewhere to go. [726]
