Maren turned the titan gear with both hands, its teeth catching on the counterweight chain that lifted the atrium's eastern gate. [21] Salt water rushed across the copper floor in its twice-daily ritual, carrying with it the small luminous shapes she had come to call the others. [46] She had no better name for them. [53]

The tidal atrium had been her station for eleven years, ever since the calendars stopped predicting what came next. [72] Scholars called it the Vanishing, the morning when tomorrow simply ceased to exist, leaving only an endless present tethered to an irretrievable past. [95] Maren called it Tuesday, because someone had to. [103]

She lifted the bamboo creel from its hook beside the gear housing and waded into ankle-deep water. [120] The creel had belonged to a fisherman she had never met, its weave still smelling of river reeds and old patience. [141] She used it now to scoop the luminous shapes before the tide pulled them back out. [157] Each shape held a person's identity: not their name or face, but the particular pressure of how they had once understood themselves. [179] These migrated constantly since the Vanishing, detaching from their original owners and drifting through flooded corridors like seed pods looking for soil. [201]

The work fluctuated with moon phases. [207] During the new moon, the identities arrived dense and cold, stiff as river ice, difficult to separate without damage. [226] During the full moon they came loose and warm, overlapping one another, sometimes fusing mid-current into composite selves that confused even Maren's careful cataloguing. [250] Tonight was the waxing gibbous, which she privately preferred: the shapes were cooperative, almost willing. [265]

Her task, the one the titan-gear mechanism made possible, was to smelt them. [278] Not destroy them. [281] Transform them. [283] She carried the creel to the forge alcove built into the atrium's south wall, where heat and tidewater coexisted in a permanent negotiation. [306] The forge had no fuel she could name; it burned on contradiction, on the impossible fact of fire thriving inside a room that flooded twice daily. [332] She had stopped questioning it. [337] Accepting such contradictions was the only method that worked. [346]

One identity at a time, she lowered each shape into the crucible. [358] They liquefied, lost their old edges, and cooled into small tablets she shelved in the archive stacks behind the forge. [378] The tablets did not restore anyone to who they had been. [389] They preserved who they had been capable of becoming. [398] The distinction mattered. [401]

Maren had no illusions about scale. [407] The atrium processed perhaps forty identities on a good tide; the Vanishing had set millions adrift. [423] She would not finish. [427] She had never expected to finish. [433] The work was not about completion: it was about showing up when the gate rose, pulling on her waders, lifting the creel, feeding the forge that should not exist, and filing the tablets in order. [468] Routine was not the enemy of wonder, she believed; routine was wonder's skeleton, the structure that let the extraordinary stand upright. [489]

She shelved the last tablet and paused to listen. [498] The water retreating across copper made a sound like distant applause, unhurried and genuine. [512] Through the atrium's high oculus, the waxing moon threw a single column of silver that touched the wet floor and stayed, trembling. [534] The titan gears clicked as the counterweight settled, a sound she had heard ten thousand times and still found startling in its precision. [557]

She hung the bamboo creel on its hook. [565] Tomorrow would not come to confirm whether any of this mattered. [576] That was fine. [579] The tide would return in six hours. [586] The identities would migrate back through the gate. [594] The forge would burn inside its impossible wetness, and she would accept that too, because wonder did not require explanation. [614] It only required someone willing to show up and notice. [624]

Maren dried her hands on her coat and began writing the night's catalog entry by the light of a moon that, in tomorrow's absence, belonged only to now. [652]
