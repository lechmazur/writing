Vera called herself a myth buster, though the title felt less like a credential and more like a dare she had never quite backed down from. [26] She lived in the abandoned observatory on the cliff above Portsmith, where the dome had rusted open and the wind moved through its iron ribs like a slow, sad organ. [56] Most nights she told herself this was cheerful. [64] Most nights she almost believed it. [70]

The painted seashell sat on the instrument console beside her half-finished cello, its surface decorated in faded blues and golds by a woman Vera had never met and could never now ask. [102] She had found it three winters ago at the base of the cliff, still bright enough to suggest intention, as if someone had placed it deliberately and then simply stepped away from the world. [136] It functioned, she had come to understand, as an ephemeral compass -- not pointing north or south, but toward the next thing that mattered, and only for as long as it took her to walk there. [171] By the time she arrived, the direction was always gone. [181] That was the riddle's first joke on her. [189]

The universal riddle was this: every myth she had ever debunked left behind a residue of truth she could not dissolve. [210] Strip away the sea monster and you still had the storm. [221] Strip away the oracle and you still had the question that drove men to ruin trying to outrun its answer. [241] She had spent eleven years dismantling stories, and the stories kept winning. [253]

What she did now, in the gloomy half-light of the open dome, was reimagine past conversations -- not as they happened, but as they should have happened if everyone, including herself, had been honest. [286] She heard them the way she heard everything here: through resonant hums in the half-finished cello propped against the base of the old refractor telescope. [311] She hadn't learned to play it properly. [318] She didn't need to. [322] She only needed to draw the bow across the strings and let the instrument retell what she fed into it, the frequencies bending each memory into something closer to its true shape. [354]

Tonight she was thinking about her mother, who had believed in things Vera spent a career refuting. [371] The cello hummed low and long, and in the resonance Vera reimagined their last real conversation -- the kitchen table, cold coffee, her mother saying, "You keep proving what isn't true." [401] "When do you plan to find what is?" [409] Vera had laughed then and said something clinical about evidence. [419] Now the cello let her retell it honestly, her own long silence filling the space where the sharp reply had lived. [440]

The painted shell pulsed faintly -- she was sure of it -- and she picked it up. [455] Tonight the ephemeral compass pointed inward, toward the floor beneath the telescope mount rather than toward anything beyond the cliff. [475] She followed the feeling and found a notebook behind a collapsed stool, soft-covered and old and nearly full, not hers. [495] Inside the whisper of its closing pages she found the riddle written plainly in a stranger's careful handwriting: what remains when you remove every explanation? [520]

The cello hummed from across the room without her touching it. [531]

Vera sat on the cold floor and laughed, a laugh that cracked slightly at its edges the way old domes crack but do not fall. [556] She had spent eleven years answering the wrong question. [565] The myths were never wrong about what they pointed toward, only about the shape they chose to point with. [584] The shell in her hand grew warm, then cooled, then let go of its direction entirely, as ephemeral compasses do when they have finished their work. [610]

She picked up her bow. [615]

The observatory groaned around her, pleased and desolate in equal measure, and she drew the first true note from the cello -- not to prove anything, not to disprove anything, but to retell the whole long argument she had been having with the universe in a language the universe might actually recognize. [666]

The cliff held. [669]

The stars above the open dome were indifferent and numerous and entirely, quietly there. [683]
