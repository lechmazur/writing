Mira moved her flat-bottomed skiff through the rooftop canal with the patience of someone who had learned to live in borrowed seconds. [22] The waterway ran six stories above the street, threading between towers of dark glass and older stone, a system built a generation ago when the lower city flooded and never quite recovered. [53] She had mapped every lock, every junction, every place the channel bent around a ventilation stack or dipped beneath a pedestrian bridge. [76] But she had never mapped what she was after tonight. [86]

She was a natural history illustrator, trained to document the world precisely as it was before it disappeared. [103] She had drawn the last colony of cliff swallows nesting in the cooling towers to the east, had rendered in fine ink the skeleton of a whale found beached on a rooftop garden. [136] Her subjects were always things ending. [142] Tonight she was after something that had never quite begun. [152]

Around her neck, on a thin silver chain, hung a pendant cast in the shape of an infinity symbol, its two loops worn smooth from years of her thumb working across it when she needed to think. [188] The pendant was not merely decorative. [194] By holding both loops simultaneously and pressing them against her sternum, she could borrow time, a few minutes drawn from some reservoir she had never located, minutes that felt like stolen afternoon light, warm and slightly guilty. [234] She had used the trick sparingly, always aware that borrowed things carry interest. [246]

The city slept the way a giant sleeps, not completely, not safely, but in a rhythm deep enough that its interruptions showed. [268] This was the window she needed: the giant's nap, the two-hour pocket between the last freight barge and the first morning delivery drone, when the canal system itself exhaled. [300] The machinery that pumped the water fell into lower cycles, and the city's constant mechanical cadence stuttered, interrupted mid-beat, leaving a pause that was not quite silence but was close enough to hear what silence hid. [338]

She had first noticed it three years ago, sketching a species of fern that had colonized a rooftop drain. [356] A sound had arrived from somewhere ahead, not an echo of something behind her, but an echo arriving first, preceding the sound that should have caused it. [385] She had assumed hallucination, exhaustion, the canal's strange acoustics. [394] But she had heard it again, and again after that, always in that interrupted cadence, always from somewhere she could not locate. [414]

She believed, after years of following the logic, that echoes did not simply bounce. [428] She believed they were born somewhere, that there was a place in the canal architecture where sound folded back on itself, where the geometry of glass and water and old stone created a womb for resonance. [463] She wanted to find it. [468] She wanted to illustrate it, to hold it still in ink so that when the city finally filled in the rooftop canals and poured concrete over its own strange history, something of this phenomenon would remain. [503]

She pressed the pendant to her chest and borrowed twelve minutes. [514]

The sensation was familiar: time thickening around her, the water slowing to near stillness, the city's interrupted cadence stretching like taffy between beats. [539] She could feel the borrowed minutes, not as extra time exactly, but as room, the way a held breath creates room inside a chest. [563]

And then she heard it. [568]

Ahead, where the canal curved around the tallest tower and met a junction she had passed a hundred times, the air was singing. [591] Not loudly. [593] A low, folded tone, an echo arriving from the future of the next moment. [607]

She stopped the skiff, set her oar across her knees, and opened her sketchbook. [621]

The sound sculpts time itself, she felt it doing so, reshaping the borrowed minutes into something with actual weight, something that pressed against her like a hand. [648] She understood then that she had not come to uncage the echoes. [660] They were not imprisoned. [664] She had come to uncage herself from the belief that she was only ever drawing endings. [680]

She drew the canal junction in a single long breath, capturing the geometry, the angle, the way sound was born in the space between two walls that had never been designed to speak to each other. [715] The giant stirred somewhere below. [720] The borrowed time dissolved. [724] The cadence resumed. [727]

She had what she came for. [733]
