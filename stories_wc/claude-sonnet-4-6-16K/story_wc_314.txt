The waterfall had no name on any map Sera had studied, but she had named it herself, years ago, when she first pressed through the curtain of fractured light it threw across the hillside. [34] Behind it lay the orchard, ancient and unhurried, where apple trees grew in spirals no human hand had planted. [53] She came here before every great work, and today's work was the greatest she had ever dared. [70]

Sera, an auburn-haired alchemist with ink-stained fingers and thirty-two years of formulas behind her eyes, crouched at the roots of the oldest tree and opened her satchel. [97] The frozen dew crystal sat wrapped in lambskin at the bottom, cold even through the wool. [113] She had carried it from the northern shelf ice for six months, telling no one why, and now she held it up to the diffracted waterfall light that spilled through the canopy like shattered stained glass. [149] Rainbows moved across it. [153] She almost smiled. [156]

Almost, because Sera did not permit herself sentiment in the field. [167] Or so she insisted, though the lambskin wrapping had belonged to her daughter, dead three winters now, and she had chosen it deliberately, because that was the kind of woman she secretly was. [200] Logic was her public face; grief was her private engine. [210]

The crystal's purpose was to hold the final catalyst in stasis until the exact moment of transformation. [227] Every formula she had written over thirty years pointed here, to this orchard, to this morning, where the air smelled of cold water and green bark and something older than either. [258] But the formulas disagreed on the trigger. [265] Her notebooks said apply heat -- flame, specifically, controlled and precise. [275] Her instinct said wait. [279]

She had spent her career trying to resolve that argument. [289] Logic built the vessel; intuition chose the moment. [297] Neither alone had ever been enough, and she knew, with a certainty she could not diagram, that both had to act as one now, before the final transformation unmade the possibility of trying again. [331]

A sound rose above the waterfall's steady roar. [339] She looked up. [342]

Above the orchard, a murmuration of starlings turned against the pale morning sky, thousands of birds bending the air into shapes that lasted only a second before becoming new ones. [372] She watched the flock fold inward, then bloom, then fold again, and she understood, the way she had understood her daughter's moods before the girl could speak, through pattern rather than proof. [404] The murmuration described a spiral -- the same spiral the apple trees grew in. [417] The same spiral her formulas approached but never quite reached. [427] It was not coincidence. [431] It was the world answering a question she had been too precise to ask aloud. [446]

Sera's hands were already moving. [451] She set the crystal in the hollow of a root, where the tree's own warmth collected, then struck a match from her coat pocket and cupped the small flame against the morning breeze. [484] The flame did not touch the crystal; it touched the air above it, the way attention touches a memory it loves. [505] The heat descended in a column no wider than a breath. [516]

The crystal did not melt. [521] It opened. [523]

There is no better word. [528] A fracture ran through its center and the interior light, the impossible blue-white stored since the shelf ice formed it over centuries, poured upward and outward across the orchard floor like a tide coming in without water. [565] The apple blossoms, weeks too early, unfurled in the path of it. [577] The air carried the smell of a morning no living person had breathed. [590]

Sera pressed her palm flat against the bark of the old tree and felt something she would never be able to publish: the touch of wonder, clean and structural as mathematics, warm as grief. [624] It did not oppose logic. [629] It completed it. [632]

Above her, the starlings broke their pattern and scattered into the ordinary sky. [645] She stayed until the light faded, her hand still on the bark, not because the work required it, but because she needed to. [668] The waterfall sang its single note behind the curtain of color. [679] The orchard held its breath, then gave it back. [688]

She was ready. [691]
