The lavender bluff smelled of old circuits and crushed bloom, the way places do when they have outlived their own explanations. [21] Mara stood at its edge, her skin casting the pale blue glow that had earned her the name the locals used without apology: the luminous skeptic. [47] Below, the forests were holding their elections. [54] She had read about it in the transit feeds, the slow consensus building between root networks and canopy councils, the ancient oaks and the young birch collectives casting something like votes through chemical pulses in the soil. [91] She did not believe it mattered. [97] She believed very little, which was why she had come. [107]

On her right index finger she wore a tin compass ring, dull and oxidized, the kind of object that looked like it had survived several futures to arrive, disappointed, in this one. [139] It had belonged to her mother. [145] Her mother, who had carried weather with her everywhere she went. [156] Mara needed to understand why. [161] The question had followed her across three transit stations and one failed apprenticeship with a climate archivist who told her the answer was grief. [185] She had not believed him either. [191]

Above the bluff, the aurora unfurled in slow deliberate curtains, green and violet, terrestrially cosmic in the way that phrase only makes sense when you are standing under something infinite that is also, somehow, local. [226] The light felt earned, not decorative. [232]

Mara tasted copper. [235] She pressed her tongue to the roof of her mouth and recognized the signal. [249] For as long as she could remember, her body had narrated the world through taste changes before her mind could form the words. [272] Copper meant something was about to emerge. [279]

She looked at the tin compass ring. [286] It was not pointing anywhere. [291] It never did, and that had always struck her as its honesty. [303] But the ring was warm now, in a way tin has no right to be, and she felt the bluff beneath her feet vibrate with the low patient frequency of a forest finalizing its decision. [338]

Then they came. [341] From the northern path and the southern switchback, two others climbed toward her vantage, strangers who moved as though the aurora had called them specifically, as though the bluff itself had routed them here. [375] They converge, she thought, using the present tense because it felt prophetic. [387]

A man with fog pooling at his shoulders, dampening everything within two meters of him, as if he exhaled a climate. [408] A woman whose arrival raised the barometric pressure enough that Mara's ears clicked and the lavender stalks leaned into a wind that was not blowing from any geographic direction. [437] These were the people her mother had belonged to. [446]

The taste in Mara's mouth shifted from copper to something green and mineral, the flavor of water found underground, of things discovered by following what you cannot explain. [474] The tin compass ring grew warmer still, and she understood then what it had always been doing. [491] It was not a compass that pointed. [498] It was a compass that waited for enough signal to emerge, a device whose direction only became real when enough participants arrived at the same location, each carrying their own atmospheric truth. [530]

An emergent compass. [533]

Her mother had never been broken or haunted. [541] She had been a coordinate. [546] And so had the fog-shouldered man, and the woman with her borrowed barometric pressure, and perhaps Mara herself, whose skepticism had always been a form of precise measurement, whose glow had always been pointing at something she refused to name. [586]

The aurora above them thickened. [591] The forest below registered its final count, and the bluff held all of them in the way that old places hold things, not with force but with the quiet insistence of accumulated meaning. [624]

Mara looked at the ring. [629] It pointed, finally, not north, not toward any pole, but inward, toward the center of the three of them standing together under a sky that was performing its oldest and most futuristic trick: reminding everything below it that it had always been here, and so had they. [676]

She tasted something she had no word for. [684] Not copper, not mineral. [688] Something like the memory of rain before you know you have missed it. [701] She believed that. [704] For once, she believed that entirely. [710]
