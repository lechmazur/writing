Mira had survived three governments and buried two wars, which made the reconstruction committees very nervous when she appeared at their hearings uninvited. [23] She was seventy-three years old, insurgent by reputation, and grandmother by choice -- to eleven children who shared no blood with her but shared everything else. [48] The volcanic hot springs at Suluk Basin were technically off-limits during the reconstruction. [61] Mira came anyway. [64] She came because the ferns told her to. [72]

She had not asked to be telepathic with plants. [81] It had happened the year her daughter left, when grief had turned inward and something in her nervous system had quietly rewired itself. [104] Now the ferns whispered in incomplete sentences, the way a radio catches half a frequency, and she had learned to trust the signal even when the grammar was wrong. [133]

The kite was caught in the mineral crust at the far edge of the warm pool, its diamond shape bleached to the color of old bone. [159] Its tail had snagged on a sulfur-rimmed rock and it lay there like a letter someone had mailed and then abandoned. [180] Mira waded to it, steam rising around her thighs, the water the temperature of a living body. [197]

The tail was covered in script. [203] Not a language she recognized immediately, but a cipher she had seen once in her mother's house, pressed between the pages of a dismantled almanac -- a rotational substitution her people had used to pass messages through occupied territories three generations back. [244] The reconstruction officials had declared the old cipher illegal to teach. [255] Mira had taught it anyway. [260]

She crouched at the water's edge and worked the tail in the fading afternoon light, her lips moving, the ferns leaning toward her with something she could only call attention. [290] By the time the sun touched the ridge, she had decoded twelve lines. [303] They described a location. [307] They described a depth. [311] They described what she would find. [317]

The amber was small enough to close in her fist, wedged in a thermal crack at the base of a travertine shelf exactly as the kite had said. [345] Inside it, suspended and perfect and ancient, floated a single embryo -- not human, not quite animal, or perhaps the point before that distinction hardened into fact. [371] It was luminous. [374] It had been waiting. [378]

Mira had heard of implanted destinies before, the way older communities had encoded instruction into object and ceremony, trusting that the right person would arrive at the right moment with the right kind of attention. [413] She had always been skeptical. [418] She believed in choices, not scripts. [424]

But she understood now that the kite was not a command. [435] It was an invitation. [439]

To unseal the amber, she would need heat and intention -- and intention, she had learned, was the part tradition always forgot. [460] Tradition handed down the gesture without the reason, the recipe without the hunger. [473] Her whole life had been a negotiation with that gap. [483]

She held the amber at the surface of the spring, where the temperature peaked. [497] Around her, the ferns went very still in a way that felt like breath held, and she spoke aloud to them and to whatever was inside the small golden stone. [527] She told it that she was not its master and not its servant. [540] She told it she was its context. [547]

The amber softened. [550] Then opened. [552]

What emerged was not a creature. [558] It was more like a capacity -- a warmth that moved up her arm and settled near her sternum, and she understood it as a question about what she intended to grow. [589] Not what tradition demanded. [593] Not what the reconstruction required. [598] What she intended. [601]

The ferns uncurled new fronds in the cooling steam. [610]

Mira set the empty amber on the rock beside her and sat with her feet in the hot water until the stars appeared, one by one, in no particular order, like any good beginning. [644]
