Mara had always been the one they warned children about -- the woman who stayed behind after the shutdown, who chose the observation deck over evacuation, who pressed her palm to the cold viewport glass as the last shuttle burned a line across the dark. [44] They called her blessed for surviving what should have killed her. [55] They called her a pariah for refusing to leave. [64]

The time dilation observation deck had been the station's crown jewel, a curved platform of reinforced viewport panels suspended at the precise altitude where relativistic effects bent the station's internal clock by eleven minutes per orbit. [100] After the shutdown, it ran on nothing but residual power and habit. [112] She ran on the same. [117]

In the echoed silence of the emptied station, every footstep she took on the grated floor returned to her as a question rather than an answer, each metallic ring asking what she intended to do with all this unstructured time stretched thin as foil around her. [163] The station's designers had never accounted for a single person carrying the whole weight of the quiet. [180]

Mara reached into the breast pocket of her thermal layer and retrieved the gilded compass rose applique her grandmother had stitched to a scrap of heavy canvas before the family emigrated off-world, eight points tipped in gold thread that had worn soft as skin over three generations of handling. [229] It was not a compass in any functional sense -- there was no magnetic north in orbit, no needle that could orient itself toward anything reliable. [254] But it had always pointed toward something her grandmother called simply the true north: the direction a person moves when they stop reacting and start choosing. [280]

Tonight was the anniversary. [284] Not of the shutdown, not of the evacuation -- of the night her grandmother had first taught her mother to sketch the sky, and her mother had later taught her, a family custom carried forward the way all essential things are carried: in the hands, with attention. [330]

Mara unrolled a sheet of drafting paper across the viewport ledge, uncapped a soft pencil, and looked up through the reinforced glass at the stars that did not twinkle here because there was no atmosphere to disturb them. [368] They were just still and exact and present, and the observation deck's faint remaining power hummed under the floor like a sleeping pulse. [391] She began to sketch. [395]

The temporally stretched quality of time on the deck had never bothered her the way it bothered the researchers who used to rotate through in carefully calculated shifts, anxious about the minutes accumulating differently inside the viewport than outside it. [435] To her it felt like the universe had simply agreed to slow down and let her look. [452]

She drew Orion first, then the soft smear she knew was the Andromeda galaxy, then the configuration she had no name for but had sketched every anniversary since childhood, a cluster of four dim stars her mother called the Lantern because they were always easier to find once you stopped searching. [503] It was while drawing the Lantern that she caught a glimpse of something she had not expected: her own reflection in the viewport, superimposed over the stars, transparent as a suggestion. [534] She saw a woman crouching over paper with a pencil, alone, after the shutdown, in the orbital silence, and the woman did not look lost. [559] She looked like someone in the middle of a sentence. [569]

Mara set the pencil down and held the gilded compass rose in both palms, looking from the reflection to the applique and back, understanding arriving the way good things tend to arrive: not announced, just suddenly present. [606] Confusion had brought her here -- the station's collapse, the evacuation she refused, the strange gift of being the only one who stayed. [628] But confusion, she understood now, was not the destination. [637] It was the terrain. [641]

She picked up the pencil. [646] She added a fifth star to the Lantern, one that was not there, one she invented because the custom had always allowed for invention -- her grandmother had told her that the sky was a question, and a good sketch was an honest answer. [689] The echoed silence of the deck held her work gently, the way silence always holds the things that finally find their shape. [711] She rolled the compass rose into the edge of the drawing to keep it from curling, pressed her forehead briefly to the cool glass, and felt, for the first time since the shutdown, the clear sensation of moving toward something rather than away. [754] True north, it turned out, was not a fixed point. [764] It was a direction you had to keep choosing. [773]
