Mira had spent thirty years reading futures in the trembling smoke of incense, and she had never once been surprised. [20] Then the pilgrim arrived. [24]

He came on a Tuesday, which felt ordinary until it wasn't. [35] He wore road-gray clothes and carried nothing except a small glass jar, empty, sealed with wax the color of old bruises. [56] He told her he was looking for the cave beneath the hill, the one that sang. [72]

She knew the cave. [76] Everyone who lived near the limestone ridge knew it, though most kept their distance. [90] The crystals inside grew in formations that vibrated at frequencies just below hearing, and if you stood still long enough, the sound moved into your chest and became something you could feel but not name. [125] She had been inside only once, years ago, and she had come out changed in a way she still hadn't fully mapped. [147]

She should have seen the danger. [153] That was what people paid her for. [160]

But when she looked at the pilgrim, the smoke in her vision stayed stubbornly clear, like a window someone had just wiped. [182] Nothing. [183] She led him to the cave entrance anyway, because refusal felt wrong in a way her gift usually protected her from feeling. [205]

Inside, the crystal walls caught the lantern light and fractured it into overlapping shadows, dark shapes layering over dark shapes until the darkness itself became a kind of texture, almost woven. [236] The pilgrim walked slowly, touching nothing. [242] Mira walked behind him and understood for the first time that her clairvoyance had been a way of staying above experience, hovering always slightly ahead of the moment, and that hovering had cost her something she couldn't name. [280]

At the center of the cave, resting in a shallow basin of mineral water, sat a cave pearl, smooth and white as a closed eye. [305] She had not known it was there. [312] Thirty years of visions and she had missed this: a sphere of calcium carbonate built up in patient layers over centuries, formed by water that moved in circles so small and steadfast that the motion was invisible. [349]

The pilgrim crouched beside it and opened his jar. [358]

She started to speak, to warn him, but the cave shifted sound around them, the crystals releasing a low harmonic that vibrated through the soles of her feet and up into her throat, and the warning dissolved before she could shape it. [400]

The pilgrim did not take the pearl. [407] He set the jar beside it, open, and waited. [416]

What happened next was steadily unpredictable in the way that weather is, each second following the last with its own interior logic while the whole pattern refused to be anticipated. [446] The overlapping shadows on the cave wall began to move without any light source moving. [461] They thickened, gathered, and then, through the overlapping shadows alone, seemed to inhabit the jar, passing through glass as though glass were merely a suggestion. [486]

The pilgrim said quietly, without looking up, that he collected the ordinary dark. [499] The shadows that fell between things, the unseen forces that held the world in its familiar shape without ever being thanked for it. [522] He said that if you could bottle even a small portion, carry it out into daylight, hold it up so the sun hit it just right, you could magnify everyday magic until other people could see it too. [560]

Mira stood very still. [564]

The cave pearl glowed faintly, as though pleased. [572]

She had not seen this coming. [578] She understood now that this was not a failure of her gift. [590] It was, instead, the first honest thing her gift had ever done for her. [604] You could not see certain things in advance because seeing them in advance would shrink them into something manageable, and they were not meant to be managed. [631] They were meant to arrive. [636]

The pilgrim sealed his jar. [641] The shadows on the wall resumed their ordinary behavior. [650]

They walked out together into the late afternoon light, which was golden and sharp and full of small bright darknesses she had never noticed before. [675]

She never charged him for the visit. [682] She didn't know how to put a price on finally being surprised. [694]
