After the empathy virus swept through the valley settlements, silence became the rarest currency. [14] People wept in markets without knowing why, laughed at strangers' private jokes, confessed debts and betrayals to faces they had never seen. [36] The virus had rewired something fundamental -- it made every person involuntarily truthful, stripping the membrane between inner life and outer expression. [57] You could not lie. [61] You could not hide. [65] You leaked. [67]

Maren had learned to work alone because of this. [76]

She foraged truffles in the hanging orchard above the ravine, a terraced grove of pear and apple trees bolted into cliff faces by iron brackets three generations old. [104] Storms came through regularly, peeling bark, snapping the oldest limbs, filling the ravine below with a roar that drowned thought. [124] She liked the noise. [128] It was the only thing that gave her respite from the broadcast static of other people's raw emotion. [146]

She was rational in a world that had become irrational by decree of biology. [160] She catalogued root systems, tracked rainfall, mapped hyphal networks beneath the clay. [172] She did not guess; she measured. [178] And she had measured, precisely, how much of herself she had lost to the virus's side effect -- not in feeling, but in the inability to occlude those feelings from the world around her. [211] Every time she knelt beside a disturbed root mass, every time she found a truffle that had grown wrong, shaped by underground pressure into something almost human, she felt grief she could not suppress, and it rolled out of her like weather. [253]

The discredited textbooks had started as a joke among the foragers' collective -- someone's uncle had found stacks of them in a flooded archive, pre-virus medical manuals from a school of thought that mainstream biology had dismissed as pseudoscience. [291] They described the nervous system as a lamp that could be shuttered. [303] They described instruments. [306]

One instrument, repeated across three separate volumes with the same engraved illustration: a glass eye, antique, hand-blown in a furnace now long cold, etched with a filament pattern along its iris. [337] The texts claimed it could interrupt the broadcast signal -- not cure the virus, but occlude it, selectively, the way a hand cupped over a candle reduced light without extinguishing flame. [367] The mechanism was vague and probably wrong. [374] Maren had read the passages fourteen times. [381]

She had found the eye through a smuggler who moved relics between the valley settlements and the coastal holds, objects too strange or too charged with history to pass through checkpoints. [412] He had been involuntarily truthful about his fear when he handed it to her -- she felt it arrive in her chest, secondhand, like a gift she had not asked for. [442] The eye sat in her palm, smooth and heavier than she expected, its painted iris the color of river clay. [462]

In the orchard, during a storm that bent the iron-bracketed trees sideways and turned the ravine into a throat of white sound, she pressed the glass eye against her sternum the way the textbook suggested -- not to her own socket, but to the body's center, which the author called the second face. [514] She felt nothing. [517] Then she felt something she could not name: a narrowing. [527] A partial door. [530] Not silence, but reduced volume. [535]

She had not wanted to be cured of empathy. [544] She had wanted to cultivate psychological safety -- not absence of feeling, but a room where feeling could exist without bleeding through the walls, where she could be present with herself before she was present with anyone else. [581] The virus had stolen the antechamber. [587] She was trying to build it back. [594]

The storm passed. [597] The ravine dripped. [600] A pear tree had lost two major limbs and they lay across the truffle bed she had been monitoring for six weeks. [622] She catalogued the damage in her notebook, felt the loss clearly, contained it clearly, and moved to the next tree. [642]

The glass eye went back into the tobacco tin she carried in her coat. [656] She did not know if it worked. [663] She knew she had spent three hours not broadcasting. [672] The textbooks were wrong about almost everything and possibly right about this one thing, the way broken clocks are right, briefly, by accident, with a kind of fractured grace that is still grace. [705]

She kept moving through the orchard, reading the soil, listening to the ravine go quiet. [720]
