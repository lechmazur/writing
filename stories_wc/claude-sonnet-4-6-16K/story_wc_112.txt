Sela arrived at the xenobiology research station just as the sun began to bleed sideways across the ridge, painting everything in amber and shadow. [24] She was an obliging sky mechanic by trade, meaning she serviced the atmospheric rigs and altitude drones that kept the station supplied through mountain winters most people refused to imagine. [54] But tonight she had not climbed four thousand meters to fix anything mechanical. [67] Tonight she carried the magnetic nexus at twilight, a palm-sized lattice of rare-earth alloys that hummed against her gloves with something she could only describe as mechanically warm -- not body heat, but the suggestion of it, the ghost of intention pressed into metal. [110]

The station's resident xenobiologist, Dr. Okafor, met her at the pressure door without surprise. [124] He had known she was coming because she had left him a voicemail three days ago that simply said, "I think I finally understand what the Mira-moths are doing." [153]

Inside, the lab smelled of recycled air and something faintly biological, sweet and alien, like pollen from a flower that had never decided what season it wanted. [180] The Mira-moths hung from their steel frames in the observation bay -- forty-seven of them, wings folded, perfectly still. [198] They were xenobiological specimens collected from the upper cloud layers of a gas giant's stratospheric shelf, creatures whose biology no one had fully catalogued. [222] For three months they had remained in this suspended state, neither dead nor properly asleep, their metabolic signatures producing readings that made Dr. Okafor's instruments argue with themselves. [250]

Sela called it subdued awakenings -- not hibernation, not death, but a continuous, imperceptible becoming. [264] She had coined the phrase the way a mechanic coins a phrase: bluntly, out of necessity, because nothing else fit the bolt. [286]

She set the nexus on the central workbench and activated it with a lever built into its base, a thin brass arm she had machined herself in the station's equipment bay. [317] The lever clicked once, twice, and the lattice bloomed open like a metal flower, its rare-earth cores aligning to the local magnetic field as twilight pressed through the observation dome above them. [349] A faint resonance filled the room. [355] The Mira-moths stirred -- not awake, not asleep, but somewhere sideways from both. [367]

"Watch the wings," Sela said. [372]

The wings did not beat. [377] But within the hush of beating wings at rest, something changed -- a shimmer passed through each folded membrane, visible only because the nexus was now projecting a holographic field across the bay, mapping what the human eye could not catch on its own. [420] Sela had spent six weeks calibrating the nexus to work through holographic principles, translating the moths' electromagnetic signatures into visible geometry, turning invisible time into lines you could almost touch. [450]

The holographic lines did not move forward. [457] They moved sideways. [460]

"There," she said, and her voice carried the station's particular icy warmth -- that tone people develop when they have been alone at altitude long enough that kindness arrives without ceremony, stripped of sentimentality, direct as cold air. [497]

Dr. Okafor leaned close. [501] "The temporal gradient," he whispered. [506]

"It's not a gradient," Sela said. [512] "A gradient implies direction -- forward or back. [519] This is lateral. [522] Time moves sideways through them while they rest. [530] That's why the metabolic readings have never made chronological sense." [540] She had spent three months trying to prove this, not because anyone had asked her to, not because there was funding for it, but because a mechanic who services the sky learns to distrust the assumption that things only move in the directions you expect. [585]

The holographic lines pulsed again, silver and slow, and the moths remained perfectly, beautifully still. [600]

Dr. Okafor straightened and looked at her with something that was not quite admiration and not quite relief, but was everything in between. [623]

"Write it up," he said. [628]

Sela closed the lever and the nexus folded back into its quiet lattice, warm in her hands again like something that remembered being useful. [652] Outside, the last light left the mountain, and the station held its breath, forty-seven wings at rest, time slipping gently sideways into whatever came next. [677]
