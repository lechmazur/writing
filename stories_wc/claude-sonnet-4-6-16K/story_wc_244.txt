Vera Moss arrived at the Kellerton station the way she always arrived anywhere -- sideways, through rumor. [16] Someone in the relief camp had heard from a supply runner that a woman was offering moon rock fragments in exchange for canned goods. [40] The supply runner had heard it from a mechanic who swore a logistician had materialized in the overgrown station on the edge of town, distributing goods nobody had ordered from a manifest nobody had signed. [75] By the time the story reached the camp coordinator, it had grown a tail: she could cure anything. [93]

None of it was entirely false. [99]

The station itself had been disused for thirty years, and ivy had made a serious architectural argument for reclaiming it. [119] Green runners laced every pillar, sealed two of the three platform exits, and had taken the clock apart stem by stem until only the rusted hands remained, frozen at 11:47. [150] It smelled of rain-wet stone and something older, something the earth remembers. [162]

Vera stood behind what had been the ticket counter, arranging water purification tablets in columns she found aesthetically satisfying. [181] She was not hard to find -- just reliably ahead of wherever you thought to look. [196] This was the paradox of her reputation: everyone knew her name, nobody could say they had seen her arrive. [215]

When the coordinator finally located her, Vera greeted him with a handshake and the immediate observation that his left boot was unlaced. [237] "A tripping hazard," she said cheerfully, "in a world already full of them." [250] This was how she opened every conversation -- with a small, bright deflection that felt like a joke but landed like a warning. [272] The desperately jocular quality in her voice was the kind that forms in people who have coordinated too many disasters to afford the weight of them all at once. [301]

She pulled the fragment of moon rock from her jacket pocket and set it on the counter between them. [320] It was smaller than a fist, dark gray with faint silver inclusions, and it had a cold somehow different from ordinary cold -- a cold that had traveled farther. [350] "Proof," she said, "that things from very far away can still land somewhere useful." [364]

The coordinator asked what she wanted in return. [372]

She told him: she wanted the camp's people to talk. [382] Not to her, but to each other. [389] She had a theory, developed across seventeen deployments and four continents, that loneliness was the disaster underneath every other disaster, and that the cure had something to do with obligation -- not charity, which flowed one direction, but reciprocity, which circled back. [430] She wanted to catalyze a system where every act of receiving required an act of giving to someone entirely different, someone the giver had never met. [456] No debt, only chain. [460]

The coordinator thought this sounded like polite anarchy. [468]

"That's exactly right," said Vera. [473]

This was when the story changed. [479]

Not loudly. [481] The way stories actually change: a single person walking back to camp, telling the others what the logistician behind the ivy-wrapped counter had proposed, and the others laughing first, then listening, then tentatively agreeing, because it was just strange enough to feel like it might work. [527] By morning the rumor had cycled through the camp in three independent versions, each slightly different, all pointing toward the same center. [549] An elderly woman brought a bag of rice she had been hoarding and received, by the chain's first turn, someone else's knowledge of water filtration. [574] A teenager who hadn't spoken in two weeks repaired a radio and was given, in return, the company of two strangers who stayed until he laughed. [600]

Vera documented none of this. [605] She didn't need to -- the chain was self-sustaining now, bound by reciprocity in a way no coordinator could have mandated and no single person could have engineered. [632] She left the moon rock on the counter because it had served its purpose, which was to make people curious enough to stop and listen. [657] She left before anyone thought to ask her name again. [667]

Outside, the ivy closed quietly behind her. [674] Somewhere further down the line, another disused place was waiting, another disaster, another population learning in increments that loneliness yields not to comfort but to obligation made mutual. [703] She walked toward it, already rehearsing the first joke. [712]
