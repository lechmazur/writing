Her father had spent thirty years teaching probability, filling whiteboards with the mathematics of chance, and losing every Saturday at the card tables downtown. [24] Mara inherited both tendencies: the equations and the compulsion. [33] After his heart attack in June, she stood at the hospital window watching summer rain streak the glass, understanding finally that she had become him. [58]

She left the hospital with his candle lantern in her bag, the one with the amber mica pane he kept on his desk to soften evening light while he graded papers. [89] She had taken it without asking, the way gamblers take things they cannot afford. [103]

The subway carried her across the city through flooded tunnels, the rain having worked its way underground. [120] She had nowhere particular to go, only the old habit of motion. [132] But she had started, weeks earlier, reading the scrawls on the subway seats, the cryptic marks riders left in pen and nail and sometimes lipstick, the abbreviated confessions and coordinates and half-equations that she alone, trained in pattern, could partially decipher. [173] It was how she traversed the city now: by interpreting cryptic scrawls on subway seats, following a grammar of strangers' desperation toward wherever it seemed to point. [200] That afternoon the marks directed her uptown to the branch library on Morningside, rain-washed and nearly empty at midsummer, its limestone face darkened to the color of old moss. [229]

Inside, the reading room smelled of wet wool and binding glue. [240] Mara set the lantern on a study table and lit it, though the overhead lights worked fine. [257] She needed the mica pane's particular warmth, its amber honesty. [267] The librarian glanced over and said nothing. [274] Libraries in summer have their own tolerance. [281]

She pulled out her father's notebook, the one the paramedics had pressed into her hands. [296] In it were probability lectures, half-finished, and beside them, in the margins, his betting records. [311] The two scripts shared the same handwriting, the same careful optimism of a man who believed numbers could be bent toward desire. [333] He had taught her that expected value is cold law. [343] He had also taught her, inadvertently, that knowing the law changes nothing when the want is large enough. [361] This was the lucid contradiction she lived inside: complete comprehension of why she would lose, combined with the intact intention to play. [383] Not ignorance but clarity turned against itself. [390]

Mara was what her graduate colleagues called theoretically rebellious, meaning she questioned every framework in seminar and obeyed every instinct at the casino. [413] She had published a paper on cognitive bias in probabilistic reasoning that her advisor called elegant. [429] She had also, the previous winter, pawned her laptop to cover a debt. [442]

The rain accelerated against the library windows. [449] Mara held the lantern close, the mica pane casting a small warm ellipse across her father's numbers. [466] What she wanted was not absolution but a kind of responsible widening, to widen perspectives responsibly, to let his collapse mean more than a cautionary repetition. [492] She wanted to look at the contradiction without flinching and carry it somewhere useful. [506]

She opened to a lecture page titled The Gambler's Ruin. [516] Her father had underlined the theorem in red and written in the margin: but the ruin takes so long it feels like living. [539] She almost laughed. [542] She almost cried. [545] Instead she felt the particular sadness that has clover in it, green and small and persistent, the kind that grows back through concrete given half a chance. [572]

She thought about the scrawl she had followed here: a sequence of numbers and a word she had read as owe but which might have been own. [599] The difference mattered. [602]

Outside, summer rain continued its indifferent erasure of the streets. [612] She would not gamble tonight. [617] She could not promise tomorrow. [622] But she lit the lantern a little higher, let the mica pane throw its amber light across the full width of the page, and began, carefully, to annotate her father's margins in a different color. [657] Not correcting him. [660] Completing him. [662]
