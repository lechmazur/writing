Mira did not know faces. [5] She had painted hundreds of them, and each one was a map she could not read from the inside. [24] What she knew was texture: the grain of linen, the drag of a brush through wet gesso, the particular weight of silence before a crowd decided whether a portrait was true. [55] Today, the silence had a different quality. [62]

The scholar's guild had hollowed the great oak decades before the building around it was raised, and the tree trunk library at the center of the condemned structure still breathed with the smell of old paper and damp bark. [101] Mira sat cross-legged on the floor between two curved root shelves, her kit open beside her, though she had not painted a single stroke. [125] Through the high, cracked ceiling she could see a rectangle of early morning sky, thin clouds moving as if they had somewhere important to be. [150]

The demolition crew was already outside. [156] She had heard the low idle of machinery and then nothing, the hush that comes when operators pause for a final check, when the building still stands and the world holds its breath around the fact of what is about to be destroyed. [199]

She had come here to craft one last piece before the walls came down. [213] Her motivation, which she had told no one because it sounded like the kind of thing that gets a person gently redirected toward therapy, was to mirror the sky inside the walls. [245] Not paint it, not depict it, but give the interior some quality of sky, some openness that had no business being indoors. [267] She thought if she got the portrait right, it would do exactly that. [280]

Her hand found the coin before her eyes did. [289] It was wedged inside a false-backed volume on the lowest shelf, a secret book safe disguised as a treatise on cartography. [310] The coin was bronze, centuries old, its face worn to near-nothing. [321] But Mira did not need faces. [327]

She pressed her thumb across it and the faint indentations came alive under her skin: a border of repeating motifs, each one subdividing into smaller copies of itself, smaller still, tinier still, the pattern folding inward with a fractally precise logic that seemed to have no floor. [374] The guild had minted it, she understood somehow, as a test. [385] Not of worth but of attention. [391]

She had always believed her face blindness was simply an absence, a blank wall where other people had a window. [411] But sitting here, in the held breath before the first hydraulic arm swung, she understood that her hands had been reading everything her eyes refused to process. [438] Every subject she had ever painted, she had touched first. [448] She had run her fingers along jawlines under the guise of measuring. [460] She had pressed palms to feel the warmth or tension in a sitter's brow. [474] The portraits were not guesses. [479] They were translations. [482]

The limit she was discovering now was not the edge of her ability. [495] It was the edge of her original assumption about herself. [505]

She began to craft. [509] She used a fine stylus to score the interior wall, not painting the sky but inscribing it, drawing the same recursive pattern she had felt on the coin: circles becoming arcs becoming horizon lines becoming the curve of a cloud seen from too far up to name. [556] The tree trunk library held its shape around her. [565] The root shelves framed the work like arms. [573]

Outside, a radio crackled. [577] A voice said something short and procedural. [584]

Mira pressed her thumb once more against the coin's indentations and felt the whole repeating structure of it, going inward without ever quite arriving at an end. [611] She had always thought her blindness was the wall. [620] Now she understood the wall was what she had been waiting to work on all along. [636]

She tucked the coin into her chest pocket and walked out into the morning. [650] Behind her the building stood for thirty more seconds in the hush, holding the inscribed sky she had given it, before the first sound of machinery broke the stillness open like a book. [683]
