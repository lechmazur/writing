They called Maren a sewer rat, and he accepted this without ceremony. [12] After the plague ended and the survivors retreated to the highland settlements, he had stayed in the tunnel systems beneath the drowned city of Vel, which suited his purposes exactly. [42] He was not, strictly speaking, a philosopher. [49] He was, strictly speaking, a man who lived in sewage and asked questions nobody wanted answered. [65] The distinction mattered to him. [70]

The city of Vel had not sunk because of the plague. [81] That was what the highland preachers taught, and they were wrong, and their wrongness had consequences. [97] Maren had spent three years digging through sedimentary layers in the eastern tunnel walls, pressing his palms into compressed gray silt, reading the city's collapse in its own deposited record. [127] The flooding had come first. [132] The plague had come second, riding the floodwater like a passenger. [143] Sequence mattered. [145] Cause and effect were not interchangeable. [151]

He had told this to two people, a salvager named Doss and a woman whose name he never learned, and both had responded with the incurious hostility of those who already possess a narrative and find additional ones threatening. [190] Fine. [191] He understood the mechanics. [195] Two competing narratives could not both govern the same future, and the one held by people with dry feet and functioning lungs would generally overpower the one held by a man in a sewer. [229] He understood this rationally, and he rejected it anyway. [238]

The chronometer cog had come out of the silt on a Tuesday, or what he counted as a Tuesday. [257] It was a small brass piece, eight-toothed, part of a seafarer's chronometer, the kind the old navigators had used before Vel built its harbor and its pride. [284] It would have measured the precise intervals that kept a ship from losing itself on open water. [301] Someone had carried it into the city, and the city had sunk around it, and now the silt had given it back to Maren. [325]

He turned it in his fingers for a long time, not with wonder, but with attention. [341] The ancient navigators had not trusted the sky alone to tell them where they were. [356] They had built instruments. [360] They had recorded intervals. [364] They had, in short, maintained methods that did not depend on consensus about what the sky meant. [381] This was what Maren wanted to preserve, not the cog itself, but the posture behind it: the insistence on measurement when everyone else was arguing about meaning. [408]

He agonized, in his quiet and abrasive way, over whether anyone would receive this. [422] Not with hand-wringing, not with despair, but with the persistent low-grade friction of a man pushing against a surface that will not give. [445] The highland councils were already drafting their official account of the plague's origin, and it was the theological account, and it would be inscribed and distributed and taught to children who would grow up certain it was true. [483] That account had no room for sedimentary evidence. [491] It had no room for chronometer cogs or sequential causality or the careful distinction between what drowned the city and what killed its people. [515]

Maren pressed the cog into a clay tablet alongside his other findings, the silt samples, the flood-line markings, the dates he had reconstructed. [538] He did not expect rescue. [543] He expected continuity. [546] Someone, eventually, would dig where he had dug, and they would find his record layered inside the sedimentary record, one stratum atop another, and they would understand that the measurement had been made and preserved. [581] Whether they would believe it over the highland narrative was a separate problem, and not one he intended to solve before supper. [603]

He set the cog at the center of the tablet and lit his one good candle. [619] The sewer moved around him, slow and indifferent, as the sunken city held its position beneath the water above. [638] He was rationally rebellious about the whole arrangement, which meant he resented none of it and changed none of his behavior because of the resentment. [663] He simply continued. [666] That, he had decided, was the closest thing to philosophy that the circumstances permitted. [680]
