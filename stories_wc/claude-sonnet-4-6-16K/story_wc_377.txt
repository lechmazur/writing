Sera had been seasonally mute since childhood -- each winter, her voice retreated like a tide, leaving her in silence from the first frost until the last snowmelt. [27] What most considered affliction, she had long since shaped into vocation. [38] She became the finest harmonious bell tuner on the bluff, her fingers knowing what her throat could not say. [57]

The whispering bluff rose above the cloudline, where the sky curved pale and enormous and no one else bothered to climb. [78] Here the air thinned to something almost sacred, and the clouds below moved like slow, indifferent rivers. [95] Sera had built her workshop at the edge of the highest shelf of stone, where the wind arrived from three directions and could be played like an instrument. [123]

The world below had changed. [128] After words became unnecessary -- after the great networks learned to translate thought into signal and signal back into understanding -- people stopped speaking in any meaningful way. [154] Language became a courtesy, then an affectation, then mostly abandoned. [164] But sound remained. [167] Sound, Sera believed, was older than language and would outlast it. [178]

She worked today on the centerpiece of her kinetic sculpture, an assembly of sixty-three bells in descending tiers, suspended from a frame of driftwood and carbon wire. [205] Each bell had been individually tuned to frequencies that bypassed cognition and spoke directly to something beneath thought. [223] The whole structure moved continuously in the wind, arms rotating, pendulums swinging, so that no chord was ever struck the same way twice. [246] The sculpture did not perform truth; it discovered it, differently, each moment. [258]

At the center of the frame, she mounted the iceberg fragment. [269] She had carried it up from a cargo hauler three seasons past, a wedge of ancient compressed ice so dense it had turned the blue of deep ocean. [297] It had survived the climb wrapped in insulating cloth, and now it hung by copper wire among the bells, slowly, imperceptibly diminishing. [319] When the light struck it right, the fragment would glisten with an interior luminosity that no bell could match -- a cold, honest light, like a fact stated plainly. [347] The bells rang around it. [352] The ice answered with silence and shine. [359]

This was the symphonies of truth that Sera had spent eleven years trying to construct. [374] Not a single performance but an ongoing one, accumulating and decaying simultaneously, shaped by weather and season and the diminishment of the ice at its heart. [400] The sculpture would eventually lose its center when the fragment was gone, and the bells would ring differently then, and that too would be true. [425]

She tightened the copper wire on the fragment's mount and stepped back. [437] Her hands ached from the cold. [443] Winter was three weeks old and her silence was total, but she felt no grief in it this year. [462] The muting had its own texture, a pressure behind the sternum that kept her attentive to everything the voice would otherwise drown out. [485]

Her motivation had never been complicated, even if it had taken years to name it clearly. [501] The world was losing its capacity for genuine astonishment. [510] People received experience pre-digested, pre-interpreted, their wonder managed before it could arrive raw. [523] She wanted to preserve the color of wonder -- that particular quality of encountering something before you understood it, before the mind moved to contain it. [548] The kinetic sculpture could not be understood. [555] It could only be experienced, and every experience was unrepeatable. [565]

The wind shifted and the bells spoke in a low, rolling cascade. [577] The iceberg fragment caught the afternoon light and began to glisten, blue-white luminance scattering across the cold stone. [595] Sera did not move. [599] She let the sound fill the space where her voice had been. [611] Above the clouds, on the bluff where nothing was explained, the sculpture continued its patient, vigorous work -- finding truth the only way truth ever allowed itself to be found. [640] One unrepeatable moment at a time. [646]
