The beekeeper had not come back. [7] Three weeks since his last visit, and the hive shelf carved into the grotto wall above the archive stood quiet, the wax cells gone pale and cold. [35]

Maren pressed the emerald-tinted opera glass lens to her eye and studied the curtain of the waterfall from the museum's uppermost gallery. [58] Through its green filter, the falling water looked like hammered copper, beautiful and impenetrable. [72] She had stood in this spot a thousand times. [80] Tonight the mist smelled of rosewater, which meant the cliff blossoms were open, which meant the season was turning. [99] Which meant the outer road, closed all winter under ice, would soon be passable. [113]

She was not sure whether that terrified her or saved her. [124]

Veradis had been built by displaced persons two centuries ago, hollowed from the limestone behind the great falls of the Drev River. [147] They had come from six erased nations, carrying what they could carry, and what they carried, mostly, was memory. [165] The museum Maren curated held that memory: embroidered funeral maps, cracked theater programs, a lute strung with someone's own hair when gut ran out. [190] She was the only formally trained guide the city had ever produced, cultured in the way of a woman who had read every catalog, who quoted restoration theory in conversation and meant it. [221] She was also the last. [226]

Her predecessor had died the winter before the beekeeper stopped coming. [237] The apprentice she had trained left through the waterfall passage in autumn, heading toward the outer cities, toward a life that did not smell of mineral water and old grief. [266] Maren did not blame her. [271] She blamed the slow catastrophe of forgetting that followed displaced people the way cold followed shadow. [286]

The beekeeper, old Piotr, had been the thread. [294] He had crossed the passage monthly, carrying jars of the cliff honey that the grotto families used to glaze their initial breads, a custom so embedded in Veradis that Maren had catalogued it as a primary cultural artifact. [331] Each family baked loaves pressed into the shapes of their first letters, the dough twisted or stamped or coiled according to a lineage method passed down through kitchens across generations, even across borders, even after the borders were abolished by force. [371] An A from the Arescu family looked nothing like an A from the Andros family, and Maren knew every distinction. [389] She had documented them all in the archive's third drawer. [399]

Without Piotr's honey, the breads would change. [406] Without the breads, something specific and unrepeatable would thaw out of living practice and vanish into description, which was the death of it. [431] That was what she was fighting: the moment when a thing became historical instead of actual. [447]

She lowered the lens and pressed her palm to the archive window. [459] The glass was cold. [463]

She had sent a letter with the last trader who passed through, addressed to the cultural ministry of the nearest recognized state, explaining what Veradis held, explaining what Veradis needed. [491] She had written with precision and without pleading, because she was historically oriented enough to know that pleading rarely survived translation. [511] The letter asked for a liaison, a supply line, an acknowledgment that the living arts of two hundred years of displaced persons constituted something worth a bureaucrat's morning. [539]

She did not expect an answer. [545] She sent the letter because sending it was the record of having tried. [558]

From somewhere below, in the narrow residences that terraced down toward the grotto floor, she heard the knock of a bread board. [580] It was late for baking. [585] Someone was working by lamplight, pressing the dough into the shape of their name. [598] The sound rose through the stone like a slow pulse. [609]

Maren held the emerald lens up one last time and watched the waterfall burn green in the dark. [627] Whatever came through it next would find the archive intact, the catalog complete, the breads still shaped by hands that remembered what the hands were for. [655] She would make certain of that. [661] The rosewater in the mist thickened, impossibly sweet, impossibly close to grief. [674] She breathed it in and stayed. [680]
