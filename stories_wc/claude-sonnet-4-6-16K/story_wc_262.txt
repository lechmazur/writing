Vera pressed the film strip between her palms and closed her eyes. [12] The projection booth smelled of machine oil and decades of burnt carbon arc, the air thick with the residue of ten thousand projected dreams. [36] She had worked stranger rooms before, but none that hummed quite like this one, as though the walls themselves were holding their breath. [60]

She was a crystal gazer by trade, though she rarely used the crystal anymore. [74] Objects told her more. [78] Tonight she was practicing psychometry, letting her fingertips drink whatever the film strip had absorbed across its long, forgotten life. [100]

Below, in the empty theater, a pianist was playing without sheet music, his left hand chasing a melody his right hand could not quite remember. [126] The tune kept dissolving before it resolved, forgotten lyrics searching for the notes that once carried them, the whole effort hovering somewhere between music and mourning. [153] Vera let the sound rise through the floorboards and into her feet like a slow current. [169]

The film strip grew warm. [174]

She was spatially imaginative in the way certain people are born: she could enter a room described to her in words and immediately sense where the weight of grief would pool, where joy would press against the ceiling, where love would collect in corners like blown snow. [223] What she felt now, holding the strip, was a room she had never physically entered but recognized instantly. [240] A small apartment. [243] A desk facing a window. [248] Envelopes, sealed, stacked, addressed and never mailed. [255]

Goodbye letters. [257]

Not one or two. [261] Dozens. [262] She counted them in her mind the way you count shadows, not precisely, but by weight. [281] Each letter had been written to someone different, and each letter said the same essential thing: I loved you and could not say so, and now I am going somewhere you cannot follow. [315] But the man who wrote them had not gone anywhere at all. [327] He had stayed, and the letters had stayed with him, and the love inside them had never been delivered, never been spent, never been returned. [353]

It had only incubated. [357]

That was the word that surfaced in Vera's mind as she deepened the reading, pressing the strip harder between her palms. [378] The love had not died or faded. [385] It had incubated in the dark of the sealed envelopes the way a photograph incubates in chemical solution, present but unformed, waiting for the light that would fix it into something real. [418] And when the man died, the love had passed from the letters into everything his hands had touched, including this film strip, which he had threaded through a projector hundreds of times in this very booth, this quiet cedar-scented room above the velvet dark. [462]

She understood now why she had been called here, though no one had called her in any ordinary sense. [481] She had simply felt the pull of a question she was built to answer: where is unused love stored. [500] The answer, she was learning, was everywhere the man had pressed his hands. [513] It was in the projector housing, in the worn brass fittings, in the cedar panels, in this strip of celluloid she now held between her own. [538]

The pianist below struck a chord and held it. [548] It was not the lost tune, but it was close enough that the room seemed to lean toward it. [566]

Vera opened her eyes. [570] The booth was lit only by the pilot light of the old projector, and it cast everything in amber, the color of resin, the color of things preserved. [599] She set the film strip gently on the felt-lined tray beside the machine and smoothed it flat with two fingers. [619] She would not take it with her. [626] She would not write it up in the small notebook she carried. [638] Some discoveries are not reports. [643] Some answers are not meant to be carried out of the room where they were found. [659]

She stood in the hushed thunder of all that stored and unspent feeling and let it move through her and past her like weather. [683] Then she turned off the pilot light and walked downstairs to listen to the pianist try, one more time, to find his way home. [707]
