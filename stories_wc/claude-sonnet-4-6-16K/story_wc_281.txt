Mara didn't believe in the pills. [6] She had said so, cheerfully, to everyone who asked -- to her sister, to the support group, to the technician at the recall center who handed her the return envelope with that apologetic tilt of his head. [42] She was an affectionate skeptic by nature, the kind of person who held your hand while explaining why your aura wasn't real. [64] The Connex telepathy pills had been pulled from market two months ago after the neural-coupling trials showed something nobody could cleanly explain -- not transmission exactly, more like resonance, like two struck bells in separate rooms. [99] She had taken them anyway, briefly, out of curiosity she dressed as due diligence. [113] And she had felt something. [118] She would not say what. [123]

Now she worked late in her light painting studio, a long black room where time opened its mouth and held still. [144] The walls absorbed everything. [148] Only the light existed here, drawn by hand, recorded in exposure lengths that turned motion into architecture. [165]

The muscovite geode sliver sat on her worktable beside the camera -- a thin flake of layered mineral her sister had mailed with a note: for when you're ready to look inward. [196] Mara had rolled her eyes and kept it because she loved her sister, not because she believed the thing did anything. [217] But it caught the light in fractally precise formations, each reflection nesting inside another like a theorem proving itself, and she kept it in her sightline while she worked. [246]

She was rigging a new exposure when the static shock came -- her hand on the lighting rig, a dry snap, a white flare behind her eyes. [272] After the static shock, something unlocked. [278]

Not memory exactly. [281] More like the ghost of the pill's effect, surfacing through the body rather than the mind. [297] Her chest tightened in a particular way. [304] Her left hand knew something her brain hadn't named yet. [314] These were somatic markers -- physical residues the Connex researchers had documented but never fully mapped, the body's way of holding what the conscious mind dismissed. [339]

She understood, suddenly, what she needed to devise. [347]

Not a replacement for the pills. [353] Something better -- a method to illuminate hidden pathways using what the body already remembered. [367] Through somatic markers, she could reconstruct the map. [375] The pills had opened a door; the body had memorized the shape of the frame. [390]

She picked up the muscovite sliver and held it against the dark. [402] Under the long exposure, light fractured through its layers and hit the back wall in branching patterns -- precise, recursive, a diagram of something almost biological. [427] She moved it slowly, deliberately. [432] The camera recorded seven minutes of motion as a single frozen image: a corridor of light, forking and forking again, each branch as detailed as the last. [459]

She had been trying to paint abstraction. [466] Instead she had painted a nervous system. [473]

She sat down on the studio floor with her back against the cold wall and let herself feel the markers one by one -- the pressure behind her sternum that meant proximity, the heat in her fingertips that meant attention directed outward, the low hum in her jaw that the research group had never named and she had privately called listening. [533] She wrote them down in a notebook, not as feelings but as coordinates. [546] Then she cross-referenced them against the light painting, which was fractally precise in the same way the somatic map was -- neither random nor rigid, branching according to a logic that could be navigated if you trusted the branches. [584]

Her skepticism remained intact. [588] She did not believe this was telepathy. [595] She believed it was something quieter and more durable: the nervous system's own architecture, briefly illuminated by a compound that was now recalled, now gone, but had left its fingerprints on everything it touched. [629]

She picked up a new light source -- a small tungsten wand, warm as a held hand -- and began again. [648] The willowglass hope she felt was not the fragile kind, not the kind that shatters when you look at it too directly. [670] It was the kind that bends, that passes light through, that survives being held up to the dark and examined. [690] Mara did not believe in the impossible. [697] She believed in the unfinished -- in pathways that existed before anyone thought to light them. [712]

She moved the wand through the dark and drew what she now knew how to find. [728]
