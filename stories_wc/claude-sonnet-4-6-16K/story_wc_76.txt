The lighthouse had no keeper, and that suited Maren perfectly. [10] She had rowed out at dusk, when the tide began its slow exhale and the exposed rocks rose like the backs of drowned things. [34] She was a clockmaker by trade, though the harbor town knew her chiefly for one unsettling habit: every clock she built ran counterclockwise. [57] She did not design them that way to unsettle anyone. [67] She designed them that way because she believed that time, read forward, was fate's preferred handcuff. [83]

The hunger stone waited at the base of the lighthouse stairs. [94] She had read of such stones — boulders carved with dates of ancient drought, exposed only when rivers fell to their absolute lowest. [116] This one had been carried from the shore and left inside, deliberately, by someone whose name no record preserved. [135] Its face was etched with markings resembling gears, or perhaps a warning, or perhaps both. [150] She pressed her palm against it and felt what she could only call latent radiance: not light itself, but the held breath just before light commits to existing. [178]

She climbed the spiral stairs with the stone in her pack and the island's cold air pressing through every crack. [198] At the top, where the great lens should have rotated, there was nothing but a workbench and a single object left in a chalk circle: an ancient yoyo, carved from a wood so dark it seemed to absorb the dim glow from outside. [241] She had been told to expect it. [248] She had not been told how long she would stand staring at it before she trusted herself to pick it up. [269]

Maren was neatly inventive by nature. [275] She could look at a broken mechanism and intuit not just its failure but its original dream. [292] The yoyo's axle was wrapped in a cord of braided silk, wound in a direction that suggested — if you followed its logic outward — a spiral that should have collapsed but didn't. [323] She unwound it slowly. [327] She studied the internal weight distribution, the groove ratios, the way the two halves were joined with a pin that turned left to tighten rather than right. [354] Through reverse-engineering the puzzle of the ancient yoyo, she began to understand: this was a key, not a toy. [373] Someone with precisely her kind of mind had built it centuries ago to initiate something that required the hunger stone to complete. [395]

She set the stone in the chalk circle. [403] She pressed the yoyo's axle against the carving at the stone's center and began to wind the cord back in the direction the grooves demanded: counterclockwise. [429] The stone did not glow. [434] It did something quieter and stranger. [440] It seemed to recall light it had once swallowed during years of killing drought, and it released that memory slowly, like a long-held breath let go under low tide. [469]

The lighthouse lens — absent, rusted, supposedly lost — assembled itself from nothing in the housing above her. [485] Not from magic. [488] From correction. [490] The world had been missing it, and now the world was repairing the error. [504]

Maren stood in the risen beam and felt bleak exhilaration crack her open at the sternum. [520] This was what stepping beyond fate's design actually felt like: not triumph, but vertigo with no bottom. [537] She had spent her whole life building clocks that ran backward to argue with inevitability, and here, in a lighthouse that should have stayed dark forever, she had finally initiated something fate had not planned for her. [574]

The tide continued its exhale below. [580] The rocks grew longer in the receding water. [588] The beam swept out across the sea and made the dark take note. [601]

She sat down on the floor beside the hunger stone and laughed, once, sharply, the way a person laughs when they realize the danger isn't behind them — it's become them. [631]

She would row back in the morning. [638] She would build a new clock, and this one would not run counterclockwise. [651] It would not run forward either. [657] It would simply hold still, because she had finally earned a moment worth keeping. [671]

The beam turned. [674] The hunger stone went quiet. [679] The island breathed. [682]
