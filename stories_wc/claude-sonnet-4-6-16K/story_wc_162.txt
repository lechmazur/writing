Before the butterfly effect unraveled her father's life, Mira had believed that corn mazes were simply geometry. [17] Now, standing on the drifting prism terrace at the top of the old survey building, she understood they were something closer to argument. [40] The terrace walls were glass panels, old and warped, and when the afternoon light passed through them it broke apart and drifted across the floorboards in slow, colored bands. [69] She had been coming here for three weeks, ever since the accident, ever since her father was gone. [87]

He had designed mazes for thirty years. [94] Mira herself had been lost in one when she was seven, wandering its green corridors for two hours before her father found her at the center. [120] Clients hired him to cut confusion into cornfields, to build living walls that forced people to pause, turn, reconsider. [139] What no one understood, not even Mira until now, was that her father had been working out a private theory. [159] The mazes were not puzzles to be solved. [167] They were arguments for necessary distances -- the idea that some separations between people are not failures but architecture. [185]

Mira set his pilot logbook on the wide oak table at the terrace's center. [199] It had belonged to her grandfather, a crop duster who had recorded each day's weather in shorthand codes no one had fully deciphered. [222] She had found it in her father's studio two days after his disappearance, open to a page where the weather codes were underlined in red. [247] She did not yet know what he had meant by underlining them. [259] She only knew she needed to intuit it before the authorities stopped looking. [272]

She had been featherly determined since the morning they told her he was a missing person -- light in her movements, careful with her breath, as though grief were a structure she could navigate if she stepped correctly. [309] The terrace helped. [312] The drifting prism light did not demand anything of her. [322] It simply moved, and she moved with it. [330]

She sat at the oak table and pressed her palms flat against its surface. [344] This was her method: analyzing the grain in wooden furniture, the way her father had taught her to read corn rows. [365] Wood grain carried the memory of slow seasons, of wet years and dry ones, of the exact angle a tree had tilted toward surviving light. [390] Her father had said that if you could learn to read grain, you could learn to read almost anything. [409] The oak table's grain ran toward the eastern window in long, patient lines. [422]

She opened the logbook. [426] The weather codes were a private language: H3 meant a high-pressure system moving northeast, T7 meant thermal instability. [444] But her grandfather had made his own additions over the years -- small notations beside the standard codes. [461] Beside T7, in a younger hand, her father had written: the threshold point. [474]

Mira stared at the phrase until the prism light shifted and lay orange across the page. [490] She had always felt it -- the way certain doorways carried more weight than others. [504] The entrance to her father's studio. [510] The exact point where the corn maze's outer wall gave way to its first corridor. [525] She had spent her childhood trying to discover why some thresholds feel sacred, why crossing them changed the pressure in her chest. [547] And here, in two words written thirty years ago, was her father's same question. [561]

She understood then what he had underlined. [568] The butterfly effect that dismantled his life had not begun with any single disaster. [582] It had begun with a threshold he had crossed without recognizing its weight -- a commitment he had made that required the impossible collapse of all necessary distances. [609] He had brought someone too close. [615] He had tried to solve the maze instead of living inside it. [627]

Mira closed the logbook gently. [632] Outside, a wind lifted off the fields and pressed against the warped glass, and the prism colors scattered and drifted and settled again into their patient argument. [659] She would leave the logbook here, on the oak table, in the light. [672] She would go back down to the fields. [680] She would walk the entrance of his last unfinished maze and stand at that first threshold until she felt its weight, and then she would cross it on her own terms. [711] The corn rows would be the grain. [718] She would read them. [722] She would find him, or she would find what he had left behind -- some encoded message in the architecture of necessary distance, in the fractured hush of a maze that had not yet been walked. [757]
