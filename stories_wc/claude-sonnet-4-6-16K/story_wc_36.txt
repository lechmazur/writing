Mara had kept many things whole. [6] She had stood vigil over bone altars and bronze seals, over urns that held the first breath of drowned cities, over objects so old they had forgotten what they were for. [37] But the guardianship was finished now, ended not by ceremony but by a single afternoon's inattention, and she carried that fact into the Thunder-Echo Range the way stone carries a crack — quietly, completely. [71]

The canyon opened before her like a wound held in suspension. [82] Its walls were the color of dried blood and ash, striated by ten thousand years of water that no longer ran. [103] Sound arrived differently here. [107] A bootfall produced no immediate response, and then the range answered — low, layered, avalanchingly quiet in the way that only vast spaces can be, silence multiplying silence until it had the density of a held breath. [143]

She moved by instinct until instinct failed, which was sooner than she expected. [156] The canyon braided into three passages, each identical in width, each exhaling the same mineral cold. [172] She had anticipated this. [176] From the pack at her hip she drew the driftwood she had collected at the river mouth two days prior — pale, salt-bleached lengths of cottonwood and willow, worn to the shape of old argument. [211] She arranged them on the gravel floor: the longest piece pointing toward the rightmost fork, a crooked elbow of branch blocking the left, and a flat splinter laid across the center passage like a closed door. [247] Arranging driftwood as directional markers had been a method she once dismissed as primitive, beneath a keeper of her discipline. [267] She no longer dismissed things on principle. [274] That, too, was something failure had given her. [282]

The rightmost passage narrowed and then opened without warning into a chamber the size of a ruined theater. [300] The floor was smooth, polished by ancient floods, and the ceiling was lost in dark. [315] Here the echo range fulfilled its name: her footsteps returned to her from every angle, overlapping and misaligned, so that she seemed to be a crowd of one. [343]

She set her pack down and removed from it the long leather case she had carried since the beginning of this — since before the guardianship, if she was honest, which she was trying to be. [378] She had never been able to uncase the flute in the presence of others. [392] To uncase it was an admission, a declaration that the world she stood in was not the only one pressing against her. [414] Here, alone in the canyon's held silence, she unlatched the brass clip, lifted the lid, and looked at the gilded reed flute lying in its channel of worn velvet. [443]

It was older than her order. [449] Its surface caught no light because there was no light to catch, and yet it glowed — the gold inlaid along the reed's seams burning with some residual warmth, as if it had just been set down by whoever had made it. [490]

She lifted it and played. [495]

The note she produced was low and impure, bent by the cold and by her own trembling lip. [513] But the chamber heard it as something else. [521] The echo that returned was not the note she had played — it was the note beneath the note, the harmonic the canyon had been holding in reserve. [548] The walls began their work. [553]

Weaving between worlds was not, she had learned, a matter of crossing. [565] It was a matter of listening until the boundary became permeable, until the world behind this one grew close enough to press its forehead against the present moment's glass. [594] She heard the relic she had lost — not its location, but its history, the long chain of keepers before her who had also, in their own ways, let go. [623] This was what she had come for. [630] Not absolution. [632] Not restoration. [634] To harvest insights from failure, you had to go where failure accumulated without judgment, where it settled like silt, where it could be read. [658]

She played until her breath gave out. [665] The canyon answered for a long time after. [673] When the last echo finally dissolved, she was still there, sitting on the smooth stone floor with the gilded reed flute across her knees, understanding, for the first time, the difference between losing something and releasing it. [710] That difference, she thought, was worth the walk. [718]
