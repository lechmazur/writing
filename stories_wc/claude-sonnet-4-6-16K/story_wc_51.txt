Maret came to the bronze grotto only after the first snowmelt, when the meltwater thinned the mineral seams in the stone and the walls ran copper and amber beneath starlight, as though the earth itself had been lit from within. [39] She carried a kerosene lamp burner whose wick she kept trimmed to the width of a thumbnail, not for illumination but for orientation, because in the shingled hush of the grotto — that layered, overlapping silence pressing in from every direction at once — light without purpose was a cruelty she refused to commit. [92] She was a fractal weaver. [97] The townspeople above, still pressing boot-prints through the softening snow, used that word loosely, imagining she worked loom and thread, but Maret worked something less visible and more necessary. [126] She wove the weights of different burdens together into patterns that could be held — grief folded into the shape of longing, longing twisted into the structure of guilt, guilt stretched into the long geometry of regret — each element echoing the whole at every scale, the way a branch echoes a tree echoes a forest echoes the idea of growth itself. [186] This was her motivation and her torment both: to collect these weights before they calcified, before they hardened past all recovery into something neither she nor the person who carried them could ever soften again. [221]

The grotto cooperated only for a window of days after the first snowmelt. [234] The meltwater seeped through limestone fractures above, carrying down dissolved burdens that the dead had pressed into stone over centuries — or so Maret had long believed, and long believing had become indistinguishable from knowing. [268] By orienting properly within the grotto, aligning her spine to the faint magnetic declination she had measured across a decade of descents, she could feel which walls were ready to yield, which mineral skins were thin enough that the weight inside could still be drawn free. [314] A wall calcified too long became useless. [321] The burden inside fused with the stone and was lost to both the living and the dead, neither carried nor released, simply preserved in the amber dark like a fly in resin. [353]

She held the kerosene lamp burner low and moved along the eastern face, where the bronze patina ran deepest. [372] Tonight the starlight entered through a vertical crack she had never measured wider than two fingers, yet it fell across the grotto floor in a stripe as deliberate as a ruled margin, and she worked within it. [409] The shingled hush around her was absolute — not empty silence but accumulated silence, the way shingles overlap so that no rain finds the seam, so that no sound from above penetrated down to where she stood. [445] She had been working in this grotto for eleven years. [455] She had not, in those eleven years, found a reliable way to leave it entirely. [470]

That was the quality of her existence she found hardest to name. [482] She was boundary-lessly trapped — not confined by walls or locked doors or any edge she could press her hand against — but caught instead in the logic of the work itself, which required her return each thaw, each descent, each careful reorientation of her body in the dark. [529] There was no border she could cross to be free of it, because the trap had no border. [547] It was patterned into her the way her own patterns were patterned into the weights she collected. [564] She had ceased to mourn this and arrived instead at something she could only describe in the register of old bone — slow, dense, enormous, unafraid. [589] A whalebone reverie, her mother would have called it, meaning the kind of peace that only comes when you have accepted the full weight of your depth. [616]

She pressed her left hand flat against the eastern wall and felt the give she had been waiting for. [635] Something beneath the mineral skin shifted — not crumbled, but yielded — and she began to weave it free with her fingers, drawing the burden out in slow fractal loops, each loop smaller than the last, until it was small enough to hold. [676] It was heavy in the way that snow is heavy after it has been on a roof for weeks, pressed and compacted into something far denser than it first appeared. [706] She placed it carefully into the cloth pouch at her hip and stood in the starlit stripe and breathed. [725] Around her, the grotto held its layered silence, shingle over shingle over shingle, and the lamp burned on, perfectly oriented, perfectly still. [747]
