After the last human dream was dreamt, the world did not end. [12] It simply stopped echoing. [16]

Maren Voss noticed the difference before anyone else did, which was not surprising. [29] She was the kind of person who felt temperature changes in stone, who heard grief in the particular way a door settled on its hinges. [54] Those who found her unsettling called her odd. [62] Those who understood her called her the historian. [70] She called herself neither, only patient. [76]

The reclaimed silo library stood at the edge of what had been farmland, its cylindrical walls now lined floor to ceiling with warped shelves and salvaged books. [103] The original steel had been left exposed in places, riveted and rust-bloomed, and when afternoon light came through the high clerestory windows it fell bronze and slow across everything, as though the air itself were cooling from some ancient pour. [143] Maren had chosen it for exactly this quality. [151] A building that had once held grain now held everything that grain could not: the accumulated, imperfect record of human interior life. [173]

She was there the morning she found the fireplace had been used. [185]

Someone had burned things in the low iron grate set into the silo's foundation, the one that predated the library conversion by decades. [208] She crouched and probed the ashes with two careful fingers, and what she pulled free were diaries, or what remained of them: half-burned, their covers gone, their spines charred to powder, but their inner pages still legible in patches, the ink preserved by the very fire that had tried to erase them. [260] She laid them on a worktable without brushing the ash away. [271] She understood instinctively that context was part of the record. [281]

The diaries belonged to a man named Oleander Peck, which she knew because he had written his name inside every cover, a habit she found both vain and poignant. [310] He had been a cartographer in the years before the dreaming stopped, and his entries described not maps of land but maps of sensation: the exact weight of certain silences, the color of his fear, the geometry of longing. [349] Reading him was like watching someone navigate by stars that no one else could see. [364]

Between two pages she found the fragment. [371]

It was roughly the size of her palm, cut or torn from something much larger, the threads medieval in their tight weave, still holding the image clearly: two dragons, one ascending, one curling earthward, arranged around a central point that no longer existed on the cloth. [417] The axis of the image had been displaced by the cut itself, both creatures now orbiting a void, a center implied but absent. [440] She held it to the bronze light coming through the windows and understood that Oleander had understood something. [458]

She wrote in her own notes: The axis is always displaced after the fact. [472] Memory is not a map. [477] It is the torn edge of one. [484]

This was why she had come here, why she had spent the three years since the last dream cataloguing not events but resonances. [507] The walls of the silo had absorbed a century of breath and argument and silence. [522] She was convinced that buildings held narrative the way wood held moisture, structurally, invisibly, releasable only under the right conditions. [542] Her work was to create those conditions. [549]

She spent the afternoon with Oleander's diaries, reading each surviving passage slowly, then pressing her palm flat against the silo's inner wall as though completing a circuit. [576] She was sensitively perceptive enough to know this looked strange. [586] She did it anyway. [590]

By evening she had transcribed everything legible, and she pinned the tapestry fragment above her desk beside a note that read: Center missing. [613] Both still true. [616]

Outside, the world went on without dreams, quieter for it, the silence no longer temporary but structural. [633] Inside the silo, Maren Voss kept working, certain of only one thing: that the stories walls had heard were the last ones left worth finding, and that finding them was no less urgent for being, probably, impossible. [670]

She turned a page. [674]

The ash on her fingers left a print. [682]

She let it stay. [686]
