Vera called herself a mystic, though she never called herself that aloud. [12] The word was simultaneously too grand and too small for what she actually did. [26] She watched. [28] She listened. [30] She stood in places where most people felt nothing and felt everything. [42]

The quartz arbor waited at the edge of the park, pale columns rising into November mist like the ribs of something ancient that had never quite died. [69] Vera arrived before sunrise, her breath preceding her in small clouds, her boots finding the frozen gravel with the certainty of someone firmly grounded in the physical world even while her attention lived elsewhere. [103]

She carried the glass prism in her coat pocket, wrapped in a cloth she hadn't washed since her mother held it. [124] That was the point, really - the whole reason she had come. [135] She needed to prove what she had spent eleven years insisting was true: that objects absorb emotions the way wood absorbs water, slowly, completely, permanently. [160] The scientific community had not been kind to this idea. [170] Her colleagues had been kinder, which was worse, because their kindness meant they had already given up on her. [189]

A car sat along the curb just outside the arbor's entrance, abandoned the night before, its windshield feathered with frost in elaborate crystalline highways and dead ends. [216] Vera stopped and pressed her fingertips to the cold glass without touching it, hovering just above the surface, and began to commune with the patterns the way others might read a letter written in a language they had always secretly spoken. [257]

The frost told a quiet story. [263] Someone had cried in that car, recently, with the engine off. [274] The grief had pressed itself outward and the cold had caught it, recorded it in ice. [290] This was the truthful lie at the center of Vera's life's work: she could not prove any of it, but she was not wrong. [314]

She unwrapped the prism. [318]

The arbor's mist shifted as she stepped beneath the quartz columns, the light doing something impossible and ordinary at once, bending through her prism into a spectrum that fell across the frost-patterns still vivid in her memory. [355] The colors landed on nothing, suspended in the wet air, and for a moment the whole space entered what Vera recognized as a shifted hush - not silence exactly, but a quality of attention that the world itself seemed to hold when something true was about to be misunderstood. [403]

She had come to prove her theory. [410] She had brought instruments, a notebook, her prism, the cloth with her mother's grief pressed into its fibers. [428] She had her method and her motive and eleven years of accumulated certainty. [441]

What she had not prepared for was the moment when assumptions shatter - not her critics' assumptions, but her own. [460]

The prism's light caught the mist and inside it, for just three seconds, Vera saw the spectrum differently. [478] Not as proof. [481] Not as demonstration. [484] As conversation. [486] The objects had not been absorbing emotions like passive sponges. [496] They had been speaking all along, and she had been translating, and the translation had always been accurate, and that meant the emotions were not stored in things at all. [526] They were transmitted through them, the way light travels through glass without belonging to it. [541]

The prism was a messenger, not a memory. [549]

She stood firmly grounded in the frozen gravel, breathing, understanding that everything she had claimed was both wrong and more right than she had known. [574] She had told truthful lies for eleven years. [582] The lie: objects hold what we feel. [589] The truth beneath the lie: nothing holds it, everything carries it, and nothing is ever truly lost. [606]

The mist pressed in around the quartz columns, patient and indifferent. [617] Vera wrapped the prism back in her mother's cloth, put it in her pocket, and stood very still inside the shifted hush until she was ready to begin again. [646] She was always ready to begin again. [653] That was the truest thing about her, and the most useful. [664]
