The fog came in without apology, swallowing the bay until the old carnival ship sat suspended between water and sky like a thought no one had finished thinking. [28] Mara had found the vessel three days ago, or the vessel had found her. [42] She was not entirely sure which version was true, and that uncertainty had come to feel like its own kind of home. [64] She was a chimera twin discoverer by profession -- a specialist who traced the biological signatures of absorbed twins, those ghost selves living inside the DNA of a single body -- but she had not taken a commission in eight months. [103] The last case had cracked something open in her, some quiet interior vault she had not known existed until it was empty. [125]

The ship still wore its carnival colors: faded red stripes across the hull, iron carousel horses bolted to the upper deck with their mouths forever open in frozen delight. [154] Ticket booths stood like hollow confessionals. [160] The whole vessel drifted on the tide's slow logic, turning fractionally, offering her new angles of fog every hour, as if the bay itself were narrating something. [187]

In her coat pocket was the emerald swatch card, a small rectangle of layered fabric samples in graduated shades of green. [208] She had found it pinned to the door of the ship's fortune-teller booth, and she could not stop returning it to her fingers. [231] The card felt like a cipher, like someone had left it specifically for her -- a coordinate system built from color rather than number. [254] Each swatch, from pale celadon to deep malachite, corresponded to something she had not yet fully named. [271]

She was here because of the garden of forgotten names. [281] Every chimera case she had ever worked eventually surfaced the same myth: that somewhere there existed a place where absorbed identities went -- not dead, not alive, but archived. [309] Her colleagues dismissed it as romantic nonsense, the kind of story grieving families told themselves. [324] But Mara had begun to believe that the myth was a map, and that the only way to read it was through narrative threads: following the stories people told about loss, pulling each one until they led somewhere real. [363]

As memories alter, the path changes. [369] She had learned this. [373] The chimera subjects she had interviewed each described their absorbed twin differently as years passed -- sometimes as a shadow, sometimes as a warmth behind the sternum, sometimes as a color they kept seeing in unexpected places. [409] Always a shade of green. [414]

She held the emerald swatch card up to the fog and felt something she could only call blissfully aberrant: a joy that had no right to exist here, no logical grounding, yet sat fully and warmly in her chest like a coal that refused to cool. [460] It was unkindled solace -- comfort that had never been lit by any particular event, that simply was, the way tidal water simply is. [483]

The ship groaned and pivoted. [488] Through the fog, a shape appeared at the waterline ahead -- not land exactly, but a density. [504] A darkening. [506] Something organized. [508]

She moved to the bow and gripped the railing, which was cold and flaked with rust beneath her palms. [527] The carousel horses watched her from behind with their open mouths. [538] She had the wild sensation of being both prisoner and explorer, of having traveled enormous distance while standing completely still. [558] Trapped freedom, she thought -- the exact phrase she had never found before but had always needed. [574]

The garden appeared slowly, the way important things always do. [584] It was real: a tangle of growth on a low spit of reclaimed land, impossible in the middle of a bay, lit from within by bioluminescence she could not explain. [614] Stone markers rose from the undergrowth, each one blank on its face. [626]

She understood then. [629] The names were not carved into the stones. [637] They were in the chimera subjects themselves, held in the second strand of every DNA helix she had ever mapped. [657] She had been trying to recapture something she already carried. [667]

Mara stepped off the ship's lowered ramp and walked into the garden with the emerald swatch card pressed against her heart, its colors finally making complete and terrible sense. [696] The fog stayed close, but now it felt less like erasure and more like witness. [711] She knelt at the nearest stone and pressed her palm to its cool blank face and said the first name she had ever found inside someone else's blood, the name she had never spoken aloud, and let it go. [750] Around her, the garden exhaled. [755]
