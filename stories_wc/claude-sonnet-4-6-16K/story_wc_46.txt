Maren had been watching stars long enough to know the difference between a sky that gave and one that withheld. [20]

Tonight, the mist had taken everything. [26]

She descended into the serpentine grotto the way a breath releases -- slowly, without ceremony, her lantern left at the ridge because she had promised herself she would find another way. [56]

The grotto coiled underground like the body of something ancient, its walls slick and green-veined, catching the faint luminescence of wet stone. [78]

Maren was an earthbound star watcher -- not an astronomer with equipment and grants, but a woman who had simply refused to stop looking up, and who, in looking up long enough, had learned to look inward. [114]

She carried a pouch at her hip, and inside it, a collection of sea glass gathered across seventeen years of coastal mornings. [136]

Each piece had been worn by salt and current until it surrendered its edges. [150]

She had spent the last months polishing them further -- the slow, meditative work of gem polishing, where the cloth moves in circles so small they seem like stillness, and the surface gradually becomes something that can hold light rather than simply receive it. [193]

The grotto twisted ahead of her, its path never straight, always curving as though the earth itself was reluctant to commit to a direction. [217]

Maren moved by feel, one hand trailing the cool wall, the other cradling the pouch. [232]

She had not brought fire. [237]

That was the point. [241]

Years ago, she had watched a child burn her fingers reaching for a candle Maren held to illuminate a tide pool, and the image had lodged itself somewhere deep and immovable -- she wanted to share light, yes, but not like that. [282]

Not with something that could take as easily as it gave. [293]

As memories resurface, they do not arrive gently -- they press up through the floor of the mind like water through sand, slow then sudden. [317]

No flame. [343]

Just the warmth of a body, the ghost-marks on glass, and the stars waiting behind them. [359]

Maren stopped at a widening in the grotto where the mist pooled thickest, breathing in its mineral coolness. [377]

She reached into the pouch and removed a piece of sea glass -- the oldest, a deep aquamarine worn to something reflectively vibrant, the kind of surface that did not produce light but organized it, gathered scattered photons into coherence the way a quiet mind gathers scattered thought. [424]

She held it up. [428]

Even here, even in the mist-dark of the serpentine grotto, something reached the glass -- the faint phosphorescence of the stone walls, the ambient silver that mist carries when a sky exists somewhere above it. [462]

The glass caught all of it and gave it back, softened, shaped. [474]

Maren closed her fingers around it and felt what she could only describe as a pearl of stillness -- not silence exactly, but a kind of interior quiet that had weight, that was substantive, that you could rest inside without sinking. [514]

She had learned to weigh such moments carefully, the way an assayer weighs ore, holding the sensation in the balance of her attention long enough to know what it was worth before it passed. [548]

This was worth everything. [552]

She set the aquamarine piece on a narrow ledge of stone, then another beside it, then another, each one polished to hold and return whatever softness the dark offered. [581]

The grotto bloomed. [584]

That was the only word for it -- a zephyr bloom of cool, diffuse light moving through the mist in the way a slow exhale moves through cold air, visible, present, gentle beyond argument. [617]

No fire. [619]

Just glass that had been worn to honesty, and light that had nowhere else to be. [635]

Maren sat down on the stone floor and looked at what she had made, and felt for the first time in years that she had given something to the darkness without asking the darkness to become something else. [673]

The stars, she knew, were doing the same thing above her -- buried in cloud tonight, their light diffusing through layer after layer of atmosphere and water until it arrived here, transformed, carried by glass into a grotto where a woman sat breathing and still. [717]

She was earthbound, yes. [721]

But she was never separate from them. [728]
