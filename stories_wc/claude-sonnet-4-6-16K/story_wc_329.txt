The sky-bridge swayed under Maren's feet as she crossed into the orchard just before dawn, her violin case strapped to her back like a folded wing. [26] The bridge was woven from petrified vines stretched between two cliff faces, and below it the enchanted orchard spread in silver rows, each apple tree glowing faintly, as though lit from within by a memory of sunlight. [63] She had heard what the orchard had become. [71] She had not yet understood it. [77]

That gap -- between knowing and understanding -- was where she now lived, suspended above the ruined beauty like a note held too long. [99]

The living statues moved between the rows. [106] They were carved from pale granite, shaped like wardens in long coats, and they turned with the slow precision of clock hands, scanning for anything that did not belong to the new order. [139] The orchard had been enchanted centuries ago to produce fruit that made people weep with gratitude. [155] Now it was managed, measured, harvested on a schedule. [164] The fruit came out uniform and tasteless. [171] Beauty, someone had decided, was inefficient. [177]

Maren climbed down from the bridge using a knotted rope she had anchored the night before. [193] Her boots touched the soil without sound. [200]

She found the locust shell almost immediately, clinging to the bark of the nearest tree at eye level, translucent and amber-colored, split perfectly down the spine where the creature had pulled itself free. [233] She pressed it between her fingers. [239] It was hollow, exact, the shed architecture of something that had decided to become more than it was. [257] She tucked it into her breast pocket, against her sternum, where she could feel her own heartbeat press against it. [277]

The statues had no ears, but they felt vibration. [286] She knew this. [289] Any string she played above a whisper would bring them converging in minutes. [302] So she had practiced a different kind of playing in the months before she came here -- resonantly soft, a technique that sent the music inward rather than outward, so that the sound lived in wood and soil and bone before it ever reached air. [346]

She set her violin beneath her chin and began. [355]

The bow moved at half the pressure she would normally use. [366] The notes were barely audible even to her own ears, but she felt them travel down through her arms and chest, and she began signaling through her heart rhythms, slowing her pulse deliberately, letting the beat fall into a pattern she had found in the oldest notation she could read -- the rhythm the orchard's first gardener had used to coax the trees into bloom. [430] The locust shell against her sternum vibrated faintly in response. [440]

The nearest apple tree shuddered. [445] It was not the wind. [450] The roots, long compacted under the warden-statues' patrol paths, began to flex and lift, as though the tree were remembering how to breathe. [473] To uproot itself -- not to flee, but to reposition, to reclaim the inches of ground it had been pressed away from over decades of managed order -- this was what the music asked. [505] One tree shifted. [508] Then another. [510] The soil cracked in soft lines. [516]

A statue turned toward the sound. [522] But the sound, truly, was not there. [529] It was below hearing, below the range of granite senses, alive only in the living. [544]

The trees were moving now in a slow circle, the way they had once grown before the wardens came, following the sun's old path. [568] Their branches touched, briefly, and in that contact something passed between them -- not information, but feeling, the tarnished wonder of being almost forgotten and then, improbably, remembered. [595]

Maren did not know if what she was doing would last. [606] She understood only that it was happening. [613] The locust shell cracked against her chest, split again, emptied itself further, and she played on, her bow arm aching, her heart steady, the orchard sighing all around her in frequencies too old and too soft for anything mechanical to name. [654]

She was not saving the world. [660] She was only opening a window in it, one resonantly soft note at a time, so that something inside might finally decide to climb out. [685]
