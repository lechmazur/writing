Maren called those hours "the interval" — the stretch between vanishing stars when the sky held its breath and the topaz workshop glowed amber from within, as if the walls themselves were thinking. [32] She ran the letterpress alone. [37] No one else could tolerate the way she worked — setting type in sequences that meant three things simultaneously, burying the real message inside deliberate ambiguity the way a surgeon hides a suture beneath skin. [71] Customers brought her grief notices, love letters, manifestos, and she printed them all with the same unhurried precision, the same cryptic care. [93] They always left slightly disoriented, unsure whether the words they had approved were exactly what they had asked for or something truer. [114]

The halo fragment sat in the compositor's tray beside the lead type. [126] It was smaller than a finger joint — a curved sliver of something that caught light and refused to return it the same color. [148] She had found it embedded in the floorboards when she bought the shop, pried it free with a compositing stick, and understood immediately, with the certainty of something remembered rather than learned, that it was a tool. [186]

When she pressed her thumb to it before setting a job, she began to borrow. [201] That was the only word for it. [208] She borrowed the personality of whoever had written the words she was about to print — their cadence, their fear, their particular shape of longing — and wore it until the last impression dried. [241] It was transposed empathy, displaced feeling that arrived in her chest like mail addressed to a previous tenant. [259] She felt their grief not as her own but as a translation, intimate and foreign at once, a chord played in the wrong key that was somehow more resonant for being wrong. [291]

She did not do this to be kind. [299] She did it for the mathematics. [305]

There was a structure to emotion she was certain she could isolate — a sequence, a prime, some underlying grammar that governed why sorrow multiplied when shared and why joy sometimes divided. [336] Every personality she borrowed was data. [342] Every grief letter or desperate love note was a variable she ran through the press and measured in the quality of the ink spread, the pressure required, the way type bit paper differently when the feeling behind the words was real. [382]

Tonight she was setting a letter for a man named Osei, who had written to his estranged daughter in the voice of someone who had rehearsed apology so long it had calcified into a kind of poetry. [419] She pressed her thumb to the halo fragment and felt him arrive — his stubbornness first, then the shame beneath it like a second floor, then deeper still, a love so undemonstrated it had turned structural, load-bearing, invisible. [456] It disoriented her the way it always did, the sudden occupation of another architecture. [470] She stood still and let the feeling settle, let her hands remember they were her own. [486]

She set the type in the topaz light, the workshop's amber warmth pushing back the pre-dawn dark while stars dissolved one by one through the skylights above. [513] She chose each letter with the deliberate ambiguity that was her signature — words that said what Osei meant and also what he could not bring himself to mean, layered so that his daughter could choose which message to receive. [552] This was the mathematics she sought: not a single answer but a function, a relationship between sender and receiver that changed depending on who was doing the reading. [580]

The press rolled. [583] The ink met paper with a sound like a careful breath. [594] Maren peeled the sheet free and held it under the amber light, reading it as Osei, then reading it as his daughter, the two readings diverging like light through a prism. [626] Between them was a space she could almost measure — an emotional differential, the gap between what one person meant to give and what another was equipped to receive. [654]

She pinned the sheet to dry and pressed her thumb to the halo fragment once more, feeling Osei fade. [673] Outside, the last stars were going. [679] She pulled out her ledger and wrote a single equation — incomplete, as always, but closer. [694] The interval was ending. [698] She would come back to it tomorrow night, between the next set of vanishing stars, wearing the next borrowed self, pressing the next true thing into paper with her careful, cryptic hands. [730]
