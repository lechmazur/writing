Mara had sent the distress signal forty minutes ago, before the current dragged her through the temple mouth and sealed her fate with a cathedral of silt. [28] The forgotten underwater temple rose around her in corridors of pale stone, its columns still standing after three thousand years of patient dark. [51] Barnacles crowned every surface like a second architecture, and schools of lanternfish drifted through broken archways, their cold light giving the whole place a quality of lonesome splendor she might have loved under different circumstances. [86] She had twenty minutes of air left. [93]

The distress signal she had broadcast from the surface buoy would bring the coast guard, but only if they could triangulate her position inside the reef shelf. [121] The temple sat two hundred feet down, beyond sonar's clean reach, and she knew the signal alone might not be enough. [141]

Her hand found the crystal perfume vial clipped to her dive harness. [153] She had no good reason to carry it on a survey dive. [165] It had been her grandmother's, a small fluted thing that still held a ghost of rose and cedar trapped inside the glass, and Mara had simply never taken it off after the funeral. [198] Now her fingers curled around it, and the gathered hush of the temple seemed to press inward, the way silence does when a place has held its breath for centuries. [229] That hush touched memories she could not afford right now: her grandmother's kitchen, afternoon light, a feeling that time could be kept. [251]

She forced herself back to the problem. [258]

The temple's deepest chamber held a thermal vent, a narrow fissure she had documented on the survey map before the current hit. [280] Thermal columns rose straight through the reef shelf, and if she could position her emergency beacon above the vent, the rising water would carry the signal's acoustic component high enough for the coast guard's hydrophones to unsnarl it from the background noise of the reef. [322]

The corridor was blocked. [326]

A collapse of ancient stone had tangled with her guide rope, and every instinct she owned told her to pull, to thrash, to fight the obstruction head-on. [354] Panic was the oldest voice in the body and it spoke now in plain, physical terms: pull harder, surface faster, do not think. [377] She overrode it. [380] By overriding instinct, by going still the way the temple was still, she could see what the rope actually needed. [400] She threaded the line back through a gap she had missed, worked the knot loose with two fingers, and the rope came free without drama. [425]

As time ran short, only fifteen minutes of air remained. [435]

The thermal chamber was smaller than the map suggested, barely wide enough to turn in, and the vent breathed upward in a visible shimmer, warm water rising through cold. [464] She understood, in the particular way exhaustion clarifies things, why people throughout history had built temples above natural phenomena they could not explain. [487] The vent was ephemeral in the truest sense, a column of invisible energy that left no permanent mark on the stone, only warmth. [510] To harness the ephemeral, you had to meet it on its own terms. [523] You could not anchor it. [528] You could only place yourself where it was already going. [538]

She wedged the beacon against the vent's lip and watched the indicator light shift from red to amber. [556]

Mara sat back against the cold wall and held the crystal vial up toward the lanternfish light drifting through the archway. [578] The glass caught it and threw small prismatic shapes across three-thousand-year-old stone, rose and cedar still locked inside, memory made into something she could hold. [604] The gathered hush of the place settled over her like a hand placed gently on a shoulder. [621] She was not at peace exactly, but she was present, which is the closest thing. [637]

The beacon shifted to green. [642]

Above her, two hundred feet of dark water carried the signal upward through the thermal column, straight into instruments that knew how to listen. [666] She breathed slowly, rationing what remained, and waited inside the lonesome splendor of a place that had outlasted everyone who had ever prayed in it. [691]

The rescue divers arrived with eleven minutes to spare. [700]
