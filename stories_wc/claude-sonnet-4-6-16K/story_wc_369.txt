Mara carried her tools through passages that no one else wanted. [11] The labyrinth inside the Stormlit Tower was her responsibility alone, which suited her perfectly. [25] She patched crumbling mortar, replaced oil in the wall-sconces, and kept a ledger of every crack she found. [43] The work was ordinary in the way that breathing is ordinary: constant, unnoticed, essential. [57]

The walls here were not stone but wood, living oak grown inward over centuries until the corridors themselves became the tree. [78] Mara had always suspected the timber remembered things. [86] She wanted, more than anything, to discover what trees witness when no one is watching. [102] That desire had no practical expression until she found the sextant. [113]

It lay beneath the third spiral staircase, half-buried under fallen plaster, its mirror cracked and its arc bent like a broken elbow. [136] The initials scratched into the brass identified it as property of the tower's last astronomer, dead forty years now. [155] Mara wrapped it in her work cloth and carried it upward. [166]

The highest room of the tower had no ceiling, only a gap where lightning had taken the roof planks six winters ago. [189] Storm clouds tonight pressed low and brilliant, lit from inside by electricity that never quite discharged. [205] But between the clouds, the moon moved quietly, indifferent to the drama below. [218]

Mara set the sextant in a band of moonlight on the stone floor and watched. [234] The crack in the mirror closed first, fine as a hair drawing back together. [248] Then the arc straightened with a sound like a long, slow breath. [260] The instrument healed in moonlight the way she had learned her own hands did, slowly and without explanation. [278] She had stopped questioning it. [283]

She pressed her palms flat against the oak wall and waited. [294]

At the moment a pin dropped in the corridor below, she heard it. [307] The silence in the tower was absolute enough that the small metal sound rose through four floors of wood and stone and arrived at her ear with perfect clarity. [338] The oak walls carried it like a nerve carries pain. [348] And then she understood that the wood had always been conducting sound this way, not just sound but memory, not just memory but grief. [373]

She picked up the sextant. [378]

Mara had read about quantified sorrow in the astronomer's journals she had found in the same storeroom as his tools. [398] He had believed that sorrow had angles, that it moved like light through a medium and could be measured if you held the instrument precisely. [423] She had thought him eccentric. [428] Now she pointed the repaired sextant at the wall and turned its wheel. [441]

The mirror caught the moonlight and threw it against the wood in a fan of soft angles. [458] And in those angles, she saw them: scattered images printed on the grain of the oak like photographs left in sun. [480] A child pressing her cheek to bark. [487] A man weeping against a root. [493] A woman sleeping in the fork of two branches, dreaming loudly enough that the dream entered the wood and stayed. [514]

To deconstruct those layered impressions, Mara did what came naturally to a maintenance worker: she made a map. [532] She moved room to room through the spiral of the labyrinth, angling the sextant at each wall in turn, noting in her ledger where the images clustered, where they thinned, where the grief ran deepest and oldest. [569] By mapping the scattered dreams, she built a diagram of everything the living oak had absorbed across four hundred years. [589]

It was not a dramatic document. [595] It looked like any other maintenance log: columns, measurements, brief notations. [606] But it told the truth that trees hold: that sorrow accumulates in rings, that each year of witness adds its weight to the wood, and that the timber standing between one lost soul and another is not a wall but a record. [648]

Mara closed her ledger. [652] She touched the wall once, with the flat of her hand, the way you thank someone after they have told you something difficult. [676] The wood was warm. [680] Outside, the storm shifted, and for a moment the moonlight fell straight through the gap in the roof and landed across her shoulders like something almost like comfort. [708] She picked up her tools and went back to work. [718]

That was enough. [721] That was, by any measure, more than enough. [729]
