Maren Soloth, the last venerable oath scribe employed by the city, kept her workshop in the ancient orchard garden on the roof of the Halverson Tower. [26] The apple and pear trees up there were older than the building itself, transplanted by crane decades ago, their roots now tangled through reinforced concrete like the fingers of buried hands. [57] She worked among them because she believed living things could hear sorrow better than walls could. [73]

Her task, as she understood it, was to archive emotional patterns. [84] Not the words of oaths themselves -- those were kept in the Hall of Records below -- but the trembling underneath the words, the specific frequency of a voice committing itself to something irrevocable. [116] She was meticulous about this. [121] She was also, according to anyone who had tried to speak with her for more than ten minutes, unsettling. [140]

She spoke dead languages. [144] Not as an affectation, but because she maintained that living languages were too approximate, too crowded with contemporary feeling, to carry the precise weight of old promises. [171] She murmured in Sumerian when she was satisfied. [179] She whispered Elamite when she doubted herself. [186]

After the foghorn falls silent was when she preferred to begin. [197] The harbor foghorn, mounted two blocks south, groaned every six minutes through the nights of thick weather, and its silence -- that sudden, vast absence -- was the only clock she trusted. [227] Tonight the silence came at eleven forty-three. [234] She set down her cup and picked up the wax cylinder phonograph shaving she had been saving for three weeks. [254] It was a thin curl of recorded wax, skimmed from a cylinder during playback, containing a ghost of grooves too faint to reconstruct cleanly. [278] Someone had brought it to her as a curiosity. [287] She had known immediately it held something unfinished. [295]

To immerse herself in the emotional residue of the shaving, she needed a clean aperture in her mind. [313] She used, as she always did when ordinary methods failed, a curious third-grade field trip memory. [329] Her class had visited a natural history museum. [337] A docent had held up a fossil and said, very quietly, that this creature had not known it was the last of its kind. [361] Eight-year-old Maren had felt something enormous and wordless move through her chest. [373] She returned to that moment now, stepped back into it the way you step into cold water, and let the feeling open her. [396]

The shaving warmed between her fingers. [402] She pressed it to her inscription plate, a flat surface of treated bone, and began to trace. [419] She wrote in Lydian, a language extinct for two thousand years, its script looping in patterns that looked, to outside eyes, like decorative nothing. [443] She was not composing. [447] She was transcribing what the wax told her -- the shape of a grief, the exact pitch of someone's resolve. [466]

She did not notice what she had written until she was nearly finished. [479] The pattern on the bone plate was not a grief archive. [490] It was a prediction. [494] Or rather, it had organized itself into a prediction the way iron filings organize themselves around a magnet -- not by intention, but by field. [518] The Lydian text described a silence larger than a foghorn's absence. [529] It described a city that would stop making promises because it had forgotten what promised things felt like. [547] It gave a date. [551] The date was fourteen months away. [557]

Maren sat back in her chair and let an apple tree branch move against her shoulder in the night wind. [577] She did not believe in prophecy. [583] She believed in residue, in the persistence of feeling across time, in the idea that sorrow recorded precisely enough becomes a kind of map. [607] But she had not meant to write this. [615] The wax had meant it. [620] The wax had held some pattern so dense with accumulated human feeling that it had resolved, on its own, into future tense. [642]

She covered the bone plate with a cloth. [650] She did not add it to the archive. [658] She did not destroy it either. [664] She sat among the old trees above the sleeping city and spoke one word in Sumerian, quietly, to herself. [683] The word meant something between acknowledgment and dread, which was, she had always thought, exactly what a living language would never have bothered to distinguish. [708] The orchard held the dark. [713] Below, the harbor waited for weather enough to need its voice again. [725]
