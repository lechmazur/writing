Marcus had done things that newspaper headlines liked to describe in passive voice — man killed, money taken, life destroyed — as though guilt could be distributed among the grammar. [28] After the procedure, the doctors called him emotionally recalibrated. [37] He called himself hollow. [41]

The baseball sat on his discharge table when he woke: a scuffed Rawlings sphere signed in silver ink by someone named R. Teller, 2087. [65] No one claimed to have left it there. [73] The signature was too steady, too certain, the loops of a person who had already seen how things turned out. [93] Marcus pocketed it anyway. [97]

The journey began with newspapers. [102]

He had developed the habit in prison — reading between newspaper lines, not metaphorically but literally, tilting broadsheets toward jailhouse light until ghost impressions of earlier stories showed through the pulp. [133] The classified ads held coordinates disguised as phone numbers. [142] The obituaries hinted at directions. [147] Three weeks after his discharge, a food-section column about underground mushroom foraging contained, in the pressure-ghost of a prior draft, a hand-drawn map to a storm drain beneath the city's oldest rail yard. [180]

He went because what else did a man do when everything he had felt had been surgically scraped clean and the world expected him to be grateful for the emptiness. [210]

The tunnel descended into a labyrinth of choices — left at the rusted ladder, right at the smell of sulfur, straight through a corridor where the walls exhaled warm air like a sleeping animal. [243] Each fork felt weighted, not random. [249] The labyrinth did not seem designed to confuse him. [258] It seemed designed to make him decide, again and again, who he was willing to become. [274]

He chose correctly, or perhaps the labyrinth simply rewarded motion over paralysis. [286]

The underground bioluminescent lake opened before him like a secret the earth had been keeping since before the city above it existed. [308] Fungi and blind crustaceans dotted the shoreline, each organism pulsing with cold blue-green fire. [322] The lake itself was delicately robust — fragile in its beauty, ancient in its indifference, the kind of thing that survives precisely because no one thought it worth destroying. [350] Marcus stood at its edge and felt something loosen behind his sternum, not emotion exactly, but the memory of the shape emotion used to occupy. [375]

A woman sat on a flat rock near the water. [385] She was old, or ageless — it was difficult to tell in bioluminescent light. [398] She looked at the baseball before she looked at him. [408]

"You found it," she said. [413]

"You left it," he said. [418]

She shrugged in the manner of people who have stopped explaining themselves because the explanations never arrive cleanly. [436] "I needed you to come here," she said. [444] "The signature opens a door, but only if the person carrying it is broken in the right way." [462]

"What door," he said. [466]

She pointed across the lake, where the blue light thickened into something almost architectural. [480] "The procedure they gave you," she said, "it does not remove empathy." [492] "It removes the permission you gave yourself to feel it." [502] "Guilt calcified over decades looks exactly like indifference from the outside." [513] "From the inside too." [517]

Marcus turned the baseball in his fingers. [524] The silver ink caught the lake's glow. [531] He thought about the men whose files he had read in prison — victims with passive-voice names, their losses condensed into paragraphs — and he had read them the same way he had read newspapers, hunting for meaning beneath the surface, and had found nothing. [574] Because he had not been looking. [580] Because looking required something the procedure had not taken but he had surrendered long before any surgeon touched him. [599]

The lake breathed its cold luminescence against his face. [608]

He did not cross to the door. [615] Not yet. [617] He sat beside the woman on her flat rock and watched the blind creatures move through their glowing water, unhurried, purposeful, alive in the dark without needing the dark explained. [647]

He held the baseball in both hands like something finally worth keeping. [659] For the first time since waking empty on that discharge table, something kindled — small, uncertain, the first genuine thing in years. [680]

It was not forgiveness. [684] It was not peace. [688] It was the first of a thousand choices still ahead of him. [700]

He let it burn. [704]
