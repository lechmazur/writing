Mara Voss kept the dead. [5] That was the official title on her badge - Corporate Memory Keeper, Vault Division, Omninet Social Archive - but she preferred the shorter version. [28] She spent her days inside the archive's cold server infrastructure, cataloguing accounts that had gone quiet: suspended profiles, deactivated pages, the digital residue of people who had simply stopped. [58] Most cases were ordinary. [63] This one was not. [67]

The patch arrived in an unmarked envelope on a Tuesday - a cloth emblem from the Cassini-7 mission, the orbital survey that broke apart over the Pacific eleven years ago. [97] Three crew members had never been accounted for. [106] The patch was scorched along one edge, its embroidered mission number still legible: 7-7-7. [122]

Mara set it beside her keyboard and opened cold-case file 4-4-4-4, the designation for Lena Marsh, a Cassini-7 communications officer listed as missing rather than deceased. [149] The distinction had always bothered her, the way bureaucracy chose its words carefully when it wanted to lie. [165] Marsh was not missing. [169] Marsh had been erased. [173]

The archive held everything Omninet had ever hosted: thirty-two years of status updates, photo albums, playlist histories, the echo of forgotten songs people had queued at two in the morning when loneliness felt loudest. [209] Mara had been hired specifically because she was brightly attentive in ways that algorithms were not - she noticed texture, noticed the shape of a silence. [237] She noticed, for instance, that Lena Marsh's account had not been deleted. [248] It had been relocated. [252]

The number seven had started appearing three weeks ago. [261] Seven comments on a dormant fan page, all posted within seven seconds of each other, each containing seven-word sentences that formed no coherent message individually but together spelled a file path. [295] Then seven songs removed from a public playlist in seven-minute intervals. [307] Then a support ticket, number 7777, opened and closed in the same instant, leaving only a timestamp and the words: still here. [333]

Mara said nothing to her supervisors. [339] She understood that telling them would mean submitting a report, and a report meant oversight, and oversight meant the file path would be flagged and wiped before she could reach it. [371] So she lied at the weekly briefing, reporting only routine maintenance on legacy accounts. [385] The lie sat in her chest like swallowed glass. [395]

The file path led her deeper than she had ever gone. [406] The archive had public layers and corporate layers and legal-hold layers, and below all of them there was something the system diagrams did not show - a thermal partition that drew no power from the main grid. [448] Her access credentials should not have worked. [455] They did. [457]

Inside were the deleted files. [462] Not purged, not overwritten - preserved, in a space the company had carved out and then officially forgotten, the way you might forget a room by nailing its door shut. [498] Thousands of accounts lived here, suspended mid-sentence. [507] Lena Marsh's profile was among them, intact, and inside it Mara found a voice memo recorded the morning of the Cassini-7 launch, forty-seven seconds long. [534]

Lena's voice was calm. [538] She said she had found something in the orbital data, something the mission backers had not wanted found, and she had uploaded it here because she trusted the archive more than she trusted the people watching her. [578] She said the number sequence would guide someone brightly attentive enough to follow it. [592] She said she did not know if she would be coming back. [604]

Mara sat in the server hum for a long time. [614] The patch lay across her wrist, warm from her body heat. [625] Seven files were attached to the memo, and she began downloading them one by one, each transfer a small act of retrieval, pulling something real back from the enormous sensed absence that corporations called routine data management. [668] She had come here to find where deleted files go. [678] She had found, instead, that some of them had been waiting. [689] Some of them had been counting. [695]

She sent the files to a journalist she trusted, attached nothing, and wrote only seven words: Cassini-7 was not an accident. [716] Then she closed the partition, cleared her session, and walked home through streets that felt newly populated by everything the world had quietly pretended was gone. [745]
