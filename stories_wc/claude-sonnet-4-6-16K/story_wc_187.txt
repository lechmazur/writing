Sera called herself a summer-quill archivist, though no season had touched the observatory in years. [15] The station floated above the cloud layer, anchored to nothing visible, lit by the continuous slow lightning that gave the structure its name: stormlit, as though the building were thinking hard and couldn't stop. [49] After the fog became solid--a quarterly event no one below had ever witnessed--she would walk the perimeter and feel the compressed air press back against her boots like cold glass. [81] Tonight the fog had set. [86]

She moved through the stormlit observatory's lower corridor and descended the outer stair to the crystal whispering forecourt, which still held the memory of rain in every facet of its floor. [117] The forecourt was the oldest part of the station, its quartz panels fed by aqueducts that no longer existed, and when water had passed through it--as it had, hours ago, during the brief warm storm--the crystal would hold voices for days. [160] She knelt and pressed one ear to the floor. [169] Fragments surfaced: a child counting, a man describing a coastline, the overlapping murmur of a crowd watching something burn. [188]

This was collective memory, the residue of every person who had ever spoken within range of the station's recording apparatus, compressed into stone and frequency. [213] Her work was to sort it, catalog it, and conjecture where the gaps were. [227]

She stood and crossed to the nearest monument, a dark column of basalt at the forecourt's edge, and studied the shadow it cast across the crystal. [253] The lightning, though constant, moved in predictable cycles, and examining shadow lengths at monuments like this one allowed her to calculate which archived memories were being activated by the storm's rhythm and which lay dormant in the lower registers. [292] Tonight the shadow was short and angled west, meaning the lower registers were open. [306]

She wrote in her logbook with a summer quill, the long pale feather that was the mark of her station--gifted at midsummer, renewed each year--though she had not been home for a midsummer in eleven years. [344] The thought arrived before she had finished the sentence: preemptively nostalgic, she used to joke, grieving things before they ended. [364] She did not joke about it anymore. [371]

The hush around her deepened as the storm's lightning slowed to its quiet phase, and in that deepening hush the voices from the crystal floor grew audible without effort--a woman singing in a language Sera recognized but did not speak, two boys arguing about a hill. [418] She wrote quickly. [421]

Her conjecture, recorded across forty-seven notebooks on the shelf above her bunk, was that collective memory did not belong to any group of people but to the places where they had gathered and then left. [456] The crystal held it because crystal was honest about what it received. [468] The monuments held it in shadow because shadow was the shape memory made of light. [483]

She had come to the observatory because she believed that if she catalogued enough of it--if she sorted carefully enough through what others had felt, feared, desired, and lost--she could sculpt identity from experience: not merely remember who she was, but choose it. [528] She had left a city full of people who loved her in order to do this, which was the thing she was most preemptively nostalgic about, because she had left knowing she would miss them, and she had been correct, and the accuracy gave her no comfort. [575]

The lightning slowed further. [579] The forecourt whispered in the voices of strangers. [587] She examined the next monument's shadow, which had lengthened, and wrote: lower registers closing, mid-register opening, possible emergence of the coastal archive. [609]

Then she sat down on the cold crystal floor, her logbook open in her lap, and listened. [626] A man described a dock at low tide. [634] A girl read aloud from a book whose title Sera could not catch. [647] Someone laughed alone in a room. [653] She wrote it all down. [658]

She thought: this is how you build a self, from all the selves that briefly overlapped with yours, all the voices the places kept. [682] Outside the solid fog, below the stormlit observatory, the world continued without her notation. [696] She did not consider this a loss. [703] She considered it a project with a long horizon--one she would keep working toward, summer quill in hand, long after the forecourt had dried and the whispers had faded and the fog had softened back to vapor and the slow lightning had returned to merely resembling thought. [751]
