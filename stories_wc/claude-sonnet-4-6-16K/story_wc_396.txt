Sela arrived at the lighthouse orchard at dawn, when the apple trees threw long shadows across the ice and the floes below groaned like old bones. [26] She was the only peacekeeper anyone had sent, which made her either an insult or a miracle, depending on which side of the table you sat. [52] Her coat was laced with shadow, not metaphorically but literally, the darkness of three arbitrations woven into the fabric like thread, each one a conflict she had carried home and never put down. [85]

She had the broken crystal ball fragment in her left breast pocket, wrapped in cloth. [100] She had found it six months prior in the back room of a vintage store on Merchant Row, its owner having no idea what it was, only that the mirrors surrounding it caught strange light. [135] Sela had stood among those mirrors for a long time, watching the glimpses they offered: a burning dock, a row of empty cradles, two brothers standing on opposite shores of a frozen sea. [168] She had used those glimpses to reconstitute what had happened between the Valso clan and the Northern Shipping Collective, piecing together grievance from reflection, mapping the shape of a wound she had never personally suffered. [203]

That was what they called a courageous anomaly in the peacekeeping corps, someone who pressed intuition into conflict rather than waiting for a mandate. [227] Most arbiters wanted documentation, testimony, a chain of evidence they could point to. [240] Sela wanted the mirrors. [244] She trusted the way a thing looked sideways more than the way a thing looked straight. [260]

Now the two delegations sat across from each other in the lighthouse's upper room, where the glass was thick and curved and the ice floes shifted far below in the grey morning water. [293] Between them sat the agenda neither had agreed to. [302] Sela placed the crystal fragment at the center of the table. [313] It was roughly the size of a child's fist, its broken edges catching the lighthouse beam as it swept past the window. [335] The piece was not decorative. [340] It was functional, in the way that a scar is functional: it held the shape of what had been. [359]

Elder Valso looked at it and said nothing. [367] The Collective's representative, a woman named Drecht, drew a slow breath and held it. [381] That pause -- the weighted breath before reply -- was Sela's territory. [391] She had trained herself to be calmly intuitive there, patient the way a tide is patient, certain the way a tide is certain. [414] She let it sit. [418]

"We lost forty-two people," Drecht said finally. [426]

"We lost the dock," Elder Valso said. [433]

"People and a dock are not equivalent," Drecht said, and her voice was steady but her hands were not. [452]

"No," said Sela, and both heads turned toward her. [461] "They are not equivalent. [465] But they are connected." [469] She turned the crystal fragment slightly so the lighthouse beam moved through it, throwing small fractured light across both their faces. [490] "You lost your dock because someone was frightened. [498] You lost your people because someone was angry. [506] The same fear. [509] The same anger. [512] Different bodies." [514]

This was the only thing Sela had ever learned to do: to reconstitute the human thread running through a disaster, to take broken pieces and hold them up to borrowed light until the pattern became visible. [550] She had learned it via glimpses in those vintage store mirrors, in the reflections of things people refused to face directly. [571] To map empathy onto action, she always told herself, you must first be willing to see what the action cost. [591]

The ice shifted below. [595] The lighthouse beam swept again. [600] Drecht reached across the table and touched the edge of the crystal fragment, not taking it, just acknowledging it. [619] Sela waited. [621] The shadow in her coat moved, just slightly, like something breathing. [632] Outside, through the curved glass, the apple trees knocked in the wind. [644]

"Tell me," Drecht said at last, "about the fear." [654]

And Sela, the shadow-laced peacekeeper, the courageous anomaly who trusted mirrors more than mandates, began. [669]
