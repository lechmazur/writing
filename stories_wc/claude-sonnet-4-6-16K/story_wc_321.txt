Marco had worked the ferry for eleven years, and he still believed in the crossing. [15] Every morning he coiled the dock lines with the same devotion a priest reserves for vestments, his hands moving in loops that felt almost ceremonial. [40] The other crew called him naive, but Marco called himself practical, because optimism, in his experience, was simply the most efficient response to an uncertain world. [67]

The Halcyon ran the commuter route between Porta Luce and the old harbor at Venn's Reach, carrying electricians and nurses and bookkeepers across the grey inlet every weekday at six-fifteen. [98] Lately, though, the ferry had been vanishing. [106] Not sinking, not delayed, not rerouted. [112] Vanishing, as though the morning mist had decided to keep it. [123] Passengers would wait at the dock, the Halcyon would depart on schedule, and then nothing would arrive on the far shore. [143] The harbor authority called it a navigational anomaly. [151] Marco called it something closer to truth. [158]

He had found the hourglass three crossings ago, wedged beneath the engine-room stairs. [171] It was glass and brass and completely empty, no sand on either side, just two hollow chambers sealed against each other with nothing to measure. [196] Marco kept it in his coat pocket because throwing it overboard felt wrong and turning it in felt wronger. [215] An empty hourglass, he reasoned, was not a broken instrument but an honest one: it was telling him that time, right now, was not behaving. [241]

The clocks had started running sideways the week before the vanishings began. [253] Not backward, not fast, just sideways, their hands sweeping horizontal arcs across the face like compass needles caught between two norths. [273] The harbor master had replaced the clock on the pier three times. [285] Each replacement did the same thing by morning. [293] Marco was resiliently positive about this, which irritated the harbor master considerably. [305]

On the night the Halcyon vanished with Marco aboard, the ferry passed the midpoint of the inlet and simply kept going in a direction the inlet did not technically contain. [336] The water brightened beneath them, phosphorescent and cold, and then the hull scraped softly into a grotto that had no geographic right to exist. [360] Starlight came through cracks in the stone ceiling like needles through velvet, illuminating walls veined with mineral deposits that branched and forked and rejoined in patterns Marco recognized immediately. [391] They were the same patterns as the tide charts, the same as the growth rings of the old dock pilings, the same as the frost that climbed the wheelhouse window every January. [422] Everything connected, he saw, via interconnected patterns that ran through water and stone and time alike, a single grammar the world had been writing since before anyone thought to listen. [452]

At the grotto's center, anchored to nothing, sat the radio. [462] It was an old unit, the kind with a cloth-covered speaker and a dial that clicked through frequencies, and it was already on. [484] Marco picked up the handset and radioed across whatever medium occupied this space between crossings, speaking steadily, giving coordinates not of place but of moment, describing the branching patterns on the walls so that whatever other version of the Halcyon was listening in another sideways instant could triangulate and find the seam. [534] His voice did not shake. [539] He had come to understand that the ferry had not been disappearing but arriving, that every vanishing was a successful docking in a timeline adjacent to his own, and that both versions of the crossing were real and both were necessary. [578] The goal was not to choose between them but to merge parallel timelines into a single coherent passage, the way two rivers become one without either river ceasing to have existed. [607]

The radio crackled, and a voice answered using his exact words in reverse order, which meant the other side had understood. [628] Marco set the empty hourglass on the grotto floor between two veins of pattern-stone, and it began, slowly, to fill with something that was not sand but light. [659] He watched it run from one hollow chamber to the other and felt the hull shudder as the Halcyon found its bearing. [681] The grotto dissolved around them like a held breath finally released. [692] The far shore of Venn's Reach appeared ahead, lit gold in the early morning, and the dock workers on the pier looked unsurprised, because from where they stood, the ferry had simply arrived on time. [728] Marco tied the bow line with the same devoted loops as always, already looking forward to the return crossing. [746]
