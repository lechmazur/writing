The village of Canara hung between the canopy and the sky, its walkways braided from ironwood and vine, its lanterns swaying in rhythms only the trees understood. [27] Maren had lived there all her twenty-six years, and still the altitude made other herbalists dizzy. [44] Not her. [46] She climbed the rope bridges with her satchel swinging and her mind already three steps ahead, cataloguing roots, timing harvests, mapping the invisible grammar of growth. [74] She was ambitious in the way that wildflowers are ambitious — not loud about it, but absolutely relentless. [91]

That evening she sat cross-legged on the broad platform outside her drying shed, the canopy stretching below like a second earth. [113] She had spread an old sheet of wrapping paper across her lap — the cream kind, with faint red chevrons printed on the outside. [136] The inside was blank, and that blankness was an invitation. [146] It was the family custom her grandmother had started: unfold old wrapping paper to its blank interior, doodle first, write the important thing second. [170] So Maren drew a moth, then a coiling root, then a series of concentric ovals that looked vaguely like a closed eye before she finally uncapped her ink and began to journalize the day. [204]

The seed sat beside her knee in a small glass vial. [215] She had traded six months of tinctures for it — a single corpse flower seed, pearl-grey and dimpled, smuggled up the mountain by a wandering root-trader who smelled faintly of the lowland marshes. [247] The corpse flower's extract could arrest certain fevers that nothing else touched. [259] She knew this. [262] The entire treetop village knew this, and the three elders who had looked at the vial had nodded in that careful way that means both yes and be careful simultaneously. [292]

The problem was Maren herself. [297] She was olfactorily sensitive to a degree that bordered on affliction. [308] Cinnamon gave her headaches. [312] Smoke made her weep. [316] The corpse flower, once it bloomed, would produce a stench so concentrated, so biologically persuasive in its mimicry of decay, that she would be unable to work within forty feet of it. [348] She had calculated this with the same precise ambition she applied to everything. [361] She had not yet calculated what to do with the calculation. [372]

At the turning of the tide's thought — that slip of dusk when the light neither advances nor retreats, when everything pauses at the rim of its own momentum — she stopped writing mid-sentence and simply listened. [407] The trees moved. [410] Below her, the canopy breathed. [415] Wind crossed the platform like a question. [422] Her doodle of the closed eye stared back from the wrapping paper. [434]

She thought of her grandmother, who had also been sensitive to smell, who had lined her pockets with dried lavender and called her nose a compass rather than a curse. [464] Her grandmother had never been ambitious. [470] Her grandmother had been wise in the way rivers are wise — always moving, never rushing, shaping stone not through force but through the faithful repetition of being exactly what they were. [501] Maren had always considered that insufficient. [507]

Sitting now above the world, watching the last filament of gold dissolve into the canopy's darkness, she felt something shift — not in the air, but underneath it. [534] An undercurrent of wisdom she had been moving too fast to feel. [546] The corpse flower seed was not a problem to be solved from a safe distance. [561] It was an invitation to design a relationship with difficulty, to build the garden in a way that allowed her to be both near enough to tend and far enough to breathe. [593]

To cultivate serenity within motion, her grandmother had said once, is not stillness. [606] It is knowing which speed belongs to which moment. [615]

Maren picked up her pen. [620] On the wrapping paper, around the closed-eye doodle, she began sketching a garden layout — a long corridor, partitioned by trellised windbreaks, with her workbench positioned at the far eastern end where the prevailing breeze would carry the bloom's exhalation away from her tools. [663] She wrote: Plant the seed where it can be itself. [673] Position yourself where you can be yourself. [680] Tend the distance between. [684]

The trees creaked. [687] A moth moved through the lantern light. [694] She capped her ink, folded the wrapping paper carefully along its old creases, and tucked it into her satchel beside the vial. [716] Tomorrow she would begin. [720]
