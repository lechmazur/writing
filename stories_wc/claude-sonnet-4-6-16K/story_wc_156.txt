The sound wave garden existed at the edge of the city where nobody was quite sure the city ended. [19] Stone arches curved like frozen crests of water, and between them taut copper wires caught wind and traffic noise and human speech, bending each sound back on itself so that every whisper arrived twice -- once as question, once as answer. [64] Maren had worked here for eleven weeks, restoring the mural that lined the garden's central wall. [80]

She was methodically playful in everything she did. [88] She kept color swatches filed by hue temperature and cross-indexed by historical period, yet she tested each pigment by touching the tip of her brush to her tongue before touching it to stone. [121] She organized scaffold heights in a laminated chart she had made herself, and then climbed them in a different order every morning, just to see what the wall looked like from a new angle. [155] The mural -- a century of human figures layering over older human figures -- responded well to her approach. [172] She believed a painting remembered how it was restored. [181]

The morning she found the hobo nickel, she was on the lowest scaffold, cleaning a section of plaster near the base. [202] The coin sat in a crack between stones as if it had always meant to be there. [219] Someone had carved into the original Buffalo nickel face, replacing the native profile with a thin man wearing a wide-brimmed hat, eyes tilted slightly upward, expression impossible to read as either hopeful or resigned. [253] Maren turned it in her fingers. [259] She did not know why it moved her. [267] She pocketed it and kept working. [273]

That afternoon a boy no older than ten wandered into the garden and stood beneath the copper wires, which were singing in the afternoon wind. [298] He watched Maren work for a long while before he called up to her: "Do you ever wake up inside a dream and then wake up again inside that?" [329]

She paused. [331] She knew the question. [335] She had asked it herself at his age, and again at twenty, and again last winter when she stood in front of the mural before restoration had begun and realized the oldest layer of paint contained shapes she recognized from a dream she had had the previous night. [382] The experience of nested awakenings -- layers of consciousness folding into one another like sediment pressing into stone -- was not something she had ever been able to answer neatly. [412]

In the pause between question and reply, the garden did something it always did and no one ever seemed to expect: the copper wires caught her silence and sent it back altered, humming it into a chord that moved through the stone arches and returned as something almost melodic. [460] The reflected sound traveled through the wall she was restoring, and she felt it in the scaffold, in the handle of her brush, in her sternum. [486] The boy's eyes went wide. [491]

That was when she understood what the mural restorer's true work was. [503] Not just pigment and patience. [508] The wall beguiled people. [512] Through reflected sound, through the way the garden bent every utterance back into the listener, the mural had always been designed to bewitch -- to make a visitor suddenly porous, open, briefly uncertain of the border between what they remembered and what they imagined. [554] The original artist had understood this. [560] The copper wires were not decoration. [566] They were instrument. [569]

Maren climbed down. [572] She held out the hobo nickel to the boy, not because she wanted to give it away but because she recognized it as the right object for a moment like this -- a face remade by an anonymous hand, identity traded for mystery, certainty traded for curiosity. [617] That was the exchange the garden wanted to make with every person who walked into it. [633]

"I do wake up inside them," she said. [641] "And then inside the next one." [647] "I don't know how many there are." [654]

He took the coin and looked at the carved face for a long time. [668]

She climbed back up and continued her meticulous, joyful work -- filing swatches, testing pigments with her tongue, feeling the reflected sound move through the wall as afternoon turned. [696] She was not sure anymore whether she was restoring the mural or the mural was restoring her. [713] She decided that was exactly the right question to carry forward without answering. [726]
