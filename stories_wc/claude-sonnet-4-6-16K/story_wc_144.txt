The depot sat where rail met harbor, and the mist that rolled off the water each morning found no reason to leave. [22] It gathered against the iron columns and the warped wood of the repair shop's outer wall, softening every edge until the whole structure seemed less built than grown. [50] Inside, Mara worked. [53]

She had lost her sight twelve years ago, but those who brought their broken things to her shop left no less convinced than when they had arrived with perfect ones. [83] She oversaw every repair from her low stool near the center workbench, her hands moving over surfaces the way water moves over stone - unhurried, inevitable, recording everything. [110]

That morning, a boy had set down an artificial hummingbird drone without explanation and backed away toward the door. [129] The machine was the size of a fist, its iridescent casing cracked along one seam, its wing-stabilizers locked at the wrong angle. [151] Mara had heard the soft clatter of its landing on her bench, then the particular silence that followed when something mechanical was neither running nor fully dead. [178]

She did not ask the boy what had happened. [187] She never did. [190]

Instead, she carried the drone to the long trough of still water she kept beneath the depot's single skylight. [209] Her method was one she had developed slowly in the years after her blindness, born from the understanding that light, when it passed through water, told the truth about surfaces and angles in ways that fingers alone could not always confirm. [250] She held the drone just above the surface and tilted it incrementally, reading the refracted patterns against her palm and the inside of her wrist - warmth where light concentrated, cool where it scattered. [283] The cracked seam caught the refraction unevenly, and her wrist read the gap. [296] The locked stabilizers threw a shadow-line she could feel like a thin wire laid across her skin. [313]

Outside, through the depot's open freight doors, the harbor was visible in the mist. [327] The great cargo vessel moored at the end of the nearest dock had been the subject of talk all week - its maiden voyage was scheduled for that morning, and now the mist carried the low groan of the anchor chain as it lifted, link by link, from the harbor floor. [377] The sound moved through the depot like a slow pulse. [387]

Mara returned to her bench. [392]

She had learned, over years of silent practice, to decode the feedback her hands and skin gave her into a direction - not metaphorically, but literally. [417] A vibration in the thumb meant too much tension on the left. [429] A warmth that spread rather than pooled meant a crack running deeper than the surface suggested. [445] She had made herself into an instrument for reading instruments, and the shop around her had become an extension of that instrument, each water trough and resonant surface placed with intention. [476]

She found the patch material by touch, a thin composite sheet she kept rolled in a tin near the trough. [496] She cut it by feel, pressed it along the seam with a calibrated thumb, and listened to the faint change in air pressure as the casing sealed. [523] The stabilizers required patience. [527] She worked the locking mechanism with a narrow tool, feeling for the click that meant true alignment, making small corrections the way a navigator makes them - not all at once, but continuously, reading each result as instruction. [564]

When it was done, she set the drone upright on the bench. [576]

The anchor chain had gone quiet. [582] Somewhere beyond the mist and the iron columns and the warped wood, the ship was beginning its passage outward, carrying whatever it carried into open water. [608] Mara sat on her low stool and rested her hands flat on the bench, feeling the residual warmth of the work. [629]

The boy came back through the door a few minutes later. [640] She heard his steps change when he saw the drone sitting whole and upright, its stabilizers level, its casing sealed. [660] He did not speak, but she heard him lift it carefully, and the small displaced air of that lifting was, to her, as legible as a sentence. [687]

She nodded once, and the shop settled back into its particular quiet. [699]

That was enough. [702]
