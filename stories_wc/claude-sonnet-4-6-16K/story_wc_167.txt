Solen arrived at Pearl Bay before the fishermen, before the gulls, before the light had committed to anything. [18] The noctilucent clouds were still doing their slow work above the horizon, those luminous blue threads that only the predawn sky permits, and he stood watching them dissolve the way a thought dissolves before you can name it. [56] He was a glazier by trade, a harbor glazier specifically, fitting panes into the warehouses and pilot stations that lined the bay, and he had long understood that glass was not a barrier but a particular kind of attention. [95]

The prism atrium stood half-finished at the water's edge, wrapped in morning mist so that its angled panels caught no light yet but only held the fog's interior grey. [124] He had designed it himself, quietly, without showing anyone the plans, because explaining it would have required explaining himself, and he had never found the language for that. [152] The structure was meant to sit at the junction of the bay's oldest pier and the new harbor walk, a glass room that would do nothing useful except bend whatever light entered it into its constituent colors, scattering them across the planks and the water below. [198] He could already anticipate the questions from the harbormaster. [207]

But it was still before everything shifts, that narrow seam between night's last accounting and day's first demand, and Solen was not thinking about the harbormaster. [233] He crouched at the edge of a tidal pond set back from the main dock, a shallow depression where the bay pooled and rested, and he chose a flat stone from the gravel nearby. [267] He held it for a moment, feeling its weight, which was ordinary. [279] Then he threw it. [283]

The rings moved outward in perfect, compressing sequence. [291] He watched them the way he always did, not as a man watches entertainment, but as a man communes with something that is trying to speak in the only language it has. [323] Each ring carried information if you were willing to read between the lines the water made: the rate of expansion told you about the pond's depth at center, the distortion at the far edge revealed where the silt had shifted overnight, and the moment of stillness before the next tiny wave reflexed back toward the impact point told you, if you were patient, something less measurable about the nature of disruption itself. [395]

Solen had learned this decades ago from his mother, who was not a glazier but a net-mender and who had no formal theory, only the habit of attention. [423] She had taught him that the mundane holds everything if you look at it long enough. [439] A thrown stone, a pane of old glass with its slight vertical ripple, the way harbor water changes color at the turning of the tide -- these were not symbols for larger things. [471] They were the large things, already. [477]

He stood and looked back at the prism atrium in its mist. [489] The noctilucent reckonings overhead were nearly gone now, swallowed by the approaching dawn that was beginning to press a pale copper seam along the eastern water. [515] Soon the structure would receive its first true light, and he would see whether the angles he had calculated would scatter it correctly, whether the interior planks would carry the colors he imagined. [548] If they did not, he would recut the panels. [557] If they did, no one might notice. [564] That possibility did not trouble him. [570]

He was not building the atrium to be noticed. [579] He was building it because Pearl Bay at dawn was the plainest, most functional harbor on the coast, all pitch and rope and rust, and he believed something was owed to plainness -- not decoration, but illumination. [615] To show what was already there by giving it the right light. [627]

The first fishing boat rounded the breakwater, its running light a small red fact against the mist. [644] Solen picked up his tool bag. [650] The prism atrium would catch its first sunrise in minutes, and he wanted to see it happen alone, while it still meant only what he intended it to mean, before the day's interpretations arrived. [684] He walked to the pier, his boots loud on the wet planks, and waited in the particular silence that belongs only to the last moment before everything shifts. [712]
