In the sound wave garden, sound grows in visible arcs -- amber and silver crests that rise from dark soil like frozen breath. [22] The fisher Maren tends these rows alone, cool-headed and deliberate, dragging a rail spike along the ground between each planted wave to keep the resonance from bleeding between rows. [51]

Sleepwalking constellations -- the stars that drift when they dream, detaching from their fixed patterns to wander the lower sky -- are what make tonight dangerous. [75] When patterns break themselves, the whole garden destabilizes. [83] The Pleiades drift south. [87] Orion's belt unthreads. [90] And the waves Maren has grown for three seasons begin to hum out of order, each one speaking a frequency it was never seeded to carry. [116]

Maren does not panic. [120] She walks the garden's edge in a zigzag, the rail spike tucked against her forearm like a divining rod, reading vibrations through her bones. [144] She transliterates emotions this way -- grief becomes a low amber hum two feet off the ground, longing rises higher and turns blue, fury cracks white at the tip and bends the nearest wave sideways. [178] She has learned to read them all. [185] Tonight she feels something she has never catalogued: a frequency that has no color yet, something ancient moving beneath the disrupted pattern like a current under ice. [212]

This is what she came for. [218]

The goddess -- whose name Maren has never spoken aloud, because names are too small for what she means -- once walked this garden before it was a garden, before sound had been cultivated at all. [252] She walked singing, and the echoes of that singing sank into the earth and eventually grew into what Maren tends now. [273] Every wave in every row carries a ghost of it. [283] But the echo is always fragmented, dispersed, inaccessible when the garden is calm and orderly. [298] It is only when patterns break themselves -- only in the sleepwalking hours when the constellations drift loose from their shapes -- that the original resonance surfaces, coherent for a moment, catchable. [328]

Maren drives the rail spike into the center row. [337]

She knows the method. [341] You do not call sound by making sound. [349] The goddess's echo, if approached with noise, retreats the way a wild thing retreats from lanternlight. [365] You catch it by voicing silence -- by opening your throat without releasing breath, by shaping the absence of a sound into a vessel for it to fill. [392] Maren has practiced this for eleven years. [399] She is cool-headed enough to do it even now, with Orion disassembled overhead and the waves moaning around her in a feral lullaby, low and sweet and wrong, the way a song sounds sung by something that learned music from carnivores. [440]

She opens her mouth. [444]

Nothing leaves it. [447]

The silence she voices is not empty. [454] It is shaped -- it has the contour of a question and the weight of every emotion she has transliterated in this garden. [476] Grief's amber hue. [479] Longing's blue arc. [482] The white fury she has touched and measured but never let herself feel until right now, pressing it into the silence like a thumb into wet clay. [509]

The rail spike begins to ring. [515] It rings on a frequency she has never recorded, a tone that moves up through the handle into her palm and her arm and her sternum, and for one moment she understands it: not as sound, not as words, but as the specific sensation of something enormous being tender. [564] The goddess did not walk here singing for any congregation. [574] She sang because the world was new and sound had not yet learned what it was for, and she was lonely, and she was glad, and those two things in her were not contradictions. [608]

The sleepwalking constellations pause. [612] One by one, slowly, they drift back toward their shapes. [622] The waves in the garden quiet to a murmur. [631] The echo releases the spike and Maren's hand at once, retreating back below the soil where it waits for the next night patterns forget themselves. [656]

Maren pulls the rail spike free. [662] She tucks it back against her forearm, notes the frequency in the small book she carries, and walks to the next row. [684]
