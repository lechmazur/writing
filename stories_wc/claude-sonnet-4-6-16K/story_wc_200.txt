Maren descended into Echo Gorge before the sun had decided anything about the day. [14] The canyon walls held the dark the way old wounds hold silence, reluctantly, and only until something disturbs them. [33] She carried a harp on her back, its frame slightly warped from years of damp storage, its strings tuned to no recognized key. [56] Around her neck, threaded on cord of braided river grass, hung a single crocodile's tooth, yellowed, curved, and older than her grandmother's grandmother. [79] She had inherited both the tooth and the vocation: she was a lost soul collector, the last of three sisters to take up the work. [104] Her sisters had chosen easier commissions. [110] Maren had chosen the gorge. [115]

The hush of the hidden sunrise arrived not as light but as a soft pressure, a held breath between night's last exhale and morning's first word. [141] She felt it against her cheekbones, that particular stillness, and she began to descend the switchback path toward the canyon floor. [162] The souls she collected were not the recently dead. [171] They were the ones who had gotten lost mid-confession, people who had begun to speak some unspeakable truth and then simply dissolved into the air before they could finish, leaving their grief to drift in places where sound bent strangely. [211] Echo Gorge collected them the way a tide pool collects strange creatures: without judgment, without agenda, just the patient accident of geography. [233]

She unslung the harp and set it across a flat stone at the canyon's narrow throat. [249] It was modestly fearless work, what she did, not grand or reckless, just a quiet refusal to be undone by the weight of borrowed sorrow. [274] She had learned early that courage in this profession looked less like armor and more like showing up with a broken instrument. [296]

She plucked the first string. [301] The note that came out was wrong, gorgeously, usefully wrong, bent somewhere between a low D and something that had no letter. [323] Through the distorted notes of the misaligned harp, she could pursue the spectral confessions that hid in the gorge's acoustic folds. [344] The wrongness of the tuning was the point: true notes slid off grief without catching; only the crooked ones hooked into the frequency of an unfinished truth. [371]

She played slowly, and the gorge answered. [378] A voice arrived first as vibration, then as something almost verbal, a man who had died mid-apology to his son, the words still tangled in his chest like fishing line. [408] She listened without interrupting. [412] That was the first rule. [417]

The second rule was the tooth. [423] The crocodile's tooth was a release valve for what she absorbed. [434] When a confession fully passed through her and into the tooth, the soul untangled and could move. [451] She closed her fingers around it now and felt the warmth of the man's unfinished apology transfer, the remorse, the love beneath the remorse, the years of not knowing how to say either thing. [485] The tooth pulsed once, dull and amber in the pre-dawn gray, and the voice in the gorge dimmed. [503]

But she stayed. [506] She had come for the harder ones, the souls who did not simply grieve, but who had forgotten how to exist without their grief. [530] Healing them required something her predecessors had never attempted: she had to integrate laughter into the work. [547] Not performance, not distraction, actual laughter, the kind that understood exactly what it stood beside and chose lightness anyway. [566]

She began to play a second phrase on the harp, this one half a song her mother used to sing badly at the kitchen table, and the distorted strings made it sound like a joke the universe was telling itself. [606] A second presence stirred, a woman's confession, something about a long-held grudge and a garden that had grown over the grave of a friendship. [630] And then, unexpectedly, the confession shifted: the woman's voice caught on the harp's crooked note and made a sound that was almost, improbably, a laugh. [655] Not at the pain. [659] At the absurdity of having carried it so long without once setting it down to rest. [675]

Maren felt the gorge walls soften. [681] She pressed the tooth to her sternum and breathed, and in the hush of the hidden sunrise, in the space where sound bent and souls forgot they were lost, something began, not quite healing, but the luminous ache that always comes just before it. [725] She kept playing. [728] The harp stayed wrong. [732] The gorge stayed open. [736] And the morning, when it finally came, arrived laughing softly at its own long delay. [751]
