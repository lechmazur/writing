The wisteria had claimed the old viaduct by late April, draping violet curtains over every stone arch until the whole structure seemed to breathe. [24] Maren set her portable camera obscura on the bridge's railing, aiming the lens toward the river below, where the last of the day's light pooled like cooling copper. [52] She was what people called spiritually curious, which was a polite way of saying she could not stop asking questions that had no comfortable answers. [77]

The image inside her box was inverted and trembling: wisteria, water, and a woman standing at the far bank who looked, impossibly, like Maren herself wearing older clothes. [105] Then the memory surfaced. [109]

It came the way such things always did — not as a vision but as a change in her breathing. [128] Her inhale shortened, grew shallow, and her exhale stretched long and ragged, as if some other body's grief were using her lungs to finish its unfinished weeping. [155] She had read about this, the breath as a doorway, but reading and standing on a twilight viaduct in bloom while your ribs betray you are entirely different educations. [184]

In that other life she had been a woman who loved carefully and late, who had stored her deepest affections the way a winter kitchen stores salt — packed away, preserved, never opened. [216] The man she loved had gone across a river very much like this one, and she had watched him from a bridge very much like this one, and she had said nothing. [248] The love had remained inside her, unused, accumulating quietly across the decades of that life and somehow — she understood this with the foggy lucidity of a half-remembered dream — across the boundary of death itself. [282]

Maren was a pilgrim of sparks, her teacher had once told her, meaning she traveled not toward shrines but toward moments of ignition, the places where the invisible world briefly caught fire. [314] She had never been sure she believed him, but she had built her practice around the camera obscura because it did what she could not: it received light without flinching and transformed it honestly. [348] Tonight the device was showing her something she had not set out to photograph. [362]

She reached into her coat pocket and found the small glass vial she had carried for three years without fully knowing why. [384] A pilgrim acquaintance in Kyoto had pressed it into her hand outside a temple and said only that it was Kwan Yin's tear — actual or metaphorical, she had never confirmed — a single bead of translucent liquid sealed in glass, said to hold the compassion of an undivided attention. [432] She had kept it because she was spiritually curious and because throwing it away felt like refusing a gift from the universe, which seemed unwise. [457]

She uncorked it now. [461]

The smell was faint: rain on warm stone, something green, something older. [473] Her breathing shifted again, widening this time, her chest opening like a room whose windows had been stuck for years and finally gave. [496] This was the method she had stumbled upon by accident in her work — that the image inside the camera obscura was never just composition and light, it was a mirror for the breath that stood before it, and what the breath revealed, the image confirmed. [541]

She understood then where unused love is stored. [549] It is not lost, not decayed, not spent on nothing. [559] It waits in the body like wisteria waits in root and tendril through the dead months, patient and specific, ready to climb whatever structure finally holds still long enough. [588]

Maren tipped the vial and let the single drop fall into the river far below. [603] She did not watch it land. [609] She put her eye back to the camera obscura instead and watched the inverted woman at the far bank, who was already turning away, already walking toward the treeline, already finished waiting. [641] The image shook once as a breeze moved through the wisteria overhead, and then it steadied. [657]

To heal, Maren had always assumed, meant to close a wound. [668] But standing on the viaduct as the last light left the water, she revised her understanding: to heal was to give the stored thing its true name and let it move again, like light through a small dark box, like breath through a body that had finally decided to stop holding on. [720]

The wisteria swayed. [723] The river received everything. [727]
