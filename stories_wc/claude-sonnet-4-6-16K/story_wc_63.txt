Aldus Venn had wound the clock tower on the tidal obsidian ridge for thirty-one years, and he had never once been late. [22]

The ridge itself was a shelf of black volcanic glass that jutted from the coastline like a broken jaw, polished by centuries of salt tide until it held the sky in its face like a mirror that had forgotten how to lie. [64]

The tower rose from its center, stone upon stone, and the great pendulum inside it swung in long, patient arcs that Aldus had come to think of as the breathing of something too old to name. [100]

He was known in the village below as ethically diligent -- a man who showed up not just on time but morally prepared, who documented every weight adjusted, every gear oiled, every correction made, in a ledger he kept with the care of someone who understood that time itself was a form of shared property. [154]

But the ledger was not the whole accounting. [162]

The broken rake handle he carried -- smooth ash wood snapped at mid-shaft, the fracture stained dark with age -- held the other record. [184]

Along its length, in letters so small they required a jeweler's loupe to read, Aldus had carved inscriptions over the course of many years: names, dates, brief summaries of harm. [214]

His sister's savings he had borrowed and not repaid before she died. [226]

A lie told to a creditor that cost a younger man his apprenticeship. [239]

A silence held when a word of truth would have saved a marriage in the village east of the ridge. [259]

Thirty-one inscriptions in total. [263]

One for each year he had wound the clock. [272]

He had not carved them as confession. [279]

He had carved them as a ledger of what remained, because incremental absolution was the only kind he believed in -- not the single-handed erasure of guilt, but the slow, careful restoration of what had been taken, one act at a time, until the column balanced. [324]

Each morning, he climbed the tower stairs as the tide pulled away from the obsidian ridge below, leaving the glass shelf gleaming and exposed. [348]

He reached the movement room and set his hands on the winding mechanism, and he waited. [364]

He always waited for the same moment: the pause in the pendulum's swing. [377]

At the apex of each arc, the great brass pendulum bob hung motionless for a fraction of a second -- not quite still, but no longer moving in any direction the world could measure. [410]

That was when Aldus would read one inscription from the rake handle. [422]

That was when he would let himself feel the full weight of the named thing. [437]

This morning he read the inscription for the apprentice. [446]

He had already spent two years finding the man, now grown and running a small cooperage in a coastal town four days north. [469]

He had written letters, sent what money he could manage, offered his account of what had happened without softening it. [489]

The apprentice had written back twice -- formally, without warmth, but without refusal. [501]

This morning's reading meant the debt had entered what Aldus privately called the healing interval: not yet forgiven, not yet closed, but no longer compounding. [526]

He began to wind. [530]

Through the tower's slit window, the ridge emerged below him as the last of the tide withdrew -- that slow, black emergence, the obsidian rising from the water like a thought the sea had been holding too long and finally released. [570]

He watched it and felt something loosen in his sternum. [580]

Not relief. [582]

Something more precise than relief. [587]

The pendulum resumed its arc. [592]

He tucked the rake handle back into his coat, where it rested against his ribs like a second spine, lighter this morning by the precise and unmeasurable weight of one name becoming easier to carry. [627]

The clock ticked on. [631]

The tide would return. [635]

The inscriptions along the ash wood numbered thirty-one, but his hand had already found the spot just below the last one, where the grain of the wood was still uncut, still waiting. [667]

He was ethically diligent enough to know that balance was not a destination. [680]

It was what you did with your hands while the pendulum swung. [692]
