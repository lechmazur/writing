The brook spoke before Maren did. [6] She had arrived at the water's edge just after the last ember of sunset dissolved, carrying only a lantern and a single folded crane whose left wing she had carefully torn free and pressed flat between her palms during the journey. [47] The nocturnal haven accepted her without ceremony — willows trailing their fingers into the current, fireflies assembling their quiet constellations above the reeds, the darkness itself warming around her like breath held in love rather than fear. [83] She set the lantern down and let its flame do what lanterns do best: burn without demanding attention. [101]

The prophecy had come to her three moons ago, encoded in the dialect of her order — syllables nested inside syllables, meanings curled inside meanings like the paper crane itself — and it had not stopped since. [136] It replayed in her chest at odd hours, a hot and pressurized thing, the kind of knowing that scorches from within while leaving no visible mark. [162] She had tried to silence it by understanding it. [171] That had only made it louder. [177] So the solitary summoner had come here, to the only place she trusted absolutely. [191]

By mapping silence, she had always worked best. [199] Other summoners chanted, drew sigils in charcoal, burned particular resins to call their art forward. [214] Maren worked differently: she listened until the world organized itself around her listening, and then she moved within that organization the way water moves, which is to say inevitably, along the truest available path. [248] Flow was not her technique so much as her nature, and the brook recognized it, murmuring lower as she settled cross-legged at its edge, as though giving her room. [277]

She placed the crane's origami wing on the surface of the water. [289] It did not sink. [293] It rotated slowly in the shallows, caught in a gentle eddy, and she watched it with the full unhurried weight of her attention. [316] Paper was an astonishing material — terrestrially cosmic in the way it could be both utterly mundane and, in the right hands at the right hour, a vessel for something vast. [346] The wing was both a fragment and a whole thing. [356] The crane it had belonged to still existed, its remaining wing folded precisely, waiting on the stone altar back at her threshold. [378] She had divided the crane deliberately: half here, half there. [388] A bridge made of symmetry. [393]

Amid whispered secrets, the brook's voice changed pitch. [401] She heard it the way she always heard meaningful things — not with her ears exactly, but with the space behind them, the interior room where language had not yet hardened into word. [433] The water was offering her something. [439] The fireflies, too, were arranging themselves differently now, their cold light tracing patterns she almost recognized. [455]

She breathed out slowly. [459] The prophecy surfaced in her chest again, sharp-edged and urgent, and she did not fight it. [475] She let it rise, let it fill her throat, let it press against the back of her teeth — and then she let the brook take it. [501] Not by shouting it into the water. [508] Not by transcribing it onto the wing and watching it dissolve. [519] She released it the way smoke releases from a wick when you pinch the flame: quietly, completely, without residue. [538]

The smoothed hush that followed was not an absence. [547] It was a presence — dense and warm and faintly luminous, like the inside of a coal just before it goes fully dark. [569] The prophecy did not vanish; it found its proper weight. [579] She had spent three moons carrying it at the wrong scale, holding it in the personal when it needed to exist at the impersonal. [603] Now it rested in the water and the air and the accumulated dark between the fireflies, no longer a burning secret lodged in one woman's ribs but simply a truth distributed across the night like starlight. [639]

The crane's wing drifted to a stone and held there. [649] Maren picked up her lantern, breathed its tranquil blaze in and out once through her cupped hands, and felt the warmth return to her on different terms — not the heat of something unresolved, but the heat of something known and set down. [691] She stood. [693] The brook continued speaking, but softer now, in the register of ordinary things. [706] She walked home without the prophecy's weight. [713] She still carried its meaning. [718]
