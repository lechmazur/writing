After the Armistice of Tides, when the world's nations agreed that violence had become impossible--not illegal, but physically impossible, the will for it simply drained from human muscle like water from a cracked hull--Sera kept working the docks because the sea still needed tending even if men no longer needed fighting. [53] She was graceful in the way dock workers rarely were: every coil of rope settled from her hands like a sleeping animal, every crate she guided to the boards arrived without a sound. [86] The other workers called her the Heron. [93] She never corrected them. [97]

The scrimshaw she wore around her neck was old--a curved wedge of whalebone etched by some long-dead pirate's blade with a coastline that matched no chart. [124] Other sailors who had seen it called it beautiful, then grew strange and quiet, then started talking about their mothers, their first loves, their failed kindnesses. [150] The scrimshaw unlocked guarded hearts the way cold salt air opens the lungs: involuntarily, completely. [165] She had inherited it meaning to sell it. [173] She kept it instead because it had made her cry the first night she held it, and she decided anything that could make her cry deserved to stay close. [202]

The sublittoral fringe where she worked was a borderland--the sea's own dock, where eelgrass meadows gave way to open water and the light turned green and thick. [230] She worked the pilings that stood in this zone, checking barnacle growth, replacing rotted wood, up to her thighs in cold water while the world moved its cargo overhead. [259] She loved it here. [263] She had never told anyone that. [269]

Then, forty days after violence became impossible, the whale called. [279] It rose from somewhere so far below it registered less as sound than as a change in the quality of silence. [300] Sera was on a piling, wrench in hand, when the call passed through the water and through her bones and through something she had been holding shut inside her chest for thirty-one years. [333] She felt it detach--the old pretense, the careful performance of being only a dock worker, only a woman doing a job--she felt it detach from her ribs like a barnacle pried free. [367] It did not hurt. [371] It felt like the first breath after surfacing. [379]

The island appeared at noon. [384] It had not been there at ten. [391] It rose slowly from the sublittoral depths, dark basalt trailing kelp and foam, seabirds screaming from ledges they seemed to remember. [412] The other dock workers stood and stared. [419] Sera was already swimming. [423]

The water was cold and she was graceful in it, more graceful than she had ever been on land. [442] She held the scrimshaw in her fist and when she reached the island's shore and pressed the carved bone to the first rock she touched, she felt the island shudder, felt it recognize something. [476] "You don't have to be afraid of me," she told the island, and her voice was the voice she used when she was alone, lower and rougher and entirely her own. [507] "I'm not going to tell anyone where you go." [516]

She didn't know if islands could be afraid. [524] But she knew about the exhausting work of disappearing before anyone could decide you were worth keeping. [541] The island stayed solid beneath her feet. [548]

Behind her, someone had swum after her--old Prentiss, who had called her cold for twenty years, who had mocked her silence and her grace. [573] He hauled himself onto the rock beside her, panting, and looked at the scrimshaw in her hand, and something in his face came apart gently, the way things come apart in the new world, without violence. [609] "I always thought you were more than you let on," he said. [621] It was the closest thing to an apology she had ever received from him. [635]

She handed him the scrimshaw, and they sat on the disappearing island, in the clean light above the sublittoral fringe, and she finally let herself be what the sea had always known she was. [669]
