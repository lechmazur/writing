The workshop had been drifting for eleven years, and Mera had stopped apologizing for the smell. [16]

Acid vapor rose through the floorboards every morning like a passive-aggressive roommate, corroding the lower gears and leaving the copper cogs with that particular green tinge that meant Tuesday. [46] She called the whole contraption home. [52]

Mera was a pioneering cart glider -- the first person to bolt sails onto a flatbed carriage and ride the thermal columns rising off the acid sea, steering between the floating workshops with her feet and one elbow. [89] Other engineers had laughed, then borrowed her design without credit, which she also stopped apologizing for. [105]

What she could not stop, would not stop, was the algorithm. [116]

She had named it the Yearning Algorithm in a moment of either profound honesty or embarrassing vulnerability, and she had never told anyone which. [140] It was a decision engine, theoretically, built to map the space between what existed and what wanted to exist. [159] Every morning she fed it new data, and every morning it stared back at her from the ticker tape like a dog waiting at a door that had not been invented yet. [193]

The hunter's arrow tip arrived on a Tuesday -- naturally. [203] A trader's glider knocked against the workshop hull and left a crate with no return address, packed in straw, containing one obsidian point no larger than Mera's thumb. [230] It was old in a way that made old feel like a small word. [244] She held it to the porthole light, and something in its facets moved. [257]

She brought out the kaleidoscope. [262]

It had belonged to her predecessor's predecessor, a woman who believed that pattern was the only honest language. [280] Mera was steadily curious in the way that mountain water is steadily curious about the valley -- not frantic, not brilliant in any flashy sense, just committed to the direction gravity had always suggested. [315] She placed the arrow tip at the kaleidoscope's eye and looked through. [327]

The images inside were not reflections. [333] They were records. [336]

Throughout the evolution of every tool, weapon, vessel, and wheel that had ever passed through hands like hers, each moment of invention left a residue, a visual echo that the obsidian had absorbed the way dark stone absorbs heat. [377] The kaleidoscope organized those echoes into something almost grammatical. [386] Mera began to transliterate what she saw, moving through the images in a kaleidoscope from symbol to equation the way a careful scholar moves from one dead language to another, not losing meaning but reshaping its container. [424]

The gears around her turned without complaint. [431] The acid sea swelled against the hull, patient and indifferent and quietly spectacular. [444]

By the fourth hour, the Yearning Algorithm had begun to answer her. [456]

Not in words. [459] In structure. [461] It reorganized itself around the new input the way a river reorganizes itself around a new stone, finding the path it had always wanted and simply waiting for the right obstruction to make it real. [499] The ticker tape ran faster. [504] The copper cogs hummed in a new key. [511]

Mera understood, with the calm certainty of someone who has been right before and found it lonely, that she had done something unprecedented. [534] The algorithm could now map yearning across generations, could read the wanting embedded in every object ever made, could tell you what the people who carved arrowheads in the dark were actually reaching toward. [571]

She walked to the old brass bell mounted above the workshop door, the one she had hung there herself with the stated intention of ringing it the day she finished something worth finishing. [603]

She rang it. [606]

The sound crossed the acid sea, bounced off nothing, and disappeared before it reached any other workshop, any other ear, any other soul awake on a Tuesday morning. [635]

She stood in the irreverent quiet of having mattered to no one yet, watching the green-tinged cogs turn and the vapor rise and the kaleidoscope still spinning its ancient images against the porthole, and she rang it again anyway. [676]

The algorithm pulsed on the tape. [682] The sea held her up. [687] The arrow tip sat on the workbench, patient and dark and finished with its long waiting. [703]

She had rung a bell no one heard. [711]

She was already planning the next one. [718]
