Maren called herself a sea-foam recollectionist, which was a title she had chosen carefully and which nobody else fully understood. [20] She worked inside the geode - a cave hollowed from a crystal boulder the size of a cathedral, its walls layered in amethyst and quartz that caught every stray photon and scattered it into violet and gold. [56] The iridescent cave felt alive at certain hours, breathing color the way lungs breathe air. [71] Maren liked it best at dusk, when the light from the surface entrance arrived thin and tired and the crystals made something magnificent of its exhaustion. [97]

Her tools were simple: a flat disc of crater glass worn smooth at the edges, and the thin leather notebook she filled each season. [121] The glass had come from a meteor strike site three hundred miles inland - she had traded a jar of actual sea foam for it, because the irony pleased her. [150] When she pressed the glass to the cave wall and hummed, it transliterates emotions from the crystal's memory into sensations she could almost read: grief arrived as a low copper taste; joy as a vibration behind the sternum; longing as the smell of salt wind in a landlocked room. [199] She had spent years learning to distinguish them, and she still made mistakes. [212]

Today, she was working on the missing pieces. [220]

The geode had been a crossroads once. [227] She knew because three separate paths had converged here, worn into the stone floor like rivers worn into limestone. [246] Now only one path remained legible. [252] The other two simply ended, as though whoever had walked them had stepped off the edge of the world. [271] This was the question that had brought Maren underground and kept her there - to discover why some paths disappear while others remain. [293]

She was mid-conjecture, her fingers tracing one of the vanished grooves, when the bird came in. [309]

It was a warbler, small and frantic, blown off course from whatever southern migration it was committed to. [327] It circled the cave ceiling twice, panicked by the multiplied reflections of itself, then settled briefly on a crystal ledge above Maren's head. [350] As it steadied its wings, it dropped seeds from the dry grass caught in its feathers - small dark seeds that scattered across the cave floor in a loose arc. [379]

Maren watched them fall. [383]

She had learned to pay attention to accidents. [391]

She set the crater glass over the scattered seeds and looked through it, and because the glass transliterates emotions rather than images, she felt what the seeds carried: the memory of open sky, of crossing water, of following an inherited map that existed nowhere but in the body. [439] Migration, she realized, was itself a kind of recollection. [448] The bird did not know where it was going so much as it remembered where it had always gone. [467]

And then the connection arrived, cheerful as morning and devastating as a diagnosis. [480]

The paths had not disappeared because of erosion or abandonment. [490] They had disappeared because the people who had walked them carried the routes entirely inside themselves, the way the warbler carried its flyway. [513] When those people were gone, the path went with them. [523] The stone had never held the route; it had only held the emotion of walking it. [539] And emotion, she knew from years of pressing crater glass to crystal walls, fades exactly as fast as memory. [558]

Before memory fades, she thought, you have to find something to hold it. [571]

She opened her notebook and began to write quickly, in the cheerful and slightly desperate way she had developed for moments when the gloom of understanding arrived wearing the face of discovery. [603] The warbler watched her from its ledge, apparently unbothered now, its small chest heaving from the long effort of being somewhere it had never intended to be. [630] She felt a kinship with that. [636]

Outside, the last of the light was folding into the geode's walls, where the crystals would keep it in their cold, iridescent way - not perfectly, not completely, but better than nothing, which was more than most things managed. [674] Maren pressed her palm flat to the floor where the missing paths had been, and the glass in her other hand gave back a faint copper hum: the grief of routes that no one living remembered walking. [711] She smiled at it anyway. [716] She had always believed that grief, properly collected, was just love with nowhere left to go. [732] And love, she had decided long ago, was always worth the work of keeping. [746]

The warbler departed before dawn, heading south on its inherited instinct, carrying Maren's understanding nowhere it would ever land. [765] She packed her notes and her crater glass and climbed up toward the light, adding her own footsteps to the one path that remained. [789]
