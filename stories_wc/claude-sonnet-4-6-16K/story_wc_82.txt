Miro had been the finest lantern maker on the floating isle for thirty years, known for glass so thin it hummed in wind, and for the peculiar trade he kept secret from the tea garden visitors above. [37] The tea garden clung to the island's summit, terraced with pale stones and iron tables, where travelers came to drink jasmine broth and watch the continent drift past far beneath them. [68] Miro rarely went up. [72] He worked instead in the lower chambers, below the glass basilica the old founders had raised to catch morning light, its thousand panes refracting sunrise into sixty colors across every surface of the island. [106]

For thirty years he had also been a dream recorder. [116] The method was this: he would take a rough gem — citrine, labradorite, tourmaline — and polish it through the night while a sleeping client lay nearby, the slow circular grinding drawing the dream out of restless breath and down through lamplight into the stone until the facets held the entire vision, fixed and retrievable, something a person could press to their temple and replay across the interior dark. [183] He had recorded thousands of dreams. [189] He had never recorded his wife's. [195]

Sera had died in the cold season, and grief had made Miro a man who descends stairs to avoid ascending into conversation. [217] But the timeframe that mattered was not her death — it was after the shadow detached. [232] For three months following, her shadow had remained. [240] It moved through his workroom with stubborn fidelity, passing over his tools at the same hour she once had, stretching across his polishing cloth at dusk. [266] He had kept working. [270]

Then one morning he woke and the shadow was simply gone, the light falling clean and indifferent across the floor, and that emptiness was worse than any haunting. [298] It was only then, after the shadow detached, that the grief became unbearable weight rather than bittersweet presence, and Miro understood he could not survive it as a passive fact. [328]

He climbed to the tea garden — he descended while ascending, because every step upward toward the place she had loved dragged him deeper into loss — and he sat at the iron table nearest the basilica's glass edge, where the panes threw violet light across his hands. [374] He had brought a single piece of rough moonstone. [383] And he had brought the box of small white papers Sera had kept, each one already folded into an origami crane by her careful hands, her private ritual whenever sorrow visited her, the way she transformed what hurt into something that could theoretically fly. [427] He set both objects on the table. [434]

His plan was specific and crafty, as all his plans were. [445] He would polish the moonstone through the night, but instead of drawing out a dreamed vision, he would press his own memory of Sera into the stone — every dream he had ever had of her, the accumulated record of a thirty-year love held in a single sleeping mind — and he would seal it in a lantern. [501] Then he would hang the lantern inside the glass basilica, where the thousand panes would refract its light, and the dream recording would transform into something ambient and architectural, spread across the entire island, Sera everywhere rather than nowhere. [540] And for each crane in the box, he would fold one grief smaller. [553]

He began polishing at dusk, the stone catching violet then amber then the blue-white of deep night. [570] The tea garden emptied. [574] The island drifted through cloud. [579] Each small crane watched from the table as Miro worked, luminous torment in his face — not the torment of a man breaking, but of a man burning slowly and intentionally, feeding himself to the only light that mattered. [617]

By morning the stone was perfect, its interior holding thirty years of dreamed warmth, and Miro carried it up through the basilica and fitted it into a lantern he had built for exactly this purpose, a lantern no client had commissioned, built in the secret hours after grief, built for transformation. [668] He hung it at the center of the vaulted glass. [678] Light moved. [680] He picked up a crane, held it a moment, and set it down again, lighter. [695]

Outside, the floating island continued its slow revolution through morning air, and the glass basilica threw sixty colors across every surface, one of them new, one of them hers. [724]
