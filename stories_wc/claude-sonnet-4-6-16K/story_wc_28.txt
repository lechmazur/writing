Sura came to the plaza every morning after the great fire, even when ash still drifted like gray confetti across the cracked flagstones. [23] The sky above had fractured during the worst of the burning, its dome of smoke crystallized into something permanent and strange, a ceiling of shattered amber that neither admitted full light nor granted full darkness. [58] People called her a gentle radical, which meant, in the neighborhood's private language, that she would argue the foundation from beneath your certainties and then offer her coat while you stood shivering in the rubble. [93]

She carried in her coat pocket a single tessera from the minaret mosaic, a blue-green tile the size of her thumbnail, all that remained of the tower after the fire had consumed its wooden frame and dropped its decorated skin across the plaza like shed scales. [139] The tessera was smooth on its glazed face and rough on its ceramic back, and she turned it between her fingers the way other people turned worry beads, not for comfort but for memory. [173]

The retrocognitive episodes had started three weeks after the fire. [183] She would be standing at the market or washing her face, and the plaza would flood her mind not as it was but as it had been: the minaret intact, the fountain running, the smell of cardamom from the tea seller's cart. [225] The doctors had a long name for it. [233] She preferred to think of herself as retrocognitively trapped, pinned like a specimen between two slides of time, the before pressing its face against the glass of the after. [262]

She was there, on the morning that mattered, when she found the man. [275] He was sitting against the half-standing arch at the plaza's eastern edge, his burns still healing in a way that told her he had been inside the fire, not fleeing it. [306] His name, the neighborhood knew, was Cassim, and the neighborhood was not wrong about what he had done. [324] He had meant to burn only a document, a deed that wronged his family, but the document had been locked in a room that was locked in a building that decided it preferred ash to silence. [360] Fourteen families had lost their homes. [366] Three had lost more. [370]

Sura sat beside him without preamble, because gentleness in her practice never announced itself. [384] She felt the familiar pull of the retrocognitive episode beginning, the old plaza shimmering at the edges of the new one, but she let it come this time, let it serve her. [416] She looked down at the mosaic stepping stone between them, one of the original plaza tiles that had survived intact enough to still show its pattern, though the fire's heat had chipped its surface into a new language. [454] She read that language slowly, the way she had learned to read everything: without hurry, without the luxury of looking away. [475]

The chipped pattern on the stepping stone showed, in its fractures, the ghost of the geometric design that had once honored the name of this place. [501] The plaza had been called The Court of Answered Questions. [511] After the fire, the city administration had declared it The Memorial of the Fourth Burning, which was accurate and also unbearable. [532]

Sura took the tessera from her pocket and set it on the stepping stone. [546] Via those chipped patterns, via the incomplete geometry that both tiles shared, she felt the two moments of the plaza touch, and she understood what she had come to do. [576] She was going to rename this place, not with bureaucratic authority but with the quiet insistence that was her only kind of power. [599] She was going to rename Cassim, too. [606]

"I am going to call this The Court of Aberrant Mercy," she said aloud, her voice carrying the warm coldness of someone who had weighed every word until the warmth and the cold reached equilibrium. [641] "And you I am going to call Salvage." [649]

Cassim looked at her with the eyes of a man waiting to be told he did not deserve softness. [668] She did not tell him that. [674] She picked up the tessera and pressed it into his unburned palm, because to cradle empathy in arms of steel meant you sometimes had to hand the tender thing directly to the person most likely to crush it, and trust the weight of your own certainty to hold. [722] The shattered sky above them neither brightened nor darkened. [731] The ash kept falling. [735] The name held. [738]
