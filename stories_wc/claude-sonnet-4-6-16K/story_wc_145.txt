Maren Solke had revised forty-three atlases without ever visiting the places she corrected. [13] She fixed other people's errors with the same dry precision she applied to everything - margins, meridians, the small lies cartographers told about elevation. [36] But the atlas she was building now was her own, and it required her to be here, at the edge of things, where the lake had frozen solid enough to walk on and the outpost's iron walls had gone the color of old blood. [80]

She wanted to find where the rivers begin. [88]

Not metaphorically. [90]

The watershed she was mapping had eight tributaries that geographers kept tracing back to separate origins - glacial melt, snowpack, artesian springs - but Maren believed they were wrong. [117] She believed, with the subtly confident certainty of someone who had read ten thousand incorrect maps, that there was a singular convergence point: one source feeding all eight, hidden under the plateau's surface, masked by the violence of spring thaw. [157]

The outpost smelled of rust and old kerosene. [165] She had spread her working sheets across the table and weighted the corners with a tin cup and a polyglot's phrasebook she'd borrowed from the expedition's interpreter before he'd turned back at the tree line. [200] The phrasebook fell open to a dog-ear at the Evenki section - survival vocabulary, mostly. [214] Words for ice thickness. [218] Words for which kind of silence meant danger. [226]

She was learning to read both. [232]

Outside, the lake lay under a sky the color of pewter, and the quiet had that particular quality she'd started to distrust - the quality of a room just before someone speaks. [263] Worrying tranquility, her grandmother used to call it. [271] Not peace. [273] The performance of peace. [277]

She was watching the window when the falcon returned. [286] It came low over the ice, riding a thermal that shouldn't have existed in that cold, and landed on the outpost's exterior rail with a sound like a key dropping on stone. [318] Maren had not sent it anywhere. [324] It belonged to whoever had been here before her - whoever had left the kerosene and the cracked mirror and the outpost log whose last entry read only: found the mouth, not the same as finding the source. [361]

The falcon's return was enough. [366] She took it as a herald of something she hadn't named yet. [378]

She turned back to the window and noticed what she should have noticed an hour ago. [394] The condensation on the glass had formed a pattern - not frost, not the random beading of moisture, but a branching design shaped by the window's hairline crack running up its center. [425] Water followed the crack, spread outward from two points near the top, and descended in eight distinct streams toward the sill. [446] Eight streams. [448] One origin per side, then one origin above those, and at the top of the crack, a single bead of condensation that fed everything below it. [474]

Maren stood very still. [478]

She picked up the phrasebook and looked at the dog-ear. [488] The Evenki word on that page, she now realized, did not mean ice thickness. [502] The interpreter had marked it for a different reason. [511] The word meant the place a river does not know it is already a river. [526]

She pulled her working sheet close and began to draw. [536]

The singular convergence point was not on the plateau. [545] It was under it - a subglacial basin, pressurized, feeding upward through eight fractures in the bedrock, each fracture a future river that had not yet decided what it would become by the time it reached light. [581] The lake was not a destination. [587] It was a lid. [591]

Maren corrected the atlas with three pencil lines and no ceremony. [602] That was her way. [606] She noted the falcon had not moved from the rail. [616] She noted the condensation was already retreating as the interior warmed. [627] She noted the worrying tranquility outside had shifted into something else - a wind beginning to read itself aloud across the ice. [648]

She had been right about forty-three atlases worth of other people's errors. [660] She was not wrong about this. [666] She capped her pencil, set it parallel to the phrasebook's spine, and allowed herself the smallest possible smile - the kind that takes years of being right to earn, and more years still to show. [700]
