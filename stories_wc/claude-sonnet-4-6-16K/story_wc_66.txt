The story famine came quietly, which was the worst thing about it. [12] After years of official silence—when the Ministry declared narrative a civic hazard and burned the library catalogs first, the actual books second—Maren Ossek still reported to the orchestra hall each dawn, still bent over her copying table, still transferred conductor's markings onto fresh score paper with the patience of someone who believed patience was its own argument. [71] She was the diligent orchestra copyist, and she had survived three years of famine by making herself useful to the State Orchestra, which was permitted to play because music without words was considered safe. [105] They were wrong about that. [110]

The ember alcove was what she called the storage cavity behind the boiler, where old performance scores went to age. [130] Mist from the canal outside crept under the loading door each evening and softened the air until the whole space felt like a held breath. [155] She had dragged a stool there, and a small table, and the shortwave radio she had found in a crate of confiscated property whose owner no one had survived to reclaim. [186] At night, the shortwave radio dial glowed amber-green in the dimness—secretly luminous, a color that belonged to no officially sanctioned list. [208] She kept it low, barely above whisper, rotating the dial through frequencies like a woman testing fruit for ripeness. [227]

The pirate broadcast found her first. [233] It came between stations, between static and a government frequency playing approved anthem excerpts: a voice, unhurried, reading aloud. [252] Not news. [254] Not instruction. [256] A story—an old one, about a river that learned to lie about its depth so fishermen would wade in curious rather than afraid. [280] The broadcast lasted four minutes before it dissolved back into hiss. [291]

Maren sat very still. [295] Her pencil had stopped mid-notation. [300] Around her the mist settled and the ember alcove held its breath, and she felt something she recognized from before the famine as hope, though it wore a different face now—thornier, less comfortable, the way hope becomes when it has been pruned hard. [344]

She began to understand her method over the following weeks. [354] The orchestra performed twice monthly. [359] She was the one who copied the parts. [367] She was the only one who saw every instrument's score before any musician did. [381] She began embedding lyrics. [385] Not obviously. [387] The orchestra performed without singers, but copyists could annotate—phrasing notes, breathing reminders, dynamic interpretations. [402] Into these marginal spaces, she wrote fragments: phrases that sounded like instructions but assembled, if you held them in sequence across an evening's program, into something else entirely. [430] A sentence. [432] A scene. [434] The fisherman. [436] The river. [438] A child who swallowed a word and grew tall. [447]

She was cloaking the stories in the music's notation the way a moth's wing cloaks its own eye—hiding information inside the thing that carries it, trusting that transport and message were, under scrutiny, one body. [483] The pirate broadcasters, she had deduced, were musicians or former musicians. [494] They received the scores she copied. [500] They heard what was embedded. [505] They assembled, translated, broadcast. [509]

She never confirmed this. [513] The thistled calm she had cultivated these three years depended on not pulling at threads, on knowing when a thing was working and leaving it alone. [539] What she allowed herself was the ember alcove, the dial glow, and the four minutes each broadcast lasted before dissolving. [559] She listened to stories travel back to her from the voice on the frequency—stories she had carried out in scattered syllables, reassembled by strangers into something a person could hold. [590]

To teach dust to tell stories, she had decided, you had to be willing to become dust yourself—scattered, unrecognizable, present everywhere in the dimness. [615] The mist understood this. [619] The amber-green dial understood this. [624] The famine continued officially. [628] Unofficially, something was being fed. [633]
