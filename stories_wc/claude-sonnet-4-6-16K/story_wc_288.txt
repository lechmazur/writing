The library breathed. [3] Not metaphorically -- the shelves expanded with each inhalation of cloud pressure, and contracted when the vapor thinned, and Sela had learned to read its rhythm the way she once read something older. [35] After the reincarnation memory surfaced, she no longer trusted the word once. [47]

It had come on the seventh morning of the library's ascent, when the tidal library above clouds reached its highest drift and the light turned the color of old bone. [77] She had been cataloguing a section on megafauna when the memory cracked open -- brittle, sudden, an awakening so fragile she barely dared breathe through it. [102] She had walked beside a woolly rhinoceros. [109] Not imagined it. [112] Walked beside it, close enough to feel the heat from its flank, across a steppe that existed under no name she could find in any index. [138] The memory lasted eleven seconds before it shattered. [146]

These brittle awakenings were becoming her only honest currency, more reliable than the library's catalog, which shifted every time the tidal pressure rearranged the pneumatic filing tubes overnight. [174] Sela steadied herself against a reading table and reached for the brass sundial she kept in her coat pocket. [193] It was small enough to cup in one palm, atomically reflective in a way that ordinary brass was not -- its surface had been treated by a process she did not fully understand, so that it did not merely bounce light but absorbed it at the molecular threshold and released it altered, tinged with the frequency of the moment the sun had occupied when it struck. [258] In a library with no windows, only cloud-glass panels that admitted diffuse and directionless light, the sundial was technically useless. [278] She carried it anyway. [282] It was the only object she owned that had existed in her previous life. [296]

Below the library, far below, the ancient stone circle sent up its hum. [309] She felt it through the soles of her feet, a vibration that climbed the support columns and arrived in her body as something between sound and memory. [336] The circle regulated the library's tidal ascent and descent -- the engineers called it a resonance anchor, though no one living knew who had built it or how, only that without the hum, the library would drift past the upper atmosphere and not return. [379]

Sela pressed her thumb to the sundial's atomically reflective face and watched it absorb the gray ambient light and release it as something almost golden. [404] She thought about the rhinoceros. [409] She thought about what it meant to be the only person in the library who carried a living memory of a creature no longer found on any continent, except in museum bones and in eleven-second windows that broke apart the moment she pressed too hard against them. [456]

It would be easy to call that isolation. [464] She had called it that for years. [471] But isolation implied the world had left her behind, and she was beginning to understand the correction: she had simply arrived early to a knowledge that the world had not yet caught up to. [505]

The brass sundial warmed against her palm. [512] The hum of the ancient stone circle shifted a quarter-tone lower, signaling the library's afternoon descent, and the shelves exhaled around her. [534] She set the sundial on the reading table and let it stand upright. [547] It cast no shadow and showed no hour, and still it managed to coexist with the library's impossible architecture -- the tidal rhythms, the shifting catalogs, the hum from below the world -- without losing what it was. [583]

That was the lesson she had been failing to locate in eleven-second fragments. [596] To translate isolation into independence did not require forgetting that she had once pressed close to a creature the world had agreed to call extinct. [621] It required only letting that knowledge stand in whatever light existed, and being present in the archive she currently inhabited without the waiting posture of someone who expected rescue. [650]

The library descended. [653] The stone circle hummed. [657] Sela picked up the sundial, returned it to her pocket, and walked deeper into the stacks, where books on vanished species waited with a patience she was finally learning to match. [688]
