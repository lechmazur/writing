Vera called herself a practical mystic, though others who knew her work used the term esoteric realist -- someone who read omens the way an engineer reads stress tests, with rigor and without apology. [33] She had climbed for three days to reach the grotto, a deep green hollow carved into the mountain's shoulder above the cloud line, where moss grew in silence and the air tasted of cold mineral and rain that had not yet fallen. [75] She arrived during the migration of the last monarch butterfly. [85] Not the last migration -- the last butterfly. [92] Scientists had confirmed it: one remaining female, tracked by a sensor smaller than a postage stamp, moving north along a corridor of memory her species had inscribed into the world over ten thousand years. [126] Vera had read the reports with the same attention she gave to medieval land surveys and the handwritten margins of forgotten census rolls. [149] She had made this climb because of those margins. [158] Because someone had to. [162]

The grotto breathed with a blushing stillness -- not silence exactly, but the quality of a room just after music stops, when the air still holds the shape of sound. [193] Ferns pressed their fronds against slick stone. [200] Thin waterfalls threaded down the far wall like sentences trailing off mid-clause. [212]

Vera set her pack down and removed the moth wing first. [223] She had found it at the base of the mountain, powder-blue and edged in rust, intact except for one small notch near the leading margin. [248] An interrupted cadence, she thought -- a stroke begun and then declined, like a name half-spoken before the speaker thought better of it. [272] She placed the wing at the center of the grotto's mossy floor. [284]

Then she began to build. [289] This was her method: building temporary structures as signals. [298] She had done it in tidal flats, in burned forest clearings, in the rubble of demolished buildings. [314] She gathered stones, lichen-covered and cold, and stacked them into a small cairn. [327] She stripped thin branches from the brush at the grotto's edge and lashed them together with grass she had gathered on the way up, pulling each knot tight with the practiced tension of someone who understood that a knot is both a binding and a mark -- a record that two separate things once met here. [387] She tied smaller bundles to the branches, each one representing a name from the census margins she had memorized: women listed only as wife of, children recorded only as child of, men whose occupations had been crossed out and replaced with nothing. [430]

The structure would last a week, maybe two, before rain and wind reduced it to a suggestion. [447] That was the point. [451] It was transiently rooted, like the butterfly herself, like every act of witness that history fails to archive. [468] The purpose was not permanence. [473] The purpose was the signal -- the brief bright interruption in the ordinary frequency of a place that had never been asked to remember anything at all. [499]

She worked until the light changed from white to amber. [509] Below the grotto's lip, the cloud layer shifted and occasionally parted, revealing distances that made the body feel both enormous and forgotten. [531]

She was nearly finished when she saw it. [539] The monarch butterfly cleared the cloud edge and rose into the grotto's calm air with a slowness that seemed impossible, each wingbeat a deliberate comma in a sentence that had been interrupted for decades. [573] Orange and black, the colors of warning and of autumn both. [584] It circled once, as though reading the structure Vera had built. [595] It landed on the moth wing at the center of the grotto floor and stayed there for a long moment, wings opening and closing in the blushing stillness like a breath being taken carefully. [629]

Vera did not move. [633] She understood, in the way only an esoteric realist can -- simultaneously skeptical and certain -- that she was witnessing the end of a very long sentence, and that her small knotted structure had given it, briefly, a place to rest before the period fell. [677] She counted the names she had tied into the branches. [687] There were forty-three. [690]

The butterfly rose, crossed the grotto's far wall, and disappeared into the cloud. [703] Vera sat beside the moth wing until dark, and she did not feel alone. [717]
