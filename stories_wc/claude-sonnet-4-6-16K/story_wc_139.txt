The prep room at Holloway's Funeral Home smelled of formaldehyde and cold metal, but Mara Voss had long since stopped noticing. [22] She was her father's daughter in every way except the one that mattered: she had never cheated anyone, not once, not for anything. [46] Her father had built a career on beautiful lies, forging papers for anyone with money enough to pay his rate. [66] She had built hers on the opposite principle, which was why the man lying on her table was not, technically, dead. [88]

His name was Drevin, and he had been delivered to Holloway's in a body bag by men who needed him to disappear. [111] He had been a minister in a government that no longer existed, and the new one wanted him returned. [130] Three consulates had refused his application for political asylum, citing procedural irregularities that were, Mara understood, entirely manufactured. [149] The bureaucracy had made a wall of itself, and Drevin had no ladder. [162]

She set her father's vintage ink pot on the steel tray beside his head. [176] The pot was small and brown, the glass gone nearly black with decades of pigment soaked into its walls. [196] Her father had used it to sign other men's names to other men's documents, and it had made him wealthy and eventually imprisoned. [220] She had kept it not as tribute but as reminder: ink was just a tool. [234] The hand holding it decided everything. [240]

Drevin opened one eye. [244] "You're the forger's kid," he said. [250]

"I'm the honest one," she said. [256]

He almost smiled. [259] He had the look of a man who had been surprised by worse things than her, which in a prep room meant he had lived a genuinely extraordinary life. [290]

What she was attempting required no forgery. [297] She had contacted the fourth consulate three weeks ago, a smaller office overlooked in Drevin's previous attempts, and submitted a legitimate application with every document in order. [325] Then she had waited, which is not the same as doing nothing. [336] Waiting actively meant she filed follow-up inquiries every forty-eight hours, each one worded differently, each one applying gentle and tireless pressure against the wall of bureaucratic inertia. [364] It meant she sat in this cold room with a living man and read case law by the light above the embalming table. [386] It meant she answered every delay with a question and every silence with a fact. [401]

The ink pot was for this moment only. [409] She uncapped it and dipped the nib of a pen inside, and the smell of it rose between them like something old deciding to matter again. [435] She had drafted a private agreement between herself and Drevin, a document with no legal standing anywhere, but one she intended to honor absolutely. [457] He would receive her full assistance. [463] He would, once free, give testimony to the international commission investigating the ministry's collapse, testimony he had been withholding because he was afraid no one would protect him in exchange. [494]

"This is the mathematics of a fair trade," she told him, setting the paper on his chest. [511] "Not a gift. Not a con. An exchange of equal weight." [523]

She watched him read it. [528] The atmosphere of the room did something to people, she had noticed, the cold, the steel, the proximity of genuine finality. [552] It liquefied rigid thinking. [556] Men who arrived convinced of their positions often left with those positions dissolved into something more honest. [572] The prep room was a place where what mattered became very clear very fast. [585]

Drevin signed. [587] She signed. [589] The ink went dark and then darker, settling into the page like a thing that intended to stay. [610]

The fourth consulate called the following morning, while Drevin was eating soup at her kitchen table, alive and smelling of the living world. [634] The asylum application had been approved. [640] She had found the breach not by forcing anything open but by being the one person in the room still willing to wait. [661]

Her father, she thought, would have found a faster way. [671] He also would have sold Drevin twice by now. [681]

She washed the ink pot under cold water and set it on the windowsill to dry in the thin morning light, which was not sentimental but simply practical. [710] She would need it again. [715] There were always people who needed someone willing to do the honest, difficult, slow work of making a fair exchange possible in a world that had decided fairness was naive. [748] She had been raised by a man who believed exactly that, and it had taught her the opposite. [765] The ink pot caught the light and held it, dark glass suddenly luminous, dead-looking and completely full of use. [783]
