[69] He was a small man, seventy or older, with fingers patiently nimble the way old watchmakers' fingers are - trained into precision without urgency. [93] He gave her the sugar and she gave back a question about the seal. [107]

"Sumerian," he said. [110] "The administrative dialect of a civilization that has been dead for four thousand years." [124] He said it the way other men might say the weather, with the mild tone of someone describing a familiar neighbor. [145]

By the third Tuesday he had stopped pretending to be surprised. The rooftop smelled of thyme and rust. [212] Mara had grown up in this building, left, returned, and still could not name the particular sound the structure made at night - a low, almost deliberate settling, like a man shifting in a leather chair. [248] It was this sound she had been trying to understand her whole life. [261]

"Buildings have a grammar," Hector told her one evening, pressing the desk seal's face gently into a square of wax he kept for no reason he had explained. [290] "Load-bearing walls carry stress the way a sentence carries a verb. [301] Remove it and everything collapses into noise." [308]

[333] What the university had not understood - what she sensed even before she could articulate it - was that Hector did not teach languages so much as he taught buildings. [362] Or rather: every language he had ever studied had eventually led him to stone, to weight, to the grammar of old structures that had outlasted their speakers. [390]

"What I want," she told him plainly, the sixth or seventh Tuesday, "is to learn how to read what this building is saying." [413]

The meteor shower arrived in August without announcing itself. [489] Hector watched with his hands folded, perfectly still. [497]

"The building was constructed in 1911," he said, not looking down. [508] "The men who built it were from Palermo. [550] "When you hear the building settle, you are hearing all of those voices at once." [565]
