The junkyard prophet arrived at the garden's edge carrying only a piece of plain chalk and the feeling that she had forgotten something enormous. [24] The fog lay across the sculptures like gauze over old photographs, softening the stone figures into suggestions of themselves. [43] She had been here before, she knew, because the grief of it was familiar, the way a melody is familiar even when you cannot name it. [69]

She was precognitively forgetful, which meant she saw the future first and lost it before the present caught up. [88] Visions arrived like tide -- full, rushing, then gone -- leaving only the wet impression of something that had mattered. [106] What remained was always an echo, and she had spent years clinging to those echoes the way a sailor clings to wreckage, certain something solid had once been there. [135]

Tonight she needed to locate the source. [142] Not a place exactly, but a sound -- the specific, unrepeatable sound of growing older. [156] She had heard it once in a vision, a low register beneath everything, like the hum inside an old television, and then she had forgotten it the way she forgot all true things before they fully arrived. [193]

She began arranging driftwood she had carried in her coat pockets, piece by piece, along the garden path. [211] Each length of pale wood pointed forward, a directional marker aimed at wherever the sound lived. [227] The fog thickened around her as she worked, and the sculptures -- a woman reaching, a child mid-sprint, a man bowed at the waist -- emerged and dissolved in the gray with each small shift of wind. [262]

The garden felt, tonight, like the inside of a room where old videotapes sat unwatched on their shelves. [280] That particular hush, dense with things that had been recorded but never played back, pressed against her ears. [298] Whole lives in magnetic ribbon, whole seasons of voice and motion, sealed in plastic cases growing brittle. [315] She understood that hush because she carried her own version of it -- futures she had witnessed and could no longer access, recorded somewhere inside her, inaccessible. [341]

The chalk came out when she reached the garden's center, a clearing she recognized by the way the fog here moved differently, rotating slowly as if the air breathed. [370] She knelt and pressed the chalk against the stone path and began to write, not words but shapes -- arcs, nested circles, the shorthand she had developed for feelings that language could not hold. [403] This was how she held on. [409] Not to the visions themselves but to their residue, their pressure, the outline they had left when they passed through her and kept going. [433]

A sound reached her then. [438] Low and tidal, full of weight the way water is full of weight. [451] It was the sound of a door closing in a house where children had grown and moved away. [469] It was the sound of recognizing your own handwriting from twenty years ago and not quite believing you were that person. [490] It was continuous, unhurried, and indifferent in the way that only real things are indifferent -- not cruel, simply ongoing. [509]

She wrote faster, the chalk scraping its pale marks across the stone, trying to preserve not the sound itself but the architecture of how it felt to receive it. [538] She knew she would forget this too. [545] The knowing was already receding, even as the sound surrounded her, because that was what it meant to be what she was. [567] She saw and then she held and then she let go, over and over, like breathing, like tides. [585]

The last driftwood marker had led her here. [593] She pressed her palm flat against her fresh chalk marks and held the cold stone long enough to feel her heartbeat in it. [616] The fog moved. [619] The sculpted woman kept reaching. [624] The sound moved through the garden and through her and continued on without her, the way time does -- the way time always has and always will -- and she stayed kneeling on the stone, grateful to have been briefly in its path. [665]
