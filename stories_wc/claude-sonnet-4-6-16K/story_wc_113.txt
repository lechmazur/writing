The water came in slowly, the way grief does. [10] Mara had noticed it first three weeks ago, when the Aldermoor Opera House flooded after the levee cracked and the restoration committee abandoned the project entirely. [46] She was the only one who kept returning. [54]

She was a violinist, or had been, until the orchestra dissolved with the building's closure and left her with callused fingers and no stage beneath them. [82] But lately she had been inspired by something she could not name, something that pulled her back through the stage door each night with her instrument tucked under her arm. [113]

The pit was half-submerged now, the water black and still as a held breath. [128] Lanterns hung from the old rigging above the stage, their flames fed by some small miracle of gas that had not yet been shut off, and they threw orange light across the surface of the water in long, wavering columns. [170] She stood on the highest platform of the pit, the last dry ledge, and looked out at the ruin of it. [193]

She had found the program floating near the conductor's stand on her second visit. [207] It was antique, printed on heavy cream stock, for a production of "The Drowned Maiden" performed in 1912. [225] The water damage had done something extraordinary to it. [234] Where moisture had crept between the fibers of the page, it left images, dark branching shapes that looked nothing like accident. [254] They looked like notation. [258]

Mara had studied them for days, rotating the program under candlelight in her apartment, convinced she was imagining things. [277] But tonight she had brought it back to the source, back to the flooded house, and beneath the lantern glow the images sharpened. [301]

They were not notes exactly, not any system she had been trained to read. [315] They lived in between spaces, occupying the gaps between staves that were never printed, the silence between phrases, the held pause before a downbeat. [340] Music she had never been taught to hear. [348]

She set the program on the ledge before her and raised her violin. [361]

She did not know why she was certain the message belonged to a ghost. [375] She only knew that the program bore the name of a violinist in the pit roster, one Elena Voss, and that Elena Voss had drowned in this house during the flood of 1913, one year after the program was printed. [416] The code, if that was the right word, felt like Elena's voice reaching across the in-between space where the dead and the living almost touched. [443]

Mara played the first shape. [448]

It was not a note she could place on any scale. [459] It sat between E and F, a microtone the instrument produced only reluctantly, and when she drew it out, the water in the pit shivered. [483] She stopped. [485]

The silence that followed was not empty. [492] It was the hush of unstruck chords waiting inside wood and wire, inside the room itself, and it pressed against her ears like a second presence. [519]

She played the next shape. [524]

And the next. [527]

Slowly, the pattern began to disrupt the stillness she had accepted as the room's nature. [542] Something in the acoustic bones of the old house answered, a resonance in the walls, a vibration in the water that sent small ripples breaking against the submerged seats in rhythmic pulses. [575] The lanterns swayed together. [579]

It was a wordless conversation, nothing Mara could transcribe or explain, but she understood it the way she understood music at its most urgent, through the body before the mind. [610] Elena was not asking to be remembered. [617] She was asking to be finished. [623]

The final shape on the program was the longest, a spreading stain that covered half the page and branched into something that resembled, unmistakably, a resolution. [651] Mara played it slowly, letting the pitch bend where the damage bent, letting the sound find the room. [669]

The lanterns steadied. [672]

The water went flat. [676]

Mara lowered her bow and stood in the silence of a chord that had finally struck. [692] She did not feel like a ghost had left. [702] She felt like she had been in the in-between space herself all this time, and something had just, quietly, let her out. [725]

She tucked the antique program under her arm beside her violin case and walked out through the dark door into the ordinary night. [748]
