The sapphire viaduct arched over the empty river at the first gray light before sunrise, its stone smooth and cold beneath Aldric's boots. [23] He was not much of a mage. [30] His instructors had called him lukewarm with the charitable precision of people delivering a verdict. [45] He could not conjure flame or summon wind. [53] What he could do was feel the weight of things slightly out of balance, the way a man with bad knees feels weather changing. [77]

The last fish had been caught three days prior. [86] Not the last fish in a lake or a stream, but the last fish anywhere, the final silver body pulled from the world's water by a trawler captain who did not understand what he was doing. [122] Aldric knew, because the knowing was the one talent no one had bothered to take from him. [138] The river beneath the viaduct had gone still and silent as arithmetic. [150]

He set the broken ship's bell on the stone railing and studied it. [163] The bell had cracked along its shoulder during some forgotten storm, and its clapper was gone, but that was fine. [182] He had not brought it to ring it. [190] He had brought it because water collected inside it through the crack, pooling in the curved basin of the bell's interior and then dripping out through a hairline fracture at the bottom. [222] Aldric had spent two days understanding dripping rates, counting each drop against the pulse in his own wrist, mapping the intervals with a patience that surprised even him. [251] At two drops per breath in cold air, with the river humidity at dawn, the bell drained in forty-one minutes. [271] At three drops per breath in the dry heat of afternoon, it drained in twenty-six. [287] The pattern was not random. [292] It corresponded to something he could not name yet, only feel, like the imbalance that had been building since before he was born. [315]

His devotion to this project was, he understood, brittle. [324] Not in his own heart, but in the structure of it: one wrong step and the whole thing would shatter, because it rested on premises no senior mage would dignify with a response. [357] He had written to three of them. [364] Two had not replied. [368] One had replied with a single word that was not appropriate for correspondence. [381]

But Aldric was earnestly naive in the way that the very young and the very determined sometimes are, not blind to the odds, but simply unable to stop. [410] He believed, with the fragile certainty of someone who has nothing else to believe, that the dripping bell was a key. [430]

The cosmic imbalance was not metaphor. [436] When the last fish vanished, something that had been held in tension for ten thousand years finally slipped. [453] The world was tilting toward a silence that would spread from water to air to soil. [469] To right it required reintroducing a frequency, a specific oscillation that living water had always carried inside it and that was now absent. [491] The bell, cracked and toneless, still remembered that frequency in the geometry of its curve. [505] The dripping rate at precisely this temperature, this humidity, this elevation above the riverbed, produced intervals that matched the lost oscillation within a margin Aldric had calculated to be acceptable. [535]

He did not have enough power to rebuild the frequency from nothing. [547] But he did not need to. [553] He only needed to introduce the pattern at the right moment, the way you can restart a stopped heart with a small and careful shock rather than a large and careless one. [586]

Dawn arrived the way it always did on the viaduct, blue fading to gold, the stone warming under his hands. [604] He placed both palms against the bell and let his lukewarm magic flow into it, not blazing, not transformative, just present and steady, a match held to a wick. [634]

The bell did not ring. [639] The water dripped. [642] The interval was correct. [646]

Below the viaduct, thirty feet of still water shivered once, and in the shiver, something moved. [661] It was small and silver and almost certainly impossible. [670] Aldric watched it breach the surface for a moment before it slipped back under. [684] He exhaled. [686] He picked up the bell and held it against his chest like a man holds something he has no name for yet. [707]

He would need to do it again tomorrow. [715] And the day after. [719] But the bell was still intact, and so was he. [729] For now, that was enough. [734] That was the most a lukewarm mage could reasonably ask. [744]
