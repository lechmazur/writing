Sera had always known the future would come, but not like this -- not three seasons early, not while the cobalt glade still held its summer warmth. [26] The sky had shifted wrong, the light gone indigo where it should have been gold, and every root beneath her bare feet was singing. [50] She was a root-bound visionary, which meant she could see what was coming but could not chase it. [68] The earth held her ankles like a mother holds a child at a cliff's edge -- firmly, without apology. [86] She had never left the glade in forty years. [95] But tonight the glade had changed shape around her, the familiar oaks pressing closer, the dusk pooling like spilled ink between their trunks, and she understood that the intersection of dreams she had spent her life seeking had finally agreed to come to her instead. [140]

In her left hand she held a maze topiary clipping, four inches of trimmed yew, its cut end still weeping green. [161] She had received it by messenger that morning: no note, just the sprig and the smell of somewhere old. [180] To anyone else it was yard waste. [187] To Sera it was a key, because she recognized the clipping as coming from the great hedge maze at Vellanthorpe, where, sixty years ago, her grandmother and a woman named Orin Dass had made an eclipsed covenant -- a promise spoken during a total solar eclipse, sealed in the particular darkness that exists only when the sun is swallowed whole. [246] The covenant had required that their bloodlines would share vision, share burden, share the knowledge of when. [263] But Orin Dass had vanished, and the covenant had sat in shadow ever since, its terms unmet, its power quietly compounding interest. [285]

Now Sera was purposefully curious in the way only the desperate can manage: not frantic, but leaning toward every detail with the full weight of her attention, the way a plant leans toward the one window in a dim room. [325] She turned the clipping in her fingers and heeded its faint warmth, following the sensation up her wrist, past her elbow, to the long pale scar on her inner forearm -- the one she had carried since childhood, the one her grandmother said marked her as a keeper of the sight. [375] The scar tingled. [378] Then it burned. [381]

Across the glade, stepping between two cobalt-shadowed oaks as though she had been walking toward this exact point for decades -- because she had -- came a young woman with a matching scar on her own forearm, visible even in the failing light. [422] The stranger's face was the face from Sera's oldest visions: sharp-jawed, wide-eyed, breathing hard. [436]

This was how the linking worked. [442] Not through ceremony or chant, but linking through scars -- the old covenant had written itself into skin, and skin remembered what minds forgot. [465] Sera extended her arm. [469] The young woman, Orin Dass's granddaughter, pressed her scar against Sera's without hesitation. [482]

The glade filled with anxious elation, a feeling like standing at a high window just before you understand you will not fall. [504] Sera saw what the young woman carried in her mind: the same dream, the long corridor with two paths converging, the light at the junction that belonged to neither of them alone. [536] They had each been walking half a map. [544]

"The future came early," the young woman said. [552] Her voice was steadier than her eyes. [559] "It always does," Sera answered, "when enough people have been dreaming it simultaneously." [572]

The clipping in Sera's hand dissolved into warm sap between their joined arms. [585] The eclipsed covenant, finally witnessed by the correct inheritors, finally released its held breath. [599] The roots beneath Sera's feet loosened, not freeing her, but acknowledging that she had done what the ground had kept her here to do. [623]

They stood together in the cobalt glade as the last of the dusk burned away, two women at the intersection of dreams, both of them trembling, both of them ready. [653]
