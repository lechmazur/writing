Vera wakes when the fever finally breaks, and the mountain is still dark outside the weather station's single window. [19] The thermometer on the wall reads minus twelve. [27] She has been lying on the cot for three days, peripherally aware of the barometric instruments clicking around her, their small readouts cycling through pressures she recorded in her ledger and then forgot. [60] The oracle trade demands this kind of fault tolerance -- the ability to absorb error, recalibrate, and continue issuing readings even when the instrument doing the reading is sick and shaking. [90] She knows this about herself the way she knows the mountain: not by looking directly, but by accumulating evidence at the edges. [112]

On the small table beside the cot, her predecessor had left a botanical illustration -- a hand-drawn rendering of alpine saxifrage, its petals precise and credible, its roots held in cross-section below the soil line. [146] The illustrator's name was scratched in the lower margin: Thomas Calwell, 1961. [158] Vera has studied this image every morning for four years, and it still tells her things. [174] Thomas had died on this mountain, in another station, in another winter. [186] She had known him only through the illustration and the log entries he left, which were meticulous and honest, which is to say they were full of uncertainty. [214]

She swallowed a truth about Thomas in her second year here, when she found his final entry written not in the main log but in pencil, along the bottom margin where the binding's shadow made it easy to miss. [253] It said: *I have been wrong about the ridge pressure for eleven months.* [266] *The readings were never faulty.* [271] *I was afraid.* [274] She had read it and closed the book and never transcribed it. [286] That was the swallowing. [290]

Now, after the fever, she wants to find the weight of it -- to finally understand what it costs to hold a true thing inside you without letting it breathe. [319] She sits up slowly and pulls the botanical illustration from the table. [331] The auroral light is beginning at the northern horizon, faint green and reluctant, as if sorrow itself had learned to glow. [352] In that light, the saxifrage petals look bruised. [360]

She picks up her grease pencil. [366] By occupying margins the way Thomas had, she begins to write in the lower border of the illustration, under his name, in the narrow corridor between the image and the paper's edge. [398] She writes: *The certainty we perform is not the certainty we hold.* [410] But her hand is still weak from the fever and the grease pencil drags unevenly, and the letters begin to smudge before she finishes the line, one word bleeding into the next, until the sentence looks more like weather than language -- which, she thinks, may be appropriate. [457]

The swaying certainties of an oracle's life are not comfortable things. [468] You learn to stand in the middle of a wind that does not resolve, to keep your instruments calibrated while your convictions shift underfoot. [492] Thomas had known this. [496] His error was not in the readings -- it was in believing that admitting uncertainty would invalidate the years of careful work. [517] It would not have. [521] It would have made them more honest, which is the only kind of reliable that matters in the long run. [541]

Outside, the weather station's anemometer begins to spin. [549] A front is moving down from the northwest, and Vera can feel it in her ears before the instruments confirm it -- that peripheral awareness again, the body knowing before the mind admits. [581] She sets the illustration down. [586] The smudged sentence sits in the margin like a bruise, or a seal, or an apology offered sideways because no other angle was available. [610] She opens the main log, finds the blank page waiting, and writes Thomas's confession into the official record at last, sixty-three years late, the weight of the swallowed truth finally given somewhere to land. [644]

The aurora deepens. [647] The anemometer spins faster. [651] Vera picks up her pencil and begins to note the pressure, the wind speed, the temperature -- recording what she knows, and what she does not, and where the margin between them lies. [683]
