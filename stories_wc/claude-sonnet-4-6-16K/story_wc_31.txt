Maren kept her post at the end of Caithe Pier, trimming wicks and raising glass shields against the salt wind, but she had been planning this descent for three seasons. [30] The hotel skeleton key tag had arrived in a dead man's coat pocket — Room 14, the brass plate read, from the Aldenmoor Grand, burned in 1943. [56] She had found his coat snagged on a piling, and the key tag had felt immediately like something meant to signify direction, a small brass tongue saying *go down*. [85]

Tonight was the sky lantern release — the annual grief ritual the harbor town performed for its drowned. [102] Hundreds of paper shells, each carrying a candle heart, lifted from the promenade while Maren walked the opposite direction: toward the iron ladder bolted to the pier's underside, toward the cold water, toward the wreck of the *Seraphel*, which had carried a municipal library to the seafloor in the winter of 1908. [155]

She had her lamp and her rebreather. [162] She had the key tag knotted around her wrist because she had made luminous vows to herself on three separate nights — promises lit by her own lanterns, witnessed only by dark water — and she believed objects should be present when debts were honored. [206]

The *Seraphel*'s hull had cracked along its spine, opening a corridor wide enough to enter. [221] Maren descended by wandering purposefully, moving neither frantically nor casually, following the logic of the wreck the way a person follows a known grief: with direction, without hurry. [251] The light from her lamp softened at thirty feet, turning the silt gold. [264]

The library was still a library. [270] That was the first shock. [275] Shelves had tilted and many books had dissolved to silt, but the architecture of scholarship remained — the reading tables, the card catalog in its wooden chest, even a globe still caged in its meridian rings, hovering slightly off-axis as though in quiet argument with the changed world. [323]

*She hides behind truth*, Maren had read in a poem she could no longer source. [338] She had thought it meant cowardice dressed as honesty. [347] Now, in the drowned library, she understood it differently: that truth is large enough to stand behind, that a person can use it as shelter without it being a lie. [379] Her luminous vows had been true, and she had hidden inside them for three years, telling herself she was simply waiting for the right moment to go down. [409]

Above her, the sky lanterns were rising. [416] She could not see them, but she felt them — felt the way the water changed texture, felt the world exhaling collectively above the surface. [440] She had come here to feel exactly that. [448] To discover the color of sound, which she believed existed, which she believed her body would register if she placed it at the precise coordinates of grief and wonder simultaneously. [480]

She opened the card catalog. [485] The entries were illegible, dissolved to suggestions of ink. [494] She pulled one small card free and held it in the lamp's glow, and what she held was not information but a kind of texture — the memory of order, of someone who had believed that naming things would help. [534]

The key tag at her wrist. [540] Room 14. [542] Someone had stayed there, had passed through that numbered door into sleep and morning, had believed in the hotel's promise of return. [564] She pressed the brass plate against the ruined card, and in the meteor hush of the deep water — that held-breath silence between one thing ending and another arriving — she felt the color of sound as amber. [602] Not heard, not seen, but registered somewhere between her sternum and her spine: amber, slow and patient as light through old glass. [624]

She had signified nothing to anyone but herself. [632] That was, she understood now, exactly enough. [639]

Maren ascended slowly, letting the pressure equalize, letting the amber find whatever permanent place revelations go. [655] Above, the last lanterns were fading into the low clouds, small fires becoming dark. [669] She climbed the iron ladder, pulled off her mask, and stood on the pier in the cold salt air. [688] Then she lit every lamp she was responsible for, each one in turn, precise and unhurried, keeping her vows. [707]
