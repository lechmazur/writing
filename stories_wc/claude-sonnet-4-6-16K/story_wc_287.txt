Mara counted petals the way other people counted losses -- carefully, without sentiment, and with the full understanding that the number would eventually run out. [24] She lived on the fourth floor of the Salthorn Building, where the harbor breathed silver light on clear nights and the fire escape ran along the building's eastern face like a wrought-iron spine. [57] Over six years she had turned that iron lattice into a garden -- terracotta pots crowded every landing, herbs spilled through the grating, and one ancient rosebush, transplanted from her father's coastal cottage, had somehow rooted itself in a half-barrel planter and bloomed every summer since. [101] Tonight, after the final petal dropped from the old rosebush -- a dark red thing spiraling down toward the alley below -- Mara exhaled and let the season close. [129]

She reached into her apron pocket and closed her fingers around the lockmaster's bitting gauge tooth, a worn steel instrument her father had carried for forty years. [156] He had managed the canal locks three miles north, raising and lowering water to move boats between elevations, and he had always kept the gauge tooth as a kind of talisman -- a tool for reading the secret geometry inside a lock. [196] When he died she did not know what to do with it, so she kept it in her apron and sometimes pressed its cold angles against her palm when she needed to think. [230]

The harbor below was luminous by moonlight, the water quilted with long ribbons of silver and the soft amber of the pier lanterns. [253] A quivering hush had settled over everything -- the kind of silence that is not empty but full, the way a held breath is full. [277] Mara leaned against the iron railing and looked down. [286]

She could see the window of apartment 2B directly beneath her. [297] The light inside was off, and had been off for three weeks, ever since the young man named Felix had moved in and then seemed to disappear into his own rooms, curtains drawn, no sound except occasional footsteps at odd hours. [338] She had left a bundle of thyme at his door once and received no response. [353]

Mara was wistfully pragmatic about human suffering -- she understood it deeply and did not pretend otherwise, but she also believed that small gestures, repeated with patience, could accomplish what grand ones rarely did. [386] She believed in this the way she believed in container gardening: limited space, limited soil, and still, if you were consistent, things grew. [409] She had been thinking for days about how to instill something in Felix -- not hope exactly, which felt presumptuous, but the raw material of it. [434] A signal that the world outside his curtains was not entirely indifferent. [446] Her motivation was simple, almost architectural: to reflect light into dark corners, because she had once lived in one herself and understood that the darkness is not the absence of light so much as the absence of a surface for it to land on. [490]

She began moving the smaller pots. [496] She worked with the rhythm of oars in water -- steady, unhurried, each motion completing itself before the next began. [515] She repositioned the white-flowering alyssum to the edge of the railing directly above his window, then the pale lavender sedum, then a tin of marigolds that caught moonlight like struck coins. [546] She angled each pot so that on clear nights, when the harbor threw its silver upward and the moon came full, the blossoms would catch that light and scatter it down toward 2B. [579] She used the bitting gauge tooth to loosen the soil in each pot as she worked, its tip tracing small furrows. [600] It was not the right tool for the job, but it was the one she had, and she thought her father would have understood that -- a lockmaster knew better than anyone that the right key and the available key are rarely the same thing. [644]

When she finished, she stood back and assessed. [652] The rearranged pots hung over the darkness of his window like a small lantern, modest and perishable and real. [671]

The harbor hush held. [675] Below, the curtain in 2B shifted -- just slightly, just enough -- and Mara, who was not a sentimental woman but was a precise one, noted the change and felt something that was not quite triumph but was close to it: the quiet satisfaction of a lock that has finally found its key. [726] She put the bitting gauge tooth back in her apron and went inside. [739]
