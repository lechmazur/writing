Mira had kept the lore for forty years, longer than the digital archives had existed, longer than the chromatic waterfall had pulsed with its engineered spectrum of light. [28] She remembered when the falls were built -- a civic project, they said, a curtain of refracted brilliance meant to honor the old world while announcing the new. [55] Behind that curtain, she had always suspected, lay something older than either. [67]

The lore keeper pressed her palm against the waterfall's shifting membrane, feeling the cold prismatic spray dissolve her hesitation, and sallied through. [90] The passage lasted only a breath, and then she stood inside the hidden orchard. [104] Ancient apple trees twisted upward, their bark laced with bioluminescent moss, their roots threading between cracked stone tiles that hummed faintly with residual power. [129] Fruit still clung to branches -- deep, improbable red -- catching the refracted light from the waterfall behind her like a thousand small fires. [153] Mira exhaled slowly, wisely adventurous enough to catalog the wonder before she moved deeper, because in her experience, the beautiful places were always the dangerous ones. [179]

She had come with a single clue: a ruby flake, no larger than a thumbnail, shaved from the cornerstone of the old civic hall before its demolition. [207] The flake had carried a frequency signature her instruments identified as resonant with something buried beneath this orchard, something the historical record had erased but not destroyed. [235] She drew it from her coat pocket now, and it began to glow against her palm with a low arterial warmth, pulling her attention toward the far wall of the orchard. [266]

There, where the orchard ended, stood a structure she had not anticipated -- a low pavilion, its concrete walls feathered with decades of layered paint, civic murals painted over civic murals, each generation covering the last. [301] Mira understood immediately what she had to do. [309] She pressed her fingernails into the topmost layer and began to peel. [321] It came away in curling sheets, revealing a brighter layer beneath -- revolutionary slogans from a century past, then geometric patterns from a century before that, then, deeper still, something that made her breath stop. [355] Through the peel of paint layers revealing hidden murals, she uncovered a stairway rendered in breathtaking gold and crimson, not painted but inlaid, the pigment itself a language. [383]

The mural showed a throne -- high, unadorned, and empty. [393] She recognized it from the oldest texts she had archived, the ones flagged as mythology, the ones she had quietly believed all along. [416] The unclaimed throne. [419] Every lore keeper before her had catalogued its legend and moved on, satisfied by proximity to the story, unwilling to become part of it. [443] Mira was not satisfied. [447]

The ruby flake burned hotter now, and the mural's stairway began to pulse with the same arterial light, the illustration and the object harmonizing like tuning forks struck together. [476] She understood: the mural was not a representation of the stairway of ascension. [489] It was the door. [493] She pressed the ruby flake against the painted lowest step, and the wall yielded -- not dramatically, not with noise or fire -- but with the quiet dignity of something that had simply been waiting for someone patient enough to find it. [534] The stones folded inward, and real stairs descended beneath the orchard floor. [546]

Mira looked back at the waterfall's kaleidoscopic curtain, still cycling through its spectrum, indifferent and faithful, the old world and the new world performing their endless negotiation. [573] She had until sunrise before the falls shifted to their daytime frequency and the passage behind them sealed for another cycle, so she moved without rushing but also without pause. [601] The stairs were cool and faintly luminous, the air below carrying the scent of stone and circuitry combined, a smell she associated with places that had never fully decided which era they belonged to. [635]

At the bottom, the throne room opened around her, vast and humming, its ceiling lost in dark, its single throne catching light from no visible source. [661] The throne was still empty. [666] She had not come to claim it, she reminded herself -- she had come to understand it, to carry its truth back into the living record. [691] But the ruby flake in her palm had other ideas, and as dawn began to filter down through cracks in the orchard floor above her, Mira found herself climbing the dais, wisely adventurous to the last, not fleeing the weight of what she might become but walking toward it with open eyes.
