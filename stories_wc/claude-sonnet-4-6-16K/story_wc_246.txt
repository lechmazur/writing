Mara had been stationed at the Fold for three years before she understood that gravity here was not a force but a question. [23] The anomaly site sat in a bowl of high desert where the land could not decide how much to hold. [43] Loose stones sometimes drifted to knee height before settling back, ashamed of their ambition. [57] Instruments read contradictory data simultaneously, and the agency had long stopped trying to reconcile the numbers. [73] They sent interpreters instead. [77]

Mara was a celestial signal interpreter, which meant she sat in the tilted observatory and listened to the sky's arguments with itself. [99] Her job was to translate the interference patterns that the anomaly cast upward into the electromagnetic spectrum, the stuttering language of something ancient trying to say something specific. [127] Most interpreters lasted eighteen months before requesting transfer. [135] Mara had stayed because the signals, even when incoherent, felt like company. [147]

The trouble had begun after planting the wrong seeds. [156] She had meant to record a minor correction to the historical signal archive, adjusting timestamps that a previous interpreter had logged in error. [179] Instead, following a chain of contingent relationships between archived events -- this signal cross-referencing that coronal event, which pulled in three atmospheric readings from separate decades -- she had propagated the error forward rather than backward. [213] The archive now believed a particular celestial alignment had occurred seventeen years earlier than it had. [229] History, inside this closed system, had been edited. [237] Not the world's history, she told herself. [244] Just the record. [247] But records were what interpreters lived inside of. [255]

She kept the dried-up fountain pen on her desk because it had belonged to the first interpreter stationed at the Fold. [276] The nib was exotic, a flex nib hand-ground from some alloy no catalog could identify, and it had never taken ink since Mara arrived. [300] She uncapped it each morning anyway. [306] She held it as she worked, the way a person holds a smooth stone -- not for use, but for weight. [326]

Tonight she held it while trying to unbend the corrupted archive thread. [338] The anomaly made the terminal screen ripple at its edges, gravity pulling slightly sideways on the photons themselves, or so she imagined. [360] She was confidently uncertain about this, as she was about most things here. [373] The confidence came from having sat long enough with the uncertainty to know its shape. [388] It no longer frightened her. [393] It was close to a friend. [399]

The method for correcting the archive, she realized slowly, was the same method that had broken it. [416] Through contingent relationships, the error had traveled; through contingent relationships, the correction would have to travel too. [433] She could not simply overwrite the false date. [441] She had to follow every thread that had touched it and let each one gently revise its neighbor, the way a bent wire will unbend only if you work from the middle outward rather than forcing the ends. [479]

She began at the coronal event. [485] Then the atmospheric readings. [489] Then the seventeen upstream timestamps that had learned from the bad one and repeated its mistake like students copying from a wrong answer. [512]

Outside, a loose stone rose to window height and hovered. [522] Mara watched it without stopping her work. [529] This was the strange comfort the Fold offered: nothing here was quite obeying, and so nothing here was quite failing either. [550] Every wrong seed planted in this soil grew into something unexpected but not useless, something that forced you to thresh meaning from experience rather than importing it ready-made from somewhere safer. [581]

By the time the stone dropped back to the earth, the archive thread was straight. [596] She did not know if the correction would hold. [605] She did not know if it mattered, in any scale larger than this tilted room and its crooked little sky. [625]

She capped the pen. [629] The exotic nib caught the light from the screen for a moment, and she thought she could see ink pooled somewhere deep in its tines -- dried so long ago it was almost mineral, almost part of the metal. [667] A record of something written once and not yet erased. [677] She felt, as she always did, that this was enough. [687]
