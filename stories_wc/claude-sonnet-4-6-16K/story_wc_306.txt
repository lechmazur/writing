Maren arrived at the lighthouse before the last campfire spark died on the beach below, because clients always came after the embers did. [23] She climbed the iron stairs slowly, listening. [30] The wind chimes she had strung from every window frame of the lighthouse were already speaking - small copper tubes and carved bone pieces catching the pre-dawn air off the water, each cluster pitched to a different register. [67] She had learned, over years of hiring out her conscience, to decipher which chimes rang longest: the low copper meant unresolved grief, the bone pieces a hunger for permission, and the highest thin wire a need she privately called the cloak of doubt - that desperate wish to wrap oneself in uncertainty rather than face what one already knew. [125] Tonight, the wire chimes sang. [130] She was ready, then. [134]

From her coat pocket she drew the hollow mirror shard - a piece of antique glass she had found in a tide pool years ago, its silver backing entirely eaten away so that looking into it showed only the faint outline of a face without any reflection behind it. [182] She set it on the windowsill and waited. [190]

Cole knocked at the door before the sky had properly decided to turn. [203] He was a broad-shouldered man who carried himself with the particular posture of someone accustomed to being right, and she recognized the type immediately - the kind whose certainty was structural, load-bearing, something he could not afford to crack even when it had already begun to. [248]

"I was told you help people think," he said. [257]

"I help people want to think," she corrected, which was sincerely insincere in the way all her best work was - true enough to be useful, false enough to keep her own walls intact. [290] She gestured for him to sit near the window where the wire chimes shivered and crossed in the breeze. [309]

He told her about the community vote the night before, the campfire at which seventeen neighbors had decided unanimously to report the family in the old Cress house to the county - a decision made clean and fast and without the one voice that should have complicated it, the voice of the person who actually knew the family. [366] That voice had been Cole's, and he had stayed silent. [376]

"I agreed with them," he said. [382]

"Did you?" Maren asked. [386]

He looked at the hollow mirror shard on the sill, frowning at the way it gave him nothing back. [405] "I needed you to tell me I was right," he said finally, and his voice went honest in a way his posture hadn't caught up to yet. [432]

This was the moment she had come to understand as the hinge of her work - not the moment of accusation, not the dispensing of answers, but the place where she needed to unhook whatever performance a person had worn through the door and let them stand in the draft without it. [483] She reached across and lifted the hollow shard so he could see how nothing stable lived inside it. [501]

"Certainty is a room," she said. [507] "Comfortable, familiar, but sealed." [511] "What I do is open a window." [518]

The wire chimes rang clean and thin, and she watched him hear them the way she always watched - for the flinch, for the slight softening around the eyes when a person began to welcome nuance into certainty instead of barricading against it. [560] The flinch came. [563] Then the softening. [566]

Outside, the lighthouse beam swept its patient arc across a water going gray-green with the approaching dawn, and the breezy dark began to loosen at its edges. [593] Cole said nothing for a long time, which was how she knew the work had begun. [609]

Silence after revelation was always more expensive than words - that was something she had never told a client, keeping it close the way people who are genuinely vulnerable keep the most precise description of their wound. [645]

"You'll go back to them," she said at last, not as a question. [658]

"And say what?" he asked. [663]

"That you stayed quiet," she said. [669] "That it matters." [672]

He left when the beam finally went dark for the morning, its work handed off to daylight, and Maren stood at the window listening to all the chimes settling into a lower, steadier note. [706] She picked up the hollow mirror shard and held it at an angle where the new light caught the empty silver, showing her nothing, which was, she had long since decided, exactly enough. [739]
