Maren was the most intrepid of Verath's orchard pilots, and she had spent eleven seasons threading her skiff between the floating rock arches that curved like cathedral bones above the canopy. [31] She knew the planet's moods better than she knew her own face -- the way amber fog pooled in the lower groves before dawn, the way the fruit trees grew in spiraling rings around the arches' anchor points, their roots drinking from thin streams that cut the plateau rock like veins. [81] But she had never flown during the inversion hour. [90]

Today she would. [93]

The trees had been dying. [98] Not one grove, not one species -- all of them, orchard by orchard, the leaves curling inward and going gray, as though the planet itself were losing its will to bear fruit. [129] Every pilot, every agronomist, every elder in the settlement had offered theories. [141] Maren had listened to all of them and believed none. [151] Resilience, she thought, had to come from somewhere specific -- a source, a root deeper than any shovel could reach. [170] She intended to find it. [175]

She was airborne when the gravity shifted. [182] It began as a hum in her chest, then a sudden, featherlight loosening, and then the world inverted. [200] The streams lifted. [203] Maren had heard about this -- Verath's sixty-minute inversion, when the planet's twin-core field momentarily reversed -- but hearing was nothing like watching a thousand silver threads of water rise vertically from their channels and hang in the air like luminous columns. [243] She gripped the skiff's manual bar and let the instruments go dark. [255] Navigation by instrument was useless during inversion; every compass spun. [265]

But water, even floating water, still flowed toward magnetic low-pressure corridors. [276] Maren had studied this. [280] She angled the skiff sideways and began navigating by water flow -- reading the slow, impossible drift of the risen streams as they curved, almost lazily, toward the planet's interior. [309] The floating rock arches drifted slightly during inversion too, rotating on their anchor-tether roots, and she slipped between them like a needle through enormous stone rings, the orchard canopy hanging beneath her in eerie inverted stillness. [345]

The silence was not quiet. [350] It whirled -- a spinning, pressured hush that seemed to swallow engine noise and birdsong alike, leaving only the thin creak of the skiff's frame and the sound of her own breath. [381] Maren followed the water columns deeper into the arch-field until she saw it: a clearing where a dozen streams converged in mid-air, braiding together around a central column of stone that jutted from nothing, untethered, a needle of rock floating at the heart of the grove network. [428]

She landed the skiff on the column's flat top. [437] The moment her boots touched the stone, she felt the vibration -- a low, resonant layering of frequencies, as though every sound the orchard had ever made was stored here and playing back simultaneously. [470] A gathering of echoes. [474] The living and the dead orchards, the old storms and the first plantings, all of it reverberating through the column's mineral core in overlapping waves. [499] This was the planet's memory, its accumulated endurance, and it was singing. [511]

At her feet, half-embedded in the stone, lay a broken arrow tip -- dark iron, old beyond any settlement record, left by someone who had found this place before her and marked it the only way they could. [548] She picked it up. [552] The moment the metal touched her palm, the vibration intensified, and below her the dying orchard visibly responded: leaves uncurled, color returning in a slow green tide as though the echoes had needed a conductor. [587] The arrow tip, she understood, was a key -- not to a lock, but to a frequency, one that accelerates plant growth by resonating through root systems that had forgotten how to draw strength. [620]

The inversion hour would end in minutes. [627] Maren closed her fingers around the broken tip and looked out across the rising columns of water, the drifting stone arches, the canopy slowly greening far below. [654] She had come to discover the source of resilience, and she had. [666] It was not a substance or a soil. [674] It was the accumulated echo of every tree that had survived before, still calling forward through the stone, waiting for someone with enough nerve to listen. [700]

She pointed the skiff home and let the water show her the way. [713]
