The library had no windows left to break. [8] Mira had known that before she descended, but the knowing became different once the blackout swallowed the surface grid and the last battery lantern in her pack became her only sun. [39] She was a logical page indexer by trade, employed by the Bureau of Recovered Texts to catalog what the flooded lower districts had not fully claimed. [65] Most of her colleagues sent drones. [71] Mira went herself. [74]

The sunken city's library occupied the fourth subbasement of what had once been the municipal archive, its ceiling barely two meters above the waterline outside, its air held in by pressure and luck and some stubborn engineering no one living fully understood. [116] She moved between the shelves with the careful economy of someone who had spent a decade arranging disordered things, her fingers reading spines the way others read faces. [144] The blackout had begun three hours into her descent, cutting the relay signal that usually kept her indexed coordinates alive in the surface registry. [168] She did not panic. [172] Panic was, to her mind, simply poor cataloging of emotion. [182]

What she needed was in the restricted annex at the library's northern end, a collection of administrative ledgers from the city's original founding decade, before the flooding, before the revisions, before the official histories had been made tidy and convenient. [222] She was here to trace honest origins, specifically to find whether the city's founding charter had been altered after the fact, as one suppressed petition from 1887 suggested it had been. [253] The petition had surfaced in a water-damaged box she had indexed eight months prior, its language careful and fearful, describing a stamp of authority applied to documents that should not have borne it. [286]

She found the annex door unlocked, which surprised her, then did not surprise her when she considered that no one else had reason to come here in the dark. [315] Inside, on the central reading table, as though placed by an archivist who had simply stepped away for a century, sat an antique ink stamp. [340] It was iron-handled, its base carved from dense rubber now cracked at the edges, and when Mira held the lantern close she could read the reversed text in its face: AUTHORIZED AND ORIGINAL, in the serif font the founding administration had used exclusively before 1891. [385] She set it down carefully and opened the nearest ledger. [395]

The stamps were everywhere, pressed onto documents dated well outside the stamp's era of use, the ink a slightly different oxidation color than the surrounding text, something only a tranquilly perceptive eye, trained on thousands of aged pages, would catch in ordinary light, let alone lantern glow. [442] This was the falsification the petition had feared. [450] But Mira paused, because she was logical, and logic required that she not simply accept the obvious reading. [468] She forced herself to readjust her approach and consider an alternative hypothesis: that the stamps were contemporary to the documents and the ink had aged unevenly due to the moisture conditions of the sunken chamber. [503] She pulled three more ledgers and compared oxidation patterns systematically, cross-referencing fiber absorption rates she had memorized from her conservation training. [524] The alternative hypothesis did not survive the comparison. [532] The stamps were applied after the fact, some by decades. [542]

She sat with that conclusion for a long moment, not rushing toward what came next. [557] The blackout, which had felt like an obstacle, had become something else: a reclaimed stillness, the city's noise and urgency locked out above the waterline, leaving her alone with a truth that the light of ordinary days had somehow never been quiet enough to find. [602] She photographed every page with the lantern held at an angle to maximize shadow contrast, then wrapped the antique ink stamp in acid-free cloth from her kit and marked it as evidence in her recovery log. [638] The founding charter was fraudulent in at least seven clauses. [648] She did not feel triumph, exactly. [654] She felt the dappled resolve of someone who has walked into a difficult place for a precise reason and found precisely what she came for, neither more nor less, which is as close to satisfaction as honest work ever gets. [694] She climbed back toward the surface when the relay signal returned, her catalog complete, the stillness still resting somewhere in her chest like a lamp she intended to keep. [723]
