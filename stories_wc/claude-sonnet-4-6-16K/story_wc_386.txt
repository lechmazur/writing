Mara pressed her palm against the glacier wall and felt the cold push back like a held breath. [18] The shrine sat thirty meters inside the ice, carved by hands she had never seen but somehow recognized -- low ceiling, curved alcoves, sixteen candles burning in colors that made no scientific sense at this depth and temperature. [55] She had found it the same way she found everything: by following the dead. [69]

She set the snow globe on the central shelf, and it sagged there, its glass half-liquefied, its little winter scene collapsed into a smear of white and blue. [97] She had pulled it from the wreck of the Adelheid three seasons ago, from a cabin sealed so tight that the pressure had done strange things to ordinary objects. [126] The globe had melted from the inside, which the salvage crew called impossible and she called familiar. [143]

Mara was a wreck-diver by training and a ghost whisperer by inheritance, and she understood that both professions were really the same job: you went into places where the living stopped and you brought something back. [179] The question was always what. [184]

She opened her notebook on the ice shelf beside the candles. [195] For four months she had been collecting dreams -- not her own, but strangers'. [208] She placed small ads in coastal papers: Describe a recurring dream. [219] Any detail. [221] Compensation provided. [223] The responses came in dozens, then hundreds, and she read each one with the patience of someone sifting sediment. [242]

A woman in Stavanger dreamed of a man in a yellow oilskin standing at the end of her hallway, saying nothing, holding his hat. [266] A retired schoolteacher in Tromso dreamed of the same man on a pier, his mouth moving without sound. [284] A teenage boy in Reykjavik dreamed of him waving from a sinking deck. [297]

By analyzing these cameo appearances in strangers' dreams, Mara had learned to read the pattern the dead left behind -- not haunting, exactly, but leaking. [321] The dead pressed against the membrane of sleep the way light pressed through frosted glass. [336] Windows of connection, she called them in her notes, the places where one world let the other one show through. [356] She weaves truth carefully, she had once written about herself in the third person during a long-ago interview, because the interviewer seemed to need the distance, and because she had found that truth delivered too directly shattered the very windows she was trying to open. [401]

The man in the yellow oilskin was the Adelheid's second officer. [412] His name had been Tobias Gren, and he had survived the sinking but not the rescue -- pulled from the water with the snow globe in his coat pocket, dead of hypothermia before the boat reached shore. [448] The globe had been a gift for his daughter. [457] He had bought it in a market in Gdansk, chosen it because the tiny figure inside wore a coat the same red as hers. [481]

Mara lit a seventeenth candle -- her own contribution to the shrine -- and held the ruined globe up to it. [500] In the warm light, the collapsed interior was still somehow beautiful, the way wreckage often was: honest, unguarded, stripped of pretense. [521] She could see the small red figure still standing in the center of the dissolved scene, intact. [538]

She had not come to expose Tobias Gren in any damaging sense. [550] She had come to expose what he had carried, to bring it out of the dark where it had been dissolving for three winters. [574] To salvage something pure from the wreckage -- not the fact of his death, not the drama of it, but the small, true thing underneath: a father who found a coat the same red as his daughter's and thought, she would love this. [616]

The candles burned in their impossible colors. [623] The glacier held its breath around her. [630] And in the ice walls, lit like captured dusk, she thought she could see something like gratitude looking back -- not bright, not warm, but real the way borrowed starlight is real: secondhand, gentle, enough. [664]

She wrote the daughter's name in her notebook and closed it. [675] Tomorrow she would find her. [680] Tonight she let the candles do their work, steady and patient in the carved-out dark, and she did not rush the silence. [702]
