The elevator cage barely fit Maren and her cart, but she had learned to love the close quarters. [18] As the mechanism engaged and the car began its long climb through the icebound spire's interior, she felt the familiar shift in her stomach -- ten floors up, and the world below dissolved into cloud. [52] The Spire of Veld existed above weather, above argument, above almost everything except the pale blue nothing of the upper sky. [73]

She was the youngest senior servant the Spire had ever certified, though she never mentioned it. [89] She was warmly inquisitive by nature, the kind of person who asked questions not to collect answers but because she genuinely loved the moment before she knew. [116] It was why she had lasted here when others fled the altitude and the cold and the long, white silences. [136]

On the second floor, the elevator's frosted panel began to let in light -- not directly, but scattered, fractured by ice crystals embedded in the glass, throwing small prismatic fans across the brass wall of the car. [172] It was in that scattered light that she noticed the object on the luggage shelf she used as a supply ledge: a pair of airship captain's goggles, amber-lensed, their leather strap cracked with age and their brass housing etched with a name she didn't recognize. [217] Somebody had left them pressed against the side wall, as though they'd been set down carefully and simply never retrieved. [237]

She picked them up on the third floor. [245] They were heavier than she expected. [251]

By the fourth floor, she had found the diary. [260] It was tucked into the goggle case, a slim journal with a cover so cracked and pale it looked like old snow. [282] She nearly set it back, but the warmly inquisitive part of her that never quite rested turned it over, opened to the first page, and immediately understood why no one had bothered to read it. [317] Every entry was encrypted -- columns of numbers and symbols arranged in tight, meticulous rows, the work of someone who had wanted badly to be honest but could not afford to be. [348]

The fifth floor came and went. [354]

She held the diary under the scattered prismatic light and watched something begin to unfold -- not the encryption itself, which remained stubbornly closed, but the emotion inside it. [382] The entries grew longer near the back. [389] The handwriting changed, loosening from controlled precision into something that curled at the edges. [403] The numbers could not hide that. [409] Even in cipher, anguish had a shape, and she recognized it the way you recognize music played in another room. [429]

The sixth floor. [432] The seventh. [434]

She was not sentimental by habit. [440] She had learned, in service to people who felt too much and showed too little, a kind of affectionate detachment -- she cared, genuinely and warmly, but from a slight distance, the way you love a fire you don't touch. [479] It protected her and, she believed, made her more useful. [489] She could be present in grief without drowning in it. [499]

But she kept looking for what she always looked for: the joy beneath the anguish. [514] It was her private discipline, the small act of excavation she performed whenever life handed her something heavy. [532] Because in her experience, no one writes obsessively in code unless they are also trying to preserve something they loved. [552]

On the eighth floor, she found it. [559] Near the journal's final pages, the numbers broke entirely, and in their place, a single clear line: The clouds from above are not what I expected. [585] They are warm from up here. [591] They look like a place you could rest. [599]

The ninth floor passed. [603] She pressed the goggles gently back into their case, closed the diary, and held it with both hands as the elevator climbed the last floor and shuddered to a stop. [633] Someone above had loved flying enough to encrypt everything except that. [644] She would bring the goggles to the Spire's lost property office. [655] She would leave a note. [660] She would ask, warmly and without pressure, whether anyone knew the name etched into the brass. [676] The door opened onto white air and cold light, and she stepped out, already composing the question in her head. [696]
