After the final grain slips through the hourglass, Margot Vane does what she has always done with grief she cannot name. [21] She goes to the tunnel. [26] The echo chamber tunnel runs beneath the old rail yard on the edge of town, a concrete throat forty feet long and shaped like a question, its walls still carrying the ghost-hum of freight trains that stopped running decades before her daughter stopped laughing. [70] Margot is, by day's accounting, a leaf blower symphony conductor. [80] She inherited the title -- and the ensemble -- from her father, who believed that ordinary machines could carry extraordinary feeling if you arranged their pitch and pressure with enough patience. [109] Her six-person crew stood at intervals along the tunnel's length each Thursday evening, and Margot would raise her hands the way a cathedral organist might, each signal telling someone when to throttle up, when to ease back, when to let the drone swell into something nearly beautiful. [156] Tonight she comes alone. [160] She carries the oldest blower, a machine held together more by habit than hardware, and she grips its pull cord handle with both hands before she enters the tunnel. [189] The handle is cracked yellow plastic, worn smooth on one side where her father's thumb had rested for thirty years. [209] It is mournfully eager in her grip -- the way a leash feels when the dog is gone but the walk still needs taking. [232] She pulls. [234] The engine catches on the third draw, and the sound blooms outward and doubles and triples inside the concrete throat, a low foundational roar that holds within it every frequency Margot has ever needed and lost. [270] She begins to hum. [274] Not cleanly. [276] She never hums cleanly anymore, not since Elena stopped. [285] What comes out of her is something via half-sung lullabies carried on an off-key wind that the blower creates and the tunnel multiplies, syllables half-remembered from bedtime -- words that macerate the silence, softening it the way water softens old bread, pressing into every sealed corner of the dark until the tunnel gives back something almost human. [341] Elena is fourteen now and has not laughed since the accident that took her friend the summer before last. [360] The house has become a held breath. [367] Margot and her husband speak in careful registers, avoiding the unspoken thing that lives in every room: that they do not know how to reach their daughter, that they are afraid of pressing too hard and cracking something already hairline-fractured, that they miss the sound of her laugh the way you miss a particular bird after winter. [424] Tending to the unspoken is the only skill Margot has ever truly mastered. [437] Her father taught her: you do not always name the grief directly. [449] Sometimes you build a space large enough to hold it and let the sound do what language cannot. [467] The blower drone shifts as she adjusts the throttle with her wrist, and the tunnel takes the new frequency and layers it across the old one, and suddenly the sound is very nearly a chord -- mournful and resonant and strangely warm. [508] She thinks of Elena at six, chasing leaves across the yard while Margot ran the blower, the girl shrieking with delight every time the wind caught a pile and sent it spinning. [540] She had laughed like something uncontainable then. [547] Margot lets the lullaby rise without words now, just breath and the off-key drone, and the tunnel gives it back to her changed, rounded, haunted. [572] She does not know if this will work. [580] She only knows that at the end of a day in which her daughter sat again at dinner without speaking, without lifting her eyes from her plate, this is the only honest thing left to do. [616] She tends to what has not been said. [624] She plays the machine like grief made mechanical. [632] And when she finally walks home, hair smelling of exhaust and cool concrete, Elena is still awake, sitting in the kitchen doorway with her knees drawn up. [659] Her daughter does not speak. [664] But she watches her mother come through the yard with the old blower on her shoulder, and something shifts in her face -- something brief and fragile and real, a corner of the mouth that does not quite commit but almost, almost tries. [706] Margot does not remark on it. [712] She sets the machine against the porch rail, the pull cord handle swaying once in the night wind, and goes inside to make tea. [736] She tends to the unspoken, and she waits. [744]
