The opal library existed above the clouds in that particular way deserted marvels tend to exist - not forgotten exactly, but released from the obligation of being found. [27] Maren had been its caretaker for what the library called thirty years and what her body called forever. [44] Her title was Glass-Orchid Caretaker, which meant she tended the twelve thousand glass orchids arranged along the shelves between the books. [66] Each orchid had been blown by a different glassmaker, and each contained, sealed inside its translucent petals, the name of someone who had died without being mourned. [94] The library called this collection the Museum of Extinct Feelings. [104] Maren called it her reason to wake up. [112]

Her work was gently systematic. [117] Every morning she moved from east gallery to west, polishing each orchid with a chamois cloth folded into precise quarters, checking the hairline seals, listening for any high, faint resonance that might mean a name was beginning to dissolve. [156] Names, she had learned, needed tending. [162] Left alone in the glass, they faded like warmth from stone. [173]

The cracked bamboo tube had been hanging in the west-facing window for as long as Maren had worked here - longer, she suspected. [196] It was the last remaining piece of a wind chime, the others having fallen one by one across the decades, tumbling into the cloudbank below where they made sounds she imagined as small, private farewells. [232] The bamboo tube's crack ran the full length of the instrument, splitting its voice into two imperfect registers, a doubled tone that was somehow richer than any whole tube could have produced. [264] She had never replaced it. [269] It was the only thing in the library that was damaged and also irreplaceable, which made it, she thought, the most honest object in the room. [295]

The problem began on a morning in late autumn - late autumn being a feeling the library measured rather than a date, a foxglove-purple ache in the air that preceded every significant ending. [329] One of the orchids had gone dark. [336] Not cracked, not fallen. [340] Simply dark, the name inside it extinguished, and when Maren lifted it to the window the glass was empty as blown breath. [363] She searched her catalog. [367] The name had been Serafin. [372] A composer, dead three centuries, who had written festival songs for his city and then watched his city be swallowed by a river. [395] Every person who might have hummed his melodies was also gone. [406] The name had run out of echoes to sustain it. [416]

Maren stood at the window with the darkened orchid in her palm and felt the particular weight of foxglove regret, which is not the regret of having done something wrong but the regret of having arrived too late at a moment that refused to be undone. [461] She could not restore what had dissolved entirely. [469] But she could light the darkness inside the glass again, if she found the right source. [485]

She knew how it had to be done. [493] The library's oldest instruction, written in the flyleaf of its founding document in handwriting so faded she had traced it with her fingertip a hundred times: names are lit by the echo of a long-forgotten song. [532] Not performed. [534] Echoed. [535] The distinction mattered. [538]

So she did something she had never done in thirty years of gently systematic work. [553] She sang. [555] She sang the fragment she had memorized from Serafin's one surviving score, eight bars of a festival melody she had studied precisely because she feared this day. [582] Her voice was imperfect, the library was enormous, and the opal windows caught the sound as cloud outside shifted to receive it. [604] Then the cracked bamboo tube moved, though there was no wind, splitting the echo into two resonant registers, and the darkened orchid trembled in Maren's palm and caught the light. [634]

When she opened her eyes, the glass glowed again. [644] Serafin. [645] Still here. [647]

She returned the orchid to its shelf, folded her cloth into precise quarters, and moved on. [663] The deserted marvels of the library surrounded her - thousands of sealed names, empty chairs where readers had dissolved into the cloud cover generations ago, the cracked bamboo tube still trembling faintly at the window. [697] There were eleven thousand nine hundred and ninety-nine orchids left to check. [709] She had all the time the museum allowed her. [718] She began. [720]
