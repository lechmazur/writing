Sela had spent three weeks aboard the Gallant Meridian without seeing another living face. [14] The antique airship had drifted to a stop somewhere above the inland marshes, its hydrogen cells punctured by a freak hailstorm, its radio crackling only static. [40] The crew had evacuated in the two functioning skiffs, but Sela had stayed behind -- diligently curious, as her mentor always called her, sometimes as a compliment and sometimes not. [69] She had maps to finish. [74]

The ship carried the ghost of a hundred voyages in its oak-paneled corridors: tarnished compass roses bolted above every doorway, charts yellowed to the color of old teeth pinned three-deep along every wall. [107] As a map apprentice, she was supposed to absorb all of it, to apprentice herself to the dead cartographers' logic until their instincts became hers. [132] Mostly she wandered, and noted things. [138]

On the fourth day of her solitude, she found the hopscotch board. [150] It occupied a forgotten corner of the main deck, near the aft storage hatch, scratched into the wood by some child who must have accompanied a crew on a long voyage years ago. [183] Dust had settled so thickly in the grooves that the squares glowed a faint amber against the dark planks -- a stranded luminance, Sela thought, light that had nowhere left to go. [214] She crouched and studied it the way she studied maps: with her whole attention, her nose nearly touching the surface. [234]

There was something wrong with the numbering. [241]

She pulled the polyglot's phrasebook from her coat pocket, the old one she had borrowed from the ship's library and never returned, its most important page still folded down at the corner -- a dog-ear marking a passage about navigational vocabulary in five languages, including one she had never fully translated. [291] She had been using that page as a bookmark for a different mystery. [304] Now she pressed the open phrasebook beside the hopscotch board and began to compare. [318]

By noting secret patterns in a dusty hopscotch board, she understood, a child had encoded something. [334] The squares did not run one through nine but skipped and doubled back in a sequence she recognized from stellar cartography -- the same skip-pattern used to mark reliable wind corridors over mountainous terrain. [367] Someone's child had been taught to play hopscotch the way a sailor learns to read swells. [383] The dog-eared passage confirmed it: the untranslated phrase meant the path a bird refuses. [397]

Sela stood and looked out past the railing. [405] The starling murmurations had been building all afternoon, enormous living clouds of birds wheeling through the copper light. [423] She had watched them abstractly before, the way one watches weather. [434] Now she watched them as data. [440] The flock bent always in one direction before reforming -- west-southwest -- avoiding a corridor of air as deliberately as the hopscotch sequence avoided certain squares. [464] They knew something the charts did not: a hidden thermal channel, too narrow for most airships, but not for the Gallant Meridian at reduced buoyancy. [489]

She went back to her drafting table and began to work. [500]

This was the tone of her weeks here -- she had grown genuinely fond of her aloneness, the way it made every small discovery feel like a conversation. [527] The ship itself was her interlocutor, and the starlings, and now this child she would never meet. [544] Sociable isolation, her mentor might have called it, if he had a poet's vocabulary, which he did not. [562]

She worked through the night, her lamp cutting a warm circle in the dark. [576] By morning she had charted the thermal corridor in three scales, cross-referenced it against the phrasebook's navigational glossary, and added a marginal note in her smallest handwriting: Discovered by hopscotch, confirmed by birds. [609] It was the kind of notation that would make future cartographers smile or frown, depending on their temperament. [627] She did not care. [631]

To chart the next great discovery was always the point -- not the credit, not the rescue that would eventually come, but the clean satisfaction of a new line drawn where no line had been. [665] Sela tucked the phrasebook into her coat again, its dog-ear still faithful to its page, and climbed to the observation deck to watch the murmurations one more time before the light changed. [697] The starlings moved like a single slow thought across the sky, and she followed them with her eyes, already planning the next map. [720] The Gallant Meridian drifted, patient as old wood, carrying her forward. [731]
