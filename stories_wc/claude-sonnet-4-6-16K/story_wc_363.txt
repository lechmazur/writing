Maren had lived beside the forgotten orchard long enough to stop counting seasons. [13] The apple trees, gnarled and unharvested, stood inside their ring of bamboo like prisoners who had made peace with their cell. [33] She was a wistful loom spinner by nature and by choice, and her loom filled the small stone outbuilding at the orchard's edge, its threads humming when the wind moved through the stalks outside. [67]

She had found the box at a rummage sale in the valley town, three miles down the mountain path she rarely walked anymore. [91] Jigsaw puzzle pieces, three hundred of them, scattered loose inside a cardboard box with no picture on the lid. [110] She had bought it for almost nothing, drawn by something she could not name -- the way the edges caught the light, the interlocking teeth that seemed to hold a grammar she almost recognized. [144]

At home, she spread them across the floor and began connecting them not by image but by edge. [162] Through jigsaw puzzle edges found in that rummage sale box, a pattern emerged -- not a picture, but a sequence. [181] A rhythm. [183] The same rhythm, she realized with a stillness in her chest, that her mother had once hummed while weaving, decades and a continent away. [208]

Her mother was dying. [212] A letter had come months ago and then nothing -- a raveling silence that spread outward from the mailbox like water soaking into cloth. [236] No phone reached the valley below. [242] No road took her there in time. [249] The impossible distance between them was not merely miles but the hush of time's unraveling tapestry, the kind of separation that geography alone cannot explain. [275]

She recommenced her weaving that same night. [282]

The loom accepted her hands like an old argument resuming. [292] She followed the pattern the puzzle edges had revealed, her shuttle moving in sequences that encoded something beyond decoration. [310] She was not weaving a blanket or a shawl. [319] She was weaving a message -- every crossing thread a syllable, every color a word her mother had taught her before language was formal between them. [344]

The tree sap amber bead she threaded in at the center, the one her mother had pressed into her palm at the last departure, saying only, carry this back when you are ready. [378] The bead was stoically sparkling in the lamplight, ancient resin holding a bubble of air trapped when the world was newer, patient as stone, giving nothing away. [406]

Maren's fingers bled a little on the rough warp threads. [416] She did not stop. [420]

The bamboo beyond the window clicked and whispered through the night, a percussion of hollow wood that sounded, if she allowed it, like a crowd of people holding their breath. [452] The forgotten orchard held its own silence differently -- that stoically sparkling quality the frost-touched branches carried at dawn, beautiful without asking to be seen, enduring without requesting credit. [482]

She understood, somewhere in the third night of weaving, what the sacrifice was. [494] It was not the bleeding fingers or the sleeplessness. [503] It was the message itself. [508] To carry a message across impossible distance, she would have to give the finished cloth away -- send it with the next traveler who passed through, trust the hands of strangers, release her only remaining thread of connection to her mother into the world's indifferent current. [553] The weaving would arrive or it would not. [561] Her mother would understand it or she would have already gone. [572]

Latent in every choice Maren had made -- to live here, to weave alone, to answer solitude with craft rather than company -- was this moment's cost. [598] She had been preparing all along to give something up without knowing what it was. [614]

She finished before dawn on the fifth day. [622] The cloth lay across her knees, dense and warm, the amber bead at its heart sealed inside the weave like a word that could not be taken back. [651]

A traveler came through at midmorning, heading south toward the valley and beyond. [664] Maren folded the cloth carefully and placed it in the woman's hands without explanation. [678] The woman looked at it, then looked at Maren, and nodded once, the way people nod when they recognize the weight of something without knowing its contents. [704]

The bamboo said nothing. [708] The orchard said nothing. [712] The raveling silence closed behind the traveler like water closing over a stone. [725]

Maren sat down at her loom and recommenced. [733]
