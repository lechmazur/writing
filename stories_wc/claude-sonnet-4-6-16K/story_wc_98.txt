The ridge had been blooming for nine days, which Sera counted as both a miracle and a deadline. [18] She was the island's reality checker -- not a title she had chosen, but one the elders pressed upon her the morning she correctly argued, at age seven, that the singing fish in the harbor were not singing but rather vibrating their swim bladders in response to barometric pressure. [66] Now she was thirty-one, and the knowledge fruit tree at the ridge's crown had produced exactly one seed. [84] It sat in her palm, small and dense as a worry stone, faintly luminous, the color of something between amber and apology. [106] The monsoon would arrive within the week. [113] She could feel it in the way the air tasted -- mineral and impatient -- the way her grandmother had taught her to taste air, which was itself an empirically whimsical form of meteorology that Sera had spent years trying to either prove or abandon and had done neither. [160] The seed, according to the old texts, held a dormant strain of the island's collective memory. [176] Every song, every judgment, every name given to a child at the edge of a tide -- all of it encoded in the cellular architecture of a fruit that only the ridge tree produced, only once per generation, only before the first monsoon of a new elder's tenure. [223] Elder Manu had died in spring, which meant the seed was both rare and urgent. [238] If the monsoon drowned the ridge before the seed was planted and awakened, the memory chain would break. [256] Sera did not entirely believe this. [262] She also did not entirely disbelieve it, which was, she had come to understand, the precise condition required to do her job well. [285] She climbed to the high flat stone where dreamers slept, the place her people called the listening shelf, worn smooth by centuries of shoulders and restless hips. [312] To waken the seed, the texts said, one had to interpret the dream of someone sleeping beside it -- not just observe the dreamer, but enter the architecture of what they saw and translate it back into waking meaning. [350] This was her specific skill. [355] She could not dream richly herself -- her dreams were dry and logical, like footnotes -- but she could sit beside another's sleep and somehow feel the shape of what passed behind their eyes, the way a blind person might read a room through reflected sound. [399] She placed the seed between her palms and lay down beside old Hana, who was already sleeping. [416] Hana dreamed in colors Sera felt as pressures: blue arriving as weight, yellow as a sudden lifting, red as a thrum along the jaw. [440] Through interpreted dreams, Sera began to sense the pattern -- a child running along a black-sand beach, a woman weaving a net out of smoke, a man pressing his forehead to the forehead of a fish -- and she understood these were not fantasies but memories, old ones, waiting. [487] The seed warmed in her hands. [493] She was not sure if that was real or if she was borrowing Hana's warmth through proximity, which was itself an empirically whimsical problem she would have to resolve later. [523] She pressed the seed into the soft prepared soil at the shelf's edge, the place where the ridge soil was darkest, worked by generations of hands she had never met. [553] The air shifted. [556] She could not say what changed -- the light did something, or the blooms on the ridge leaned a degree -- but she felt it the way she felt Hana's dreams: as pressure, as meaning slightly larger than she could hold. [595] The collective memory did not speak to her. [603] It did not need to, she thought. [610] It only needed to survive the season -- held in roots, in cellular dark, in the patient language of soil -- so that next time someone needed to remember what the island had known, the knowledge would be there, neither proven nor dismissed, waiting to be interpreted by someone willing to sit quietly beside it. [663] The first cloud of the monsoon appeared on the horizon, enormous and approaching, full of its own empirically whimsical certainty. [683] Sera wiped her hands on her knees and climbed down from the ridge. [696] Behind her, the seed was already doing the slow, quiet, luminous work of becoming. [710]
