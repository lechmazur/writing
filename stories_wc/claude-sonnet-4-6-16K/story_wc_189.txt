Mara had spent twelve years cataloguing the lives of creatures that existed in neither one world nor the other. [19] As a tidal-pool chronicler, she understood better than most that the most honest truths lived in thresholds. [36] She turned the jade cicada over in her palm now, feeling its cool carved weight against the humid air of the ancient grotto. [59] Above the grotto's wide mouth, the stars had arranged themselves with what felt like deliberate patience. [75]

[91] The silence had been growing for three full minutes, swelling out of the moment when she had told him she was leaving the Institute, and he had said only, "I see." [122] She was warmly observant by nature, the kind of person who noticed which way a man's shoulders tilted when he was holding something back. [146] Oswald's were tilted in now, curved like a mussel shell pulled closed. [158]

"Someone had lost it there." [226] She held it up so starlight caught the green stone. [236] "A jade cicada in a tidal pool struck me as the most honest accident I had ever seen. [254] A thing made to represent emergence, sitting in a place that was always becoming something else." [270]

Oswald was quiet again, but differently now. [277] The hard silence had cracked along its edge. [285]

She had learned long ago that you could not approach certain conversations the way you approached a shoreline head-on, crashing through the surf. [308] You moved tangentially, along the coast, until the sea let you in. [320] This was her method with tide pools, with data, with people she loved. [333]

[663] The stars continued their own patient transit above them, and the grotto held them both a little longer inside its ancient, tide-carved dark. [686]
