Maren had repaired broken clocks for thirty years, yet she had never once trusted a schedule. [16] She kept her workshop at the base of the limestone shaft where the underground river spilled into a roaring subterranean waterfall, the mist cool against her wrists as she worked. [46] The noise erased distractions. [49] The rhythm of falling water, she had found, was the only clock that never lied. [64]

She had come to the waterfall that morning through the neglected greenhouse above — the only passage down — pushing through the swirling leaves that the broken ventilation fans kept perpetually aloft, papery oak and willow fragments circling her shoulders like a slow bronze storm. [107] By the time she descended the iron ladder, she smelled smoke. [118]

The shelf above her workbench held the ashes of the fire she had not quite extinguished three nights prior. [137] A candle, a stack of old logbooks, a poor decision. [147] She had swept most of it away, but she had not swept carefully. [160] Beneath the ashes, half-buried under a collapsed ledger cover, she found it: the compass rose. [175]

It was brass and wide as her palm, its cardinal points etched so precisely they still caught light despite the soot. [196] She recognized it from the museum catalog she had borrowed last autumn — Admiral Cresswell's lost navigational instrument, reported stolen in 1887, then lost again in a flood, then simply gone. [226] She turned it over. [230] On the back, engraved in small letters: For when the chart runs out. [243]

She set it beside the clock she was currently repairing — a German bracket clock with a cracked escapement — and tried to think logically. [266] The compass rose had not simply appeared. [273] Someone had left it here, or it had traveled here by water, or she herself had carried it unknowingly inside one of the salvaged logbooks. [298] Three unlikely events, each separately dismissible. [304] But this was when coincidence becomes pattern, she understood: not when one strange thing happens, but when strange things accumulate until they start pointing the same direction. [331]

She was uncertainly certain — that specific condition she had learned to recognize in herself, that interior state where doubt and conviction occupied the same breath — that the compass rose was meant to be used now, today, for a purpose she had not yet invented. [375]

The clock on her bench needed a replacement verge staff she did not have. [389] She had planned to order the part, wait three weeks, then finish. [401] But the museum curator had called that morning. [409] The clock was promised at an exhibition in four days. [419] Four days was not three weeks. [425] Her plan was ash. [429]

So she decided to improvise. [434]

She took the compass rose apart — carefully, with apology — and found inside its pivot assembly a small brass rod of nearly identical gauge to the missing verge staff. [462] Admiral Cresswell's instrument had carried, for over a century, the precise dimension of someone else's emergency. [478] She machined it down on her lathe, fitted it, and listened to the escapement tick back to life with a sound like a knuckle popping in relief. [505]

She sat back. [508] The waterfall continued its indifferent thunder. [514] The mist settled on her forearms. [520]

She had spent three decades trying to plan her way around uncertainty — ordering parts in advance, keeping catalogs, building schedules. [540] The broken clocks had kept teaching her the same lesson and she had kept refusing to learn it: that beneath the ashes of every collapsed plan, something useful was usually waiting. [571] Not the thing she had ordered. [577] Not the thing she expected. [582] But something that, if she was willing to change her hands from a planner's grip to a tinker's, would fit. [602]

She wrapped the clock and wrote the curator a confirmation. [612] Then she swept the remaining ash from the shelf and set the compass rose, reassembled, in its place. [630] She would return it to the museum eventually. [638] But for now it belonged here, at the bottom of the shaft, beside the waterfall, in the workshop of someone who had finally learned to befriend uncertainty in planning rather than fight it — to let the chart run out and see what the current offered instead. [684]

Above her, through the greenhouse, the leaves kept swirling. [693]
