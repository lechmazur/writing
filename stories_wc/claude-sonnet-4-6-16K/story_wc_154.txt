Mara had spent four years translating ceasefire negotiations in the Solomon Islands, learning to carry the weight of words that could stop a bullet or send one. [27] Now she sat in the hospital atrium as twilight pressed mist against the glass walls, watching a child's chest rise and fall. [49]

The girl had been airlifted from the outer islands three days ago. [61] A reef dive gone wrong, a compound fracture, a field cast applied by someone with more courage than supplies. [80] Ocean water had reached the plaster before the rescue boat, smudging penciled notes into ruin. [95] Dosages, coordinates, a name. [99]

After the anesthetic kicked in from the procedure to reset the bone, the girl slept with her mouth slightly open, a small sound escaping her like a question she could not finish. [131] In her free hand she held a brain coral fragment, smooth on one side and labyrinthine on the other, something she had gripped through the rescue and the intake forms and the IV insertion. [165] The nurses had gently tried to take it. [173] She had held tighter. [177] Mara understood. [179]

She sat close not because anyone had asked, but because the ward interpreter had gone home and because she knew what it was to be surrounded by language you could not reach. [211] She tilted the cast toward the atrium's last pale glow and began to read. [225]

The smudged notes came from three hands. [232] One was medical shorthand, which she parsed slowly but parsed. [242] Another was a northern-atoll dialect she recognized -- a teacher's careful script listing the girl's allergies and her mother's radio frequency. [262] The third was the girl's own printing, large and unsteady, and Mara had to sound the word out twice before it resolved into meaning. [286] Home. [287]

She unknotted it all by decrypting the smudged notes on the cast, with the same patience she had brought to rooms where men with guns needed to believe the other side had said something they could live with. [325] She wrote the translations on a fresh notepad, softly, as though the act itself were a kind of suturing. [344]

What surprised her was the coral. [350] She had seen fragments like it on negotiators' desks and on windowsills in aid offices, kept the way people keep things that remind them the world existed before the argument. [380] The brain coral's surface mapped its own anxieties, every ridge a record of slow pressure, every groove a season survived. [400] She had carried her own fragment since Honiara, since the night a ceasefire dissolved at three in the morning and she sat outside the meeting room translating nothing for no one, holding the coral because it was the only thing in reach with no agenda. [445]

She set her notepad beside the girl's cast. [453] The mist pressed against the atrium glass, and the atrium itself felt held apart from the world outside, a space where time moved differently. [477] The girl's face in sleep was shrewdly innocent -- open enough to invite honesty, careful enough to have learned why caution mattered. [498] Mara recognized the expression because she had trained herself to wear it in negotiation rooms, in places where what you showed and what you felt had to be very different things. [529]

The coral rested between them, hers and the child's nearly identical. [540] Shared anxieties, she thought, were not the same as shared damage. [551] The difference was small but survivable. [557] It was the difference between a wound that closes and one that teaches the skin what pressure feels like before it arrives. [579]

She stayed until the ward lights came on, low and amber. [590] When she left, she left the notepad open on the bed rail, not to explain what could not be explained, but to graft new meaning onto old wounds -- to say in three dialects and one clear English word that someone had been here, had read what the child was carrying, and had found it worth the careful work of keeping.
