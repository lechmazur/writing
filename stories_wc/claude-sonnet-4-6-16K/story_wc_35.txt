Maren had not slept since the rain. [7] The desert fortress of Keth-Sarr collected water in its stone gutters, and by morning the courtyard had gone glassy and still. [28] She stood at the kitchen threshold and watched the puddles. [38] They had arranged themselves the way tidal pools always did in her grandmother's stories -- nested rings, shallow at the edges, dark at the center -- and she knew what they were telling her. [70]

She was a chef who tasted memories. [77] Not metaphorically. [79] A spoonful of someone's lentil soup and she could feel the argument they had had before cooking it, the low burn of it on her back teeth. [107] A bite of bread made in grief left grit on her tongue for hours. [121] She had learned to use this. [127] She had also learned it cost her. [134]

The soldiers in Keth-Sarr called her quiet and capable, words that meant they did not know what to do with her. [155] They left food at her door after she cooked their feasts, offerings of dried figs and hard cheese, as though proximity would make her gentle. [181] They did not know that if she touched them, her emotions passed through her hands like current through water. [200] She kept her gloves on. [205]

Analyzing the tidal pool formations in the courtyard, she traced their rings with the toe of her boot. [223] The pools pointed northwest, the way they always did in her grandmother's maps -- toward whatever was buried. [240] She had been searching for the coral icebound pavilion for eleven years. [252] Rumor called it a myth, a structure sunk beneath the oldest dune ridge, sealed in some mineral cold that the desert had no right to contain. [279] But Maren knew better. [283] She had tasted it once, in a broth she had made from bones bought off a trader -- a flash of blue cold and overwhelming sorrow, someone else's sorrow, packed tight as salt. [316]

That was stored remorse. [320] She recognized it the way you recognize a sound you heard only once in childhood. [335]

She went northwest, through the fortress gate, into the wet-smelling dark before dawn. [348] The dunes had softened from the rain, their crust broken, and she descended by feel, pushing through grainy resistance until her hands found the pavilion's outer arch -- coral, pale orange-pink, glazed with an unnatural cold that held the shape of old grief. [390]

She did not hesitate. [394] At the close of an unspoken dream, the world holds its breath, and she had learned to move inside that pause. [415] She pressed both palms flat against the coral wall. [424]

The remorse came through her immediately -- not a trickle but a flood, decades of it, the regrets of everyone who had ever cooked for this fortress, every meal made in anger or love or desolation, all of it preserved in this impossible cold. [466] She felt it subsume her, fill her from the feet up, heavy and specific: a mother who had salted food too harshly the night she sent her son to war, a man who had fed his dying friend something sweet because he could not bring himself to speak. [512]

Her goal was not to erase these things. [520] She wanted to sandpaper rough memories -- not destroy them, but wear them smooth enough that whoever still carried them could breathe. [541] She pushed back. [544] She pushed her own steady, brittle will against the flood, her gloves gone now, her bare hands reading every texture of the cold. [567] The cold cracked. [570]

When she climbed back to the courtyard, the pools had stilled. [581] She returned to the kitchen and cooked without tasting anything. [591] She cooked with her hands, pressing warmth into the dough, pressing steadiness into the broth, letting her emotions run out of her the way water runs down stone. [619] The soldiers ate without knowing what they received. [627] By evening they sat together more quietly, more gently, and one man who had not spoken in months said something small and true to the man beside him. [655]

Maren washed her hands. [659] The water ran clear. [663] She was not fixed, and neither were they, and that was the most honest thing she could offer -- a meal that did not lie. [686]
