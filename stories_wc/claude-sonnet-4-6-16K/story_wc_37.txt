In the mirror world, night had always been a permanent condition, not a season. [14] Maren kept her memories the way broken clocks keep time - in scattered, overlapping fragments that occasionally aligned. [31] She was the last memory keeper in the alchemical orchard greenhouse, a cathedral of glass and copper tubing where fruit trees grew fruit that no longer glowed, where the alembics sat cold and the roots drew from dark soil with diminishing faith. [73] The greenhouse was shrouded in endless night, as all things were now, since the woven dawns had come undone. [92]

She pressed her palm against the mica window each morning - or what she called morning, measured only by her own hunger. [113] The mica was thin as skin, layered and translucent, and once it had shown her the colors of a breaking sky. [134] Now it showed nothing but her own faint reflection and the dark beyond. [147]

The realm was dying. [151] She could feel it in the trees: copper-leafed persimmons dropping their fruit before it ripened, moonbloom apples forgetting how to bloom at all. [174] Without dawn, nothing completed its transformation. [180] The alchemical orchard existed to convert night into nourishment, dark into gold, grief into sweetness - but the process required the woven dawns, those slow-braided confluences of light that had once knit communities together across every quarter of the mirror world. [220] Villages had gathered at the edges of the greenhouse at first light, sharing the warmth that spilled from the mica windows like poured honey. [244] Now the villages were silent. [249]

Maren closed her eyes and searched her scattered interior. [258] The memories were not arranged in any orderly fashion. [267] She found a childhood she was not sure was hers - a woman singing over a cradle, her voice circling the infant's breath like a shawl. [292] She found a harvest festival, three generations of hands pressing identical recipes into clay tablets that crumbled the moment she tried to read them. [316] She found a man on a hill, arms wide, teaching children to locate the dawn by sound before they could see it. [338]

By sound. [340]

She had forgotten - or rather, she had not yet remembered - that the woven dawns were not only light. [358] They were song. [361] Each community had carried a thread of the weaving in its own particular melody, and together, sung simultaneously across distance, they had braided the sky open each day. [389] But those songs had been lost when the communities scattered. [399] They lived now only here, in her, broken and interleaved with strangers' grief and strangers' joy. [415]

She began to hum. [419]

It came out ragged at first, half a melody colliding with the ghost of another. [434] She walked the rows of failing trees, letting the sound find its own shape, not forcing it but following it the way water follows slope. [459] One by one, the fragments settled. [465] The harvest song found the lullaby. [471] The lullaby found the hill-man's teaching chant. [478] The teaching chant curled back to the harvest song, and suddenly she held a whole thing, a living rope of melody she had never known she carried. [505]

The mica window began to warm beneath her free hand. [515]

She pressed closer and let the song do what it had always been built to do: swaddle the darkness. [534] Not destroy it - never that - but wrap it, hold it, give it a boundary so that something else could exist alongside it. [556] Bound and releasing at once, the way a seed both contains and surrenders its whole self in the moment of germination. [577]

The first thread of light appeared in the mica, faint and amber. [589]

Then a second, silver. [593]

They crossed. [595]

Outside the greenhouse, in the villages she could not see, she understood that something was waking - that her song, leaking through the walls, was reaching people who had forgotten they knew how to answer. [629] She could feel them the way she felt her own memories: indistinct at first, then gathering weight, then suddenly, unmistakably, present. [650]

The woven dawn returned in a single trembling braid of sound and light. [663]

Not triumphant. [665] Not loud. [667] Simply there, the way morning always was when you had not stolen it - arriving as though it had never meant to leave, stitching the mirror world back together one remembered note at a time. [701]

Maren stood at the mica window and let herself be counted among the living. [715]
