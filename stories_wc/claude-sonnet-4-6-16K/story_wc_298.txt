Sela had burned the card on purpose. [7] The Wheel of Fortune, her mother's card, had promised her a life cycling endlessly between grief and duty, and she had held it over a candle flame until the paper curled black. [39] That act had cost her something she could not name, a warmth behind the sternum, an interior light that once made ordinary mornings feel worth surviving. [65] Now there were holes in her soul, and she had come to the catacomb orchard to patch them. [83]

The half-lit corridors underground smelled of wet limestone and something sweeter, a rot that had long since become earth. [102] She carried a lantern in one hand and the burned tarot card in the other, its edges still flaking charcoal against her palm. [125] The roots of apple trees pressed down from the ceiling of the tunnel, coiling between femurs and cracked skulls as though the dead had always been a kind of soil worth using. [157] Sela moved fiercely gently among them, lifting her feet over knuckle-bones without letting her pace slow, because slowing, she knew, was how this place trapped you. [183]

The dealer sat at the junction where three corridors met, cross-legged on a flat grave marker, surrounded by roots that had accepted him into their pattern. [209] He was old in the way that stones are old, not aged but compressed. [223] He inlaid meaning everywhere: in the positioning of two finger-bones arranged like a compass on the stone beside him, in the single apple hanging from the lowest root above his head, red as a decision. [258] Sela had heard he could barter destinies, trade the shape of one life for another, splice futures the way a surgeon splices nerves. [281]

She held up the burned card without preamble. [289] "I had a destiny," she said. [295] "I destroyed it." [298] Her voice was steady, and she meant it to be, because reckless as she was, she had come here with full knowledge of the cost. [323] She had read everything she could find about the catacomb orchard, about what the dealer required, about what he kept. [343] Idealism had not made her ignorant; it had made her brave in directions that wiser people called foolish. [361]

The dealer looked at the card. [367] "You burned it to get free," he said. [375] "And freedom made you hollow." [380]

"I want to patch the hollow," she said. [388] "Tell me what you need." [393]

He reached beneath the grave marker and produced a card of his own, pristine and cool, the Wheel unburned, turning. [413] "There are other destinies unclaimed," he said. [420] "Ones whose owners bartered them away or died before they could be used." [433] He set it on the stone between them. [441] "You may take one." [445] "But I will trammel one thing you love, one capacity, something you use without knowing how much you need it, and I will keep it here among the roots." [474]

Sela looked at the apple, at the bones, at the roots pressing so patiently through the dead. [491] She thought about the holes inside her, the places where the burning had let cold air in. [508] She thought about her mother, who had lived inside a destiny without ever questioning it, and about how that kind of completeness had looked, from the outside, like a kind of armor. [540]

She was a reckless idealist, which meant she believed that meaning could be found in the aftermath of every bad decision, that you could inlay meaning into wreckage the same way roots inlaid themselves into bone. [576] She believed, with a fierceness that had no cruelty in it, that the soul was always worth the cost of repair. [597]

She picked up the card. [602]

The dealer's hand moved and something left her, quietly, like a color she could no longer see. [619] The corridors glowed their half-light around her, patient and unchanged. [629] She stood and walked back through the orchard of bones and roots, a new destiny cool in her pocket, one old wound patched, and one new hollow she had not yet learned to name. [663] She would name it eventually. [668] She was good at that. [673]
