Maren had spent forty years learning the language of trees--where to cut, where to spare, how a single wound made with patience could coax a branch toward light rather than shadow. [32] She stood now on the verdant parapet beside the waterfall, where stone and moss had grown together so long they were indistinguishable, and she held the heirloom seed between two fingers like a secret she was not yet ready to speak aloud. [74]

The world on this side of the falls was quiet. [84] Early spring had arrived upon the hush of budding hope--that particular stillness before the first leaves unfurl, when the air holds its breath and everything trembles on the edge of becoming. [116] Maren had always loved this hour more than any other. [126] It was the hour when the veil between what was and what could be grew thin enough to step through. [146]

She had been weaving between worlds since childhood, though she had not always known what to call it. [164] Her grandmother had called it dreaming with your boots on. [174] Her orchard master, old Tev, had called it the pruner's gift--the ability to see not only the tree before you but the tree it was reaching toward, and to help it arrive there. [208] Over decades, Maren had come to understand that this gift extended beyond bark and branch. [223] She moved through thresholds invisible to most--between the waking world and the dreaming one, between the orchard of the present and the orchard of memory. [249]

It was in that deeper orchard where she had found the child. [261]

He sat in the hollow of an ancient pear tree that no longer existed in the physical world, knees drawn up, eyes dull as winter clay. [287] His name was Solen, and he was nine years old, and he had stopped believing that the world held anything worth seeing. [309] Grief had done it--the kind that descends on a child without explanation and refuses to lift. [326] Maren had recognized the look immediately. [332] She had worn it herself, once, for a terrible year after her mother's passing. [346]

She could not pull him back by force. [354] What he needed was succor of a deeper kind--not comfort exactly, but restoration. [368] She needed to give him back the color of wonder, which grief had leached from everything he saw. [386]

This was why she carried the heirloom seed. [394]

It had been pressed into her palm by Tev on the morning he died, a small dark oval that smelled of autumn and distant rain. [419] "It contains everything a growing thing has ever wanted to become," he had told her, and she had assumed he meant it for the orchard. [444] She understood now that she had been wrong. [452] The seed was not for planting in soil. [460] It was for planting in a moment. [467]

She was insatiably creative in how she worked--always finding angles others dismissed, always pressing past the obvious solution. [486] So rather than simply speaking to Solen, she chose a different approach. [498] She pressed the seed against the wet stone of the parapet, where the waterfall's mist had gathered, and she began to hum. [520] The sound rose and wove itself into the falling water, into the green moss, into the particular light that filtered through the early canopy above. [545] By capturing essence--the essence of this place, this threshold, this trembling morning--she drew it through the veil and into the hollow where Solen sat. [571]

He looked up. [574] His eyes found the light before they found her. [583]

The hollow filled slowly with color--the deep green of the moss, the silver thread of the water, the gold at the rim of every unfurling leaf. [610] Maren watched the dullness leave his face the way frost leaves a window when warmth returns. [626] He reached out one small hand and touched a beam of that woven light. [640]

"Is it real?" he asked. [645]

"It is the most real thing I know how to make," she said. [658]

She climbed back through the threshold and stood again on the verdant parapet, alone now, the empty hand still warm where the seed had been. [683] Below, the waterfall continued its ancient, unhurried work. [691] Above, the first leaves of spring finally opened. [699] Maren lifted her pruning shears and got back to the trees. [710]
