Maren had always understood that a maze was not meant to be solved. [13] She worked in the living bridge garden at the valley's edge, where fig trees had spent three centuries learning to become architecture. [35] Their roots, trained by patient hands long dead, arched over the ravine in curves that obeyed no straight geometry, forming bridges that breathed in the rain. [61] It was here that Maren laid out her designs -- not on paper, but through hyperbolic geometry, sketching paths that folded inward upon themselves until the interior space exceeded what the exterior could logically contain. [95] Visitors entered her mazes and felt the ground multiply beneath their feet, felt the hedges lean with a quiet, vegetable intelligence. [116]

She carried the halo fragment in the breast pocket of her coat, wrapped in linen. [131] It had come to her from a demolished chapel -- just a curved sliver of gilded plaster, no larger than a finger bone, that had once framed the head of a painted saint. [163] She was not religious, but the fragment trembled when she held it near old growth, and she had learned to trust that trembling. [186]

The photographs had begun fading three months ago. [194] They were archival prints, a collection she had assembled from estate sales and library donations -- images of the living bridge garden across its long history. [219] In the earliest, the fig trees were young, their roots barely reaching the opposite bank. [234] In the middle years, the bridges were thick with secondary growth, colonized by ferns and pale orchids. [251] But the images were losing their silver, bleaching out from the edges inward, as if time were quietly consuming its own evidence. [273] Maren had tried to halt the decay with archival sleeves and controlled humidity, but the photographs continued to disintegrate, not through neglect but through some principle she could not yet name. [304] She suspected the garden itself was responsible. [311]

This was the nature of being retrocognitively trapped: when Maren pressed her palms against the bark of the oldest bridge-tree, she did not merely feel wood. [337] She felt the specific weight of the hands that had tended it before hers -- the seasonal anxiety of a gardener who feared a dry summer, the grief of a child who had carved initials into a low branch and returned as an adult to find them swallowed by growth. [386] The garden held its history in its tissue, and Maren could not choose when to receive it. [403] It arrived the way weather arrives: without asking. [411]

She had designed this maze to be her last. [420] The paths curved through hyperbolic space in a pattern she had derived from the growth rings of the oldest tree, translated into walking distance and turn-angle. [446] To walk the maze was to move through a compressed record of the garden's life -- not metaphorically, but structurally, the geometry itself a kind of reading. [472] She had not told anyone this. [478] She was not certain she could explain it without sounding as though she had come undone. [494]

On the morning she finished, she brought the photographs into the maze. [506] The halo fragment was warm against her ribs. [514] She laid the prints along the central path, one by one, and stood at the heart of the hyperbolic spiral she had grown from living yew and box and climbing rose. [545] The garden was very quiet. [550] She could feel the old roots beneath the stone path, pressing upward with a slow and ancient patience. [568]

The photographs began to go. [573] Not burning -- dissolving, the silver lifting in a fine luminous mist that did not disperse but hovered at eye level, trembling. [594] The images of the garden rose out of their paper bodies and hung in the curving air of the maze, overlaid upon the living garden itself, past and present occupying the same coordinates of space. [629] Maren reached out and let her fingers pass through a hovering image of the bridge in winter, ninety years ago. [649]

She felt it. [652] The texture of time was not smooth. [659] It was fibrous, layered, warm in some places and cold in others, shot through with grief and with the specific happiness of people who had not known they were happy. [689] The halo fragment in her pocket grew still at last, as though it had been waiting for exactly this. [708]

Maren stood in the quaking light of dissolved photographs and understood that she had not been designing mazes. [726] She had been designing the instrument through which she could finally feel what she had always suspected: that time is not a river but a root system, vast and tangled, holding everything it has ever touched. [762]

She let the light take her hands. [769] She did not try to find the exit. [777]
