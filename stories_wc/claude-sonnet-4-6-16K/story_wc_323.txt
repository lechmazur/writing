After the aliens arrived, the sky meant something different. [9] Even from the bottom of the ocean, where the benthic city of Threnody pressed its glass domes against the abyssal floor, people looked up through two miles of dark water and thought about stars they could not see. [47] Maren Solis had been a navigation instructor for eleven years, and she understood this longing better than most. [65]

Her classroom was a narrow chamber at the city's outermost edge, where the dome curved so steeply that students could press their foreheads against the cold glass and watch bioluminescent worms drift past like slow, patient comets. [102] She preferred this room because the curvature reminded her of something the alien envoys had said during their first broadcast: that space was not empty but folded, that what humans called horizons were merely creases waiting to open. [140]

Unfolded horizons. [142] The phrase had traveled through every language on Earth within hours, and it had not left Maren alone since. [161]

She held up a single chain link from an old boat anchor, iron, corroded green at the joints, roughly oval. [181] It had belonged to her grandmother, who had navigated the surface seas before the coastal floods drove most of humanity downward. [202] "Look at this," Maren told her six students. [210]

They were adults, all of them reassigned from surface professions that no longer existed. [224] A former lighthouse keeper, two cargo pilots whose routes had been grounded during the contact quarantine, a marine biologist, and two young engineers who had grown up knowing nothing but the benthic city's recycled air. [259] They were, each in their own way, balancedly curious: not panicked by the alien disclosure, not recklessly thrilled, but holding the new knowledge carefully, the way you hold something heavy and fragile at the same time. [295]

"This link connects to nothing right now," Maren said, turning the chain link in the low blue light. [313] "But it still has geometry. [318] It still has an inside edge and an outside edge, and the difference between those two edges is how we survive any navigation problem, alien contact included." [345]

She moved to the transparent wall and placed the link flat against the glass. [359] Outside, the benthic city expanded in both directions: towers of pressurized concrete and biolamp arrays, research platforms suspended above the sediment like enormous mechanical jellyfish, transit tubes threading between domes at odd, organic angles. [393] It had grown without a master plan, this city, the way coral grows: following pressure, following need. [410]

"Edge geometry," she said, tracing the link's inner curve with one finger. [422] "The aliens navigate by collapsing the distinction between inside and outside. [433] Their ships don't travel through space; they travel along the boundary where one kind of space meets another. [451] Every horizon is a seam. [456] Every seam can become a door." [462]

The lighthouse keeper, Dav, raised his hand. [469] "But we're trying to learn starlight navigation," he said. [478] "Stars we can't even see from here." [485]

"Exactly," Maren said, and she meant it with a weight she hadn't intended. [498] To learn to navigate by starlight when you are buried beneath miles of ocean, when the stars have just been revealed as inhabited and watching, this was not a technical problem. [529] It was a spiritual one. [534]

She set the chain link down on the table between them. [545] The iron made a soft sound, patient as a held breath. [556]

"The aliens gave us one instrument," she said. [564] "Not a device. [567] A principle. [569] They told us that every closed system has an exterior, and every exterior can be read like a map if you understand the shape of its boundary." [596]

She looked at her students, their faces somber, open, suspended between the world they had known and the one currently pressing against the dome glass. [621] "We are going to expand what navigation means," she said. [631] "We are going to use edge geometry to find the stars from inside the dark, the same way those envoys found us: by reading the curve of the boundary from the inside out." [664]

Outside the dome, a thermal vent released a slow column of particles that rose and scattered and disappeared into the black above. [686] It looked, in the low blue light, almost exactly like a constellation learning to breathe. [701]

Maren picked up the chain link again and held it toward the glass, framing nothing, framing everything. [718] "This is where we begin," she said. [725]
