After the phoenix rose, ash still threaded the air above the glassblower's courtyard, settling like grey snow on every cracked tile and scorched post. [24] Sera had chosen this place deliberately, though she would never say so plainly. [37] She was innocently cunning that way, always appearing to stumble into exactly the right situation. [52]

She arranged three white daisies against a pot of dark laurel beside the courtyard gate, which meant: I am open, and I am willing. [76] Anyone who had traded with her before understood the language of her arrangements. [89] Those who did not were not yet meant to find her. [100]

Sera was colorblind, a fact she kept not as a secret but simply as a quiet companion. [117] She had never seen the famous amber of the glassblower's molten rivers, or the scarlet plume of the phoenix in its rising. [139] What she had seen, and what she painted, was the world as a study in pressure and weight: the way glass bulged before it found its shape, the way a body leaned into grief. [173] She had learned that imperfection was not the absence of beauty but its truest address. [188]

On the third morning after the phoenix's ascent, a man named Dov crossed the courtyard carrying almost nothing. [206] He held between two fingers a concertina reed tongue, a small metal slip that had once made music before the instrument cracked in the fire. [231] He set it on the low wall beside Sera's newest arrangement -- a bruised orchid flanked by two sprigs of rue -- which said: I know you are broken, and I am not afraid. [263]

"They say you trade awakenings," he said. [270]

She did not answer right away. [276] She picked up the reed tongue, pressed it lightly against her own lip, and felt its cold edge. [294] "What did you bring it for?" she asked. [302]

"I used to play," Dov said. [308] "I thought maybe you could make something of it." [317] His eyes moved to her canvases stacked against the courtyard wall, their surfaces mapped with grey tones and lines of extraordinary precision. [339] "I don't understand what I'm looking at," he admitted. [348] "But I feel like I should." [354]

This was how bartered awakenings worked. [360] Not with money, not with explanation, but with an honest object placed in honest hands, and the willingness to receive something you could not predict. [385] Sera had learned this in the weeks after the phoenix rose, when the old currencies of comfort had burned with everything else. [407]

She took the reed tongue to her worktable, set it at the center of a small arrangement: a single bent stem of grass, an unblossomed bud, two smooth pebbles placed not symmetrically but honestly, one slightly higher than the other. [447] The arrangement said: this is what remains, and it is enough. [458] Then she picked up a brush. [464]

She painted Dov the way she saw him: not in the colors he thought he wore, but in the shapes his history had pressed into him. [490] The slouch at the left shoulder where he had carried the concertina. [502] The angle of his jaw, which was not symmetrical, which was better for being uneven. [517] The particular cant of his hands, which still knew the motion of the bellows even with nothing to hold. [536]

When she turned the canvas toward him, he went very still. [547] "That's what I look like?" he asked. [554] "That's what you are," she said. [560]

It was not a comfortable thing to be seen that precisely. [571] But something in him, some old and resistant valve, opened. [581] He felt himself awaken to his own outline, as though the fire had burned away a fog he had mistaken for his face. [604]

He left the reed tongue with her. [611] She placed it in the next arrangement, resting across the bent stem of grass, a small silver tongue in a language she was still learning to speak. [638] Outside the courtyard gate, the ash was settling into the cracks between tiles, and already, in those grey lines, small things were beginning to press through. [664]
