Maren had worked in the mountain bookstore for eleven years, ever since remembering became too painful to do anywhere else. [20] The store had no name anyone could agree on. [29] It occupied the entire interior of Mount Graval, carved by booksellers long dead into a labyrinth of ten thousand shelves spiraling down toward the mountain's dark core. [56] She had mapped only a third of it. [64] The rest she navigated by instinct, by the particular cold that rose from certain corridors, by the weight of silence near the restricted tiers. [88]

She was a mercury-glass seer, which meant she saw truth the way a funhouse mirror shows faces: recognizable, warped, and always just beyond correction. [112] What she saw was real, but tilted. [119] What she told customers, when she told them anything, came out sideways. [131] This was why she had stopped telling. [138] This was why she shelved books instead, running her fingers along spines, letting other people's words do the work her own had failed. [161]

At night, she substitutes reality. [166] Every seer did, in their own way, but Maren did it with unusual thoroughness. [180] She would descend into the lower corridors, where the stone walls dripped and the gas lamps burned amber, and she would build. [202] Not with her hands. [206] With the part of herself she had buried so deeply she had nearly forgotten it was a talent at all. [226] She could layer new atmosphere over the existing one, peel back the mountain's cold facts and replace them with something warmer, something that felt like the shape of home, even if she could not name what that shape was. [265] She had never seen it clearly. [271] She only knew its edges from what it was not. [281]

The lost language stone came to her on a Wednesday. [291] A customer left it on the counter beside a stack of cartography volumes, and then simply did not return. [310] It was small, pale gray, worn smooth, and carved with script that no living linguist had yet catalogued. [328] Maren recognized it the way you recognize a word you've never learned but have always somehow known. [345] She carried it for days without understanding why. [353]

The answer arrived via the texture of a feather. [362] She had found a hawk's feather wedged between two shelves on the seventeenth tier, impossibly pristine, and when she ran her thumb along its vane, the barbules separating and rejoining under her touch, something in her chest opened like a door that had been swelling in its frame for years. [412] The stone in her coat pocket grew warm. [420] She pulled it out, and beneath her fingers it was suddenly legible, not in any language she could speak aloud, but in the language the buried part of her had always used: the one made of sensation and gravity and the particular ache of almost-arriving. [465] It was telling her to seed something. [472] She understood. [474]

Maren went deeper into the mountain than she ever had, past the tiers with names, past the tiers with numbers, down to the level where the bookstore became something older than books. [506] She pressed the stone into a crack in the floor, into the living rock, and she felt the buried talent rise through her like hidden thunder, not loud, not explosive, but immense. [538] The kind of sound that arrives before you realize you have heard it. [551]

What she seeded was not a seed at all, not exactly. [562] It was a question she had been too afraid to ask. [573] It was the question of whether home was a place you return to or a place you build from the inside of a mountain, shelf by shelf, night by night, in a language no one else has yet found words for. [614] The stone did not answer. [619] But the mountain did, in the way mountains answer: slowly, and from the ground up, and with the confidence of something that has always known exactly where it stands. [648]

Maren climbed back toward the amber light. [655] She was still warped, still sideways, still a mercury-glass seer in a labyrinth that had no final page. [673] But the buried thing had broken surface now, and the texture of it against her palms felt, for the first time, like something she did not need to substitute. [702] It felt like the shape she had been mapping all along. [713]
