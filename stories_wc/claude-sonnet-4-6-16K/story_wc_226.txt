The canyon had no birds. [6] It had only bone towers, white and tall as accusations, and the echoes that lived between them like old arguments refusing to die. [32]

Sera arrived two days before the ritual, which gave her exactly enough time to cause exactly the right amount of trouble. [53]

She was a defiant artist, known among the canyon settlements for carrying chalk in her boot and conviction in her jaw. [74] The elders called her innocently mischievous the way people call a fire small before it spreads -- technically accurate for a moment, then suddenly not. [100] She had not burned a bridge yet, but she had drawn things on several. [113]

The ritual required silence. [117] The canyon demanded it. [121] When the bone towers hummed at dawn, the sound carried for miles, and everyone who had ever been forgiven by the canyon's old god was expected to gather, kneel, and present their accounting -- every harm done, every price still owed. [161] The god, they said, would settle the ledger. [169]

Sera did not believe in ledgers. [175]

What she believed in was the pale spire in the mist, visible from the canyon's highest ridge at first light. [194] She had seen it three times in her life, and each time it looked different -- once like grief, once like relief, once like a question she had not thought to ask. [229] This morning it looked like a door left open. [238]

She set up near the base of the central bone tower before the others woke. [253]

Her method was simple and, to the elders, deeply irritating. [263] She would begin speaking -- not preaching, never preaching -- just arranging words aloud until they caught on something in a listener's chest. [287] Impromptu poetry sessions, she called them. [293] The canyon did the rest, carrying each line between the towers, bouncing it off bone and limestone until a single phrase returned as five, as ten, as a chorus no one had intended. [327]

That was the point. [331] That was her whole art. [336]

To refract a single truth into many -- to send one hard thing out and let the canyon split it into every color of itself. [360]

She began before the first elder stirred. [367]

Into the forged hush of pre-dawn, she said, "Forgiveness is not free." [380]

The echo answered: not free, not free, not free, free, free. [391]

She let it settle. [395]

"It costs the one who gives it everything they had planned to stay angry about." [410]

That one traveled longer. [414] She heard it folding into the towers, heard it return slower, softer, as though the bone itself were considering the argument. [438]

People emerged. [440] You could not ignore a voice that the canyon had decided to repeat. [454] A woman wrapped in a grey traveling cloak sat on a stone nearby and said nothing. [470] A boy of perhaps twelve stood at the canyon's edge and listened with his whole body tilted forward. [488]

Sera was not supposed to be doing this. [496] The elders had been specific: the hours before the ritual were for quiet reflection, for personal accounting, not for unraveling everyone's settled grief in public. [524]

But she had seen the pale spire in the mist, and she understood what it meant now. [541] It meant the god's door was already open, which meant the god was already listening, which meant the ritual was already beginning whether the elders declared it or not. [570]

"The price of forgiveness," she continued, her voice low, shaped for the canyon's acoustics, "is agreeing to carry the same truth the person who hurt you carries." [598] "You are not released from knowing them." [605]

The echoes turned it strange. [610] Carry. [611] Truth. [612] Know them. [614] Knowing you. [616]

The woman in the grey cloak pressed her hand to her mouth. [628]

The boy sat down in the dirt. [635]

Sera did not stop. [639] She let each line go like a stone into still water, and the canyon did what canyons do -- it refused to let anything fall quietly. [667] Everything she offered it, she received back transformed, multiplied, strange. [678]

By the time the elders arrived, flushed with ceremony and visible irritation, every person in the canyon had already begun their accounting. [700]

Sera pocketed her chalk. [704]

She had not written a single word on a single surface, and yet somehow everything had shifted. [720]

The pale spire had dissolved into full morning. [728]

The door, she supposed, was now closed from the inside -- which meant someone on the other side had decided to stay. [750]
