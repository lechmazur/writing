The last ship had cleared the harbor two hours ago, and Maren sat alone in the palace garden, where the season had stripped the elms to bare arithmetic. [28] She had watched it go -- a dark comma pulling away from the quay -- and felt neither relief nor regret, only the particular lonesome splendor of a sky too large for one person to hold. [62]

In her coat pocket, wrapped in a fold of waxed paper, she kept a single thread pulled from the weft of the great carpet in the palace's eastern hall. [91] The carpet had depicted a woman walking toward water, and when Maren had first arrived in this city six months ago, she had pressed her palm to its surface as though it were a map. [126] She had worked a thread free not from vandalism but from need -- a tactile anchor, something to touch when she was composing. [148]

Maren's profession was difficult to explain at dinner tables. [157] She wrote obituaries for the living. [163] Not eulogies delivered at retirements, not anniversary tributes, but true obituaries -- vividly descriptive accounts of a person as they presently were, written as though mortality had already made its considered selection. [194] Her clients were people suspended in extraordinary transitions: the man resigning from a dynasty, the woman departing a marriage of twenty years, the daughter emigrating to a country whose language she did not yet speak. [229] They came to her because they needed their old selves formally witnessed before those selves dissolved into whatever came next. [249]

The work required her to understand explained absences. [257] Not simply why a person had vanished from a room or a life, but the geography of that removal -- the negative space shaped like a body, warm and pressurized, which persisted long after departure. [291] This was why she traveled. [296] She needed to visit the places people had left in order to write them accurately. [311] She needed to understand the geography of healing -- how a wound in space closes, how a town adjusts its posture once someone beloved has gone. [336]

In the garden, autumn had performed its ruthless inventory. [345] Persimmons hung like lanterns where leaves no longer competed, and the flagstones beneath her bench were cobbled with rust and shadow. [366] She opened her notebook and touched the loose thread against her lips -- an intimate gesture she allowed herself only when working, a small ritual of concentration. [391]

She was writing for a man named Aldous, who had lived in this palace for thirty-one years as the city's cultural minister, and who had boarded that last ship this morning without ceremony. [424] He had not explained his leaving to anyone in an official capacity, which meant the city was now doing what cities always do: filling his absence with speculation. [452]

But Maren knew. [455] She had spent three evenings with him in this very garden, listening while he described the particular exhaustion of projecting presence long past the point of genuine feeling. [483] He had maintained his office, his appearances, his careful public warmth -- by projecting presence like a man operating a puppet of himself from an increasing distance -- until the thread connecting him to all of it had grown nearly invisible. [522]

She pressed her pen to paper and began. [530]

The sentences arrived slowly, the way light does at dusk -- not fading but redistributing itself, finding new angles. [548] She described his voice, which held the specific timber of someone who had loved a city and watched that love mature into something more honest than passion. [575] She described the way he always sat with his left shoulder slightly raised, an old defensive posture he had never corrected because he claimed it kept him humble. [603] She described the emptiness he had left in the garden tonight -- the persimmons blazing in the last of the light, his chair vacant and somehow still warm in her imagination -- and how that emptiness was not abandonment but longitude. [642]

Maren set the thread flat against the final sentence as a bookmark. [654]

The city would explain his absence many ways in the months to come. [667] She was writing the only explanation that mattered: that a man had lived here fully, and had left before that fullness could become performance. [691]

The garden held the dark gently. [697] She stayed inside it, small and certain, one more thing the season had not yet decided to release. [715]
