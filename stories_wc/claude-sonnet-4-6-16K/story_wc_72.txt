The statue had stood in the garden for four hundred years, longer than anyone remembered the city above the waterline. [19] Now the city was below, swallowed by the sea after the great subsidence, and the garden had become something stranger than a garden - columns draped with anemones, flagstone paths thick with silt, roses calcified into coral shapes that fooled the eye. [58]

The diver found her on a Tuesday, which the statue knew because it read this from the diver's mind before the diver had descended twenty feet. [83]

The statue's name had been Maren once, when the sculptor chiseled the name into her base. [99] She did not move in the way living things moved. [109] She shifted like architecture shifts - slowly, with intention, with the grinding patience of stone that has decided. [126]

The diver's name was Sorrel. [131] She carried, in a mesh pocket against her ribs, a foxglove-stained glass ampoule - the purple bruise of it visible even in the dim cathedral light beneath thirty meters of water. [165] Sorrel was a researcher of old medicines. [172] She had followed old records to this place, records that described a garden where a particular preparation had been distilled, a cardiac remedy precise enough to kill or cure depending only on the dose. [203]

She had come certain of what she would find: ruins. [213]

Maren sent the first thought like a stone dropped into still water. [225]

*You are looking for something that no longer exists in the form you imagine.* [239]

Sorrel spun, her fins churning silt into slow clouds. [249] Then she was still. [253] Then she looked at the statue and understood she was not alone in any sense she had previously meant. [271]

This was the first revelation. [276]

*The ampoule,* Maren sent. *You already have it. It was in the archives you recovered, sealed these four centuries.* [295]

Sorrel's hand went to her ribs. [301] She had assumed the ampoule was empty, decorative, a relic. [312] She had not had it analyzed yet. [319] She had been certain it held nothing. [326]

This was the second revelation. [331]

Maren was incisively kind in her telepathy - she did not simply hand Sorrel truths, she aimed them at precisely the place where Sorrel's certainty was most brittle, and she did it gently, the way a surgeon is gentle with a blade. [373] After revealing the secret of the ampoule's contents, she waited for Sorrel's understanding to settle before she offered the third thing. [396]

*The garden still grows.* [400]

And it did. [402] Sorrel had mistaken the coral shapes for calcification, but now she looked again. [416] The roses had not died. [421] They had adapted. [424] They were something between rose and coral, a hybridization that four hundred years of pressure and salt and solitude had made possible. [448] The foxglove grew likewise - purple, alien, patient, changed. [457]

Maren had spent four centuries certain that the city's sinking was an ending. [470] That certainty had kept her company in the dark, kept her identity intact. [482] But watching Sorrel look at the roses with wonder rather than mourning, watching the researcher hold the foxglove-stained ampoule up in a shaft of filtered light and understand that what it contained had not degraded but transformed - Maren felt her certainty develop a crack. [529]

She did what she had not done in four hundred years. [540] She moved. [542]

She reached one stone hand toward a broken pillar, found the structural weakness where a fault line ran through the marble like an old argument, and began to thread a living foxglove stem through it - carefully, with her blunt fingers, feeding the plant through stone the way you feed thread through the eye of a needle. [591] It was an act of maintenance. [597] It was an act of welcome. [603]

Sorrel watched and understood: the city had not ended. [613] It had only exchanged one certainty for a question with more possible answers. [624]

This was the cascading kind of revelation - each one loosening the next like stones on a slope, until what had seemed like solid ground rearranged itself into something livelier and less predictable. [655]

Maren sent one last thought, and for the first time in centuries it surprised even herself. [671]

*Stay a while. I have more to show you than I knew I had.* [684]

The garden moved around them both - slowly, in the way that living things move when they have all the time that water carries and no reason left to be still. [714]
