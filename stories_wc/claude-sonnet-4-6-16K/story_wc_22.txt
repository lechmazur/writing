Old Maren had driven the Gloom Hill coach for thirty years, hauling salt and silence between the upland farms and the coast, and when the last reef finally bleached to bone in the summer's heat, she came down to the jetty alone. [42]

She carried only her seed pod collection in a canvas satchel -- sixty-seven pods, dried and rattling, gathered from battlefields across three provinces where armies had churned the earth to mud and left nothing but iron in the soil. [80]

The jetty itself bloomed improbably, its pilings wrapped in flowering sea-vine that had taken root when the fishing boats stopped coming, purple blossoms trembling in the twilight air as if embarrassed by their own survival. [115]

She stood at the dock's far end and looked out across the water, which was vast and close at the same time, a narrow corridor of dying color pressed between the darkening sky and the pale dead shallows below. [154]

The reef had been the battlefield. [160]

Not in any war that generals recorded, but in the longer contest between warming water and living coral, between industry and the slow calcium patience of the polyp -- a war waged in silence for decades, and now conceded. [198]

Maren set down her satchel and waited. [205]

She had learned patience from horses, and she had learned languages from her grandmother, who had pressed dying tongues into her like seeds pressed into clay: Dalmatian, Cornish, Livonian, Elamite -- languages that the living world no longer required but that the natural world, she had come to believe, still remembered in some form. [258]

The terns came first. [262]

Three of them, banking low over the dead shallows, calling in tight syllables that most people heard as noise and dismissed. [283]

Maren heard the word beneath the sound: here, here, soft ground, the sand accepts. [297]

She had never been able to explain how bird language folded into human grammar inside her mind, only that it did -- the way a dead tongue unlocks meanings in living words, the way old structure persists beneath new sound. [336]

The terns circled and she followed their pattern with her eyes, reading the quadrants of water they mapped for her, understanding which sections of the reef's ghost still held enough mineral sediment to receive life. [371]

She opened the satchel. [375]

The pods had come from Morvaine, from the river bend at Sector Nine where two armies had spent eleven months poisoning the ground, and from the ridge above Keslan where the trees had burned three times in the same decade. [415]

She had soaked them for a week in a tea brewed from kelp and estuary mud, a preparation she had found described in a Cornish agricultural text so old its author's name had dissolved from the page. [452]

The idea was simple and ancient: to fertilize ruin with ruin's opposite, to drop seeds into places so thoroughly destroyed that they had circled back around to a kind of openness. [483]

She dropped the first pod into the water. [491]

It fell without drama, a small dark shape swallowed by the pale shallows, and Maren felt the weight of it -- not the pod's weight, which was nothing, but the weight of the intention behind it, which was everything she had carried up and down that coastal road for thirty years without knowing it. [544]

The terns adjusted their circle, confirming the location. [552]

She continued along the jetty, dropping pods at intervals the birds prescribed, fertilizing each marked quadrant in turn while the light narrowed to a single copper thread along the horizon. [582]

What the reef had been in its living peak was a concord of ten thousand organisms holding one another in place -- a submerged agreement so intricate and so quiet that humans had not recognized it as agreement at all, had treated it as scenery. [626]

Now she was proposing a new concord, slower and stranger: not coral but something rooted, something that would rise toward air. [647]

It would not be what was lost. [653]

But a battlefield does not need its old war returned to it. [665]

It needs something that has never been there before, something that chooses the ruined ground precisely because nothing is competing for it, and grows in the vacancy with a ferocity that looks, from a distance, almost like peace. [703]

Maren dropped the last pod, listened to the terns confirm it, and stood a moment longer on the blooming jetty while the dark came in. [728]

Then she walked back up the hill to where the coach horse waited, patient and warm in the cooling night, and she climbed onto the box and took the reins, and drove. [760]
