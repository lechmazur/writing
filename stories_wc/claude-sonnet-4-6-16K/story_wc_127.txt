The amber grotto deepened at dusk, resin walls catching the last light like trapped centuries of honey. [17] Maren moved through it the way she always did — unhurried, precise, a professional whose thoroughness extended even to the angle at which she set her tools along the basalt ledge. [47] She was a dealer in counterfeit emotions. [54] Not the crude black-market kind that left clients feverish and regretful, but the calibrated, almost tender variety she had spent twelve years perfecting. [77] Her clients came wanting grief without devastation, love without surrender, longing without the inconvenience of an actual object. [95] She gave them something close. [100] Something half true. [103]

The blotter petal lay on the ledge, a fragrance chemist's tool she had repurposed with characteristic ingenuity. [120] Its cellulose fibers held the compound she had distilled that week — a synthetic nostalgia keyed to the olfactory signature of rain on warm stone. [144] The grotto provided its own version of that smell for free. [155] She appreciated the economy. [159]

Her client arrived on time. [164] He was a historian who had studied loss for thirty years without once feeling it, and the gap had begun to show in his lectures, in his writing, in the way his students left his seminars unmoved by the suffering he catalogued so brilliantly. [208] He needed empathy, he said. [213] What he meant was he needed to feel what he already understood. [225] The distinction mattered to Maren. [230]

She did not greet him with warmth. [237] Warmth was one of the emotions she sold; she could not afford to give it away casually. [254] Instead she offered him the quiet attention of someone who had already decided he was worth her effort. [272]

She reached for his hand before he offered it, trailing two fingers along the inside of his wrist, and felt the compound intelligence she had trained over years: the static charge his skin carried, the faint voltage differential that mapped his actual emotional state against the one his face performed. [322] He was afraid. [325] Not of her, but of what it would mean to finally feel the history he had spent his life inside without touching. [347]

She picked up the blotter petal and held it between them. [358] The scent opened slowly, the way dusk itself was opening the grotto — not arriving, exactly, but completing. [375]

"Half of what you feel in the next few minutes will be manufactured," she told him. [391] She always said this. [395] It was the most honest disclosure she made, and it was, as she knew, itself only half true. [413] The compound did not create emotions; it cleared the path to ones already present, waiting behind the meticulous architecture of a mind too trained to let them through. [441] She was not selling him grief. [447] She was selling him permission. [452]

He breathed in. [455]

The transformation was not dramatic. [460] It never was, in her experience. [466] The most real things completed themselves quietly. [473] She watched his face as the compound worked — professionally thorough in her observation, tracking the microexpressions the static reading had predicted, confirming that what moved across him was not performance but something older and more embarrassing and more true. [512]

As the transformation completed, she felt the familiar pull she never discussed with clients: the urge to subsume her own careful detachment into whatever they were experiencing, to let his newly unlocked grief pour through the same channels she had kept sealed for so long. [557] It was the reason she had entered this work. [566] Not cruelty. [568] Not profit. [570] The desire to fuse insight with empathy, to know what another person felt at the exact moment they finally allowed themselves to feel it — to stand that close to the threshold without crossing it. [604]

She withdrew her hand. [608]

The grotto held them both in its amber quiet, the resin walls indifferent as always to the small human negotiations conducted beneath them. [631]

He did not thank her when he left. [639] He was crying too earnestly for courtesy, and she respected that. [650]

She returned the blotter petal to its sleeve, smoothed her notes, and waited for dusk to finish its own slow work. [671]
