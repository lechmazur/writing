The canyon had no name anyone living could pronounce. [9]

Maret called it the Throat, because that is what it was -- a long cut through limestone, its walls lined with towers of bone pushed up from the earth over ten thousand years, white and ribbed as the inside of some enormous chest. [51]

She was the last storyteller who carried the copper veins -- not metaphor, but fact -- thin lines of mineralized blood that ran beneath the skin of her forearms and throat, tracing the branching paths of a circulatory system older than her grandmother's grandmother. [93]

She was also, as those who had known her longest would confirm, quietly articulate, the kind of person whose words arrived with the weight of stones placed deliberately, not thrown. [123]

She carried the diamond in a pouch against her sternum, and she tried not to touch it more than necessary. [143]

It was the size of a molar, clouded at its center with a crack that fractured the light into something angry, and people in three settlements had told her it was cursed -- had told her this, and then handed it to her anyway, relieved to be rid of it. [192]

She did not believe in curses. [198]

She believed in texture. [202]

The language she had come to preserve -- Vethani, the tongue of the canyon people -- had died across fifty years, one speaker at a time, until the last elder exhaled a final sentence that no living ear had understood, and the words dissolved into the Throat's permanent echo. [249]

That was the peculiar quality of this place: sound did not leave. [261]

The canyon worked as a tunnel of echoes, its geometry and bone-dense walls conspiring to trap resonance, layering every sound that had ever been made here into a low, sustained chord that hummed beneath all other noise. [298]

Maret believed the language was still here. [305]

Not as memory, not as record -- but as vibration, impressed into the flawed diamond the last elder had worn around her neck for sixty years, speaking into it the way others spoke into journals. [339]

The diamond had absorbed Vethani the way wood absorbs smoke. [349]

But absorption was not transmission. [354]

Texture was not sound. [358]

To translate the one into the other -- to pull the language's grain and pressure back out into audible form -- she needed a resonance she could not produce alone. [386]

She had been watching the birds for three weeks. [396]

Every morning, a flock of pale starlings erupted from the bone towers at the canyon's northern end and moved through the Throat in shifting formations, the whole mass of them bending and folding as though the air itself were thinking. [436]

Maret had learned to read these formations the way cartographers read coastlines. [448]

The patterns in a flock of birds in flight were not random; they were a distributed intelligence responding to invisible pressures -- wind, proximity, the geometry of the space around them. [478]

And that geometry, she realized, matched the internal fracture map of the diamond. [491]

The crack inside the stone was not damage. [499]

It was a record of the movements Vethani made inside a human mouth -- the tongue's curve, the palate's slope, the breath-path of consonants that had no equivalent in any surviving language. [530]

On the twenty-second morning, she stood at the canyon's narrowest passage, where the echo-tunnel was most concentrated, and she held the diamond out at arm's length in the early light. [560]

When the flock rose -- all at once, a single organism made of a hundred small bodies -- she watched their leading edge align with the angle she had calculated, and she did not hesitate. [594]

She did not speak. [598]

She let the canyon speak for her -- let the accumulated resonance harness itself through the pattern the birds inscribed against the pale sky, their motion conducting the hum of the Throat into a frequency that matched the texture locked inside the stone. [640]

The diamond rang. [643]

It was a small sound, and then it was not small at all -- it was a voice, or the memory of a voice, rising from the flaw at the diamond's heart, shaped by fifty years of a woman speaking her mother tongue into a stone she trusted someone would one day know how to open. [686]

Maret listened. [688]

She did not understand a single word. [695]

But she had not come to understand -- she had come to carry, and the canyon was generous, and the birds turned once more overhead, holding their pattern long enough for her to begin. [728]
