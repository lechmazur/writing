Father Aldric Voss had been a penitentiary chaplain for nineteen years, and he had learned that apologies, like broken mechanisms, rarely fixed themselves. [23] He pushed open the door of Calloway's Antique Music Box Repair, a shop wedged between a pawn house and a laundromat on Marsh Street, and stood a moment in the vibrant hush of the place. [58] Shelves rose to the ceiling, crowded with lacquered boxes of every size, each one silent and waiting, their inner workings stilled but not dead. [82] The smell of machine oil and old cedar settled over him like a vestment. [96]

"Second opinion?" said the woman behind the counter without looking up. [107]

Her name, a small placard announced, was Sena Calloway. [116] She was bent over a walnut box with a loupe pressed to one eye, her movements aptly deliberate -- no gesture wasted, no adjustment made twice. [141]

"Grover Wahl at the clockwork market said it was beyond saving," Father Aldric said, setting his cloth bag on the counter. [162] "I was hoping you might disagree." [168]

He drew out the object carefully. [174] It was a clockwork heart -- a fist-sized brass mechanism, each chamber segmented like a real anatomical heart, with a winding stem at its base and a ring of tiny pipes along the top that would, when working, release a sequence of notes. [216] A man named Delos Crane had built it in cell block C over eleven months, using scraps he had earned and traded and begged for. [241] Crane had killed a man eight years ago. [249] He had spent the time since trying to understand what that meant. [261]

Sena set down her loupe. [266] She picked up the heart and turned it slowly, the way you might turn a sleeping thing you did not want to wake. [289]

"Who does this belong to?" she asked. [296]

"The victim's daughter," Father Aldric said. [302] "She doesn't know it yet." [307]

Crane had written her a letter three months ago -- the real kind, the kind that cost something to write. [326] She had returned it unopened. [331] Father Aldric had understood her refusal completely, and had still felt, in the quiet of his office, that the story wasn't finished. [353] An apology left as words was only a beginning. [362] He had decided, carefully and without certainty, to turn that apology into action -- to carry it forward himself, to see if the heart could be made to sing before it was offered again. [395]

Outside, a storm that had been threatening since morning finally broke, and rain hit the shop window in long silver diagonals. "What do I owe you?" he asked. [644]
