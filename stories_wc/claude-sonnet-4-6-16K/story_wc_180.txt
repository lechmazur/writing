Mira called herself a quartz-eye mender, which meant, depending on who asked, that she fixed the crystalline sight-gauges used by glassblowers, or that she saw clearly through things other people preferred to leave opaque. [34] After the guild breached her contract without ceremony or apology, she did what she always did when institutions failed her: she walked until the city thinned and the old cathedral appeared at the road's edge like a bad memory she had never quite shaken. [78]

The breach had been clean, at least. [85] Three years of service, one morning's notice, and a locked gate. [96] She was endearingly feisty about it in the way only people who have outlasted worse can be -- not bitter exactly, but crackling, like wood that refuses to catch. [124]

Inside the cathedral, apple and pear trees had claimed everything above the pews. [137] Their roots gripped the old stone floor and their canopies spread wide through the broken vaulting, leaves pressing against the rafters in a dense, living ceiling that filtered the afternoon light into something green and cathedral-still. [173] Fruit hung among the carved angels. [179] The place smelled like September and like prayer, which Mira thought were probably the same thing. [195]

She sat in the last pew and set her bag beside her -- the old work bag she had grabbed by habit, not intention, because her good bag was still locked behind the guild gate. [229] She did not know what was in this one. [238] She had not opened it in years. [245]

She opened it now. [249]

Inside: a coil of copper wire, three cracked sight-gauges she had meant to mend and forgotten, a stub of chalk, a folded piece of wax paper containing something that had once been a sandwich, and, at the very bottom, her daughter Pell's yellow hair ribbon, the one Pell had lost at eleven and cried over for a week. [307] Mira sat with the ribbon in her hands and felt the specific weight of time that only small objects carry. [327]

Pell was thirty-four now and lived two provinces over and called once a month, which was precisely the frequency of a relationship that had not been ruined but had not been tended. [359]

The breach of contract, Mira realized, had not been with the guild. [371] That was merely the most recent example. [378] The original breach was older. [383]

She had taken what she privately called deciduous vows when Pell was born -- promises she understood from the start were seasonal, meant to be shed and renewed as her daughter grew, the way the orchard above her was already dropping its earliest leaves onto the stone floor in small bright circles of yellow. [436] You vowed to hold, then vowed to loosen. [444] You vowed to teach, then vowed to listen. [452] The vows changed because the child changed, and a mender who understood sight-gauges ought to have understood that a lens correct for one distance is wrong for another. [480]

She had, instead, persisted. [484]

She had persisted in the way she was endearingly feisty about everything: with conviction, without checking whether conviction was still what the situation required. [508] She had tried to bridge generational wisdom -- her own mother's hard-edged practicality, her grandmother's careful patience -- and had managed mostly to transmit the parts that were already outdated. [536]

Using the forgotten bag's contents now felt less accidental. [545] She straightened the copper wire between her fingers, the way she always did when she was thinking, and she looked up through the orchard canopy at the light. [573] The cracked sight-gauges, she could mend today. [580] They just needed time and a steady hand. [588]

She did not know if she could say the same for Pell. [600] But she took the ribbon from the bag and folded it once, carefully, and put it in her coat pocket -- the one she would not forget to empty. [628] Then she picked up the copper wire and the first cracked gauge and began. [642]

The cathedral held its breath around her. [649] Somewhere above, a pear dropped through the leaves, struck the stone, and split open, and the smell of it reached her like a kind of answer she had not known she was waiting for. [683]

She would call Pell. [687] She would not know what to say. [694] She never did, and she was beginning to think that not-knowing, offered honestly, might be the wisest inheritance she had left to give. [717]
