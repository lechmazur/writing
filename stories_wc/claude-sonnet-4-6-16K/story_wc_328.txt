Mara had been cataloguing strangevine for three weeks before she admitted she was afraid of the garden. [17] Not afraid of the dark — she'd worked enough midnight digs to know darkness was just an absence of light, manageable, mappable. [38] She was afraid of how alive the place felt underground, the way the roots seemed to lean toward her like curious animals, gravitationally playful in their slow impossible tilts. [67]

She'd taken the job as Theodora's apprentice to get close. [77] That was the honest truth she told no one. [86] Mara was a cryptid researcher, the kind who published under a pseudonym and kept her day job teaching soil science at a community college. [110] The reports coming out of this hollow had been too specific to ignore: something large, semi-translucent, humming in frequencies that made dogs lie flat and stare at the sky. [139]

But Theodora's underground garden — carved into the limestone shelf beneath her property, lit by strings of amber bulbs — had its own pull. [161] It smelled of loam and something older, mineral and green. [171]

The flint arrowhead turned up in week two, nested between the roots of a bloodroot plant. [187] Mara had pocketed it without telling Theodora. [194] It was worked with a precision she didn't recognize, the flaking patterns spiraling inward like a question rather than a point. [215] She kept it in her left hand while she worked, rubbing its edges until they warmed. [231]

After the missing return changed — that was how Theodora put it, flatly, stirring tea she never drank — Mara stopped pretending she was just here for the botany. [258] Three people from the hollow had gone missing over eighteen months. [269] They'd come back. [272] But they'd come back differently: slower in their speech, faster in their eyes, prone to standing at field edges at dusk and laughing at things no one else could hear. [301] Theodora had watched this happen before, she said. [309] She was not frightened. [313] She was waiting. [316]

The old cordless phone hung on a nail by the garden's entrance, its antenna bent, its screen long dead. [335] Mara had assumed it was decorative. [341] Then one night it crackled to life, and a voice — layered and grained with static — began to confess. [359]

It spoke in the rhythms of the missing. [367] Via the static-laden confessions on that old cordless phone, they described what had happened: not abduction, not transformation in any violent sense, but an encounter with what they called the untamed harmonic — a resonance that lived below the audible spectrum, in the bedrock, in the roots. [413] A harmony untamed by any human key or human want. [423] It did not take them. [428] It played with them. [432]

Mara had built her career on bitterness. [439] She'd lost a sister to a river when she was twelve, and the world's failure to offer any explanation had curdled in her chest for twenty years. [466] She'd gone into cryptid research the way some people go into theology: furious, demanding an accounting from the universe. [485] She'd imagined that finding the inexplicable would let her finally condemn it. [497]

But standing in Theodora's garden with the phone pressed to her ear and the arrowhead warm in her palm, she felt something shift. [520] The confessions weren't frightening. [524] They were tender, almost sheepish. [529] The returned ones laughed as they described it. [537] The harmonic, they said, had no malice. [544] It was gravitationally playful — it tugged and spun and let go, the way a creek plays with a leaf, not cruel, not kind, just exuberantly itself. [570]

She thought about her sister. [575] She thought about how twenty years of fury was just grief that hadn't found its punchline yet. [592] To metamorphose, she realized, you did not need to be taken anywhere. [604] You just had to stop insisting the world owed you a straight answer. [617] The bitterness she'd mistaken for purpose was only the setup — and the universe, it turned out, had always been waiting to deliver the cosmic joke. [642]

She set the arrowhead down between the bloodroot roots where she'd found it. [655] Outside, above the limestone, the hollow hummed at a frequency she was only beginning to learn to hear. [673] The amber bulbs swayed slightly, though there was no wind underground. [684] Mara laughed — a small, surprised sound, like something cracking open in the cold. [697] She was still afraid. [701] But fear, she was discovering, could be the opening note of something vast and unhurried and wildly, cosmically funny. [720] She picked up the phone again and listened until the static sounded, improbably, like music. [735]
