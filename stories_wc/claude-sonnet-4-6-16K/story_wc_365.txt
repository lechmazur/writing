Maren had spent twelve years mapping what others could not see. [11] Not terrain, not stars — sensation. [16] The tremor of grief in a doorway. [23] The static hum of anticipation along a corridor. [31] She charted the invisible architecture of feeling, and cities paid well for it, because knowing the emotional topography of a place was worth more than any blueprint. [58]

But she had come to the abandoned spaceport repair bay for nothing as clean as a commission. [75]

The bay stretched wide beneath her, its vaulted ceiling fractured and dark, old scaffolding draped in rust-colored shadows. [93] Three docked hulls sat gutted, their interiors stripped to ribs of cold alloy. [106] The air smelled of grease and something older — ozone, perhaps, or the particular stillness of places where mechanical things had once screamed with life and then gone quiet. [134] Maren moved through this silence the way she always did: slowly, fingertips reading the walls like braille. [151]

She had found the green glass shard two nights ago, wedged between the floor grating and a collapsed workbench. [170] It was small, no larger than a thumbnail, and perfectly smooth on one face. [184] She had pressed it to her cheek before she understood why, and in that contact she had felt — briefly, unmistakably — the sensation of someone else's memory. [210] Not hers. [212] A childhood kitchen. [215] A conversation just beyond hearing. [220] A truth being folded away before she could catch its shape. [231]

That was when she knew what she was actually mapping. [241]

The shard was a seam. [246] And through it, something was weaving between worlds. [254]

Maren set her instruments in the center of the bay floor: the resonance coils, the phase chalk, the copper bowl filled with salted water she had carried from the bay's old service sink. [287] A sensory map maker's tools looked liturgical to most people, and she had stopped correcting that impression. [304] She placed the shard at the center of the bowl and watched the water settle. [319]

In the murmur of half-remembered dreams — that was how her mentor, Cassia, had described the liminal frequency. [336] Not sleep, not waking, but the space between, where the mind remembered it was not entirely singular. [353] Maren had thought it was poetry. [359] She understood now it was cartography. [365]

The shard began to pulse. [370] Green light, slow and cyclically spontaneous — no rhythm she could predict, no pattern that repeated, only an organic surge that came when it chose to come, like breath in a sleeping body. [402] The water in the bowl responded, rippling outward in rings that did not decay at the edges but instead bent, curving back in geometries that had no business existing in three-dimensional space. [434]

Maren felt the doorways open. [439]

Through parallel universes, the path threaded — she sensed them as pressure variations, as the specific weight of different atmospheres, each one dense with its own version of history. [467] She had never gone this deep before. [474] Most sensory maps stopped at the surface of what was. [484] This went underneath, into what else was, what had been and what had been refused. [499]

She followed the thread to the withheld truth. [507]

It arrived not as language but as mass. [515] A thing Cassia had known and chosen not to carry forward. [526] That the mapping itself was a form of taking — that to chart the emotional weight of a place was to diminish it, to lift the feeling from its host and press it flat into notation. [561] Every map Maren had made had cost something she had never been asked about. [575] Every city that paid her well had grown colder in her wake. [587]

The weight of it was enormous. [593]

Maren lifted the green glass shard from the bowl. [602] The light faded. [605] The parallel corridors sealed themselves, one by one, like rooms accepting their doors. [618]

She could redirect this. [622] That was still possible. [626] She could learn to map without extraction — to describe sensation without consuming it, to mark what was present without drawing it away. [648] The method would mean unlearning twelve years. [655] It would mean returning fees, or at least offering something back. [666]

She pressed the shard into her coat pocket, gathered her tools, and walked out of the repair bay into the cold open dark of the spaceport's dead yard, where the ships had not flown in thirty years and the ground still remembered the scorched shape of their departure. [714] She began her first honest map. [720]
