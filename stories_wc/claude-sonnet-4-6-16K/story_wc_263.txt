Maren had always heard what others could not say. [9]

When her mother stood in the doorway of the old observatory, wringing her hands and speaking of supper, Maren heard the grief underneath -- the silent plea that her daughter would someday come down from the rooftop and return to something ordinary. [50]

She never did. [53]

The observatory itself had gone dark three winters ago, its great brass orrery seized by rust, its dome half-hinged and groaning at every gust. [77]

But Maren kept returning, because the structure still breathed with something she could not name. [92]

She called it remembered stardust -- the residue of every observation ever made inside those walls, pressed into the stone like a fossil of wonder. [116]

She was a moonstruck dreamer, and she knew it, and she wore the label the way saints wear thorns: with a kind of painful pride. [141]

Tonight she stood beneath the aurora-lit archway at the observatory's eastern entrance, where the carved stone pillars framed a sky gone violent and green. [165]

The light pulsed like a slow heartbeat, and she heard it speaking -- not in sound, but in the unspoken language she had always understood, the language of things that have no mouth. [197]

The aurora said: it is time. [203]

She carried the sextant under one arm, her grandfather's astronomical instrument, brass-heavy and cold as a held breath. [221]

Its mirrors had been ground by a man who believed that angles were prayers, and when Maren pressed her thumb along its arc, she believed it too. [248]

For years she had tried to make something -- paintings, poems, music scored on the backs of receipts -- but every attempt collapsed before it reached form, like stars that cool before igniting. [279]

Her creativity was not absent; it was trapped. [287]

She felt it behind her sternum, a pressure that had no exit, a song stuck at the base of the throat. [308]

She had been searching for a way to free it across what she could only think of as an unwritten calendar -- no dates, no seasons she recognized, only a long sequence of restless nights that blurred into one another like watercolors left in rain. [352]

She stepped through the archway and let the aurora wash over her. [364]

The method had come to her in a half-dream two weeks ago: trace the path of falling stars. [382]

Not metaphorically -- physically, with the sextant raised, measuring the angle of descent as each streak cut across the dome of the world. [404]

She believed -- she heard this belief spoken in the silence between her own heartbeats -- that each meteor followed a line laid down before time was counted, and that those lines, if mapped, would form something like a key. [442]

She raised the sextant. [446]

The first star fell at eleven degrees west of the meridian, a brief bright scratch that healed over instantly. [465]

She tracked it, whispered the angle, felt something loosen in her chest. [477]

The second fell nearly vertical, plunging like a dropped needle. [487]

She tracked that one too, and the loosening deepened into an ache that was almost sweet. [503]

Then she did something she had not allowed herself to do in years: she rested. [518]

Not collapsed, not surrendered -- but rested, letting the sextant hang at her side, letting the aurora do what it wanted with the air around her, letting the remembered stardust in the old stone hum without her trying to translate it. [558]

The unspoken words of the sky gathered in her, and she did not try to speak them back. [576]

She just received. [579]

And then, without drama, without the flourish she had always imagined would accompany such a moment, she understood that the trap was not in her chest at all. [607]

The trap had been the constant reaching -- the straining forward into an unwritten future while the stardust behind her went unclaimed. [628]

She turned and looked back through the aurora-lit archway at the dark observatory, and she saw it finally as it was: not a ruin, but a record. [655]

Not a place of forgotten science, but a library of every patient night a human being had ever spent looking up and listening. [678]

She lifted the sextant one more time, not to measure, but to salute. [691]

The aurora answered in its voiceless tongue, and she heard it perfectly, and at last the song in her throat opened like a door that had only ever been resting against its frame, waiting for someone to stop pushing and simply let it swing. [735]

She began to walk home, composing as she went, the night enormous and generous around her. [751]
