Eleanor kept meticulous records of everything that died. [8] For twelve years she had run the Millbrook Nursery, cataloguing every frost-damaged fern, every root-rotted begonia, every vine that surrendered to the relentless storms of late autumn. [37] Storm season was her most productive research period — and her most humbling. [49]

The trellis behind the main greenhouse had stood there longer than her ownership. [62] Moss had colonized every crossbar, thick and spring-green, drinking the rain that came down in curtains each October. [80] She came to it every morning after a storm, notebook in hand, to study how the climbing plants responded to stress — which vines tightened their grip, which ones let go. [111]

This particular morning she found a linden leaf pressed against the lowest rung, plastered there by the night's downpour. [130] It had traveled some distance; the nearest linden stood at the edge of the property, past the herb beds. [149] She peeled it carefully from the wet wood and held it to the grey light. [164] Its shape was perfect, heart-formed and veined like a map of something interior, something she had no clinical name for. [183]

She set the leaf in her notebook and told herself it was a specimen. [197]

She told herself this the way she told herself most things — circuitously, with detours through practicality and productivity before arriving, quietly, at what she actually meant. [224] What she actually meant was that she was tired. [233] Not of the nursery, not of the plants, but of being the person who only watched transformation without allowing it to happen inside her own skin. [258]

The research had started honestly enough. [264] She had wanted to understand how plants reconstructed themselves after trauma — how a broken stem calloused over, how a flooded root system slowly reestablished its oxygen pathways. [293] The plants, she had observed, did not resist their own changes. [304] They simply continued, pressing growth into whatever space the damage left behind. [316]

She, by contrast, had held every aspect of her life with both hands. [329] The nursery layout, the supplier contracts, the precise schedule of her feeding rotations — all of it controlled, ordered, buffered against surprise. [350] Even her grief, when her mother died three years ago, had been organized into manageable tasks: estate paperwork, obituary, the transplanting of her mother's beloved rosemary into the nursery's front beds where Eleanor could tend it daily and call that tending something other than mourning. [395]

The storm that had passed through last night was the worst of the season. [409] Three climbing roses had pulled the eastern trellis section loose. [420] A jasmine she had been training for two years lay tangled and broken at the base. [436] Her first instinct, as always, was to reconstruct — to tie, prop, redirect, restore order before disorder could spread. [454]

But the linden leaf was still in her notebook. [463]

She stood at the mossy trellis with her tools in her bag and found she could not open the bag. [483] The moss was extraordinary after rain, almost luminous, the green of something that had given up competing and simply become itself completely. [505] It had never asked permission to colonize the wood. [514] It had simply arrived and stayed. [520]

Eleanor set her bag on the wet ground. [528]

She sat beside the broken jasmine and did not fix it. [539] She watched water move along its broken stem, bead at the wound, catch light. [554] She understood, slowly, in the way she understood most true things, that she had been reconstructing around her own metamorphosis for years — building structure over the exact place where she was changing, trying to prevent what was already in progress. [594]

Releasing control did not feel like freedom at first. [603] It felt like standing in a storm without a coat, ridiculous and exposed. [616] But the jasmine was still alive. [622] The moss did not care about the damage to the trellis, only about the moisture and the surface. [640] The plants she studied every season had never needed her permission to transform. [653]

She opened her notebook to the linden leaf and wrote one sentence: the specimens do not resist. [669]

Then she left the broken jasmine where it lay and walked back through the wet garden, already different, still becoming. [688]

The change was not finished. [693] She suspected it would not be finished for some time. [703] Storm season still had weeks left, and she had, she realized now, a great deal more to lose control of. [723]

That was, she decided, the beginning of the real research. [733]
