Mara called herself a portrait thief, though she had never stolen a wallet in her life. [16] What she took was harder to replace: the precise geometry of a stranger's face under golden-hour light, the thirty-minute window when the sun dissolved into copper and every human being looked briefly divine. [48] She had been quietly savvy about it for years, moving through cities with a small camera and smaller ambitions, until the jar arrived. [71]

It came wrapped in brown paper, delivered to her apartment by a courier who smelled of rain. [88] Inside was a glass jar sealed with black wax, and inside the jar - she had to hold it to the window to believe it - light moved. [114] Not reflected light, not phosphorescent chemicals. [120] Moonbeams. [121] Someone had actually captured moonbeams, bottled them like preserves, and mailed them to her. [135]

The note said only: Emerald Station. Sunset. Follow the flowers. [145]

She had heard of the emerald station, a pre-war terminal on the city's eastern edge whose tiled walls had gone the color of old jade. [170] By day it was a ruin. [176] By moonlight, supposedly, it became something else. [183]

She went at the dying of the afternoon, when the fleeting golden sunset still clung to the rooftops and the streets ran orange as embers. [208] The station was half-collapsed but breathing, its archways intact, its jade tiles catching the last of the sun and holding it like a throat holds a secret. [235]

The flowers were already there. [240]

Three arrangements, placed on a crumbling ticket counter, each one a careful architecture of stems and petals: red dahlias wrapped around white lilies, yellow freesia threaded through dark ferns, a single blue iris standing alone at the center of a ring of marigolds. [283] To anyone else, they were just flowers. [290] But Mara had spent two years photographing a botanist who sent love letters through bouquets to a woman across an embassy line - she knew how to reinterpret a coded flower arrangement, how to read the alphabet hidden in color and position and number. [333]

She worked quickly, the golden light thinning behind her, and translated: The jar opens what the portrait locks. [350] Use both or use neither. [355]

She looked at the jar in her satchel. [363] She looked at the portraits on her camera. [371]

There were thousands of them - years of golden-hour faces, strangers mid-laugh or mid-thought, caught in illuminated moments they would never know they had surrendered. [396] Each one was a door, she had always felt that privately, a door into another person's interior life. [413] She had never admitted why she really did it - not for art, not for money, but for something more embarrassing, more essential. [435] She wanted to press her ear against destiny's door. [444] She wanted to hear if something on the other side was listening back. [457]

The sun dropped. [460]

The emerald station became exactly what people said it became. [470] The jade tiles absorbed the rising moonlight and began, quietly, to glow - not brightly, not impossibly, but the way a living thing glows with its own interior warmth. [498] The ruined arches looked like cathedral ribs. [505] The broken clock on the far wall read a time that no longer existed. [519]

She unscrewed the jar. [523]

The moonbeams did not escape so much as expand, filling the station with a luminous silver that made every shadow seem temporary. [545] And in that light, something happened to the faces on her camera screen - they shifted, not moving exactly, but becoming more than they had been, the illuminated moments deepening, each portrait opening like a room she could step inside. [584]

She stood in the emerald station in a state of luminous disbelief, holding a jar of impossible light, reading coded flowers in a dead language, surrounded by the faces of strangers who had handed her, unknowingly, the doors she had always needed. [626]

And from somewhere behind the tiles, or behind the air itself, something listened back. [640]
