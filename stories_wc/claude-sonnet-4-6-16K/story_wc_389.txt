The observatory sat above the cloudline like a cracked tooth in a giant's smile, all iron scaffolding and ambition welded together by someone who never once planned to come back down. [33] Mira had been that someone. [38] For eleven years she had lived among the instruments and the static, tending banks of radio receivers that hummed and spat like feverish animals, pulling signals from frequencies the broadcast authorities had declared either dead or dangerous. [75] She called them her choir. [80]

The cobalt glass portico at the observatory's eastern face was where she spent her dawns, watching lightning lacerate the clouds below while her receivers murmured and crackled in the rooms behind her. [112] The glass was thick and old, the color of a deep ocean held vertical, and it transformed every bolt of storm light into something cathedral and slow. [138] She had built the portico herself from salvaged panels, hauling each one up the access shaft because she liked how the blue light made her feel like a thought still being formed. [171]

The frequencies had started changing three years ago, right around the time the prophet Solen began drawing crowds in the lowland cities. [193] Mira had noticed first, because noticing is what lonely visionaries do when there is no one else around to share the noticing with. [220] The signals came in broken, stuttering, beautiful, and she transcribed them by hand into notebooks whose pages had started to feel less like records and more like scripture. [249]

Through fragmented radio frequencies, something was trying to unfold. [258] She was certain of it the way she was certain of very little else, with the bone-deep certainty that bypasses logic entirely and sits directly in the chest like a warm coal. [291] What unfolded was a map, or perhaps a song, or perhaps both, the kind of thing that resists category the way water resists being folded. [317] It described old shadow geometries, arrangements of power that predated every current allegiance, every flag, every agreement between cities and councils and the tired men who ran them. [346]

Solen, she suspected, was reading a version of the same map. [357] She had seen his broadcasts on the small screen she kept only for evidence, watched how he moved his hands in patterns that matched frequencies she had charted, how his speeches were lyrically prophetic in the way that true things sometimes are, full of image and rhythm and the feeling that the speaker has swallowed something older than language and is trying politely to give it back. [427]

But Solen's allegiance was variant, and Mira had not missed that either. [439] He spoke of dawn but described it in the shadows' terms, not to dispel them but to wear them, to shape a new dawn using ancient shadows the way a lens shapes light by bending it through darkness and glass. [477] The crowds heard hope. [481] Mira heard the frequencies underneath. [486]

She was not, she had decided, a counter-prophet, because counter-prophets require followers and she had none, not even a dog, and the word prophet itself felt like a coat worn by too many strangers. [521] She was simply the woman in the stormlit observatory above the clouds, reading what the sky was trying to say. [541]

On the night Solen declared his movement had a name, lightning struck the eastern scaffolding and every receiver in the observatory screamed at once, a single unified frequency she had never heard, clear and unbroken and almost gentle. [579] She stood in the cobalt glass portico with blue light washing over her hands and understood that variant allegiance was not betrayal but navigation, the choosing of a different road through the same dark wood. [613] She picked up her transmitter, the old one with the cracked dial and the handwritten frequency list taped to its side, and she began to broadcast. [640] Not against Solen. [643] Simply beside him, the way a second instrument joins a song already in progress and changes what the song had always meant. [664]

Below, the clouds roiled and split, and for one brief second she could see the lights of a city, warm and scattered like a sentence someone had dropped and not yet picked up. [698] She kept broadcasting. [701] The frequencies kept coming. [705] The dawn, as it turned out, was large enough for more than one kind of shadow to help make it. [724]
