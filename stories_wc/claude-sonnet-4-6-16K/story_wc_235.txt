The grave marker was pine, not stone, which somehow made it worse. [12] Maren had found it three weeks ago at the edge of the tree line, fifty yards from the mountain ranger cabin she had occupied since autumn -- her name carved plainly, no dates. [44] She had stood there long enough for the cold to migrate into her boots, then walked back inside and unrolled her birch bark scroll across the table without a word to anyone, because there was no one. [81]

She was a lucid dream chronicler. [87] It was not a job that came with colleagues. [96]

The scroll was four feet long and covered on both sides in her own cramped notation -- dreams layered over dreams, margin notes correcting earlier margin notes, arrows looping back through weeks of entries that no longer tracked linear time. [135] This was the occupational hazard: spend enough years navigating the architecture of sleeping minds, and the waking world starts to lose its timestamps. [158] Maren was chronologically confused in the precise clinical sense, her neurologist had said once, before she stopped seeing neurologists. [177] Tuesday felt equidistant from both last March and 1987. [186] She had learned to live inside that particular vertigo. [195]

But the grave had done something new to her. [204]

She kept returning to it in her dreams -- which was itself a professional perk, the ability to revisit and examine. [224] In the dreams, the marker always had dates, though they shifted each time she looked. [239] Born in one version, dead in another, the spans between expanding and contracting like breath. [254] She documented everything on the birch bark scroll, pressing hard because the surface resisted ordinary ink and she used a sharpened stick dipped in pine resin. [280] The act of writing felt ancient and necessary. [288] It was the only method she trusted anymore: to distill chaos into narrative, to make the howling static of experience become something a person could hold. [314]

The problem was the earlier entries. [320]

Reading them back, she found a version of herself she no longer recognized -- sharper, more certain, writing with a velocity that made her current hand feel arthritic. [347] The early Maren had documented a dream in which she had learned to soar above a coastal city, the sensation so precisely rendered that it occupied a full eight inches of bark, every thermal and updraft mapped like a nautical chart. [388] She remembered writing it. [392] She did not remember the feeling. [398]

That was when the jealousy arrived -- not the soft domestic kind, but something older and less mannered. [415] Feral nostalgia, she wrote at the top of a new section, underlining it twice. [429] The past self had soared. [434] The past self had not yet found her own name carved into pine wood at the tree line. [452] The past self had woken from that coastal dream and presumably eaten breakfast and felt, however briefly, like a person moving forward through time in the conventional direction. [480]

Maren set down her stick and looked at the cabin walls. [491] The ranger who had built it was long dead, his tools still hanging on their pegs, everything organized for a future he had expected to inhabit. [517] She understood him now in a way she would not have three weeks ago. [531]

She understood that seeing your own grave -- even an unmarked, dateless, possibly metaphorical grave -- does not make you afraid of death so much as it makes you covetous of every previous moment you treated as unremarkable. [567] You become jealous of the self who complained about rain. [577] You become jealous of the self who slept without dreaming and called that a waste. [592]

She picked up the stick again. [598]

Outside, the first snow of December was beginning, each flake falling with the unhurried confidence of something that had no concept of its own ending. [623] She watched it through the single window and felt the old tug -- the pull toward wonder that her jaded years had thinned to a filament but never severed. [651] She began to write, and in writing, she began, improbably, to soar. [663]

The scroll received her -- patient, fibrous, smelling of old forest -- and she filled it with the only honest account she had ever managed of what it costs to outlive your own idea of yourself, and what it means, still, to press the stick down and insist on the mark. [712]
