The ridge road ran like a spine along the high plateau, and on clear nights the aurora draped itself over the stone in curtains of pale green and violet. [29] Maren had walked this ridgeway a hundred times with packages she was not permitted to open, and she had stopped being amazed by the lights somewhere around her fortieth crossing. [59] That, her old teacher Doris had once told her, was the real danger of the work. [75] Doris was gone now. [79] Not dead, as far as Maren knew, simply absent in the way that certain people become absent when the world no longer holds them still. [104] She had left behind three things: a wax-sealed manual of courier protocols, a list of clients whose names had already begun to sound like invention, and the dragonfly-wing pendant. [133] Maren wore the pendant at her collarbone. [140] It was translucent and ribbed and so delicate that she held her breath during high winds, though it had survived conditions that had shattered the wax seal on the manual twice over. [172] She did not understand it. [177] She had stopped expecting to. [182] Tonight she carried a small crate across the ridgeway at the hour when the aurora was brightest, which was also the hour when her nerves ran hottest. [209] She had always been jittery by nature, checking behind her at sounds that turned out to be nothing, adjusting the crate straps every twenty steps, counting her breaths. [237] Doris had called this a gift. [243] She had said that jittery people notice the world while calm people walk through it dreaming. [259] The pendant began to hum against her sternum. [267] It was faint, less sound than pressure, but she felt it. [278] She slowed. [280] The aurora above the ridge rippled in a long, slow wave, as if the sky were breathing, and the pendant vibrated sympathetically with some frequency she could not locate in the world around her. [314] Then she saw him: a boy sitting on a milestone at the road's edge, perhaps twelve years old, wrapped in a coat two sizes too large, staring upward with an expression that was not awe exactly but its raw material. [354] She stopped. [356] She almost did not stop. [361] Her schedule was tight, her client particular, and the crate was heavier than its paperwork suggested. [377] But the pendant vibrated sympathetically again, and Maren had learned to read that signal the way she read weather. [396] She set the crate down and sat on the milestone beside the boy. [409] He did not look at her. [415] He said, "Does it ever look the same twice?" [424] He meant the aurora. [428] "No," she said. [431] "Not once in a hundred crossings." [437] He nodded slowly, filing this away. [443] She felt the pendant against her chest, steadier now, as if something had been confirmed. [458] This was what it did: it extended brief encounters, made them linger past their natural edge, held them open the way a hand holds a door. [484] Doris had told her that the pendant was old enough that no one remembered its origin, only its behavior. [503] It existed, like the aurora, at the far boundary of the fade from memory to myth, in that territory where a thing stops being explained and starts being accepted. [532] The boy finally looked at her. [538] "You cross here a lot?" [543] "I do." [545] "Is it worth it?" [549] She thought of what Doris would have said, and then she set that aside and answered for herself. [567] The pendant hummed once more, low and resonant, as if approving the pause. [580] "There is a moment," she said, "right when the green turns to violet, when something in my chest does what I imagine seeds do underground." [605] "It germinates," she said. [609] "Every time, even after a hundred crossings, something small germinates." [619] "I walk out here to coax wonder from the routine," she said, "and so far the routine has not refused." [639] The boy looked back at the sky. [646] The aurora shifted, green bleeding into violet exactly as she had described. [658] She watched his face when it happened, and the raw material arranged itself into something finished. [674] She picked up the crate and walked on. [682] The pendant was quiet now, its work complete. [690] Behind her, the boy stayed at the milestone a long time, long enough that he would remember this crossing, long enough that someday someone younger might call it a story someone told them once.
