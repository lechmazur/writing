The midnight glade above the clouds held no stars Mireille had ever charted. [13] She was an onyx-ink cartomancer by trade and by blood, her maps drawn in the particular darkness of pressed squid-shell ink that her grandmother had called the color of knowing better. [44] She spread a fresh sheet on the cold grass and looked up at nothing. [58] After the birth, she had left while her daughter still slept in the hospital bassinet -- not fleeing, she told herself, but searching for the thing she had been searching for before the child arrived: the place where lost time accumulates. [98] She believed in such a place the way she believed in fault lines. [111] You couldn't see them, but the earth knew. [119]

In her satchel, wrapped in chamois cloth, was the cracked saucer from her childhood dolls' tea set. [136] The crack ran from rim to center, a single thin fault she had made at age seven by pressing too hard with the tiny painted cup, trying to make the ceremony real. [168] Her mother had kept it anyway, had set it on the birthday tablecloth every year when the family gathered to do the thing they always did: trace their hands. [197] Each person pressed palm flat to the cloth and someone else drew the outline in whatever marker was nearest -- blue one year, red the next, a silver pen the year her father was too sick to lift his arm and Mireille traced it for him, her fingers following the strange familiar geography of his knuckles. [252] By now the tablecloth held years of hands overlapping, a cartography of belonging. [265] She had not been home for the last three birthdays. [275] That was the lost time. [280] That was some of it. [285]

She unwrapped the saucer and set it in the grass. [295] The crack caught the moonlight and held it like a seam. [306] She was willingly perplexed by what she expected to find here -- she knew she had no answer, only the clear feeling that the answer existed, the way a map exists before it is drawn. [340] The cartomancer's faith: the territory precedes the telling. [348]

She began to draw. [352] Onyx ink on white paper, her hand moving in the rhythm she trusted more than speech. [368] She drew the glade as it was, the circle of pale grass floating above the cloud layer, and then she drew what she could not see -- the clouds beneath, dense and slow, and she understood suddenly that clouds were not absence but accumulation. [411] Every vapor that had ever risen from a grief or a ceremony or a sleeping child in a hospital bassinet. [431] The sky did not discard things. [437] It held them in suspension. [442]

Cloudbound repentance, she wrote in the margin, which was the cartomancer's notation for places where travelers must account for what they carried before they could pass through. [469]

She set down the pen. [474] She took the cracked saucer and placed her hand over it, feeling the fault line beneath her palm the way you feel a scar. [498] Then she reached to her collar and began to unfasten the silver clasp that held her satchel strap across her chest -- a clasp her mother had sewn on after Mireille had left the last birthday without explanation. [535] A small thing. [538] A thing meant to keep her tethered. [545] She did not remove it. [550] She held it open for a moment, feeling the cold air against her sternum, and breathed. [566]

Far below, her daughter was sleeping. [572] The hospital room had a window. [578] Dawn would come up through clouds that held, she now understood, every morning she had ever slept through, every tablecloth birthday she had missed, every cup pressed too hard against a saucer until something cracked and was kept anyway, because her mother kept things. [622]

She fastened the clasp again. [627] She rolled the map carefully -- the glade, the clouds, the notation in the margin -- and tucked it beside the saucer in the chamois cloth. [651] The beauty of this place was choking, the kind that squeezes the chest not because it is terrible but because you do not deserve it and it comes to you regardless. [682] She stood. [684] She began the long descent. [689] She had a daughter to return to, and a tablecloth to press her hand against, and years enough, she hoped, to let the outlines overlap. [714]
