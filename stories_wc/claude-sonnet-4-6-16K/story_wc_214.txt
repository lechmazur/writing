The shooting star tracking station sat at the edge of a plateau no cartographer had bothered to name. [18] Through the hush of an overlooked era, when governments had abandoned their interest in falling light and redirected their funds toward louder pursuits, the station continued to breathe. [47] Its instruments still hummed, its domed roof still pivoted on ancient gears, and Maren still came every night to sweep the floors. [69]

She was called a cosmic janitor by the few technicians who still filed reports no one read. [86] The title amused her, not with embarrassment, but with a quiet pride she kept tucked beneath her sternum like a folded letter. [108] Her job was to maintain what others had forgotten mattered. [118]

Maren was spiritually curious in the way deep water is curious about the sky above it — always reaching upward, never leaving its depth. [141] She had grown up watching her grandmother press a palm to the earth after storms, feeling for something she never named aloud. [163] Maren understood now what her grandmother had been feeling for. [173]

On the worktable beside the central telescope, she kept a piece of yellow lantern glass she had found in the station's storage room years ago. [198] Someone had used it once as a filter, fitting it over a lamp to reduce glare without losing warmth. [216] She kept it because when she held it to the window on clear nights, the stars through it looked like seeds suspended in honey. [240] It reminded her that light had many qualities, and none of them were wrong. [254]

Tonight the main computer had logged eleven meteors before midnight. [264] Maren pulled the logs and sat with them the way a reader sits with a letter from someone they have lost. [284] She had learned to empathize with the stars themselves, not as a poetic exercise, but as a practiced discipline. [303] She did it by tracing the path of falling stars across the printed charts, pressing her fingertip along each arc, feeling the geometry of departure. [328] Every streak held a story of momentum interrupted, of something hurtling forward and then surrendering to gravity, to atmosphere, to the simple fact of a larger world. [355]

She thought about surrender often. [360]

Three months earlier, Maren had received a diagnosis that arrived like a stone dropped into still water. [377] The rings had not stopped spreading. [383] She had told no one at the station, because there was no one to tell, and because telling would have required her to become something other than what she was here — steady, quiet, useful. [416] But tonight the weight of it pressed differently. [424] It pressed like something asking to be set down. [433]

She picked up the yellow lantern glass and held it to the domed skylight above. [449] The glass threw a warm oval of amber light across the instrument panels. [462] In that light, the station looked ancient and sacred, like a place that had always known it would outlast its keepers. [483]

Maren breathed. [485]

She understood then what flourishing stillness meant — not the absence of motion, but the fullness that gathers when all motion is finally witnessed. [509] The stars fell, and she watched them fall, and in watching she did not feel small. [525] She felt like the ground that receives them. [533]

To find strength in vulnerability, she had always assumed, meant enduring it. [545] Standing firm while something tore at you. [552] But the stars did not endure the atmosphere. [560] They gave themselves to it, and in giving, they made light. [571]

She pressed her fingertip to the last arc on the chart. [582] A long descent, southeastern trajectory, estimated mass of two grams at most. [594] Something so small, burning so completely. [600]

She set the yellow lantern glass on the chart beside it, and the warm amber circle fell exactly over the end of the arc, like a period placed by the universe at the end of a sentence it had been composing for centuries. [643]

Maren laughed, softly, with the whole of herself. [651]

Outside, the plateau held its darkness with the confidence of something that had never needed to explain itself. [669] Inside, a cosmic janitor sat in borrowed light, tracing the paths of things that had fallen beautifully, learning that to be seen in your descent was its own form of arrival. [700]

She would tell someone tomorrow. [705]

She would begin there. [709]
