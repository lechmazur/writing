Marcus Velde had driven seven hours to reach the quarry office trailer, which sat like a forgotten thought at the edge of a limestone pit in western Ohio. [28] The drive gave him time to rehearse explanations for what had happened at the Dayton Antiques Fair two weeks prior, when he had confidently declared a so-called Civil War field desk authentic, only to watch the auctioneer pull a hardware-store receipt from 1987 out of the drawer. [75] The crowd's laughter was the kind that carries - not mean, exactly, but absolute. [88]

He was still a roadie at heart, which meant he understood reverb better than most: how sound keeps bouncing long after the source goes silent. [113] After the applause dies, after the laughter settles, the walls keep returning it to you. [128]

The quarry supervisor, a broad woman named Delores Fitch, had called the appraisal firm because workers had struck a sealed storage room embedded in the cliff face during a controlled blast. [159] Inside they found wooden crates, Edison cylinders, a stereopticon, and various goods wrapped in oilcloth. [174] The firm sent Marcus because no one else wanted the job - two hours from Cincinnati, no hotel within thirty miles, no real explanation for why anyone had walled up a room in limestone. [207]

The trailer smelled of diesel and burnt coffee. [215] A box fan pushed hot August air around without improving it. [226] Marcus spread his appraisal kit across the fold-out table and began: age, provenance, material composition, market comparables. [243] Facts were the scaffolding he built around himself when feeling became structurally unsound. [256] He had learned this trick not in appraisal school but during eleven years on the road with second-tier rock bands, loading and unloading, watching musicians collapse inward after bad reviews, after thin crowds, after the applause fell short of what their ambitions required. [299] You either learned to insulate yourself emotionally with facts or you dissolved. [311]

He was halfway through the third crate when he found it. [322] Tucked beneath a brittle newspaper dated September 1931, wedged against a cracked mahogany cabinet, was a small rectangle of woven fabric - a scrap of speaker grille cloth, unmistakably, the kind used in cathedral radio cabinets of that era. [360] The weave was a burnt-orange twill, tightly loomed, the edges fraying in the particular way aged cotton does when moisture has worked at it for decades. [386]

He held it in both hands. [392]

Fact: RCA introduced grille cloth of this fiber composition around 1929. [403] Fact: the color matched catalog samples from the Atwater Kent Model 60. [415] Fact: a scrap this size, authenticated, was worth perhaps forty dollars. [426]

He kept holding it. [430]

What he could not write in his appraisal notes was the way it made the trailer feel like a concert hall at load-in, before anyone arrived, when the empty space still held potential and the air was clean with possibility. [470] He had spent eleven years touching other people's sound equipment, tightening bolts that held speaker cabinets together, stretching this exact kind of cloth across wooden frames so that music could push through it and reach someone standing in the dark. [510] He had never played a note. [516] He had built the vessel and then stepped aside. [525]

After the band broke up - not his last band, but the one before, the one that should have made it - he had needed something to do with his hands and his habit of knowing what things were worth. [563] Appraising antiques was not a second choice so much as a translation of the same impulse. [579] He was still trying to wrest meaning from objects that other people had abandoned. [593]

The grille cloth was warm from his hands now. [602]

He set it in the documented column, filled out the tag, and clipped it to his board. [619] Confidently uncertain - that was the only honest posture available to him, both for the provenance of recovered things and for the wider question of what he was supposed to be now. [650] The quarry walls outside held their shape around the absence of what the blast had freed. [666] Some containers, he thought, become more themselves once emptied. [675] He did not know yet if that applied to him. [685] He wrote it down anyway, in the margin of the appraisal form where no one was supposed to write anything personal, because the limestone outside had kept its secret for ninety years and he figured he was owed at least one margin.
