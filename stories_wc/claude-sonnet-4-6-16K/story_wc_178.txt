The palace between kings breathed differently. [6] Nara had noticed this in the weeks since the last dynasty dissolved, when the great cedar doors stood unlatched and the tax collectors stopped coming. [31] She was frugal by temperament and astronomer by vocation, which meant she spent her nights mapping stars on salvaged clay tablets and her days eating whatever the palace kitchens forgot to lock away. [64] The interregnum suited her. [68] No new king meant no new demands on her star charts, no summons to interpret omens for men who had already decided what the omens meant. [94]

It was during one such quiet evening, the sky above the ziggurats flushing amber into violet, that she found the tile. [115] It lay half-buried beneath a collapsed column in the eastern courtyard — a square of fired lapis-flecked stone, no wider than her palm, etched with a grid of numbers. [143] She recognized it immediately as a magic square, each row and column and diagonal summing to the same value. [162] But these numbers were not simply balanced for elegance. [171] They were arranged in a pattern she had seen before, embedded in the schematic representations she used to chart celestial movements — spiraling outward from a fixed point, mimicking the precession of stars across a century. [206] Someone had encrypted something inside the arithmetic. [213]

Nara brought the tile to her workroom, a spare chamber she kept lit by a single oil lamp she refilled only when absolutely necessary. [237] She set the tile beside her largest star map and began transcribing its values onto papyrus, translating the number grid into positional diagrams. [260] The schematic grew slowly: concentric arcs, indexed angles, a language that was neither purely mathematical nor purely devotional but somehow both. [281] It was, she thought, empirically ironic — that the most rigorous proof of celestial order she had ever encountered was also a prayer. [303]

The message took shape over three nights. [310] The ancient astronomer who had carved the tile — she could tell by the stylus marks it was someone careful and underfunded, someone like herself — had used the magic square to conceal coordinates. [342] Not of a place, but of a moment. [350] A specific alignment of Jupiter, Venus, and a star cluster the Babylonians called the Furrow, which would next occur within the month. [372] The numbers were not a curiosity left for posterity. [381] They were an invitation. [385] And at the center of the schematic, rendered in a notation so compressed it had nearly escaped her, was a phrase she translated as: to seal a covenant with the unknown. [416]

She sat with this for a long time in the dusky harmony of her lamp-lit room, the palace settling around her like an old man finding sleep. [443] No king had issued this commission. [449] No temple priest had sanctioned this work. [456] The original astronomer had acted entirely alone, spending weeks or months encoding devotion into pure arithmetic, hiding love for the cosmos inside a structure that could survive any regime, any burning of records, any erasure of names. [493]

On the night of the alignment, Nara climbed to the palace roof with her clay tablets and her borrowed reed pen. [514] She had spent nothing — not a coin, not a lamp-wick beyond her usual ration — to be here. [531] The sky was vast and indifferent and precisely on schedule. [541] Jupiter blazed low in the west. [547] Venus hung above it like a nail driven into dark wood. [558] The Furrow stretched faint across the zenith. [565]

She uncloaked the alignment in her charts, drawing the final schematic line that completed what the old tile had begun, connecting her observation to the observation encrypted a century before. [595] Two astronomers, separated by time and regime and the ordinary catastrophes of history, had now looked at the same convergence and recorded its truth. [619] Nara pressed her clay tablet to her chest for a moment before the impression dried. [634] She did not know what the covenant was, exactly, or with what it had been made. [650] She suspected the original astronomer had not known either. [659] That seemed to be the point. [665] The universe did not negotiate terms. [671] You simply showed up, watched carefully, and wrote down what you saw, as honestly and as precisely as your materials allowed. [692]

She set the tile at the edge of the parapet, facing the sky, and let the stars do the rest. [712]
