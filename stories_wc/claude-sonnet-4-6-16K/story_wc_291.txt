Vera Strand could not recognize a face to save her life, yet she painted them for the dead. [18] This was not irony. [22] It was the foundation of her method. [29] Prosopagnosia had stripped away her instinct to read features as belonging to a person and left only geometry: the slope of a nose, the arithmetic of cheekbones, shadow pooling beneath a jaw. [61] She translated grief-fogged descriptions into portraits that medical examiners and detectives found uncanny in their precision. [77]

The file on the Aldermoor quarry case was fourteen years thick. [88] A woman found at the water's edge, name never established, evidence catalogued and then, after two department transfers and a storage flood, gone. [111] After the evidence disappears, Vera had learned, the face becomes the only surviving witness. [125]

She arrived at the quarry before dawn. [132] The mist sat low against the stone walls, turning the excavated cliffs into something between ruin and dream. [150] The water below was perfectly still, a dark mirror that reflected nothing useful. [163] Vera moved along the rim very methodically, placing numbered markers every four feet, noting the orientation of lichen, the direction of old boot scuffs preserved in limestone dust. [191] She was building a coordinate system for a place that had held its secret for over a decade. [209]

It was in the third quadrant, caught in a crack where the lip of the quarry crumbled, that she found it: a narrow strip of satin, no wider than two fingers, faded from what might have been crimson to a dusty brown. [251] A ribbon from a masquerade mask. [257] The eyelets where the cord had been threaded were still intact, the satin preserved by its shelter in the stone. [277] She bagged it. [280] She did not know yet what it meant, but she trusted the method. [293]

Back in town, she found the detective who had inherited the cold case, a man named Orell who wore his skepticism like weather-gear. [316] He had stopped feeling much about the unidentified woman years ago. [327] Cases without names became geometry too, but of a crueler kind. [338] Vera set the ribbon fragment on his desk beside her sketch pad. [350]

She asked him to describe every masquerade event in the county from the decade in question. [366] He stared at her. [370] She explained: the face-blind painter and the detective who had lost feeling. [382] Two people emptied of the usual human apparatus for this kind of work. [395] By unifying opposites, she told him, they might accomplish what neither could alone. [408] She would give the nameless woman a face; he would give that face a world to inhabit. [425] She needed his memory of context; he needed her immunity to assumption. [437]

Orell was quiet for a long time. [444] The furrowed hush that had settled over the case for years sat between them like furniture. [460] Then he opened his files. [465]

The ribbon, cross-referenced against his notes, pointed to a private charity gala held at an estate near the quarry. [484] Vera began to draw as he spoke: not a face she had seen, but a face assembled from fourteen years of secondhand description, from the coroner's measurements, from the shape of grief in everyone who had spoken about the woman and then stopped. [527]

The portrait that emerged was specific enough to lance something open in Orell. [540] He recognized the line of the jaw. [547] Not from evidence. [550] From a photograph on a missing persons report filed in a neighboring county, never cross-referenced because the woman in that report had a name and the woman in the quarry did not. [582]

They had been treated as opposites: named and nameless, present and absent. [594] Vera had unified them into one person. [601]

The detective sat back, and for the first time in years something moved across his face that Vera, face-blind as she was, could not identify. [626] She did not need to. [631] She had felt it cross the room, warm and reluctant and long overdue. [644] A dormant empathy, finally woken, pushing up through fourteen years of procedural sediment like a root through stone. [662] She began to label her portrait. [668]

Outside, the mist over the quarry thinned. [675] The water caught a little light. [681]
