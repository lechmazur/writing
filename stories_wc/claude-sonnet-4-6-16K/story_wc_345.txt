The settlers of Arven's Reach had learned, after strangers became family, to stop questioning the chalk. [16]

Every dawn, before the twin moons dissolved into lavender sky, messages appeared chalked onto the deserted sidewalks threading between the orchards. [37] The words surfaced in careful, looping script beneath the floating rock arches that bridged grove to grove, their stone bellies draped in hanging moss and the nests of glass-winged birds. [67] Nobody admitted to writing them. [72] Nobody needed to. [75]

Mira had lived on Arven's Reach long enough to recognize the handwriting as strangely trustworthy, the way old maps feel reliable despite their smudged edges. [100] The messages never lied or flattered. [106] They simply told you what needed doing: which arch was cracking at its keystone, which orchard row had root-blight spreading west, which family was running short on water before the dry season tightened its fist. [141]

She found today's message outside the communal press house, white chalk bright as bone against grey stone. [158]

REEXAMINE THE LOWER GROVE. THE BELL IS WHERE MARET LEFT IT. USE IT. [171]

Mira stared up through the nearest floating arch, where the nebula hung visible even in daylight, that soft bruise of violet and gold the settlers called the Respite. [199] The original survey logs had named it a nebula of respite, though no one used the formal name. [217] They looked at it and felt that somewhere in the universe there was still room to breathe. [234]

She went down to the lower grove. [241]

The butterfly garden watering bell hung from a low branch of the oldest pear tree, tarnished copper shaped like an open blossom, its clapper a smooth river stone. [269] Old Maret had fashioned it thirty years ago, before the blight, before the drought cycles worsened, before she died and left the grove to whoever was willing to tend it. [299] The bell was designed to be rung at the irrigation network's center point, where all the underground channels branched from one source. [321] Its resonance, Maret believed, would clear the sediment valves without any machinery. [332] Nobody had tested the theory. [337] Nobody had rung it since Maret. [343]

Mira took it down. [347] She turned it gently, copper warm from morning sun, the river-stone clapper shifting with a low, promising sound. [366]

She understood what the chalk was asking. [373] The dry season had cracked the main channel, and repairs required everyone working the orchard stations simultaneously, releasing pressure from all sides at once. [402] No single person could coordinate that timing. [409] But a bell could. [413]

When she brought it to the morning gathering, eight faces looked at her and then at the bell, and something passed between them, the same recognition she had felt reading the chalk. [445] They had all received messages that morning, she realized. [455] Mira to the bell, Dovin to the eastern valve, Cassidy to the channel mouth, the others to their stations, each piece of a plan assembled by someone who understood the whole shape of their need. [489]

"Who is it?" Cassidy asked. [494] She was new enough to still ask. [500]

Brul, the oldest among them, who had come on the second ship and survived two droughts, shook his head slowly. [520] "Someone who cares more about the grove than about being known for caring," he said. [535]

That was enough. [538] They did not need to understand a thing completely to trust it. [550]

They spread through the arches at midmorning, sunlight catching each stone formation so it blazed briefly gold, and Mira stood at center and rang the bell. [576] Its note traveled the root-threaded earth, clear and low, and she heard the distant clank and release of valves answering, one after another, the sound of collective effort completing itself across every corner of the grove. [612]

That night, Mira sat beneath the Respite's pale glow and thought about the ghost she had never seen. [630] Altruistic, patient, content to remain chalk on stone, it had given them only what they needed and asked for nothing. [650] She believed in it the way she believed in good soil: not because she understood it, but because it kept producing life. [672] Somewhere in the orchard dark, chalk was meeting stone again. [682] Tomorrow there would be new instructions, new work, the next knotted problem unraveled into something they could hold together. [701]

Brambled, all of it. [705] Hard going and beautiful. [709] Hope, like the grove itself, growing crooked and green toward the light. [721]
