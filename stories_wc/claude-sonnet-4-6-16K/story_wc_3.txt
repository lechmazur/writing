Maren had spent eleven years translating the dead, which was why the magnetic arbor suited her so well. [18] The structure floated above the cloud layer on invisible rails of lodestone force, its iron lattice draped in vines that bore pale, heavy fruit no one had yet eaten. [48] She had accepted the posting after her mentor, Devash, collapsed mid-sentence at his desk, leaving a cuneiform tablet half-read beneath his cooling hand. [71] The arbor was his project, his obsession, and now it was hers. [83]

She was kindly methodical in everything she did. [91] She catalogued the tablets Devash had carried up in crates before his death, arranging them by period, then by subject, then by something harder to name - by what they seemed to want from her. [124] She watered the vines at the same hour each morning, touching each tendril with the back of her hand the way a nurse checks a forehead for fever. [153] She did not rush. [157] She had learned from Devash that the dead do not reward impatience. [170]

What haunted her was not grief exactly - she had moved through grief and come out somewhere quieter and stranger on the other side. [194] What haunted her was ambition: the specific, inherited hunger to understand the physics of transition. [210] Devash had believed the ancient Sumerians encoded it in their tablets. [222] Not metaphor, he insisted before he died - actual physics, the mechanics of what moves between states when a thing stops being one thing and becomes another. [249] She had inherited his belief the way a house inherits its previous owner's smell. [262]

In the third month, she found it. [269] Inside the hollow of the arbor's oldest fruit, split accidentally when she dropped it against the iron railing, was a seed unlike anything she had catalogued. [299] It was pale and dense and somehow warm. [306] The tablets called it, in their careful wedge-marks, the knowledge fruit seed - not a symbol but a proper name, repeated in three separate archival texts as though the scribes wanted to be certain it would survive the languages that would come after them. [347]

She held it and did not put it down for a long time. [360]

Devash's spirit had lived in the arbor - she had not questioned this because it explained everything: the vines that grew faster when she read aloud, the occasional sensation of a presence at her left shoulder, the warmth that lingered in the iron frame on cold mornings. [404] She had grown used to it the way you grow used to a sound you cannot locate. [422] But in the fifth month, the warmth left. [431] After the spirit leaves, she had read in one tablet, the ground remembers the weight. [447]

She understood that sentence differently now. [453]

She worked by analogy because it was the only honest method available to her. [467] Through analogical thinking, she mapped the seed against the tablets' description of transition: the seed did not grow by accumulating from outside but by converting pressure within itself, the same way grief converts to something unnameable, the same way a signal converts to meaning in the moment a reader's eye locks onto a word. [514] Transition, the tablets argued, was not movement from one place to another. [526] It was a change in what a thing was made of while remaining in exactly the same position. [543]

She stood at the arbor's edge. [549] Below her, clouds moved in slow, unreadable script. [557] She held the seed in both hands and understood, in the touching-distance way you understand things you cannot prove, that she was close. [580] Not close to an answer. [585] Close to the question itself, which was the rarer and more necessary thing. [597]

She began to reestablish Devash's archive from the seed outward, cataloguing not what the tablets said but what they were reaching toward when the words gave out. [625] Her handwriting was small and patient and careful. [633] Above her, the iron lattice hummed against the magnetic current that kept the whole structure aloft, that kept anything suspended between the ground and sky from falling either direction. [661] The vines put out new growth toward her hands as she wrote, the way a child leans toward a voice reading in the dark. [684]

She did not know what she would find. [692] She was, after all, just a translator - which meant she had spent her life standing in the exact space between what was said and what was meant. [718] It turned out that was precisely far enough. [727]
