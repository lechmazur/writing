After time became visible—appearing as slow amber threads through the vaulted corridors of Asteroid Velanthos—the bureaucrats grew nervous. [20] They installed mirrors everywhere, believing they could track the threads' drift, measure their decay. [34] They were wrong about that, as they had been wrong about most things. [47]

Maren Sollace had once led the Velanthos Liberation Front with the kind of conviction that moves mountains, even artificial baroque ones. [68] Now she swept grit from the excavation platforms and wrote her small, unread poems in a notebook with a cracked spine. [89] The revolution had ended not in glory but in a gradual, embarrassing exhaustion, and she had traded her manifestos for metaphors. [110] She did not mind, mostly. [115] Burnished by loss, she had learned that beauty could survive where ideology could not. [129]

The asteroid's inner halls were carved in the style of some long-dead Earth emperor's palace—gilded scrollwork, arching nave-ceilings, cherubim rendered in pale stone. [153] The original settlers had been strange, romantic people. [161] That was precisely why the dispute over the Velanthos dig had grown so ugly. [175]

Director Prast claimed the crystal vase shard found in Sublevel Nine belonged to the administrative archive—evidence, he argued, that the asteroid's founders had been merchants, not pilgrims. [203] Curator Fen claimed the opposite with equal fury, placing the shard inside a glass case and surrounding it with speculative placards. [224] The shard was thumb-sized, pale violet, and when held up to the amber time-threads drifting through the corridor, it scattered their light into something almost religious. [250] Maren had held it once, briefly, without permission. [258] She had written four lines about it that night and crossed all four out. [272]

What no one asked—because she was defunct, peripheral, holding a broom—was what Maren thought the shard meant. [291]

She thought it meant everything. [296]

She had begun to devise a counter-interpretation, quietly, without announcing it to anyone. [309] Her notes filled the cracked notebook: she was tracing the shard's glasswork against the scrolled friezes on Sublevel Three, finding repetitions, a visual grammar shared between decorative stone and shattered vessel. [340] The founders, she was beginning to believe, had been neither merchants nor pilgrims but poets—a civilization organized around elegy, around the ritual of making beautiful objects precisely to lose them. [371] To reimagine them entirely required patience and a kind of grief she had practiced for years. [387]

The time-storms complicated everything. [391] When amber threads compressed into visible weather—whirling, dense, capable of warping perception—the mirrors in the corridors reflected not the present moment but some adjacent one, and people reported seeing themselves older, or younger, or standing in rooms that hadn't existed yet. [434] Protocol on Velanthos required covering mirrors during storms with black velvet panels kept in a cabinet near each junction. [453] Maren was usually the one who did it, moving through the baroque halls with an armful of velvet while administrators ran for their quarters. [477] She did not mind this either. [483] In the strange, amber-churned quiet of a covered-mirror storm, the asteroid felt most like itself—its ornamentation visible without distortion, its sorrow unhurried. [506]

It was during one such storm that she laid the crystal vase shard on the floor beside a frieze of a weeping figure and finally understood. [532] The weeping figure's tears were carved as vase-shapes. [540] The shard had once been a tear. [547] Somewhere in the asteroid's founding logic was the idea that grief, made solid, made beautiful, should eventually break—not as catastrophe but as completion. [571]

She wrote it down in seven lines that she did not cross out. [584]

She did not bring her notebook to Prast or Fen. [594] She understood that the dispute would continue because disputes were how bureaucracies justified their existence. [609] But she slipped her seven lines beneath the glass case that held the shard, where they would rest against violet crystal, and she thought: let the next excavator find them. [639] Let someone, centuries hence, lift the velvet from a mirror and see a defunct revolutionary grinning back. [656] Let them dispute what she meant. [662] That was how you reimagined a civilization—not with declarations but with small, patient, carefully placed truths, left like shards for someone else to hold up to the light. [691]

Outside the covered windows, time moved in its golden threads, visible and patient, not unlike a poem. [708]
