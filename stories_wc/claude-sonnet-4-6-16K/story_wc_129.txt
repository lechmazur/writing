Sela had shaped marsh reeds for eleven years in solitude, bending green stalks into figures that no hand but hers had ever held. [23] The crystalline orchard where she lived perched at the very edge of a shimmering crater, its glass-bark trees chiming softly when the mineral mist rolled up from below. [51] Beyond the murmur of lost languages, beyond the old scripts eroded from the crater walls, this place existed in a silence that was almost kind -- almost. [77]

She was boldly sympathetic by nature, the kind of person who felt strangers' loneliness the way a reed feels water: entirely, and from within. [101] Yet she had never managed to graft herself onto the community that lived a half-mile north in the crater settlement, a constellation of warm lights she watched each evening from between the glass trees. [135]

What changed things was the tag. [141]

It appeared first on a boulder near the crater's southern lip: a looping sigil in pale gold, like a knot that had learned to breathe. [166] Then it surfaced on a drain cover inside the settlement, then on the curved wall of the communal kiln, always in that same impossible gold. [191] No one knew the artist. [196] No one had seen them work. [202] And yet the whole settlement began to orbit the mystery, people gathering in doorways, comparing sketches, debating meaning. [220]

Sela heard about it from a girl named Oris who wandered into the orchard one afternoon chasing a sketch she'd dropped to the wind. [244] Oris was twelve, fiercely opinionated, and carried a notebook dense with traced copies of the tag. [260] "We're all looking for whoever makes them," she said, studying Sela's reed sculptures with the same intensity she gave the sigils. [281] "You should come." [284]

The invitation was small. [288] The ache it opened in Sela was not. [296]

She came the next morning, carrying a bundle of unworked reeds the way someone carries an offering. [313] The search had its own cartography: a shared map pinned to the settlement's central post, threaded with colored string to mark each sighting. [336] People she had never spoken to explained their theories with the kind of generous urgency that only a shared puzzle produces. [357] Sela listened, and then she talked, and then she laughed at something the old glassblower Prem said about the tag resembling an ancient water-rights diagram. [382] Laughter, she found, was one of the few things that could stitch a stranger into a web of kinship without ceremony. [403]

The search stretched across four days. [409] They fanned out along the crater's rim each morning, notebooks open, voices calling across the mineral air. [426] Sela showed them how to read bent grass, how a careful foot leaves a signature even on crystalline ground. [445] Her knowledge of the orchard became the group's knowledge, the first real exchange in a life that had offered her too few. [467]

On the fifth morning, she found it. [474]

At the orchard's deepest point, where the glass trees grew densest and the crater light gathered into something almost liquid, stood the glimmering obsidian arbor. [499] She had passed it a thousand times, a natural arch of fused volcanic glass overgrown with pale lichen, and never thought it remarkable. [522] Now it was covered, every inch of its curved underside, in the gold sigil repeated so many times it became a texture, a language, a skin. [548] The artist had been working here for months, invisible among trees that only Sela frequented. [563] They had used her solitude as shelter without knowing they had also left her a gift. [579]

When the settlement came and stood beneath the obsidian arbor that afternoon, heads tilted back, breathing in the strangeness of it, Sela felt something shift inside her chest. [607] She did not know the artist's name. [614] She knew these people's faces now, their theories, the way Prem laughed and how Oris bit her thumbnail when she concentrated. [635] That evening, Sela cut a new figure from the freshest reeds, a shape that was not a person but suggested many, arms extending outward like roots seeking water. [663] She set it at the arbor's base and, before the group dispersed into the warm crater dark, she turned and spoke clearly: she would dedicate all work she made from this season forward to this place and the people in it. [704]

No one cheered. [707] Several people touched her arm. [712] Oris wrote something in her notebook, and Sela understood, in the luminescent ache of that moment, that this was how community was made -- not declared, but slowly, searingly accumulated, like light filling a crater no one knew was empty. [751]
