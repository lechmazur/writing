The atrium had not been warm in thirty years. [9] Mara kept vigil there anyway, among the glass panes sealed over with ice, beneath a January sky that pressed stars against the roof like seeds against a palm. [37] She was an heirloom seed saver, the last of a lineage that had never believed in throwing anything away -- not knowledge, not grief, not the spiral obligations passed from one generation to the next. [71] Her grandmother had called this vortex inheritance: the idea that what one ancestor begins, every descendant is pulled into completing. [91]

Tonight, after years of patience, the egg had hatched. [100]

It was not a bird's egg. [106] It was a seed casing, ancient and leathery, sealed shut around something her grandmother's grandmother had pressed dormant three generations back. [127] Mara had kept it warm against her body for two winters. [138] Now, in the icebound atrium under starlight, the casing had split open along its seam, releasing a smell like turned earth and something else -- old sugar, like the breath of a century. [170]

She moved to the stone table at the atrium's center with a furiously tranquil deliberateness: not calm, not frantic, but the particular steadiness of a woman standing at the center of a storm she herself had summoned. [207] In her palm she carried an oak acorn cap, no wider than her thumbnail, that her grandmother had tucked into the seed ledger with a note that said only: use it when the time comes. [242]

The time had come. [246]

Mara understood her task. [250] The vortex inheritance did not merely pass objects forward; it passed open questions -- the kind that wanted answering in the body, not the mind. [274] Her motivation was not restoration, not preservation. [281] She needed to discover the texture of borrowed time, to run her fingers across the edge of all the years her ancestors had spent on her behalf, before she spent them. [312] The acorn cap was the instrument her grandmother had designated. [322] Mara had never questioned why. [327] Now she saw: its cup-shape was a perfect vessel for holding what had no physical form. [343]

She set the acorn cap over the split seed casing and breathed out slowly. [357]

To reopen the dormant lineage stored inside, she had to work by decreasing entropy -- not by force, but by imposing order so precise it ran backward against decay. [385] She sorted. [387] She named. [389] She placed every dried seed from her coat pockets into rows on the stone table, each beside its written name, each touching the next in deliberate sequence: amaranth, then tepary bean, then the small dark corn her great-uncle had carried across a border in his shoe. [435] The arrangement was meticulous, a lattice of intention pressing back against chaos. [447]

The ice on the glass above her began to thin. [457] Not from heat -- there was no heat -- but from the logic of the act, as though the atrium itself recognized the shape of what she was doing and chose to participate. [488] Starlight came through more clearly. [493] The split seed casing beneath the acorn cap began to move, trembling in a slow clockwise spiral that Mara understood was the vortex completing one of its oldest circuits. [522]

What rose from the casing was not a plant. [531] It was a texture. [535] She felt it in her hands before she saw anything -- a rough tenderness, like the back of an aged hand, like the grain of oak, like the seam of a sky pressed to glass. [569] Borrowed time had a surface, and it felt like everything given without expectation. [582]

She stood in the wild serenity of the icebound atrium, seeds arrayed around her, acorn cap at the center, the inheritance spinning itself closed and open and closed again in a rhythm her body already knew. [618] She had not come here to extend her life. [627] She had come to feel, for one honest moment, the full weight of what had been lent to her -- and to decide, consciously, what she would send forward. [655]

Mara reached for her ledger and wrote the first line of a new entry. [669]

Outside, the stars held their positions, indifferent and ancient, exactly as they always had. [683]
