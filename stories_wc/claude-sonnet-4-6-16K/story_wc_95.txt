Margolaine Voss arrived at the glass atrium forty minutes before the scheduled debate, which was precisely the strategy. [18] The atrium stood at the edge of the university grounds where a quiet brook ran beneath the foundation's southern wall, its current pressing glass-green against the stones. [45] Steam fogged the lower panels in slow scrolls, and somewhere above, a sparrow had found its way inside and was trying to find its way back out. [72] It was, she thought, the appropriate architecture for what was about to happen. [85]

She set her leather satchel on the iron bench and withdrew the danish tin sewing needle card, flat and silver-blue, its lid embossed with a crown of windmills. [113] Inside, the needles were arranged in descending gauge, twenty-three in total, pressed into a strip of cream wool. [131] Most people saw a sewing kit. [137] Margolaine saw a cipher. [141]

The half-painted sundial stood at the atrium's center on a pedestal of veined marble. [155] Someone had abandoned its restoration midway: the hour lines on the eastern face were crisp and gilt, while the western half remained raw limestone, the angles still roughed in with charcoal. [186] This unfinished state was not an accident. [193] She had arranged it herself, three weeks ago, by bribing the university's head of restorations with a bottle of brandy and a letter of philosophical compliment. [220] The charcoal angles, read against the needle gauges on the card, spelled coordinates. [233] Forty-nine degrees north, two degrees east. [239] A village in Alsace. [243] A farmhouse. [245] A man who had been asking the wrong questions for the right reasons. [258]

The door at the far end of the atrium opened. [268]

Professor Aldric Senn entered with the deliberate weight of someone who had spent thirty years being right in rooms. [287] His mechanical arm swung with a faint hydraulic whisper at his right side, brass fingers slightly open, as though perpetually prepared to catch something or release it. [314] He was a dueling philosopher in the old tradition: he had fought seven formal arguments before magistrates, won five, and lost two in ways that had only increased his reputation. [344] He saw Margolaine and smiled in the manner of a man who does not bother to conceal that he considers a meeting a contest. [368]

"You're early," he said. [372]

"I'm thinking," she replied. [376]

He crossed to the sundial and studied it, brass fingers tracing the charcoal lines without touching them. [393] He was, she knew, reading it. [399] He was not supposed to be able to. [407]

"Forty-nine north, two east," he said quietly. [414]

The brook murmured beneath the glass. [420] The sparrow found a high vent and disappeared. [428]

Margolaine did not move, but something in her posture gathered, the way a diplomat gathers before a word that could end a career or begin a treaty. [455] She had spent twenty years learning to be diplomatically fierce: to press without bruising, to yield in ways that advanced her position, to fence with ideas the way a fencer uses distance as a weapon, never letting an opponent decide where the real fight was happening. [500]

"You were supposed to decode that after the debate," she said. [511]

"I decoded it last week." [516] He turned from the sundial, brass hand falling to his side. [527] "You left the needle card at the symposium in Geneva." [537] "I kept it." [540] "I've been waiting." [543]

The phrase landed precisely. [547] While waiting. [549] She had believed she was the one waiting, and he had transformed that assumption into a location, a destination, a rearranged geography of who was pursuing whom. [576]

This was the hidden overture he had been composing. [585] Not tonight's debate. [588] Not the arguments about epistemic sovereignty or the limits of rational consent. [600] The real negotiation was the farmhouse in Alsace, the man asking dangerous questions, and whether two people who both wanted answers badly enough could agree on what to do when they arrived. [632]

She picked up the tin, pressed it shut with a soft click. [644]

"You could have simply written to me," she said. [653]

"You would have replied with more questions." [660]

"And that bothers you." [664]

His mechanical arm rose slightly, brass fingers spreading in something that was almost a shrug. [679] "It fascinates me," he said. [684] "Every question you ask is actually a direction." [692] "You turn questions into destinations." [697] "I wanted to see where this one was going." [706]

The brook ran on beneath them, indifferent and patient. [715]

Margolaine stood, smoothed her coat, and smiled in a way that offered nothing and promised everything. [731]

"Then let's go find out," she said. [738]

They left the atrium together, the glass walls holding the morning light a moment longer behind them, as though the room itself was not quite ready for the argument to be over. [770]
