Maren had been herding eels since before she could name what she saw in the dark behind her eyes. [19] The visions came without invitation - flash floods three days before the rain, shipwrecks she could not prevent, the face of a child she had never met drowning in light. [48] Being precognitively cursed meant she carried futures like stones in her pockets, dragging her toward moments she could not change. [68] She had learned not to speak of it. [76]

Tonight, though, she climbed the tidal bluff alone, the aurora bleeding green and violet across the northern sky, and she felt the familiar weight of a lingering promise pressing at her sternum. [108] Her grandmother had died with one hand curled around Maren's wrist. [119] The old woman had whispered that the patterns were sacred, and made Maren promise to stop running long enough to understand them. [140] Maren had been running for eleven years. [147]

She set down her satchel at the bluff's edge, where the tide had pulled back to expose the dark shelf of rock below. [170] The eels were there, silver and slow, moving in their ancient loops through the pools left behind. [187] They did not scatter at her presence. [194] They never did. [197] She had a way with them that no one could explain, which was perhaps why she herself was called elusive - the eel herder who appeared at the margins of towns and vanished before morning. [231]

From the satchel she withdrew the painted rocks. [239] She had spent the winter preparing them, each sealed with ash and ochre, each numbered in a system she had traced from the aurora's own geometry. [265] She began placing them across the bluff's flat crown in a Fibonacci spiral, each gap measured, each stone deliberate. [284] The method had come to her in a vision she had not fled - the first one she had ever allowed to finish. [306]

The collapsed star dust was stored in a small iron vial at the center of the spiral. [323] She had recovered it from a meteorite fracture two autumns ago, a silver powder that held no light of its own but amplified whatever touched it. [349] She uncorked the vial and tipped a slow line of dust along the spiral's innermost arc. [365]

Then she waited. [368]

After friction disappears, the old texts said, the pattern speaks. [378] She had not understood that phrase until this moment, when the aurora above her seemed to settle rather than move, as though the sky had exhaled and simply forgotten to inhale again. [410] The wind dropped. [413] The eels below went motionless in their pools. [421] Even the sound of water ceased, not violently but gently, like a word completed. [435]

The star dust began to glow. [441] It traced the spiral she had made with the painted rocks, connecting each stone in a line of pale silver fire, and the shape it described was not the spiral alone but something larger embedded within it, recursive, alive in the way mathematics is alive, folding back on itself without ever repeating. [493]

Maren felt the visions rise. [498]

She did not run. [502]

She let them come, and for the first time understood that what she had always seen as catastrophe was actually sequence. [523] The floods, the shipwrecks, the drowning light - they were not warnings she had failed to act on. [540] They were data points in a pattern larger than her life, sacred not because they were good but because they were true. [562] To harness the curse was not to stop the visions but to read them the way the eels read the tide - not fighting the current but knowing where it goes. [592]

The aurora returned to motion above her, slow and borrowed, its light not truly hers to keep but given freely for now. [614] She pressed her palm to the star dust and felt her grandmother's hand close around her wrist, eleven years late and right on time. [638]

"I understand," Maren said, to no one and to everyone who had ever made her a promise she was only now ready to receive. [662]

Below the bluff, the eels resumed their loops, indifferent and faithful, tracing the same sacred patterns they had always traced, in water that remembered everything and forgave it. [690]
