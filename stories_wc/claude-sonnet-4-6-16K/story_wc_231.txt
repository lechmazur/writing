The mist came in before the light failed, which was how Maren always knew the day was truly ending. [19] She had kept the Greyhollow lighthouse for eleven years, long enough that the coast had stopped being a place and become a habit. [42] Visitors called her eccentric for the way she catalogued tides in verse, or for the wind chimes she built from driftwood and fishing wire that sang different notes depending on which way a storm was coming. [78] She preferred the word attentive. [83]

That evening, at nautical twilight, she walked the opal dock as she always did -- the hour when the sun sat exactly twelve degrees below the horizon and the sky became a bruise that couldn't decide between blue and black. [122] The dock's planks, pale and iridescent with salt and age, seemed to glow from within in the mist, which moved in slow sheets off the water and swallowed the pylons at the far end. [156] She was alone. [159] She was nearly always alone. [164]

She found it pinned to the mooring post at the dock's midpoint: a dried Edelweiss boutonniere, the petals pressed nearly flat, the stem wound with a single thread of silver wire. [195] It was delicate and wrong, the way something precious is wrong when it appears where it has no business being. [215] Edelweiss grew in alpine meadows, not seaside fog. [223] Someone had carried it a long way. [230]

Maren unpinned it carefully and held it in her palm. [240] She knew a final gesture when she saw one. [249] A man had come to the dock three days ago -- she had watched him from the lighthouse gallery through her brass scope, the way she watched everything she couldn't quite reach. [280] He had stood at this exact mooring post for nearly an hour, still in a way that had nothing to do with peace. [303] Then he had left, and she had told herself it was none of her business, and she had not slept well since. [325]

She began to investigate the only way she knew how: by observing gaps. [338] Not what was there, but what was missing. [346] The dock's visitor log showed no name, only a smear where the pen had dragged. [361] The harbormaster remembered a man who paid cash for a dinghy rental and returned it an hour later without going anywhere. [382] The flower itself had no note, no ribbon, no second pin to suggest it had been part of something larger. [402] The gaps were the message. [407]

Maren was softly persistent in all things -- she didn't force conclusions, she waited for them, the way she waited for the lighthouse beam to complete its rotation and come back around. [438] She went home and sat at her kitchen table with the boutonniere under the lamp and let her mind do what it did naturally, which was find patterns in absence. [468]

She had borrowed memories, or that was what she called the faculty she'd developed from years of solitary watching -- the ability to reconstruct experience from residue. [494] A child's drawing pinned to a wall told her the room had once held laughter. [509] A worn patch on a dock post told her someone had gripped it, hard, more than once. [526] The Edelweiss told her this: a man who had once stood in a high, clean place with someone he loved, who had carried one dried proof of that altitude across years and miles and set it down here, where the world ran out of land. [571]

She didn't know if he had wanted to be seen or had believed himself invisible. [586] That was the only question left, and it was the one she could not answer from gaps alone. [604]

She pinned the boutonniere to the collar of her coat. [614] She would wear it tomorrow when she walked the dock again, in case he came back, in case the gesture had been pointed outward instead of inward. [641] The mist wouldn't remember him. [646] The water wouldn't. [649] But Maren would, and the lighthouse would keep turning, and the beam would come back around. [665] It always did. [668]
