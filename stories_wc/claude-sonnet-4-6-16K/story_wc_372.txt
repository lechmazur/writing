The library hung above the clouds like a held breath. [10] Maren had been its orderly records chief for eleven years, though "years" was a word that had grown unreliable at this altitude. [32] The cold here was not the cold of winter; it was the cold of permanence, of things preserved past their natural term. [54] Every shelf was rimed with frost that never melted, and every book she catalogued she handled with gently meticulous hands, turning pages as though they were sleeping faces she refused to disturb. [86]

The library sat at the edge of oblivion, and Maren understood this with the calm of someone who had already made her peace with it. [111] Below, the clouds erased the earth. [117] Above, the atmosphere thinned into silence. [123] Here, between those two erasures, she kept the records of things once promised. [136]

The magic lantern slide arrived on a morning when the frost patterns on the eastern window had consumed the glass entirely. [157] It appeared on her cataloguing desk, slipped beneath a stack of vow-ledgers, its glass panel dark and cold. [175] When she held it to the single lamp she kept burning, the image inside it bloomed: two figures standing at the rim of something she could not name, their mouths open mid-utterance, their faces lit by whatever they were in the act of swearing. [219]

Luminous vows. [221] The label on the slide's wooden frame said nothing else. [231]

Maren understood immediately that this was the library's newest and most urgent acquisition, because the image was already fading. [250] Not deteriorating the way ordinary photographs do, but dissolving the way illusions fade to memory - not from the edges inward, but from the center out, as though meaning were the first thing to go. [284]

She moved with purpose, which for her always looked like stillness. [295] She carried the slide to the frequency chamber, a small annex where the library's one working machine sat: a resonance reader originally built to preserve oral archives by sampling frequencies in old wax cylinders. [329] She had repurposed it with painstaking care over the course of what she believed was a single winter but might have been three. [352]

To measure the slide, she would need to measure what remained inside it - not its image, but its intent. [371] She placed the slide against the reader's aperture and watched the needle swing. [384] The machine began to sample frequencies: the harmonic of a promise still vibrating in the glass, a resonance left by two voices that had once meant every syllable they spoke. [414] The needle moved with painful slowness, and Maren watched it with the patience of someone who had catalogued the last words of a thousand dying things. [440]

The quartz hush of the library pressed in around her. [450] No wind reached this place. [455] No sound traveled up through the cloud layer below. [464] There was only the soft tick of the reader and the faint glow of the slide, dimming now in real time, the two figures in the image growing transparent as the certainty they had once embodied leaked away into the cold archive air. [507]

This was always the crisis: not the arrival of a thing, but its departure. [521]

Maren noted the frequencies in her ledger, column by column, her handwriting small and even as stitching. [538] She was not recording the vows themselves - those were beyond recovery, beyond reconstruction. [551] She was recording proof that they had been made, that two people had once stood somewhere and offered each other the full weight of their intention, and that this had been real. [583] To conjure meaning from the edge of oblivion, she had long understood, you did not need to save the thing itself. [604] You needed only to save evidence that the thing had mattered. [615]

The slide went dark. [619] The needle settled. [622] Maren closed her ledger, slid the blank glass into a protective sleeve, and filed it between two others just like it, in the drawer marked simply: given. [649]

She returned to her desk. [654] The frost on the window had made new patterns. [663] She picked up her pen and began again, measuring what remained of the world one frequency at a time. [682]
