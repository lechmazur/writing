<story>

The recycled spaceship drifted through the dark like a cathedral built from the bones of older cathedrals. [18] Its hull was stitched together from decommissioned frigates and colony transports, welded and rewelded until no original surface remained, every panel carrying the ghost-memory of a different mission. [43] Mei Lin floated through its corridors with the careful grace of someone who had spent decades refusing to let gravity define her. [62]

She was the last caretaker aboard the Sutura, and she had never raised a hand against another living thing. [81] This distinction mattered less now than it once had, because there were so few living things left. [97] The last human had been born forty-three years ago on a station orbiting Kepler-442b -- a girl named Sable, who reportedly never cried, as though the species had finally exhausted its supply of tears. [128] Mei Lin had wept enough for both of them when she heard the news. [141]

Now she moved through the archive room, where thousands of photographs lined the curved walls in overlapping layers, pinned and taped and stitched onto recycled fabric panels that billowed gently in the ship's recycled air. [174] The photographs were humanity's residue: family portraits, war dispatches, birthday parties, hospital beds. [185] Mei Lin had collected them from abandoned stations for thirty years, not out of nostalgia but out of necessity. [200] Each photograph, she had discovered, carried something no digital copy could preserve -- the faint shimmer of long-dried tears on faded photographs, visible only when the light caught the emulsion at a precise angle. [231] Salt and grief and joy, pressed into paper by fingers that no longer existed. [244]

These traces were the key to the locked dimension. [253]

Mei Lin called it the Undershadow. [258] Every culture had sensed it -- the place where the unacknowledged self resided, where humanity's collective denials and repressions had pooled over millennia like groundwater beneath stone. [280] Shadow work, the old psychologists had named the process of confronting it. [291] But no one had understood that the shadow was not merely metaphorical. [302] It was a dimension, gravitationally adjacent, sealed shut by the sheer weight of all that had been refused. [318]

She believed that to push open a locked dimension, one needed not force but honest sorrow. [334] This was why she had remained a steadfast pacifist even when raiders had stripped the Sutura's engines, even when loneliness gnawed like vacuum at a cracked seal. [358] Violence added weight to the shadow. [364] Only undefended grief could lighten it. [370]

She unclipped the small glass vial from her neck: Kwan Yin's Tear, the crew had called it before they left, one by one, for stations that promised more life than this drifting reliquary could offer. [401] Inside the vial, a single luminous droplet hung suspended, neither water nor light but something between -- collected, according to the ship's oldest log, from a statue on Earth that had wept during the Final Silence. [436] The bodhisattva of compassion, weeping for a world that had forgotten how. [448]

Mei Lin held the vial against a photograph of a mother holding an infant, the emulsion scarred with tear-tracks that shimmered like rivers seen from orbit. [472] She felt the familiar shift in her chest -- the deep, clean sadness that paradoxically made her lighter. [487] It was the ship's strangest phenomenon: when Mei Lin was genuinely, openly sorrowful-happy -- that bittersweet state the Japanese called mono no aware -- she became gravitationally lighter, her feet lifting from the deck plates, her body rising as though the universe wanted to meet her halfway. [527]

She rose now, vial pressed to photograph, light cascading through dried tears and living ones. [542] The air around her shuddered. [546] The Undershadow pressed back against the hull like a hand against frosted glass, and she could see shapes in it -- every unspoken apology, every swallowed scream, every tenderness crushed into silence across a hundred thousand years of human refusal. [582]

She did not fight it. [586] She never fought anything. [590] Instead she took her needle -- actual thread, actual steel, the oldest technology on the ship -- and began to stitch the shimmer into the vial's light, pulling the photograph's grief through the glass, joining relic to relic. [623] The tear inside the vial bloomed. [629]

The dimension did not open like a door. [637] It opened like an eye. [642]

Light that was not light poured through, carrying voices she almost recognized, fragments of every shadow ever cast by a human being who had chosen to look away. [670] Mei Lin floated in the center of it, weightless with sorrow, luminous with resolve, and whispered the only greeting that mattered. [690]

"I see you," she said. [695] "I see all of you." [700]

The shadows trembled, thinned, and for the first time in the long silence after the last human birth, something answered back. [721]
