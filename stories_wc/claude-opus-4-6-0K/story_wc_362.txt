She came to the quartz arbor on a Tuesday, when the mist hung so thick it erased the boundary between ground and sky. [78] The arbor itself was a strange monument -- pillars of raw quartz grown through with climbing roses now dormant in winter, their thorns crystallized with ice. [103] Someone had built it decades ago as a meditation garden. [113] Sera had chosen it for more practical reasons: quartz, she insisted, held feeling the way a sponge held water. [132]

She was an attentive mystic, the kind who noticed when a room's silence shifted -- not broke, but shifted, the way a sleeping person changes position without waking. [159] She called this phenomenon the shifted hush, and she tracked it the way meteorologists track pressure systems. [192] Most people experienced it as that strange chill in certain rooms, that inexplicable comfort near a grandmother's chair. [210] Sera simply had vocabulary for it. [216]

Then she walked back to her car. [373]
