The rain had stopped twenty minutes ago, but the mossy trellis still wept. [14]

Maren crouched beneath its arch, watching droplets trace the green filaments of moss before falling into the gravel below. [30] She owned the nursery—had owned it for eleven years—but lately she felt less like its keeper and more like its captive. [50] The plants were changing, and she couldn't look away. [59]

It had started during storm season, three weeks prior, when a linden tree at the back of the property began dropping leaves out of cycle. [82] Not dying leaves—living ones, impossibly green, as though the tree were shedding perfectly healthy skin. [96] Maren had collected them one by one, pressing each linden leaf between the pages of her field journal, trying to study the behavior. [118] She was a woman who needed to understand things before she could accept them, and this she did not understand. [137]

The other plants followed. [141] Her climbing roses unraveled from the trellis overnight, not wilting but deliberately unwinding, as if they had decided the structure no longer suited them. [164] The ferns in the greenhouse curled inward and then outward again in patterns she'd never seen documented. [180] Her supplier said it was weather stress. [187] Her assistant said it was fungal. [193] Maren said nothing, because the truth she suspected was circuitously sincere—it arrived at honesty only after winding through every rational explanation first—and she wasn't ready to speak it aloud. [222]

The truth was that the nursery was reconstructing itself. [231]

Not in the way a gardener reconstructs a bed after winter, pulling dead roots and laying fresh soil. [248] This was something the plants were doing on their own, rearranging their growth patterns, their root networks, their very architecture, as though responding to a blueprint Maren hadn't drawn. [274] She had spent her career imposing order—training vines along wire, pruning branches into shape, dictating which species grew beside which—and now the nursery was gently, unmistakably refusing her design. She turned the latest linden leaf over in her fingers. [312] Its veins had rerouted. [316] Where a linden leaf should show a symmetrical branching pattern radiating from the midrib, this one had veins that curved and reconnected, forming closed loops like tiny circuits. [372] Storm season meant the air was perpetually thick, the sky a rotating gallery of bruise-colored clouds, and everything in the nursery existed in a state of saturated waiting. [397] She stopped at the linden tree. [403] Its canopy had thinned from the shedding, but the remaining leaves looked different—larger, darker, with that same strange venation. [421] The tree was becoming something else, and it was asking her, in the only language it had, to let it. [440]

She recognized the feeling because it mirrored her own. [449] For months she had resisted the slow understanding that the nursery didn't need her control—it needed her attention. [466] There was a difference. [470] Control meant she decided the outcome. [476] Attention meant she witnessed it. [481] The reluctant metamorphosis wasn't just the plants'; it was hers. [491]

To study plant behaviors honestly, she realized, she had to release the assumption that she already knew what they should do. [512] She had to reconstruct her entire relationship with this place by releasing control, by letting the roses choose their own path along the trellis, by allowing the linden to become whatever it was becoming. [595] She unlocked the greenhouse vents and left them open to the storm-season air. [608] It felt like a small destruction, and it terrified her, and she did it anyway. [623]

Over the following weeks, the nursery transformed. [630] The roses wove themselves into the mossy trellis in spirals more intricate than anything she could have engineered. [648] The linden tree's strange leaves multiplied, capturing light with an efficiency that made the canopy glow. [663] Customers stopped at the gate and stared. [670]

Maren kept her journal, recording everything. [676] She studied, she sketched, she documented. [682] But she did not direct. [687] She let the metamorphosis sustain itself, and in sustaining, it sustained her—reshaped her understanding of what it meant to tend a living thing. [710]

On the last page of her current journal, beneath a pressed linden leaf with its impossible looping veins, she wrote a single line: The garden was never mine to design. [742] It was mine to watch become. [748]
