The ancient observatory held its breath at dusk, its crumbling dome split open like a skull remembering the sky. [21] Maren Voss sat cross-legged on the observation platform, her drone controller balanced across her knees, her sharp eyes scanning the feed from her quadcopter as it traced lazy circles above the ruin. [50] The screen glowed with thermal signatures—stone cooling, lizards retreating, and somewhere below the fractured mosaic floor, a heat anomaly she couldn't explain. [71]

She had been waiting three days for the pilgrim. [80]

Everyone called him that, though his real name was Davu, and he walked everywhere, following routes that existed on no modern map. [101] When he finally appeared on the eastern path, his silhouette bent under a leather pack, Maren felt the familiar tightness in her chest—the frequency she'd been chasing for eleven months, the signal that meant she was close to something she couldn't yet name. [140]

Belonging, maybe. [142] She'd spent her career piloting reconnaissance drones over conflict zones, reading landscapes for what was missing rather than what was present. [161] Absence was her expertise. [164] Missing pieces told stories that presence never could. [172]

"You brought it?" she called down from the platform. [181]

Davu looked up, his face perpetually surprised, as though every moment contained some minor revelation he hadn't anticipated. [198] He reached into his pack and produced a small object wrapped in oilcloth. [210] Even from this distance, Maren recognized the shape—a type foundry matrix chip, brass and dense, engraved with a single character from an alphabet that had no known language. [237]

She'd found the first seven chips scattered across observatory ruins on three continents. [249] Each one stamped a different character. [254] Together, they were supposed to form a sequence—coordinates, a word, a frequency—but the pattern had gaps. [269] Missing pieces. [271] The eighth chip was the one Davu carried, pulled from a collapsed wall in a monastery two hundred kilometers south. [290]

[462] She pulled her signal analyzer from her pack and input the ratios. [473] The device returned a single frequency: 194.18 hertz. [679] The brilliant dimness held them both, and the observatory exhaled at last. [691]
