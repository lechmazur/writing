<story>The rational truffle forager descended into the hanging orchard after the empathy virus had stripped away every shared feeling. [13] Rain lashed the ravine, loosening roots so entire rows of ancient apple trees swung like broken pendulums above the flooded creek. [32] He carried only a battered satchel, a discredited textbook whose spine had split years earlier, and the antique glass eye hidden in his coat. [54] The eye had been smuggled out of a shuttered medical archive; its single lens still held a faint swirl of mercury that caught moonlight even under storm clouds. [79] He needed the relic because his own speech had become involuntarily truthful, and truth now drew predators who once called themselves neighbors. [101] Psychological safety required occlusion, and the textbook offered rumored cures that no licensed physician would acknowledge. [118] He opened the book beneath a dripping ledge, traced faded ink diagrams with one finger, and prepared a paste of fermented bark, iron filings, and rainwater collected from a hollow stump. [148] The mixture stung when he applied it around the empty socket, yet the glass eye seated itself without resistance and dimmed the urge to confess every thought. [173] For three nights he tested the method while storms battered the ravine walls, sending sheets of mud across the narrow terraces. [194] On the fourth morning he located a cluster of black truffles pushing through leaf litter beneath a tilted tree. [213] Their scent rose sharp and clean despite the rot surrounding them, and he knelt to harvest without announcing his discovery aloud. [234] The glass eye cooled against his skin, sealing the words that once would have escaped: the size of the find, the exact coordinates, the memory of hunger that drove him here. [265] He carried the truffles upslope toward the single remaining cable bridge, pausing only when lightning revealed a figure waiting on the far side. [290] The stranger claimed to trade medicine for food, yet the forager recognized the empty stare of someone whose own empathy had never returned. [312] Instead of admitting he carried nothing to share, he let the eye occlude the reply, answering only that the orchard had nothing left worth taking. [337] The stranger turned away, and the forager crossed the swaying planks, each step measured so the bridge would not announce his passage. [360] By dusk the storm eased enough for moonlight to silver the wet leaves. [372] He reached his ledge shelter, peeled the hardened paste from his face, and examined the glass eye under the textbook’s marginal notes. [395] The mercury swirl had cleared slightly, allowing a single truthful sentence to surface before the occlusion returned: he would repeat the cure at sunrise. [421] Days passed in fractured rhythm; he foraged at dawn, applied the paste at dusk, and slept without the old compulsion to describe every bruise and loss. [447] The ravine continued to erode, yet the hanging trees that remained formed a lattice of temporary safety, their roots gripping rock just long enough for one more harvest. [475] On a clear afternoon the forager discovered the eye had begun to cloud permanently, its surface etched by repeated applications of the iron paste. [499] He considered discarding the relic but found that without it the old words pressed forward again, threatening the fragile boundary he had built between himself and the emptied world. [528] Instead he returned to the textbook, copied one final formula onto the inside of his coat, and descended once more into the terraces where truffles still pushed through storm-turned soil. [559] The orchard creaked above him, a chorus of strained wood and dripping water, yet the silence inside his own mouth felt like the first shelter he had known since the virus. [588] He worked until the light failed, then climbed back to the ledge carrying the last harvest and the smuggled eye now sealed against further confession. [613] The bridge behind him swayed in the returning wind, and he did not look back. [626]
