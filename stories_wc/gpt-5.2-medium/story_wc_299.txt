<story>After the livestreamed appraisal blew up in my face, I drove east with my license still valid and my name newly poisonous. [22]People had watched me on camera call a battered guitar amp original, and then the owner peeled back the tolex to show a modern circuit board. [48]The chat laughed, the host frowned, and the applause that used to rescue me at the end of a show felt like it had been switched off. [75]I used to be a roadie for touring bands, the guy who taped down cables and listened to arenas breathe, until the night our singer died and my ears filled with silence. [107]I reinvented myself as an antiques appraiser because facts felt like earplugs, and if I could date a hinge or name a wood grain, I did not have to feel. [137]Now, after failing publicly, I took a short contract in an echo valley where an old quarry was being surveyed for a museum project. [161]The only office was the quarry office trailer, bolted to gravel, its thin walls humming when trucks rolled by, and its window looking down into the scar of stone. [190]The foreman, Marla, slid me a clipboard and said they had pulled artifacts from the abandoned maintenance shed, and the grant required proper documentation. [214]I answered with confidently uncertain nods, the kind I once gave sound engineers, and started insulating myself emotionally with facts: manufacturer marks, corrosion patterns, probable dates. [240]In the last crate, under a tarp that smelled of rain, I found a small scrap of speaker grille cloth, black with a silver thread, torn like it had been wrested away. [272]Marla said the quarry used to host summer concerts before the pit flooded, and the cloth might have come from the PA stack that fell during a storm. [300]I measured the weave with calipers, listed its cotton percentage from a burn test, and wrote down a catalog number that my hands remembered from touring catalogs. [327]But the more I stared at it, the more I heard the old after-applause hush, when stagehands move quietly and everybody pretends the night is not ending. [354]That hush was where my loss lived, because it was always the moment our singer would lean into me and ask, half joking, what any of it was for. [383]I had no answer then, and after my televised mistake I had even less, so I asked Marla if I could take the scrap into the trailer overnight. [551]The scrap on my board could have been from that same rig, and suddenly my job was not about proving I was right, but about tracing where sound had tried to stay.
