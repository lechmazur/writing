<story>The disused railway station was overgrown with ivy, and even the old departure board had been swallowed by green. [19]I waited under the cracked clock, because rumor said an elusive disaster relief logistician would arrive there whenever a town forgot how to share. [43]Her name was Mara Quill, and people described her as desperately jocular, the sort who apologized before stealing your chair and then brought you soup. [68]The soup, they said, came from nowhere, ferried by drone, mule, or teenage bicycle gangs, all coordinated by Mara through improbable rumor chains. [91]In flood seasons she moved pallets like chess pieces; in drought she traded fuel for water rights; in every crisis she kept records only in her head, so no one could seize them. [124]I had come for a different delivery: a cure for loneliness, which in my city spread faster than the river and left people alive but unclaimed. [150]The station, abandoned since the last war, was the only place neutral enough for strangers to meet without feeling recruited. [170]A wind pushed paper tickets along the platform, and every ticket carried a scribbled phrase about a fragment of moon rock hidden in the baggage room. [196]The first time I heard it, the story sounded like nonsense; the tenth time, from a barber, a priest, and a scrapyard dog, it began to feel like a map. [226]Mara arrived with no footsteps, just laughter, and when she saw me she tipped an invisible hat and said, 'Congratulations, you look terrible, so you must be serious.' [254]Her grin was bright but hungry, as if she made jokes the way others made fire, to keep the dark from sitting too close. [278]'I can move medicine and tents,' I told her, 'but I cannot move myself out of my own silence, and someone says the moon rock can catalyze a change.' [307]She led me past ivy curtains into the baggage room, where a cracked suitcase sat on a table like an altar built by pranksters with excellent manners. [334]Inside, wrapped in disaster paperwork and a child's comic book, lay the thumb sized fragment of moon rock, gray as ash and oddly warm to touch. [360]Mara did not take it; she only placed her hands around mine, not quite holding, and said, 'You cannot own this, because it is bound by reciprocity.' [387]'The rock came down in a meteor storm,' she went on, 'and every time someone used it to feel less alone, they had to deliver a kindness to the next person, or it went cold.' [422]That rule had traveled, she said, along the same crooked channels she used for aid, through improbable rumor chains that made neighbors ask after neighbors they had ignored. [450]This was when the story changes, because I realized she was not the courier of the cure but its first patient, hiding her need behind logistics and punch lines. [479]Outside, a group of evacuees and petty thieves had begun to occupy the platform, politely arguing about who would sweep the floor and who would write the rules for stealing. [509]Mara walked among them like a mayor who refused a badge, trading batteries for blankets, insisting on thank yous, and making everyone laugh just enough to keep hands open. [538]I kept the moon rock in my pocket, feeling its warmth rise and fall as I matched her exchanges: I gave my last cigarette to the barber, the barber mended a torn coat for a thief, the thief returned a lost child. [580]Each link was small and slightly ridiculous, yet it catalyzed a steadier current than any sermon, and the station filled with names, favors, and invitations to sit close. [608]Near midnight the rock cooled, not from failure but from completion, and Mara finally let her grin fall and asked if I would walk her home, not as a client, but as company. [641]I said yes, and before we left we set the fragment back in the suitcase with a note of where it would be next, because cures that work are the ones you pass on.
