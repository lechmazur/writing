She was a cryptic letterpress operator, paid to print invitations, warnings, and last messages, and she had a habit of borrowing personalities the way other people borrowed coats. [57]
A client would sit across her chase, speak in grief or rage, and Kestrel would catch that voice, wear it in her mouth, and answer as if she had always been them. [89]
It made business smooth, but inside her head the borrowed selves crowded, arguing in different tenses, until her own thoughts felt like misregistered prints. [113]
