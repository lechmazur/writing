[70] The displaced persons had gathered as they did every seventh day, their faces turned toward the chamber where she stood in her guide's uniform, still wearing it even though there were no longer museum walls to protect. [134] She had been a cultured museum guide before the borders closed, before the crackdown on traditional arts drove her people into these hidden chambers behind the monumental waterfall, and she had carried with her only two things: her knowledge and her grandmother's lens. [261] He had spoken of the outer world, of how the new regime burned handcrafts and scattered the families who practiced them, calling their work unnecessary luxuries in a age of efficient manufacture. [321] But after his last visit, when he had pressed into Maren's palm a small jar of rosewater-scented honey and whispered that the authorities were closing their net too tight for even bees to fly through, she had not seen him again. [565] Her family's custom of baking bread shaped like initials had sustained her through these dark months, the ritual of kneading dough into letters carrying the weight of ancestral memory. [621] Each morning she rose before dawn and pressed the initial of each displaced artisan into loaves, delivering them to chambers throughout the grotto city, a silent alphabet of sustenance and recognition. [1265] The thaw outside meant danger, yes, but it also meant opportunity.
