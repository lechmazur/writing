[68] The link had belonged to her grandmother, a surface fisherwoman who had never imagined her granddaughter would one day teach navigation in an underwater metropolis built after first contact. [124] Mira's students called her "balancedly curious," a phrase she had come to appreciate for its accuracy—she balanced the weight of old ocean wisdom against the strange new astronomy that the visitors had awakened in all of them. [195]
The aliens had arrived eighteen months ago, and their presence had unfold-ed horizons that no human calculation had ever predicted. [254] They did not speak in words but in mathematical relations, and from their transmissions, humanity had learned that the cosmos was far older and stranger than anyone had dared to imagine. [313] Mira had spent her life teaching sailors how to read currents, how to trace invisible pathways through open water, but now she burned with a different question: could one navigate by starlight through water so dense that the stars themselves seemed folded into distant memory? [384]
Tonight she would attempt her first calculations using edge geometry, a method the visitors had demonstrated during their third month of contact. [441] The technique involved understanding how light bent at the boundaries between media—not just the mathematical abstractions she had studied, but the actual geometry of light's edges as it transitioned from vacuum to atmosphere to water. [503] The benthic city's research tower rose before her, its crystalline windows glowing with the amber light of a hundred scientists who, like her, could not sleep while the universe had suddenly grown so much larger. [564]
Dr. Chen met her at the laboratory entrance, his expression carrying the same somber marvel that Mira felt in her own chest. [618] "The anchor link again," he observed, nodding toward the chain segment she had not yet released. [667] "You still carry it even now." [686]
"My grandmother used to say that a chain is just a series of connections between what holds you down and what pulls you forward," Mira explained, securing the link to her belt where it would rest during the calculations. [758] "The visitors taught us that every point in the universe is connected to every other point by invisible threads. [807] I think she understood something about that, even without ever knowing they existed." [862]
They entered the calculation chamber, where screens displayed the captured light of stars filtered through miles of ocean above them. [919] The light arrived faded and scattered, its edges softened by the weight of water, but it was not gone. [975] This was what the visitors had shown them: the stars were not lost to those who lived beneath the waves, only transformed. [1032] Mira began to expand her calculations, tracing the edge geometry of each photon as it made its patient journey from distant suns to the benthos where humanity had rebuilt civilization. [1096]

[1139] With each measurement, Mira felt herself expanding into a larger understanding of how navigation might work in this new universe where humanity was no longer alone. [1200] The boat anchor chain link rested against her leg, a grounding presence as she reached toward starlight. [1254]

[1280] When she finally looked up from her calculations, Dr. Chen had left a message on her screen: the visitors were departing in the morning, taking with them the ships that had allowed humanity's first journeys beyond the solar system. [1350] Mira understood then why the aliens had shared their knowledge of edge geometry—not to solve humanity's problems, but to show them that the cosmos was a sea of its own, navigable by anyone willing to learn its currents. [1483] Her grandmother had used it to hold a boat steady against storms.
