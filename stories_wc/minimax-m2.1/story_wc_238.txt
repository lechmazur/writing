<story>The hush before the orchard fell settled over the valley like a held breath. [29] Eleanor stood at the edge where the apple trees met the library's back entrance, watching chainsaws sleep in trucks, waiting for morning to make good on its threat. [74] In her pocket, her grandmother's antique perfume bottle—a curvy vessel of violet glass with a faded gold label—held nothing but air, yet she could still smell their mother's garden whenever she uncorked it. [134] She'd come to the sheet music archive to find Margot, her sister, the one she'd stopped calling six years ago, the one who had always understood music the way Eleanor understood departures. [186]
Eleanor worked as a gossip-column exorcist, documenting the unfinished business of the departed the way society columnists tracked the living's scandals. [237] She chronicled souls the way others chronicled lovers' spats—with detached sympathy and careful metaphor, never stating the wound directly but always circling close enough to let readers feel its shape. [290] It was easier that way, advising through metaphors, letting people discover their own pain rather than having it handed to them like a bill. [341] And she had become good at imperfect miracles, the kind that worked just enough to matter but never quite completely—fleeting contacts, stuttering connections, the electrical hum of something trying to bridge a gap that was simply too wide. [399]
This was one of those miracles, paid for with favors owed to a researcher who owed her one in return. [448] The induced currents would reach wherever Margot's consciousness had drifted after the accident, creating a temporary channel, a flickering window through which they might speak. [502] Eleanor had only minutes before the orchard crews arrived, before the chainsaws roared, before the trees that had witnessed their childhood fell into silence. [555] She descended into the archive's basement, where the sheet music of dead composers waited in faded folders like letters never sent. [604]
Margot was there, as Eleanor had known she would be, hunched over a manuscript in the archive's dim light, her form flickering like a candle in a draft. [659] The incomplete symphony on the table had been their mother's favorite—a composition abandoned mid-measure when her hands grew too weak to play. [714] Neither sister had finished it. Neither had known how. [743]
"You always did this," Margot said without looking up, her voice carrying the static of a poor connection. [785] "You always spoke in riddles when the truth was too heavy to carry straight." [822]
Eleanor stopped three steps away, the weight of six years of silence pressing against her chest. [861] She had come to deliver an overdue apology, to use these induced currents to bridge what she had let fall into ruin, but now that she stood here, the words had tangled in her throat like loosened strings. [919] The apology she owed was not a metaphor. It was simple and ugly and would not be solved by clever comparison. [969]
"I'm sorry I wasn't there when you woke up," Eleanor said, and the words felt like stones dropping into still water. [1022] "I'm sorry I couldn't face what the accident had made of us. I'm sorry I pruned myself from your life instead of learning to grow around the damage." [1077]
Margot's form flickered brighter for a moment, the induced currents responding to something in her sister's voice, and then steadied. [1131] The silence stretched between them, carrying the particular weight of something long broken finally being acknowledged. [1177] Above them, through the library's old windows, the first chainsaw cough broke the morning's hush, and the orchard began its slow dying. [1234]
"I kept the perfume bottle because it smelled like the garden," Margot said softly. "I kept it because even after everything, I wanted to remember what it felt like to belong to something." [1295]
The currents began to fade, Eleanor could feel them thinning, the connection stuttering toward its inevitable end. [1344] She wanted to say more—to explain that her silence had been fear, not cruelty, that she had stayed away because seeing Margot broken by that invisible attacker would have meant admitting that some things could not be fixed by wishing hard enough. [1414] But the miracle was imperfect, as all miracles are, and the current gave out before she could find the words. [1465]

[1535] She pocketed the antique perfume bottle and climbed the stairs into the gray morning light, carrying with her the particular ache of an apology delivered too late to matter and just in time to begin again. [1604] Somewhere in the valley, a tree fell, and the sound was not an ending but a beginning—the sharp, clean break that came before growth could start anew. [1668]
