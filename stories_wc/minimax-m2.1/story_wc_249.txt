[60] The treatment had reduced her to this— guinea pig for Dr. Vasquez's experimental protocol, a woman whose neural pathways were being mapped in real-time by machines that hummed their own secret songs. [122] She did not mind being the subject of study, not really, but she minded the way the scientists spoke around her as if her consciousness were already a completed equation waiting to be solved. [185] There was a quiet rebellion in simply being here, in choosing to stay when she could have withdrawn consent after the third session left her vomiting stars into the recovery basin. [250]
The garden itself seemed to drum in answer to her thoughts—a responsive ecosystem where sound made color and color made meaning. [311] She had learned to drum her fingers against the crystalline trunks of the resonance trees, each tap producing auric ripples that spread across the garden's floor like liquid light. [374] Everything here responded to touch: the harmonic ferns that sang when stroked, the bass moss that pulsed deep violet at low frequencies, the chiming orchids that required exactly the right pressure to release their fragrance. [440] The scientists had designed this place to be a tool, a therapy space where patients could externalize their internal chaos, but Elara had discovered something they had not intended. [504]
She climbed to her usual perch on the observation platform, a raised platform of black stone that caught every vibration from below. [561] From here she could see the auric lagoon beyond the garden's boundary—that stretch of golden water that only appeared under starlight, as if the night sky poured itself into the earth at precisely the right hour. [625] The researchers called it a natural phenomenon, a geological accident, but Elara had watched it for seventeen sessions now and she knew better. [688] The lagoon was breathing. [696]

[772] They came as they always did: not as her own, but as theirs. [798] Hers and Mira's. [809] The twin frequencies that the treatment had accidentally linked, creating a bridge between Elara's present and her sister's past. [866] Mira, who had dreamed of building a sanctuary for consciousness, who had died believing the dream had died with her. [920]

[998] She pressed her palms flat against the stone and drummed her fingers in that ancient pattern, and the garden answered.
