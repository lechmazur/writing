Iris had spent seventeen years in the lighthouse, making clocks that turned backward because forward motion had become unbearable to witness. [44] As a counter-clockwise clock maker, her days were measured in rewinded hours and inverted minutes, each gear deliberately placed against time's natural progression. [91] The solitude had sharpened her mind into something precise and patient, qualities that served her current purpose well. [134]
The hunger stone emerged with the low tide, its weathered surface bearing the warning marks of generations who had ignored similar portents. [191] She had watched it break through the retreating water line at dawn, its granite face scarred with dates and names—sailors and dreamers who had attempted what she was about to attempt. [249] The stone's message was unambiguous: some futures were written to consume those who refused to accept them. [304]
Iris descended the spiral stairs to examine the ancient device she had discovered three months prior in a crate that had washed ashore during a storm. [362] The hunger stone's warning strengthened her resolve rather than diminishing it; she had come to this isolated lighthouse specifically to step beyond fate's design. [420] Her motivation had crystallized during her final year as an apprentice, when she had seen her own death reflected in a customer's timepiece—a vision that had sent her fleeing across the continent to this rocky island where navigation charts showed only question marks. [485]

[540] She had builtdevices that measured regret, instruments that captured fading memories, and the great counter-clockwise chronometer that dominated the eastern wall—a machine designed not to tell time but to unmake it. [601] But none of these inventions prepared her for the ancient yoyo she now held, its wooden body carved with mathematical sequences that no civilization had ever recorded. [658]

[768] When she first spun the yoyo downward, it had risen again without any apparent force pushing it, defying every principle she had ever studied. [827] The device seemed to exist in deliberate opposition to gravity, time, and expectation. [874]

[939] The light responded to her calculations, brightening when she solved another sequence of the ancient carvings. [988] Through weeks of careful analysis, she had determined that the yoyo was not a toy at all but a repository, a mechanism designed to capture and store time's excess energy like a compressed spring. [1163] Outside, the sea waited with patient cruelty, knowing that failure meant becoming another warning carved into the hunger stone's surface by someone who came after. [1222] Iris had prepared for this moment by emptying the lighthouse of everything except her tools, her clocks, and her growing certainty that fate was merely a habit that could be broken. [1284]

[1342] The latent radiance poured from it in waves, illuminating the workroom with cold fire that cast shadows backward instead of forward. [1398] She felt bleak exhilaration surge through her veins—the terrifying knowledge that she was simultaneously on the brink of liberation and annihilation. [1454] The clocks on the walls began spinning in reverse, their pendulums swinging against every law of physics. [1507]

[1563] Through the dissolving walls, she could see the hunger stone submerged once more as the tide returned, its warning irrelevant to someone who had already stepped beyond its reach. [1623] Iris continued spinning, her counter-clockwise world collapsing into light, her fate finally written in a language that had no past tense. [1678]
