<story>
The morning after the phoenix rose from the ashes of the old world, Mira walked into the glassblower's courtyard carrying her paintbrushes and a small wooden box that contained her most treasured possession—a concertina reed tongue, worn smooth by generations of music-making. She had bartered awakenings with the old harmonium player who had given it to her, trading three paintings of shadows for this small piece of wood that somehow contained the memory of every song ever played through it.
The glassblower himself was already at work, his furnace roaring against the grey dawn, but he nodded to her with the easy recognition of someone who had seen her come here every morning for many months. Mira set up her easel near the pile of discarded glass pieces—those the master had deemed imperfect. Cracked vessels, bubbles trapped mid-escape, colors that had bled together in ways he hadn't intended. These were her favorite subjects.
She was colorblind, though not in the way most people assumed. The world presented itself to her in a constant subtle shift of light and shadow, texture and form, and she had learned to see beauty where others saw only error. This was her gift and her burden—to find beauty in imperfection when the world around her insisted that perfection was the only worthy goal.
The plant arrangements began as accidents. Mira would set down her brush to stretch her hands and notice how the fallen leaves from the courtyard's old oak had arranged themselves in patterns that spoke to her. A curved leaf pointing toward the broken blue glass. Three small petals gathered near the base of a potted plant. She began to move them deliberately, creating small compositions that seemed to communicate something she couldn't quite name.
The glassblower's apprentice was the first to notice. He was a young man with steady hands and curious eyes, and he would pause during his work to study her arrangements. Mira noticed his attention but said nothing. Instead, she began arranging the dried flowers and leaves in more complex patterns, watching him from the corner of her eye as he tried to decipher their meaning.
"You're speaking to me," he said one afternoon, his voice uncertain but delighted. "Aren't you?"
Mira smiled her innocently cunning smile. She had been waiting for him to ask, had been carefully constructing a language of leaves and petals specifically for him. She nodded and gestured to the arrangement at his feet—a spiral of ivy leading from his workbench to her easel, with a single perfect rose petal placed at the center.
The apprentice studied the pattern with the intensity of someone receiving a secret. "What does it mean?" he asked.
She picked up a small piece of blue glass, held it up to the light, and placed it carefully beside the petal. Then she pointed to his hands, still red from the heat of the furnace, and made a gesture of cradling something delicate.
"You see the beauty," he whispered, understanding blooming across his features like the first color a colorblind person might ever truly perceive. "In the broken things. In my clumsy hands."
Mira nodded again and reached into her pocket to pull out the concertina reed tongue. She held it out to him, this small piece of her past, this bartered awakening she had received and now wanted to pass forward. The apprentice looked at it with wonder, not understanding its significance but sensing the weight of the gift.
She placed it in his palm and then began a new arrangement—this time using the fallen glass shards and the dried plants together, creating something that existed between sight and sound, between what could be seen and what could only be felt.
The glassblower stopped his work to watch them, two young people communicating in a language he didn't understand but recognized nonetheless. It was the language of awakening—of seeing something for the first time and knowing it had always been there, waiting to be discovered in the imperfections that made every cracked vessel and misblown glass unique.
The sun rose higher over the courtyard, burning off the last smoke from the phoenix's rising, and Mira painted by its light, her colors may have been wrong to others but right to her, and her plant language spread across the courtyard like a quiet revolution, teaching anyone who would look closely enough that awakening could be bartered, traded, passed from hand to hand like a single reed tongue worn smooth by generations of music.
