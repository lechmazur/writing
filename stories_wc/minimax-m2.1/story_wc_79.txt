She had been mapping for eleven years, ever since the gate had rusted shut and the expedition she loved most simply vanished into the marshlands beyond. The Council said they had chosen to leave, that the scholars of the Spiral Compass had found their patterns and departed into the pattern-between. Mira knew better. The abandoned campsites, the half-finished sketches of shell formations, the reed whistle still tied to the oldest explorer's belt—all suggested an ending that refused to reveal itself. [220]
The patterns had changed since the rust. Where the shell spirals once pulsed with faint bioluminescence, now they merely gleamed dully under the relentless sun that poured through the cloudless sky like something dying. Sunwashed dread had become the texture of everyday life in Veloth-Seres. The light revealed too much—the cracks spreading through the great shells, the way the turtle city was slowly settling into the mud from which it had risen centuries ago. [322]
Mira knelt at the intersection of three major spiral-roads, her fingers hovering above the shell surface as she listened for the frequency that the old cartographers called the hum-of-origin. The shells still resonated if you knew how to hear them, their patterns forming a language older than the city itself. She had learned to crack the code of these pattern languages from her mentor, from the journals of explorers who had mapped the turtles before the gate rusted, before the watchers appeared on the horizon. [422]
They stood motionless in the shallows of the distant marsh, those watchers—tall silhouettes that might have been men or might have been something wearing the shapes of men. They had arrived the morning after the gate seized shut, standing in patient rows along the tree line where the expedition had disappeared. They watched. They waited. They never spoke. [511]
The Council forbade questioning them. The Priestesses of the Closed Gate said they were guardians of the threshold, spirits bound to the rust, and that to approach was to invite destruction. But Mira had found something in the spiral patterns—a recurring sequence that her mentor had marked with a single ink dot before vanishing herself. The sequence, when read through the proper pattern language, formed a question. Where do the watchers look when they are not watching the city? [614]
She pressed her palm flat against the shell and felt the faintest tremor beneath her fingers. The turtles were dreaming again, or dying, or simply remembering something that had happened in waters so old the sun had not yet learned to burn. Mira pulled her reed whistle from the pocket at her hip—a simple thing, crafted from the marsh reeds that grew where the expedition had last been seen. The Council said the whistle was a superstition, a cartographer's charm with no real power. But the pattern language disagreed. [722]
She placed the whistle against her lips and blew the three-tone sequence that the lost expedition had used to signal their camps. The sound emerged thin and reedy, carrying across the empty spiral-plaza like a question asked in a language the turtles had forgotten. For a moment, nothing happened. Then the shells beneath her palms began to warm, and Mira felt the great creatures shifting in their ancient sleep. [798]
The spiral beneath her feet had begun to glow. [815]
