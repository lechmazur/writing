The fever broke at 3:47 AM, and Mira woke to the sound of wind tearing at the mountain weather station's steel bones. [67] She lay still for a long moment, feeling the absence of heat that had consumed her for three days, listening to the station's instruments chitter their midnight reports. [131] Her mouth tasted like copper and regret, and somewhere in the thin air beyond the glass, the aurora borealis draped itself across the darkness like a curtain of pale grief. [196]
She had been dreaming about the woman who held this post before her—Elena, whose botanical illustration now hung above the desk, pressed between two panes of glass Elena had salvaged from a broken window. [263] The illustration depicted a mountain flower that grew only in the high passes, a pale blossom with petals that seemed to tremble even in the static image. [322] Elena had drawn it the week before the storm that took her, that storm Mira had failed to predict with sufficient urgency. [382]
Mira swung her legs over the side of the cot, peripherally aware of the frost patterns crawling across the window, the way the aurora's green light made them look like tiny branching rivers. [451] Her fault tolerance as an oracle—that was what the administration called it when a forecaster learned to live with the weight of errors—was tested nightly in dreams where Elena's face appeared not accusatory but understanding, mouth opening to release a single word Mira could never quite hear. [529]
She rose and moved to the weather console, her movements still uncertain, her body remembering the fever's battle. [581] The displays showed pressure dropping steadily, a system building somewhere to the west where warm air from the valley was beginning its slow collision with the cold dome settled over the peaks. [645] Any competent oracle would see the pattern and sound the warning. [679] Mira's hands hovered over the controls, and she thought about certainty, about how it swayed in her like a damaged antenna receiving two signals at once. [743]
The botanical illustration caught her peripheral vision again, and she turned to study it fully. [792] Elena had been peripherally aware of her own limitations, had once told Mira that the art she made was not about capturing the flower but about remembering that flowers outlast the storms that threaten them. [862] There was a smudge near the flower's stem where Elena's thumb had rested during a long evening of work, a small imperfection that became the illustration's most honest feature. [930] Mira had always wanted to touch it, to feel the paper where Elena's skin had left its mark, but she never had. [989]
She touched it now. [1010]
The paper was cool and slightly rough, and Mira felt something shift in her chest—not the last remnants of fever but something older, a truth she had been swallowing since the night Elena walked out into a storm Mira had predicted would pass to the south. [1085] The truth had weight; she had carried it in her body like a stone, and now she was trying to find its exact mass, to understand how much grief a single error could hold. [1153] The aurora pulsed outside the window, and Mira understood with sudden clarity that Elena had not blamed her, that the word in her dreams was probably "forgive," spoken in a voice Mira had been too terrified to recognize. [1226]
She turned back to the console and began typing her forecast, her fingers finding the keys with renewed precision. [1282] The margins of the screen filled with data points, each one occupying its small space in the larger pattern, each one insignificant alone but essential when taken together. [1348] That was how certainty worked, Mira realized—not as a monolith but as a collection of observations from the edge of perception, swaying in the wind but still standing, still reporting. [1419]

[1480] She swallowed it along with the truth she had finally allowed to settle in her chest, and she began the work of warning the valley below. [1543] Elena's illustration hung in its frame, the smudge on its stem catching the auroral light, a small gray monument to the way truth could be held gently instead of swallowed whole. [1615]
