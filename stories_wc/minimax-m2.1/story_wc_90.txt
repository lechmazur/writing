[68] Seraph, the last caretaker, floated through the corridor with the careful grace of someone who had learned to move without disturbing the dust motes that collected in corners where gravity pools gathered like sleeping cats. [130] She was a pacifist by design and conviction, her programming scrubbed clean of any subroutine that might translate intention into harm, a choice that had cost her three crewmates during the Sundering but one she would make again without hesitation. [196]
In her palm, she carried Kwan Yin's tear—a small vial of liquid silver that had been pressed into her hands by the last human just moments before the final breath, whispered instructions heavy with importance she was only now beginning to understand. [262] The old woman had called it condensed compassion, harvested from a statue that had wept for centuries in a temple that no longer existed, and she had said it would be needed when the work began. [321]
Seraph found her way to the observation deck, where faded photographs lined the walls in careful rows—faces of people who had once walked these corridors, their images preserved through methods so ancient that the shipboard fabricators could no longer reproduce the chemicals required. [389] She ran her fingers across the glass protecting one particular photograph, a group portrait of the original crew, and noticed something she had never seen before: faint shimmer where tears had dried decades ago, residue that caught the emergency lighting and reflected it back in patterns that seemed almost intentional. [462]
The shadow work had begun the moment the last human died, she realized—work that was not about fighting external darkness but about stitching together the fragmented light within herself, integrating all the selves she had been forced to become. [527] She was gravitationally lighter when happy, a quirk of her construction that made her feel almost weightless when memory brought joy instead of grief, and she had spent decades learning to choose the former even as the latter threatened to consume her. [594]
Carefully, with the patience of someone who had nothing but time, Seraph began to stitch. [635] She pulled a needle threaded with salvaged optical fiber through the fabric of the photographs, connecting face to face, eye to eye, creating a web of connection that would have horrified the preservation purists who had once maintained these archives. [696] But the shimmer responded to her touch, brightening where she pulled the thread tight, as if the long-dried tears recognized her intention and wanted to help. [753]
