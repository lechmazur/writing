The stone pillars breathed. [5]

Not in the way living things did, but in the slow, deliberate pulse of roots pressing against ancient rock, the way the temple orchard had always done since the first caretakers coaxed vines to climb its bones. [30] Elira knew this because she had spent thirty years mapping their rhythms, her fingers tracing the grooves where oak branches met limestone as if the two had grown into each other by choice. [55] The botanical diary in her lap was thick with sketches of this symbiosis—ink renderings of copper wire spiraled around boughs, notes on how the metal oxidized just enough to feed the trees without poisoning them. [85]

Six months, the healers had said. [89] A generous estimate, given how the sickness had already turned her joints to brittle hinges and her breath to something thin, like wind through dead leaves. [108] She had laughed, because what else was there to do? [115] The temple elders offered her a quiet corner to wither in, a place of honor among the other fading scholars, but Elira had refused. [131] If she was going to die, she’d do it with her hands in the dirt, remodeling the orchard one last time—not as an act of defiance, but of devotion. [153]

The architectural secrets of the place were never meant to be hoarded. [161] The pillars, hollow in places, held chambers where roots and fungi traded nutrients in the dark, a system so gently systematic it shamed the rigid doctrines of the temple’s inner circle. [185] They called her work heresy: wrapping copper around the oaks to "corrupt their purity," as if the trees hadn’t leaned into the wire like lovers. [206] But Elira had proof. [209] In her diary, pressed between pages of bark rubbings, were the first blossoms from a branch she’d bound two decades ago—petals veined with faint green, the tree’s way of thanking her for the trace minerals. [255] "It disrupts the symmetry," they’d said, as if symmetry had ever fed a single hungry mouth. [267] Elira knelt, her knees cracking like old timber, and began to wrap the sapling’s thin trunk with copper wire, her fingers remembering the motions even as her body forgot how to move without pain. [292] The metal was warm from the sun, pliable. [297] She hummed, a tuneless thing, and the sapling’s leaves trembled in response. [306]

[342] "There’s a difference." [345] The copper would guide the sapling’s growth, coax it to twist around the pillar’s flaws instead of fighting them. [359] A marriage of sorts. [362]

[378] "Then I’ll have spent my last months doing what I should’ve done my whole life," she said, tapping the diary where a new sketch waited—this one of the sapling and the pillar, their lines blurring at the edges. [405] "Proving that respect isn’t earned by standing straight. [413] It’s earned by bending." [416]

[432] Elira sat against the central pillar, her diary open to a blank page, her breath shallow but steady. [444] She didn’t need to write the ending. [448] The trees would remember it for her. [452]
