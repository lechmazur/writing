The facility had been submerged for decades, its glass domes cracked but still holding back the Pacific’s slow, insistent weight. [20]
Dr. Elara Voss adjusted the brass eyepiece of her celestial scope, tracing the constellations not in the sky but in the bioluminescent algae blooming across the ruined walls—her maps were alive, shifting with the tides. [50]
She was a landscape astrologer, though no one had called her that in years; the title belonged to a time before the oceans rose, when people still believed the earth and stars could be read like a shared language. [85]
Tonight, she was reading them for herself.

Her fingers brushed the burnt feather tucked behind her ear—a relic from the last surface expedition, where she’d watched a murmuration of starlings scatter as the research vessel’s engines ignited. [115]
The feather’s edge was singed black, but the vane still shimmered iridescent under the facility’s dying emergency lights, as if holding a memory of flight she couldn’t name. [139]
Elara exhaled, fogging the scope’s lens, and there it was: the first firefly of summer, not an insect but a pulse of light from the abyss, rising like a held breath finally released. [165]
She laughed, softly ingenious, because of course the deep had its own fireflies—tiny, drifting jellyfish with photophores that flickered in Morse code only she seemed to understand. [190]

The facility’s AI, a ghost in the corroded wiring, hummed a question through the speakers: *"You’re recording the luminous respite again."* [205]
Elara didn’t answer. The respite wasn’t for data; it was the hour before sunrise when the pressure of the water above lessened, when the city’s bones groaned in harmony with the planetary tide, when she could almost hear the rhythm of belonging. [235]
She had spent three years in this drowned lab, mapping the way light bent through salt and ruin, trying to prove that the ocean’s currents carried the same patterns as the wind through a wheat field—that home wasn’t a place but a frequency. [265]

Then the new arrival came. [268]
His name was Kael, a salvage diver with a oxygen tank strapped to his back and a scar across his palm that he claimed was from "holding onto something too tightly." [290]
He moved like he expected the water to betray him, but when he saw the jellyfireflies, he stilled. [300]
Elara watched as one landed on his wrist, its glow seeping into his skin like ink. [308]
"You ever see anything like that topside?" she asked, already knowing the answer. [317]
Kael shook his head, captivated—not by the light, but by the way it made the burnt feather in her hair cast shadows like constellations against the rusted steel. [335]
She realized then that he wasn’t here for scrap. [339]
He was chasing the same thing she was: the moment when the world felt like it was breathing with you. [349]

They didn’t speak again until the respite peaked. [353]
Side by side on the observation deck, they exhaled in unison, their breath condensing into a single cloud between them, and the jellyfireflies surged upward in a spiral, as if pulled by the promise of dawn. [375]
Kael’s hand found hers. [377]
His palm was calloused, but his grip was careful, like he was afraid she’d dissolve into the dark. [386]
Elara didn’t pull away. [388]
For the first time in years, the weight of the water above them felt like an embrace, not a burial. [399]

By sunrise, the fireflies were gone, and the facility’s groans had stilled. [406]
Kael surfaced with a fragment of the dome’s glass in his pocket, its edges smoothed by time. [415]
Elara stayed below, pressing the burnt feather to her lips before tucking it into the logbook’s final page, next to the coordinates of the respite’s brightest pulse. [432]
She didn’t need to follow him to know they’d meet again. [437]
The ocean had its own way of keeping time, and so did she. [443]

Above, the first real fireflies of summer would be waking, their light a pale echo of the deep’s secret glow. [455]
Elara smiled. [456]
