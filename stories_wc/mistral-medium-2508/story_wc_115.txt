The tunnel exhaled damp like a sleeping beast, its ribs of rusted track curving into darkness where the flashlight barely reached. [17]
Elias crouched at the mouth of the abandoned line, his boots crunching broken glass—relics of the last crew who’d fled mid-shift, tools still scattered like bones. [36]

This time, the lure was the *weight*—not of iron, but of the thing his father had choked out over static three nights prior: *"They didn’t all leave."* [75]

The radio in Elias’s jacket hissed, tuned low to a late-night psychic hotline, where a woman’s voice cracked like thin ice over a lie. [90]
*"—something *moves* in the dark, caller, but it’s not what you think—"* [98]
He twisted the volume knob until the words blurred into white noise, a buffer against the silence that pressed in after his father’s confession. [116]
The old man had worked the night shift at the textile factory above this tunnel, back when the looms still hummed and the foremen called the third shift the *ghost shift*—twelve hours where the air thickened with something worse than lint. [139]

Elias’s fingers found the beaded valve wheel half-buried in the gravel, its brass surface pitted like a molared jaw. [153]
He appraised it not by touch but by the way his pulse jumped—too heavy for its size, as if it had absorbed the panic of the hands that last turned it. [172]
The factory had used these wheels to regulate steam pressure, but this one sat alone, unbolted, at the tunnel’s lowest point where the track dipped into a sump of black water. [193]

A draft sighed through the tunnel, carrying the scent of wet wool and something older, like burnt sugar left to rot. [232]
Elias’s skepticism was a reflex, honed by years of watching his mother light candles to saints who never answered, but the valve wheel’s weight in his palm felt like a rebuttal. [254]
