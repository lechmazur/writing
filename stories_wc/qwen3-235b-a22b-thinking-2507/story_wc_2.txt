Aris adjusted the fractured pendulum of the grandfather clock, his fingers smudged with oil and soot, the rhythmic tick-tick-tick replaced by an anxious silence. [25] He was a broken-clock tinkerer, sought only when mechanisms surrendered to inexplicable failure, not for repair but for diagnosis of the inevitable. [50] Lately, the failures followed a pattern: gears fused by heat no fire could explain, springs brittle as ancient parchment, all whispering of the Great Conflagration that had buried the city above in ashes decades ago. [92] Beneath the ashes, the truth lingered—not of loss, but of transformation. [115] Aris felt uncertainly certain that these clocks weren’t merely broken; they were messengers, their damage a language he strained to decipher. [151] His shop, crammed with timepieces frozen at 3:07, the hour the flames peaked, had become a museum of endings. [187] Yet he clung to precision, mapping each fracture with calipers, desperate to impose order on chaos, even as his own plans felt like sand slipping through his fingers. [237] To befriend uncertainty in planning was his quiet, aching motivation, a vow made after his last clock—a child’s music box—shattered mid-repair, scattering tiny, melted notes. [293] He needed proof that surrender to the unknown could be wisdom, not weakness. [326] The legend of the admiral’s lost compass rose surfaced in his mind, a relic said to guide through darkness, lost when the naval commander fled the burning city, its intricate brass petals vanishing into the catacombs below. [394] Aris decided he must find it, not for direction, but as a symbol: if he could retrieve something purposefully lost, perhaps he could trust the unplanned path.
