[16] In his right palm, a brain coral fragment presses against the lifeline, its calcium grooves sharp enough to anchor him to something real. [34] He’s a translator, or was, before the peace talks in the Solomon Islands shattered along with three of his ribs. [53] Now words swim in the space between nerve and nothing, and meaning refuses to hold its shape. [69] On the gurney beside him, a plaster cast—small, child-sized—leans against the chrome railing like a forgotten prayer. [90] The smudged pencil marks across its worn surface are a code he’s been trying to crack for seventeen years. [110] His daughter’s handwriting, from before the divorce, before he chose the Pacific’s broken languages over the quiet dialect of home. [136] The atrium’s glass walls weep condensation, each droplet catching the last bruised light of a tropical day. [159] Through the mist, he can make out the silhouette of a banyan tree, its aerial roots reaching down like unasked questions. [182] This is where he used to meet the village elders in Honiara, where he learned that translation is not about words but about carrying the unspeakable weight between them. [214] He had carried too much: the militants’ fear of erasure, the government’s fear of fracture, the children’s fear of the dark that tasted like cordite and salt. [244] The coral in his hand is from that beach on Malaita, where a boy once showed him how to find the brain-patterned stones beneath the war’s debris. [275] The boy had been shrewdly innocent, offering the fragment like a confession: “It remembers every tide, even when we forget to notice.” [304] Kael’s fingers trace the cast’s smudged notes, the anesthetic making his touch both clumsy and unbearably precise. [329] A stick figure with too many arms—maybe a drawing of him, always waving goodbye, always leaving. [381] Another layer, a phrase in his daughter’s invented script, the cipher she used when she didn’t want the grown-ups to understand her true cartography. [412] He begins to unknot the tangle of guilt and memory, each deciphered glyph a loosening of the cord that binds his punctured lung. [574] Each polyp’s ghost is a word he failed to translate, a fear he absorbed without releasing, a shared anxiety that colonized his silence. [604] The village elders had taught him that wounds must be opened to be grafted with new tissue, that pain is just information the body doesn’t yet understand. [643] Kael had thought they meant the grenade’s shrapnel in his side, not the silence in his daughter’s room that grew teeth. [676] But the two are the same now, both entry points for the same shared anxiety: that we become archipelagos despite ourselves. [712] The atrium’s mist thickens into something like forgiveness, indifferent and therefore kind.
