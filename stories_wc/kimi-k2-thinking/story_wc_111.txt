Silas believed that gardens know their gardeners, and he harbored a quiet need to prove this truth to the skeptical heirs who visited each spring. [68]
He kept a collection of cracked marbles in a small leather pouch, each one a relic from the days before luck became a number on the manor's central gauge. [97]
Every morning, Silas would polish a single marble, rubbing it with a soft cloth until the shattered surface caught the light like a tiny star. [122]
This ritual was his way of commemorating small victories elaborately: each polish marked a new tendril that had climbed the maze walls, a bloom that had opened despite the odds. [152]

Determined to mend the deception, Silas decided to reveal the marbles to the heirs, to show that the garden's vitality was tied to his own. [281]
