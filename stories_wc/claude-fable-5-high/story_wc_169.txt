Imre Voss was a logical dreamer, the kind of person who entered sleep with a notebook's discipline and left it with proofs scrawled across his memory. [26] Every night for a year, his dreams had delivered him to the same tide-polished grotto, its walls worn smooth as poured glass by centuries of patient water. [53] He did not believe in mysticism, so he treated the grotto as data. [66] The strange part was that the grotto was never quite the same grotto twice. [80] Some nights the shells on its floor spiraled clockwise; some nights his sister Lena's initials were carved by the entrance, though in waking life she had died before learning to write. [111] Imre formed a hypothesis: the grotto was a junction, a single chamber threaded across parallel timelines, and his lucid mind was the needle passing through. [136] To test it, he needed an instrument, and the dream supplied one with suspicious generosity: his grandfather's balloonist's altitude gauge, brass-rimmed, salvaged from the old man's last flight. [164] In the grotto, the needle did not measure height; it measured divergence. [176] When the dial read low, he stood in a timeline near his own; when it climbed, he had drifted somewhere stranger, somewhere kinder or crueler. [201] Awake, Imre was a man whom grief had hollowed quietly, a statistician who had stopped believing his own confidence intervals. [221] What he wanted, with an engineer's embarrassment about wanting anything, was to puzzle out new equations for hope: a rigorous reason to keep going that grief could not refute. [250] So he borrowed a method from his grandfather's trade and began launching trial balloons. [264] Not literal ones at first; a trial balloon, his grandfather had taught him, was any small thing sent up to learn which way the wind truly blew. [291] Each night Imre released a hypothesis into a different timeline and watched what the world did with it. [309] His messages had to survive the crossing, so he wrote them the only way the grotto would carry: by creating patterns with decorations. [332] He strung limpet shells in Fibonacci runs along the waterline, hung ribbons of dried kelp in prime-numbered intervals, arranged sea glass into lattices that encoded simple questions. [359] Are you well, the patterns asked whatever version of himself dreamed here next. [372] Did she live, in yours. [377] What do you know that I do not. [385] For weeks, nothing answered, and Imre remained lucidly watchful, refusing the dreamer's usual surrender, counting shells the way sentries count stars. [406] Then one night the gauge spun higher than he had ever recorded, and the grotto answered. [422] His shell spirals had been rearranged, his kelp ribbons reknotted, the sea glass reset into a lattice he recognized as his own notation, extended. [446] Some other Imre, in some other current of time, had finished his unfinished sentence. [460] Translated, the pattern said: she died here too, and I am still glad I was her brother. [477] Night after night the correspondence deepened, a slow tide of answers from selves who had grieved differently, loved differently, survived differently. [498] One timeline's Imre had become a teacher; his pattern was joyful, chaotic, full of children's names spelled in cockle shells. [518] Another had never recovered, and his lattice was a single broken line that Imre gently completed before leaving. [536] The work began to revitalize him in ways he could actually measure. [548] He slept eight hours. [552] He answered letters. [555] He noticed, with statistical surprise, that he was humming. [564] Each morning he woke into the same quiet upheaval: nothing in his life had changed, and everything had, the way a coastline is identical and utterly different after a single tide. [595] It was a serenity made entirely of upheaval, and he learned to stand inside it. [610] From the accumulated patterns, he finally derived what he had been hunting. [622] Hope, his data showed, was not a probability that things improve; it was the demonstrated existence of at least one timeline in which a self like you went on. [651] Not a feeling, but a proof by example. [659] On the final night, the altitude gauge read zero for the first time. [672] He stood in his own timeline, his own grotto, and understood the instrument's last lesson: his grandfather had never used the gauge to learn how high he was, but to know when it was safe to come down. [710] Imre built one last pattern, a spiral of every shell the grotto had ever offered him, an answer for whichever stranded self arrived next. [734] It said: the wind has been tested, and it holds. [744] Then he woke, walked to the actual sea near his actual house, and began, in daylight and with steady hands, to live like a man with evidence. [771]
