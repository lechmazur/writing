The guardianship had ended in failure, and Maren had carried that failure for three years like a stone sewn into her coat. [22] She had been the last relic keeper of the Hollow Order, sworn to guard the gilded reed flute until the worlds drifted close enough to touch. [48] On the night of the crossing, she had played it, and nothing had answered. [62] The Order dissolved; the keepers scattered; the flute stayed with her because no one else wanted to hold a thing that had refused to work. [87] Now she walked back into the Thunder-Echo Range, not to try again, but to understand. [102] Failure, her first teacher had said, is a harvest if you have the patience to glean it. [119]

The range earned its name honestly. [125] Storms broke against the far peaks and their thunder rolled down the canyons for hours afterward, folding over itself, echo answering echo until sound became a kind of weather. [154] But between storms, the canyons held a hush so complete it pressed on the ears. [169] Maren descended into that hush on the second morning, and it swallowed her footsteps whole. [184]

A dry riverbed threaded the canyon floor, littered with driftwood carried down by flash floods and bleached silver by the sun. [205] The Order's oldest texts claimed the boundary between worlds was not a wall but a weave, and that thin places wandered through the range like slow fish. [232] A keeper could track them only by triangulation, by patience, by marking where the air went strange. [249] So Maren began arranging driftwood as directional markers, dragging the pale branches into arrows on the riverbed, each one aimed at the place where her compass needle shivered. [277] By dusk she had laid eleven arrows, and they did not agree. [289]

She stood among them and felt the old shame rise: three years ago, on the night of the crossing, she had stood in a circle of keepers and played the flute toward a thin place that had already moved. [328] The arrows told her what no ceremony had: the weave never stopped drifting, and the Order had anchored its ritual to a map a century stale. [354] First insight, gleaned from the wreck of everything she had served. [365]

She slept beside the arrows and woke before dawn to a silence deeper than any she had known. [383] It was avalanchingly quiet, a stillness with mass and momentum, the kind that gathers above a slope and waits for one wrong sound to bring everything down. [410] Her eleven arrows had shifted in the night, or rather her perception had: now every one of them pointed at a shallow bend fifty paces upstream, where the air shimmered like heat over stone, though the morning was cold. [449]

Maren walked to the bend with the case under her arm. [460] She knelt and uncased the flute slowly, the way her teacher had taught her, leather straps first, then the cedar lid, then the silk. [484] The gilded reed caught the first light and held it like a held breath. [498] She raised it to her lips and then stopped. [507] Three years ago she had played into a thin place and shattered it, the way a shout shatters the poise of snow. [529] The texts said play; perhaps the texts, copied and recopied, had lost a word. [543] Second insight: the flute was a reed, and reeds do not only make sound; they receive it. [560]

So she did not blow. [565] She held the flute into the shimmering air and listened through it, ear to the open end, as one listens through a keyhole. [588] Far away, distant thunder rolled along the high peaks, and the canyon began to braid the echoes downward, fold on fold. [609] The flute caught them. [613] Through its gilded throat she heard the thunder doubled, one peal from her own sky and one from a sky she had never stood beneath, the two sounds weaving together like fingers interlacing. [646] The shimmer widened. [649] She glimpsed, through it, a riverbed like her own, and on it eleven arrows of pale driftwood pointing back at her. [670] Someone on the far side had been gleaning failure too. [680]

She did not cross. [684] The opening was narrow and the hush around it still felt poised to fall, and she had learned, finally, the difference between forcing a door and learning its hinges. [713] Instead she laid a twelfth arrow at the bend, pointing the way for whoever came next, and recorded everything: the drift of thin places, the listening reed, the thunder that belonged to two skies. [747] The guardianship was over, but the keeping, she understood now, had only begun. [760] Her failure had not been an ending; it had been the first marker in an arrow she was still laying, branch by branch, toward the bend where two worlds waited to be woven. [793]
