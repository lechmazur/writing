The bookstore occupied the whole of Mount Veil, its shelves drilled into granite like roots that had decided to read instead of drink. [23] Iska climbed its stairways every day after remembering became painful, because the store substituted reality nightly, and a person who cannot bear her past can simply wait for a new one. [54] Each dawn the corridors stood rearranged: doors where ladders had been, atlases shelved as confessions, yesterday's grief quietly misfiled. [73] It was mercy, of a kind, and Iska had taken it for three years. [87] But mercy erodes; lately she woke with the sense of a word on her tongue that no shelf in the mountain contained. [109] The word was home, and she could no longer remember its shape. [121] So she went looking for the seer. [128]

The mercury-glass seer lived in the Cartography Stacks, a figure blown from silvered glass, her surface holding every reader's reflection and returning none of them honestly. [154] Storm light moved inside her like hidden thunder, a weather that had not yet decided to speak. [171] "You want the lost language stone," the seer said before Iska opened her mouth. [185] "I want to understand the shape of home," Iska said. [195] "Same request, older grammar," said the seer. [202] Her glass chimed when she moved, a small thunder kept under the skin. [215] "The stone is shelved where the mountain hides what people bury in themselves. [228] You buried a talent here, the night you arrived. [237] The store has been filing it deeper ever since." [246]

Iska's hands went cold. [250] She remembered nothing of arriving, which was the point of staying. [261] The seer lifted one slender arm, and within her glass a gray feather hung suspended, like a fossil of flight. [281] "I cannot give you a map; the corridors will not be tonight what they are now. [297] But texture survives rearrangement." [301] She cracked her own wrist open like a bottle and drew the feather out, wet with quicksilver, then pressed it into Iska's palm. [324] "Read it with your fingers when the store begins to change. [335] Its barbs are notched in the alphabet you buried." [344]

That night Iska did not sleep through the substitution. [353] She stood in a corridor of dictionaries and felt the mountain inhale, shelves sliding like tectonic patience, and when the dark tried to hand her a kinder yesterday, she ran her thumb along the feather instead. [389] The notches spoke through her skin in a language she had no memory of learning and could not fail to understand. [410] Left at the ledger of droughts. [416] Down through the stairwell that smells of rain that never fell. [427] Each instruction arrived as texture: ridge, gap, ridge, the grain of meaning under her fingertip. [442] The labyrinth fought her gently, offering false rooms, but a feather cannot be reshelved. [456]

At the mountain's root she found a door of unbound pages, and behind it, a small dark chamber where the lost language stone sat on a reading desk like a heart removed for study. [490] When she touched it, it did not show her the past. [501] It showed her what she had buried: she had been a speaker of the old tongue, the last one, and home had burned with everyone who shared it, and she had come to the mountain and asked the store to file her grief where she could not find it. [550] Her talent for the language had been filed with it, since the two could not be separated. [567] The thunder she had carried for years finally broke, and she wept in the dark with the stone in her lap. [588]

Then she understood the seer's word: seed. [595] A buried thing is not always a corpse; sometimes it is waiting for permission to grow. [611] She carried the stone up through the changing corridors and planted it in the seer's stacks, where every reader in the mountain would pass it. [636] She began to teach. [640] At first to no one, speaking the old words aloud so the shelves could hear them; then to a child lost among encyclopedias; then to anyone who paused. [668] Each learner became a place the language lived, and a language, she discovered, is the shape of home: not walls, but the sound made by people who belong to each other. [699]

The store still substituted reality every night, rearranging its granite chapters. [710] But the words rode in bodies now, not on shelves, and bodies do not get misfiled. [726] Remembering still hurt; she had stopped asking it not to. [736] Some mornings she climbed to the Cartography Stacks and sat with the seer, whose glass had gone clearer since the wrist was opened, the storm inside her quieter, as if some of the thunder had been hers to give away. [776] "You found the shape," the seer said once. [784] "No," Iska said, running her thumb along the feather she still kept. [796] "I'm growing it." [799]
