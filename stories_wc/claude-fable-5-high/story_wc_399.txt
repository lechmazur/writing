Sela wore her mask on the left side only, lacquered bone over cheek and eye, because a line walker must keep half her face open to the desert. [28] The lines she walked were not drawn on the sand but under it, where the kinetic parapet beneath the dunes still turned its old wheels and sent slow tremors up through the grains. [61] No one alive had seen the parapet, only felt it, a buried rampart of pistons and counterweights that the first builders had set spinning to keep the desert from swallowing the pavilion. [93] Sela could not find the lines by looking. [101] Stare straight down and the tremor went shy, scattering like minnows; the trick was to walk with her gaze loose on the horizon and let the vibration arrive through peripheral awareness, a flicker at the masked edge of her sight, a pressure in the corner of her bare eye. [150] That was why the mask covered half of her: to dull one side so the other could listen. [168]

Everything changed after the prophet arrived. [174] He came out of the east with cracked lips and no sandals, and he spoke for nine days in the murmuring pavilion, the great tent of wind-bells and woven flues at the desert's heart, where every breeze that entered was folded into a low, continuous hum. [220] The pavilion did not just murmur; it remembered. [228] Its flues caught spoken words and circulated them, fainter each season, so that the wisdom of the dead stayed in the air like the smell of rain. [255] On the tenth day the prophet lay down in the shade and did not get up. [271] By the next moon his words had already begun to thin. [282]

The elders blamed the parapet. [287] Its buried wheels were slowing, they said, and as the tremor weakened, the dunes crept closer and the pavilion's hum grew ragged, shaking the prophet's sayings apart before their time. [317] They asked Sela to walk the lines out to the failing sections and do what line walkers had always done in emergencies: conjure clearings. [341] A conjured clearing was a circle of stillness coaxed from the machine, a place where a walker pressed her palms to the sand, matched her pulse to the parapet's rhythm, and persuaded the tremor to hold a patch of desert flat and quiet. [384] In such clearings the pavilion's flues could anchor an echo and keep it from fraying. [399] But a clearing only held if the walker consecrated it with something true. [412] That was the old law, and it was why the order had always chosen the strangely honest for this work, the ones who could not soften a fact even to comfort a child. [445] Sela was that kind. [449] She had once told a dying man exactly how long he had, and he had thanked her for it. [468]

So she went out at dusk, when the heat lifted and the tremor ran clean. [483] She walked with her eyes on the violet sky, reading the ground sideways, and where the flicker at her vision's edge went dim she knelt. [508] Palms down. [510] Breath slowed to the wheel-beat far below. [517] The sand around her settled into a perfect disk of calm, and into that whisperfield calm she spoke her consecration. [537] "I did not believe him," she said, because it was the truest thing she carried. [552] "I stood at the back of the pavilion and thought he was only a tired man talking, and I want his words to last anyway, because they were better than my believing." [584] The clearing took the truth and held. [591] She felt it through the edge of her sight, a steadiness blooming outward, and far off the pavilion's hum smoothed by one degree. [614]

Night after night she went farther, consecrating clearing after clearing, ringing the pavilion with circles of anchored quiet. [632] At each one she gave up another truth: that she envied the prophet's certainty; that she feared the parapet would die in her lifetime; that she sometimes hated the mask. [662] The clearings drank every confession and grew stronger. [670] Honesty, it turned out, was a kind of fuel. [679]

On the last night she returned to the pavilion and stood beneath the flues. [693] The hum had steadied into something almost like a voice, and within it she could hear the prophet again, faint but whole, his sayings circling the woven channels the way the tremor circled the buried wheels. [729] His echo would outlast her. [734] That was all she had wanted: not to be believed, not to be remembered, only to preserve the echo of wisdom for whoever came walking after. [760] She turned her bare eye to the dunes and her masked eye to the lamplight, and somewhere at the edge of her vision the lines trembled, alive, going on. [789]
