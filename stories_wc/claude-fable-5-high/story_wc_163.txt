The elevator shaft dropped Mireille Voss two hundred meters through black water, into the forgotten research facility that still clung to the drowned city of Tethra like a barnacle with windows. [31] She was a landscape astrologer, the last of a strange profession: she read the stars not in the sky but in their reflections on terrain, in how light pooled across a place and told you what the place wanted. [70] Tethra had been underwater for sixty years, and no light reached it at all. [84] That was the commission's cruelty and its dare. [92] The salvage consortium wanted to know whether the city could be raised, whether anyone would ever want to live in it again; they had hired her to find out if a drowned place could still hold a sky. [130] In her breast pocket she carried a burnt feather, all that remained of the rooftop observatory fire that had killed her mentor and her own certainty in the same hour. [160] Since then she had drifted between commissions the way smoke drifts between rooms, never staying, never asked to. [178] She did not say it aloud, but she had come down here to learn the rhythm of belonging, or to prove it did not exist. [203]

The facility was not empty. [208] A caretaker named Osei met her at the airlock, an old hydrologist who had refused every evacuation order for two decades. [229] "You have until sunrise," he said, tapping the tide chart taped to the bulkhead. [243] "The supply skiff surfaces at dawn and won't come back for a month." [256] One night, then, to read a sky that wasn't there. [266]

Osei was softly ingenious in the way of people who have repaired one home for twenty years with whatever the sea coughed up. [289] His patches were elegant: valve wheels carved from coral, a desalinator humming on salvaged turbine blades, gauges relabeled in his patient handwriting. [311] He led her through corridors stitched with shadow, where her lamp made every seam in the metal look like a horizon at dusk, and she felt the old shadowseam wonder rise in her, the sense that darkness was not absence but a fabric with light sewn into its joints. [360]

In the observation dome, Tethra spread below them: streets, plazas, a cathedral wearing anemones like candle wax. [377] "There's nothing to read," she said. "No stars. No reflections. No sky." [389] Osei only smiled and killed the lamps. [396] "Wait," he said. "It's the first warm current of the season. Watch the boulevard." [410]

They waited in the dark so long that Mireille's breath fell into step with his, two tides in one room. [430] Then, in a shared exhale, they spotted them: the first fireflies of summer, as Osei called them, a migration of bioluminescent plankton sweeping into the drowned streets, kindling the city window by window, the way lamplighters once had. [468] It captivated her utterly. [472] Not because it was beautiful, though it was, but because it had pattern: the lights drifted along the old avenues, eddied in the plazas, gathered at the cathedral the way crowds once had. [505] The city had taught the water its shape, and the water remembered. [517] It was a sky after all, only inverted, written in plankton instead of stars. [531]

She worked until her hands ached, charting the luminous respite the way she had once charted constellations: the boulevard as ecliptic, the cathedral as polestar, the eddies as wandering planets. [561] Osei brought her tea and named each current like an old friend, because to him they were. [578] "The lights come every summer," he said. "That's why I stayed. Not for the city. For the returning." [596]

Mireille took out the burnt feather and turned it in the blue-green glow. [609] Her mentor had told her, the night before the fire, that belonging was not a place but a recurrence, a rhythm you kept until the rhythm kept you. [637] She had carried the feather like an ending. [645] Here, in a facility the world had forgotten, watching light come back to a city the world had drowned, it felt instead like a seed. [670] She set it on the chart table, quill end down, and used it to weight her finished map. [688]

When the tide chart said sunrise, the dome dimmed as the plankton thinned, drifting onward with the current. [706] The skiff would be waiting. [711] Her report would say: do not raise the city; it is not dead, only keeping different hours. [728] At the airlock, Osei shook her hand. [735] "Next summer," he said, not quite a question. [743] "Next summer," she answered, and for the first time in years the words were not a courtesy but a coordinate, a fixed point she could navigate by. [770] Rising through the dark water toward morning, she finally understood the rhythm: belonging was simply a light you agreed to return to. [792]
