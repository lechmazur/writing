The sanctuary had no sound, not even the kind a body makes when it breathes. [15] Mara arrived there the way everyone did, mid-stride, while reality shifted beneath her like a floor being pulled into a different room. [37] One moment she was raising a pistol at the man who had burned her village; the next, she stood among white pillars under a sky the color of held breath. [67] The pistol was gone. [71] Her rage was not. [75] Brother Ivo was already there, because Brother Ivo was always already there. [87] He reappeared in the sanctuary whenever someone needed him, which he privately considered both a gift and an excellent tactical advantage. [108] He had never struck a person in his long life, but he schemed the way generals did, mapping a soul's defenses, probing for the gate left unlocked. [135] Mara's mouth moved in a shout that produced nothing. [144] Ivo signed calmly with his hands: sound did not survive here, only what the body remembered. [160] She did not know sign language, and yet she understood him, because the sanctuary translated meaning the way water translates light. [181] "Send me back," she demanded with her hands, which somehow knew the shapes. [194] "I was one trigger-pull from justice." [200] "You were one trigger-pull from carrying him forever," Ivo replied, and produced a small brass music box from his robe. [220] He had planned this, of course. [226] He had been planning it since the moment her grief first rang through the shifting places like a struck bell. [246] He opened the lid. [250] Inside, the cylinder turned, but one pin was broken, snapped off at the root, and the comb's teeth passed over the gap finding nothing to play. [276] In a soundless place, a broken music box and a whole one are identical, except to the hands that hold them. [297] "Hold it," he urged. [301] She refused. [303] So he waited, because waiting was his sharpest weapon, and the sanctuary obligingly stretched the hour into something shapeless while reality outside rearranged its furniture, wars becoming gardens becoming wars. [333] Eventually, boredom defeated fury, as Ivo had calculated it would. [343] She took the box. [347] The moment her fingers closed around the brass, her body began to play what her ears could not hear. [366] Cellular memory, Ivo called it: the way a melody lives in the muscles of a girl who was rocked to it, in the skin of arms that once held her, in the marrow that grew while it played. [404] Her mother's lullaby rose through her hands like heat. [413] But it stumbled where the broken pin should have been, a note missing, a hole in the song the exact shape of her loss. [437] "You feel the gap," Ivo said. [443] "Your resentment lives there, filling the missing note so the song never has to end on absence." [460] "And you think I should simply let it go," she signed bitterly. [472] "No," he said, and here was the heart of his scheme, the trap built entirely of honesty. [489] "I think you should teach it to let go of you." [500] "Resentment is loyal, like a dog guarding a grave, and it must be convinced its watch is over." [518] He guided her fingers to the cylinder and showed her how to hum the missing note, not aloud, since nothing here was aloud, but through her bones, the way her mother had hummed it against her infant skull. [556] Her cells remembered. [559] The note arrived from inside her, supplied by her own living body, and the song completed itself for the first time since the fire. [583] This was what the wanderers of the shifting places called grace: not a gift given once, but a thing that moves, that leaves one vessel and finds another, that crosses a gap when something living offers itself as the bridge. [623] Mara wept without sound, which is the oldest kind of weeping. [634] She felt the resentment loosen its grip, not banished, but relieved of duty, a soldier finally told the war was won by other means. [658] "You planned all of this," she accused. [665] "I plan everything," Ivo admitted cheerfully. [671] "It is how a man who refuses to fight still wins." [682] Reality finished its shift. [686] Mara stood again in the burned village, facing the man she had come to kill, her hand empty where the pistol had been. [709] In her other hand sat the music box, which should not have crossed over, but some things reappear when they are needed. [731] She opened the lid. [735] The cylinder turned, the broken pin passed in silence, and she hummed the missing note aloud, here where sound was possible, her voice steady over the gap. [762] The man who had burned her village stood waiting for a bullet and received a lullaby instead. [779] Somewhere between realities, an old pacifist smiled at a scheme well played. [791]
