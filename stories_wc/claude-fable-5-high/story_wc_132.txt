Mara Quist had owned the Greenhollow Nursery for nineteen years, and in all that time she had never let a single vine choose its own direction. [26] Every tendril was tied, every stem staked, every bloom scheduled. [36] She told customers she ran the place to study plant behaviors, but the truth was simpler and harder: she studied them so she could correct them. [62] Storm season changed the argument. [67] The first big gale of October tore through Greenhollow at two in the morning, and by dawn the great trellis behind the greenhouse lay folded over like a broken hand. [97] Mara stood in the wet grass and stared at it. [107] The trellis had been her mother's, cedar lattice gone green with moss, and after rain it always smelled like the beginning of the world. [131] It smelled that way now, even ruined. [138] Tucked in her field notebook was a linden leaf, pressed flat twenty years ago, the last thing her mother ever handed her. [160] "A leaf knows how to fall," her mother had said, which was the kind of circuitously sincere remark she specialized in, arriving at honesty only by the longest possible road. [190] Mara had spent two decades deciding the words meant nothing. [200] She decided to reconstruct the trellis exactly as it had been. [211] She measured the old joints, photographed the moss patterns, drew the lattice to the quarter inch. [227] For three days she rebuilt it, and on the fourth night another storm took it down again. [244] She rebuilt it a second time, bracing it with steel. [254] The third storm of the season did not even slow at the reinforcements. [267] On the morning after, Mara walked out to the wreckage and noticed something she had been too furious to see before. [288] The honeysuckle she had trained along the trellis for years had survived every collapse. [302] It had not survived by holding rigid. [309] Where her wires had snapped, the vine's own loose spirals had flexed, bent, sprung back. [324] Everything she had bound tightly was dead. [331] Everything she had missed, neglected, failed to control, was green. [341] She sat down on the soaked grass with her notebook and, for the first time in years, observed without intervening. [361] It felt like undressing in winter. [367] Every instinct she owned told her to reach for the twine. [378] She did not want to become a different kind of gardener; she had been this one too long. [396] But the data in front of her would not negotiate. [406] So the third reconstruction was a surrender disguised as an experiment. [417] She rebuilt only the trellis posts, anchoring them deep, and left the lattice loose, slats lashed with cord that could give an inch in any direction. [443] Then she carried the honeysuckle to it and did the hardest thing of her professional life: nothing. [460] No ties. [462] No training wires. [465] She let the vine find the wood itself. [473] For two weeks she recorded its progress in her notebook, the linden leaf marking her page like a thumb on a pulse. [495] The vine climbed slowly, wrongly, choosing routes no manual would approve. [506] It also climbed alive. [510] When the fourth storm came, the worst of the season, Mara stood at her window all night, hands aching to interfere. [531] At dawn she walked out through ground fog and dripping cedars. [542] The trellis stood. [545] It leaned a few degrees east, its loose joints having swayed where rigid ones would have sheared, and the moss on its old salvaged slats glittered after the rain like something newly minted. [578] The honeysuckle had wrapped the whole eastern face in its crooked, improvised spirals, and not one runner had torn free. [598] Mara pressed her palm against the wet moss and laughed, a short startled sound. [612] She understood her mother at last, the way you understand a riddle years after the teller is gone. [630] A leaf knows how to fall meant a thing survives by yielding. [642] It had taken three wrecked trellises and twenty years to walk that long road to the plain truth. [660] She did not change all at once; metamorphosis taken willingly is still metamorphosis taken slowly. [675] All through the rest of storm season she caught herself reaching for twine and put it down again. [693] Her notebooks filled with what the plants actually did instead of what she made them do, and the studies grew strange and rich. [716] Customers noticed the nursery loosening, going greener at the edges, and Mara loosening with it. [731] On the last day of October she unpinned the linden leaf from her notebook, carried it to the mossy trellis, and set it in a fork of the honeysuckle. [760] The wind would take it eventually. [766] She found she could let it. [772]
