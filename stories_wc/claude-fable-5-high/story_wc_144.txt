The repair shop occupied the last bay of the mist-coated rail depot, where old freight tracks ended and the harbor began. [21] Mara Voss had kept it for thirty years, and for the last nine she had kept it blind. [39] Blindness had not closed the shop; it had only changed the language of the work. [54] She oversaw every bench by ear now, reading the health of a mechanism in its hum the way she had once read it in its shine. [80] On the morning the Caldera was to sail, a courier ran the length of the depot and set a velvet box on her counter. [104] Inside lay the ship's pilot bird: an artificial hummingbird drone, no heavier than a spoon, its left wing membrane torn and its guidance core stuttering. [129] The Caldera was new, untested, and the channel out of this harbor was a maze of sandbars that shifted with every tide. [151] The hummingbird was meant to fly ahead of the bow, taste the water's surface with light, and decode that feedback into direction for the helm. [176] Without it, the maiden voyage would end on a bar before it began. [189] "You have until the anchor lifts," the courier said, breathless. [199] "Two hours." [201] Mara did not hurry, because hurry was a kind of noise. [212] Her calm was the magnetic sort; it pulled the panic out of the room and rearranged it into work. [231] She called her apprentice, Tobin, to the bench and lifted the bird onto her palm. [246] Her fingers found the tear at once, a ragged mouth in the wing's silk-thin membrane. [261] "The wing I can patch," she said. [268] "Tell me what the core is doing." [275] Tobin woke the bird and it shivered, throwing its lidar in useless circles. [288] "It reads everything as open water," he said. [296] "It can't tell shallow from deep anymore." [303] Mara nodded slowly. [306] The bird judged depth by how light bent through moving water; somewhere in its memory, the rules of refraction had been scrambled. [328] You could not rewrite those rules at a desk; the bird had to relearn them from water itself. [346] "The depot fountain," she said. [351] "The old one by platform six, with the stepped basins." [361] First, the patch. [364] She worked by touch, laying a film of cured silk over the tear, sealing its edges with a heated stylus she guided by the smell of the resin and the changing pitch of the tool. [399] When the wing beat against her thumb, even and dry, she allowed herself one small smile. [415] Then they carried the bird out into the mist, down the platform where the fountain's basins spilled into one another in shallow, ordered steps. [439] Each basin held a different depth, and each bent the gray morning light differently. [453] It was a teaching pool, if you knew how to use it. [465] "Hover it over the first basin," Mara said. [473] "Read me the numbers it returns." [479] She could not see the refraction, but she did not need to; she knew the fountain's depths by heart, two fingers, one hand, one forearm, and she knew what a true reading should sound like when Tobin spoke it. [518] Basin by basin, she compared his numbers against her memory and called corrections, and Tobin keyed each one into the core. [539] The bird was wrong, then less wrong, then nearly right. [549] From the harbor came the long groan of the Caldera's capstan beginning to turn. [563] "The anchor," Tobin whispered. [567] "One more basin," Mara said, and her voice did not rise at all. [580] The deepest basin caught the light at its hardest angle, and the bird hesitated above it, clicking, deciding. [598] Then it returned a number, and Tobin laughed, because it was the right one. [612] "Run," Mara said. [615] He ran, the bird darting ahead of him through the mist like a thrown spark, down the tracks toward the water. [636] Mara walked behind, unhurried, listening. [641] She heard the anchor break the surface as it lifted, heard the crowd at the quay, heard the ship's horn answer the depot's old bell. [666] And under all of it, faint as a pulse, she heard the hummingbird's wings settle into their working rhythm, flying out ahead of the bow to read the bending light and turn it into a course. [702] That had always been the heart of her trade, she thought, blind or sighted: taking the world's confused signals and patiently decoding them into direction. [727] The Caldera found the channel and held it. [735] Mara turned back toward her shop through the thinning mist, already listening for the next broken thing that needed a steady hand. [757]
