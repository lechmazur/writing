The quarry office trailer sat at the lip of the pit like a tooth waiting to be pulled. [18] Marty Voss climbed the cinder-block step with his appraiser's case, three weeks after the Calloway auction had made him famous for being wrong. [41] He had certified a fake Martin guitar as 1937 on a livestream, and the internet had clipped his face at the exact moment the truth arrived. [67] Facts were what he had left, so he carried them like sandbags. [79] The trailer belonged to Denny Crews, dead six weeks, foreman of the gravel operation and, forty years ago, the soundman for a band called Echo Valley. [105] Marty had been their roadie, eighteen and strong, hauling cabinets up fire-escape stairs while Denny rode the faders like a man calming horses. [128] Denny's sister wanted an estimate on whatever was inside before the quarry company reclaimed the trailer. [144] Marty stepped in and began reciting. [150] Steel desk, Cole brand, circa 1968, forty dollars. [158] Bakelite rotary phone, modified, sixty if the wiring held. [167] Naming things kept them at arm's length; a dated object could not ask him how he was holding up. [186] Then he opened the bottom drawer of the filing cabinet and stopped reciting. [199] Inside, folded in wax paper like something baked, lay a scrap of speaker grille cloth, black and silver basketweave, eight inches square. [221] He knew it before he touched it. [228] It was cut from the Marshall cabinet that had blown out during Echo Valley's last show, the night the label man left at intermission and the band quietly ended. [257] Marty had wrested that cabinet off the stage himself, alone, after everyone else had gone to drink. [274] He remembered the room after the applause, that strange hollow minute when the audience becomes a smell of spilled beer and the lights come up on cables. [301] Somebody has to be the one who stays for that part. [312] That had been his whole job, and he had loved it without ever saying so. [327] He turned the cloth over and found Denny's handwriting on a masking-tape label: For Marty, who carried us. [345] His professional voice tried to save him. [352] Grille cloth, likely Marshall checkerboard weave, mid-seventies, negligible resale value, perhaps ten dollars to a restorer. [368] The facts came out steady and did not help at all. [379] He sat down in Denny's chair and looked out the dusty window at the pit, terraced and gray, a stadium carved by subtraction. [402] When the conveyor shut down for lunch, the silence rolled across the quarry and came back to him as a faint ring, the way an empty hall rings after a final chord. [434] Denny had ended up here, he realized, for the same reason Marty had ended up among antiques. [451] They were both men who tended what was left behind. [461] Since the auction disaster, Marty had been confidently uncertain about everything, delivering each opinion in a firm voice while privately believing none of it, because his ear for the genuine had betrayed him once on camera and might again. [500] But the cloth in his hands was not an opinion. [510] He had stood in front of that cabinet when it blew; he had felt the paper cone tear like a held breath finally let go. [535] Denny had kept the scrap for forty years and labeled it with Marty's name, which meant that somewhere in all those gravel-dust mornings, Denny had been waiting for him to come and claim it. [569] Marty had spent three weeks asking what he was for, now that his judgment was a punchline. [586] The drawer had answered him in eight square inches. [595] Value was never the number; value was the keeping. [604] An appraiser's real work was not pricing the past but vouching for it, saying to the living: this mattered, someone carried this, it is true. [629] He could be wrong about a guitar and still be the man who stayed after the applause to do that work. [650] He wrote the inventory for Denny's sister carefully, each item dated and described, and at the bottom he listed the grille cloth. [672] Beside it, instead of a price, he wrote: bequeathed, accepted, priceless to one party. [686] Then, for the first time since the livestream, he signed his name without his hand hesitating. [702] Outside, the conveyor started up again, and the pit swallowed the sound and gave a little of it back, softened, the way valleys do. [726] Marty stood on the cinder block step with the cloth folded in his breast pocket, over the spot where a stage pass used to hang. [751] Somebody has to stay for the quiet part, he thought, and load out what is worth saving. [768] He was, he decided, exactly that somebody, and for once he was certain. [781]
