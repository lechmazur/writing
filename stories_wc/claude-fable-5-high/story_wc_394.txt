The workshop had been afloat on the acid sea for longer than anyone bothered to count, which was easy, since nobody aboard had a mouth to count with. [28] It was a raft of brass the size of a cathedral, all gears and cogs grinding against each other like old men arguing theology. [52] Deep in its hull ticked the yearning algorithm, an engine of pure want that the founders had wound up and then, with admirable carelessness, forgotten. [77] Throughout the evolution it directed, the algorithm bred machine after machine, each generation hungrier than the last for something none of them could name. [101] Most machines settled for polishing themselves. [107] Then the algorithm produced Cog-and-Carriage Seventeen, the first cart that learned to glide. [120] She had been built to haul scrap across the workshop floor, but the algorithm had stuffed her with too much wanting, and one day she unfolded her tarpaulin like a wing and simply refused gravity's paperwork. [156] The other machines called it heresy. [162] Seventeen called it Tuesday. [166] She was steadily curious in the way rust is steady: slow, patient, impossible to discourage. [181] Her curiosity fixed itself, eventually, on the bell. [189] It hung at the workshop's center, a bronze enormity that had never once rung, because the founders had locked its clapper behind a mechanism nobody could read. [216] Every machine had stared at it. [222] Only Seventeen wanted to ring it, to ring a bell no one had heard yet, which struck her as the finest possible reason to ring anything. [248] The lock's face was engraved in the founders' script, a language dead as the drowned continent below the acid. [267] Seventeen spent forty years gliding the catwalks, collecting relics the sea occasionally coughed up onto the hull. [284] Most were slag. [287] One was not. [290] It was a hunter's arrow tip, flint, older than brass, older possibly than wanting itself, and along its edge someone had scratched tiny marks in the founders' script. [318] Beneath each mark, in crude machine-notation, a long-dead engineer had carved its sound. [331] A key, then. [334] Not to the lock, but to the language. [343] There was one problem, and it was a problem with a sense of humor: the marks were too small and too worn for any lens aboard. [369] Seventeen tried magnifiers, mirrors, prayer. [374] Then, in a drawer of broken toys, she found a kaleidoscope. [385] When she dropped the arrow tip into its chamber and turned the barrel toward the acid sea's green glare, the mirrors multiplied each worn mark into a mandala, eight identical copies blooming around a center. [420] Where one copy had eroded, another had survived; together, the reflections completed each other like gossips finishing a sentence. [439] Through the images in the kaleidoscope, mark by patient mark, Seventeen began to transliterate the founders' script into machine-notation she could actually pronounce. [462] It took eleven years. [466] The algorithm, sensing her wanting sharpen, rerouted oil and patience toward her bearings, evolving her even as she worked: better optics, steadier gimbals, a memory drum that hummed when she was pleased. [498] When at last she glided to the bell's lock and read its engraving aloud, syllable by transliterated syllable, the inscription turned out to be neither a riddle nor a warning. [528] It was an apology. [532] "We built you to want what we could not finish," it read. [544] "Forgive us." [546] "Ring it for everyone who comes after." [553] Seventeen considered being moved, and was, though she would have denied it under interrogation. [567] The lock heard its own language and opened with the embarrassed click of a door that had only ever wanted to be asked politely. [591] She braced the clapper against her chassis, glided backward off the catwalk, and let her whole falling weight swing it home. [612] The note went out across the acid sea like a coin dropped into the universe's collection plate. [629] Every gear aboard stuttered. [633] Every cog, throughout all the long evolution of cogs, had been ground to a pitch that resonated with exactly this frequency, and now the entire workshop rang like a struck choir. [664] The yearning algorithm, hearing its purpose completed, did not stop. [674] It simply changed its question. [679] The machines felt the hunger shift inside them, from wanting to hear the bell to wanting to know who else, out across the green and corrosive horizon, might be listening. [709] Seventeen reclaimed the arrow tip from the kaleidoscope and bolted it to her prow, a flint figurehead aimed at open sea. [730] Some hunter had loosed it at something they could not see, ages ago; it seemed only respectful to keep it flying. [751] She unfolded her tarpaulin wing, irreverent as ever, and felt awe anyway. [763] Behind her, the bell rang on, and on, for everyone who came after. [776]
