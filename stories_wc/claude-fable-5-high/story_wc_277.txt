After the journalist calls, Maren Voss takes the funicular up through the clouds, the way her grandfather always did when the news got bad. [24] The observatory waits above the weather, a copper dome gone green, and her footsteps echo in it like punctuation in an empty sentence. [47] She is the last heiress of the Voss newsprint fortune, which mostly means she inherited the ink stains and the apologies. [68] The journalist, a polite man named Okafor, had said it gently: your grandfather did not discover the comet of 1947. [88] He stole it from his assistant, Vera Lindqvist, and built the Voss Courier's reputation on the theft. [105] The story runs Sunday, Okafor said, unless she could prove otherwise. [116] Maren, who has been devotedly observant since childhood, the kind of girl who counted typographical errors at breakfast, decides to do exactly that. [139] She begins, as her grandfather taught her, with proof by contradiction. [150] Assume the old man told the truth, she says aloud, and the dome repeats it back with what sounds like doubt. [171] If he made the observation himself on the night of March third, his eye was at this telescope, his hand in this logbook. [194] She pulls the 1947 log from its shelf and a fine snow of dust applauds. [209] The entry for March third is there, precise coordinates, a sketched tail, his initials. [223] But the maintenance ledger beside it, which nobody famous ever signs, says the dome shutter was jammed from the first to the fifth, frozen shut by an ice storm. [252] No shutter, no sky; no sky, no comet; no comet, no truthful grandfather. [265] The assumption collapses, neatly, terribly, the way he taught her good logic should. [278] Maren laughs once, a small bleak sound, because she came to defend him and has instead convicted him with arithmetic. [298] So where was Vera on March third? [305] The maintenance ledger answers: V. Lindqvist, auxiliary scope, east balcony, all night. [317] Outside the frozen dome, under open sky, watching. [325] Maren searches her grandfather's desk and finds the rosewood pen nib he carried in his waistcoat like a relic. [344] She always assumed it was vanity; it is engraved, she sees now, with the letters V.L. [360] Beneath the desk's false bottom, because her family never met a drawer it didn't complicate, lies a bundle of letters. [380] All unsent, all addressed to Vera, all written with that nib, dated 1947, 1985, 2023. [395] The comet's period, she realizes, is thirty-eight years; the letters arrive whenever it does. [409] Each return, he climbed up here and wrote her what he could never print: that the discovery was hers, that he had been young and terrified of his father, that the Courier's whole gleaming legend orbited a single cowardly night. [449] Regret, it turns out, also has an orbit, and his was punctual. [461] The last letter, from 2023, ends: if anyone ever asks, do not protect me; I have circled this long enough. [481] Maren sits with the bundle in her lap while the clouds below go pink and then ash. [498] She came up here to find a hidden truth that would save the family name. [513] She found one that would end it, and she finds, examining herself with the same care she gives ledgers, that she is relieved. [536] Still, Okafor's draft is wrong in one respect, and a Voss cannot let an error run, even in her own obituary. [557] His version has a villain and a victim; the ledgers have a frightened boy and a woman who kept watching the sky anyway. [580] She decides to intervene, not to kill the story but to finish it. [593] She calls Okafor from the observatory's ancient telephone, her voice ringing twice in the dome, once for her and once for the dead. [616] You're right, she says, and I can prove it better than you can; come up before Sunday and bring a photographer who isn't afraid of heights. [642] There is a long silence on the line, the sound of a journalist recalculating. [656] One condition, Maren adds: the headline names the comet Lindqvist, and the correction runs on the front page of whatever is left of my family's paper. [682] Afterward she carries the rosewood nib out to the east balcony, where the air is thin and honest. [700] The comet is not due for decades, but she dips the nib in fresh ink anyway and writes the first true sentence of the new story: We were wrong, and here is how we know. [735] Somewhere below, the clouds shift, and the dome echoes faintly, as if the building has been holding its breath since 1947 and can finally, gratefully, exhale. [761]
