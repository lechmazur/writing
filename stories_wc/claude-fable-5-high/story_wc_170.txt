The mirrors of Cantorel showed only what was, and the city had learned to stop wanting anything else. [18] They hung in every hallway, honest and pitiless, reflecting tired faces back at tired faces until even children forgot how to imagine themselves otherwise. [42] Veshma was the exception, though almost no one had seen her. [53] People knew the mirage painter only by her work: a doorway of water shimmering on dry stone, an orchard hovering above the fish market, gone by noon. [80] Mirages were easy; air believes anything. [86] Mirrors were another matter. [90] A mirror reads the heartbeat of whoever stands before it, she had learned, and it paints only what that heartbeat holds as certain. [113] Her own heart, after sixty years of vanishing acts, had grown too careful to hold anything uncertain at all. [132] So she had borrowed one. [137] Pira, nine years old and bedridden with the slow fever, had pressed her small hand to Veshma's wrist one evening and offered a trade. [161] "Carry mine for a season," the girl had said, "because it still believes in things, and bring it back when the mirrors believe them too." [186] By some old grace of the fever-houses, where the dying may lend what they cannot spend, the exchange held. [205] Now Pira's heartbeat moved in Veshma's chest, quick and unguarded, while her own steady pulse kept watch over the sleeping girl. [226] It made her dizzy with hope, which was the point. [236] Spring rain had passed over the city all afternoon, and by dusk the great terrace above the bell tower stood washed and empty, its stones echoing every footstep twice. [265] The parapet there was cut from old prism glass, and after rain it did something no mirror in Cantorel could: it took the plain gray light and broke it into seven honest colors. [298] That was Veshma's proof, the argument she meant to make: light itself contains more than one possibility, and a surface need not lie to show it. [324] She had chosen her hour with care. [331] Mirrors listen best in the hush before the evening bells, those few suspended minutes when the whole city holds still out of habit, waiting for the bronze to speak. [360] Below the terrace, on doorsteps and in courtyards, mothers were singing their children toward sleep. [375] Veshma had spent the winter on those songs. [383] She could not gather a crowd, elusive as she was, so she had slipped new verses into the old lullabies instead, teaching them to street singers who never knew what they carried. [415] Each verse was a cipher built on rhythm: the pulse of a child's heart, set in rhyme, rising from a hundred windows at once. [439] "Sleep, little sparrow, the door is not a wall, the river you remember may not be there at all." [458] Nonsense to adults; instructions, of a kind, to mirrors. [467] The lullabies rose now through the wet air, dozens of small voices braided into one dusky harmony, and Veshma felt the borrowed heart in her chest answer them, beat for beat. [498] She set her hand mirror on the prism parapet, facing the broken colors. [511] Perceptively calm, she noticed everything and feared none of it: the cooling stone, the last raindrops ticking from the eaves, the way the glass watched her like a skeptical judge. [541] She did not paint. [545] She simply stood where the parapet's spectrum fell across the mirror's face, let Pira's quick heartbeat sound against the silver, and let the children's coded singing do the rest. [574] The mirror resisted. [577] It showed the terrace, the parapet, an aging woman with rain in her hair, all of it stubbornly, precisely true. [597] Then a lullaby's last verse drifted up, slightly off-key, sung by some child fighting sleep, and the borrowed heart leapt. [617] Veshma graced the glass with one slow fingertip, the way you bless a sleeping child's forehead, asking nothing, only welcoming. [637] The reflection wavered. [640] In the mirror, the woman's hair was rain-dark but the sky behind her was full of orchards, doorways, rivers that might or might not exist, all seven colors arguing pleasantly with one another. [673] Not lies. [675] Possibilities. [676] Across the city, in that same suspended hush, every mirror that had heard the lullabies shivered and softened, and somewhere a tired man saw himself, briefly, as a man who could still leave. [709] The bells rang. [712] Veshma was already gone, of course, descending the tower stairs while the bronze still trembled. [727] In a fever-house across the river, Pira woke to her own heartbeat returning, season finished, debt paid. [744] It came back changed, the girl would say later, carrying an old painter's patience inside its rhythm. [761] And in the mirror beside her bed, where there had only ever been a sick child, she saw a girl who might one day climb a terrace in spring and teach the light to argue. [796]
