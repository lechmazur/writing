Maren had set bones for thirty years, and she had never once believed in fairies. [15] She believed in calcium, in splints, in the slow honest knitting of the body. [29] So when the river gave up the substitute body, a wax-and-willow infant dressed in her daughter's christening gown, Maren forgave the village its gasping and went home to make tea. [59] Forgiveness came easily to her; certainty was the harder craft. [69] The village remembered the old story now: how thirteen years ago a midwife swore the cradle had been disturbed, how the baby's cry changed pitch overnight. [95] They looked at Ivy differently after the finding, and Ivy, shrewdly innocent, looked right back. [110] She was the kind of child who asked questions like a lawyer and laughed like a bell. [127]

Three days after the finding, Ivy fell from the top of the lord's hedge maze and broke her forearm clean across. [148] Maren set the bone the way her mother had taught her, traction and counter-pressure, splint and linen, hands steady as a level floor. [171] But under her palms the bone moved wrong, light as a bird's, warm as a coal, and it knit before she finished tying the bandage. [196] A cleft opened in Maren's certainty then, thin as a hairline fracture, and she felt thirty years of calcium drain through it. [218]

That night she took down the tin box where she kept Ivy's baby tooth, shed at age six, which she had never shown anyone. [242] It was pearled like the inside of an oyster, and it had never stopped being faintly warm. [259] She had told herself for seven years that teeth were only teeth. [271] Now she sat with it in her palm and yearned, fiercely and without shame, to know what her daughter was, and to discover that the answer did not matter. [300]

Ivy found her like that, arm in its sling, eyes too old for thirteen. [314] "Come with me," Ivy said. [319] "I want to show you where I put your mistakes." [329]

She led Maren by lantern light into the lord's hedge maze, taking turns no map recorded, until the hedges opened on a garden no one was meant to find. [358] It was a round green room under the open sky, and the ground glittered. [372] Pressed into the dark soil, in deliberate patterns, were small white things: the splint pegs from the leg Maren had set crooked in her first year, the cracked plaster from old Tam's wrist, every failure she had ever buried in the midden behind the house. [417]

"You threw them away like they were shameful," Ivy said. [427] "But look, I arranged them." [432] "That one is the Plow." [437] "That one is the Swan." [442]

Maren saw it then: her daughter had spent years digging up her mother's errors and laying them out as constellations, so that the things Maren regretted made the same shapes as the stars. [475] "Why?" Maren asked. [478] "Because you taught me bones heal stronger where they break," Ivy said, "and I wanted somewhere to put the parts of us that broke." [502]

"Did you make this garden?" Maren asked. [509] "Or did you find it?" [514] Ivy smiled the smile that the village had begun to fear, the one that knew more than a child should and meant less harm than anyone guessed. [541] "Does it matter?" she said. [546]

And Maren found, standing in the cleft of her own certainty, that it did not. [561] Whatever had been left in that cradle thirteen years ago, she had set its bones when it fell, fed it, taught it the names of the wrist's eight stones. [590] The wax thing in the river was somebody's daughter too, perhaps, or nobody's; the village could bury it and grieve or wonder as they pleased. [615]

Maren knelt, her knees remembering every floor she had ever knelt on beside the hurt and frightened, and took the baby tooth from her pocket. [640] "This belongs to your sky," she said. [647] She pressed it into the soil at the center of the garden, where no constellation yet had a heart. [666] It glowed there, faintly, the way old mistakes do when you finally stop hiding them. [681]

Afterward, Maren changed how she practiced. [687] She still set bones with traction and linen, the old grammar of her mother's hands, but she began keeping what she once threw away. [711] Every failed splint, every misjudged fracture, she carried through the maze and gave to Ivy, who set each one in the soil like a surgeon setting a star. [739] It was a new method built from the oldest materials: error, patience, and a mother's stubborn love. [756] She never learned what Ivy was, and she never needed to. [768] Some bones heal straight; some heal strange; all of them, given a sky, can shine. [783]
