Wren had chronicled the Marsh River for eleven years, filling notebooks with flood marks, ferry ledgers, and the testimony of anyone who had ever loved or feared its water. [29] People called her a truth-seeker, though she knew the title was a kindness; she sought one truth only, and it had drowned with her brother Caleb at Hollis Bend. [58] Three weeks after stopping the medication, the river got loud again. [69] The pills had made grief survivable by making it distant, a country viewed through fog, and she had decided, with her doctor's wary blessing, that she could not chronicle anything from inside a fog. [103] What returned first was not sorrow but a rumor. [112] A kayaker she interviewed mentioned writing on the walls of the old Ferring railway station, the disused one upstream where ivy had swallowed the platform whole. [138] "Strange stuff in the gents'," he said. [145] "Wisdom, almost. [147] And numbers." [149] Wren went the next morning because she and Caleb had sheltered there as children, waiting out storms while he wheezed melodies from his secondhand accordion, its bellows patched with silver tape. [180] The station stood half-eaten by green, the ticket window blinded with leaves. [192] In the men's washroom, above the cracked basin, someone had written in careful pencil: THE RIVER DOESN'T KEEP WHAT YOU GIVE IT, ONLY WHAT YOU HIDE -- 51.42, -1.07. [220] Map references. [222] Below it, more lines, each ending in coordinates: BLAME IS A KNOT TIED BY SOMEONE ELSE'S HANDS; GUILT FLOWS, IT DOES NOT POOL; FORGIVE THE SWIMMER, NOT THE FLOOD. [251] The handwriting tilted left, the way Caleb's had. [259] Her chest tightened; he had been dead two years, but he had wandered this valley for months before Hollis Bend, busking and sleeping rough, refusing her calls after their last argument. [290] She did what she always did with the river: she made a method of her hunger. [306] She photographed every line, transcribed each coordinate into her survey grid, and plotted them against her hydrology maps, daringly methodical, treating her brother's possible ghost like a tributary to be charted. [337] The references traced the river downstream, point by point, toward Hollis Bend. [349] She visited each one. [353] At the first, she found the willow snag where he had taught her to skip stones. [369] At the second, the weir whose roar he claimed was the river clearing its throat. [384] She understood, slowly, what the graffiti was doing: it never named her, never named him, but every line seemed to allude to the night she told him to stop calling until he got sober. [418] Three weeks later he went into the water at the bend, alone. [430] She had spent two medicated years believing she had pushed him there. [442] The final coordinate did not point downstream. [449] It pointed back to the station itself, to the washroom, to a loose tile she had walked past a dozen times. [470] Behind it, wrapped in oilcloth, sat the accordion. [478] The bellows were patched with new tape over the old, layer upon layer, mended the way a person mends a thing they intend to keep. [503] Tucked inside was a page in his leftward tilt. [512] "Wren, if you ever chase the numbers, know this. [521] I wrote on walls because I could not yet write to you. [533] I was getting well here, by the water, slower than either of us wanted. [547] If the river takes me before I finish, it took a man who had already stopped blaming his sister. [566] Practice forgiving her too." [570] Wren sat on the cold tile until the light through the ivy turned amber. [584] She did not weep prettily; she wept the way weirs roar, the river clearing its throat. [600] Then she lifted the accordion, pressed its taped bellows, and let one wheezing chord fill the station. [617] It was an ugly sound and an honest one, and honesty was the only solace she had ever trusted. [636] In her notebook she began a new chapter of the river's chronicle, the first to include herself. [653] She recorded the coordinates, the graffiti, the tile, the letter, each fact set down plainly, because the truth she had finally found deserved a methodical witness. [679] The last line she wrote was not about the river at all. [691] Forgive the swimmer, it said, and underneath, in her own steady hand: working on it. [706]
