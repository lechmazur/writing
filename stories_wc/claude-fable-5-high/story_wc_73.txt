Mira had been climbing for longer than counting allowed, and still she smiled at every step. [16] The staircase coiled upward through gray light, infinite by construction, and it had no doors, no windows, no end she could disprove. [38] She called her situation boundless confinement, which sounded like a paradox, which was exactly the point. [54] Paradoxes, she believed, could be ruptured. [60] In her pocket she carried two dice, one pristine, one chipped at the corner of its six. [77] Tomas had thrown that die against a cafe table the night he asked her to leave with him, the night she chose her equations instead. [102] She had calculated the odds of happiness and declined to roll. [113] He rolled for both of them, the die struck the table edge and chipped, and the number came up in his favor, and he left anyway, alone. [140] The next morning she finished her proof of a perpetual motion engine, a staircase of forces that fed on its own descent, and the equations folded around her like a closing hand. [172] She woke inside her own theorem. [178] The staircase was the engine: every step she climbed lowered her, every descent lifted her, energy from nowhere, motion without end. [199] A grim optimist, she took inventory: chalk in one pocket, dice in the other, and a flaw somewhere in the architecture, because perpetual motion was impossible, and impossible things owed the universe a debt. [233] Somewhere, the proof contradicted itself, and a contradiction, properly pressed, becomes a rupture. [246] So she worked. [249] She chalked lemmas on the risers, theorems on the landings, covering marble with mathematics until the staircase read like a confession. [270] She was balancedly daring about it: bold enough to test wild conjectures, careful enough never to erase a step that might hold the error. [294] At each landing the stairs branched, and she rolled the dice to choose her path, honoring at last the randomness she had once refused. [318] But the chipped die rolled wrong. [324] Its missing corner biased it toward six, a small, persistent injustice in the probabilities. [338] She logged thousands of throws, climbing and recording, and the bias never washed out. [352] There was no night here, only the same gray hour repeating, but on a landing chalked white as bone, she understood. [373] Her proof of perpetual motion assumed perfect symmetry: every step identical, every force returned without loss. [389] But nothing real was perfectly symmetric. [395] The chipped die was her counterexample, a body whose flaw changed its future, proof that imperfection carries consequence, that loss leaves a measurable mark. [419] And this, she realized with a hard, glad laugh, was what she had truly been chasing: not escape first, but the texture of a missed opportunity, the precise shape of the corner broken off the life she did not choose. [459] If she could write the mathematics of that absence, she could write the mathematics of any flaw, including the staircase's. [479] So she measured the chip. [484] She derived its volume from the bias in ten thousand recorded rolls, working backward from probability to geometry, from chance to wound. [506] The result was a number, small and irrational, and when she substituted it into her original proof as the asymmetry of a single step, the equations buckled. [533] One imperfect stair, and the perpetual engine bled energy; bleeding energy, it could not be infinite; not infinite, it had to end. [555] All she needed was to find the flawed step and prove it flawed, formally, to its face. [572] She found it where she should have looked first: the step she had been standing on when she woke, worn faintly at one corner, like a die struck against a table. [603] She knelt and chalked the final argument across it, line by line: the symmetry assumption, the measured chip, the contradiction, the conclusion. [625] Therefore this staircase cannot exist. [630] Quod erat demonstrandum. [633] The rupture did not roar; it sighed. [640] A seam of ordinary daylight opened along the chipped corner of the step and widened, marble unraveling into theorems, theorems into light. [662] Through the gap she could see a street, a cafe, a world that had gone on without her. [680] She took out both dice and weighed them in her palm. [691] The pristine one she left on the landing, a perfect object for a perfect prison. [706] The chipped one she kept, because its flaw had freed her, and because she finally knew the texture of what she had missed: not smooth, not whole, but real. [735] She stepped through the rupture, grim and hopeful in equal measure, and somewhere behind her the infinite staircase finished, at last, its single impossible turn. [760] If Tomas still rolled dice in that cafe, she meant to ask for one more throw. [776]
