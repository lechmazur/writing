<story>
Mara had stopped painting faces the day the world cracked. [10] Not metaphorically: she would lift her brush and find her subject's left eye drifting two inches from the socket, the mouth repeating itself like a stammer. [36] Reality had fragmented, gently, the way ice does before anyone hears it. [48] A crestfallen portrait artist is a strange thing to be when no face will hold still long enough to be loved. [69] So she went where the fractures pointed: north, to the lighthouse her grandmother had kept, and to the library everyone had forgotten was inside it. [94]

The library wound up the tower's throat, shelves following the spiral stairs, books swollen with salt. [110] Light from the dead lamp room fell down the stairwell in pale, aching sheets, illuminating dust like drowned plankton. [129] On the lowest shelf, wedged behind the tide tables, she found her grandmother's tarot card box, its hinge snapped, its lid split clean across the painted moon. [156] Inside, the cards had not survived as cards. [164] Each one had unraveled into threads, image into fiber, the Tower now a snarl of grey wool, the Star a knot of silver floss. [188] Tucked beneath the tangle lay a note in her grandmother's hand: When the box broke, so did everything else. [207] Then, beneath that: Who could possibly mend a thing like the world? [219]

Mara had always distrusted rhetorical questions. [225] They were doors painted on walls, questions wearing the costume of surrender. [237] "Who could," she said aloud, and answered it the way her grandmother never had: "Someone with patient hands." [255]

That night the walls began to close in. [263] Not as a figure of speech: the library's curved wall crept inward, shelves grinding, the spiral tightening like a fist deciding. [284] The fragmentation was contracting, she realized; a broken world does not stay broken at rest, it collapses toward its wound. [304] And the wound was below her. [310] Every loose thread in the box trailed downward, through a gap in the floorboards, into the lighthouse's unlit root, where the foundation met the sea. [335]

She understood then why she had come. [342] Not to hide among ruined books, but to descend, because some darkness is not an enemy but a workshop, and the only honest repair happens where the damage lives. [371] She gathered the broken box against her chest and took the stairs down as the walls took them too, stone shoulders pressing close enough to feel like breath. [399]

At the bottom there was no light, and then, slowly, there was: the threads themselves glowed faintly, each fiber a vein of trapped lamplight, aching with color the dark could not put out. [432] They ran everywhere, knotted through the bedrock, a snarled loom of the world's image. [446] Here was the truth her grandmother had kept: reality was not painted but woven, and the cards had been its pattern book. [468] When the box broke, the pattern tangled, and every face Mara tried to paint had been telling her so. [487]

She knelt and began untangling. [492] It was slow the way grief is slow. [500] Some knots she eased apart with her fingernails; some she had to follow for what felt like hours, hand over hand through the glowing dark, learning where each thread had come from before she could ask where it ought to go. [541] And that was the second understanding: she could not simply restore the old pattern, because the old pattern had broken once and would break again. [566] The work was not repair. [571] The work was redesign. [575]

So she wove like a portraitist, not a restorer. [584] Where the Tower's grey wool had snapped, she crossed it with the Star's silver, so that ruin and hope shared a seam. [606] Where the Moon's pale thread frayed, she doubled it with her own dark hair, pulled and twisted in. [624] She gave the pattern give. [629] Above her, the grinding of the walls slowed, then stilled, the tower exhaling around its old stone ribs. [647]

When the last knot opened, the threads dimmed to ordinary fiber, and a thin grey morning leaked down the stairwell to find her. [670] She climbed back up through the library, which had loosened, shelves standing easy, books settling like roosting birds. [688] On the worktable she set the tarot box, still broken, and decided it should stay that way. [705] A whole box hides its pattern; a broken one teaches you to hold the threads yourself. [721]

Then she propped a salvaged board against the window and mixed what paint she had. [736] Her own face came back to her in the glass over the sea, whole, both eyes where eyes belong. [755] Who could mend a thing like the world? [763] It was not rhetorical anymore, and that, she thought, lifting her brush, was the whole point of going down into the dark. [785]
