After the story famine, the city of Vell kept its silence the way a wound keeps its stitches. [18] The Ministry had not burned the books, exactly; it had rationed them, then taxed them, then quietly let them starve. [38] What survived was music without words, approved and bloodless, and Marel copied it for a living. [54] She was the orchestra's copyist, diligent to the point of invisibility, her quarter notes so exact the conductor swore they had been printed. [77] Invisibility was the plan. [81] Because Marel had a second ink. [87] It was secretly luminous, brewed from crushed fireflies and archive dust, and it dried as plain black until the lights went out. [109] In darkness, her margins glowed with the words the Ministry had starved. [121]

Each night she climbed the slag hill above the city, where the old kiln's mouth still held warmth like a memory of bread. [144] Mist pooled there every evening, thick enough to swallow lanterns, and inside the kiln's ember alcove she kept her transmitter. [164] The shortwave radio dial glow was the only light she allowed herself, a thin amber line like a struck match held underwater. [186] By that glow she ran her pirate broadcast, four minutes, never more, never the same hour twice. [203] She did not read stories aloud; the listening vans would triangulate a voice telling tales within a week. [221] Instead she sang, and she cloaked the contraband inside elusive song lyrics, verses that sounded like nonsense lullabies unless you knew the key. [244] "The miller's third daughter wore Thursday for shoes" meant: begin at the third word of the third line, count by Thursdays, and a banned fable would assemble itself in your head like frost on a window. [280] The censors heard a tired woman humming. [287] The hungry heard a library. [292]

Marel did not do this for the listeners alone, though she loved them in the abstract way you love strangers who share your fever. [316] Her true motive was stranger and more patient. [324] When the Ministry pulped the archive, the books became dust, and the dust blew everywhere: into flour, into mist, into the rosin on violin bows. [349] Marel believed, with a copyist's literal faith, that the dust had forgotten its own sentences. [364] So she sang to it. [369] She meant to teach the dust to tell stories again, syllable by syllable, until every gray drift in Vell carried plot like pollen. [392] Call it superstition; she called it copying in reverse. [401]

In autumn the Ministry sent an auditor to the orchestra, a soft-spoken man named Corv who counted ink. [419] He noticed Marel's requisitions ran heavy and asked, mildly, what she did with the surplus. [434] "Mistakes," she said. [437] "I am diligent because I throw away everything imperfect." [446] Corv smiled and stayed. [450] He stayed through October, and one evening he followed her into the mist with a signal meter in his coat. [470] She heard his boots on the slag before she saw him, and a thistled calm came over her, prickly and steady at once, the composure of a woman who has rehearsed her own arrest. [504] She did not switch off the transmitter. [511] She sang the verse about the miller's daughter, and the dial glow lit Corv's face as he ducked into the alcove. [532] "I know this song," he said, and his meter hung forgotten at his side. [546] "My mother hummed it during the famine." [553] "She never told me what it meant." [560] Marel watched him the way you watch a knife decide whether it is a tool. [575] Then she taught him the key. [581] Third word, third line, count by Thursdays. [588] She watched the fable assemble behind his eyes, watched a starved part of him sit down to eat. [606]

Corv filed a report stating that the hillside signal was atmospheric interference, common in mist. [621] Then he began requisitioning ink of his own. [629] By spring there were four transmitters in four alcoves, each crewed by someone who had decoded a lullaby and could not go back to silence. [654] Marel kept copying by day, flawless and forgettable, and by night she added new verses, hiding new stories in plainer and plainer tunes. [677] And the dust, she swore, was learning. [684] On windless nights the motes above the kiln drifted in patterns too deliberate for chance, settling on her open notebook like an audience taking seats. [709] Once, sweeping the alcove at dawn, she found her luminous ink glowing faintly where no pen had touched, a thin line of words in the grime of the wall. [738] It said: the miller's third daughter walked out of the famine wearing Thursday for shoes. [753] Marel did not erase it. [758] She corrected one comma, copyist to the last, and left the door open for the mist to read. [776]
