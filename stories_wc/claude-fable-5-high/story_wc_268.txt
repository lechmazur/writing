Brann was a titan no taller than a child, and she had kept the temple library since the rune of drift unmoored it from time. [25] The old priests had carved that rune into the foundation stone to save the archives from a burning age, and it worked too well. [49] The library slipped sideways out of history, and Brann slipped with it. [61] She was regally unpretentious in her exile, wearing a mended robe, sweeping her own floors, addressing each book by its title as though it were a visiting lord. [89] Titans had ruled once, and her blood remembered ceremony even when her hands chose work. [104] Time outside moved only when the constellations shifted, and between shifts came the pause, a held breath in which the rune slackened its grip. [128] During each pause, one book could drift back into the world. [139] Brann learned this by accident, when a hymnal vanished from its shelf and returned a pause later, annotated in a stranger's hand. [161] From those notes she pieced together the news she had dreaded: the royal line her order had sworn to record was broken, and a pretender wore the crown. [189] The library held the only true genealogies, sealed beyond his reach, which was surely why his ancestors had let the rune be carved at all. [214] She could not leave; the rune bound its keeper as firmly as its walls. [228] But a book could leave, and a book could carry words. [239] In the scriptorium she found a single iron gall ink pellet, hard as a beetle's shell, the last of the old priests' stores. [262] Dissolved in rainwater, it gave her ink that bit deep into vellum and darkened with age, ink that could not be scraped away without leaving a scar. [289] She rationed it like water in a drought, for when the pellet was gone, her voice was gone. [307] Plain accusation would be burned by the pretender's censors, so she wrote in riddles instead. [322] In the margin of a herbal she inked a verse about a cuckoo in an eagle's nest. [339] In a treatise on bells she hid a cipher naming the chapel where the true heir's birth was witnessed. [358] Each pause, she chose one book, kissed its spine, and set it on the drift-stone to be carried away. [377] Then she waited the long dark between constellations, alone with the dust and her doubt. [392] Answers began to drift back. [397] A scholar named Ivette wrote questions in pencil beneath Brann's verses, hesitant at first, then hungry. [413] Pause by pause, across the slow turning of the sky, the two of them built a case neither could have built alone. [435] Brann supplied the genealogies, line by careful line; Ivette gathered witnesses, seals, the chapel register with its torn page. [454] But each message cost more than ink. [461] Sending a book through the rune strained it, and Brann felt the strain in the building's bones. [478] Cracks spidered along the foundation stone. [484] Shelves leaned like tired elders. [489] Sunlight from no particular century came through the dome in thinner and thinner threads. [503] She understood the bargain she was making: every truth she smuggled out would, in the end, sunder the rune entirely and let time take the library back. [530] She did not hesitate. [534] A library that hoarded truth while a lie sat the throne was only a tomb with good shelving. [552] The last pause came when the ink pellet was worn to a sliver. [565] She ground it down, every grain, and wrote her final message plainly, because riddles were for hiding and she was done hiding. [587] She named the pretender's grandfather, the forged adoption, the midwife paid in silence. [600] She signed it as keeper of the temple library, last of the small titans, and laid the book on the drift-stone. [621] The rune flared once, like a candle drinking the last of its wick. [634] Then it split down its center, quietly, the way old promises end. [646] The collapse was not violent. [651] Centuries arrived gently, all at once, like guests who had waited politely at the door. [666] Vellum softened into amber dust; pillars bowed and knelt; the dome opened its hands and let the true stars in. [686] Brann sat among the settling gold and watched the constellations move freely for the first time in her long memory. [706] Far away, in a lit hall, Ivette opened a plain brown book and read a titan's confession aloud to a stunned court. [728] The pretender's face, witnesses said later, went the gray of old paper. [740] His crown did not survive the season. [747] Of the library, nothing remained but a foundation stone with a broken rune, and a small figure beside it, unmoving, regal in her rags, finally part of time again. [776] Travelers said the dust there smelled of iron and oak gall, like ink, like a long letter finally finished. [795]
