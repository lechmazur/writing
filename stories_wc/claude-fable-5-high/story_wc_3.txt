Maren Ilsouf had translated cuneiform for thirty years, and she counted words the way other people counted heartbeats. [18] After the spirit left her teacher's body, she did not weep; she catalogued. [31] Old Behram had spent his last decade in the magnetic arbor, a grove of iron-veined trees rooted in a basalt disc that floated above the clouds, suspended on the planet's field lines like a note held on a wire. [70] He had brought her there to read the tablets the arbor's builders left behind. [84] He had also brought a seed. [90] It sat now in a copper dish beside his cold tea: the knowledge fruit seed, dull as a river stone, which the tablets called 'the word that has not yet been spoken.' [122]

Behram's ambition had haunted him into the grave. [130] He wanted to understand the physics of transition: how a thing stops being one state and becomes another, how seed becomes tree, how a living field becomes a dead one. [160] Now his ambition haunted her instead, the way unfinished sentences do. [171] Worse, the arbor itself was failing. [177] Behram's spirit had been, in some way she did not yet have grammar for, part of the grove's circuit; since his death the trees had begun to lose polarity, and the disc dropped a hand's width every hour. [215] She had perhaps three days to reestablish the field before the arbor sank into the cloud deck and was crushed. [235]

Maren was kindly and methodical, traits people mistook for opposites. [245] She made tea for the empty chair, because ritual steadies the hand, and then she laid the tablets out in sequence. [266] The builders had written in a late dialect, dense with agricultural metaphor. [278] One line recurred: 'The orchard stands while the gardener's breath moves through it; when breath departs, plant the unspoken word in the place where pulling becomes letting go.' [306] Behram had read that line as poetry. [313] Maren read it as instructions, because translation had taught her that ancient minds were rarely vague, only foreign. [331]

Her whole craft was analogy: she could not enter a dead language directly, so she crossed into it on bridges built from the living one. [356] She decided to cross into physics the same way. [365] If the arbor was a sentence, she reasoned, then the trees were words, the field lines were grammar, and Behram had been the verb that bound them. [392] A sentence missing its verb does not collapse all at once; it sags, exactly as the disc was sagging. [411] You repair such a sentence not by resurrecting the old verb but by planting a new one in the same grammatical position. [433] The unspoken word. [436] The seed. [438]

But where was 'the place where pulling becomes letting go'? [448] She walked the grove at dusk with a compass and Behram's brass plumb line, testing each tree. [465] Near the arbor's heart, between two leaning iron oaks, the plumb line went strange: it neither dropped nor rose but hung slack, weightless, undecided. [489] A null point, where the opposing fields cancelled, where attraction crossed over into release. [503] Pulling becoming letting go. [507] This, she understood, was what transition actually was: not a wall between states but a narrow country where both were briefly true. [529] Behram had spent decades looking for that country in equations. [539] It had been growing in his garden the whole time. [549]

She knelt and dug with her hands, the soil cold and faintly humming. [562] She set the seed in the null point and, because translation also demands a speaker, she said the words aloud that Behram never finished, the dedication line from his last notebook. [593] The seed took the field the way a verb takes a sentence. [605] She felt it through her knees before she saw it: the disc steadied, the trees re-aligned with a sound like a chorus inhaling, and a pale shoot rose between her fingers. [636] For one moment, in the slack place between pulling and letting go, the air had a familiar weight, like a hand almost resting on her shoulder. [662] Not touching. [664] Touching distance. [666] Then it passed, as transitions do, and the arbor held. [676]

Maren stayed until dark, recording everything in her careful hand, because grief is also a text and she intended to translate it honestly. [699] In the morning the shoot bore one small fruit, and she did not pick it. [714] Some knowledge, she wrote, is the verb of a sentence still being spoken; you do not pluck it, you let it hold the orchard up. [739] She poured two cups of tea anyway, set one on the cold side of the table, and began the next tablet. [760]
