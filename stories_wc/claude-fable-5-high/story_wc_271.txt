The arboretum clung to the cliff like something that had decided, against all evidence, to stay. [16] Nadia had worked there for six years, ever since the last lighthouse keeper packed his lamp oil and left the tower to its automated bulb. [41] The town said the gardens would fail without him watching the weather, but Nadia learned to read storms herself. [60] She was quietly talented in the way the town only noticed afterward: a graft that took, a dying cypress that didn't. [81] Nobody saw her hands shake before the work; they only saw the work. [94]

Seven years ago, at her sister's wedding in another country, a drone had mistaken celebration for threat. [111] Nadia survived because she had stepped behind the cistern to fix her shoe. [124] Her sister survived too, missing a hand, married anyway, laughing now in voice messages Nadia saved like seeds. [142] Eleven others did not survive, and their names were stitched into Nadia's breathing. [155] In her coat pocket she kept a crumpled photograph from that morning: the wedding canopy, her sister's hennaed hands, both still whole. [177] She had crushed it in her fist in a hospital corridor and never smoothed it flat, because flat would be a lie. [199] The creases were the truth; the faces were also the truth. [210] She carried both. [213]

The trouble was the freeze. [218] Any small engine droning overhead - a surveyor's quadcopter, a hobbyist's toy - and her body would lock mid-step, secateurs in hand, a statue among the maples. [243] A clinic therapist in the city had taught her to work through somatic markers: the cold bloom between her shoulder blades, the tightening jaw, the way her feet went distant. [273] Catch the body's first signals, the therapist said, and you can meet the wave before it breaks. [290] Nadia practiced among the trees, narrating her own body like weather: pressure falling, chest tight, storm coming. [307]

In June the town council announced a drone survey of the cliff's erosion, flights every morning for a week. [326] The arboretum's funding depended on the survey; the cliff was crumbling, and the gardens would go with it unless engineers knew where to shore the rock. [352] Nadia could have taken the week off. [359] Instead she asked to guide the pilot through the grounds herself. [370] She did not believe in pretending the sky was safe; she believed in standing under it anyway, eyes open. [389] Hope without truth was just anesthesia; truth without hope was just drowning. [401] She wanted the third thing, the one she had to build by hand. [414]

The first morning, the quadcopter rose from the lighthouse lawn and her back went cold before her ears even registered the whine. [436] There it is, she thought. [441] Shoulder blades. [443] Jaw. [444] Feet. [445] She named each marker aloud, and the naming was a handhold. [456] She froze anyway. [459] But it was different this time, and the difference mattered. [469] Inside the lockup of her muscles she found, for the first time, a small clear space - a pearl of stillness, hard and luminous, formed around the grit of everything that had happened. [501] Not the freeze of prey, but the stillness of a keeper watching a storm he cannot stop and refuses to look away from. [524] She stood inside it and counted the drone's passes like waves. [535] When her feet returned to her, she walked the pilot to the eastern terrace and pointed out the fissures the cameras should see. [558] By the fourth morning, the freeze lasted only seconds, a tide she could stand in. [573]

The survey found what surveys find: the cliff was worse than feared and better than hopeless. [589] Engineers could buy the arboretum forty years, maybe sixty, if the work began now. [603] Nadia liked the honesty of the number. [610] Forty years was not forever, and it was not nothing; it was exactly enough time to plant trees she would never see grown. [633]

That evening she climbed to the lighthouse gallery, where the automated bulb turned its indifferent circle. [649] She took out the crumpled photograph and did not smooth it. [660] She looked at her sister's hands and felt her own jaw, her own shoulders, her own feet, all present, all hers. [681] Below, the new saplings she had grafted that spring stood in their rows, leaning into the salt wind, learning it. [701] She recorded a voice message for her sister: the survey is done, the cliff will hold a while, come visit before the wedding anniversary. [725] She almost said the world is getting better, and stopped, because she did not know that, and she had promised herself no anesthesia. [748] She said instead: the trees took. [754] It was true, and it was hopeful, and it was both at once, which was the only kind of hope she trusted now. [777] She put the photograph back in her pocket, creases and faces together, and went down to water the rows. [796]
