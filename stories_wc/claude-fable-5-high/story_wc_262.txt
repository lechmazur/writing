They still billed her as a crystal gazer at the fair, but Vesna had retired the crystal years ago, because glass only ever showed futures, and futures were not where her grandmother's love had gone. [35] What remained of her gift lived in her palms now: psychometry, the slow listening to objects. [51] So when the lawyers handed her a shoebox of twelve goodbye letters, all sealed, all stamped, none ever mailed, she did not read them first with her eyes. [79] She held them, and the paper hummed like a storm behind closed doors. [92] Every letter was addressed to Emil Brandt, projectionist, the Alhambra Theatre. [103] Every letter ended the same way: I am keeping the rest of it somewhere safe. [118] The rest of what, the lawyers could not say. [127] Vesna could. [129] The rest of the love, the unused portion, the part a woman saves when she marries the sensible man instead. [149] She wanted to know where unused love is stored, because she suspected she was carrying some of her own. [168]

The Alhambra still stood downtown, a vintage movie palace boarded into silence, scheduled for demolition in spring. [185] Vesna went in through the alley door at midnight, humming. [195] The melody was her grandmother's, a tune the old woman had sung at sinks and windows, but the words had died with her, and so the whole night unfolded while forgotten lyrics searched for their tune, circling Vesna's mouth, finding nothing to land on. [239] The dark inside was absolute, but Vesna had always been spatially imaginative; she built the theater in her head from echoes. [260] Her footsteps returned to her shaped like a lobby, a staircase, a mezzanine; she climbed the architecture of sound until her hand found the projection booth door. [287] Inside, her flashlight woke the old machines: two carbon-arc projectors like sleeping bulls, rewind benches, reels stacked in rusted cans. [307] Emil Brandt had worked this booth for forty-one years. [316] Taped beneath the rewind bench, exactly where a careful man hides a careless heart, she found a single film strip in a paper sleeve. [340] Twenty-two frames, hand-spliced, worn soft at the edges from handling. [350] The moment she touched it, thunder moved through her quietly, the way it does when the storm is still miles out but already certain. [374]

A reading this deep could not be rushed. [382] Old objects are like eggs; their memories must incubate against living warmth before they hatch. [397] So Vesna wrapped the film strip in her scarf, pressed it over her heart, and sat all night in Emil's wooden chair, letting her pulse teach the celluloid that it was held again. [430] She hummed the tune to keep herself awake, and the missing words pressed closer, like moths against the booth's little glass portholes. [452] Near dawn, the reading broke open. [458] She saw Emil in this chair, decades younger, threading a strip he never projected for anyone, watching it by lamplight, winding it back, watching again. [483] Twenty-two frames of her grandmother in the lobby, 1958, caught by a borrowed camera: less than one second of her, laughing, mouthing something at the lens. [509] The psychometry gave Vesna what the silent frames could not: sound. [520] Her grandmother was singing. [524] The lost words arrived all at once, sliding into the tune Vesna had hummed all night, locking in like sprockets catching film. [546] If I cannot send my heart, I'll leave it threaded in the dark, where the light goes through. [564] The song was the thirteenth goodbye letter, the one she never wrote down. [577]

Vesna understood then where unused love is stored. [585] Not in heaven, not in regret, but in keeping: in whatever a person warms and winds and returns to, in a strip of film handled until its edges go soft, in a tune carried for sixty years by a woman who would not let go of it even after she let go of the man. [640] Love unused is not love wasted; it is love banked, accruing in objects, waiting for warm hands. [657] At sunrise she threaded the strip into the smaller projector, coaxed the old arc to life, and gave the empty Alhambra its final show: one second of laughter, looped, her grandmother flickering enormous and young across two thousand vacant seats. [697] Vesna stood at the porthole and sang the whole song, words and tune rejoined, her quiet voice rolling through the dead palace like distant thunder kept respectfully low. [725] Then she mailed the twelve letters to Emil's surviving daughter, and kept the film strip against her heart, where it had learned to live. [749] Some things should go on incubating. [755] She walked out into the morning, spending her own stored love at last, humming with all the words. [773]
