Meridian Deep had no stars, only the slow constellations of bioluminescent traffic overhead. [13] Yet every morning Halea Voss taught navigation to children who had never seen a sky. [28] Eight years had passed since the contact, since the things in the trench unfolded themselves and proved we were not alone. [49] Humanity had answered by building downward, a benthic city pressed against the trench rim, close enough to listen. [67] Halea taught dead reckoning by current and sonar, but her own longing pointed upward. [81] She wanted, before she died, to learn to navigate by starlight, the way her grandmother had steered fishing boats across the old Pacific. [104] All she had inherited from that life was a single boat anchor chain link, iron worn smooth, its edges rounded by forty years of salt and strain. [131] She wore it on a cord and let her students pass it around like a relic, which it was. [150]

The visitors did not speak in sound. [157] They spoke in edge geometry, reading the world by running their feelers along the boundaries of things, the rims and folds and broken corners. [181] To them, an object's history lived in its edges, the way a coastline holds the memory of every storm. [200] Linguists had spent eight years trading polyhedra with them and learning almost nothing. [213]

Then the council announced the city would expand. [221] Meridian Deep was crowded, its air recyclers failing, and the only safe shelf for new habitat rings lay across the trench itself. [243] No human chart covered that darkness; sonar returned garbage from the visitors' folded structures below. [258] The council needed a navigator the visitors would trust, and the visitors had, for reasons no one understood, kept returning to the windows of Halea's classroom. [284] She suspected she knew why. [289] Whenever her students handled the chain link, the creatures pressed close against the glass, feelers splayed, attentive as choirboys. [308]

Halea was balancedly curious by temperament, equal parts wonder and caution, and that balance had kept her alive through two hull breaches. [330] So when she volunteered to carry the link into the trench, she did it with open eyes and a sealed will. [351] She descended alone in a one-person sphere, the link clipped outside the hull where their feelers could reach it. [370] They came out of the dark like unfolding paper, vast and quiet. [382] One traced the link's worn curve for a long time, and Halea understood, watching, what they had been reading all along. [403] The link's edges were not smooth by accident; they were an archive. [415] Every voyage her grandmother ever made had ground its signature into that iron, every anchorage, every heading held against a current. [436] To the visitors, it was the first honest human document they had encountered: a life of navigation written in edge geometry. [457]

In answer, the creature folded itself flat against her viewport and then opened, slowly, joint by joint. [474] Across its inner surface ran a map, but a map of a kind no human had drawn. [491] Where human charts ended at a horizon line, this one simply kept going, the horizon unfolded like a hinge, sea floor flowing into water column flowing into sky. [519] She saw the trench rendered in ridges of cartilage, and above it, impossibly, the stars. [534] They had been listening to starlight through eleven kilometers of ocean, reading the sky's faint pressure the way they read edges, and they had charted it. [560] Halea wept inside her sphere, quietly, the way one weeps at a funeral for something beautiful. [576] Her species had fled the surface and forgotten the sky; these beings had never seen it and knew it by heart. [597]

She left the chain link with them. [604] It was the hardest navigation she had ever performed, releasing the last fixed point of her old life, but a fair trade demands a true price. [630] In return they taught her their charts, fold by fold, over months of slow descents. [645] The expansion crews crossed the trench safely along routes she translated, and Meridian Deep grew new rings of light. [664] But that was never, for Halea, the point. [672]

On the night the first habitat ring opened, she took her students to the observation dome and unrolled the chart the visitors had grown for her, a membrane of translucent cartilage. [703] There, traced in raised edges her fingers could follow in the dark, lay Polaris, Vega, the slow wheel of the southern cross. [725] She would never see those stars. [731] But she could navigate by them now, reading them by touch, steering by a sky she carried in her hands. [751] Somewhere below, an old iron link was telling its own story to patient readers, one worn edge at a time. [771] Above and below, the horizons kept unfolding. [778]
