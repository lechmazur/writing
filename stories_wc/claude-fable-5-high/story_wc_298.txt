The catacomb orchard grew where no orchard should, its tree roots snaking between bones in the dark beneath the city. [20] Mara descended within the glow of half-lit corridors, candles guttering in skull-niches, carrying the only payment the Gardener accepted: a destiny, still warm. [43] She was a reckless idealist, everyone said so, and she had stopped arguing. [56] Her brother Tomas had come down here a year ago and traded his future for their mother's last good winter. [76] All he kept of the bargain was a burned tarot card, the Wheel of Fortune scorched to a brown crescent, which he carried until the day he stopped carrying anything at all. [108] Now the card rode in Mara's pocket like a tooth pulled from the world's mouth. [123]

The Gardener was waiting among the apple trees that fruited without sun, their roots threaded through femurs and ribcages like patient white serpents. [146] She was old in a way that had nothing to do with years, and fiercely gentle, the way a midwife is gentle, the way a surgeon is. [173] "You've come to barter," the Gardener said. [180] "Everyone who finds this place has a hole in the soul they hope a trade will patch." [197]

"Not patch mine," Mara said. [202] "Undo his." [204] She held out the burned card. [210] "Tomas traded everything he was going to be, and the hole it left swallowed him whole." [226]

The Gardener took the card and turned it over. [235] "Destinies, once bartered, are not refunded," she said. [243] "They are planted." [246] She gestured to a sapling whose bark bore the faint grain of a face Mara knew better than her own. [266] "Your brother's future grows there, trammeled in root and ring, netted so it cannot wander back to him." [284]

"Then take mine instead," Mara said, too fast, the recklessness arriving before the thought. [298] "Dig his out and plant mine in the same soil." [308]

The Gardener studied her for a long moment. [316] "You would give a stranger's price for a brother who may not thank you." [330]

"I'm not doing it to be thanked." [337] "I'm doing it because the world keeps tearing holes in people and somebody has to sew." [353]

The Gardener almost smiled. [357] "A trade is not a gift," she warned. [365] "But there is one labor I will accept in place of your whole life." [379] She pressed a small knife into Mara's hand. [387] "The dead beneath this orchard were buried nameless. [395] Nameless bones grow bitter fruit. [400] Stay one season and give them meaning, every bone, every branch, and the orchard will loosen its grip on what it holds." [422]

So Mara stayed, and learned the work of inlaying meaning everywhere. [433] Into a cracked skull she carved a fisherman's knot, deciding this one had loved the river. [449] Into a child's small vertebrae she set chips of green glass, a story about a girl who collected pieces of the sea. [471] She did not know if her stories were true. [480] She learned that it did not matter; meaning is not found, it is given, pressed in like gold leaf into a wound. [502]

Within the glow of the half-lit corridors, time moved like sap. [513] The trees changed where she worked. [519] Bitter apples sweetened. [522] Roots that had clutched the bones like fists began to cradle them instead. [535] And something in Mara changed too. [541] The recklessness that had carried her down here cooled into a dusky resolve, the slow-burning kind that does not need an audience. [563]

On the last day of the season, the Gardener led her to the sapling with her brother's face. [581] Its roots had risen from the soil of their own accord, untrammeled, holding the future loosely, like a hand opening. [601] "Take it home to him," the Gardener said. [609] "Tell him a destiny is only a story a life agrees to tell." [622] She pressed the burned tarot card back into Mara's palm, and where the scorch had been, faint gold lines now showed: the Wheel, repaired, inlaid by someone's patient hand. [651]

"Yours?" Mara asked. [654] "Yours," the Gardener said. [658] "You have been mending more than bones all season." [667]

Mara climbed back toward daylight with a sapling in her arms and dirt under her nails. [683] Behind her, the orchard breathed in its bone-rich dark, fed by every name she had invented, every story she had pressed into the nameless dead. [708] She understood now that the hole in her brother's soul would not close all at once, and neither would hers, and that this was the real trade: not destiny for destiny, but despair for work. [743] Some holes are not patched by what you take back. [753] They are patched by what you give meaning to, one careful inlay at a time. [768]
