The mist came up the cliff like a slow tide, and Maren stood in it, counting nights. [17] She was the only prisoner exchange negotiator both kingdoms would still tolerate, which said less about trust than about exhaustion. [37] The tower at Greyhallow belonged to no one, a neutral territory written into the armistice because neither army could hold it through a winter. [61] Its storm-worn parapet faced both borders at once, windblown and slick, the mist erasing whichever flag a visitor hoped to see. [82] Neither side trusted the other's clocks, so the exchanges were timed by synchronizing through moon phases: prisoners crossed only on the first night of the full moon, when both watchtowers saw the same light and could not argue about it. [122] Tonight the moon was a pale coin behind the fog, but it was full, and full was law. [140]

In her coat pocket Maren turned an antique drawer pull, brass gone soft-edged with handling, a small cold weight shaped like a sleeping hound. [164] She had pried it from the desk at Cathel six years ago, the desk where she had signed an exchange list too quickly and sent eleven men to the wrong gate. [195] Ten had died. [198] The eleventh was crossing tonight. [203] She kept the pull not as punishment but as practice; she meant to recycle her mistakes into wisdom, the way a smith reclaims bad iron, and the brass hound reminded her that haste was the flaw she had to keep burning out. [245]

Boots on the stair. [249] The eastern escort came first, two soldiers and a thin man in a borrowed coat, wrists unbound as the terms required. [270] Maren knew his face before the mist finished giving it back: Doran Vell, the one survivor of Cathel, captured again two winters later. [293] Six years ago, in a prison yard, she had told him she was sorry. [307] He had said nothing, and she had lived ever since in the long country between apology and forgiveness, where every road is uphill and unmarked. [332]

The western party arrived with their own prisoner, a young officer with fever-bright eyes. [346] A furrowed hush settled over the parapet, the particular silence of people concentrating on not making history worse. [364] Maren read the terms aloud twice, slowly, the second time against her own old hunger to be done. [382] Then the western captain frowned at his list. [390] "This name is wrong," he said. [396] "The man we were promised is Aldric Vane; this is his brother." [408]

The old Maren would have signed anyway, traded name for name and let the ledgers argue later. [425] Instead she closed her hand around the brass hound and made herself uncommonly thoughtful, slower than the wind wanted, slower than the soldiers wanted. [449] She questioned the feverish officer gently and learned the truth in pieces: Aldric had died of the same fever a week ago, and his brother had taken his name so the exchange would not collapse, so someone, at least, would go home. [491] A false name on a signed list had killed ten men at Cathel. [504] A false name, refused tonight, would kill two more, because neither side would feed a worthless prisoner through another winter. [524] Wisdom, she decided, was not the opposite of her mistake; it was the same knowledge, melted down and recast. [543] She amended the list in her own hand, recorded the true name and the death beside it, and made both captains witness it under the lawful moon. [570] "No one crosses under a lie tonight," she said. [579] "But everyone crosses." [582] The captains grumbled, then signed, perhaps because the mist made the war feel far away, perhaps because slowness, done well, looks like authority. [605]

The crossing itself was a small mercy of rope and wood. [616] A basket-ferry hung beneath the parapet on a doubled line, and tradition demanded the negotiator untether it herself, so that the first hand on the rope belonged to no army. [646] Maren worked the wet knot loose with cold fingers and sent the false Aldric west, then drew the basket back and watched Doran Vell climb in. [672] He paused, mist beading on his lashes. [679] "You checked the names," he said. [685] "Six years ago you didn't." [690] "I know," she said. [694] She took the brass hound from her pocket and offered it on her open palm, an account rendered rather than a gift. [716] He looked at it a long moment, then closed her fingers back over it. [730] "Keep it," he said. [734] "It's working." [736]

It was not forgiveness, but it was a step taken on that uphill road, witnessed by the moon both sides agreed on. [758] Maren untethered the line a last time and let him go down through the mist, slowly, the way careful things are done. [780]
