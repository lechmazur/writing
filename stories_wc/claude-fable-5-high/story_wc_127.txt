Mirelle sold feelings that were almost real, and she was honest about the almost. [14] Her shop occupied an amber grotto on the cliff's seaward face, a hollow of fossilized resin that glowed like held breath whenever dusk came through the water-line. [41] Customers descended the resin steps at that hour because amber light flatters counterfeit things. [55] Mirelle was a counterfeit emotion dealer, and she preferred the word counterfeit to fake, because counterfeits required craft. [73] Her medium was scent. [77] She had trained as a fragrance chemist before the grief trade found her, and she still dispensed her wares the old way: a single blotter petal, dipped and offered, held beneath the customer's nose. [111] One petal, one feeling, one hour of borrowed weather inside the skull. [123]

Her counterfeits worked because they were half truths. [131] Pure invention smells like nothing; the nose knows a lie immediately. [142] So Mirelle anchored every formula in something true she had taken from a real person: a widower's relief, a bride's specific dread, then diluted it past recognition and dressed it in bergamot. [174] How she took the true part was her secret and her gift. [186] Emotions ride the body's small electricities. [192] When a customer's sleeve brushed the amber walls, the resin gathered static, and Mirelle could read the charge with her fingertips: grief crackled long and low, envy snapped bright and quick. [223] She had chosen the grotto for exactly this; amber is the friendliest insulator, holding static the way it once held insects, perfectly and forever. [247]

It must be said that Mirelle loved her customers the way a cartographer loves coastlines, attentively and from above. [266] She could map any feeling; she could not enter one. [276] This was the flaw in her craft and, lately, the ache in her ribs. [290] Insight without empathy, she had begun to suspect, was just surveillance. [301]

On the evening this story concerns, her last customer was a young dockhand named Petra, who wanted to buy courage and could only describe it as the opposite of Tuesday. [331] Mirelle, professionally thorough as always, did not laugh. [339] She walked the girl along the amber wall, let the static gather, and laid two fingers against her wrist. [358] The charge that came through was not fear. [366] It was loneliness, a steady, patient hum, the signature of someone who had stopped expecting to be read at all. [386]

Mirelle's protocol was clear: extract the true note, dilute, dress, sell. [397] Instead, for the first time in nineteen years, she did the opposite of dilution. [411] She let the charge run up her arm undimmed and chose to subsume it, to take the loneliness whole into herself rather than skim it into a vial. [439] She had theorized this was possible. [445] She had not theorized that it would hurt. [453]

The grotto's amber went from honey to ember as dusk thickened, and Mirelle gripped the workbench while another person's whole weather system moved into her chest. [479] Petra, alarmed, asked if she should fetch someone. [487] "Stay," Mirelle managed, "you are the someone." [494] What she had wanted, what she had built nineteen years of careful half truths toward, was this: to fuse insight with empathy, to know the coastline and also drown a little in the sea. [528] The loneliness did not visit her; it settled in, unpacked, learned her name. [541]

As the transformation completed itself in her, Mirelle understood at last that Petra did not need courage. [558] Courage was Tuesday's twin; what the girl needed was the certainty of having been witnessed. [573] No counterfeit on her shelves could do that, because witness cannot be diluted. [586] So Mirelle did something unprofessional and entirely thorough. [594] She took a fresh blotter petal, the chemist's plain paper kind, and pressed it to the pulse of her own wrist, where the skin's chemistry carries what the heart is doing. [625] It was the first honest product the grotto had ever sold. [636] Petra breathed it in and began, quietly, to cry, which is what witnessed people do. [651] Mirelle watched her with the old affectionate detachment, except the detachment had a crack in it now, and the affection was leaking through. [674] She charged the girl nothing, which was bad business and good chemistry. [686]

After Petra climbed back into the dark, Mirelle sat alone in the ember-colored grotto and inventoried herself the way she would any new acquisition. [710] The loneliness was still in her, but it had changed flavor; subsumed, it had become something nearer to company. [729] She understood that her inventory was now compromised, that every petal she sold from this night forward would carry a trace of real feeling, and that her counterfeits would slowly stop being counterfeit. [762] Half truths, she thought, ran in both directions; if you kept adding the true half, eventually you arrived somewhere whole. [782] She turned off the lamp and let the amber keep glowing anyway.
