The alchemist's market hangs above the city on ropes and salvaged masts, a web of stalls strung between rooftops, and I climb to it every dusk the way other people go home. [32] I am a translator, though not of languages anyone still speaks. [43] I translate between worlds: this one, loud and forgetful, and the drowned one underneath it, which remembers everything. [61] The work began between childhood and now, in the gap where my grandmother used to stand. [77] She kept the lighthouse at Harrow Point before the harbor towns were flooded for the reservoir, before the city decided those towns had never mattered. [102] When she died she left me one thing: a fragment of the lighthouse lens, a curved wedge of glass heavy as a fist. [125] Hold it to your eye and the world bends; hold it to your chest and the world holds still. [144] The alchemists taught me what it was: an anchor of intuition, one of the old instruments that let a person feel truth before they can prove it. [171] Without an anchor, a translator drifts; with one, the drowned world becomes elusively tangible, like the warmth of a hand that has just let go. [196]

Tonight I cross the rope bridge to Veska's stall, where she sells pigments ground from river stones and, more quietly, paintings rescued before the flood. [221] This is how the drowned speak to me: through hidden details in paintings. [234] Their artists knew the water was coming, so they buried their history inside their art, where census takers and demolition crews would never think to look. [260] A fisherman's net painted with seventeen knots: seventeen families evicted from Lesser Quay. [273] A gull's wing angled like a clock hand: the hour the floodgates opened early, while people were still carrying out their beds. [295] I translate these details into testimony, and the market's archivists copy my pages, because someone should honor histories the city overlooked on purpose. [318]

Veska has saved something for me: a portrait of a lighthouse keeper, oils darkened by smoke, the frame fastened shut with corroded brass clasps. [342] My grandmother's face looks out of it, younger than I ever knew her, holding a lens that is whole. [361] "The clasps," Veska says. [365] "They were made to be opened by the right hands." [375] My fragment grows warm against my sternum, the old signal: truth nearby. [387] I study the painting the way she taught me without ever saying she was teaching. [402] In the painted lens, almost invisible, a reflection: not the sea, but this market, these ropes, these lanterns, me. [421] She painted the future into the past, or the past reached forward; between worlds, the difference dissolves. [438]

I unfasten the clasps. [442] They open under my fingers as if oiled yesterday, and the back panel of the frame swings loose. [460] Inside, wrapped in waxed sailcloth, lies the rest of the lens, broken into wedges, each one labeled in her handwriting with a name. [483] Not places: people. [486] The drowned dead, one anchor each, waiting for translators not yet born. [498] She did not leave me a keepsake; she left me the first piece of an instrument meant to be scattered, so that intuition itself could be inherited. [525]

I lift my own fragment and fit it against its siblings, and for a breath the lens is whole in my hands. [547] Light moves through it that no lantern in the market is casting. [559] Time stops being a line. [564] I am eight years old on the lighthouse stairs and I am here on the rope bridge and I am old, telling this story, all in one eternal instant, the moment the drowned world has been keeping for me. [603] In that instant I see Harrow Point unflooded, washing on lines, my grandmother painting in the lamp room, hiding her town inside brushstrokes because she trusted someone would learn to read them. [635] Then the light folds shut, and the wedges come apart in my hands, gently, like a thing agreeing to be shared. [656]

Veska watches me wrap them, one by one, in their names. [667] "You will deliver them," she says, not asking. [675] "All of them," I say. [680] Somewhere below, the city goes on forgetting; up here, among the high stalls and the lantern smoke, remembering is a trade like any other, with apprentices. [706] I pocket my fragment, the first anchor, still warm. [715] Every history is a lighthouse, my grandmother used to say; it only needs someone to carry the light back up the stairs. [737] I climb down toward the dark city with my pockets full of dawn. [750]
