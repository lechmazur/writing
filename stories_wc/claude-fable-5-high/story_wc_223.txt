The interregnum was four months old when the dome's shadow stopped obeying the sun. [14] In Veil, every citizen knew the story: when a monarch dies without an heir, the great shadow unmoors itself and wanders, a nomadic penumbra drifting through the districts like slow gray water. [46] Wherever it passed, people forgot small things: a neighbor's laugh, the way a grandmother folded bread. [62] Mara was paid to remember loudly and falsely. [70] The Regency Council hired her as a crisis actor, to perform composure at public drills, modeling the approved face of mourning. [91] What the Council did not know was that Mara had survived the actual collapse of the palace's east wing, the disaster that killed the queen and a hundred others. [120] She had been pulled from the rubble by a stranger who tapped twice on her wrist before lifting the beam, a code meaning still here. [145] No record kept that gesture. [150] No record kept any of them: the coat surrendered to a shivering boy, the woman who hummed to the dying because names had failed her. [175] Mara decided, somewhere between the rubble and the rehearsals, that she would become the historian of small gestures, even if she had to invent the discipline herself. [202]

The Regent Avesh summoned her to the whispering gallery, the curved walk beneath the dome where a murmur on one side arrives intact at the other. [228] He was benevolently ruthless, a man who signed rationing orders with tears in his eyes and never once revised them. [248] Between them sat a chessboard missing all the pawns. [257] Each pawn had been carved for a district of Veil, he explained, and after the collapse he had buried them with the dead, refusing to play with pieces that no longer had people. [290] "A mercy," he called it. [295] Mara saw it differently: a board where only the powerful moved, and the small were simply gone. [312] "The penumbra reaches the gallery tonight," Avesh said. [320] "I intend to bind it to the throne." [328] A bound shadow would mean legitimacy; the Council could rule as if crowned. [341] "And what it carries?" Mara asked. [347] Because she had walked inside the penumbra once, deliberately, and felt it brush past her like a crowd of unfinished sentences. [368] It did not steal small memories, she had realized; it gathered the ones nobody had bothered to keep. [386] Bind it, and every unrecorded kindness of the collapse would be fixed beneath the Council's feet, an archive sealed into a foundation stone. [409] "What it carries," Avesh said gently, "will hold us up." [419] That was his ruthlessness: he would mortar the dead into stability and call it shelter. [434]

Mara knew one thing Avesh did not. [441] The gallery's masons had built a secret into the floor: walk its stones in deliberate patterns and the curve translates footsteps into speech, whispering the spelled words around the dome. [471] An old conservator had taught her the alphabet of paces during a drill, tracing letters with his cane, another small gesture no history would keep. [496] The penumbra, born of the dome, listened only to the dome's own voice. [509] Whatever the gallery whispered, the shadow obeyed. [516] Avesh planned to walk the binding words himself at midnight. [526]

Mara arrived at dusk, as the gray tide crossed the river district. [538] She set the pawnless chessboard on the gallery floor as her map, eight steps by eight, and began to walk. [558] Not the binding. [561] She paced the word UNBIND, each letter a figure of turns, her bootheels striking the stones where pawns should have stood. [582] Then she walked the gestures themselves: TWO TAPS ON THE WRIST. [593] THE COAT. [595] THE HUMMING. [597] Her legs burned; spelling is slow when your body is the pen. [609] Around the dome the whisper circled, soft and exact, and the penumbra poured into the gallery like ink remembering it had once been light. [633] Avesh found her on the final letter. [640] "You are unmaking the only mortar we have," he said, and his grief was real, which made him no less dangerous. [661] "You buried the pawns," Mara answered, still walking. [669] "I am digging them up." [674] He could have stopped her with one shout. [682] Instead he watched the shadow loosen, because even ruthless benevolence recognizes a better mercy when it walks past in plain letters. [703]

The penumbra came apart gently, not into darkness but into a luminous absence, a brightness shaped exactly like everything the city had failed to record. [728] All across Veil, people paused mid-step, remembering kindnesses they had never been told. [741] When the coronation finally came, the new monarch found a chessboard waiting in the gallery, its pawns recarved, and a woman beside it with a ledger of small gestures, already on her second volume. [775] The dome's shadow obeys the sun now, but the gallery still whispers for anyone who knows how to walk. [794]
