Halvey Crane had spent twenty years fixing weather rigs above the polar shelf before the xenobiology research station hired her, and she still introduced herself as a sky mechanic, obliging to a fault. [33] If a scientist needed a vent unjammed at three in the morning, Halvey came with her tools and her quiet smile. [54] Nobody asked why she always volunteered for the aviary wing. [64] The station studied the venn-moths of Kessler's World, creatures the size of kites whose wings beat once an hour and whose sleep lasted decades. [88] The biologists called them dead ends, beautiful failures of evolution, animals that wasted time. [102] Halvey believed something stranger: that the moths did not waste time at all, because for them time moved sideways. [121] She had noticed it first in their neural scans. [130] Every reading looked like an interference pattern, the kind a hologram leaves on film, where each fragment contains the whole image. [151] A mechanic learns to trust patterns. [157] If the moths' minds were holographic, then every instant of their lives existed in every other instant, spread laterally, like rooms along a corridor rather than rungs on a ladder. [187] They were not sleeping through time; they were walking beside it. [198] She told Dr. Imari this in the mess hall, and Imari laughed into her coffee. [213] "You fix antennas, Halvey," she said, not unkindly. [221] "Leave temporal physics to people who failed at it professionally." [231] The dismissal was gentle and absolute, an icy warmth Halvey had come to expect from scientists: kindness in the voice, frost in the verdict. [255] So she stopped asking permission. [260] The station's power core was a magnetic nexus, a knot of field coils that aligned with the planet's flux lines twice a day, strongest at twilight. [286] Halvey had maintained it for three years and knew its moods better than its designers did. [302] Her plan was simple in the way that only a mechanic's plan can be. [316] A hologram is read by shining a reference beam through the interference pattern at the correct angle. [333] If the moths' sideways time was stored holographically in their nervous systems, then the nexus at full twilight alignment could serve as her reference beam, and the aviary's sensors would record whatever image emerged. [367] Not a theory anymore; a measurement. [373] She chose the hour when the whole colony rested between wingbeats, that long station-wide hush of beating wings at rest, when even the ventilation seemed to lower its voice. [402] Twilight came down violet over the dome. [409] In the nexus chamber, the coils hummed toward alignment. [418] The master lever stood waist-high, mechanically warm under her palm, its housing heated by decades of current, the one honest handshake the station ever offered her. [444] She levered the coupling open one degree past regulation, routing the aligned field through the aviary's sensor grid. [462] What happened was not an explosion or a light show. [472] It was quieter than that, and far stranger. [480] Across the aviary, four hundred sleeping moths opened their eyes at once, without moving a single wing. [497] Subdued awakenings, the log would later call them: no panic, no flight, just attention arriving from somewhere alongside the present. [517] The sensor grid lit with an image. [524] Not a brainwave, an image: the aviary itself, rendered in interference fringes, but populated with moths in flight, moths hatching, moths dying, all overlaid in the same instant, every moment of the colony's existence shining through every other. [562] Cut the recording anywhere and the whole remained. [570] Time, for them, was not a line to crawl along but a wide field to stand in. [587] Sideways, exactly as she had said. [593] Imari found her there at midnight, the alarms having dragged half the staff out of bed. [609] She stared at the recording for a long time. [618] "You bypassed a regulated coupling," she said at last, voice cold as the polar shelf. [633] Then, more quietly: "And you were right." [640] The same icy warmth as always, except now the frost and the kindness had traded places. [656] Halvey did not gloat. [660] An obliging woman to the end, she handed over her notes, her field calculations, the angle of the coupling, everything. [680] She asked only one thing in return: that the moths be reclassified from evolutionary failures to something else. [698] The report Imari filed used the word pioneers. [706] Afterward, Halvey returned to the aviary alone. [713] The moths had closed their eyes again, settling back into their wide, sideways country. [727] She stood in the hush between wingbeats, and thought about her twenty years above the polar shelf, all those repairs, all those nights, and wondered whether somewhere alongside this moment she was still up there, wrench in hand, sky overhead. [767] She hoped so. [770] It would mean nothing was ever truly behind her, only beside her, waiting. [783]
