In the cobalt observatory, autumn arrived as a thin gold light through the high lancet windows. [16] Old Vesna sat beneath the great brass telescope, embroidering galaxy charts for the Astronomer Royal. [31] Everyone agreed she was harmless, a foolish woman who hummed nonsense and stitched stars in the wrong places. [49] The wrongness was deliberate. [53] Each misplaced star was a word, each crooked constellation a sentence, and her charts held everything the observatory's masters said when they believed no one wise was listening. [81] Vesna had learned long ago that foolishness was the safest costume wisdom could wear. [95] She had come to the observatory not for wages but to listen for unheard stories, the kind whispered by clerks and generals who thought silence kept their secrets. [123] Silence, she knew, only echoed. [128]

That autumn, the echoes grew sharp. [134] Lord Calder, master of the observatory, began receiving visitors after the lamps were doused. [148] They spoke beneath the telescope, trusting the dome's vastness to swallow their words. [161] But domes do not swallow; they echo. [168] Vesna, dozing theatrically in her corner with needle in hand, heard everything twice. [181] They were planning a coup. [186] On the night of the equinox observation, when the Regent climbed the tower to view the comet, the doors would be barred and the succession rewritten in the dark. [215]

Vesna stitched it all into a spiral nebula and considered her position. [227] She could not simply warn the Regent; a fool's word would be laughed away, and a wise woman's word would get her killed. [250] Unraveling a conspiracy, she decided, was like unraveling embroidery: never tear the cloth, only find the one thread that releases the rest. [272] She worked through adjacent possibilities, each step small, deniable, and one inch from innocence. [286]

First the thread of the doors. [292] A fool may oil hinges badly; she jammed the bar of the tower door with wax so it could not drop. [313] Then the thread of timing. [318] The conspirators meant to synchronize their strike with the chapel bell at the comet's zenith. [333] In the chapel below the dome burned the eternal candle, lit at the observatory's founding and never allowed to die. [353] The bell-keeper, an old believer, set his hour by its flame, trimming the wick at each watch. [370] Vesna, muttering happily about moths, carried a taper to the chapel and borrowed a flame from the eternal candle. [389] With it she lit a second candle, a shorter one, and swapped it into the keeper's marked holder so the watch would burn down early. [414] The bell would ring before the conspirators were in place. [424]

Last, the thread of witness. [429] On the equinox eve, before the coup could gather itself, Vesna presented her newest galaxy chart to the Regent's astronomer as a fool's tribute. [453] She giggled, curtsied badly, and pointed at her spiral nebula, saying the stars had been gossiping again. [470] The astronomer, a careful man, knew the true sky. [479] He saw the misplaced stars and frowned, and Vesna watched understanding climb his face like dawn up a tower wall. [499]

That night the comet rose over the cobalt dome, and the bell rang a full watch early. [516] The conspirators surged toward doors that would not bar, into a tower already lined with the Regent's guards. [534] Lord Calder's shout died against the dome and came back to him doubled, an echo with no answer. [552]

Afterward, the Regent's men searched for the informant and found only an old woman snoring in her corner, thread tangled in her lap. [575] The astronomer said nothing of the chart, and Vesna said nothing of anything, which was her oldest skill. [593] When the leaves finished falling, she returned the borrowed flame, touching her taper back to the eternal candle as if repaying a small debt. [617] Then she sat beneath the telescope and began a new chart, stars in their proper places for once. [635] The observatory had grown quiet, but Vesna kept her needle moving and her ears open. [650] Somewhere in the cobalt hush, another story was waiting to be overheard, and she intended to be foolish enough to hear it. [672]
