My father built bunkers for a living, and I could not stand to be inside them. [16] Every month, when gravity inverted for an hour, the whole town climbed down into his concrete throats and bolted the lids, and I sat at the bottom of the ladder counting my own breath like coins I was afraid to spend. [57] The walls leaned. [60] They always leaned. [63] My mother, before she went up and did not come back, left me a tiny porcelain doll with a hollow body and a cork no bigger than a grain of rice. [94] She had used it to collect the flavors of different silences. [105] Snowfall silence tasted of cold tin, she told me; the silence after a slammed door tasted of pennies and regret. [125] The trick, she said, was limiting exposure: uncork the doll for one breath only, or every silence bleeds into every other and you are left with mud. [152] One flavor she never caught. [157] The gilded hush. [160] It lived, the stories said, on the xenial trellis above the clouds, an old lattice of vine and iron built by the first sky-farmers to catch whoever fell upward, a structure so hospitable it grew a handhold wherever a stranger reached. [201]

The year I turned twelve, the sirens sang the inversion warning and my father reached for the bunker lid, and I did not follow him down. [227] I stood in the yard with the doll in my fist and let the ground let go of me. [246] Falling up is a long exhale. [252] The town shrank to a scatter of bolted lids, the clouds opened like a door I had always wanted, and then the trellis caught me, gently, a rung sprouting under my palm exactly where my panic expected nothing. [290] Up there, the hush was gilded. [296] I do not mean golden light, though there was that too. [307] I mean the silence itself had weight without mass, warmth without source, a thing intangibly tangible, like a hand laid on the shoulder by someone who is not there. [336] It pressed everywhere and confined nothing. [342] For the first time in my life, my chest stretched instead of contracted. [355] I wanted to gulp it. [360] I remembered my mother's rule instead. [366] One breath only. [369] I uncorked the doll, counted the single inhale, sealed it while my whole body begged for more. [386] On the vine beside me hung a seedpod, gold-skinned, humming faintly with the same hush, and the trellis bent its lattice toward me as if offering a guest a parting gift. [417] I took it. [420]

When the hour ended, gravity remembered me, and I fell home through the cloud floor with the doll warm against my sternum and the pod cool in my pocket. [449] My father was waiting at the open lid, gray-faced, certain he had lost me the way he lost her. [468] He did not shout. [472] He climbed down into the bunker and I, for once, climbed down after him, because I had something to show him and one breath of courage left. [499] The walls leaned. [502] I uncorked the doll. [506] One breath of the gilded hush spilled into that concrete throat, and the room did not get larger, but the fear did get smaller, which is nearly the same thing. [536] My father's shoulders, contracted for years around her absence, stretched. [546] "She found it," he said, and it was not a question. [557] She had gone up to taste it and stayed, or fallen past it, or become it; we would never know, and the not-knowing tasted, I realized, like the hush itself. [587]

We planted the seedpod in a coffee can of yard dirt and set it in the bunker's deepest corner. [606] It sprouted by the next inversion: a pale vine that climbed the ladder rung by rung, exhaling small gilded quiets the way other plants exhale air. [632] My father began cutting windows into his bunkers after that, against every instinct of his trade. [648] Shelter, he decided, should hold a person without holding them down. [659] And I practice the bunker the way my mother taught me to practice silence: by limiting exposure, one breath longer each month, my fear contracting as my lungs stretch. [688] On inversion days I still let go of the ground. [698] The trellis still sprouts handholds for me, hospitable as ever, and I uncork the doll for exactly one breath, collecting flavors my mother never finished: the hush of dawn frost, the hush after laughter, the wide gilded hush above the clouds that tastes, every time, like her. [745] Someday I will stay a second breath. [752] Not yet. [754] Some silences are too good to spend all at once. [764]
