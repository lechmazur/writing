After time became visible, it hung in the air like dust in slanted light, and the people of the asteroid Veshara learned to read their own grief in it. [29] Veshara was a baroque thing, carved by some vanished hand into spirals of stone lace, gilded balconies, and saints with eroded faces. [51] Nobody knew who had carved it, which was precisely the problem. [62] Two archaeological missions had landed within a month of each other, and their dispute had curdled the settlement's air worse than any storm. [85] The Terran Institute insisted the carvers had been a warrior theocracy; the Belt Consortium insisted they had been miners with delusions of grandeur, and that the ornament was machine-cut kitsch. [115] Between them, in a tower whose mirrors he kept shrouded, lived Ilya Voss, a defunct revolutionary. [131] Forty years ago he had written manifestos that set three colonies burning; now his movement was a footnote, his comrades ash or accountants, and he had retired to Veshara to be forgotten with dignity. [165] He was poetically inclined, which his old party had considered a flaw and which he now considered his last working organ. [186]

When the temporal storms came, time thickened into visible currents, and any mirror became unbearable: it reflected not your face but your whole duration, every version of you smeared into one shining wound. [219] So everyone on Veshara covered their mirrors during storms, with velvet, with sailcloth, with old flags. [235] Ilya used the banner of his dead revolution, and found this efficient and bitterly funny. [250]

The dispute reached him because of the shard. [258] A Consortium digger had pulled a crystal vase shard from beneath the asteroid's oldest chapel, a curved sliver no longer than a finger, and both missions claimed it as proof. [288] Crystal held time better than any other matter; in storm-light, a polished shard could replay what it had once reflected. [308] Whoever read it first would own the carvers' history. [317] They came to Ilya because he was the only neutral party with storm experience, and because he was, conveniently, already ruined. [338] He agreed, though not for their reasons. [345] He had spent his life trying to remake one civilization and failing; here was a chance to reimagine an entire civilization from a single splinter, to get it right at last, even if only backward. [380]

The storm arrived on schedule, time pouring through the corridors in slow amber rivers. [394] Ilya shrouded every mirror in his tower, then sat alone with the shard uncovered, the one reflection he would permit. [414] He devised a reading frame from storm protocols he knew by heart: angle the crystal against the current, let duration pool in the curve, breathe slowly, and watch. [442]

What surfaced was neither theocracy nor mining camp. [450] He saw hands, thin and patient, turning the vase on a wheel under lamplight; he saw the carvers were few, perhaps thirty, refugees from some war the records had eaten. [480] They had carved the asteroid baroque not from faith or vanity but from mourning, ornament as a way of refusing to vanish quietly. [503] Every saint's eroded face was a portrait of someone left behind. [514] Then the vision turned, and the shard showed him its own breaking: an argument, a thrown vase, a small civil fury inside that tiny civilization, after which the carving stopped forever. [545] Ilya sat with that for a long time, the storm humming gold against the shrouded glass. [561] He understood it as only a defunct revolutionary could: how one quarrel can end a world that survived a war. [581]

When the missions convened, both expecting victory, he gave his testimony in the chapel itself. [596] He did not give them data. [602] He gave them a poem — the only honest format, he said, for what crystal remembers — describing thirty mourners carving lace into rock so their dead would have a place to be beautiful. [634] He told them the quarrel that broke the vase looked exactly like the quarrel in this room. [651] Neither institute could publish a poem, and neither could refute one. [662] In the end they drafted a joint report, grudging and historic, the first thing the two missions had ever built together. [683]

Ilya kept the shard, by quiet mutual surrender. [691] He set it on his windowsill, where between storms it was only glass, catching ordinary light. [707] Sometimes, when the amber currents rose and he draped the old banner over the last mirror, he let the shard stay bare and watched the patient hands again. [735] It was not redemption; it was something quieter, a burnished sorrow, like brass worn bright by handling. [752] He had failed to remake the living, but he had given the dead a truer history than either empire of experts would have allowed. [776] On Veshara, that counted as a revolution that finally held.
