Mara Voss had spent eleven years chasing legends that other people had abandoned, which was why everyone in the trade called her errant rather than expert. [26] She did not mind the word. [32] Errant meant wandering, and wandering had brought her here, to a twilight barge canal in the fen country where her grandfather swore he had sunk a fortune in 1963. [61] The story had every hallmark of a lie. [69] She knew this, and she had come anyway, because lies had become her true subject. [84] Mara no longer hunted treasure; she hunted the architecture of lies, the way a falsehood was framed and joisted and roofed so that a family could live inside it for generations. [115] Her grandfather's lie had been load-bearing. [121] It explained his poverty, his bitterness, the way he checked his antique pocket sundial as if real time could not be trusted. [143] That sundial sat in her coat now, a brass disc with a folding gnomon, useless in the dark and somehow honest because of it. [167] A sundial cannot lie; it can only refuse to answer. [177]

She set up her salvage rig at the lock gate as the light went violet over the water. [195] The canal lay thoughtfully clear, so still that the first stars doubled themselves in it, and the reeds stood like patient witnesses. [217] Salvage in low visibility was not a science; it was a discipline of the hands. [232] Mara had spent two seasons training her instincts for exactly this, dragging weighted lines through test ponds blindfolded until her fingers could read the difference between root, stone, and worked metal. [263] The eyes were liars after dusk; the hands were not. [273] She worked the grapple slowly along the silt while a small radio on the bank murmured the night programs, her only clock. [295] The local station went dead at five, and she had promised herself she would stop by the silence of morning radio, whatever she found or failed to find. [323]

Hours passed in that strange weather of the soul she had no better name for than serene upheaval: the surface of her perfectly calm, the depths rearranging themselves. [351] Near three, her fingers reported something through the line: not root, not stone. [364] Worked metal, edged, hollow. [368] She hauled with the patience of someone lifting a sleeping child and brought up a strongbox furred with sixty years of silt. [390]

Inside, wrapped in oilcloth, there was no fortune. [398] There were letters. [401] She read them by lamplight on the bank, the radio murmuring behind her like a tide. [417] Her grandfather had not sunk a fortune in 1963. [426] He had sunk evidence: ledgers and correspondence proving he had embezzled from the barge cooperative he managed, ruined three families, and let a friend take the blame. [453] The treasure story was the roof he had built over that pit. [465] Tell people you lost a fortune and they pity you; tell them you stole one and they leave. [483]

Mara sat very still and felt her whole childhood reorganize itself. [494] This was the hindsight clarity she had chased through eleven years and a hundred dead legends: the moment when the past stopped being a story and became a structure she could finally walk through with the lights on. [532] His bitterness had not been about lost money. [540] It had been the rent he paid to live inside his own lie. [553] Even the sundial made sense now. [559] He had carried it because a sundial keeps no record; it casts a shadow, the shadow moves on, and nothing is written down. [582] A clock accumulates; a sundial forgives. [588] She understood, too, why her instincts had needed training rather than luck. [600] Lies are built by craft, and they are unbuilt the same way, joint by patient joint. [616]

The radio's last program ended, and the dead air arrived like snowfall. [628] In that silence, with the canal going pewter under the first gray light, Mara made her decision. [645] The blamed friend had a granddaughter still living two villages over; Mara had interviewed her once, chasing the legend, and had recorded the careful way the woman said the word thief. [676] She dried the letters carefully, packed them in her satchel, and dismantled the rig. [690] What she had salvaged from the canal was not her grandfather's secret; it was someone else's good name, sixty years overdue. [711]

Before she left, she took out the pocket sundial and set it on the lock gate, facing east. [729] When the sun cleared the reeds, the gnomon threw its first thin shadow, and for once the old instrument told the truth: morning, plainly, with nothing hidden in it. [758] She left it there, a small brass confession, and walked toward the village with the letters and the lightness of a building finally condemned. [782]
