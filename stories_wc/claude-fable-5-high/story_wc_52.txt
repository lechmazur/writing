Marisol Quaye had learned that grief, like a lion, could not be tamed, only contained. [15] Her husband Dario had taught her that much before the heart attack took him mid-performance, here and gone between one crack of the whip and the next. [42] Three years on, she lived above a shuttered watch shop, in a loft where rain beaded across the skylight like seconds refusing to fall. [66] She was a hauntingly practical woman; she had sold the trailer, the costumes, even the brass cage bolts. [84] But she had kept the whip stocks, lengths of seasoned ash that Dario had trusted with his life. [102] Each month she sawed one down into a carving blank, square and pale as a held breath. [119] Tonight the last blank waited on her bench beneath the wet glass sky. [132] She had a rule, the kind that keeps a widow sane: she carved only while the kettle boiled. [150] From flame to whistle was seven minutes, and grief was permitted exactly that long to come out of its cage. [170] Any longer and it would maul her; any shorter and it would starve and grow strange. [186] She filled the copper kettle, set it on the ring, and struck the match. [200] Rust-colored light from the streetlamp climbed through the skylight and lay across the bench like an old cat. [218] She took up the knife. [223] Eleven blanks had become eleven lions already, each one carrying a lesson she had cut free from the wreckage. [242] The first lion crouched: never turn your back on what you love. [254] The fourth lion slept: rest is not surrender. [262] The ninth lion roared at nothing: some pain is only noise. [273] This was her harvest, the only crop loss had ever offered her, and she refused to let it rot in the field. [295] The twelfth lion would be different. [301] Beside the blank sat a drop of amber, a bead Dario had worn on a cord at his throat for thirty years of shows. [325] Resin that had wept from some ancient tree and hardened around a single trapped mayfly: crystallized time, he had called it, a creature here and gone, yet held forever mid-flight. [355] Tonight she would set it as the lion's eye. [364] The kettle began its low murmur, and she cut. [373] The ash gave way in clean curls, the grain running straight and honest, the way Dario's voice had run when he called the cats by name. [399] She found the haunch first, then the spine, then the great blunt head, lifted as if listening. [416] Rain ticked against the skylight, soft applause from an audience of weather. [428] Her hands remembered him: the chalk smell, the scar on his thumb, the way he said her name like the end of a trick. [452] The murmur deepened toward a boil, and so did she. [462] Tears came; she let them, because the rule contained them too. [473] Seven minutes was a cage with the door propped open, and what entered freely could leave the same way. [492] With the small gouge she hollowed the eye socket and pressed the amber home. [506] It seated with a click, and the lamplight caught it, and suddenly the lion was looking at her. [524] Inside that golden eye, the mayfly hung in its eternal instant, and she understood the twelfth lesson at last. [543] Everything is here and gone; the holding is the only part we are given to keep. [559] Dario had not held the lions still; he had held their attention, moment by moment, and called it a life. [579] She could do the same with sorrow. [586] The kettle screamed. [589] She set down the knife mid-stroke, as the rule demanded, and turned off the flame. [604] Steam rose and bloomed against the cold skylight, fogging the beads of rain into one soft silver field. [622] She made the tea in Dario's chipped cup, because using it was more practical than enshrining it, and kinder besides. [642] Then she placed the unfinished twelfth lion among its pride on the windowsill. [655] It would stay rough another month; some lessons should not be rushed to a polish. [670] She drank her tea in the rust-hued lamplight, in a peace the color of old iron, weathered but load-bearing. [689] Tomorrow there would be errands, ledgers, the watch shop's landlord to argue with. [702] Tonight there was only the rain, the twelve lions, and the amber eye glowing in the dark. [719] Here, she thought, touching the warm cup. [726] Gone, she thought, and was not afraid of the word anymore. [737] The mayfly held its instant; the widow held hers; the rain held its beads against the glass, each one a small clear cage with the whole streetlamp's light contained inside. [767] It was enough.
