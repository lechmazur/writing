The tide gauge at Station Forty-One hums all night, and Mara has learned to hum with it. [17] Twelve years after the fossil fuel ban, the sea has not stopped rising; it has only stopped accelerating, like a runner easing into a long, patient stride. [44] Her job is to watch it do so, one millimeter at a time. [57] She is good at watching; it was her first profession. [67] At nineteen she was certified as a coral bleaching witness, one of the divers paid by the tribunal to document the reef's last colors under oath. [93] Her testimony helped pass the ban, which is the kind of victory that arrives too late to feel like one. [113]

On the desk beside the gauge readouts lies a single page torn from her mother's atlas, page forty-seven, soft as cloth from handling. [136] It shows this coast as it was: the headland, the village, and a dotted line called the Pilgrim's Stair, a footpath that ran along the shore to the chapel rock. [166] The path is gone now, though paths on either side of it, lower paths, older paths, still surface at spring low tide like the spines of sleeping animals. [194] This is the question that moored her here: why do some paths disappear while their neighbors endure? [211] The sea is not so simple as a bathtub filling; she knows that better than anyone on this coast. [230]

While she thinks, she taps. [235] Her mother taught her the practice: tide tables turned into rhythms, drummed on tabletops, railings, the hulls of boats. [254] Two quick taps for the flood, a long pause for slack, three slow for the ebb, and the whole pattern shifting night by night with the moon. [281] Fishermen here had carried tide knowledge in their knuckles for generations, a rhythm that was periodically eternal: never the same twice, never anything new. [305] Mara taps the station's metal counter and listens to the gauge answer in its own slow pulse. [322]

Through the winter she begins to assemble an answer the way her mother assembled tides: fragment by fragment. [340] She gathers wave-energy logs from the station archive, parish records of storm damage, her own witness depositions from the bleaching years, and the torn page with its dotted line. [369] She spreads them across the floor of the monitoring station and walks among them in her socks, tapping the flood rhythm on her thigh. [393] For weeks the pieces refuse each other. [400] The sea level data says the water rose evenly; the Pilgrim's Stair should have survived as long as its neighbors. [420]

Then, on a night of hard rain, her finger stops mid-rhythm over a wave-energy log from twenty-six years ago. [439] The wave heights at the headland doubled in a single season, long before any storm worth the name. [457] She checks the date against her depositions and feels the floor tilt. [469] It is the season she testified to: the summer the fringing reef off chapel rock bleached white and died. [488] The reef had been the path's breakwater. [495] Alive, it broke the swell a quarter mile out; dead, it crumbled within a decade, and the full weight of the ocean came ashore and chewed the Pilgrim's Stair to nothing. [526] Paths do not drown, she understands at last; they are abandoned by their guardians first. [541] The answer had been in her own testimony all along, sealed in tribunal files, waiting twenty-six years for her to read it beside a map. [566]

And the page itself was a second delayed message. [575] She turns it over and finds, faint in pencil, her mother's hand: a date, the same drowned summer, and one line, walked it the last time today. [602] Her mother had torn out the page not to forget the path but to keep it, the way you keep what you have already given up. [628] She had known, somehow, before the science did. [636]

In spring, Mara files a report recommending reef restoration as coastal defense, citing her own old testimony as evidence. [655] The tribunal that once heard her describe dying coral now funds nurseries of it, heat-hardened, planted along the headland where the breakwater used to live. [680] It will take longer than she has. [687] She stays anyway, moored to the station, drifting through its slow nights, recording millimeters. [701] On the counter, under glass now, lies page forty-seven, dotted line up, pencil note down, both true at once. [720] Some evenings, when the gauge hums and the rain comes in off the water, she taps the old ebb rhythm on the glass. [743] Never the same twice; never anything new. [750] Somewhere offshore, in the dark, small polyps are assembling a path back. [762]
