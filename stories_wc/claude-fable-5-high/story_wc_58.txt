The last ship sailed at dusk, and Mirei watched it go from the harbor wall, her satchel of unfinished obituaries heavy against her hip. [24] She wrote them for the living, not the dead: portraits of people who had left a place, composed for those who stayed behind. [47] Absence, she believed, was a wound that wanted explaining, and she had crossed three seas to learn how places healed. [67] This island, with its emptied palace and its winter of no ships, would be her last lesson or her longest. [87]

The palace gardens had gone to shadow by October, hedges grown into dark corridors, fountains holding leaves instead of water. [107] It was there she found Ondine, the last weaver of the court, kneeling at a frame loom beneath a bronze-leaved beech. [128] In her fingers ran a single weft thread, crimson, pulled from the great carpet of the throne room. [146] "When someone leaves the island," Ondine said without looking up, "I take one thread from the palace carpet and weave it into this." [169] The cloth on her loom was a map: harbors of blue, mountains of undyed wool, and dozens of bright threads running outward like roads. [193] "The carpet thins, but nothing vanishes," she said. [201] "It only travels." [204]

Mirei understood at once that they practiced the same craft in different materials. [217] Her obituaries did not mourn; they explained. [224] When a son left a village, she would sit with his mother and write him so vividly -- the salt smell of his coat, the way he hummed while mending nets -- that his absence became a shape the family could live around. [265] An explained absence, she had learned, scars cleanly. [273] An unexplained one festers. [277]

Through November they worked together in the garden, wrapped in wool against the deepening cold. [292] The islanders came one by one along the leaf-drowned paths, each carrying a name. [306] A fisherman whose brother had sailed on the last ship. [316] A girl whose mother had gone to the mainland hospitals and never written. [329] Mirei listened, then wrote, and Ondine wove the corresponding thread into her map while the words were still wet. [348] It was intimate work, closer than confession. [355] To write someone truly, Mirei had to hold their life in her hands the way Ondine held the thread: gently, and without flinching. [378]

Each evening she read the day's obituary aloud beneath the beech, and something strange happened in the listening. [396] The absent seemed to gather in the shadows between the hedges. [407] Her words projected their presence so completely that the fisherman swore he heard his brother laugh, and the girl smelled her mother's lavender soap on the wind. [434] This was the method she had refined across three seas: not resurrection, but presence projected through language, the missing made vivid enough to keep company with. [460] There was splendor in those evenings, and loneliness threaded through the splendor like the crimson weft. [476] The garden in its decay was magnificent: gold leaves on black water, the palace windows catching the last copper light, two women working in a kingdom of the gone. [505]

One frost-bright morning, Mirei asked the question she had carried across the world. [518] "Does it work?" [521] "Does a place ever heal?" [526] Ondine set down her shuttle and unrolled the woven map across the cold stone bench. [541] The bright threads did not simply leave the island; they curved, crossed, doubled back. [555] "Healing has a geography," Ondine said. [561] "It is not a closing of the wound." [569] "It is the building of roads to and from it." [579] She traced one crimson line that left the harbor, wandered the map's far edge, and returned. [595] "My daughter," she said. [599] "Gone eleven years." [602] "She came home last spring because someone had written to her -- a stranger's letter describing her mother so vividly that the distance became unbearable." [626]

Mirei remembered the letter. [630] She had written it in a mainland port two springs ago, for a homesick weaver's daughter who could not say why she had stayed away. [655] She had described a garden, a loom, a mother's patient hands, never knowing the island existed. [671] Her own road, it seemed, had been on this map all along. [683]

When the first ship of spring finally came, Mirei did not board it. [696] Instead she sat in the waking garden and wrote, at last, her own obituary for the living: a woman who had wandered three seas explaining other people's absences because no one had ever explained her own. [732] She sent it to the village she had fled as a girl, folded around a single crimson thread. [750] Then she took up a pen and waited beneath the beech, present, vivid, and at last a fixed point on somebody's map. [772]
