Marisol Vance kept two catalogs in the town of Hollow Creek: one of sheet music, and one of grudges. [19] As the choral librarian, she shelved hymns in German, Czech, and Spanish, the three languages that had built the town and then stopped speaking to each other. [46] The grudges she could not shelve; they sat out in the open, tall as the three grain silos at the edge of the fields. [70] Each family faction filled its own silo, sold its own grain, and let the shared astronomical clock tower at the town square go dark and silent. [96] The tower had been the town's one act of common faith, its painted dial tracking moon, planets, and harvest stars. [116] When the feud over water rights split the families twenty years ago, someone stole the drive weight from the mechanism, and time in Hollow Creek simply stopped being shared. [145]

Marisol decided, with the empathy of a woman who had sung beside all three factions at funerals, that this would end. [166] She was gentle about people and ruthless about plans. [175] She announced the grain elevator talks: meetings held in the old cooperative elevator, the only structure all three families had once owned together. [198] Neutral ground, she said, smells like everyone's wheat. [206]

The first talk nearly collapsed in twenty minutes. [214] Old Brauer would speak only German when he was angry; Mrs. Kovar answered in Czech; the Ortega brothers muttered in Spanish, and every translation was a fresh insult. [242] So Marisol did what choral librarians do: she gave them a score. [254] She wrote the talks like a motet, each agenda item set down in all three languages, lines interleaved so that no one could read their own tongue without their eyes crossing a neighbor's. [287] When voices rose, she made them sing the disputed sentence, German verse, Czech response, Spanish refrain, until the words wore smooth. [308] It was absurd, and it worked, because nobody can harmonize and hate with full force at the same time. [327]

By the fourth talk, she named her real ambition. [336] She wanted to rehabilitate the astronomical clock tower for the harvest centennial, to make the town keep one time again. [356] The room went still as a held breath. [364] Then Old Brauer stood, left, and came back carrying a burlap sack. [376] Inside was a cuckoo clock weight, a cast-iron pinecone from his grandmother's Black Forest clock. [391] My father took the tower's weight the night of the split, he said, and melted it down for shame. [410] This is the closest thing I have to give back. [420]

Marisol weighed the pinecone in her palm and made her decision in the space of a downbeat. [437] Too light alone, she said, but not if every family adds iron of their own. [452] Mrs. Kovar surrendered a hinge from the gate she had locked against the Brauers; the Ortegas brought a broken plowshare from the disputed field itself. [477] The blacksmith recast it all around the pinecone's core: one weight, three grievances, melted into ballast. [493]

They installed it at night, because Marisol insisted the dial's first turning should be witnessed by the stars it was built to track. [516] Under the hush of borrowed starlight, light that had left its sources before any of their grandparents quarreled, three families climbed the tower stairs together for the first time in twenty years. [548] Old Brauer hooked the weight to its chain with hands that shook. [560] Mrs. Kovar released the escapement. [565] The eldest Ortega pushed the moon hand to its true position, checking it against the actual moon. [582] The mechanism gathered itself like a choir before the conductor's hands fall, and then it sang: tick, answer, tick, answer. [602] Below, in the square, the rest of the town heard time return and cheered in three languages at once, the words tangling together until nobody could say where one tongue ended and another began. [636]

Marisol stood at the tower window and looked toward the three silos on the dark horizon. [652] Next year, she told them, we build the connecting gallery the cooperative drew up in 1924, catwalks between the silos, so grain can move where it is needed and no harvest fails alone. [685] Bridges from silos, Old Brauer said, tasting the phrase in English, then German, then, haltingly, in Czech, offering it to Mrs. Kovar like a hand. [710] She took it. [713]

The grain elevator talks continued every month after that, fierce and warm by turns, argued in three braided languages under one shared clock. [736] And somewhere in its mechanism, a cast-iron pinecone wrapped in old grievances kept perfect time, pulling steadily downward so that everything above it could turn. [761]
