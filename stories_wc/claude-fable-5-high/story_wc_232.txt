Mara Voss sold antique maps from a narrow shop that smelled of cedar and forgotten rivers. [16] She was lonely in the particular way of people who trade in other lives' journeys while never taking one. [35] Her grandfather had been a typesetter, and his last gift was a brass type foundry matrix chip, a small mold engraved with a glyph no font ever used. [63] With it came a lingering promise, spoken on his last midsummer: I will show you the doorway of decisions, the place where I chose my whole life. [90] He died in autumn, and the promise stayed unkept, a splinter under her years. [104]

Mara was empirically intuitive; she trusted hunches only after they survived evidence. [116] So when she noticed the glyph on the matrix matched a blank notch on her grandfather's hand-drawn survey of Hollow Creek, she did not dismiss it. [142] She tested it: laid the chip over the notch, measured the scale, traced the contour lines three times. [160] Each time the glyph aligned with a fold in the limestone hills where the map simply stopped. [177] Hypothesis confirmed enough, she closed the shop on the solstice and drove out. [190]

Midsummer rain had been falling for two days, and the creek was rising, brown and impatient against its banks. [209] The map's last marked path ended at a sycamore; beyond it, her grandfather had drawn nothing but a single ant, inked small as a comma. [234] She had always taken it for a flaw in the paper. [245] Standing under the dripping sycamore, she saw the real thing: a line of harvester ants carrying white petals along a seam of stone, walking out of the rain. [273] Ants do not forage in downpours unless home is close and dry. [285] It was the kind of fact her grandfather collected like type. [296] So she followed the ants home. [302]

The line threaded through fern and root, down a slope where the rising creek already licked at her boots, and vanished into a crack barely wider than her shoulders. [331] She hesitated; the water would rise faster than she could climb back if she lingered. [346] Then she thought of forty more years of selling other people's journeys, and slid inside. [361]

The passage opened into a cavern that should have been dark and was not. [375] A sinkhole far above let down a column of stormlight, and around it the cavern was blooming. [392] Moonflowers and pale wood sorrel had seeded in the fallen soil, opening at midsummer when light reached deepest, their petals the same white the ants carried. [418] Across the chamber, two limestone pillars framed a gap shaped unmistakably like a doorway. [432] Carved into the lintel, in her grandfather's careful letterforms, were the words: HERE I DECIDED. [447] Below them sat a row of square sockets, each holding a brass matrix, except one. [462] Her hands shook as she pressed her chip into the empty socket. [474] It fit with the soft, final click of type locking into a chase. [487] The completed line read: HERE I DECIDED TO LIVE. [496]

Behind the doorway lay no treasure, only a small dry chamber and, wedged in a niche, a tin box. [515] Inside was a letter, ink faded but legible. [523] Mara, it began, I found this place the summer I meant to leave town forever. [538] I followed ants out of a storm and stood where you stand, and I understood that every doorway is a decision wearing stone. [561] I stayed, and I had your mother, and I had you. [572] I promised to bring you here because maps can only show where others went. [586] If you are reading this, you found it the way truth is found: by testing the small evidence the world leaves. [607] Now decide something of your own. [613]

Water murmured at the cavern's mouth; the creek was still rising, marking time the way only water can. [631] Mara stood in the column of light among the blooms and let herself feel the question she had avoided for years. [652] Around her, petals seemed to glimmer as raindrops fell through the sinkhole and caught the storm's gray shine. [670] She did not believe in signs, but she believed in data, and the data of her life was clear: the shop was a museum of other people's courage. [698] She would keep the shop, but she would no longer hide in it. [711] She would walk the blank places on her grandfather's maps and ink them in herself. [726] The decision settled into her like a matrix into its socket, soft and final. [740] She tucked the letter against her heart and climbed out ahead of the water. [754] Behind her, the ants kept carrying their petals home, and the doorway stood in the blooming dark, its promise finally kept. [775]
