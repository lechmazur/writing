Wren was a jittery relic courier, the kind who counted her own footsteps and apologized to milestones. [17] Twice a month she walked the aurora-lit ridgeway between Hollow Fen and the mountain shrines, carrying relics in a padded satchel that never quite stopped her hands from shaking. [46] The shaking was not fear, exactly; it was attention with nowhere to go. [59] Her mentor, Old Sayel, had given her that diagnosis along with the dragonfly-wing pendant, then vanished into the high passes before she could ask what either was for. [87] The pendant was a sliver of true wing pressed in resin, and it did one strange thing: when a relic in her satchel still held a living story, the wing trembled, vibrating sympathetically, a hum she felt in her sternum like a second pulse. [131] Dead relics stayed silent. [135] Most relics, Sayel had warned, die not from age but from neglect; a story unspoken too long simply stops. [154]

So Wren developed a habit that made her employers sigh: she extended every brief encounter on the road. [172] A shepherd asking the time got the time plus the tale of the bell-shard she carried, which had once rung a flood warning that saved his grandmother's village. [200] A tax rider who wanted only directions left with directions and the knowledge that the comb in her satchel had untangled a queen's hair on the morning of a surrender. [230] It slowed her deliveries terribly. [235] It also kept the pendant humming. [241] She told herself she did it for the relics, but the truth was simpler: she wanted to coax wonder from the routine, to make a damp Tuesday on a windy ridge feel like the middle of an epic, because Sayel had done that for her once and then was gone. [291]

Years moved the way the aurora did, in slow green folds. [302] Wren watched Sayel fade across the long arc from memory to myth. [314] At first, travelers remembered the old courier herself: her limp, her terrible singing, the way she paid for ale with riddles. [335] A decade on, they remembered only a wandering keeper of stories, no name, taller in each telling. [352] By the time gray threaded Wren's braid, children on the ridgeway spoke of the Lantern Woman who had hung the aurora itself as a signal fire and would someday return for it. [384] Wren never corrected them. [388] She noticed something instead. [392] Whenever a child told the Lantern Woman story in her hearing, the pendant shivered against her collarbone, faint but unmistakable. [412] Sayel had become a relic herself, and the story was still alive. [424]

Then came the winter delivery that nearly broke her habit. [434] The shrine wardens sent down a sealed box marked urgent, and the pendant lay dead silent against her chest the whole climb. [456] Inside, when she finally broke her own rule and looked, was a seed: dark, fist-sized, ancient, labeled in a hand she knew at once. [480] Sayel's writing read: It will not germinate in soil. [489] It germinates in telling. [493] Carry it until it hums. [498]

Wren stood on the ridgeway under a ceiling of green fire and understood the whole shape of her life at once. [519] The shaking hands, the pendant, the maddening habit of stretching every encounter past politeness: Sayel had not abandoned her. [538] Sayel had planted her. [542]

So Wren carried the seed for three more years, and she told its story to everyone she stopped. [560] She told tinkers and soldiers and a bride running from her own wedding that she carried the last seed of the lantern trees, whose blossoms had once burned cold and bright enough to write the aurora across the sky. [599] She did not know if any of it was true. [609] She knew that every telling made the pendant tremble a little longer, the seed warming under her palm like a sleeper turning toward morning. [633]

On a night when the aurora hung so low it seemed snagged on the cairns, a boy stopped her and asked, jittering himself, whether she was the Lantern Woman. [662] Wren opened her mouth to say no and felt the pendant sing harder than it ever had, wing and seed vibrating in sympathy, two notes finding each other across the dark. [693] So she said yes. [697] She knelt, pressed the humming seed into the frost-hard earth, and told the boy the whole story while a pale shoot rose between them, glowing faintly green, drinking the telling like rain. [729] Somewhere ahead of her, in memory or in myth, she could almost hear Sayel singing terribly along. [746] She handed the boy her satchel and walked on, hands finally steady, toward the high passes. [762]
