Mira Voss had drawn eleven thousand faces, and she remembered every one. [12] The gift came uninvited: a memory that filed away cheekbones and crow's feet the way other people filed away grudges. [32] She spent it cheaply, sketching tourists in the Lumen Caverns for twelve dollars apiece, her fingers permanently charcoal-gray to the second knuckle. [54] The caverns were her grandmother's discovery, a subterranean crystal cave where quartz columns caught lantern light and broke it into slow, drifting color, like an aurora trapped underground. [82] Tourists laughed at the colors. [87] Mira waited every evening for the moment after the last echo of laughter faded, when the cave went silent and the light kept moving anyway, beautiful and grieving at once. [117] That was when she felt it: the pull toward real work, toward color, toward the painting she had promised herself she would never attempt. [141]

Grandmother Ila had attempted it. [146] She had been a painter once, before she became a cave guide, before she became practical. [162] Her single surviving canvas hung in the cavern's ticket office: a dull landscape, brown hills, a sky like dishwater. [181] Mira had always wondered why a woman who lived beneath an underground aurora would paint something so deliberately dead. [200] The answer came on the anniversary of Ila's death, when Mira stayed late to rehang the crooked frame and felt the canvas shift wrong, heavy at the back. [228] Behind the painting, set into the wall, was a small steel door with a combination lock. [244] The lock was broken. [248] Its dial spun loose and useless, the mechanism long since snapped, and the door had been held shut by nothing but the painting's weight and forty years of nobody looking. [278] Mira understood the joke immediately, and it hurt. [286] Ila had not really locked anything away. [293] She had only hung dullness in front of it and trusted the world not to check. [309]

Inside the recess lay rolled canvases, a wooden box of pigments ground from cave minerals, and a letter in handwriting Mira's memory matched instantly to the grocery lists of her childhood. [340] The canvases were unfinished attempts at the cave itself, each one abandoned mid-stroke, each one already better than anything in the ticket office. [363] The letter said: I told myself the calling could be locked up like money. [377] It cannot. [379] The lock broke the first week and I left it broken, because some part of me knew the truth and was ashamed to say it plainly. [405] If you have found this, you are already too late to save me and exactly on time to rectify what I did to us both. [430]

Mira read the letter three times, though once would have fixed it in her forever. [445] She thought of her own suppressions: the twelve-dollar sketches, the way she drew only what sat in front of her, never what burned behind her eyes. [471] Eleven thousand faces, and she had memorized every one precisely so she would not have to imagine a single thing. [491] Memory had been her hiding place the way the dull landscape had been Ila's. [505]

She carried the pigments down into the cavern that night. [515] The tour groups were gone; she waited anyway, by old habit, for the last echo of laughter to fade up the entrance stairs and die. [540] Then the silence arrived, and with it the light: violet sliding into green along the quartz, a sorrowing radiance, the glow of something magnificent that had been waiting underground a very long time to be witnessed. [576] She stretched Ila's largest unfinished canvas against a flat stone. [586] Her charcoal-blackened fingers, so long trained to copy, trembled when she opened the pigment box and touched real color for the first time in years. [611] She did not paint the cave as it appeared. [620] She painted it as her memory and her grief together insisted it was: the aurora light, and inside the light, faintly, her grandmother's face, reconstructed perfectly from ten thousand stored glances, looking out at last with no dull landscape in front of her. [663]

She worked until her hands forgot their fee schedule. [672] Near dawn she carried the broken lock up from the office and set it on the stone beside her like a relic. [694] It had never worked, and that was the whole inheritance: callings do not stay shut. [709] You can only hang something boring in front of them and hope, and hope fails. [724] When the painting was finished, Mira hung it in the ticket office where the dishwater landscape had been, the steel door behind it standing open, empty, hiding nothing now. [753] Tourists would ask who painted it. [759] She would tell them, and then she would tell them about Ila, and the cave's lit silence would feel a little less like mourning each time, though never entirely; some sorrows are meant to glow, not fade.
