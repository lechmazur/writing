Wren Calloway carved gravestones the way her teacher had taught her: slowly, listening, as if the granite were confessing. [19] Since the ferry sank in October, taking eleven of Harrow's people, the town had asked her for one stone to hold them all. [42] She had refused twice, because she did not yet know the shape of collective grief, and she would not carve a lie. [64] Then the monarchs arrived. [68] Every autumn they funneled through Harrow on their migration south, and every autumn they roosted overnight in the ancient greenhouse behind the church, a ruin of green glass and iron ribs older than any grave Wren had cut. [106] At dusk on the first day of the migration, she went there to think, and found Aldous Pike sitting on the potting bench, dead four years and patient as ever. [136] He looked the way teachers look in memory: dusty apron, chalk-white hands, eyes like a level held to the horizon. [156] "You refused the commission," he said. [162] "I refused to fake it," she answered, and was surprised that her voice did not shake. [178] Talking to the dead felt, in that humid dark, no stranger than talking to stone. [193] "Good," Aldous said. [196] "Now learn it honestly. [200] You have until the butterflies leave." [206] He nodded at her satchel, where his one bequest to her rode everywhere: a woven ribbon roll, soft cloth tape striped in faded colors, the measure he had used because cloth never scratched a finished face. [242] "Eleven households," he said. [246] "Measure them. [248] Retrace the dead through the living, one dinner at a time." [259] Wren's own family had kept one custom her whole life: at dinner, each person traded a favorite poem before anyone touched the food. [282] It was the only door she knew how to knock on. [293] So across the butterfly migration, while the greenhouse filled nightly with folded orange wings, she ate at eleven tables. [312] She brought bread and her family's custom, offering a poem and asking for the drowned one's favorite in return. [331] The Marsh widow recited her husband's sea ballad with her eyes shut. [343] The Okafor boys shouted their mother's nonsense rhyme and then could not stop laughing, and then could not stop crying, and neither thing was wrong. [368] Old Teller Brandt, who had lost both daughters, traded her a psalm so quietly the candles seemed to lean in. [388] After each dinner Wren unrolled the woven ribbon and tied a knot at the length of the poem's longest breath, the place where the speaker had to stop and survive the line. [420] Eleven dinners, eleven knots, unevenly spaced, like a tide line. [430] Each dusk she returned to the greenhouse and reported to Aldous, who never touched anything, who flickered when the wind pushed through the broken panes. [455] "Grief is not a portrait," he told her on the ninth night. [467] "It is an interval. [471] You are measuring silences." [475] Her work in the shed those weeks became fiercely meditative: hours of breath and mallet, the same blow retraced until her arm forgot it was an arm. [502] Outside, storms came off the water all month, and Wren learned to carve inside them, a thundering calm, the sky raging while her hands stayed level as a sleeping lake. [532] On the last night of the migration, the monarchs hung from the greenhouse iron in shuddering curtains, ten thousand small griefs holding one another up. [557] Wren stretched the knotted ribbon along a beam and finally saw it. [569] The knots did not mark eleven separate sorrows; held taut, they made one rhythm, irregular but continuous, like wingbeats, like a town breathing. [592] "There is your shape," Aldous said, fainter now, his edges going the color of dusk. [607] "Not a wall of names. [612] A line that pauses where the living pause, and goes on." [623] She understood then why he had stayed: mentorship does not end at the grave; it only changes which side of the chisel it stands on. [648] "Trade me one," he said, "for the road." [656] So she gave him her favorite poem, the one about light persisting in cold rooms, and he gave her his, four plain lines about water finding its level, and that was their dinner, eaten on air. [692] At dawn the butterflies lifted all at once, and Aldous went with them, not upward exactly, just elsewhere. [710] Wren carved the memorial through the winter: a long granite ribbon, rising and dipping, knotted in eleven carved folds, each fold cradling a name and one borrowed line of verse. [740] The town came on the unveiling day and read the lines aloud to one another, trading poems over bread, strangers becoming a family by custom. [765] And every autumn after, when the monarchs returned to the greenhouse, Wren went at dusk to retrace the ribbon's folds with her thumb, knot by knot, and found the stone warm. [796]
