Vess had been engineered to glow, which made her skepticism a public inconvenience. [13] People trusted luminous folk; the old gene-lines had been built for honesty work, lit from within so lies dimmed the skin. [34] But Vess doubted everything, brightly. [39] She especially doubted the weather-carriers. [44] Everyone had seen them: the woman trailed by her own small rain, the boy whose shoulders frosted in July. [63] The meteorological guilds called it personal climate; Vess called it unexamined coincidence, and she had come north to prove it. [83]

She timed her expedition for election week, when the forests voted. [94] Every seventh autumn the root networks polled themselves, chemical ballots flowing through mycorrhizal channels, deciding which species would be permitted to spread and which groves would be let go to meadow. [125] During elections, the carriers always converged on the lavender bluff above Halse Bay, and nobody could say why. [143]

Vess climbed it at dusk, the aurora already unspooling overhead in green ribbons that her grandmother would have called terrestrially cosmic -- sky weather wearing earth colors. [169] Her grandmother had been a carrier, supposedly. [176] Vess remembered childhood summers under a porch awning while a private drizzle followed the old woman through drought years, and she remembered deciding, at nine, that memory was not evidence. [206] Still, she wore the inheritance: a tin compass ring, cheap and dull, its needle painted on, pointing nowhere. [224] Tin does not magnetize; that was the point, her grandmother had said. [236] "A compass that points at nothing has to learn to listen." [247] Nonsense, Vess had concluded, beautifully phrased. [253]

The lavender grew thick on the bluff, silvered by auroral light, and the carriers were already arriving. [270] She counted eleven of them, each haloed by a different sky: a man in his own warm fog, twins sharing a pocket of summer stillness, an old pilot under a single revolving cloud. [303] Vess unpacked her sensors and found every reading ordinary. [312]

Then her mouth changed. [316] Her honesty-line mods had come with an obsolete feature: chemoreceptors that translated air chemistry into taste, a navigator's trick from before satellites were rebuilt. [340] She tasted copper, then cold mint, then something like her grandmother's kitchen -- woodsmoke and pear skins. [356] Each carrier who passed her shifted the flavor of the air, and the flavors were not random. [373] They sharpened in one direction, the way a word sharpens toward meaning. [385]

She walked the bluff with her eyes closed, tongue parted to the wind, following the gradient of taste like a needle following north. [408] The carriers were doing the same, she realized -- drifting, tasting, correcting. [419] No one of them knew the way. [426] The direction existed only between them, assembled from twelve small weathers the way the forests' decisions were assembled from a billion root-hairs: an emergent compass, pointing at nothing until enough of them listened together. [460]

Vess opened her eyes and saw her own light reflected in the lavender, pulsing in rhythm with the aurora. [479] Around her shoulders, faint as breath, hung a glowing haze of her own -- not rain, not frost, but light: weather made of luminescence. [502] She had carried it her whole life and called it engineering. [513]

The old pilot drifted near, his cloud turning slowly. [522] "First election?" he asked. [526] "I came to debunk you," she said, because her skin would have dimmed at anything less true. [543] He laughed, tasting the air the way she now understood everyone here tasted it. [557] "The forests can't sense the upper sky," he said, "roots vote on soil and rain, but they can't feel the aurora seasons coming." [580] "So we converge, and what we carry tells them." [589] "Our weathers blend into one reading, and the roots under this bluff take the count down into the ballots." [608]

Carriers were not anomalies, then; they were instruments. [616] Sensors the planet had grown the way it grew everything: slowly, locally, out of bodies that did not understand themselves. [636] Her grandmother had known, or guessed; why else give a skeptic a compass that refuses to point? [653]

Vess turned the tin ring on her finger and tasted, beneath the copper and mint, a slow sweetness rising from the ground itself -- the bluff acknowledging the count, the election absorbing twelve weathers into its long, vegetable arithmetic. [691] Her skin held its light, steady and bright. [699] She believed it, all of it, and the believing felt less like surrender than like an old machine, long unplugged, warming up again. [722]

She stayed until the aurora paled, learning names. [730] When she finally walked down through the lavender, her small bright weather went with her, and the painted needle of the tin ring seemed, in the dawn, to be pointing exactly where she was going. [765]
