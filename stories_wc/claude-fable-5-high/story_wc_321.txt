Mara Voss had worked the Harbor Line ferry for nine years, and she still ran the mooring lines like a prayer. [21] She was an ardent deckhand in a way that embarrassed the other crew, who treated the crossing as a job and not a calling. [45] Then, on a Tuesday in late October, the 6:14 commuter ferry vanished between the breakwater and the channel buoy, taking forty-one passengers and her sister Lena with it. [73] The water never gave back so much as a life vest. [84] Everyone else grieved and moved inland. [90] Mara stayed, resiliently positive in a way people mistook for denial, because she had found something in Lena's locker: an empty hourglass, both bulbs dry, the sand simply gone. [119] Sand does not evaporate. [123] Time, Mara decided, had been spent somewhere else. [131]

She kept her job on the surviving boat and began to listen. [143] Every night after the last run, she would radio across the dead channels, calling the vanished ferry's sign into the static: Harbor Six, Harbor Six, this is the Esther May, do you copy. [176] For months, nothing. [179] Then, in February, an answer arrived in her own voice, half a second delayed, saying words she had not said. [199]

The other Mara explained it in fragments between squalls of interference. [210] The 6:14 had not sunk; it had slipped sideways into a parallel crossing, a harbor almost identical, where the passengers disembarked each morning into lives that almost fit. [238] In that world, it was the Esther May that had vanished, with another Mara's sister aboard. [254] Two timelines, each missing what the other held. [262] The hourglass, the other Mara said, was the hinge. [271] Its sand had been split between worlds when the ferry crossed over, and until the two halves rejoined, both harbors would keep bleeding into each other, losing boats, losing people. [301]

So Mara studied the harbor the way Lena once had, hunting the breach via interconnected patterns: tide tables laid over radio static, gull migrations laid over the timetable of disappearances, star charts laid over the wakes of departing boats. [340] Alone, each chart was noise. [345] Overlaid, they converged like spokes on a single point beneath the northern cliffs, and a single hour: the moment the spring tide stalled, when the second hands of every clock aboard quivered and swung horizontal, running sideways instead of forward. [385]

On that night she took the harbor skiff out alone, the empty hourglass zipped inside her jacket. [402] The cliff face opened where her charts said it would, into a grotto she had passed a thousand times without seeing. [423] Inside, the water glowed faintly, and the ceiling had worn away to bare sky, so that starlight fell straight down through the stone and lay on the surface like frost. [453] Everything in the spectral grotto was doubled: two reflections of her skiff, two shadows of her hands, two of every star. [474] And gliding toward her out of the dark came the 6:14, hull translucent, windows full of faces she knew. [493] Lena stood at the rail beside the other Mara, holding the hourglass's twin, its lower bulb heavy with sand. [512]

No one needed to explain the work. [519] Mara unscrewed her empty glass; her double poured; the sand fell between worlds and somehow rose, filling both bulbs at once, as if it had never been divided. [547] The doubled stars slid together overhead. [553] The two reflections of her skiff merged into one. [562] The ferry's hull thickened into solid steel, and forty-one passengers blinked in the starlight like commuters waking past their stop. [582]

Lena gripped her shoulders on the dock at dawn and asked how she had never given up. [599] "Giving up was never on the timetable," Mara said, and meant it. [611] But the merging had a price the other Mara had warned her of, gently, over the radio. [628] Two timelines stitched into one cannot hold two of the same soul ashore. [641] One Mara could land; the other had to keep crossing. [651] She did not flip a coin. [657] She simply looked at Lena, safe on solid ground, and stepped back aboard the Esther May. [673]

Now she works the harbor still, in every weather, the hourglass full and lashed beside the wheel. [690] She cannot stay ashore longer than a turning of its sand; the merged world tolerates her only in motion, a permanent passenger of her own crossing. [716] Commuters know her face and never quite remember her name, which suits her. [729] Lena waves from the dock each morning, and Mara waves back, and the distance between them is the price, paid daily, gladly. [751] Some nights, when the tide stalls and the clocks lean sideways again, she steers past the grotto and tips her cap to the stars that once were doubled. [779] Home, she has decided, is not a shore; it is the crossing itself. [792]
