The elevator of the Aldwyn Spire took its time, ten floors of frost-rimed cable between the servants' hall and the observatory at the summit. [24] Mara had ridden it twice in nine years of service, and both times she had been carrying something that belonged to Captain Elsbeth Vane. [48] Tonight she carried two things: the captain's brass goggles, and the diary nobody could read. [63]

The captain had died in autumn, when the last airship of the season failed to outrun the ice. [81] Since then the Spire had stood icebound above the clouds, its hundred windows glazed white, its great mooring mast empty as a held breath. [105] Mara had found the diary while clearing the captain's quarters, wedged behind the chart drawer like a secret too heavy to fly with. [128] Every page was cipher: columns of letters that meant nothing, arranged with the patience of a woman who meant everything. [148] The steward had ordered it burned. [154] Mara, who had spent nine years learning when not to hear an order, had instead spent her evenings with it. [174] She was not clever in the way of scholars, only warmly inquisitive, the kind of curiosity that asks a locked door how its day has been until it answers. [203] The answer, it turned out, was in the goggles. [212]

Floor one slid past, the cage rattling like teeth. [221] Mara turned the goggles in her hands, fond of them the way one is fond of a difficult aunt. [240] Their lenses were etched, faintly, with a lattice no flier needed. [251] Three nights ago she had held a diary page behind one lens, a candle behind the other, and watched the flame come apart through scattered light, throwing bright threads across the cipher. [283] Where the threads crossed a letter, the letter counted. [292] The rest was chaff. [296]

By floor three she had the first page open on her knee. [308] The dawn outside the cage's frosted pane was thin and silver, but it was enough; the lattice caught it and scattered it obligingly across the paper. [334] The captain's first true sentence read: I am writing this so that someone, someday, will know I was not only sad. [355] Mara read it twice and felt something in her chest unfold, slowly, like a map of a country she had always lived in without ever seeing its borders. [383]

Floor five. [385] The household remembered the captain as a tragedy: the dead husband, the grounded ship, the long winters pacing the observatory. [405] Mara had served her tea through all of it and had decided, privately, that grief was only ever half a story. [426] She had kept the diary for one reason: to seek out the joy beneath the anguish, because she could not believe a woman who flew above clouds had felt nothing but the weight of them. [461]

Floor six gave her proof. [466] Today the ice on the mast sang in the wind, the next page confessed through its scattered light, and I laughed alone for an hour and was not ashamed. [495] Floor seven: Mara is the only one who knocks as if she hopes I am happy in here. [513] Mara laughed, then pressed her sleeve to her eyes, and regarded her own crying with affectionate detachment, the way the captain must have regarded storms: real, passing, no reason to land. [544]

Floor eight, and the reason for the cipher at last. [554] They want a widow on a mountain, the page said. [564] They would forgive me my sorrow forever and my joy never. [575] So I write the joy in lock and key, for whoever is curious enough to be kind. [592]

Floor nine. [594] The cage slowed; the summit was near, and so was the steward, who waited in the observatory to confirm that the captain's effects had been disposed of. [621] Mara had been summoned for another purpose too: the household needed a new keeper of the upper floors, and nine years of quiet competence had finally been noticed. [649] An ascending servant, the steward had called her in the letter, as though she were weather. [665] Keeper of the upper floors meant keys; keys meant the observatory, the chart room, the captain's sealed quarters. [683] She slid the diary inside her coat and settled the goggles around her neck, where a servant might plausibly carry a dead woman's things on their way to the fire. [713]

Floor ten arrived with a chime like a struck icicle. [723] Beyond the grate, morning poured through the observatory's frost-feathered glass and broke into a hundred small brightnesses on the floor. [743] The captain had stood in this light and laughed alone, and nobody had known. [757] Somebody knew now. [760] Mara stepped out to accept her keys, her promotion, and her duty to a happiness that had asked, in lock and key, to be found. [785]
