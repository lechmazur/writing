The grotto held its breath the way sycamores do at dusk, a hush that was less silence than listening. [19] Ilo had climbed down to it every night for a month, following the pull of the lodestone walls and the woman who lived among them. [44] Her name was Doctor Mara Venn, and she was the youngest old woman he had ever met. [61] Forty years ago she had published her last paper as a white-haired authority on disappearance; now she looked barely thirty, and the looking-younger had not stopped. [87] She aged backwards the way rivers run, certainly and without apology. [98] The grotto's magnetism kept a slab of basalt suspended above the black pond at its center, a floating rock wide enough for two to sit on, swaying gently under the starlight that fell through the broken ceiling. [135] Mara called it the meditation platform, and she meant the word precisely. [147]

"Tonight you go under," she said, handing Ilo a smooth gray stone. [159] "Throw it when I tell you, and read what comes back." [170]

He had asked her, the first night, what she was teaching him. [182] She had answered with the care of someone weaving truth on a narrow loom, choosing which threads to show and which to hold beneath the cloth. [208] "I am teaching you to see what leaves," she had said, and nothing more. [222] Every night since, he had felt the shape of something withheld, an answer she carried like a stone in her own pocket. [244]

They crossed to the platform on stepping stones the magnetism held just above the water. [259] The basalt dipped under their weight and steadied. [267] Mara sat cross-legged and began the breathing she had taught him, slow as sap. [281] The trance came on like evening coming under trees, a gradual gold going gray. [295] Through the trance, the grotto sharpened instead of blurring. [304]

"Now," Mara whispered. [307]

Ilo threw the stone. [311] It struck the pond, and the rings opened outward, silvered by starlight, and he understood at last what she had meant by reading. [334] The rings were not all the same. [341] Where they crossed the grotto's magnetic field, they flattened and braided, and in the braiding there were figures: a woman's silhouette, repeated, each repetition smaller than the last. [369]

"The stone vanishes," Mara said softly, "but the water tells you it was there. [383] "That is the whole discipline, Ilo. [389] "Anyone can vanish. [392] "The art is making the vanishing appear." [399]

He watched the silhouettes shrink ring by ring, woman to girl to child, and a cold understanding climbed his spine. [419] "It's you," he said. [423] "The figures in the water are you." [430]

She did not deny it; denial had never been her method, only the patient withholding of what he was not yet ready to carry. [454] "I have perhaps two years left," she said. [462] "Then I will be an infant, and then I will be a ripple, and then nothing the eye can hold. [482] "My whole career I studied disappearance from the outside. [491] "Now I am the experiment, and you are the instrument that will record it." [505]

"Why didn't you tell me the first night?" he asked. [515]

"Because a truth arrives whole only when it arrives late," she said. [527] "If I had told you then, you would have grieved me instead of learning me. [542] "I needed your eyes clear. [547] "Grief reads the rings wrong." [552]

The platform swayed beneath them, held aloft by forces neither could see, which seemed suddenly the truest image of everything she had taught him. [576] Invisible things hold us up; invisible departures leave legible wakes. [586]

"When I am gone," Mara said, "people will say I simply left, or never was. [601] "You will know how to throw the stone and make my leaving visible. [614] "That is why I chose you, and why I waited." [624]

Ilo looked at the pond, where the rings were fading now, the last small silhouette dissolving into stillness. [642] He felt the trance loosening its hold, and with it came not grief but a strange, weight-bearing calm, the kind a branch must feel when it learns it can hold a sleeping bird. [675]

"Teach me the rest," he said. [681]

"There is not much rest," she answered, and for the first time he heard the girl already arriving in her voice. [702] "Only this: throw gently, watch long, and never tell the water what you hope to see." [718]

Above them the stars wheeled slowly through the broken ceiling, and the grotto kept its sycamore hush, listening, as all good witnesses do, to what was leaving and what, because of that leaving, was finally beginning to appear. [756]
