Iris Vane thought in spirals. [5] Where other engineers drew straight schematics, she sketched helixes, certain that every problem coiled back on itself one level higher. [25] That was why the canyon called to her: it was a spiral cut into stone by water that had given up and gone elsewhere. [49] She arrived at moonrise, during the strangest month of her life, the month her brother Theo died and her own mind began rewriting itself. [73] In her pack she carried one object: the broken periscope mirror from the submarine fort they had built as children. [93] Theo had angled that mirror so they could spy on the adult world without being seen, and when it cracked he had wept as if the future itself had split. [123]

Since the funeral, Iris had been remembering things that were not hers. [135] She remembered the taste of the river Theo swam alone; she remembered a girl he never told her about; she remembered the dream he abandoned at nineteen, the observatory he never built. [167] The grief counselor called it confabulation. [173] Iris, who trusted structure over comfort, called it what it felt like: entangled memories, two childhoods wound around each other like strands of a single molecule, so that pulling on her past tugged his loose as well. [210]

An engineer cannot leave a system half-understood. [217] So she had built a method. [223] Memory, she knew, is not a vault but a workshop; each act of recall rebuilds the memory through synaptic plasticity, and whatever you hold in mind while remembering gets soldered into the recollection itself. [257] She could not retrieve Theo's lost dreams, but she could modify her own remembering until it became an instrument fine enough to excavate them. [281] If grief insisted on making her a different person, she would steer the transformation. [295] She would become the archeologist of abandoned dreams, beginning with his. [306]

The canyon at night was the right laboratory. [314] Moonlight fell down the sandstone walls in pale terraces, and the silence had a pressure to it, like deep water. [334] She set the broken mirror upright in the sand and knelt before it, trembling, half in terror and half in awe, the way you tremble at the edge of something holy. [365] Her procedure was chaotically precise: she let the memories surge in whatever wild order they chose, but she timed each one, anchored each to a breath, a shard, a degree of moonlight, never recalling the same moment twice without changing one variable. [407] Disorder in the input, rigor in the recording. [415]

The mirror helped in a way no intact glass could. [425] Its crack split her reflected face into two misaligned halves, one lit, one shadowed, and when she gazed at the seam the entangled memories sorted themselves: hers gathering on the bright side, his on the dark. [461] A periscope, after all, exists to show you what you cannot see from where you stand. [477] Even broken, especially broken, it kept its purpose. [485]

On the third night the observatory surfaced whole. [493] She remembered, in Theo's borrowed voice, the hilltop he had chosen, the dome he had sketched on a napkin, the scholarship letter he had folded away unanswered because their father got sick that winter. [527] The memory shook through her like a fault line slipping, and she sobbed and laughed at once, quaking inside a reverie she had engineered and could no longer fully control. [557] She wrote down every coordinate of the dream before it could sink again. [570]

Then something unplanned happened, the way it does in every honest experiment. [582] On the bright side of the crack, her own abandoned dream rose to meet his. [597] She had wanted, at twelve, to build machines that mattered to someone, not turbines for companies but instruments for people, and she had buried that wish under salaries and deadlines so deep she had forgotten the burial. [634] The two dreams twisted around each other in the seam of the mirror, complementary strands, and she understood at last why the memories had entangled: he had carried half of her, she had carried half of him, all along. [673]

Dawn found her changed, the transformation no longer happening to her but through her. [687] She climbed out of the canyon with the mirror wrapped in cloth and a notebook dense with excavated wishes. [706] She would buy the hilltop. [711] She would build the dome herself, weld by weld, and mount the cracked mirror at its entrance, so every visitor would see their own face split into the person they became and the person they shelved. [747] Other people's relinquished futures were strata waiting for a careful trowel, and she now possessed the only tool that mattered: a mind that rebuilt itself each time it remembered, spiraling upward, one turn of the helix at a time. [786]
