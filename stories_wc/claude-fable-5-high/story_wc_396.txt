<story>
Mirei was a peacekeeper laced with shadow, dark threads stitched through her skin like ink under glass. [17] Her kind had been bred for war, but she had walked out of the dark legions and offered her hands to treaties instead. [40] The elders called her a courageous anomaly, a shadow that chose to hold light steady for others. [57] Her post was the lighthouse orchard, a terrace of salt-hardy apple trees perched above shifting ice floes. [74] Below, the Floe Clans drifted with the broken sea; above, the Orchard Keepers tended fruit warmed by the lighthouse lamp. [94] For a generation, peace between them had rested on the Communion Glass, a crystal ball kept in the lantern room. [114] Anyone who touched it felt the other side's hunger, grief, and hope as if it were their own. [132] Then a winter storm threw the lighthouse into darkness, and someone, in fear or malice, smashed the Glass. [150] Mirei had recovered only one broken crystal ball fragment, a shard no larger than her thumb. [166] Without the Glass, the clans accused the keepers, the keepers blamed the clans, and harpoons began appearing along the cliff path. [187] Mirei was calmly intuitive, the way still water is calm because it knows the whole shape of its basin. [206] She did not believe the Glass's visions had died; broken light, she reasoned, only scatters. [221] It had to have landed somewhere reflective. [228] At the lighthouse's base, in a storeroom a keeper long dead had run as a vintage store for shipwreck salvage, dozens of old mirrors leaned against the walls. [256] Gilt frames, fogged silvering, a barber's glass from a drowned town. [267] On the third night after the breaking, Mirei carried the fragment down and saw it: a glimpse moving in the barber's mirror that was not her reflection. [294] A clanwoman wept over a seal-skin cradle; the vision flickered, orphaned, looking for its home. [309] Mirei pressed the fragment to the cold glass, and the glimpse poured into the shard like water finding a crack. [329] So she began to reconstitute the Communion Glass, vision by vision, via glimpses caught in the vintage store mirrors. [348] Each night her shadows reached into the silvered dark and held the images still long enough to gather them. [367] A keeper's boy sharing apples with a clan girl across the tide line. [380] A floe hunter saving a drowning orchard dog and telling no one. [392] The fragment grew heavier and warmer, swelling toward roundness, a world reassembling in her palm. [407] But the mirrors also showed her the breaking itself. [416] In a tarnished hand mirror she watched old Keeper Aldous swing a pruning hook at the Glass, sobbing, because the visions had shown him his son freezing on a floe and no one going to help. [452] The Glass had given everyone feeling, and feeling alone had saved nothing. [464] Mirei understood then what her work truly was: not to restore empathy, but to map empathy onto action. [482] On the solstice, delegations climbed to the orchard, harpoons and pruning hooks barely sheathed. [496] The clan matriarch, Sefa, spoke first, her breath white over the ice far below. [510] Return the Glass whole by dawn, she said, or the clans cut the cliff ropes and the orchard starves alone. [530] Every face turned to Mirei. [535] She held the nearly whole sphere, one last sliver of absence glinting in its side like a closed eye. [554] During the weighted breath before reply, while the ice groaned far below and the lamp turned its slow gold through the trees, Mirei drew the tarnished hand mirror from her coat. [585] She tilted it toward Sefa, and in its fogged silver the last orphaned glimpse stirred. [600] It showed Sefa, years younger, crossing breaking ice toward a stranded keeper's boy, reaching him too late, and bearing his body to the cliff stair so his people could bury him, never saying a word. [635] Mirei touched the fragment to the mirror, and the sphere closed itself with a sound like spring ice letting go. [655] Then she spoke. [658] The Glass would not return to the lantern room, she said, where feeling had pooled and rotted into grief. [677] It would stand in the old store among the mirrors, at the meeting of stair and ice, and no one could touch it without bringing proof of a deed: fruit carried down, fish carried up, a rope repaired, a name learned. [718] Empathy, she told them, would be a ledger now, not a lamp. [730] Sefa stared at her own younger face in the glass for a long time, then unstrung her harpoon. [748] Aldous, weeping again, went down the cliff stair that night carrying apples. [760] And Mirei, shadow-laced and steady, stood in the orchard above the shifting floes, keeping a peace that finally had hands. [780]
