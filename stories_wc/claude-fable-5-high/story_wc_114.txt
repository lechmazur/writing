The fever broke on the fourth night, somewhere between the wind reading and the dawn balloon. [16] I woke in the bunkroom of the Sirin Pass weather station with my mouth full of a sentence I had carried up the mountain and failed, for three delirious days, to say. [48] Vesna was at the instrument table, and the aurora was in the window behind her, green light moving like grief that had learned patience. [72] The valley people called her the oracle, though she hated the word. [84] Her forecasts were famous for being impossible to break: she never promised a single sky, only a band of skies, so that whatever the mountain did, some part of her prediction held. [116] A fault-tolerant oracle, Anton used to call her, laughing, the only prophet engineered to survive her own errors. [134] Anton was why I had come. [140] His field notebook sat in my pack by the door, recovered from the slide on the north couloir, and inside it, on the last finished page, was a botanical illustration of a saxifrage blooming four hundred meters higher than any saxifrage should. [182] He had drawn it the morning he died. [190] I had swallowed that truth at the threshold three days ago, when Vesna met me with soup and certainty, and the fever had kept it down ever since. [218] Now I lay still and tried to find its weight. [228] A swallowed truth must weigh something, I reasoned; my chest had been heavier on the climb than the pack ever was. [249] "You talk in fever," Vesna said without turning. [257] "Mostly numbers." [259] "Once, a name." [262] She was peripherally aware the way the station itself was aware: barometers in her skin, a seismograph in her spine, registering the smallest shifts at the edges of a room while seeming to look only at her charts. [300] She had known something arrived with me. [307] She was waiting to learn its mass. [314] I got up, unsteady, and brought her the notebook. [323] She did not open it at once. [330] Instead she showed me her own logbook, the official ledger of the Sirin Pass: columns of pressure, temperature, wind, the state's narrow arithmetic of sky. [355] And in the margins, crowding every white edge the columns left behind, were flowers. [369] Gentians, moss campion, saxifrage, each dated, each drawn at an altitude written in her small hand, each higher than the last. [390] "The columns have no row for this," she said. [399] "The forms ask what the air did. [406] They do not ask what the mountain is becoming. [415] So I keep the becoming where the forms cannot evict it." [426] She had been smuggling the warming world into the record by occupying margins, the only territory bureaucracy never audits. [445] Anton had taught her the flowers; she had taught him the weather. [457] Between them the mountain had become legible, and now half of its alphabet was under the snow of the north couloir. [478] She opened his notebook to the saxifrage. [485] Her thumb hovered, then pressed the petals, and the graphite gave; she smudged the lowest leaf into a grey blur and stared at the silver on her skin. [513] Unfixed graphite. [515] He always sprayed his finished drawings the same evening he made them. [527] The smudge told her what my swallowed sentence would have: that he had not lived to see that evening. [546] "Say it anyway," she said. [551] "A truth you swallow only gets heavier. [558] I want to know what it weighs while I can still carry it." [571] So I said it: the slide, the rope, the hour it took, the fact that he was reaching upslope, toward something growing, when the snow let go. [598] The sentence left me with an actual lightness, as if I had set down a second pack. [615] So that is its weight, I thought: exactly the difference between the man who climbs with it and the man who climbs without. [638] Vesna stood at the window for a long time. [647] The aurora swayed, and I understood that this was what her forecasts had always confessed: that certainty itself sways, that the honest prophet predicts in ribbons, not in lines. [676] Even her grief was fault-tolerant; it had been built, all these margin-flower years, to survive partial truth, late truth, truth arriving fevered and three days delayed. [702] In the morning she made the dawn observation, then took Anton's saxifrage and copied it into the margin of the official ledger, beside the day's pressures, at the altitude he had recorded. [734] She left the smudged leaf smudged. [740] "The record should show where the hand stopped," she said. [750] I descended at noon, lighter and heavier in ways no instrument at the station could parse, while above me the sky kept every part of her prediction. [777]
