Marisol kept her garden on the fire escape, six floors above the harbor, in pots salvaged from dumpsters and crates begged off fishermen. [23] She was wistfully pragmatic about it: she missed the yard she had lost, but iron grating drained better than clay soil ever had. [46] Her father had been the harbor's lockmaster, cutting keys for warehouses and trawler cabins until his hands gave out. [65] All she had left of his trade was a single bitting gauge tooth, a sliver of steel he had used to measure the depths of a key's cuts. [93] She planted seeds with it now, pressing each one to a precise depth, because he had taught her that small measurements decide whether things open. [118] Along the railing she had wired a row of mirror shards, angled downward. [131] The alley below her building never saw the sun, and the children who cut through it walked fast, shoulders up. [151] So she caught what light the harbor offered and threw it down into the dark corners, a few bright coins of it scattered across the bricks. [177] It was not much, but her father had spent his life letting people into locked places, and she figured light was her version of a key. [203] The oldest pot held his rosebush, dug up from the yard they had lost and dying slowly ever since. [222] She had made herself a bargain: when the final petal dropped, she would stop pretending he might somehow still be in it, and she would row his ashes out past the breakwater the way he had asked. [259] On a windless night in October, while she was watering the basil, the last petal let go. [276] It did not fall so much as surrender, drifting through the grating into the alley below. [292] A quivering hush came over the fire escape, the kind of silence that trembles because it is holding something. [311] Marisol stood in it for a long moment, then went inside for the tin urn and her coat. [329] On the stairs she met Teo, the boy from the third floor who sometimes watched her garden through the window and pretended he wasn't. [353] He saw the urn and asked, too quickly, if she needed help carrying anything. [367] She almost said no, out of habit, the way she kept her tenderness behind glass where weather could not get at it. [389] Instead she handed him the lantern. [395] The harbor was luminous under the moon, the water lit from above and somehow also from within, as if the tide had swallowed the sky and was carrying it carefully. [425] They borrowed a skiff from a fisherman who owed her tomatoes, and she took the oars herself. [442] As she rowed, she talked, and her words fell into the rhythm of oars in water: a pull, a pause, a quiet dripping rest. [466] She told Teo about depth and patience, about how a key only works because someone measured its cuts honestly. [485] She was not just talking; she was trying to instill something in him, stroke by stroke, the way her father had instilled it in her, so that care would feel less like weakness and more like craft. [522] Past the breakwater she shipped the oars and let the skiff drift. [534] The hush found them again out there, vast and trembling on the bright water. [548] Her hands shook as she opened the urn, and she did not hide it from the boy; some things deserve to be witnessed shaking. [572] The ashes went into the moonlit water and became part of its glow. [585] She took the bitting gauge tooth from her pocket and held it over the side, meaning to let her father keep his tool. [608] Then, pragmatic even in grief, she closed her fist around it. [619] Seeds still needed planting at a precise depth, and the dead are not honored by drowning what they taught us. [639] She gave the tooth to Teo instead and told him it was a measuring tool, and that he would learn what it measured. [662] Rowing back, she let him take one oar, and they found the rhythm together, clumsy at first, then sure. [681] In the spring she would pull the dead rosebush and let the boy choose what to plant in the old pot. [702] And every clear night, her mirrors would go on catching the harbor's shine and throwing it into the alley, into the dark corners, until even the people hurrying through them learned to slow down and look up.
