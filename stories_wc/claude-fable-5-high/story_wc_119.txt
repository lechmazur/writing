For forty years, Edie Mott wrote fortunes for the Golden Junk takeaway, and for forty years they kept coming true. [20] Not all of them, but enough that the village stopped reading them aloud. [33] It was an open secret, the kind everyone carried and nobody unwrapped. [45] Edie herself never claimed to be prophetic; she claimed only to be tired. [58]

The man found her at the Anchor on a Tuesday, after last orders, when the landlord was stacking chairs and pretending not to listen. [82] He laid a fortune slip on the table, brittle as a moth wing. [95] "You wrote this in 1989," he said. [102] "The light you tend was never yours." [109]

"I tend no light," Edie said, though that was a lie the whole village shared with her. [126] Since her father died she had rowed out to the lighthouse on Carrick Skerry every night, keeping the lamp turning over the rocks that had no official keeper since 1974. [156] Everyone knew; no one said. [161]

The man said his name was Hollis, which it obviously wasn't. [172] "Your fortunes weren't fortunes," he said. [178] "The Golden Junk was a dead drop. [185] Your slips carried microdots: lens specifications, emulsion ratios, the whole film process. [197] Someone has been printing the specs onto your words for decades, and someone overseas still wants them." [214]

Edie thought of Mr. Liu, who had paid her in cash and corrected her grammar, and who had wept, once, without explanation, over a fortune that read only: Soon. [243] "So I was a courier who never knew the route," she said. [255] "My whole life, a message in someone else's envelope." [264]

"I'm offering you a chance to seal the envelope yourself," Hollis said. [276] "A boat comes tonight for the last microdot, the master specs. [287] We want the boat. [291] You have the light." [295]

Edie had spent her life writing other people's futures in a borrowed voice. [308] What she wanted now, what she had wanted since her father's funeral, was to transpose her life into a new key: same notes, her own arrangement. [334]

She rowed out to Carrick Skerry under a sky the color of wet slate. [348] The lighthouse rose from the rocks like a question nobody had finished asking. [361] Inside, her father's logbooks still lined the stairwell, and inside the last one, where Hollis said it would be, she found the microdot pressed under a stamp: the complete film specifications, small as a flaw in the eye. [399] Her father had been the network's anchor; she had been its unwitting pen. [412] The village had known, the way villages know, and had loved them both anyway. [426]

A keeper does not chase ships; a keeper orchestrates the sea by directing its flow. [441] Edie climbed to the lamp room and began to conduct. [451] She narrowed the beam and swept it wide of the safe channel, so the fishing boats turned home early. [470] She darkened the western reach, herding the ferry's wake away from the skerry. [483] Then she opened a single bright corridor across the rocks, an invitation written in light, leading the stranger's boat into the one cove where Hollis's men waited in the dark. [513] The boat came on faithfully, reading her beam the way the village had read her fortunes: as truth too convenient to question. [535]

When the engines cut and the shouting started, Edie did not watch. [547] She sat at her father's desk and wrote one last fortune on the back of a logbook page. [565]

Hollis climbed the stairs at dawn, salt-wet and satisfied. [574] "The microdot," he said, and she gave it to him folded inside the slip, the way her words had always carried more than they said. [599] He read the fortune and frowned at its familiar enigma: The keeper leaves; the light remains; the secret was never the dot. [621] "What does it mean?" he asked. [627] "I never know until afterward," Edie said. [634] "That's the trouble with being accidentally prophetic. [641] You only write the truth; you don't get to choose which one." [653]

She rowed back to the village in the gray morning and walked into the Anchor, where the landlord, against all law and custom, poured her a whisky long after last orders had any meaning. [687] Nobody asked where she had been. [693] Everybody knew. [695] It was the same melody the village had always hummed, only now Edie heard it transposed, raised into a key she had chosen herself. [719] On the bar she left a slip of paper, her last fortune as a free woman: You already know. [738] By morning, everyone in the village had read it, and, as with all open secrets, every one of them was certain it was meant for them alone. [765] And out on Carrick Skerry, the light kept turning, keeping its own counsel, exactly as she had written it would. [785]
