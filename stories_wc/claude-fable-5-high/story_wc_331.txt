The Brachistochrone Library opened only between midnight and dawn, which suited Ines, who had been hired as its odd-hour librarian precisely because she did not mind being awake when the rest of the city dreamed. [35] It was a scientific poetry library, the only one she knew of: shelves of verse about entropy, sonnets on cell division, villanelles that rhymed orbit with augur. [62] Patrons came at strange hours, insomniac physicists and grieving poets, and Ines watched them browse in a hush she had come to think of as observed solitude, each person alone but lit, somehow, by being seen. [98] The library's founder, Dr. Halloway, had been famous for being sanely eccentric: she filed Maxwell's equations under love poetry and insisted, calmly and with footnotes, that the building itself remembered. [128] Nothing here was digitized. [132] Halloway had believed in analog memories, in the way a page holds the pressure of a pencil long after the graphite fades, the way a stair dips where ten thousand feet agreed. [164] When she died, she left the library a single instruction, typed on a card in the empty drawer of her desk: Find the place where stories live. [191] For three years Ines had treated the card as a koan. [202] Then, on a Tuesday at two in the morning, while patrons browsed the thermodynamics elegies, she found the scarf. [221] It had been jammed behind the radiator in the map room, a tattered silk scarf gone soft as breath, its edges ruffling outward in frills that no flat cloth should hold. [252] Ines knew those frills. [256] She had shelved enough mathematical verse to recognize a crocheted model of the hyperbolic plane, a surface that crams infinite area into finite reach by curving away from itself everywhere at once. [288] Halloway had not knitted it for warmth. [295] She had stitched a map. [300] Along the scarf's ruined hem, in thread the color of old tea, ran a line of embroidery: The shelves are not parallel; follow the lines that refuse to meet. [329] Ines walked the stacks that night with the scarf draped over her hands like a divining instrument. [346] In Euclid's world, two aisles laid side by side stay side by side forever. [360] But the Brachistochrone's aisles diverged, subtly, the way Halloway's frills diverged, and where the scarf's curvature matched the floor's, the frill lay flat and quiet against the boards. [388] Through hyperbolic geometry, the building had been hiding more interior than its exterior allowed. [402] Ines followed the flattening silk past the astronomy ballads, past a patron asleep over Lucretius, deeper than the floor plan admitted, until the aisle opened into a small round room she had never been able to find from any other direction. [443] The room held no books. [448] It held objects. [451] A child's shoe, a wax cylinder, a pressed fern, a tram ticket, hundreds of small things on narrow shelves, each tagged in Halloway's handwriting with a date and a name. [481] Analog memories, the real kind: matter that had been present when something happened to someone. [496] Stories, Halloway's card had implied, do not live in words. [506] Words are only where stories sleep. [512] They live in the things that touched them, waiting for a witness to reawaken them. [527] Ines lifted the wax cylinder and felt, absurdly and certainly, a summer she had never had: a porch, a fiddle, somebody laughing at a joke now ninety years dead. [556] Whether the feeling rose from the object or from her own starved imagination, she decided, was a distinction Halloway would have called Euclidean: true only in a flatter world than this one. [588] She did not tell the patrons. [594] Some discoveries are not for announcing; they are for tending. [604] Instead she changed her habits in small ways. [612] When the grieving poet returned at three a.m., Ines left the tram ticket where the woman would find it, tucked into a poem about geodesics, and watched from the desk as the woman held it and went very still. [651] Observed solitude, she thought; it was the library's whole liturgy, and now she understood it as a method. [669] A memory wakes more fully when someone watches it being remembered. [680] On her last round before dawn, Ines added an object of her own to the hidden shelves: a button from her father's coat, tagged with his name. [707] Then she wound the tattered scarf around her throat, frills and theorems and all, and walked back out into the aisles that refused, gently and infinitely, to meet. [735] Somewhere behind her, a story was waking up. [743] Somewhere ahead, a patron was browsing toward the place where it lived. [755]
