In the canyon of endless echoes, every word ever spoken still wandered between the towers of bone, looking for an ear. [21] The towers had been grown, not built, from the ribs of the leviathans that died there before anyone kept time. [41] Sefa lived in the lowest tower, the one the Wardens called crooked, and she liked it crooked. [58] She was an artist, and a defiant one, which in the canyon meant the same as dangerous. [75] Here, art was echo, and echo was permanent, and permanence belonged to the Wardens. [89] Tomorrow, at first light, her brother Calder would kneel at the Ritual of Absolution and ask her to forgive him. [109] He had lied to the Wardens, and his lie had sent old Maren over the rim with a rope cut too short. [131] The canyon's law was simple and terrible: forgiveness had a price, and the price was the truth itself. [149] When Sefa spoke the words of pardon beneath the pale spire, the Wardens would sing the counter-echo, and every memory of Calder's crime would be unmade from the air. [178] Maren would have died of clumsiness, officially, forever. [186] Sefa loved her brother. [190] She also loved the truth, and she refused to bury one to save the other. [205] So in the night before the ritual, she did what artists do with light they cannot keep whole: she decided to refract it. [228] One truth, split into many small colors, scattered too widely for any counter-echo to gather. [243] Her instrument was poetry, spoken aloud, impromptu, unrepeatable. [251] The Wardens could erase a confession, because a confession had one shape. [263] They could not erase a hundred metaphors that each held a splinter of it. [277] She went tower to tower in the forged hush of the canyon night, a quiet that felt hammered into place, dense as cooled iron. [301] At each landing she held a small session, waking weavers and bone-carvers, asking only that they listen once. [319] To the rope-makers she gave a poem about a cord that was measured twice and cut once, and cut wrong on purpose. [341] To the midwives she gave a verse about a tongue that traded an old woman's breath for a brother's standing. [361] No single poem accused Calder. [366] All of them together did, the way a prism holds no sunlight and yet proves the sun. [383] Trailing her was Pip, the bell-ringer's daughter, eleven years old and innocently mischievous, who treated the canyon's gravest law as a game of catch. [407] Pip repeated each poem into the dark with small deliberate errors, giggling, and the echoes carried her flawed copies down side ravines where no Warden ever bothered to listen. [436] Sefa had not asked her to do it. [444] She understood, watching the girl, that mischief was a kind of genius: every error was a new facet, and every facet bent the truth a little differently without breaking it. [474] By the gray hour, the canyon was full of refracted light no one could un-shine. [489] Then the mist rose, as it always did before a ritual, and the pale spire appeared above it like a needle through cloth. [512] It was the tallest bone in the canyon, the leviathan's last vertebra, and it alone produced no echo at all. [532] Words spoken there died at once, which was why pardons were spoken there. [545] Calder knelt on the white platform, hollow-eyed, and the Wardens raised their throats to sing. [560] Sefa climbed the spire and looked at her brother, who had wept all night and meant it. [577] "I forgive you," she said, and the words dropped dead off the bone, soundless, costing her nothing she had not already spent. [599] The Wardens sang the counter-echo, and below the mist the bare account of Calder's crime, the one shape the law could find, dissolved into satisfied silence. [625] But the poems remained, because the poems had never been the truth, only its colors. [640] Calder rose forgiven, and forgiven he stayed; the canyon's law was honored to the letter. [655] Yet that evening the rope-makers measured their cords twice and cut them long, and the midwives whispered about breath that should not be traded, and Maren's name moved through the ravines in a dozen crooked verses. [691] Calder heard them everywhere, and understood what his sister had paid and what she had refused to pay. [709] He came to her tower and asked why she had forgiven him at all. [723] "Because forgiveness should cost the forgiver mercy," she said, "not the world its memory." [737] He carried that answer down the stairs like something hot. [747] Years later, when Pip became a poet with errors of her own, travelers swore the canyon spoke in fragments, and that if you walked it end to end, listening, the fragments assembled into one bright unbearable truth. [784] No Warden ever found the prism. [790] She had made it out of everyone. [797]
