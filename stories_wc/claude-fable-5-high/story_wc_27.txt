Mara went barefoot because the stones spoke through the soles of her feet. [13] The ancient circle had stood on this hill for four thousand years before anyone thought to build a spaceport beside it. [34] Now the port was a carcass, and the repair bay where she sheltered was its hollow ribcage. [51] Eleven months ago the rebellion had filled this bay with welders and shouting and hope. [66] Then the Directorate jammed every frequency, the cells scattered, and the war simply stopped mid-breath. [81] Nobody surrendered; nobody fought; the lull settled over the valley like dust on a dead engine. [97] Mara stayed behind to discover meaning in the mess. [106] That was how she said it, anyway, when the last fighters asked why she would not run. [123] She had said it awkwardly, chin up, voice cracking, utterly certain. [134]

Every evening, in the margin of daylight between sunset and true dark, she climbed the gantry with her grandmother's vintage opera glasses. [156] The brass was dented and the velvet worn, but the lenses still pulled the first stars close. [173] Her grandmother had carried them to the opera house in the capital; Mara carried them to watch for ships that never came. [195]

Tonight, scanning the violet horizon, she noticed something wrong with the stones. [207] The circle hummed. [210] It had always hummed faintly, a vibration the old port engineers blamed on groundwater, but tonight the sound climbed through the gantry steel and into her bare feet like a chord finding its key. [244] She descended and crossed the cracked tarmac to the circle. [254] Standing inside it, soles pressed to cold granite, she felt the hum resolve into rhythm: three pulses, pause, three pulses. [274] Her breath stopped. [277] Three-and-three was the rebellion's all-clear signal, the one the Directorate's jamming had silenced eleven months ago. [293] Someone, somewhere, was transmitting through the ground itself. [301] The stones were resonating; the circle was a receiver older than any radio, and the jammers could not touch bedrock. [321]

She laughed once, a startled sound, and pressed her palm to the tallest stone. [335] Then she understood what the bay still held. [343] In the dark beneath tarpaulins sat a seismic survey rig, abandoned by the port's builders, designed to pound coded pulses into the earth and read the echoes. [370] She had inventoried it months ago and dismissed it as junk. [381] Junk, she thought, grinning at the wreckage around her, was only meaning that nobody had read yet. [398]

It took her three nights to wake the rig. [407] She worked barefoot on the freezing deck plates so she could feel the circle's hum and match her machine to its pitch. [429] She spliced cables wrong twice, apologized aloud to the rig both times, and kept going with the awkward confidence of someone who has never once believed she would fail, only that she would be embarrassed first. [465] On the fourth evening, in the margin of daylight, she hammered her answer into the planet. [481] Three pulses, pause, three pulses, then the old rallying code: the bay stands, the bay waits, come home. [499] The stone circle caught the rhythm and sang it outward through granite veins the Directorate had never thought to map. [519]

Then she climbed the gantry, raised the opera glasses, and waited. [530] For six nights, nothing. [534] She kept transmitting anyway, because the hum kept answering, three and three, steady as a heartbeat under the hill. [553] On the seventh night the horizon changed. [560] Through the worn brass lenses she saw them: not stars, but running lights, low and slow, a dozen freighters hugging the valley floor with their transponders dark. [587] Behind them came ground convoys, headlights off, navigating by the same pulse she had sent through the bones of the world. [608]

The first ship settled into the repair bay with a roar that shook rust from the rafters, and yet Mara felt no chaos in it, only a vast, thunderous serenity, like a storm that had finally decided where to rain. [648] Fighters she thought dead climbed down from cargo ramps and stared at her bare feet, at the opera glasses around her neck, at the humming stones beyond the doors. [677] "We pinged the bedrock for months from the mines, hoping someone still knew the old code," their commander said. [696] "The hill found it four thousand years ago," Mara said, blushing and standing very straight. [711] "I just took off my boots and listened." [719]

That night the bay filled again with welders and shouting and hope, the lull breaking like ice on a spring river. [740] The rebellion reignited not with a bomb or a speech but with a rhythm in the ground, patient as stone. [760] And every evening after, in the margin of daylight, Mara stood barefoot in the ancient circle, opera glasses raised, watching the stars arrive like reinforcements. [785]
