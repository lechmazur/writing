Mirren sold clean slates. [4] Not metaphorically: actual slates of fogged glass, each one a life scrubbed of its past, traded over her counter for whatever the customer could no longer bear to carry. [33] Her shop occupied the east wing of the glass orchard library, a structure of grown crystal that floated on a swirling sea of clouds, its trees heavy with books instead of fruit. [65] Today was her first opening day. [71] The morning had been a crush. [77] A widower traded his grief. [82] A forger traded her hands' memory of other people's signatures. [92] A child, brought by anxious parents, traded nothing, because Mirren refused; children's pasts were still becoming, not yet weights. [111] Each surrendered past she distilled the same way. [119] Into bottled moonlight. [122] Pale, restless light, corked in glass, never quite still. [131] The library's covenant called these bottles a glimmering parole: the pasts were not destroyed, only released under conditions, free to drift the orchard as light until their owners returned to claim them or died never wanting to. [168] Most never returned. [171] Then came the lull. [175] Noon, the clouds below turning slow as cream, the shop empty, the orchard quiet enough to hear pages ripening. [194] Mirren locked the till and took down the one bottle that was hers. [207] She had been her own first customer, years ago, before there was a shop. [221] But she had not erased herself the way her customers did, all at once, in panic. [237] She had been mindfully scattered. [242] Piece by chosen piece, she had hidden fragments of her past throughout the orchard, pressed into books like flowers, so that one day she could gather herself back in the right order. [274] The trouble: she had scattered the map along with everything else. [285] What remained was a single intention, worn smooth from handling: find the place where stories begin. [301] Because that, she was certain, was where she had hidden the first fragment, the one that explained all the others. [321] She walked the rows with the bottle in her apron and a stylus in her fist. [337] You could not find a beginning by searching what was present. [348] Every book in the orchard was a middle. [356] But absence could be measured. [361] At each tree she paused, read what grew there, and incised a tally into the glass bark: how many pages each book was missing, how many names its index could not hold, how many endings broke off mid-breath. [399] She was quantifying absence, row by row, walking a gradient of lack. [411] The numbers climbed toward the orchard's center. [418] Where stories begin, she reasoned, nothing has been written yet; the beginning is the point of total absence, and absence, mapped carefully, would point like a compass. [445] It did. [447] At the orchard's heart stood a sapling she had never noticed, glass so clear it was barely there. [465] Its branches held no books. [470] Its tally would have been infinite. [476] Mirren knelt and searched its roots for the fragment she had promised herself. [489] Nothing. [490] No pressed page, no folded memory, no map. [498] She sat back on her heels and laughed, short and stunned, because she understood. [512] She had not hidden the first fragment here. [520] She could not have. [524] The place where stories begin holds nothing, by definition; that is what makes it a beginning. [540] Her past self had not left her an answer. [549] She had left her a choice. [555] Mirren uncorked her bottle. [559] The moonlight rose, hesitant, a parolee at the gate, glimmering with everything she had once been. [575] She could drink it back and be who she was. [585] Or. [586] She poured it over the sapling's roots. [593] The light sank in like rain. [599] By the time the afternoon customers arrived, a single book had budded on the clear branches, small and unfinished, its first page bearing one incised line in her own handwriting: A vendor of clean slates opened her shop above the clouds, and during the lull, went looking for herself. [648] Mirren read it twice. [652] She did not remember writing it, and she did, both at once, the fragments of her settling into a shape that was not whole but was integrated, the way a mosaic is truer than any single stone. [689] Stories begin where someone gives the emptiness something to hold. [699] She walked back to her counter, turned the sign, and waited for the next person who wanted to forget. [718] She would sell them their clean slate. [725] She would bottle their moonlight gently. [731] And she would tell them, as she corked the glass, that parole was not exile: the orchard kept what they let go, and beginnings waited at the center, patient, perfectly empty, for anyone with the courage to pour themselves out.
