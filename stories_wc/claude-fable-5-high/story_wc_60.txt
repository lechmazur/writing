The migrating geese stopped over the city the way a held breath stops a sentence. [15] For three days they circled the dry plazas, refusing to land, refusing to leave, and from that pause the whole strange season began. [38] Mira Voss watched them from the rim of the Lion Fountain, which had not spat water in forty years. [57] People called her the luminous skeptic, half as insult, half as awe, because she carried a surveyor's lamp everywhere and aimed it at every miracle until the miracle apologized and turned back into plumbing. [91] Hers was a city of forgotten fountains: a hundred basins and no memory of any of them working. [109] That was the part Mira could not light her way out of. [121] Records existed; names existed; but ask anyone what the water had sounded like and their faces went smooth as soap. [141] Her own mother had died unable to recall her wedding day, which had happened, witnesses swore, beside the Heron Fountain in June. [163] Mira did not believe in curses. [169] She believed in mechanisms, which is a more dangerous faith, because mechanisms can be found. [184]

On the second day of the geese, she found the first breadcrumb. [196] A literal one: a knuckle of stale rye on the Lion Fountain's rim, then another two meters on, a deliberate line of them leading down Cistern Street. [223] Crumbs in a city where pigeons fought wars over crusts did not survive by accident. [238] The geese overhead were holding the birds of the street in some anxious truce, and someone was using that truce to write her a path. [263] She followed it, lamp lit, jaw set. [270]

The trail ended at the house of Old Teller Brandt, the clockmaker everyone politely assumed was dead. [287] He opened the door before she knocked. [294] On his table sat a stained glass box, small as a heart, its panes leaded into constellations: the Swan, the Water Bearer, the long V of geese in flight. [323] Light moved inside it like trapped weather. [330] "I left the crumbs," Brandt said, "because you are the only person in this city who would follow evidence instead of a feeling." [353]

He explained, and Mira, to her own irritation, found the explanation realistically imaginative: outrageous in shape, sound in every joint. [373] The fountains had been the city's memory system, water threading recollection through every district like blood. [389] When the old council dammed the source to sell the river, the memories did not die; they stuck, pooled in pipes, sealed under plazas. [413] The box was the master valve's key, cut by Brandt's grandmother, who had hidden it and then, cruelly, forgotten why. [433] "It only opens under the right stars," Brandt said. [442] "The panes align with the sky once in a long while. [453] I have waited sixty-one years. [458] Sidereal patience, my grandmother called it: you do not count days, you count returns." [472] "And the geese?" Mira asked. [477] "They navigate by those same stars. [483] When the sky comes right above this city, they cannot read past it. [496] Their pause is our clock striking." [502]

Mira wanted to refuse it all. [508] Instead she tested it. [512] That night, on the dry basin of the Heron Fountain, she set the box down and watched the leaded Swan slide into agreement with the actual Swan overhead. [540] The panes loosened like a held jaw. [547] Inside lay no key at all, only a single tuned brass reed and instructions in a dead woman's hand: the valve was not locked, only stuck, fused by decades of mineral silence, and the right tone, played into the pipes, would shiver the scale loose. [592] The council's wardens patrolled the plazas; remembering had been quietly made a nuisance, then quietly made a crime. [610] So Mira knelt in the basin in a defiant hush, lamp dark for the first time in years, and breathed through the reed into the Heron's rusted mouth. [638]

The note went down the pipe like a thought finding its sentence. [650] Deep below, something unstuck. [654] It was not a roar. [659] It was a hundred small surrenders, valve after valve, district after district, and then water, and in the water, voices. [679] Mira heard her mother laughing beside the Heron Fountain in June. [690] All over the city, people stood up in their dark kitchens, struck still by the sudden return of themselves. [709] The wardens came running and found nothing to arrest: only fountains doing what fountains do. [724] Above, released from the corrected sky, the geese broke their pause and flew on. [738] Mira relit her lamp, not to doubt this time, but to read the last line of the instructions: Patience is just skepticism aimed at time. [763] She believed it instantly, and for once did not mind. [773]
