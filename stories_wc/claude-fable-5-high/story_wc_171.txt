The terrace had been carved into the mountain before either nation had a name, and Mirel climbed its worn steps the way her predecessors had: slowly, counting. [27] She was the forty-first negotiator of the forever war, and the first to come back twice. [43] Twelve years ago she had stood here at midsummer and signed an accord that lasted nine days. [60] On the tenth day, a border town called Saven burned, because her treaty had been beautiful and vague, all ideals and no instruments. [83] She had spent twelve years learning that redemption is not a feeling but a bill, and tonight she had come to pay it. [106] In her hands she carried the ebony clipboard, black wood polished by a century of nervous thumbs. [123] Every negotiator before her had held it; the names of the dead from every failed accord were etched in tiny script along its back. [147] Saven's names took up four lines. [153] She had etched them herself, one letter at a time, because the ritual demanded that the hand that failed must carve. [174] Both delegations were already waiting under the midsummer sky, where the old astronomers had decreed treaties could only be sealed when the two wandering stars, the Red Pilgrim and the Pale Sister, drew close in their slow dance across the heavens. [215] The conjunction came once every twelve years and held for a single night. [228] Some called the rule superstition. [233] Mirel had come to understand it as wisdom: a deadline written in the sky that no king could bribe and no general could delay. [257] General Tashan of the eastern host arrived armored, as if peace were an ambush. [271] Envoy Liss of the west arrived grieving; her son had died in the spring offensive. [286] Mirel was operatively gentle with them both: softness with structure inside it, a hand steadying the blade so it could be set down. [309] She poured the salt water of the old rite into three cups and made them drink before any word was spoken, because grief shared through ritual loosens the jaw that anger has wired shut. [343] Then the work began. [347] Twelve years ago she would have spoken of brotherhood and the dawn of a new age. [363] Tonight she spoke of grain quotas, of who would garrison the river forts, of the schedule by which prisoners would walk home. [385] An ideal that cannot be inspected is a lie waiting for its anniversary, she told them, and she made every noble clause carry a date, a name, and a consequence. [415] This was her vow after Saven: never again the beautiful and vague, only ideals anchored in practice, bolted down where weather could not move them. [440] Past midnight, the talks broke on the question of Saven itself. [451] Tashan would not admit his soldiers had lit the fires; Liss would not sign beside a man who denied her dead. [472] Overhead, the Red Pilgrim and the Pale Sister were already beginning to part. [485] Mirel felt the old failure rise in her like smoke. [495] She turned the ebony clipboard over and laid it on the stone table, names upward, under the lamplight. [513] These four lines are mine, she said. [520] I did not light the fires, but I wrote the treaty that left the door open for them. [538] Guilt is not a stain you scrub out; it is a debt you service every year for the rest of your life. [560] I am asking you both to take on a debt. [570] She proposed it then, the clause no negotiator had dared: that Tashan and Liss would return to this terrace every midsummer, together, to carve the names of any who died under their signatures. [603] Not punishment, but ritual: accountability given a shape the body must perform. [615] The general looked at the clipboard for a long time. [625] Whatever courage he had spent his life mustering for war, he had to muster differently now, and it cost him visibly. [646] He took up the carving awl first. [653] Beside Saven's four lines he scratched a fifth: the name of the captain who had given the order, his own brother, dead since winter. [677] Liss watched him cut each letter, and something in her face unclenched without forgiving. [691] They signed as the two stars stood at their closest, a breath apart in the slow dance that would carry them away from each other by dawn. [718] The treaty was not beautiful. [723] It was full of inspections and ugly compromises, and one of its clauses required two powerful people to kneel on old stone once a year and remember. [750] Mirel descended the worn steps at sunrise, the clipboard lighter in her hands than it had been in twelve years. [770] It was not absolution; the names remained. [777] But the price of redemption is not paid once; it is paid on schedule, and now the schedule was written down. [798]
