Vesh was the last harmonic stone skimmer, and her knees had given out forty years ago. [16] She lived where she had fallen, on a shelf of rock above the canyon of endless echoes, where towers of bone rose from the dust like the ribs of a buried god. [48] Nothing that sounded in that canyon ever truly died. [57] Sound struck the bone towers and rang onward, folding back on itself, thinning but never ending. [73] The skimmers of her order had once walked the canyon floor, casting tuned stones across the dust and reading the returning chords to map what no eye could survey. [102] They were gone now, buried through centuries of layered dust, and only their oldest cast remained: a single pulse left wandering among the towers, the echo of the founder's first throw, still circling after six hundred years. [139] Vesh heard it some nights, a low note threading between the vertebrae, looking for the stone that had made it. [159] She could not follow it. [164] So she had set herself a harder discipline: to learn to navigate without moving. [178]

Her instrument was the canyon's one living chorus, a flock of gray cliff-swifts that nested in the bone towers' hollows. [198] The birds felt the wandering pulse before any ear could. [208] When it passed beneath them they lifted and wheeled, and the shape of their turning betrayed the pulse's path. [227] For forty years Vesh had read the flock the way her teachers once read returning chords, charting the canyon through the patterns of birds in flight, sketching towers she would never touch. [259] Her map was nearly whole. [264] Only the canyon's deep end stayed blank, where the pulse never seemed to go. [278]

Then a dust-pilgrim climbed to her shelf, carrying something wrapped in oilcloth. [290] "Found it at the deep end," he said. [298] "They say you can appraise old stone." [305] Inside lay a stone arrowhead, knapped flint, its edges worn to velvet. [317] Vesh's breath caught. [320] The founder had not skimmed a river pebble, the histories said; she had cast her grandmother's arrowhead, the only tuned stone she owned, and it had never been recovered. [349] Vesh was historically reverent to her marrow; she handled relics of the order the way priests handle bones of saints. [369] She appraised it slowly: the chevron flaking of the founder's school, the notch filed for a skimmer's grip, the faint resonance when she breathed across its edge. [396] It was genuine. [399]

And that truth landed on her like a falling tower. [409] If the arrowhead lay at the deep end, then the wandering pulse was an orphan, a sound that had outlived its stone by six centuries, searching for a source it would never find. [442] That was why it shunned the deep end. [450] It circled the place where its stone had fallen and could not bear to cross it, and so the flock never wheeled there, and Vesh's map stayed blind. [478]

The pilgrim wanted to sell the relic in the lowland markets. [489] "Name its worth," he said. [494] "Its worth," Vesh said, "is one more throw." [502] She traded everything she had left: her brass charts, her teacher's tuning forks, the deed to her shelf of rock. [522] The pilgrim left rich and bewildered. [528]

At dawn she sat at the rim, the arrowhead warm in her ruined hands. [542] Reverence argued for a shrine, for glass and silence. [551] But the deepest reverence, she decided, was to let a relic finish its work. [565] A skimmer's stone was made to fly, not to be mourned. [576] Her wrists remembered what her knees had forgotten. [584] She waited until the flock rose on the morning thermals, until their turning told her the pulse was sweeping up the canyon toward her, and then she cast. [612] The arrowhead skipped across the dust in seven flat strikes, each one ringing the bone towers like struck bells, and went out singing into the deep end. [639]

The wandering pulse turned. [643] For the first time in six hundred years it heard its own voice answered in its own key, and it followed, crossing the ground it had refused for centuries. [672] The flock exploded into shapes Vesh had never seen, spirals and shears and braided turns, and she read them as fast as her stylus could move. [698] Tower by tower the deep end gave up its architecture: a drowned arch, a fallen spire, a hollow where the founder's bones might lie. [722] Then the two sounds met, old pulse and new, and braided into a single chord that did not fade but settled, steady as a heartbeat under the dust. [750] Vesh laid down her stylus over a finished map. [759] She had not taken a step in forty years, and now she never needed to; the whole canyon stood inside her, rung true at last. [784]
