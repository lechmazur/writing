Mirei was a loom spinner in the valley where sound had begun to fray. [14] First the river lost its murmur, then the temple bell forgot its bronze tongue, and last winter her neighbor's name dissolved before anyone could speak it. [40] The elders called it a raveling silence, a loosening of the world's weave, and they looked at Mirei when they said it. [62] Her grandmother had been the last keeper of the great tapestry, and her grandmother had walked into the bamboo forest twelve years ago and never returned. [88] All she had left behind was a tree sap amber bead, warm as a held breath, with a single silk thread coiled inside it like a sleeping word. [116] Mirei wore it at her throat as she spun, a wistful loom spinner weaving cloth nobody bought, listening to the quiet spread. [138]

The rummage sale came through the valley in autumn, carts heavy with things the silence had orphaned. [155] In a battered box Mirei found jigsaw puzzle pieces, edges only, every middle piece gone. [170] The peddler shrugged and gave it to her for nothing, since a border around an absence was worth nothing. [189] She assembled the edges that night by lamplight, and her hands began to shake. [203] The completed frame showed the painted fringe of a bamboo forest, green stalks crowding inward around a hole shaped exactly like an orchard. [226] Her grandmother had spoken of it once: the forgotten orchard where the first weavers tapped the trees, spinning sap into the threads that held time together. [252] The missing middle was not lost; it had unraveled, the way the river's murmur had unraveled. [268] The bead at her throat grew warmer, and Mirei understood she was being summoned. [282]

She walked three days into the bamboo, following the puzzle's border like a map, the stalks closing behind her with a sound like breath withheld. [307] On the fourth morning the bamboo parted, and the forgotten orchard stood before her, rows of ancient trees bleeding slow gold from their bark. [331] At its center waited the great loom, vast as a temple gate, its threads severed and trailing in the grass. [351] Frost covered everything, stoically sparkling, refusing to melt even as the morning sun pressed against it. [367] And there, woven into the unfinished tapestry itself, was her grandmother. [378] Not dead, not alive: a figure of thread, her face turned toward Mirei, her mouth open around a word that had not finished arriving in twelve years. [405]

Mirei finally understood the bead. [410] Her grandmother had not abandoned her; she had woven herself in to slow the unraveling, and she had sealed her last message in amber because sap was the only substance the silence could not eat. [445] But a message means nothing unless it is carried, and the distance between a woven woman and a living one is the most impossible distance there is. [472] To cross it, someone had to recommence the weaving. [481]

Mirei cracked the bead open against the loom's frame. [490] The silk thread inside unspooled, longer than any bead could hold, and where it touched the severed warp, the strands rose like grass after rain. [515] She took her seat at the great loom and began to throw the shuttle. [529] She wove through the hush of time's unraveling tapestry, mending the hole row by row, and she felt the cost immediately. [550] Each pass of the shuttle took something: the memory of her mother's lullaby, the color of her first winter, the sound of her own laughter at nine years old. [579] The loom did not weave with silk alone; it wove with the weaver. [592] Her grandmother had known this and gone anyway, and now Mirei knew it too, and her hands did not stop. [612]

As the new cloth grew, her grandmother's woven mouth completed its word at last. [626] It was not a warning or a secret. [634] It was Mirei's name, spoken with such tenderness that the orchard's frost finally let go and ran down the bark as bright water. [657] The message had crossed; it only needed someone willing to walk the distance and pay the toll. [674] Mirei wove until the hole in the world closed, and as her own arms began turning to thread, her grandmother's hands, freed at last, settled over hers. [701] Together they finished the final row. [707]

In the valley, the river remembered its murmur. [715] The temple bell found its tongue. [721] And travelers who wander deep into the bamboo sometimes find an orchard where two figures sit at a loom, neither wholly thread nor wholly flesh, weaving the world's sounds back into being. [753] They do not look up, but the new cloth glows, and the silence, wherever it still hides, dares not come near. [774]
