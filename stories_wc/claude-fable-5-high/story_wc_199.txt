Tilba sorted seeds the way other people prayed: daily, kneeling, and in strict order. [14] She lived in the Ashen Arbor, a grove of gray trees that drifted above the valley on roots of cloud. [34] The valley folk below had folklore about it, recited at every harvest. [46] Whoever climbs into the Arbor, the saying went, emerges transformed always. [57] Tilba had climbed up nine years ago and never bothered to come down and check. [72] Her work was simple and green-hearted. [78] The Arbor dropped leaves, and the leaves, everyone agreed, were a message. [90] Nobody agreed on what the message said. [97] Tilba meant to decode it before she died, which gave her a pleasant sense of deadline. [113]

Her method was her own invention. [119] Each leaf that fell carried a seed cradled in its curl, and each seed, she believed, was a syllable. [138] So she sorted them: round from ridged, pale from freckled, sweet-smelling from sour. [151] Then she built tiny monuments to forgotten things out of the leftovers. [163] A cairn of acorn caps for the bridge that washed away before her grandmother was born. [179] A spire of maple keys for a song whose tune survived but whose words did not. [195] The monuments were not decoration. [200] They were her dictionary. [204] Forgotten things, she reasoned, were exactly what an old grove would talk about. [217] If she rebuilt enough of what was lost, the leaves would finally have a vocabulary she shared. [234] Moss had grown over half her tools, and she found this delightful rather than troubling. [249] Everything soft and persistent felt like encouragement. [256]

Then came the equinox, and with it the hush where past meets prophecy. [269] Once a year the Arbor stopped drifting, the wind held its breath, and no leaf fell for one whole hour. [289] Valley folklore said the grove spent that hour remembering forward. [299] Tilba spent it inspecting her monuments, because silence made her itchy. [310] That was when she saw the speck. [317] On the cairn for the washed-away bridge sat a single grain of dust, glowing faintly blue-white. [333] She knew that sheen from her grandmother's ring: moonstone, cut as a cabochon, smooth as a held breath. [351] But this was only a dust speck of it, as if some vast gem had been ground down to almost nothing. [372] Folklore again: the valley said the Arbor had once been a moonstone, worn into a seed, planted by the first forgetful god. [394] Tilba had always filed that tale under nonsense. [402] Now nonsense glittered on her monument and waited. [410]

She did what any careful sorter does with an unknown specimen. [421] She prodded it, gently, with the tip of a pine needle. [432] The speck rang. [435] Not loudly; it rang the way a memory rings, somewhere behind the ribs. [448] And in the hush, every monument she had ever built answered. [459] The acorn cairn hummed the groan of bridge timbers. [468] The maple spire sang the lost words of the song, and they were ordinary and wonderful: a lullaby about counting seeds. [489] Tilba sat down hard in the moss and laughed. [498] The message had never been in the falling leaves at all. [509] The leaves were not the letter; they were the postage, payment for her attention. [523] The message was whatever she rebuilt. [529] The Arbor was not speaking to her; it had been listening, all nine years, while she taught it what the valley had forgotten. [552] The dust speck was its reply: the smallest possible piece of its oldest possible self, set on her best monument like a medal. [575]

When the hour ended, the wind exhaled and the leaves resumed, but they fell differently now. [591] They fell in spirals around her monuments, as if reading them. [602] Tilba climbed down the cloud roots that autumn, the moonstone speck folded in a dock leaf. [618] The valley folk crowded around, eager to see how the folklore had treated her. [632] Emerges transformed always, they whispered, looking for horns or wings or silver eyes. [645] She showed them seeds instead. [650] She taught them the lullaby, and the old ones wept, because their mothers had hummed that tune without words. [669] She showed them where the bridge had stood, and that winter they rebuilt it, a monument too large to fit in her hands. [692] The transformation, she explained, had happened so slowly she had mistaken it for routine. [706] She had gone up a sorter of seeds and come down a sorter of memory, which was the same skill aimed at softer material. [730]

In spring she planted the moonstone speck in the valley square, in good black dirt, just to see. [748] A gray seedling rose, ashen and patient, with moss already delighting at its foot. [762] Someday it would drift skyward, and someone orderly would climb it. [773] Tilba prodded the soil once, gently, the way you knock on a friend's door. [787] It rang. [789]
