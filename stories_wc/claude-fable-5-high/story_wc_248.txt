Three years after going off the grid, Mira Voss still smelled smoke in her sleep. [15] She had burned the Halloway Lending Library on the night the county voted to pulp its collection, including every sketchbook her teacher Odile had donated. [40] If the books could not be saved, she had decided, they would at least refuse to be garbage. [58] Only one thing left the fire with her: a faded tapestry fragment Odile had hidden inside a hollowed atlas, woven with a mountain split by a river of light. [87] Mira fled north and disappeared into a town with no cameras and no questions. [101] Then, last month, a package found her anyway. [109] Inside was her own self-portrait, the one she had painted at nineteen under Odile's volcanic tenderness, that teaching style of eruptions and embraces. [132] Someone had vandalized it. [136] Five precise cuts crossed her painted face, and ochre symbols circled her painted hands. [150] Anyone else would have seen desecration. [156] Mira, analytically intuitive since childhood, saw grammar. [163] The cuts matched the weave-angles of the tapestry fragment; the ochre marks matched glyphs Odile used to doodle in margins, the script of the Veshlan, a civilization buried by its own mountain two thousand years ago. [199] Odile had vandalized the portrait herself, then mailed it before dying, the obituary tucked behind the canvas like an apology. [219] The cuts, overlaid on a map, pointed east, toward the basalt coast. [231] So Mira followed a trail of Odile's artistic creations, each one a syllable in a sentence her teacher had spent decades writing. [253] A mural on a grain silo in Dunmore hid a glyph in the curve of a heron's wing. [271] A mosaic bench outside a shuttered chapel repeated the tapestry's river of light in chips of bottle glass. [289] A carved lighthouse door gave her a number; a weathervane shaped like a loom gave her a direction. [307] Each artwork only made sense against the fragment in her coat pocket, its threads the key Odile had trusted her, and only her, to keep. [332] Walking, she remembered why. [336] At nineteen, drunk on cheap wine and ambition, she had promised Odile that if anything ever happened to her, Mira would carry the fragment home. [361] Home, Odile had said, meant the archive of the Veshlan, sealed in lava tubes her grandmother had found and resealed, fearing looters. [383] Mira had laughed, agreed, and forgotten, the promise sliding under years of galleries and grievances until the fire melted everything down to what mattered. [407] Odile had not forgotten. [411] Knowing Mira was hiding, knowing no letter could safely name a location, she had written her message the only way a hunted student could read it: in art, in cuts, in tenderness disguised as ruin. [446] The trail ended at a sea cliff where black rock folded like cooled cloth. [460] Mira counted seams, matched the weathervane's bearing, and found the seam that breathed. [473] Behind a slab balanced like a door, the lost civilization's archive opened around her. [487] Lamplight from her torch slid across shelves cut into lava stone, holding clay tablets, bone styluses, and looms still strung with mineral-dyed thread. [510] The Veshlan had archived themselves as their mountain died, weaving their history while ash fell, choosing memory over escape. [529] On the far wall hung a great tapestry with one corner missing. [541] Mira's fragment fit it like a returned tooth. [549] Whole, the weaving did not portray a mountain at all; it portrayed a woman, arms open, holding a river of light that poured from her chest into a crowd of small figures below. [582] Beneath it, wrapped in oilcloth, lay Odile's final piece: a small panel painting of two women on a cliff, and on its back, the hidden message the whole trail had spelled toward. [614] You burned a library because you could not bear to watch memory die, it read. [629] I forgave you before the smoke cleared. [636] Now keep the promise you forgot: tend this place, catalogue it, give the Veshlan back their names. [653] Mira sat down on the cold stone and cried the way the mountain must once have erupted, all heat and surrender at once. [676] Then she did what arsonists and archivists share: she made an inventory of what could be saved. [693] She stayed off the grid, but not out of the world. [704] Tablets were cleaned, threads stabilized, a quiet letter sent to a museum under a false name with true coordinates to follow after her death. [728] Her slashed self-portrait she hung beside the great tapestry, repaired with golden thread along every cut. [744] Some vandalism, she had learned, is just love that needs decoding. [755] Some fires, too. [758]
