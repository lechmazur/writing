Mara counted spores the way other people counted blessings, because spores did not lie and, since the empathy virus, neither could anyone else. [23] She foraged truffles in the hanging orchard, where apple trees grew sideways from the ravine walls and storms came through like arguments nobody had finished. [48] The trees had been trained on cables decades ago, roots gripping shale, fruit dangling over the river like lanterns. [67] Beneath them, in the wet dark of fallen leaves, the truffles waited, indifferent to honesty. [82] The virus had not killed many bodies, but it had killed every secret. [95] Whatever you felt, you said; whatever you said, you meant. [105] Mara's settlement, forty people in a barn above the ravine, had survived the fevers only to discover that involuntary truth was its own slow plague. [130] Old Teodor told his daughter he had always loved her brother more; she stopped speaking entirely. [146] Two marriages ended in a single supper. [153] People learned to live far apart, shouting weather reports across the orchard, because distance was the only privacy left. [172] Mara was rational about it, which is to say she refused to grieve what evidence could not restore. [190] But she read. [193] In the ruins of the valley library she found the discredited textbooks, the ones pulled from shelves before the collapse: nineteenth-century treatises on mesmeric occlusion, on objects that could supposedly blunt the transmission of feeling between minds. [230] Quackery, every page of it. [235] Yet three of the books agreed on one rumored cure: a glass eye blown in Bohemia, ground with cobalt and ash, said to occlude the gaze that carries truth out of a person before they choose to give it. [274] Mara did not believe it. [279] She believed in the people who would believe it, and that was a different calculation. [294] What she wanted to cultivate was not a cure but psychological safety, soil in which a mind could rest unexposed. [314] The eye itself was in the sealed museum quarter downriver, behind quarantine wire, catalogued among relics the new authority forbade anyone to move. [337] Smuggling it took her a winter. [343] She traded truffles to a river guard whose honesty, when she asked what he wanted, came out as a blunt confession: white truffles, and to never be asked anything again. [373] She carried the relic home in a hollowed loaf, through three checkpoints where she could not lie, so she rehearsed questions in her head until the truthful answers were also useless ones. [405] "What's in the bread?" a sentry asked. [412] "Something old," she said, and it was true, and he waved her on into the rain. [428] The antique glass eye was smaller than she expected, iris a storm-colored blue, a hairline fracture running through it like lightning held still. [451] She set it in a cleft of the oldest orchard tree, the one whose cables sang in high wind, and she told the settlement, plainly, what the textbooks claimed. [480] She could not tell them it worked; she could not lie. [491] She told them only that under the eye, by old account, a person's truth stayed their own until they offered it. [512] Then something happened that she had hypothesized but not dared promise. [523] People came to the tree. [528] Teodor's daughter came first, stood under the fractured blue iris, and said nothing for an hour, and nobody asked her anything, because the eye, by custom, was watching instead of them. [559] Silence under the eye did not feel like withholding; it felt like rest. [572] The relic occluded nothing measurable, Mara knew. [579] What it occluded was expectation. [584] It gave people a place where not speaking was sacred rather than suspicious, and in a world of compulsory truth, that shelter was everything. [608] The settlement began eating together again. [614] Teodor planted a sapling below the eye and tended it without once explaining himself. [628] When the spring storms came, battering the ravine until the cables howled, the whole settlement climbed down to lash the trees, working in a silence that no longer frightened anyone. [658] One night a girl asked Mara directly whether the eye was magic, and Mara felt the virus pull the truth up through her throat. [682] "No," she said. [685] "It is a broken piece of glass that a discredited book taught me to steal." [700] The girl waited, and because the question had ended, no more truth was required. [714] "But," Mara added, freely this time, "what we built around it is real." [727] The girl seemed satisfied with a magic made of agreement. [737] Mara went back to her foraging, rational as ever, kneeling in the storm-soaked dark beneath sideways trees, unearthing what the soil had been kind enough to hide. [764] Some things, she thought, should stay buried until offered. [773] Above her, the cracked eye held its storm of blue, seeing nothing, sheltering everyone. [787]
