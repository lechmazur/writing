Mira Voss repaired broken clocks because broken clocks, unlike people, could be made to keep their promises. [17] Her workshop occupied the gutted east wing of the Aldercott estate, which had burned the winter the old admiral died. [37] She liked the ruin precisely because nothing about it was unpredictable anymore; the worst had already happened. [54] On the morning the floor gave way, she was prying up scorched boards to level her workbench. [71] Beneath the ashes, wrapped in oilcloth that the fire had somehow spared, lay a brass compass rose stamped with the admiral's initials. [93] Everyone in the village knew the story: Admiral Aldercott had lost his compass rose on his final voyage, and without it, he claimed, he could no longer trust any course he set. [125] Yet here it was, hidden under his own house, which meant he had never lost it at all. [143] He had buried it. [147]

The needle did not point north. [153] It trembled, swung, and settled toward the estate's neglected greenhouse, the one place Mira had never bothered to map. [172] She told herself she would not follow a broken instrument. [182] She followed it that same afternoon. [188]

Inside the greenhouse, autumn had come indoors; dead vines hung from shattered panes, and a draft from somewhere below sent dry leaves swirling in slow spirals across the flagstones. [217] The leaves moved like the needle: trembling, swinging, settling. [226] Mira distrusted coincidence, but she had a tinkerer's rule: when coincidence becomes pattern, it is mechanism, and mechanism can be understood. [247] She traced the draft through the swirling leaves to a potting bench bolted, absurdly, to the floor. [264] Beneath it, a hatch; beneath the hatch, stairs descending into wet darkness and a sound like applause that never stopped. [284]

The stairs ended at a cavern where a subterranean waterfall poured out of the rock, silvered by daylight falling through some fissure far above. [308] Built into the cavern wall, half-consumed by mineral crust, stood an enormous clock. [321] It had no numbers. [325] Its single hand pointed at words etched in a ring: CERTAIN, LIKELY, POSSIBLE, UNKNOWN. [339] A waterwheel beneath the falls had once driven it; the wheel was jammed, the hand frozen on CERTAIN. [357]

Tucked in the casing she found the admiral's logbook, water-warped but legible. [369] His last entry read: I built this engine to measure doubt, because every wreck of my career came from sailing certain. [390] I buried my compass rose so I would have to learn to befriend uncertainty in planning, to chart courses that bend. [411] Whoever finds it will be someone who digs through ashes for level ground. [424] That person will need this machine more than I did. [434]

Mira read the entry three times, feeling uncomfortably seen by a dead man. [447] Her whole life was a fortress of schedules and contingencies, every hour escapement-tight, because plans, like clocks, were supposed to protect you from surprise. [471] The fortress had not protected her from the fire, or from anything that mattered. [485]

She set down her toolbag and studied the jammed wheel. [495] None of her tools fit it; the admiral had machined his own threads, eccentric as he was. [512] So she would have to improvise, which she hated, which was, she suspected, exactly the point. [528] She wedged the compass rose itself into the gap where a gear tooth had sheared away; brass against brass, it fit with a click that sounded almost smug. [556] She freed the wheel with a pry bar made from a bench bolt, packed the bearing with candle wax, and stepped back. [578] The waterfall caught the wheel. [583] The great hand shuddered off CERTAIN and began to drift, slow and alive, between LIKELY and POSSIBLE, never resting anywhere for long. [605]

Mira watched it for an hour and understood the admiral's instrument at last: it did not tell you what would happen. [626] It told you how tightly to hold your plan. [635] She climbed back up through the swirling leaves feeling uncertainly certain, a sensation she could not name and did not want to fix. [658]

In the weeks that followed, she changed her trade in small ways. [670] She still repaired clocks, but she stopped promising the village exact dates, offering windows instead, and discovered her work improved when she left room for the metal to surprise her. [700] Each morning she descended past the greenhouse to read the doubt-clock the way sailors once read the sky. [718] On days it leaned toward UNKNOWN, she planned loosely and packed extra tools. [731] On days it leaned toward LIKELY, she let herself trust the schedule, a little. [745] It never again touched CERTAIN, and she came to think that was the truest thing the admiral ever built. [764]
