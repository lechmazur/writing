The carousel halted on a Tuesday, or a decade ago, or tomorrow; in the lagoon above the clouds, Mara had stopped trusting the order of things. [26] She was a scavenger, and she was resolute about it the way a wound is resolute about scarring. [44] The kinetic lagoon had once churned on the carousel's spinning, water lifted and folded by the same brass engine that turned the painted horses. [68] Now the water hung in glassy folds, mid-wave, a stillness so fractured it looked like applause caught between hands. [87] She had climbed the tether cables to strip copper, but copper was an excuse, and she knew it. [105] What she truly collected were fragments of overheard stories, the way other scavengers collected wire. [120] The lagoon held them: voices soaked into the frozen water, releasing in slivers whenever her boots disturbed the surface tension. [140] "Promise me you'll come back rich," said a wave near the boarding dock. [153] "She said she'd build a whole city up here," sighed a curl of foam. [167] Mara wrote each fragment in her ledger, though the dates she assigned them contradicted one another, because time in the lagoon had eroded the same way her ambitions had: not collapsing all at once, just thinning, sentence by sentence, until only the habit of wanting remained. [213] On the third day, or the first, she found the disposable camera wheel. [226] It stood at the carousel's hub: a vendor's rack shaped like a ship's wheel, each spoke ending in a cheap cardboard camera, the kind tourists once cranked and clicked and abandoned. [257] Twelve cameras, twelve unwound rolls, twelve unspent afternoons. [265] She pried one free and thumbed the ridged winding wheel, and the click traveled through the dead carousel like a heartbeat remembering its job. [289] The lagoon shivered. [292] A figure assembled itself out of suspended spray: a younger woman in a surveyor's coat, blueprint tubes strapped to her back, eyes bright with the particular arrogance of someone who has never yet failed. [326] Mara knew the coat because she had pawned it. [335] She knew the face because it had been hers, back when she was the engineer who designed this lagoon, before the funding fell and she fell with it, down to the ground towns, down to scavenging the bones of her own blueprint. [377] "You came back," the ghost said. [383] "I came up," Mara said. [388] "There's a difference." [391] The ghost gestured at the frozen waves, the rusted horses, the silence where the engine's song should be. [409] "You stopped winding," it said. [414] "The carousel only halts when its keeper stops believing it should turn." [426] Mara understood, then, why the overheard stories had been gathering in her ledger like evidence. [441] They were the testimony of everyone she had promised this place to: the vendor who had stocked these cameras on credit, the families who had ridden waves above weather, the girl who had whispered about building a city. [479] She had come to negotiate with her own ghost, and the ghost had come to collect. [495] "What do you want," Mara asked, "to let me cut the copper and go?" [509] "One photograph," the ghost said. [514] "Of what we actually built. [519] Taken honestly." [521] Mara raised the disposable camera and turned its wheel, and with each ratcheting click a fragment of story surfaced in the water and replayed, out of order, chronology dissolving entirely: her bankruptcy hearing, her first sketch of the lagoon, her mother's funeral, the ribbon-cutting, all happening now, all happening always. [571] She could not have said which came first, and for the first time, that confusion felt less like sickness and more like mercy. [594] If everything was happening always, then nothing was finished, and nothing finished can be entirely failed. [610] She framed the shot: not the grand panorama from her old brochures, but the truth of it, a stalled wave, a rusted horse, a ghost holding blueprints she would never unroll. [641] Regret moved through her, but for once she did not flinch from it; she let it pass through like weather, the way the lagoon had once let storms pass through its waves. [673] The shutter clicked. [676] The ghost looked at her, and something in its bright arrogance softened into recognition. [690] "Keep winding," it said, and dissolved back into spray. [699] Mara did not cut the copper. [705] She knelt at the carousel's hub instead and began turning the great central crank, the same motion as the camera wheel, scaled up to the size of a life. [734] Somewhere beneath her, gears that had waited years took up the rhythm. [746] The first wave broke loose with a sound like a held breath releasing, and the painted horses began, slowly, to turn. [767] In her ledger, under no date at all, Mara wrote the last overheard fragment, the one in her own voice: she came back. [790]
