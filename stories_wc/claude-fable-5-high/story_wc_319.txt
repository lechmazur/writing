Every night since my grandmother died, I have fallen asleep with her pendant cold against my sternum, and every night it has carried me somewhere. [25] She had belonged to a cult that mapped the boundaries of light, and the pendant was their instrument: a sliver of brass holding a bead of glass that glowed faintly at the edges of dreams. [60] Tonight, through the veil of sleep, it brings me again to the frozen garden. [74] Mist hangs between hedges of black ice, and the roses are clenched fists of frost that never open. [92] Everything here is lucid and blurred at once, the way a lamp looks through fog: I can see precisely that I cannot see. [115]

The guardian lives here. [119] I have glimpsed it on other nights: a long shape moving on all fours between the hedges, dressed in the rags of a librarian's robe, eyes like reading lamps left burning in an abandoned wing. [154] My grandmother's journals named it: the guardian of the Library of Thresholds, gone feral when the library was unmade, when the cult pushed the boundaries of light too far and the light pushed back. [188] Her last entry was a single instruction: wait. [196]

So I wait. [199] I do not call out, do not chase the shape in the mist, do not pretend to certainty I do not have. [221] This is harder than it sounds. [227] Awake, I am a person who needs answers; I came here first demanding to know where my grandmother went when she died, and the garden gave me nothing but fog. [257] Now I am trying something else: I am trying to make friends with uncertainty, to sit beside not-knowing as if it were a shy animal. [282]

On the ninth night, the shy animal comes. [290] The guardian emerges from the mist and crouches an arm's length away, breath steaming, frost in its matted hair. [309] Up close I see what feral means: not savage, but unshelved. [320] "You waited," it says, in a voice like pages turning. [330] "Certainty walks in straight lines and frightens me off; only the uncertain can stand still long enough to be found." [350]

It looks at the pendant and flinches. [357] "Cultist's work," it says. [361] "They believed light ended somewhere, that they could fence it, catalog its borders." [374] "But light has no boundary except what looks at it." [384] "When they proved that, the library fell out of its dimension, and so did I." [399] It says this the way you confess a fever. [408] I understand then that it is dimensionally homesick: not homesick for a place, but for a direction, a plane of existence that no longer intersects any map. [435]

"How do you bear it?" I ask, because I have my own absence I cannot map. [451] "I interiorize exteriors," it says, and opens its robe. [460] Inside is not a body but a hall: shelves receding into warm dark, and on the shelves, landscapes. [478] A summer orchard bottled in a jar of amber light. [488] A coastline folded like a letter. [494] An entire morning, shelved spine-out. [499] "Whatever outside I cannot return to, I take inside," the guardian says. [511] "I cannot go home, so I have become a place things can come home to." [526]

It extends one cracked hand. [531] "Your grandmother shelved something here, near the end." [539] "She said you would come demanding answers, and that you must not receive it until you stopped demanding." [557] From the warm dark it draws a small glass bead, twin to the one in my pendant, except this one is lit from within. [581] Inside it, tiny and entire, is her kitchen on a winter morning: steam, bread, her laugh caught mid-rise. [599] Not an answer to where she went. [606] Only proof of where she had been, which the fog lets me see with perfect, merciful blur. [623]

I press the bead into the pendant's empty setting, and the brass goes warm. [637] The cult made it a fence; we have made it a window. [649] "Will you come back?" the guardian asks, and I hear how long it has waited to ask anything of anyone. [669] "I don't know," I say, and mean it as a gift. [680] It smiles, frost cracking at the corners of its mouth. [690]

The mist begins to thin, which is how mornings arrive here. [701] I wake with the pendant glowing softly against my chest, lit from a dimension I will never locate and no longer need to. [724] Some nights I return; some nights the garden does not open. [735] I have stopped needing to know which it will be. [745] Uncertainty and I are friends now, the way the guardian and the dark of its own shelves are friends: not at peace, exactly, but at home. [771]
