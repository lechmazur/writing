Vera Lin painted murals the way other people confessed: in color too vivid to take back. [16] She had covered three city blocks with sunflowers tall as houses, yet the commission that undid her arrived in a shoebox: her grandmother Odile's collection of old postcards, left to her with no letter at all. [52] Every card showed the same scene: an amber pavilion in autumn, its lacquered roof burning against bare birches, painted by Odile's own hand year after year. [78] On the back of each, the same six words: the flowers only bloom at night. [93] Vera had watched her grandmother kneel in the garden at midnight, cupping moonflowers as if listening to them, and she had never asked why. [117] Now the asking had nowhere to go, so it went into her hands. [130]

Quietly driven in the way of people who cannot grieve out loud, she traced the pavilion to a hill town two provinces north, where it stood beside a small cathedral, both half-forgotten under winter's last snow. [166] She rented a room and stayed across the melting of that snow, watching the drifts shrink from the pavilion's amber posts like a tide going out. [192] The sexton, a stooped man named Brother Anselm, told her Odile had lived there one autumn, sixty years ago, apprenticed to a glass etcher who restored the cathedral's east window. [222] "She left before the first frost," he said, "and the etcher never finished anything again." [237]

Vera began sketching the window by day and reading the postcards by lamplight, arranging them by postmark like the rings of a tree. [260] The earliest cards were joyful, the pavilion crowded with painted figures; the later ones grew emptier, until only the building remained, glowing alone. [283] Whoever Odile had loved here, she had erased him one autumn at a time. [297]

The east window puzzled her. [302] By daylight it showed an ordinary garden of saints, but Vera, who knew how images hide inside images, noticed the glass was doubled: two etched panes mounted a finger's width apart, their lines refusing to align. [338] Sunlight flattened them into one picture. [344] But light from inside, she realized, light at night, would cast the hidden layer outward, and the saints' garden would become something else. [367] She had to reckon, then, with what she was about to do: trespass on a dead woman's secret, the one mercy Odile had been denied in life. [394] She sat in the pavilion until dark, postcards fanned across her knees, and decided that secrets left in shoeboxes are not hidden but waiting. [418]

Brother Anselm unlocked the cathedral before dawn and lit every candle on the east side without asking why. [436] Vera went outside and stood in the slush of the dying snow, facing the window. [451] The candlelight pushed through the hidden layers of etched glass, and the saints dissolved. [465] In their place bloomed a field of moonflowers, hundreds of them, etched so finely they seemed to breathe, and at their center two figures in a pavilion, one holding out a blossom to the other. [500] It was a wedding portrait no church would have sanctioned then: two women, faces tilted together, rendered in lines only darkness could reveal. [523] The etcher had not been a man at all. [532]

Vera understood her grandmother's question at last. [539] Why do flowers bloom at night? [545] Because some love is only permitted to open when no one is watching, and it opens anyway, fully, fragrant, refusing to apologize for its hour. [570] The moonflowers in the garden had been Odile's anniversary, kept for sixty years in a language no one thought to read. [591]

A dawning hush settled over the hill as the sky paled, that suspended quiet when night has ended but morning has not yet agreed to begin. [617] In that hush the window faded, the moonflowers folding back into sainthood, and Vera let them go gently, the way one lowers a sleeping child. [642]

She stayed through the last of the thaw. [650] On the pavilion's inner walls, with the town council's bemused blessing, she painted her answer: an autumn evening, amber light, and moonflowers opening between two shadows whose hands nearly touch. [680] She worked in colors so vivid that even strangers slowed when they passed, though she kept the figures soft, umbral, tender, half-given to the dark that had sheltered them. [709] On the final morning she mailed the postcards back to the town's small museum, all but one. [726] That one she kept in her coat pocket, where her hand could find it, six words facing her heart. [745] The flowers only bloom at night. [751] But now somebody knew, and knowing, Vera thought, is its own slow sunrise. [764]
