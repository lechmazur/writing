Mara had spent forty years as the tidal-pool chronicler of Selkie Cove, and she had learned that nothing important happens at high tide or low tide, only in between. [29] Her notebooks recorded the slack hours: the crab molting in the half-drained pool, the anemone closing, the water deciding which way to move. [52] Tonight she sat in the ancient grotto at the cove's north end, where the ceiling had collapsed centuries ago and left a ragged window of stars. [78] Across from her sat her granddaughter Iris, who had just said the hard thing. [92] "I'm not taking over the chronicle," Iris had said. [101] "I'm leaving in spring." [105] Then the pause began. [109] Mara did not fill it. [114] She had never trusted direct answers; they were like staring at a pool, which makes the creatures hide. [132] Everything true she had ever seen, she had seen sideways. [142] So instead of looking at Iris, she took the jade cicada from her pocket and set it on the wet stone between them. [165] It was small, green as deep water, carved by someone dead a thousand years. [179] Her own grandmother had told her that the old empires placed jade cicadas on the tongues of the dead, because a cicada spends years underground and then breaks open into another life. [211] It was not a symbol of ending. [218] It was a symbol of molting. [224] Iris looked at it and said nothing, and the pause deepened, and inside that pause Mara went looking for what she had hunted all her life: the texture of time. [254] She had always believed time was not smooth. [262] It had a grain, like wood or sand, and the grain showed itself only in transitions: the turning tide, the molting shell, the silence between one sentence and the next. [292] A pause was a tidal pool of time, and if she watched it closely, warmly, without grabbing, she could see what lived in it. [316] So she watched. [319] She watched the way Iris's thumb circled the strap of her bag, the way her eyes went to the star-window, not the exit. [342] She watched her granddaughter's breath catch on the word she had not yet said, the way water catches on a hidden rock. [364] And in that watching, sideways, never head-on, Mara began to foresee. [375] Not magic, or not only magic. [381] Foresight, she had decided long ago, was just observation of the present so patient that the future stopped being able to hide. [403] She saw Iris on a ship, yes, in spring. [412] But she also saw the notebook in her granddaughter's coat, the one Iris thought was secret, full of sketches of harbor pilings and the creatures that gripped them. [440] She saw a young woman who was not abandoning the chronicle but molting out of it, the way a cicada leaves its split shell clinging to the bark, still shaped like itself. [472] She saw, further out and fainter, letters arriving from foreign tide lines, observations in Iris's handwriting, the chronicle growing larger than the cove. [495] The texture of time, she understood at last, was not the texture of stone or water. [511] It was the texture of a shed skin: fragile, exact, and left behind on purpose. [526] Time felt like everything we outgrow without losing. [534] Above the broken ceiling, the stars did their own slow molting, constellations sliding out of the shapes the ancients had drawn on this grotto's walls in ochre, fish and hunters and a winged insect she had once taken for a bee. [575] She knew better now. [579] It was a cicada. [583] Someone in this cave, a thousand transitions ago, had understood the same thing she did. [598] The pause had lasted perhaps a minute. [605] It had been enough. [609] Mara slid the jade cicada across the stone until it touched her granddaughter's hand. [623] "I wasn't going to ask you to stay," she said gently. [634] "I was going to ask you to keep writing things down." [645] Iris's shoulders dropped, the way a wave drops when it has finished arriving. [658] "You knew," Iris said. [662] "You always know." [665] "I never know," Mara said. [670] "I only watch the in-between, and the in-between is honest." [680] Iris picked up the cicada and turned it under the starlight, and its green caught fire the way the pools did at dawn. [703] "When the tide turns tonight," Mara said, "come down to the pools with me one last time as my apprentice, and the first time as my colleague." [730] Iris laughed, wet-eyed, and the difficult conversation ended the way the best transitions do: not with a door closing, but with water beginning, quietly, to move the other way. [759]
