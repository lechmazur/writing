Mira had been the last sky-bridge violinist, playing for commuters who crossed the iron spans between towers until the city replaced applause with efficiency audits. [25] When the Bureau confiscated her violin, they left her one thing they considered worthless: a locust shell her grandmother had glued inside the instrument's case for luck. [52] Hold it to your ear, her grandmother had said, and you will hear what the world sounded like before it forgot itself. [74] Mira held it now, at the rusted gate of the orchard the city pretended did not exist. [91]

The orchard was enchanted in the old, inconvenient way: trees that fruited music, blossoms that chimed, roots that drank starlight instead of water. [114] The Bureau could not demolish it, so they policed it instead, with living statues cast from the bodies of the last gardeners, marble sentinels who arrested anything that moved with intention. [145] Mira knew the statues hunted by sound and sight, but rumor said they listened deeper than that, down to the pulse. [167] Knowing was not the same as understanding, and she stepped through the gate inside that gap, trusting it would close around her like a door. [192]

Her plan was simple and probably treasonous: uproot one singing sapling and carry it back into the city, plant it in the gear-courts where the children had never heard an unscheduled note. [224] If beauty could not be performed anymore, perhaps it could be grown. [236]

The first statue stood among the pear trees, a woman of grey stone with lichen for hair, her head turning with the slow certainty of a clock hand. [264] Mira froze, and her heart did not. [271] It hammered, and the statue's eyes ground open. [279] So the rumor was true. [284] She pressed the locust shell against her sternum, an old performer's trick turned desperate, and breathed the way she once breathed before a difficult passage: four counts in, six counts out, until her heartbeat slowed into rhythm, into meter, into something resonantly soft, like a bow drawn across muted strings. [334] The hollow shell caught the pulse and rounded it, the way a violin's body rounds a string's thin cry. [353]

The statue tilted its head. [358] Then, impossibly, it stepped aside. [363]

Mira understood, all at once, what she had only known before: the statues were not listening for intruders. [381] They were listening for music. [386] The gardeners had been turned to stone with their longing intact, and a heartbeat played in waltz time was the first song anyone had offered them in thirty years. [415]

She walked deeper, signaling through heart rhythms, shaping her pulse the way she had once shaped phrases on the bridges: a slow sarabande for the statue by the well, a lullaby for the kneeling one whose stone hands still cupped a dead seedling. [458] One by one they let her pass, and one by one something hairline-fine cracked across their faces, less like breaking than like waking. [481]

At the orchard's center grew a sapling of silver-barked apple, humming a single sustained note. [496] Mira knelt and worked her fingers into the soil to uproot it, and the ground resisted, not like dirt but like a hand holding on. [521] She almost wept at the wrongness of it, taking the orchard's child to ransom a city that had jailed her violin. [542] But tarnish is not ruin; under it, the silver waits. [552] She set her heart to the boldest rhythm she knew, the opening of the concerto she had never been allowed to finish, and the roots let go. [579]

Behind her came a sound like a quarry exhaling: the statues, breaking free of their stillness, stone flaking from shoulders that remembered how to move. [604] The gardeners, grey-skinned and blinking, followed her to the gate. [614] They did not stop her; they carried saplings of their own. [625]

At dawn the city's gear-courts woke to find trees planted in the seams of the pavement, already blossoming, already chiming, and a woman standing on the highest sky-bridge with a locust shell pressed to her chest like a tiny borrowed violin. [666] The commuters slowed. [669] Some of them, in the gap between knowing what they heard and understanding what it meant, felt their own pulses shift, soften, begin to keep time. [695]

The Bureau would come, of course. [701] Bureaus always come. [704] But statues with gardeners' hands were already teaching children to listen to their own hearts, and a heart, once taught its music, is very hard to confiscate. [731] Mira held the empty shell to her ear and heard, beneath the city's grinding, something green and resonant beginning to grow. [752]
