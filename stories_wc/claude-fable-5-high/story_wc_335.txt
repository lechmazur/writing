The library had been forgotten so thoroughly that even its dust had stopped expecting visitors. [15] Vess liked it that way. [20] She was the shard-mirror archivist, the last one, and her shelves held no books anymore, only fragments of silvered glass, each one carrying a shadow that had been peeled from an exile on the day the town cast them out. [60] Exile here was reversible, in principle. [66] Reclaim your shadow, walk it home before sunset, and the banishment unwrote itself. [79] Almost nobody managed it, because almost nobody believed the law meant what it said. [93]

The girl arrived at the hush of late afternoon, when the empty playground swing beyond the window drifted in the breeze as if remembering a child it once held. [122] Vess heard the door and began, immediately, to walk backwards between the stacks. [135] She always walked backwards when nervous; retreating face-first felt like surrender, and she refused to give any moment her spine. [155]

"We're closed," she said, still receding. [161] "You're forgotten," the girl said, "which is different." [169] She was perhaps twelve, hollow-cheeked, wearing the gray armband of an exile's daughter. [182] "My mother was cast out four years ago, and I want her shadow back." [196]

Vess stopped retreating. [199] Four thousand shards lined her walls, and most were anonymous; the council burned the registries when it discovered exile could be undone. [221] So Vess had spent twenty years rebuilding the catalog her own way, annotating each shard with a name divined by analyzing the geometry of tea leaves. [247] A shadow, steeped in hot water with the right leaves, left angles in the cup: the slant of a shoulder, the pitch of a remembered walk. [273] Geometry did not lie, even when councils did. [281]

"This isn't a wishing well," Vess snapped, already filling the kettle, which was its own kind of yes. [299] "Sit down, touch nothing, and stop looking grateful; gratitude makes people careless." [311] The girl, whose name was Odile, sat. [318]

For three days they worked, and Vess was merciless in the way of someone who could not afford to be soft and could not stop being kind. [345] She made Odile brew every cup herself, read every angle herself, log every failure in the annotation ledger in her own shaking hand. [368] "Wrong," Vess would bark, and then, quieter: "Wrong the same way I was wrong for six years, so write it down and try again." [392] She never said she was teaching the girl to survive disappointment; saying it aloud would have ruined the lesson. [411]

On the fourth afternoon, Odile lifted a shard from the lowest shelf and went still. [426] Inside the glass, an afternoon shadow lay caught, long and slanted, the shadow of a woman pushing something, and through the window behind them, the empty swing moved, and the shadow in the shard moved with it. [463] "She used to push me on that swing every afternoon," Odile whispered. [475]

Vess found herself walking backwards toward the kettle. [483] "A resemblance proves nothing," she said, harsher than she meant, because hope was the most dangerous thing on her shelves. "Brew it." [505] Odile brewed it alone, hands steady now, and tipped the cup. [516] The leaves settled into two arcs meeting at a point: the geometry of a swing's chains, the exact angle of a mother's reach. [539] Vess annotated the shard in red ink, the color she had used only once before, for her own. [557]

"You knew exile was reversible all along," Odile said, "so why did you never reclaim yours?" [573] "Because somebody has to keep the door open," Vess said, "and because going home means there's no one left here who knows the leaves." [597] "Until now," Odile said. [601]

Vess scowled at her for a long moment, then took down her own shard from behind the desk, the one annotated in red twenty years ago and never carried home. [631] "Don't make this sentimental," she warned, eyes wet, voice like a slammed drawer. [644] "I'm only leaving because you'd ruin my catalog otherwise." [653] Odile, who had learned exactly how to hear her, said nothing and smiled at the ledger instead. [670]

They left together at the hush of late afternoon, two shards wrapped in cloth, while behind them the forgotten library held its breath and four thousand shadows waited their turn. [700] At the playground gate, the empty swing drifted once in the breeze, and the shadow in Odile's shard leaned forward in the glass, as if reaching for the push. [729] Vess walked forward the whole way. [735]
