Maren had not asked to keep the orchard, but the orchard had asked to be kept, and on the cliffs above the labyrinth there was no one else left to refuse. [31] The trees were cursed in a quiet way: any skin that touched their bark gave up its warmth forever, so she had spent eleven winters learning to tend them without touching anything at all. [65] She pruned with the pressure of her presence, leaning close until a branch remembered which way to grow, leaving impressions without touching, the way breath dents a fog. [93] It was a graceless skill, all hunched shoulders and held breath, and it was the most beautiful thing she did. [113] Below her, carved into the cliffside, the labyrinth coiled toward the sea, patrolled by mechanical birds whose brass wings had not been oiled in a generation. [139] They had been built to guide travelers through, once, but they had forgotten their song, and now they only circled, creaking, misleading everyone equally. [163] At dusk she heard voices down in the stone corridors: a family, by the sound, three of them, arguing about a turn they had taken an hour ago. [191] The night frost was coming; she could smell it, metallic and patient. [203] When frost formed in the labyrinth, the walls slicked, the birds roosted, and lost people stopped being lost and started being dead. [225] Maren went to the oldest tree, the one the architect had planted before anyone alive was born. [242] It had given exactly one fruit this year: a small nut, dull as a knuckle, hanging where she could not reach without ruin. [265] She crouched beneath it and exhaled, slow and deliberate, until the branch dipped under the weight of her attention and let the nut fall onto flat stone. [292] It split clean. [295] Inside, the kernel was etched with whorls too regular for nature: corridors, dead ends, a spiraling route to the sea gate. [316] The architect had grown the orchard from the labyrinth's own seed, and the trees had kept the forgotten blueprint in their fruit all along, waiting for someone patient enough not to grab. [348] Maren studied it the way she studied everything, sidelong and unhurried, and her reading was offhandedly exact: forty paces east, third gap, double back at the cracked lintel. [376] Eleven winters of measuring nothing had made her incapable of being wrong. [388] She could not enter the labyrinth herself; the curse traveled in her now, and the walls were the orchard's roots wearing stone. [410] But the birds had been built to read the architect's hand, and now the architect's hand was hers to copy. [430] She climbed to the cliff's rim, where the first rime was already silvering the rock, and began to write in it without writing. [453] Her palms hovered an inch above the frost, and the frost, feeling watched, refused to form beneath them, leaving dark marks shaped like turns and tallies. [479] It was ugly work, lurching along the rim on her knees, but the marks shone like wet ink under the rising moon. [501] One by one the mechanical birds dropped out of their useless circles to read her. [516] They were broken in different ways, each holding a fragment of the old guiding song, and Maren understood that the route would only sound true if she could assemble them in the blueprint's order. [550] So she marked perches along the rim, one dark impression for each bird, spaced like notes on a stave. [569] The birds settled onto them, rattling, and when the wind crossed their open throats in sequence, the fragments locked together into one long, climbing call. [594] Down in the corridors, the voices went quiet, then turned. [604] Maren crawled the rim ahead of them, her breath ragged, pressing the next mark, the next perch, the next note, always one corridor in front of the family's lanternlight. [633] Twice she nearly fell. [637] Once a bird's wing brushed her sleeve and stuttered, and she had to coax it back into the song with nothing but the steadiness of staying near. [664] The frost chased her the whole way, sealing each mark behind her like a door closing, so that the path existed only for as long as it was needed. [693] At the sea gate, three strangers stumbled out onto open sand, breathing hard, alive. [707] They looked up and saw only a hunched figure on the cliff and a line of rusted birds singing one impossible note. [729] Maren did not wave; waving was a kind of touch she had given up. [743] She walked back through her graceless, beautiful trees, set the split nut at the old one's roots, and felt the orchard, for the first time in eleven winters, lean toward her like something kept.
