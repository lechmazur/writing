Imka called herself an esoteric realist, which meant she believed in everything she could touch twice. [16] Once with the hand, once with the memory. [24] She had lived three seasons in the verdant grotto above the clouds, a green throat of stone where ferns drank the mist that rose past the mountain's shoulder. [52] She was transiently rooted there, the way a seed is rooted in a passing bird. [67] She would leave when her work was done; she just no longer knew what done would look like. [85] Around her neck, in a glass locket, she carried a single moth wing. [98] It had belonged to the last Ridgeway silk moth, a species that vanished forty years ago without a single obituary. [118] No one had counted its final flight. [125] Imka had decided that no one would make that mistake again, not while she had hands. [141] This was the season of the last monarch butterfly. [150] The naturalists in the lowlands had confirmed it: one tagged female, the final survivor of the great orange rivers, was migrating south alone. [173] Her route would carry her over the mountain, through the cold ceiling of cloud, past Imka's grotto. [190] Migration is a cadence, Imka knew, a rhythm kept by millions of wings agreeing on a direction. [207] One butterfly alone is a cadence interrupted, a sentence stopped at the comma. [220] Without the others, the survivor might circle, falter, drop into the cloud sea and end the story mid-measure. [238] So Imka built signals. [242] She did not build monuments; monuments were for histories already honored. [253] She built temporary structures: arches of bent willow, towers of pale stones, frames strung with wet leaves that flashed when the sun crossed them. [277] Each one stood for a day, maybe two, then she unmade it and built the next farther south, a relay of brightness laid across the ridge like stepping stones for the eye. [309] Butterflies navigate by light and landmark. [315] If the mountain offered no flock to follow, it could at least offer a path. [330] On the seventh morning the air turned rose-colored and utterly still, a blushing stillness, as if the sky were holding its breath out of politeness. [355] Imka knelt at the grotto's lip and began to knot. [365] She knotted long grasses into a wide spiral on the southernmost ledge, each knot pulled tight, each loop catching dew that burned pink in the early light. [392] Her grandmother had taught her these knots, fisherwoman's knots from a drowned village no map remembered. [408] Using them was a way of saying: you happened, you mattered, your hands are still working through mine. [426] At the spiral's center she opened the locket and laid the moth wing down, weighting its base with a pebble. [446] Let the forgotten guide the nearly gone, she thought. [455] It was the most realist thing she could imagine, and the most esoteric: an offering that doubled as an instrument. [475] The wing's scales scattered light the way monarchs prefer it, ultraviolet and slanting. [488] Then she waited, and the stillness deepened until she could hear the ferns release their mist. [504] Near noon, a fleck of orange rose out of the cloud sea. [516] The monarch flew in stutters, four beats and a pause, four beats and a pause, the broken meter of a creature keeping time for an orchestra that no longer existed. [546] She drifted toward the first willow arch, hesitated, and dropped. [556] Imka did not breathe. [560] The butterfly caught herself on the rising thermal that the stone tower had been built to mark, lifted, and followed the relay of structures ledge by ledge. [587] At the knotted spiral she circled twice, descending, and landed beside the moth wing. [601] For one long minute, the last of one history rested beside the relic of another, and the grotto held them both in its green quiet. [626] Imka recorded everything in her notebook: the hour, the wind, the exact angle of the wings at rest. [644] Witness is the only afterlife I can verify, she wrote. [654] When the monarch rose again, her cadence had changed. [663] The pauses were gone; the four beats ran into eight, into twelve, a sentence finally finding its way past the comma. [684] She slipped over the southern ridge, into the warm lands, toward whatever ending or beginning waited there. [701] Imka unmade the spiral that evening, knot by knot, leaving the ledge bare. [714] Only the moth wing she kept, returning it to the locket, a small archive against her sternum. [731] Then she packed her tools and her notebook and stood at the grotto's mouth, transiently rooted no longer. [749] Her work here was done, and now she finally knew what done looked like. [763] It looked like a witnessed departure. [769] She started down the mountain in the blush of evening, following the butterfly south, already planning the next signal for the next history someone had almost forgotten to count. [798]
