The blood test was supposed to settle a paternity question; instead it told Mara she had been two people all along. [21] Chimerism, the doctor said: a twin sister absorbed in the womb, her cells still living in Mara's skin, her liver, perhaps her mind. [44] That was when the memories began arriving wrong. [52] She would recall her seventh birthday and find a second girl in it, laughing at the edge of the frame, and each time Mara looked, the girl stood closer. [81] Her mother had worked the carnival ship Calliope's Wake before it was condemned, and the only thing she left Mara was a paint company's emerald swatch card, the corner worn soft, one shade circled in pencil: Garden Gate Green. [120] On the back, in her mother's hand: We painted her name in the garden, where the forgotten ones go. [139] So Mara rowed out into the bay where the Calliope's Wake still drifted, slipped from its moorings years ago, circling on the tide, trapped by the harbor's arms in a fog that never fully lifted. [174] It was a free ship that could go nowhere, and climbing its rusted ladder, Mara understood it perfectly. [192] The deck was a drowned midway: a carousel furred with salt, ticket booths sagging like tired mouths, banners reduced to gray threads. [214] Mara had learned, in the months since the diagnosis, that her sister's fragments could not be forced. [231] They could only be recaptured through narrative threads: she had to tell the memory aloud, as a story, and follow where the telling bent away from her own truth. [260] "Once," she said to the carousel, "a woman brought her daughter to a ship of lights." [276] The fog seemed to lean in. [282] "No," the story corrected itself in her mouth, "her daughters." [292] The word arrived like a key turning. [299] She kept telling as she walked: the hall of mirrors, where her reflection lagged half a heartbeat behind; the gutted big top below decks, where she narrated a trapeze act she had never seen and her hands remembered chalk. [338] Each story altered the memories it touched. [345] Her seventh birthday rewrote itself: now the second girl held the knife with her, two grips on one handle, cutting the cake together. [368] Mara should have been frightened by what she was becoming. [378] Instead she felt blissfully aberrant, a happy error, a body that had refused to be only one person. [396] The doctors called her condition a malformation; she was beginning to call it company. [410] Deep in the stern she found a door painted the exact emerald of the swatch card. [426] She held the card against the flaking paint and the two greens agreed like rhyming lines. [442] Beyond it lay the garden of forgotten names: a greenhouse hold where the carnival folk had grown tomatoes and roses between seasons, every pot and beam painted with the names of stillborn children, vanished workers, dead loves, anyone the world outside had stopped saying aloud. [487] The plants were long dead, but the names survived in Garden Gate Green. [500] Mara walked the rows, telling each name like a sentence in a longer story, until she found, on a clay pot small as a cupped hand, the word ILSE. [529] "Ilse," she said, and her whole body answered, both bloods at once. [541] She had expected the name to be a flame, a warmth she could finally light herself by. [558] What she found instead was an unkindled solace: comfort without fire, the cool peace of a lamp that does not need to burn because the dark has stopped being empty. [588] Ilse had never left; she had only been unnamed. [597] Mara sat among the dead roses and told the whole story again from the beginning, this time with two daughters in every scene, and felt her past settle into its truer shape. [629] When she climbed back to the deck, the fog had not lifted; it never would. [644] The ship still circled the bay, free of its moorings and prisoner of the harbor, and Mara stood at the rail and recognized her own life: a body that was a cage holding two lives, and also the only place those two lives could be together. [690] Trapped, she thought, and free; the same drifting circle, depending on the telling. [703] She slid the emerald swatch card into the clay pot, a marker, a promise to return. [719] Then she rowed back through the fog, talking the whole way, because the memories would keep altering with every telling, and she wanted Ilse to hear what they became. [748] Somewhere in her blood, a sister was listening, finally named. [758]
