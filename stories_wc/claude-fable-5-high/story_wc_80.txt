The drawbridge rose at six, and the channel town sealed itself like a closing eye. [15] Mara Voss waited for that hour, because only then did the reservoir belong to her alone. [31] She was a shore surveyor by trade, firm in her measurements and firmer in her silences. [47] The county paid her to chart erosion along the drowned town's edges, where a valley had been flooded forty years before. [68] What the county did not pay for was the pavilion. [78] It stood submerged in the shallows, an octagon of stone columns that broke the surface only in drought years like this one. [100] Tonight the water had dropped low enough to wade its perimeter, and the setting sun lit the lake like a hushed blaze, all fire and no sound. [127] Mara waded in with her transit case and her habit of noticing what she was not paid to notice. [146] Colleagues called her cold; in truth she was stoically sensitive, feeling everything and reporting none of it. [163] The pavilion's foundation had been patched, decades ago, with mortar a shade paler than the original stone. [180] She had documented such repairs before, but never ones that rang hollow under her knuckles. [195] She tapped along the base and counted nine hollow places, spaced evenly, deliberate as a sentence. [211] With her chisel she opened the first patch and found a glass vial, corked, holding a folded note. [229] The ink had survived the years: For the daughter I never met, I built this place to dance in. [248] Mara stood very still in the cooling water. [256] The second vial held another note: For the orchard we planted and never harvested. [270] The third: For my brother, who left before the flooding and never wrote back. [284] Each note was a loss, and each loss was threaded through the others, an interlaced absence holding the foundation together as surely as the mortar did. [310] Whoever had repaired this pavilion had not been fixing stone; they had been burying what the rising water could not be allowed to take. [334] The ninth cavity was deeper than the rest, and it did not give easily. [348] Mara had to wrest it free, bracing her boot against the column and pulling until the mortar surrendered with a wet crack. [370] Inside, wrapped in oilcloth, sat an antique traveling inkwell, brass-capped, its glass heart still dark with dried ink. [388] A final note was wound around it: This wrote every promise made in the valley. [403] Let it write no more lies about forgetting. [411] Mara turned the inkwell in her palm and understood she was holding the instrument of all nine confessions. [429] Someone had dipped a pen into this small darkness and inventoried everything the lake had erased. [445] She thought of her own ledgers, full of contour lines and elevations, recording where the shore stood but never what the shore had cost. [469] Forty years of survey reports, and not one of them named the orchard, the daughter, the brother who never wrote. [489] The light was failing now, the hushed blaze of the sunset banking down to embers on the water. [507] Mara made her decision the way she made all of them, quietly and completely. [521] She would finish the county's erosion charts, because she was a professional and the shore deserved a witness. [539] But after that, she would become something the county had no title for: an archeologist of abandoned dreams. [557] She would map every flooded valley in the district, find every patched foundation, and read what people had sealed inside their losses. [579] First, though, there was a debt to pay here. [588] She took her field notebook, tore out a clean page, and pried the cap from the antique inkwell. [606] A few drops of lake water revived the dried ink enough for a thin, stubborn gray. [622] With the tip of her chisel she wrote: For the surveyor who measured everything and admitted nothing, until tonight. [641] She rolled the page into the ninth cavity, repacked the mortar she had broken, and pressed it smooth with her thumb. [662] Her absence would interlace with the others now, the tenth strand in the weave. [676] The pavilion would hold it the way it held everything: patiently, underwater, waiting for the next drought and the next reader. [697] She kept the inkwell. [701] It would travel with her, as it was built to do, from one drowned valley to the next. [719] When she waded back to shore, the drawbridge was still raised, the town still sealed, the lake gone black and quiet. [740] Mara sat on the embankment and waited for the bridge to lower, the inkwell warm in her pocket like a banked coal. [762] Somewhere beneath the surface, nine small fires of memory burned on, and now a tenth, hers, hushed but unmistakably alight. [782]
