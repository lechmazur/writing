After the spoons bent, the world stopped trusting straight lines. [10] Nobody knew why a drawer of cutlery in a Ministry canteen had folded itself into impossible curves, but reality had folded with it, and every rune written before that morning went quietly dead. [43] Mirelle Asch, the youngest scholar ever admitted to the Order of the Drawn Line, had to relearn her entire craft from the first stroke. [67] She did it the way she did everything: quietly, deliberately, one corrected line at a time. [83] The Order posted her to the nocturnal observatory on Calder's Isle, a shard of granite that drifted a mile above the sea and only did its work in darkness. [112] Its instruments did not watch stars. [118] They watched shadows. [121] Because after the bend, shadows had stopped behaving. [129] At noon, when the sun stood directly overhead, they did not shrink to nothing; they left. [145] Mirelle had watched the plates a hundred times: her own shadow thinning, then sliding sideways like water finding a drain, gone until one minute past twelve. [171] Where shadows went at noon was the question she had crossed an ocean of air to answer. [188]

The observatory's archive held the wreckage of the old craft: rune plates that no longer hummed, and one sealed vault the previous resident had labeled, in shaky charcoal, AWAKENINGS - DO NOT. [219] The sentence ended there, which Mirelle found more persuasive than any complete warning. [232] Inside the vault stood an automaton, man-shaped, its brass chest covered in runes that, unlike every other rune on the isle, still glowed. [255] Around its neck, on a leather cord, hung a clockwork key. [266] She did not turn it. [271] She studied it for nine nights instead. [278]

The glowing runes taught her what the Order's manuals had missed. [289] Old runes had been straight lines, open figures, marks that pointed somewhere; the bend had broken every line that pointed. [309] The automaton's runes were circles. [314] They survived because they ended where they began. [322] Power, after the bend, lived only in closed loops. [331] So she relearned, night by night, by closing loops: tracing each broken rune in the archive and curving its dead ends back into themselves until they hummed alive again. [360]

On the tenth night she found the previous scholar's journal wedged behind the vault door. [375] The shadows are not destroyed at noon, it read. [384] They are unanchored. [387] A shadow is a loop between a body and its light; the bend cut those loops, and at noon, when the loop is shortest, the cut ends slip free and fall into whatever still holds a closed circuit. [425] Mirelle looked at the automaton, the only closed circuit left on the isle, and understood why its runes glowed brighter every day at one minute past twelve. [452] The shadows were going into it. [458] It was filling. [461]

The journal's last page explained the label on the vault. [471] The old scholar had sealed the automaton mid-awakening, key half-turned, afraid of what a machine packed with the world's lost shadows might become if it finished waking. [498] But a sealed awakening, Mirelle realized, was an open loop: a turn begun and never completed, a cut end into which everything kept falling. [522] The fear that sealed it was the very thing feeding it. [533]

She waited for noon. [537] It felt wrong to do daylight work in a nocturnal observatory, but the loops only opened when the sun stood overhead. [558] At eleven fifty-nine she stood before the automaton, her own shadow already thinning at her feet. [574] She took the clockwork key from its cord. [582] Quietly, deliberately, the way she corrected a flawed line, she finished the turn the old scholar had abandoned. [600] The key clicked through its final notch, a small circle completed. [611]

The automaton's chest runes flared, and at noon exactly, the shadows reversed. [623] They poured out of the brass body in a dark tide, sliding across the observatory floor, out the door, down off the floating isle's edge, each one racing home to the body and light it belonged between. [660] Mirelle's own shadow snapped back beneath her feet and stayed. [670] The automaton opened two eyes of polished obsidian. [678] It was not a monster. [683] It was empty now, and awake, and it looked at her the way a student looks at a teacher. [702] "You closed me," it said, in a voice like a clock settling. [714] "The other one only opened me and ran." [722]

"Loops have to end where they begin," Mirelle said. [731] "I had to relearn that the hard way." [739]

That night the observatory turned its instruments back to the dark, and for the first time since the spoons bent, the shadow plates recorded nothing unusual at all. [767] Mirelle filed her report in a careful circle of script, beginning and ending with the same word, so it would hold. [788]
