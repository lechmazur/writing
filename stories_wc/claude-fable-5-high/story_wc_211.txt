The foghorn fell silent on a Tuesday, and the city below the orchard forgot how to apologize. [17] Mirena Vasht, venerable oath scribe of the hundred-and-fourth floor, noticed because apologies were her trade. [32] For sixty years she had taken oaths in the ancient orchard garden atop the Calder Spire, where apple trees older than the building itself grew from soil hauled up before anyone living could remember. [66] People climbed to her to swear things they could not say at street level. [80] She wrote each oath in a dead language, because dead languages cannot be argued with, and she spoke all of them: Hittite for grief, Old Avestan for love, a drowned coastal tongue for promises made to the sea. [118] But ink kept only the words, and Mirena had come to believe the words were the least of an oath. [138] So she recorded the voices too, on wax cylinders, building an archive of emotional patterns: the catch before a vow, the tremor after, the silence where the lie would have gone. [169] Someday, she reasoned, someone would need to know not what people promised, but how it felt to promise. [187]

The night after the foghorn went quiet, she was shaving down an old cylinder for reuse when the curl of wax dropped into her lap and did not behave like waste. [218] It lay there glinting under the orchard lanterns, its groove catching light like a thread of writing. [235] Mirena had shaved a thousand cylinders and never once looked at the leavings. [248] But the city's hush had made her attentive to small sounds, small things, and the shaving seemed to hum against her palm. [270]

A memory surfaced, unbidden: third grade, a field trip to the Maritime Sound Museum, a curator with chalk-white gloves. [289] He had dropped a wax shaving into a basin of warm water, and the children leaned over the rim as the curl relaxed and unspooled into a flat ribbon. [318] "Even the scraps remember," the curator had said, drawing a needle along the softened groove by hand, coaxing out a ghost of foghorn song. [342] Mirena had not thought of that morning in seventy years. [352]

Now, in the lantern dark, she warmed a copper bowl of water among the apple roots and let herself immerse the shaving, watching it slacken and stretch the way memory does. [383] With a sewing needle and her good ear, she traced the groove. [395] A voice rose, faint as breath on glass, speaking Old Avestan. [406] It should have been a love oath; she had recorded dozens on that cylinder. [420] But the shaving had been cut at an angle, slicing across years of recordings, splicing syllables from a hundred different vows into one continuous utterance. [445] And the utterance made sense. [450] No one had spoken it; it had assembled itself by accident, the way driftwood sometimes washes ashore in the shape of a hand. [473] Mirena translated as she listened, her lips moving: When the horn forgets the harbor, the keeper of feelings must give them back. [495]

The needle slipped from her fingers. [501] She sat among her cabinets of cylinders, sixty years of archived trembling, and felt the soft wrongness of being addressed by an accident. [524] A prophecy no one intended is still a prophecy, she thought; perhaps it is the only honest kind. [542] The foghorn had fallen silent because the harbor authority had gone bankrupt, nothing more. [556] And yet ships still moved in the fog below, blind now, and people still climbed her stairs with feelings too heavy to carry alone, and she had been keeping those feelings instead of returning them. [591] Archiving, she saw suddenly, was a gentle word for hoarding. [601]

Before dawn she hauled the phonograph to the orchard's east wall, where the parapet overlooked the harbor's gray throat. [620] She fitted the great brass horn, the one shaped, she realized, like the dead foghorn's mouth. [636] Then she began to play the archive outward, cylinder after cylinder, into the fog. [650] Vows in Hittite, apologies in Sumerian, a fisherman's promise in the drowned coastal tongue rolled down the skyscraper's flank and over the water. [673] No one below understood the words, but understanding was never what she had archived. [687] They heard the catch, the tremor, the held breath, and the harbor pilots steered toward the sound the way their grandparents had once steered by the horn. [714] In the orchard, apples loosened in the dark and fell, soft drumbeats keeping time. [728] Mirena played until daylight thinned the fog, then kept the unspooled shaving in her breast pocket, the only record she would never give away. [752] It had asked something of her in a language no living mouth had assembled, and she, who spoke for the dead, had answered. [775]
