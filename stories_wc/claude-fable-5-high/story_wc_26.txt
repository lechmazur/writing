After dreams became shareable, the world stopped keeping its own dark. [11] Mireille understood this better than anyone, because she was a dusk noticer, sharp-eyed and licensed, paid by the Archive to watch what the light surrendered each evening. [38] Her post was the blooming library above the clouds, a tower where wisteria had been bred to climb the shelves and flower against the spines, so that the books smelled of pollen and old rain. [73] Below her, the cloud deck burned orange at sunset; above her, the first stars opened like careful eyes. [91] In her pocket she kept a river stone, gray and thumb-worn, the only object her grandmother had ever refused to dream about. [113] "Some things," the old woman had said, "should stay unshared, so hope remembers its own shape." [129] Mireille had not understood until the shadows started leaving. [138]

It began as a flaw in the dusk reports: silhouettes that arrived a half-second late, outlines that lingered after their owners turned away. [161] The Archive blamed lens decay. [166] Mireille blamed the dreams. [170] So she did what noticers do: she compared silent video footage from decades apart, reel against feed, the pre-sharing world against the present one. [194] The silence mattered; without sound, the eye stops forgiving. [203] In footage from sixty years ago, a girl on a bridge tossed a stone, and her shadow leapt with her, joyful and bound, stitched to her heels like loyalty. [232] In footage from last spring, a boy on the same bridge laughed at something, and his shadow stood still, watching him the way a stranger watches a train depart. [261] Frame by frame, decade by decade, Mireille watched humanity's shadows metamorphose: from faithful dark twins into something separate, restless, unbound. [281] She understood why, and the understanding arrived like grief. [290] When dreams became shareable, people stopped keeping anything private, and the shadow, the oldest private thing, the shape of what a person hopes and fears in secret, had nothing left to anchor it. [323] Hope, broadcast nightly to millions, had been smoothed into one bright, identical wish. [336] And shadows cannot live on identical light. [343]

That night, one came to the library. [350] Mireille saw it at dusk, naturally, sliding up the cloud deck like spilled ink climbing a wall, softly deliberate, unhurried in the way of things that have already lost everything. [380] It moved through the wisteria without bending a single blossom. [390] It had no owner; she checked the windows, the stairs, the sky. [402] A shadow unbound, the first she had seen whole, and it had come to her shelves the way the starving come to bakeries: not to steal, only to remember. [431] It pooled before the old projection wall where her comparison footage still ran, and it pressed itself against the image of the girl on the bridge, trying on her bound shadow like a coat it used to own. [469]

Mireille could have reported it. [474] The Archive dissolved anomalies; that was policy. [481] Instead she took out the river stone. [488] Her grandmother had carried it from a river that no longer existed, had held it every night before sleeping, and had never once let it appear in a shared dream. [518] It was, perhaps, the last object on earth still soaked in private hope, hope with edges, hope with a particular weight and a particular gray. [543] She set it on the floor in the projector's beam. [553] The stone cast a small, stubborn shadow, and the unbound thing turned toward it the way a face turns toward warmth. [574] "Here," Mireille whispered, though it had no ears. [582] "This is what a kept wish looks like." [590] Slowly, softly, deliberately, the shadow gathered itself around the stone's outline and learned it, the way water learns a riverbed. [610] It did not become bound again; that grace was gone. [620] But it took a shape, a small, definite, riverworn shape, and held it. [633]

Mireille spent the rest of her life as a quiet heretic. [644] She never reported the shadow that lived in her library, drifting among the blossoms at dusk in the shape of a stone. [666] She began, instead, to collect unshared things from the people who came up through the clouds: a key, a braid, a child's crooked drawing, each one kept secret, each one casting its own honest dark. [701] Other unbound shadows found their way to the tower, year after year, and one by one they chose shapes and kept them. [723] She knew she could not undo what sharing had unmade. [733] The girl on the bridge was decades dead; the world's hope would never again fit a single pair of heels. [753] But on clear evenings, when the sun fell through the cloud deck and the library bloomed gold, Mireille stood among her gathered shadows and felt something that was not quite sorrow and not quite peace. [788] Hope had lost its body, but she had preserved its shape. [799]
