The universe had been contracting for nine hundred years when Sefa inherited the observatory. [14] Light arrived compressed now, blueshifted into needles, and the deck where she worked had been glazed in amber resin to soften it. [36] Everything on the ambered observatory deck glowed the color of preserved honey: the consoles, the brass rails, her own hands. [56] Sefa was the station's decision maker, though she never decided anything herself. [68] She cast lots. [71] Her instrument was a collection of foreign coins, gathered by her predecessors from worlds that no longer existed: a square zinc piece from Theret, a holed disc from the Lantern Moons, a coin of pressed glass minted by a people who had melted back into their sun. [118] Heads meant yes, tails meant no, and the edge meant wait. [129] She trusted the coins because she had stopped trusting the stars. [140] The stars had promised expansion forever, and the stars had lied. [151] That doubt lived in her like a low hum, stellar and constant. [163]

What the observatory received now was not starlight but static: bursts of it, arriving in clusters, as the collapsing sky folded old signals back toward their sources. [190] Her predecessors had dismissed the bursts as noise. [198] Sefa, quietly enthusiastic in a way that never raised her voice, suspected otherwise. [211] She would smile at the receivers each morning, a small private smile, and say, almost inaudibly, today you will tell me something. [233] Her work was to distill the static, boiling away the random crackle until only structure remained, the way her grandmother had distilled bitter leaves into medicine. [259] It took her eleven years. [264]

What remained, after the distillation, was a voice. [272] It spoke in reverse. [276] Of course it did, she realized, sitting very still on the amber deck: in a contracting universe, transmissions returning home arrived time-flipped, last word first. [301] The voice was a prophecy, and the prophecy ran backwards. [311] Played as received, it began with an ending: and so the keeper chose, and the choice was wrong, and the dark came early. [334] Reversed properly, it ended there instead. [340] It began: there is a frequency at which a person and a purpose are the same wave. [357]

Sefa played it forty times. [362] The middle of the prophecy was instruction: the station's great dish could be tuned to broadcast a counter-signal, a single sustained note that might slow the contraction in their corner of the sky, buying centuries. [397] But the tuning required a keeper to stand inside the dish's focus and match it, body and signal, wavelength aligned with purpose, for as long as the broadcast lasted. [426] Which might be the rest of her life. [434] And the prophecy's first-heard line, its true last line, still waited: the choice was wrong, and the dark came early. [454]

Wrong which way? [457] If she broadcast and the prophecy condemned broadcasting, she would hasten the end. [470] If she stayed silent and the prophecy condemned silence, the same. [481] Her stellar doubt flared: even this voice had crossed the lying sky to reach her. [496] So she did what keepers did. [502] She spread the foreign coins across the amber deck and chose the glass coin, the one from the people who had gone into their sun. [527] Heads, broadcast. [529] Tails, silence. [531] She flipped it. [534]

The coin rose through the honeyed light, turning, and she watched it the way she had once watched stars, and she understood, suddenly, what the prophecy had been trying to teach her all along. [568] A backwards prophecy is read in the wrong order by everyone who receives it honestly. [583] Its warning was not about which choice she made. [592] It was about how. [596] And so the keeper chose: the wrong choice was the choosing by chance, surrendering the one decision that demanded a self behind it. [619]

She caught the coin out of the air without looking at it. [631] Then she set it down, face hidden, among its dead-world kin. [642] Sefa walked to the dish at the deck's edge, where the focus point waited like a held breath. [660] She stepped into it. [664] The hum found her bones first, then her pulse, then something deeper that had no name, and she let herself match it, note for note, a person and a purpose becoming the same wave. [698] Her doubt did not vanish; it simply joined the signal, one more honest frequency. [712]

Far out, where the sky was folding, the contraction did not stop. [724] But it slowed, in one amber corner, the way breath slows when someone finally means it. [740] On the deck behind her, the glass coin lay where she had left it, unread. [755] Whatever face it showed, it showed to no one, and the dark, for now, came late. [771]
