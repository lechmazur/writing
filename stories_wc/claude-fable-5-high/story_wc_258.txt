The cryptocurrency crashed on a Tuesday, and by Friday, Mira had nothing left but a key. [16] The key opened her grandmother's loft, a place she had not entered since the funeral two years before. [34] Rain beaded on the skylight overhead, scattering gray light across a worktable still dusted with onyx ink. [51] Her grandmother had been a cartomancer, but not the candle-and-incense kind. [62] She had drawn her own cards by hand, every figure rendered in the same dense black ink, and she had read fortunes for the whole neighborhood for forty years. [91] Mira had been a systems analyst until the crash erased her firm, and she did not believe in fortunes. [110] She believed in patterns. [114]

The deck waited in the table's drawer beside a cloth measuring tape coiled like a sleeping snake. [131] Seventy-eight cards, each one slightly different in size, which made no sense for a printed deck and perfect sense for a handmade one. [154] Except her grandmother had been meticulous about everything else. [163] Mira, steadily curious in the way that had once made her good at her job, began to measure. [181] The Tower was eighty-nine millimeters tall. [187] The Star, one hundred and forty-four. [193] The Moon, two hundred and thirty-three. [199] Fibonacci. [200] Her hands went cold. [204] The widths formed a second sequence; the corner radii a third. [215] This was not mysticism. [219] This was encoding. [222]

She spent a week reverse engineering the deck the way she had once dissected competitors' software: isolate variables, find dependencies, work backward from output to architecture. [248] The output was forty years of readings; the architecture was hidden in the cards themselves. [263] Behind a loose panel she found the notebooks. [271] Hundreds of them, each reading logged in her grandmother's slanted hand: the client, the cards drawn, the question asked. [290] Marriages, debts, hauntings, layoffs, illnesses, lottery hopes. [297] Her grandmother had not been telling fortunes. [304] She had been collecting data. [309]

Mira sat under the rain-beaded skylight and read until the gray light failed. [322] The notebooks revealed a method she slowly came to admire. [332] For every client, her grandmother had sketched not one future but many, multiplying paths outward from each card: if he takes the job, if she forgives him, if the building sells. [363] Where the multiplied paths converged, she marked the page with a small black sun. [377] Those convergences, traced across decades of clients, formed a map. [387] Not of individual fates, but of the neighborhood itself: which fears spread street by street, which hopes recurred every generation, how a rumor on Delancey became a divorce on Ludlow became a christening on Grand. [422] Her grandmother had been mapping cultural patterns the way meteorologists map storms. [434] The cards were just the instrument that made people confide. [444] Mira understood that part immediately. [449] Nobody tells the truth to a survey. [456] But across a candlelit table, holding a stranger's inked cards, people confess everything. [469]

On the ninth day, someone knocked. [475] A man around sixty stood in the stairwell, soaked through, holding a fistful of worthless crypto wallets printed on paper. [495] "Your grandmother read for me in ninety-one," he said. [504] "She told me a crash was coming and I didn't listen, not then and not now." [520] He needed to confide in someone, and Mira, almost without deciding, sat him at the worktable. [536] She did not know the readings, but she knew the architecture now. [548] She drew three cards and asked him questions the notebooks had taught her: not what he wanted, but what he was afraid his children would inherit. [574] He talked for an hour. [579] When he left, lighter, Mira logged the reading in a fresh notebook. [591]

She understood the inheritance now, and it was not the loft. [602] The deck had been built to outlive its maker, its measurements a manual for whoever was patient enough to take them. [623] Her grandmother had not left her money; she had left her a method. [636] The crash had emptied Mira's accounts but also her certainty that numbers on a screen meant anything at all. [655] These numbers were different. [659] They were measurements of fear and hope, taken one confession at a time, multiplied along every path a life could take. [680]

That night the rain stopped, and Mira laid the measuring tape across the Tower one more time. [697] Eighty-nine millimeters. [699] On the back, in onyx ink so faint she had missed it for days, a single line of her grandmother's handwriting: For the one who measures first and believes second. [729] Mira laughed under the clearing skylight, alone in the loft, holding the whole neighborhood in her hands. [746] Tomorrow she would start her own notebook in earnest, and the map would keep growing. [761]
