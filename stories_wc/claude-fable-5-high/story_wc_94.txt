The bus had stopped running to the observatory years ago, but Mara climbed the mountain road anyway, her breath thinning as the air began to rarefy. [26] She was a poet whose name had been inked out of every anthology in the lowlands, and she carried what remained of her work in her coat: an old bus timetable with a sealed envelope wedged between its yellowed pages. [66] The envelope was not hers. [71] It had arrived the week the censors came, slipped under her door by Ilya, the astronomer who had once set her banned verses to the rhythm of star charts. [100] Open it at the observatory, his note had said, when the storm sits below you and the masks slip. [119] She had waited two years, a vigil held in pause, because Ilya had vanished and opening the envelope felt like agreeing he was gone. [143] Tonight the storm had finally come. [149]

The observatory rose out of the cloud deck like a white skull, lightning flaring beneath it so the whole mountain seemed to stand on a floor of burning silver. [178] Above, the sky was furiously tranquil: stars utterly still while thunder raged under her boots. [193] Mara forced the rusted door and found the dome intact, the great telescope sleeving the dark like a patient throat. [213] In the back room, Ilya's old darkroom waited, trays dry, chemicals crusted, and on the shelf a mechanical darkroom timer, its white face scarred with thumbprints. [239]

She had come, she told herself, to understand the cartography of scars: the lines the censors cut from her poems, the lines the years cut into her hands, the line Ilya's absence had cut through her life. [276] If she could map them, perhaps she could read them. [286] She drew out the timetable, its routes to nowhere, and worked the envelope free. [300] Her hands shook. [303] Lightning washed the dome's slit window white, and in that flash she broke the seal. [318]

Inside lay a strip of photographic negatives and a single line in Ilya's hand: Eleven minutes. [334] Nothing else. [336] She almost laughed; the man had hidden whole symphonies in marginalia, and now he gave her a recipe. [354] But the darkroom still held its bottles, and storms made their own urgency. [367] She mixed developer by lantern light, slid the first negative beneath the enlarger, and twisted the timer to eleven. [386] Its tick filled the room like a second heart. [395]

Eleven minutes is a long time to stand in red darkness with the dead. [409] Thunder shouldered the walls. [413] She understood, slowly, that this too was the message: Ilya had built her a vigil with an ending, a pause that the timer itself would break, because he knew she could not break it alone. [448] The bell rang. [451] She bathed the paper, and the image rose like a drowned thing surfacing. [464] It was not a star field. [470] It was a page of her own poetry, photographed before the censors' ink, every forbidden line intact. [487]

Negative after negative, eleven minutes at a time, her erased books returned to her, and between the stanzas Ilya had written tiny coordinates, the declinations of stars he had named, privately and illegally, for each poem. [523] He had smuggled her voice into the sky itself. [532] On the last sheet, a photograph of Ilya, unmasked at last: not the careful state astronomer, but a thin man grinning beside the telescope, holding a sign that read THEY CANNOT CENSOR LIGHT. [565]

Mara carried the prints up into the dome as the storm began to thin. [579] Through the slit she found his coordinates one by one, and the stars stood where he had promised, ordinary and miraculous. [600] She traced them with a fingertip on the cold glass and felt the map complete itself: the scars on the page, the scars on her hands, the bright unkillable points above. [631] A scar, she understood now, was only a route the censors could not find on any timetable. [648]

Dawn came up through the dissolving clouds, gold flooding the silver, and Mara stood in storm-kissed wonder, her vigil finally moving again, like a clock wound by a friend's hand. [678] She set the darkroom timer in her pocket beside the empty envelope. [690] There were lowland towns where banned words still moved hand to hand, and she had eleven minutes of courage now, renewable forever. [712] She started down the mountain, breathing air that thickened with every step, carrying poems the sky had kept safe for her. [733]
