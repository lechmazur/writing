After the first snowmelt, the pilgrims came down the ravine to the bronze grotto, and Issel the fractal weaver heard them before the hush did. [25] The hush in that place was shingled, laid down in overlapping plates of quiet, each silence lapping the edge of the one beneath it, so that no sound ever fell straight through. [57] Footsteps arrived as memory arrives, softened, already half a dream. [67] Issel sat at her loom of branching thread, weaving the same pattern at every scale, spirals inside spirals inside spirals, because the pattern was the grotto and the grotto was the pattern. [99] She had tried, in her first century, to walk out. [109] Every direction unfolded into a smaller copy of where she already stood, bronze walls ribbed like the inside of a whale, starlight pooling through an oculus that repeated overhead no matter how far she went. [144] There was no wall to beat against, no door to curse. [155] She was boundary-lessly trapped, held by repetition instead of stone. [165] So she had given herself a purpose: to collect the weights of different burdens. [179] Not the burdens themselves, which people needed, but their weights, which people did not. [193] Each spring they brought her objects, and she wove the heaviness out of them, thread by thread, into her endless spiral. [214] The weights, once gathered, calcified. [219] That was what the grotto's ribs were: centuries of sorrow gone hard and pale, whalebone arches of other people's grief, holding up the bronze like the hull of something that had swallowed her long ago. [254] This year the pilgrims brought the usual cargo. [262] A soldier surrendered a boot lace; its weight was a friend's name. [274] A midwife gave up a cracked thimble; its weight was a child she could not save. [290] Issel weighed each on her palm, found the strand of heaviness inside, and drew it loose, and the pilgrims left lighter, climbing back toward the thaw. [316] Last came a girl with a kerosene lamp burner, brass, wickless, unscrewed from a lamp that no longer existed. [335] "My grandfather's," the girl said. [340] "He read to me by it every winter." [348] "The house burned; this is what the ash gave back." [358] Issel set the burner on her palm and waited for the familiar drag of grief. [373] Nothing came. [375] The burner was heavy as brass and no heavier, which had never once happened in seven hundred years. [393] "There is no weight in this," Issel said. [401] "I know," said the girl. [406] "I didn't bring it to be lightened." [413] "I brought it because a burner only works one way up." [424] Issel turned the thing in her fingers. [431] It was true. [434] A spiral could be read inward or outward forever, but a burner had an orientation: fuel below, wick through the channel, flame above, always above. [459] Fire did not repeat at every scale. [466] Fire pointed. [468] Her hands began to shake inside the shingled hush. [477] The grotto held her by sameness; every path she had ever walked curled back because she had walked it like a weaver, following pattern, trusting recursion. [503] But a flame would not curl back. [510] By orienting properly, by aligning herself to the one direction the fractal could not fold, she might find the seam at the top of all the repeating skies. [538] She pulled a strand of calcified grief from the nearest rib, pale as whalebone, and threaded it through the burner's channel for a wick. [562] Sorrow, she had learned, burns slow and long. [570] The girl struck a spark from her flint, and the flame stood up like a sentence finally spoken aloud. [589] It pointed at the oculus. [594] Issel rose and walked beneath it, holding the burner level, keeping the flame upright, refusing every spiral her feet had memorized. [615] The grotto tried its old trick; the walls budded into smaller grottos, the oculus multiplied like reflections of reflections. [634] But the flame did not multiply. [640] It chose one sky and held it, and she followed, the layers of the hush peeling open above her like shingles lifted by wind. [664] At the seam, she paused. [669] Below, the whalebone ribs of seven hundred years gleamed under starlight, every weight she had ever gathered, calcified into shelter for the next weaver, if a next weaver ever came. [699] She hoped no one came. [704] She climbed out into the cold mud of the thawing world, where water ran loud and nothing repeated, and the girl was waiting. [727] Issel offered back the burner, but the girl shook her head. [738] "Grandfather always said a light is only a burden if you forget which way to hold it." [755] So Issel kept it, her first possession with its weight still in it, and found she did not mind the heaviness at all. [778]
