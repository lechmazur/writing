After the last fish was caught, the sea stopped moving. [10] Not stormed, not sulked, simply stopped, lying flat from harbor to horizon like cooled wax. [25] The priests called it a cosmic imbalance, a ledger with one column emptied. [38] Tamsin, who was only a lukewarm mage, believed them completely. [48] Her magic worked on nothing hot and nothing cold, only the tepid middle where bathwater goes to be forgotten. [67] The Academy had graduated her with sympathy instead of honors. [77] But she had one inheritance the great fire-callers lacked: patience with in-between things. [90] Everyone knew the story of the bell. [97] The Verity's bell had rung the tides into rhythm for three hundred years, struck at every dawn from the sapphire viaduct that carried the old sea over the city. [126] When the last fish came up gasping in a net, the bell cracked clean through, as if the two facts were one fact. [149] Now it lay in the Tidewright's chapel in five pieces, and the sea lay dead, and the city was learning what silence weighs. [172] Tamsin went to the chapel with a wheelbarrow. [180] "You can't recast it," said the old Tidewright, not unkindly. [190] "Bronze wants a furnace, and a furnace wants a fire mage, and the fire mages have all refused." [208] "They say the bell is cursed with brittle devotion." [217] Tamsin asked what that meant. [222] "The founders poured pure faith into the casting, no doubt to temper it." [235] "Faith without doubt sets hard and shatters." [242] "The bell was always going to break; it just waited for a reason." [255] Tamsin, earnestly naive in the way that gets people drowned or sainted, said she would rebuild it anyway. [273] The Tidewright laughed, then saw her face, then stopped laughing. [283] She could not make a fire. [289] But cooling metal passes through lukewarm on its way to cold, and in that narrow corridor her magic was absolute. [309] If someone else melted the bronze, she could shape it in the moment between too-hot and too-late, knitting the grain so it would flex instead of crack. [336] The problem was knowing the moment. [342] Thermometers lie near magic; every apprentice learns that. [350] So she went to the viaduct. [356] The sapphire viaduct leaked, as it had for a century, thin drips falling from its blue underbelly into catch-basins below. [376] Cold stone holds water tight; warm stone lets it go. [386] The drips quickened as the day warmed and slowed as it cooled, a clock made of patience. [403] For forty days Tamsin sat beneath the arches with a tally-board, learning the dripping rates by heart. [420] Eleven drips a minute meant frost. [426] Nineteen meant the precise tepid hour when her power peaked. [436] She learned to read temperature in falling water the way sailors once read weather in gulls. [452] She built her foundry in a dry arch directly under the leak. [464] The Tidewright hauled coal and worked the bellows, because somebody had to be the fire she wasn't. [481] Into the crucible went the five pieces of the bell. [491] Into it also went a handful of her own failures: her rejection letter, ash now; an honest list of everything she did not believe. [515] "Doubt for temper," she said. [520] "Devotion alone is what broke it." [526] They poured at midnight and waited for dawn. [534] The bronze glowed, then dimmed, and Tamsin watched the leak instead of the metal. [548] Fifteen drips. [550] Seventeen. [551] At nineteen drips a minute, exactly as the sun touched the sapphire and turned the whole viaduct the color of a remembered ocean, she put her hands into the warmth. [581] The metal was not hot to her; nothing tepid ever was. [592] She felt the old devotion in the bronze, rigid and frightened, and she folded her doubt through it like a baker folding air into dough. [617] Where it wanted to be certain, she taught it to bend. [628] When the drips slowed to eighteen, she stepped back, and the bell stood whole. [642] It was not beautiful. [646] It was seamed and humble and slightly asymmetrical, like everything that has decided to survive. [661] They hung it from the viaduct rail at full dawn. [671] Tamsin struck it once. [675] The note that came out was not pure. [683] It wavered, questioned itself, resolved, and rolled out over the dead flat water like a tide rehearsing. [700] Nothing happened for a long minute. [706] Then, far out, the sea shrugged. [712] A single swell traveled toward the harbor, unhurried, as though the ocean were remembering an appointment. [728] And in the catch-basin at her feet, where forty days of drips had pooled, something small and silver flicked its tail. [749] One fish, returned to the ledger. [755] Not balance yet, but the beginning of arithmetic. [763] Tamsin rang the bell again, lukewarm and certain enough, and the water everywhere began, doubtfully, to move. [780]
