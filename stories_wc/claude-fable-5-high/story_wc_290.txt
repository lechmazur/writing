Mara Voss hated narrow places, which made her an unlikely explorer of the labyrinth beneath Hallowmere. [16] She went down anyway, every winter, because somebody had to. [26] During the fallow period, when the fields slept under frost and the church stood unlocked and empty, the passages beneath the village mutinied. [49] Corridors traded places overnight. [53] Stairways folded themselves into walls. [58] Cellars wandered off beneath other houses, taking the winter stores with them. [70] The elders called it the spatial mutiny, and they spoke of it the way sailors speak of storms: with respect, and a little envy. [94]

Once, the mutiny had been held in check. [102] Old Architect Voss, Mara's great-great-grandmother, had designed the labyrinth and tamed it with music. [116] Every fallow season, the church organist played a single sustained chord, and the stones, hearing it, remembered their places. [135] But the key — the precise key, the vital note around which the whole design had been drafted — died with the last organist eighty years ago, and no surviving score recorded it. [166] Mara meant to uncover it. [171]

She was prudently adventurous: she charted every descent, carried twice the rope she needed, and turned back at the first taste of panic. [194] But prudence had limits, and so did the village's patience with vanishing cellars. [207] In her pocket she carried her inheritance: the steel point from the Architect's blueprint drafting compass, worn bright by five generations of thumbs. [230] Family legend held that the point which had drawn the labyrinth could also pin it. [245] Press it into mutinous stone, and that one corridor would hold still — briefly, grudgingly, like a dog held by the collar. [266] It was the only reason a claustrophobic explorer survived down there at all. [279]

On the longest night of the fallow period, Mara descended through the church crypt. [293] The walls breathed around her, narrowing and widening like a throat, and she pressed the compass point into the stone whenever her chest began to clench. [319] Pinned passages sulked but stayed. [324] She moved from anchor to anchor, sketching her path, whispering apologies to the walls she fixed in place. [342] She understood them better than she liked to admit. [351] They had been built to serve, drafted into stillness without consent, and once a year they remembered they had never agreed to any of it. [376]

Near midnight, a corridor she had not pinned slid open like a curtain, and she stepped into the shadowed nexus. [396] Six passages converged in a round vault whose dome had collapsed generations ago, leaving an oculus open to the winter sky. [417] Starlight fell in a pale column onto a drafting table of stone, and the dark pooled deep around its edges. [437] Carved into the table was the original blueprint of the labyrinth, and at its center, a small socket exactly the size of a compass point. [462] Beside it, in her ancestor's careful hand, ran a single line: the key is D, held soft and long — ask, never command. [484]

Mara laughed, and the sound came back to her doubled, as if the vault laughed too. [500] The vital key had never been lost to malice or fire; it had simply been hidden where only someone willing to walk the mutiny could find it. [527] She set the compass point into the socket. [535] The blueprint glowed faintly, every corridor of it, and the six passages straightened like soldiers caught slouching. [552] Through the open passages, clear and whole, she could see all the way back to the crypt stairs. [570]

She climbed out before dawn and woke the sexton, and together they coaxed the lonely church organ awake for the first time in eighty years. [595] Mara held down a single soft D and let the reverberation pour through the floor, through the crypt, through every mutinous seam of stone. [619] The note did not bind the labyrinth; it transfixed it. [629] She felt the passages below pause mid-wander, caught and held, listening the way one listens to a voice not heard since childhood. [651] Doorways drifted home. [654] Cellars returned, sheepish, full of someone else's apples. [662]

But Mara did not hold the note as long as the elders wanted. [675] Ask them, the Architect had written; do not command. [684] So each fallow season afterward, she played the key gently, and only until the stores were found and the stairways stood where children expected them. [709] Then she lifted her hands from the keys and let the labyrinth wander. [722] It seemed only fair. [726] She knew what it was to be shaped by walls and to ache against them, and she could not bring herself to take from the stones the one rebellion they had left. [758] Some winters, standing in the empty church, she almost envied them. [769] The compass point stayed in its socket, glinting under starlight, a promise rather than a chain. [785]
