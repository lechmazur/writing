Maren Holt had spent eleven years correcting other people's geography, which made her excellent at spotting lies drawn in blue ink. [21] The lie that finally got her out of the office was a quiet one: seven rivers, on seven separate survey sheets, all bending toward the same blank spot above the sixty-eighth parallel. [53] Rivers diverge from sources; they do not converge toward one. [63] Either the surveyors were drunk, or the continent kept a singular convergence it had never confessed to any atlas, and Maren intended to find where the rivers begin. [91]

The outpost she reached in late October was mostly rust held together by habit, crouched on the shore of a lake frozen so flat it looked argued into stillness. [120] Its last occupant had been Ilya Voss, a polyglot hydrologist who had walked north in spring and never walked back. [140] His falcon had, though. [144] The bird returned to the outpost every autumn, the caretaker in the last village had told her, as if still running messages for a man who no longer existed. [173] "Wait until after the falcon returns," the caretaker had said. [183] "If you go before, the lake will not let you read it." [195] Maren, who edited superstition out of maps for a living, wrote the phrase down anyway. [210]

She found Voss's phrasebook on the second day, swollen with damp, its margins crowded with six languages arguing among themselves. [230] Only one page was dog-eared. [235] The fold pointed, with the precision of a man who trusted no one's memory including his own, at a single entry: the word for source, rendered in every northern dialect, and beneath it, in pencil, 'They all mean mouth.' [274] Maren read that three times and decided the cold was getting to her sense of humor. [290] A source pours out; a mouth swallows. [297] Seven rivers bending toward one point was beginning to sound less like bad surveying and more like appetite. [315]

The falcon arrived on the fourth morning, dropping out of a sky the color of unwashed tin to land on the weather vane as if it owned the deed. [344] It carried nothing. [347] It simply watched her through the window with the patience of a herald who has already delivered the message and is waiting for the slow recipient to notice. [375]

That night, the temperature inside and outside the outpost found their difference, and the glass began to sweat. [393] Maren noticed the condensation because it was wrong. [401] Moisture should bead randomly, obeying nothing grander than physics. [410] Instead it gathered in thin branching lines, fine as engraving, fanning across the pane from a single clear point near the sill. [432] She had drawn that pattern a hundred times at her desk. [443] It was a drainage map. [448] Seven tributaries, rendered in breath and cold, all running toward the dry point as if the glass itself were draining into it. [470] She laid her survey sheet against the window. [478] Line for line, the condensation matched her seven impossible rivers, and the dry point sat exactly over the blank spot above the sixty-eighth parallel. [502]

"Well," she said to the falcon, "Voss certainly knew how to file a report." [516] Her voice came out steadier than she expected, subtly confident, the tone of an editor who has found the error and now only needs to decide whether to correct it. [546]

Outside, the tranquility deepened into something that worried her more than any storm could have. [561] No wind moved over the lake. [567] No ice cracked. [570] The frozen surface lay perfectly still, the way water lies still when it is being held by something, not when it is merely cold. [594]

In the morning she packed Voss's phrasebook, her sheets, and a copy of the window's pattern traced onto vellum before the glass dried. [617] The dog-ear she left folded. [622] Some corrections deserve to stay marked. [628] The falcon lifted from the weather vane as she stepped onto the ice, gliding ahead of her in a straight, unhurried line north, herald to the last. [655] Maren followed, because that was the whole trouble with being an atlas editor: you could not leave a blank spot alone once you knew it was hungry. [682] She told herself she was going to find where the rivers begin. [694] She suspected, with the dry clarity Voss's pencil note had given her, that she was going to find where they end instead, and that the distinction would turn out to be the most important erratum of her career. [732] Behind her, the outpost's window held its map a little longer, then let it run. [747]
