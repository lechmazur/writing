After the severance, when the dead stopped crossing back and the living stopped pretending they would, Oren took his bonsai shears to the islet's last juniper and waited for the tide. [31] He was a slow-spoken man, a tutor of patient trees, and his students had always teased that his sentences grew the way his junipers did, one careful inch a year. [61] The rain had passed an hour before, and the breeze combed salt and wet cedar across the islet, lifting the smell of things half remembered. [86] That was the danger of it. [92] Since the severance, the tides of remembrance came strongest after rain, hauling memory ashore like wreckage, and whatever a person had failed to say in life returned as weather, as color, as flood. [125] Oren's failure had a name: Mira, his student, drowned three winters ago with his last words to her still being criticism of a crooked graft. [150] He had never apologized. [154] Now his unspoken apology was gathering offshore as a stain in the water, a blue he could not name, and the islanders feared that when it reached shore it would drown them all in a grief that belonged to him alone. [195] He wanted, before that happened, to see it clearly just once: to find the color of unspoken apologies and learn whether it could be released instead of feared. [223] Two tools lay beside him on the wet stone. [232] The first was a compass salvaged from the ghost ship that had drifted past the islet on the night of the severance, its crew transparent at the rails, none of them waving. [264] The compass did not point north; it pointed toward whatever the holder most regretted, and in Oren's hands its needle had aimed at the sea for three years without trembling. [294] The second was a square of antique lace, Mira's last gift, which he had been too proud to examine until last night. [316] Held to lamplight, the pattern had revealed itself as instruction: knots arranged like pruning diagrams, openwork shaped like tide charts, a sequence woven by some grandmother of the severed coast who had known that grief, like a root-bound tree, dies if you cut it and dies if you leave it, and survives only if you diffuse it slowly through new soil. [377] Mira had been trying to teach him something all along. [387] He read the lace the way he taught: slowly, aloud, one knot at a time. [402] "First," he said to the breeze, "you must walk to where the needle points, and not one step less." [421] He waded in to his knees, the compass steady in his palm, the sea rhythmically placid, each small wave arriving like a held breath let out at last. [449] The stain came to meet him. [455] It was cerulean, he saw now, but a cerulean that had been crying: the color of sky reflected in water that no one had looked at in years. [483] So that was it. [487] The color of unspoken apologies was not black, not red; it was the exact blue of a horizon you turned away from too soon. [511] The second row of knots instructed: speak the apology into the lace, one word per opening, no faster than the tide. [532] For once his slowness was not a flaw but a fitting. [543] "Mira," he said, and waited for a wave. [551] "I was wrong," and waited. [556] "Your graft was not crooked," and waited. [563] "It was reaching for light I could not see." [572] With each phrase, the lace darkened, drinking blue out of the water the way roots drink rain, and the stain thinned, not vanishing but spreading wide and pale until it belonged to the whole sea instead of to him. [611] Diffused, the grief did not disappear; it became weather any heart could survive. [624] The compass needle shivered, swung, and settled on the juniper behind him on the shore. [639] His remaining regret, it seemed, was only that he had a tree still to tend and fewer years to tend it. [660] He could live with that. [665] He climbed back onto the wet rock, wrung the sea from his cuffs, and folded the blue-stained lace into his breast pocket, where it lay against his heart like a map of everything he had finally said. [702] The breeze moved through the juniper, and the islet smelled of rain already becoming memory. [717] That evening, islanders found him at his bench, shaping the tree with Mira's old shears, speaking to it slowly, the way you speak to someone who has all the time in the world to forgive you. [753] When they asked what color the apology had been, he took a long while answering, as always. [770] "Cerulean," he said at last, "but lighter now." [778]
