Maren had spent sixty years learning the sky from the ground, an earthbound star watcher who knew Vega's rising the way other people knew their own doorsteps. [27] She had been the lighthouse keeper once, until the night the lamp room caught and the whole tower burned with a sailor's son inside it. [52] After that she could not strike a match, not even for tea. [64] But boats still came past the headland, and the dark still swallowed them, and Maren still believed that light was owed to anyone moving through it. [90] So she made a vow that sounded impossible when she said it aloud: she would share light without using fire. [110] The answer came from the tideline, where the sea had been quietly making jewels of the town's old bottles for a hundred years. [133] Sea glass, frosted and humble, carried light inside it the way a sleeper carries a dream. [149] Frosted, it only hinted; polished, she suspected, it might truly shine. [160] She taught herself gem polishing from a lapidary's manual left behind by her late husband, working through the grits in patient order, coarse to fine to a leather wheel dressed with cerium. [192] Her workshop was the serpentine grotto below the ruined tower, a winding green-stoned throat of rock where mist came in off the water and hung like breath. [219] The grotto suited her because no flame could live in that dampness, and because the curving walls carried even starlight deep inside, bending it corner by corner like a secret being passed along. [252] Each evening she would weigh the day's gathered glass on a small brass scale, because weight told her about density, and density told her how a piece would hold and return the light. [285] The heavy pieces became anchors for her design; the light ones became its laughter. [299] Her plan was a mosaic set into the grotto's mouth, hundreds of polished lenses angled to gather star and moon and hurl them seaward, a cold beacon that no wind could snuff and no spark could betray. [336] The work was slow, and it was not quiet inside her. [347] As memories resurfaced with each piece she ground smooth, she found the past rising the way wrecks rise after storms. [367] A green shard brought back the boy's green coat. [376] A brown one brought back the smell of the lamp oil. [387] A rare blue piece brought back the sailor's face at the funeral, and her hands shook so badly she nearly ruined the wheel. [410] But polishing, she discovered, demanded a pearl of stillness at its center; the wheel punished hurry and grief alike, and the only way through was a calm she had to grow grain by grain, exactly as the sea had grown the glass. [452] She learned to hold the memory and the stone at once, letting both become smoother. [467] By midsummer the mosaic was nearly done, and on clear evenings the grotto's mouth looked reflectively vibrant, alive with borrowed sky, every facet trembling with stars it did not own but gladly returned. [500] Then came the night of fog and a fishing boat overdue. [511] The town gathered on the headland with lanterns, but lantern light died within arm's reach in that mist. [529] Maren went down the winding path alone. [536] Above the fog bank the sky was clean, and she knew without looking where Vega stood. [552] She tilted the final lens, the blue piece she had nearly ruined, until it caught the star and passed it along the serpentine curve, facet to facet, out through the grotto's mouth. [584] The mist did a strange and merciful thing: instead of swallowing the light, it bloomed with it, a soft silver flowering that spread across the water like a breeze made visible. [615] There was no glare, no flame, only a wide gentle glow with the grotto at its heart. [632] The fishing boat saw it and turned. [639] When the crew came ashore, the youngest among them was the lost boy's nephew, born the spring after the fire. [659] He asked Maren what burned inside the rock. [667] Nothing burns, she told him, and the words came out easier than she expected. [681] It only remembers light, and gives it back. [689] Afterward she sat at the grotto's mouth while the fog thinned, weighing one last unpolished shard in her palm, not on the brass scale this time but against everything it had cost and everything it had finally bought. [727] The shard was dull now, but she knew what waited inside it. [739] Sixty years of watching had taught her that stars do not insist; they simply offer, night after night, to anyone patient enough to look up. [764] She had finally learned to do the same. [772]
