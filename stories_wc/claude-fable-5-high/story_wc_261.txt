The anesthesia wore off at dawn, and the first voice Mara heard belonged to the ivy. [16] It came through the tower's cold stones like a rumor: hungry, hungry, the light is wrong. [32] She lay on the surgeon's cot in the quiet tower, her side stitched, her head full of green murmuring. [51] Mara was a closure seeker by trade, the kind who opened wounds to let grief finish its sentence. [69] Mourners climbed her stairs with sealed-over sorrows, and she cut them open gently, and they left lighter. [86] But she had never learned to read her own scars, which was why she had come under the knife at all. [107] The surgeon had removed a knot of scar tissue older than her memory, and now plants spoke to her. [126] On the windowsill, a fern explained, patiently, that a miracle had gone astray three nights ago. [142] Miracles, the fern said, travel like seeds, and this one had been meant for the dying orchard below. [160] Instead it had fallen into Mara on the operating table, drawn into the open wound like rain into a furrow. [180] That was why she could hear them: the orchard's blessing, displaced, was humming in her stitches. [196] The trees were tunneling, the fern added, almost apologetic. [205] Their roots had been boring through the tower's foundation for two nights, climbing toward what was theirs. [222] Mara could feel it when she pressed her palm to the floor, a slow insistence under the stone. [240] Her mentor Iren had died in this tower, leaving a desk littered with cups and a frosted hourglass. [258] The hourglass had been frosted since the night Iren died, its sand suspended mid-fall, refusing to land. [275] Mara had always believed it was simply broken. [283] Now she understood it as a held breath: time waiting for the miracle to arrive where it was sent. [302] Iren had ordered the miracle herself, the fern confirmed, a last working to save the orchard she loved. [320] Mara rose carefully, one hand on her bandaged side, and limped to the old desk. [335] Iren had never written things down; she had thought in coffee, leaving rings on every paper and map. [353] Mara had spent a year unable to grieve her, unable to open that particular wound. [368] Now she examined the coffee rings the way she examined scars, looking for the story under the stain. [386] The rings overlapped on a survey of the orchard, circling one tree again and again: the grafted apple by the well. [407] A pale ring crossed Iren's own name on a letter, half a halo, half a target. [423] The story assembled itself in Mara's hands: Iren had meant the miracle for the tree she was buried beneath. [442] The tunneling roots were not thieves; they were carrying Iren's last intention up through the dark to collect it. [461] Outside, the dawn held the particular hush of a meteor passing, the sky bright and soundless and waiting. [479] Mara understood, then, what closure would cost her, as it always cost her clients. [493] The miracle had sealed itself into her wound; to release it, the wound had to open. [509] She had spent her life opening other people's scars and calling it mercy. [522] She unwound the bandage and, with the surgeon's small knife, parted two of the new stitches. [538] It hurt less than grieving had. [544] Something left her, not blood, or not only blood, a warmth like a word finally said aloud. [561] It sank through the floor, into the stone, into the roots that had tunneled so far to meet it. [580] On the desk, the frost on the hourglass broke into water, and the sand began to fall. [597] The green voices dimmed as the miracle traveled home, but they did not vanish entirely. [612] The grafted apple spoke last, in a voice that was mostly tree and slightly Iren. [627] Read it, the voice said, and Mara looked down at her oldest scar, the one below the new cut. [646] For the first time it was legible: a winter, a fall from a tree, Iren's careful hands, a graft that took. [667] She had fallen from Iren's apple tree as a girl; Iren had stitched her shut and never said so. [686] Every scar, Mara realized, was a story someone had been too kind or too afraid to tell. [703] Down in the orchard, blossom broke from the grave-tree out of season, soundless as the dawn. [719] Mara rebandaged her side and sat at the desk while the hourglass spent its sand.
