The university radio station hummed at three in the morning, its tower blinking red against a sky that Mara Voss no longer trusted. [23] She was the only celestial signal interpreter the astronomy department had ever hired, and most of the faculty believed her job was a joke. [47] They had stopped laughing three weeks ago, when the town began tearing itself apart. [61] It started with the school board meeting that ended in fistfights. [72] Then the churches split, then the unions, then families who had shared Sunday dinners for decades. [88] As the community divided, everyone blamed politics; Mara blamed the sky. [99] Spread across the broadcast desk lay her grandmother's astronomical charts, hand-inked maps annotated in a cipher Mara had spent ten years learning to read. [123] Her grandmother had called them the Meddlers: old, bored intelligences threaded through the dark between stars, who fed on discord the way moths fed on light. [149] Cosmic tricksters, playing power plays with whole towns as their game boards. [161] Their weapon was almost insultingly simple. [167] They rode the station's signal, layering subsonic frequencies beneath the late-night jazz program, vibrations too low to hear but deep enough to set human nerves grinding against each other. [196] Mara had found the pattern by accident, watching the spectrum analyzer shiver at 17 hertz every night the moon crossed Saturn's line on her grandmother's charts. [222] Tonight the charts predicted the largest convergence in sixty years. [232] Tomorrow the town would vote on dissolving itself, splitting into two bitter municipalities, and the Meddlers wanted that wound made permanent. [253] She could not block their signal; she had tried, and they slid around every filter like water around stones. [272] But her grandmother's marginalia held one strange instruction: do not silence a trickster; out-trick it. [287] So Mara would interfere. [291] She had spent six sleepless nights building a counter-tone, a second subsonic frequency tuned to collide with theirs and fold their dissonance into something else entirely. [317] Not silence, but resonance. [321] The mathematics said the two waves would interlock and produce a harmonic the human body read as calm, as kinship, as the bone-deep memory of being held. [348] The mathematics also said she might be wrong, and that wrongness at these amplitudes could give half the county migraines or worse. [370] At 3:47 the analyzer shivered. [375] The Meddlers' tone slid in beneath a saxophone solo, patient and cold, and Mara felt her own jaw tighten with borrowed anger. [397] That was how they worked: they made their malice feel like yours. [409] She breathed, checked the charts one last time, and keyed her counter-tone into the transmitter. [424] For a moment nothing happened, and despair rose in her like floodwater. [436] Then the spectrum display bloomed. [441] The two frequencies wrestled, beat against each other, and locked. [451] The studio filled with something she could not hear but could absolutely feel: a pressure like standing inside a struck bell, ethereal and gritty at once, starlight filtered through gravel. [481] Her shoulders dropped. [484] Her jaw unclenched. [487] Somewhere out in the dark, the Meddlers pushed harder, and she matched them, riding the gain like a sailor leaning into weather. [509] They could not withdraw their signal without forfeiting the game; her grandmother's notes were clear that tricksters never fold, they only escalate. [531] And every escalation fed her harmonic more power. [539] She laughed aloud in the empty studio, a raw, exhausted sound. [550] She was using their own strength against them, judo at the scale of constellations. [564] At dawn the Meddlers' frequency simply stopped, the way a gambler leaves a table that has turned. [581] Mara walked out into gray morning light and found the streets strangely quiet. [594] Two neighbors who had screamed at each other across a fence line last week stood at the bus stop, talking about rain. [616] The vote that afternoon failed by nine hundred ballots; the town stayed whole. [629] Mara knew better than to call it victory. [637] Tricksters lose interest, but they do not forgive, and her grandmother's charts mapped convergences stretching decades ahead. [654] That night she returned to the station, re-inked a fading line on the oldest chart, and set the analyzer to record. [675] She felt cautiously hopeful, the way you feel after winning one hand against a player with infinite chips. [693] The sky above the tower was full of cold, patient watchers. [704] But the tower blinked back at them, steady and red, and beneath the jazz, too low for any ear, her harmonic kept humming its small, stubborn song of holding together. [734]
