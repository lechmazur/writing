After tomorrow disappeared, the tide still came in. [8] It rose through the floor of the tidal atrium twice a day, sliding between the shelves like a patient reader, and went out again carrying whatever the city no longer remembered how to keep. [42] Oale, the titan-gear librarian, kept the hours anyway. [50] Her chest was a cage of enormous bronze gears, salvaged from a fallen titan, and they turned faster or slower with the phases of the moon. [76] At full moon she moved like a festival; at new moon she was nearly still, a statue that breathed. [95] Without tomorrow, the moon was the only calendar left, and her own body its only clock. [111]

The city above had settled into a single repeating day, and the people in it had begun to migrate. [130] Not their bodies -- their identities. [135] A baker woke believing she was a soldier; a soldier woke with a midwife's hands. [150] With no future to anchor them, selves drifted on the tide like everything else, and the tide brought them to Oale. [171] They arrived in vessels. [175] Identities, she had learned, cannot travel naked; they need a container shaped by use. [189] This morning the water left a fisherman's bamboo creel on the atrium steps, its lid woven shut, its slats silvered by decades of salt. [213] She lifted it and felt the weight of a whole life: knots, patience, cold dawns, the silver shudder of a catch. [234] Inside, instead of fish, lay a man's name, coiled like line. [245]

The fisherman himself came at low tide, wearing a tax collector's certainty and hating it. [260] "I counted ledgers all day," he said, "and my hands kept reaching for water." [274] "Give me back to myself." [279]

Oale could have simply handed him the creel. [287] She had done that in the first weeks, returning selves like overdue books, and watched them drift loose again by the next identical morning. [311] Routine without a future is a solvent; it dissolves whoever you decide to be. [325] A returned identity had to be fixed, and fixing required fire. [336] Behind the reference stacks she kept a small forge, fed by the heat of her own titan gears. [354] There she would smelt a self back into its bearer: not metal exactly, but the ore of memory, melted until the impurities of borrowed lives rose to the surface. [383] But the smelting only held if the bearer accepted the contradictions in the melt. [397]

"You will keep some of the tax collector," she warned, opening the creel above the crucible. [413] "You cannot pour out only the parts you like." [422] The fisherman's jaw worked. [426] "I hated counting." [429] "And yet you counted fish your whole life," Oale said. [439] "Counted hooks, counted waves between casts." [445] "A man can be a thing he hates and a thing he loves at once." [460] "Say yes to both, and the melt will hold." [469]

He stood in the rising water of the atrium, winter sun falling through the glass roof in long pale bars, and at last he nodded. [494] The name in the creel softened, brightened, ran like gold. [504] Oale poured it slowly back into him, and his shoulders settled the way a boat settles when its ballast is right. [525] He wept a little, calmly, the way people weep at sunrise rather than at funerals. [540]

"What do I owe you?" he asked. [547] She gave him the empty creel. [553] "Fill it tomorrow," she said, and they both smiled at the old joke, because there was no tomorrow, only this day again, the tide and the moon and the same gray light. [585] "Then fill it today," she said, "as if you had never done it before." [599]

That was her real work, she had decided, more than cataloguing or smelting. [612] A day that repeats forever will kill its people not with hunger but with familiarity, and the only antidote is wonder, deliberately kept, like a fire banked overnight. [640] So she shelved each returned self with ceremony. [648] She announced the tide aloud as if it had never come in before. [661] She let her gear-heart race at full moon and forgave it for crawling at the dark of the new, accepting her own contradiction: a machine built for constancy that fluctuates like water. [693] Perhaps that was why the migrating selves trusted her crucible. [703] She was a contradiction that held. [709]

At dusk the fisherman returned with the creel full of smelt, small fish bright as filings, and laughed at his own accidental pun. [732] Oale laughed too, gears chiming, the sound moving over the water of the atrium like light. [748] Tomorrow would not come. [752] But the tide would, and the moon, and someone newly strange to themselves, and she would be here, patient as winter sun, making the same day astonishing again. [780]
