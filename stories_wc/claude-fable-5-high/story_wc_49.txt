Pell the tinkerer had spent forty years mending the village's kettles, clocks, and weathervanes, but she had never once mended a silence. [22] The silence had settled over Brackenhollow the autumn the last song-keeper died, taking with her every verse of the harvest rites. [43] Since then, the great arbor at the village's heart had refused to fruit. [56] Its vines stayed green but barren, curling in on themselves like fists. [68] Pell was a whimsical soul, the sort who fitted bells to her bootlaces and taught her teakettle to whistle in harmony, and the village had long ago stopped expecting sense from her. [100] But she remembered the rites, even if she could not remember the songs. [113] She remembered how the whole village once gathered beneath the arbor when the harvest moon hung low, singing the vines awake until grapes swelled gold by morning. [140] She wanted that back, not for the fruit, but because a village without its sacred practices was just houses standing near each other. [163]

So she built a machine to listen. [170] The song-keeper's cottage still held her spinning wheel, and Pell knew the old woman had hummed at her work for sixty years, the melodies sinking into the wood the way smoke sinks into curtains. [204] Pell rigged the wheel with a horn of hammered copper, a needle of whisker-fine silver, and a crank that turned slower than honey pours. [228] The children came to watch, as children always did, and while she worked she spun them golden stories: of moons that leaned down to taste the grapes, of vines that danced when nobody watched. [262] The stories were her way of keeping the rites breathing until she could give them back their voice. [280]

For three weeks the machine yielded only creaks and the dry whisper of old wool. [295] Then, on a night when frost first silvered the fences, the needle caught something: three notes, rising, like a question asked kindly. [317] Pell wept, then worked. [321] Night after night she coaxed fragments from the wheel, stitching them together by ear, testing each phrase against a vine tendril she had potted on her windowsill. [348] When she sang a phrase wrong, the tendril hung limp. [358] When she sang it true, the tendril lifted and turned toward her like a small green ear. [375] It was the only judge she trusted. [382]

By the season's turning she had the song whole, or nearly, but a song alone was not the remedy. [401] The old rite demanded more: the harvest moon setting in the west as the sun rose in the east, the village singing in the narrow hour when both lights touched the vines at once. [435] Only when everything aligned, moon and sun and voices, would the arbor remember itself. [449] Such a dawn came once a year, and it came in nine days. [462]

Pell went door to door with her potted tendril and her patched-together melody. [475] Some neighbors laughed; more of them listened, because the children had carried her golden stories home, and the stories had made the old rite feel less like a funeral and more like a door left ajar. [511] She taught the song in kitchens and barns, one forgotten verse at a time, and watched the tendril rise for farmers and bakers as it had risen for her. [540]

On the ninth morning, Brackenhollow walked out in the dark. [550] The arbor stood verdant and waiting, its leaves black-green in the last of the moonlight, the harvest moon huge and amber above the western hills. [575] A hush gathered as the villagers took their places beneath the canopy, the kind of hush that is not absence but attention, every breath held like a tuned string. [604] Pell set her potted tendril at the arbor's root and watched the east. [617] When the first thread of sun touched the highest leaf while the moon still rested on the hills, she raised her hand, and the village sang. [643]

The forgotten song moved through the dawn air like warmth through cold water. [656] The clenched vines loosened. [660] Tendrils uncurled by the hundreds, reaching toward the singers, and where the doubled light of moonset and sunrise fell across them, small green grapes budded and blushed toward gold. [689] The villagers sang until the moon slipped under the hills, and when the last note faded, nobody spoke for a long while. [711] The hush remained, but it had changed; it was the hush of a held thing finally set down. [729]

That winter, the village chose three children to be song-keepers, and Pell built them a music box apiece, so that no melody would ever again depend on a single memory. [759] On her windowsill, the little tendril kept growing, still leaning toward any room where someone sang. [775]
