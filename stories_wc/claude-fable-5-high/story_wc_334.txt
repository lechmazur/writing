Maren Voss had spent forty years collecting other people's stories because she could not find her own. [17] She was a hands-on folklorist, the kind who climbed into barrows and tasted well water, and her colleagues called her chronically curious as if curiosity were a disease. [45] Perhaps it was. [48] A disease needs a wound to enter by, and Maren's wound was a missing year. [63] When she was nine, the summer she lived at her grandmother's orchard greenhouse on the Skellan coast, something took her memories of that place clean out of her head. [92] Decades later, she came back to find them. [100]

The coast had changed; a storm surge in the seventies had drowned the valley, and the greenhouse now sat sunken under thirty feet of cold green water, its iron ribs furred with kelp. [133] Around it lay a ring of shipwrecks, nine hulls dragged there over the years by currents nobody could chart. [152] The locals avoided the cove and would not say why. [162] Maren, naturally, rented a rowboat. [167]

Her field notebooks held one consistent rumor, gathered from coastlines on three continents: memory-thieves leave a mark on the sky. [187] Where a memory has been stolen, the stars above the theft go subtly wrong, a constellation broken like a necklace with one bead pocketed. [211] Most people never notice. [215] Maren had trained herself to detect the breaks, lying on her back night after night with star charts and a measuring cord. [237] Over the drowned cove, the Plough was missing its second star. [248] She wept a little when she saw it, not from grief but from the crisp relief of a hypothesis confirmed. [268]

Her grandmother's stories, half-remembered from before the missing year, said the orchard folk paid their rent in recollection. [286] They took what you would not miss, which is to say, what you loved most and therefore assumed was safe. [306] And they could only be approached when habits break, in the seam between one routine and the next, because habit is the armor of the ordinary world. [333]

So Maren broke hers, deliberately, one by one. [341] She who always woke at six slept until noon. [350] She who took coffee black drank it sweet and white. [360] She wrote with her left hand, walked her evening route backward, and stopped, for the first time in forty years, asking questions. [382]

On the ninth night, the water in the cove went flat as window glass. [396] She rowed out between the wrecks, which stood from the shallows like the standing stones of some patient congregation. [415] Below, the greenhouse glowed faintly, its drowned apple trees still leafed, impossibly, in silver. [429] And on the surface of the water, directly above the broken place in the Plough's reflection, floated a perfect circle of fairy ring mushrooms, pale caps riding the swell as if the sea were a lawn. [465]

Every folklorist knows the rule: never step inside a fairy ring. [476] Maren had repeated that rule in lecture halls for decades; it was her oldest habit of all. [493] She broke it. [496]

She slipped over the gunwale into the ring, and the water did not take her under so much as open, the way a door opens. [521] She stood dry on the greenhouse floor among the silver apples, and a small figure made of root and lichen looked up from its ledger. [546] You came back, it said, with neither surprise nor welcome. [556] The summer is still here, paid in full by your grandmother, kept in trust against your return. [573] It held out an apple. [578]

Maren, who had handled cursed bells and bargained with bog-wives, understood the price before it was named: a memory returned must be traded for a habit, permanently, and curiosity was the only habit she had left. [614] She thought of forty years spent asking questions because she could not remember the answer that mattered. [631] She bit the apple. [635]

It tasted of August, of her grandmother's hands sticky with windfall fruit, of a girl lying in greenhouse warmth counting whole, unbroken stars through the glass. [661] The missing year poured back in, bright and ordinary and hers. [672]

When she surfaced, the boat was waiting and the Plough was whole. [684] She rowed ashore past the patient wrecks and felt the change settle: the itch of wondering was gone, replaced by the strange weight of knowing. [709] She never asked another question in her life, and never needed to. [721] Instead she wrote it all down, the orchard and the rent and the ring of riding mushrooms, so that some other thief-struck wanderer, decades on, might look up at a broken constellation and know exactly where to begin. [759]
