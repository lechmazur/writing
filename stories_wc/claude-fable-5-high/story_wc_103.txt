Mara Voss had spent thirty years coaxing hedges into puzzles, but the living bridge garden of Cael's Hollow was the first commission that frightened her. [25] The garden grew on roots trained across a river gorge, generations of fig trees braided into walkways that breathed and shifted underfoot. [47] Her patron, a dying archivist named Ilsa, wanted a maze woven through the bridges, and she wanted it built through hyperbolic geometry. [69] "Euclid's space holds too little," Ilsa said, spreading fading photographs across the table like a losing hand of cards. [88] "In hyperbolic space, paths multiply faster than they can be walked. [99] I need somewhere with room enough to hold what is leaving these." [111] The photographs were of Ilsa's daughter, drowned in the gorge twenty years ago, and every print was bleaching toward white no matter how she stored them. [137] Mara took the job for the money, she told herself, but the truth trembled beneath that: she wanted to discover the texture of time. [161] Every maze she had ever built was a question asked of duration, how long can a person be lost, and she had never once felt the answer in her hands. [191] She planted hornbeam along the root-bridges in curves that bent away from each other, seven corridors meeting at every junction where flat ground would allow only four. [218] The garden accepted the geometry hungrily. [224] Branches arced into saddle-shapes; the bridges flexed to carry corridors that should not have fit above one river. [242] On the morning the maze closed its final loop, Mara found the halo fragment. [256] It surfaced from the riverbank silt at the maze's heart: a curved sliver of gold leaf and gesso, broken from some drowned chapel's saint, no wider than her thumb. [285] When she lifted it, the garden inhaled. [292] The light went the color of old paper, and Mara understood, with a quaking, dreamlike calm, that she was no longer standing in now. [316] She was retrocognitively trapped, her senses pulled backward along the maze's impossible corridors into days the garden remembered. [334] She saw the chapel before its flooding, candle-bright above the gorge. [345] She saw root-weavers, centuries dead, guiding saplings across the water with their bare hands. [359] She saw a girl in a yellow dress, Ilsa's daughter, unmistakable from the photographs, running the bridges on the last morning of her life. [383] Time, Mara discovered, had a texture after all. [391] It felt like bark: ridged, layered, every year still present beneath the surface, none of it gone, all of it sealed. [412] The fragment was the splinter that let her under the bark, and the hyperbolic maze gave the past room to unfold, so many paths that every vanished hour could keep a corridor of its own. [447] But the corridors did not lead back. [454] She wandered the remembered garden for what might have been days, watching the girl run, watching the chapel burn its candles, while somewhere ahead of her, in the present she could not reach, her own body stood at the maze's heart with gold cupped in its palm. [501] She understood why the photographs were fading. [508] The fragment had been drinking the gorge's past for decades, pulling every image of the dead girl out of the world and into its hoard of light. [535] Ilsa's prints were not decaying; they were being collected. [544] Mara found the girl at the center of the remembered maze, sitting where the silt would one day hide the gold. [565] "You can stay," the girl said, in a voice made of every photograph at once. [580] "Nothing fades here." [583] And Mara wanted to; that was the trembling truth of it. [594] She had spent her life building places to be lost in, and here was lostness perfected, time with its grain laid bare. [616] But she thought of Ilsa at her table, dealt a hand of whitening cards, dying without even a face to grieve. [637] A maze is honest only if it can be left. [647] So she did the one thing a designer can always do: she closed her remembered hand around her present one and willed the fragment to disintegrate. [673] It came apart like frost, gold dissolving into the river air, and every image it had hoarded poured back along the seven-way corridors toward the world. [699] Mara woke on her knees at the maze's heart, palm empty, the bridges trembling beneath her like a held breath released. [720] That evening, Ilsa came down the gorge path weeping, holding a photograph grown suddenly vivid: a girl in a yellow dress, laughing on a living bridge. [746] Mara never told her where she had been. [754] But sometimes, walking her maze at dusk, she ran her fingers along the hornbeam bark and felt the years ridged under her hand, and she shivered with something between grief and gratitude. [786] Time had a texture, and it held. [793]
