The waterfall froze the week after the beekeeper's last visit, and the grotto city went quiet behind its new wall of green ice. [23] I had been a museum guide once, in the capital, before the capital stopped existing for people like us. [42] Now I led tours through caves for the displaced, because even refugees deserve a curator. [57] We had carried almost nothing through the mountains. [65] What I carried was an emerald-tinted opera glass lens, popped from its brass housing the night the museum burned. [84] It had belonged to a soprano whose name the new government had ordered unsung. [98] Through it, the frozen falls looked like a curtain about to rise. [110]

The grotto city had been carved generations ago by smugglers, then forgotten, then remembered by the desperate. [127] Four hundred of us lived in its chambers, lit by oil lamps, fed by what the beekeeper used to bring. [147] He had been our only link to the valley: honey, flour, news, and the unspoken promise that someone outside still knew we existed. [170] His last visit, he left double his usual load and would not meet our eyes. [185] Then the cold came, and the falls sealed us in. [195]

There is a particular dread that smells sweet, like rosewater on a coffin. [208] It filled the grotto that winter: politeness over panic, lullabies over the sound of the flour sacks emptying. [226] I responded the only way I knew, which was historically. [236] If we were going to vanish, we would at least be documented. [248] I began an inventory of what the displaced had carried in their heads: a lacemaker's knots, a cantor's modes, a potter's glaze recipe that existed nowhere on paper. [276] Every evening I gathered people in the great chamber and made them teach. [289] They thought I was distracting them from hunger. [297] I was rescuing arts that would die in the spring if we did. [310]

The bread was Mira's doing. [315] Her family, bakers for five generations, had a custom: every loaf shaped into the initials of the person it would feed. [336] A loaf with your letters could not be hoarded, traded, or stolen without everyone knowing. [351] When rationing began, I asked her to bake that way for the whole grotto. [365] Four hundred small loaves, four hundred names in dough. [374] It used more flour than plain rounds would have, and the council argued against it. [389] I argued back that a person handed their own initials remembers they are a person. [404] Displacement strips names first; the bread gave them back daily, warm. [415] The council relented. [418]

Something in the city loosened after that. [425] People came to the evening lessons holding their letters, eating slowly, listening to the cantor explain how a mode could carry grief without breaking. [449] The dread stayed, sweet and floral as ever, but it had company now. [462]

Late in the third month, the children noticed it first: a sound under the silence, water moving inside the ice. [482] The thaw. [484] I climbed to the viewing ledge and raised the soprano's lens to my eye. [498] Through the emerald glass, the cracks spreading down the frozen curtain looked deliberate, like a score being written. [516] We did not know what waited in the valley. [525] Perhaps the beekeeper's silence meant soldiers; perhaps it meant only that he, too, had been made to move on. [544] The council met under the dripping stone and asked me, of all people, what history advised. [560] I told them the truth: history advises nothing, but it remembers everything, and it remembers best the people who wrote themselves down. [582]

So before the falls fully opened, we made our record. [592] The lacemaker knotted her patterns into cord; the potter scratched his glaze into clay tablets; the cantor sang into the ears of six children until each could carry a mode alone. [623] Mira baked one final batch, every set of initials in the grotto, and we ate our names together in the spray-light. [644]

When the curtain of water finally fell open with a roar, it sounded less like doom than like applause. [663] We walked out in small groups, carrying cord and clay and song, scattering the way seeds do. [680] I went last, as a guide should. [687] At the threshold I lifted the emerald lens one more time and looked back at the empty chambers, green and gleaming, already becoming an exhibit. [712] Someday someone will find the tablets and the knotted cords and wonder who we were. [727] The dread never quite left me; it travels with the displaced like perfume in a packed suitcase. [744] But so does the other thing we baked into ourselves that winter. [756] Wherever I land, I shape the dough into letters, and I teach whoever is hungry that art is only a name refusing to dissolve. [780]
