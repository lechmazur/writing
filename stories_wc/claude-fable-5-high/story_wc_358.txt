The town had gone silent two days before Mara Voss arrived, evacuated after the cargo plane came down in the port. [21] Fuel had bled into the channel, and the authorities had cleared everyone out until the air tested clean. [39] Now she walked alone through a towering labyrinth of shipping containers, steel stacked five high, corridors of rust-orange and faded blue running toward a sea she could hear but not see. [70] Fog hung over everything, softening edges that her instruments insisted were exact. [82]

Mara was an accident investigator, and she had learned to distrust the obvious. [95] The preliminary report blamed engine failure, a clean and comfortable verdict. [106] But the flight recorder told a stranger story: the aircraft had been stable on approach until, in its final eleven seconds, it was shoved sideways as if by a hand. [136] Engines do not fail sideways. [141] So she had come to survey the ground the plane had crossed, the last half mile of its descent, which ran directly over the container yard. [167]

Her method was unglamorous: documenting wind patterns between buildings, or what passed for buildings here. [182] The stacks formed canyons, and canyons funnel air. [190] She planted anemometers at intersections, released streamers of ribbon, logged readings on a clipboard going damp in the fog. [209] The silence of the evacuated town made the work eerie and pure. [221] No trucks, no cranes, no voices, only wind threading the steel corridors with a low harmonic moan. [238]

By noon her map showed something troubling. [245] Each corridor accelerated the sea breeze a little, bending it a few degrees. [258] Individually the effects were trivial. [263] But three corridors converged near the yard's northern edge, beneath the flight path. [276] There the streams stacked, gust upon gust, into a single rising column of disturbed air. [291] A singular convergence, she wrote, then underlined it twice. [300] It would only occur when the wind came from the southwest at more than twenty knots, and only since last month, when a new tenant had restacked the eastern block five containers high. [333] The trap had existed for thirty-one days before it caught its first aircraft. [346]

She was kneeling to recover an anemometer when she saw the patch. [358] It lay against a container's base, a small embroidered oval, scorched at one edge: a flight engineer's patch, wings over a stylized turbine. [381] Debris from the breakup, carried here by the same winds she had spent the morning charting. [397] The crew had been three; the flight engineer, Davi Okonkwo, had not survived. [410] She held the patch in her gloved palm and felt the abstraction of her data collapse into a person. [429] Okonkwo's last logged words, per the recorder, had been a calm callout of fluctuating airspeed. [444] He had noticed the anomaly four seconds before anyone else. [454] Four seconds had not been enough. [460]

The fog thickened as the afternoon breeze swung southwest, and Mara felt the corridors come alive around her. [478] Her ribbons snapped taut; her instruments climbed past eighteen knots. [488] The convergence was assembling itself in real time, and somewhere above the fog, the evening freight run from the mainland was scheduled in forty minutes. [513] The airport had reopened that morning; her warning was still a draft on a clipboard. [528] Procedure said file the report, wait for review, recommend a study. [539] Procedure was foresight in slow motion, and slow foresight had already cost a life. [553] She had become an investigator to cultivate foresight in action, not in paperwork. [566]

So she adapted, rapidly, the way the wind did. [575] She climbed the stack at the mouth of the northern corridor until she had signal, called the tower directly, and identified herself by case number. [600] She gave them numbers, not feelings: wind direction, gust velocity, corridor bearings, the altitude band where the column would strike an aircraft on final. [624] The controller hesitated, then rerouted the freight run to the eastern approach. [636]

From her perch atop the container, Mara watched the fog churn through the convergence point, a visible braid of vapor twisting upward exactly where her map said it would. [665] Minutes later, engine noise crossed the harbor, distant and safe, on the far side of the sky. [682]

She climbed down and finished her survey in the failing light. [693] Her final report would recommend stacking limits, corridor baffles, a permanent wind sensor at the convergence point. [710] Foresight, she would write, is not prediction; it is the discipline of seeing how small things gather. [727] Before she left the silent yard, she pinned the flight engineer's patch to the inside cover of her clipboard, where she would see it at the start of every case. [757] Four seconds had not been enough for Davi Okonkwo. [766] Forty minutes had been enough today. [772] In the hazy corridors of steel, she had learned to measure the difference. [785]
