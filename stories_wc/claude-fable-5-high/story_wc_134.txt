Mirel had seen the deaths of kings before they were born, yet she did not see the pilgrim coming. [19] For thirty years she had kept the singing cave, where crystal columns hummed whenever water moved through the mountain's hidden veins. [40] Every visitor announced themselves in her visions weeks ahead: a face, a question, a grief. [55] So when boots scraped the threshold one evening, unannounced, the sound frightened her more than any prophecy ever had. [74] The pilgrim was small, road-worn, carrying nothing but a lantern with no flame in it. [89] "You're not in my sight," Mirel said, which was not a greeting but a kind of accusation. [106] "Good," the pilgrim said. [110] "Then maybe you can finally see what's actually here." [119]

After the pilgrim arrived, the cave changed its music. [128] The crystals had always sung the same six notes, steady as a heartbeat; now they wandered, finding sevenths and strange harmonics, steadily unpredictable, like a heart learning a new emotion. [158] Mirel lit her three lamps and watched the pilgrim watch the walls. [170] "People climb this mountain for visions of great things," she said. [181] "Thrones. [182] Storms. [183] Love that shakes nations." [187] "I climbed it for smaller things," the pilgrim said. [196] "The magic in bread rising. [201] In a child's first word. [206] The world is full of small enchantments nobody notices, and I want them magnified until no one can look away again." [227] Mirel laughed, harsh in the humming dark. [234] "Magic doesn't work that way." [239] "You don't know how it works," the pilgrim said gently. [249] "You only know how it has worked so far." [258]

That night Mirel dreamed nothing, which had never happened, and woke to find the pilgrim arranging her lamps in a triangle around the cave's deepest pool. [284] Three flames cast three shadows from every column, and where the shadows overlapped, the darkness was not darker but brighter, dense with something that moved. [309] "They've always been here," the pilgrim said. [316] "The unseen forces that turn seed to wheat and sorrow to song. [328] They cannot enter through light, because light defines things too sharply. [339] They come in through overlapping shadows, where edges blur and one thing is permitted to become another." [356]

In the pool's center, on a pedestal of dripstone, lay the cave pearl. [369] Mirel had guarded it for thirty years without understanding it: a smooth white sphere the mountain had polished, layer by patient layer, turning in its stone cup with every tremor of water. [401] "Everyday magic," the pilgrim said, nodding at it. [409] "Just water and time and grit. [415] And look what it made." [420]

The shadows pooled and rose. [425] The forces did not appear so much as inhabit: they slid into the columns, into the pool, into the pearl itself, and the cave's music swelled into something Mirel had no notes for. [458] She understood, suddenly, why she had not foreseen the pilgrim. [468] Her gift ran on patterns, on the world repeating itself, and the pilgrim had come to break the pattern open. [488] You cannot foresee what has never happened before. [496]

The pearl began to glow, not with light but with a bright darkness, a radiance that made the cave more visible the deeper its shadows became. [522] Mirel saw her own hands as if for the first time: the small miracle of fingers, of blood doing its quiet work. [544] She saw the pilgrim's empty lantern fill itself with the pearl's strange shine. [557] "I'm carrying it down the mountain," the pilgrim said. [566] "To the villages. [569] So that when a woman kneads bread, she will feel the wheat remembering sunlight. [583] So a child's first word will ring like a bell." [593] "And me?" Mirel asked. [597] "What happens to a seer in a world that stops repeating itself?" [609] "You learn to be surprised," the pilgrim said. [617] "It's the better half of wonder." [623]

The pilgrim left at dawn, lantern glowing dark and bright at once, and Mirel did not try to foresee the road ahead of those small worn boots. [650] She went back inside and listened. [656] The cave still sang its new wandering songs, and the pearl, dimmer now but changed, kept turning in its cup of stone. [678] For the first time in thirty years, Mirel did not know what tomorrow held, and the not-knowing felt like a door swinging open. [701] She made tea and tasted, truly tasted, the leaves remembering rain. [712] It was, she decided, the finest vision she had ever been given, precisely because no one had given it to her in advance. [735]
