Marrow kept her workshop beneath the hill, where an orchard grew in a cavern fed by dripping stalactites. [18] Each drop carried mineral sweetness down to the pale apple trees, and each tree had been coaxed into being by her quiet conjuring. [41] Nobody in the village above knew what she was. [50] Secrecy was not vanity; it was the last promise she had made to Ilse. [64] Ilse, the taxidermist, who had walked into the winter fog three years ago and never walked out. [81] No body, no farewell, no proof of death; only an absence shaped exactly like a person. [97] The grief had no edges, so Marrow could not put it down. [109] She decided, in her gently inventive way, to measure it instead. [120] If sorrow obeyed laws, she reasoned, then it could be mapped, and a map always shows a way through. [139] She called the project her mathematics of emotion. [147] Her instruments were strange because her materials were what Ilse left behind. [159] Chief among them was a taxidermist's pair of glass eyes, amber-brown, the ones Ilse had saved for a fox she never finished. [181] Marrow had learned that conjured light, passed through them, bent according to feeling. [194] Hold the eyes over a memory and the light fractured into angles she could record. [209] Joy split wide and shallow; fear drew tight spirals; grief, ordinary grief, made clean descending arcs. [225] But her own loss produced something else: a pattern that never closed, lines that circled and reopened, equations without a final term. [247] For three years she failed to solve it. [255] The breakthrough waited in the orchard path, underfoot the whole time. [266] Ilse had laid a line of mosaic stepping stones between the trees, fitted from broken plates and river tile. [285] Years of dripping water had chipped them, and Marrow had mourned each chip as another small theft. [302] Then came the night the orchard grew silent beneath the moon. [313] The stalactites, which had dripped without pause for as long as she had lived there, simply stopped. [330] Moonlight fell through the cavern's sink-hole mouth, and in that unfamiliar quiet she heard her own pulse and went walking to calm it. [353] On the third stepping stone, the moon caught the chipped patterns at a slant, and Marrow stood still. [371] The chips were not random. [376] Water falls where stone directs it, and Ilse had laid every tile. [388] The damage traced curves Marrow knew by heart: her own unsolvable pattern, the open loops, the lines that circled and reopened. [409] Ilse had written something here, slowly, using years of dripping water as her pen. [423] Marrow ran for the glass eyes. [429] Kneeling on the cold stone, she held them above the mosaic and breathed a thread of conjured light through their amber centers. [451] The light split, fell across the chipped patterns, and the two geometries began to dovetail. [466] Her open loops slid into the stone's broken grooves like joinery, like two cut boards made for each other by the same hand. [489] Together they formed a figure neither could make alone: not a closed circle, but a spiral that climbed. [507] Marrow read it the way she read all her equations, slowly, aloud, tasting each term. [522] It did not say Ilse was alive. [529] It did not say she was dead. [536] It said, in the grammar of curves, what Marrow translated through tears: an unfinished line is not a broken one. [556] Ilse had known she might go where fog goes. [565] Whatever errand or danger had called her, she had prepared for the possibility of vanishing, and she had left the only message that could survive ambiguity: mathematics, patient as dripping water. [596] She had even chosen the glass eyes as the key, knowing Marrow would never throw away an unfinished fox. [615] Marrow sat among the silent trees until the moon moved off the stone. [628] Then, somewhere far above, the rain must have returned, because a single drop fell from a stalactite and struck the mosaic with a sound like a struck bell. [656] The orchard's breath resumed. [660] Marrow laughed, once, raggedly, surprised by the noise of her own hope. [672] Her loss was still ambiguous; no equation could resurrect or bury anyone. [684] But she finally understood what the mathematics of emotion had been trying to teach her: some sums are not meant to resolve, only to continue. [709] In the days after, she carved a new stepping stone and set it past the last of Ilse's, leaving its surface bare for the water to write on. [737] She placed the glass eyes in a niche above it, facing the path, so anything that came out of the fog would be seen coming home. [763] And every night, beneath whatever moon there was, the orchard dripped its slow arithmetic, adding one drop, then another, toward an answer she could wait for. [789]
