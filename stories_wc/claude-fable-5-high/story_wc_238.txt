Vesna Adler wrote the dead's gossip column for the county paper, and the dead, vain as anyone, read it. [19] Print a ghost's secret and it loses its grip; this was her whole trade as an exorcist, ink instead of incense. [40] Her miracles were imperfect ones, always: the haunting ended, but something stained the air where it had stood, like smoke after a candle. [63] The county had sold the old orchard to a warehouse firm, and the saws were scheduled for Monday dawn. [82] That gave Vesna one Sunday of hush, the strange held breath before trees are cut down, to do the thing she had dodged for nine years. [108] Her sister Lenka haunted the sheet music archive at the edge of the orchard, a brick annex where she had catalogued scores until the night she died. [135] Vesna had never printed Lenka's secret, because Lenka's secret was Vesna's fault. [147] They had quarreled over a man neither of them kept, and Vesna had said the unforgivable sentence, and Lenka had driven into the fog and not out of it. [176] The apology was nine years overdue, and Monday the archive came down with the apple trees. [192] She brought two things: a coil rig her late mentor had built, and Lenka's antique perfume bottle, glass gone green with age, still holding a breath of orchard blossom. [221] Ghosts cannot speak, but they can move charge; pass a conductor through their cold and they induce currents, faint as a pulse under a thumb. [246] Wire the coil to a speaker and the current becomes a voice, scratchy and thin, like a record played through rain. [267] The archive smelled of dry paper and old varnish, shelves of scores rising into dark like steps nobody climbed anymore. [287] Outside, the orchard stood in its last silence, no birds, no wind, the trees waiting like witnesses before testimony. [306] Vesna set the coil on the catalogue desk and uncorked the perfume bottle, because scent is the oldest summons there is. [327] The cold arrived the way evening does, gradually and then all at once. [340] The speaker crackled. [343] You came smelling of me, Lenka's voice said, threaded through static. [354] Vesna's throat closed around nine years of rehearsed sentences. [363] What came out instead was her trade reflex, a metaphor, because she had only ever known how to advise sideways. [383] "An orchard doesn't blame the frost," she said, "but the frost should still say what it is." [400] Say it plainly, the current hummed; I have been dead too long for poetry. [414] So Vesna said it plainly. [419] "I told you that you were the lesser sister, and you believed me, and you drove away believing it, and I am sorry." [442] The words were small in the big room, and that was right; apologies should not be performances. [459] The static softened. [462] I stayed for the scores, Lenka said, not for spite; somebody had to keep the music in order until somebody living wanted it. [485] But I also stayed because I hoped you would come, and hope is a hinge; it keeps a door from being only a wall. [509] Vesna laughed wetly; even dead, her sister out-metaphored her. [518] They talked until the perfume thinned, reconnecting in scraps: the kitchen songs, the man neither missed, the mother they had divided between them like an estate. [544] Then Lenka asked for the column. [550] Print me, she said; print the quarrel and the fog and the apology, all of it, so the grip lets go before the saws come. [575] Vesna wrote the column at the catalogue desk by phone light, her own confession set in gossip's clothes. [593] She read it aloud, which counts as publication where the dead are concerned. [606] The cold lifted the way grief does, incompletely. [614] This was the imperfection in all her miracles, and she had braced for it: a residue, a remainder, something that refuses the clean ending. [638] What remained this time was the smell of orchard blossom, though the bottle now stood empty and the trees outside had never bloomed in October. [663] She corked the bottle anyway and pocketed it, a reliquary for an imperfect grace. [677] On her way out she pulled one score from the shelf, Lenka's favorite, a nocturne with her sister's pencil marks still arguing with the tempo. [702] Somebody living wanted it now. [707] Monday at dawn the saws started, and the hush broke into noise and sawdust, and the orchard went down row by row. [729] Vesna stood at the fence and did not look away, because the frost had finally said what it was, and witnesses owe the felled at least their eyes. [757] In her pocket, faintly, impossibly, the empty bottle still smelled of blossom. [769] Her next column ran under the usual cheeky header, but the regular readers, living and otherwise, noticed it read less like gossip and more like a hymn. [796]
