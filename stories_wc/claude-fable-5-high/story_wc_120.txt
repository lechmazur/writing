For eleven years, Wren had written fortunes that no one understood. [11] She slipped them into cookies at the Lucky Gate factory: not platitudes, but riddles, half-melodies in words, instructions for songs that had never been played. [36] Customers complained; she kept writing. [41] Because Wren was homesick for a time rather than a place, and the fortunes were her letters home. [59]

The time was the winter she turned nine, when her grandmother still lived in the glass spire by the frozen lake, an abandoned acoustics laboratory the old woman had bought for almost nothing. [92] Grandmother Ila had pressed a nautilus shell segment into Wren's palm that winter, one curved chamber sawn from the spiral, and said that the whole song was too big for anyone, so we each carry one chamber of it. [131] Ila had been a conductor once, of orchestras that existed; she dreamed instead of conducting the symphonies no one had heard, the ones still folded inside the world. [159] She died before she found the way in. [167] Wren inherited the spire, the shell segment, and the homesickness, a longing aimed backward through her own life like a telescope. [188]

This December, after the factory closed for the season, she finally went back. [201] The spire stood where memory kept it, a needle of glass panes above white ice, humming faintly when wind crossed the lake. [223] Inside, Ila's notebooks waited under dust, and on the last page, in handwriting like rigging, a single instruction: map the way back, and the music will meet you at dawn. [253]

Wren understood it the way she understood her own fortunes: not at first, then completely. [268] She had been writing the map for eleven years, one cookie at a time. [282] Each cryptic fortune was a childhood memory in disguise: the smell of cold glass, her grandmother counting silences, skates carving figure eights that never closed. [307] She had carried a box of rejected fortunes in her car, the ones the factory called too strange. [325] Now she spread them across the laboratory floor and began to arrange them, not by date but by feeling, plotting her childhood as geography. [349] The memory of mittens drying on the radiator went north. [359] The first time she heard ice boom and shift beneath her boots went east, toward the lake. [376] The night Ila held the shell segment to the window and said, listen, the horizon is rehearsing, went at the center. [397]

Hour by hour the map grew, and the spire's old acoustic panels seemed to lean in, attentive. [414] Wren worked fast, racing her own body, because she knew this kind of clarity came only once, a thin window before exhaustion hit and turned vision back into paper. [443] Twice her eyes blurred; twice she pressed the cool nautilus segment to her forehead, and its spiral steadiness held her upright. [464]

Near five in the morning, the map closed its last figure eight. [476] She saw it then: her memories did not form a line of time at all, but a spiral, chamber after chamber, widening like the shell. [501] Homesickness was not a wound; it was an ear, tuned to a frequency the present muffled. [517]

She climbed the spire's narrow stair with the shell segment in her fist and stepped onto the observation deck just as the sky began to pale. [543] Below, the frozen lake had been polished by wind into a flawless plate. [556] Dawn came up double: one sun lifting from the world's edge, one rising out of the ice to meet it, a mirrored horizon closing like two hands about to clap. [586] Everything held its breath, but upward, the hush of an audience the instant before the first note. [603]

Wren raised the nautilus segment like a baton. [611] She did not know how to conduct what could not be heard, so she conducted what she remembered: the boom of shifting ice as percussion, the radiator's tick as metronome, her grandmother's counted silences as rests. [647] The glass panes of the spire caught the doubled light and trembled, and the lake answered with a long, low note that traveled shore to shore. [673] It was not music exactly. [678] It was the shape music leaves in a person who has waited her whole life to hear it. [696] Where the two suns touched, the seam of sky and ice, Wren felt the symphony pass through her, vast and unheard by anyone else, and she kept time for it anyway. [727]

When the suns merged and the horizon became single again, the note faded, and exhaustion arrived at last, gentle now, like applause from very far away. [753] Wren slept on the deck in her coat and woke at noon, no longer homesick for then. [770] She wrote one final fortune and folded it into the shell's open chamber: the song was always here. [788] You only had to come home to conduct it. [797]
