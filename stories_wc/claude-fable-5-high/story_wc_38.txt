They lowered Wren into the sunken laboratory at dusk, down a throat of rusted ladders, because she was the last code breaker the Directorate had not broken. [27] The sea pressed its green weight against the cracked viewports, and old instruments ticked in the dark like patient hearts. [47] Decode the dead woman's journals by dawn, the warden had said, or the prisoners in the lower cells drown with the tide. [69] Wren could not answer him; fever had taken her voice years ago, and the Directorate mistook her silence for surrender. [89] It was not surrender; it was a calm defiance, folded small and carried everywhere, like a blade sewn into a hem. [110] The journals waited on a steel table beside two yellowed bone cubes: hangman's dice, carved from the gallows-trade, their pips drilled deep as wells. [134] In a high corner, a pale spider began its web, and Wren understood her true clock: when the web closed its last spiral, the tide would turn. [161] She opened the first journal and felt the floor tilt beneath her, though the sea had not moved. [179] The handwriting leaned left, the way hers did; the loops of the letters were her loops; the cipher hummed with a mysterious familiarity, like a lullaby heard through a wall. [209] Wren worked the way she always worked: lyrically, hearing codes as songs, hunting the meter beneath the noise until the hidden rhyme confessed itself. [233] This cipher was a ballad with a missing chorus, and the chorus, she realized, was numerical. [249] Each entry began with a small sketch of two dice; she set the bone cubes to match the drawing, summed the pips, and counted that many words deep, where the true sentence began. [282] The dead woman had keyed her whole life to the dice that once chose who hanged, turning the instrument of execution into the instrument of escape. [308] Entry by entry, the journal unfolded: I write my fears in cipher so they cannot be used against me, the dead woman confessed. [331] I encrypt my vulnerability until it reads as strength; let the Directorate find only armor where my soft places live. [351] Then, deeper: My daughter cannot speak, but she will read this someday, because silence learns every language eventually. [369] Wren's hands stilled; the familiarity was no longer mysterious; it was her mother's. [382] Her mother had built this laboratory before the Directorate sank it, before they filled its lower cells with everyone who refused them. [404] The final entries were not memoir at all; they were schematics in verse, a coded map of the flood gates, the pressure locks, the one drowned corridor that still led to open water and a forgotten diving bell. [442] Above her, the spider had finished half its spiral. [451] The warden had retreated topside to wait, certain the sea was guard enough, so Wren slipped down to the cell block, where forty prisoners watched her through bars slick with brine. [482] She could not tell them the plan, so she explained it the only way she had ever explained anything: through dance. [503] Her body became the corridor: arms arched into flood gates, fingers counting pip-numbers, a slow turn for the pressure lock, a held breath and a rising gesture for the diving bell. [534] The prisoners read her the way she read ciphers, and one by one they nodded. [549] The cell locks were old Directorate tumblers, six-numbered, and Wren almost laughed: six faces, like a die. [566] She did not need to roll her mother's dice; the journal's final stanza, keyed by snake eyes, spelled the combinations outright, and the doors opened without protest. [593] They moved through the drowned corridor as the spider above tightened its last rings, the web and the escape completing themselves together, strand by strand, breath by breath. [621] At the diving bell, the last prisoner hesitated, terrified, and Wren took his shaking hands and pressed the hangman's dice into them. [643] What once measured deaths would now count the living; that, too, was a kind of decryption. [659] When the warden descended at dawn, he found only an empty laboratory, a finished web trembling in the corner, and a single page in Wren's hand, written in her mother's cipher. [690] He would spend years failing to break it, never guessing it was not a threat but a confession: I was afraid every moment, and I went anyway. [717] Somewhere above the waves, forty people Wren had liberated learned to read her dancing, and her silence, encrypted, read as strength. [738]
