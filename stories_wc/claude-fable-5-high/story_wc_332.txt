Mara kept the library on Hush Island open only at odd hours, from midnight until the ferries woke. [18] Nobody questioned the schedule, because nobody on the whispering archipelago questioned anything the wind had already explained. [35] The islands talked at night, salt syllables moving between the pines, and the library was where their sentences came to rest. [56] Mara shelved them the way other librarians shelved books, and she did it alone. [70] She had been alone since her brother Ilo rowed out one June dawn and never rowed back. [87] He left no note, only his watercolor palette, twelve dried pans of color worn down in a pattern too deliberate to be accident. [110] Cobalt hollowed to the metal. [115] Rose touched once, as if regretted. [121] Gold scraped clean entirely. [125] For three years she read the palette the way she read the wind, certain it was a cipher of longing, a message Ilo had been too shy to speak aloud. [155] Then, last winter, she found his journal wedged behind the atlas shelf. [167] One line, repeated: I keep dreaming a color that does not exist yet, and every morning it runs from me. [187] A runaway dream, then. [191] Not a destination he had fled toward, but a color he had chased off the edge of the map. [210] Mara could not paint. [214] But she owned a camera, and she understood, in the way librarians understand index systems, that the worn pans were coordinates: each color a direction, each depth of wear a distance. [245] Cobalt meant the deep channel east. [251] Gold, used up completely, meant sunrise, and sunrise meant the hour Ilo had vanished into. [266] So she began closing the library at five and rowing out with the camera, decoding her brother one dawn at a time. [288] Where his palette said cobalt, she anchored in the channel and photographed the light coming up through the deep water. [308] Where it said rose, touched once, she waited at the reef where flamingo grass blooms a single week each year. [328] He had painted with pigment; she would paint with light, long exposures that let the sun drag its colors across the sensor like a loaded brush. [354] Each photograph was a translation, an attempt to vitalize the dried husks of his palette, to give blood back to colors he had worn down to nothing. [381] If she could resurrect every pan, she reasoned, she could reconstruct the color he had been chasing. [398] She could capture the runaway dream and, by capturing it, finally know where it had taken him. [415] Eleven pans took eleven months. [420] The twelfth pan was empty, never filled, a white square at the corner of the tin. [436] She almost dismissed it. [440] But the wind that night came through the library stacks in a syntax she had never shelved before, and it kept returning to the empty square the way a tongue returns to a missing tooth. [475] An empty pan was not nothing. [481] It was a color Ilo had not found yet. [490] It was the dream itself. [495] She rowed out on the anniversary of his leaving, in the hush of improbable beginnings, that thin blue minute when the night has ended and the day has not agreed to start. [527] The archipelago held its breath around her; for once, the islands had nothing to whisper. [542] She opened the shutter and left it open. [550] The sun rose through a fog bank, and for four seconds the light was a color she had no name for, not gold, not rose, not anything in twelve pans or eleven months of mornings. [585] The shutter closed. [588] When she developed the photograph, the color was there, and so, faintly, was a rowboat at the edge of the frame, light-blurred, impossible, four seconds of someone pulling steadily toward the brightness. [620] She did not pretend it proved anything. [627] Light bends; longing bends further. [632] But she understood, finally, what the palette had been telling her all along. [645] Ilo had not drowned chasing a color. [652] He had become the kind of absence that gives off light, the way a window gives off morning, and the cipher was never instructions for finding him. [679] It was instructions for seeing what he saw. [687] Mara printed the photograph and hung it in the library above the atlas shelf, where the journal had hidden. [706] She kept her odd hours still, midnight to ferry-light, but now she closed early on clear mornings. [723] The wind learned her new schedule and adjusted its whispering accordingly. [734] And the empty twelfth pan stayed empty, by choice now, a held breath, a door left open in case the color, or its painter, ever came rowing home. [762]
