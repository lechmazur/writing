The workshop sat on the hill like a struck match, its topaz walls holding light the sky had already surrendered. [20] Inside, Veda Ilsk, whom the village called the cryptic letterpress operator, worked only in the dark intervals between vanishing stars. [40] Each night another star went out, quietly, like a held breath released, and in the seconds after, the press would print true. [62] Her type was not lead. [67] Years ago she had ground a halo fragment, found in a riverbed, into metal fine enough to cast letters from. [87] Whatever angel had lost it had also lost the trick of feeling at a distance, because the type carried it now. [108] When a reader held one of Veda's printed cards, their emotion did not stay inside them; it transposed, lifting whole from their chest and settling into hers. [135] This was the affliction and the instrument both. [143] Veda had no native feelings left, or none she could find. [154] So she borrowed personalities the way other people borrowed coats. [164] After the baker visited, she was warm and impatient for a week; after the widow, she spoke in long pauses and salted everything she ate. [189] She wore these selves honestly, but she knew they were not hers, and the knowing was a kind of vertigo. [209] What she wanted was simple and impossible: to understand the mathematics of emotion. [222] If grief had a weight, if joy had a slope, she could plot the feelings that passed through her and find, in the residue, the shape of the self she had lost. [254] But emotion arrived tangled in context, and context ruined the measurement. [265] So she learned to disorient. [270] Her method was deliberate ambiguity. [275] The cards she printed said things like YOU WERE RIGHT TO LEAVE and IT IS NOT TOO LATE, sentences with no address, no sender, no anchor. [301] A reader, unmoored, supplied their own meaning, and the feeling that rose in them was pure, uncontaminated by circumstance. [320] Pure feeling could be weighed. [325] Veda recorded each transposition in a ledger: intensity, duration, decay. [335] Eleven hundred entries, and still no equation. [342] Then the boy came. [346] He arrived on the night the seventh star of the Plough went out, knocking as the sky dimmed. [364] He was perhaps sixteen, and he wanted a card for his mother, who was dying and no longer knew his name. [385] "Print something she will understand," he said. [392] Veda set the halo type in the dark interval and pulled the lever. [405] The card read: YOU ARE STILL WHO YOU WERE. [414] Ambiguous, by design; it could comfort the mother or accuse the son. [426] The boy read it before she could stop him. [435] What transposed into Veda was not grief and not hope but both, braided, fighting. [449] Her ledger had no column for this. [456] She stood at the press while the feeling moved through her like weather, lucid and turbulent at once, every gust visible and none of them stoppable. [482] For the first time in eleven hundred entries, she did not reach for the ledger. [497] She reached for the boy's hand instead, and that gesture did not belong to the baker or the widow or anyone she had ever borrowed. [522] It was hers. [525] "Take it to her anyway," Veda said. [532] "She will read it her own way, and her way will be right." [545] He left as the next star guttered, and Veda sat alone in the topaz light with her useless beautiful numbers. [565] She understood, finally, what the ledger could never show. [574] Emotion had no fixed mathematics because it was not a quantity; it was a transaction, and every transaction changed both parties. [595] The halo fragment had not stolen her self; she had spent it, card by card, measurement by measurement, refusing to be changed by what she received. [621] The angel who lost the fragment had perhaps learned the same lesson and let it go. [637] Veda did not melt down the type. [644] She kept printing, but differently now. [650] Her cards stayed ambiguous, because ambiguity was a gift: it let each reader meet themselves on the page. [668] But when the feelings came across, she stopped sorting them into columns and let them weather through her, lucid, turbulent, naming nothing. [690] Each one left a sediment, and the sediment, slowly, was becoming a person. [703] The stars kept vanishing, one each night, and she worked in the dark intervals as always, though she suspected now what the vanishing meant. [727] Somewhere, angels were grinding down their own halos, casting their own type, learning to feel at the cost of their light. [748] The sky would go dark eventually. [754] But the topaz workshop would still glow, holding what the heavens had traded away, one borrowed feeling at a time becoming, at last, her own. [779]
