The carnival never stopped, not even for drowning. [8] Velmara was a thousand rafts lashed into a floating city, its lanterns burning through every fog, its calliopes wheezing from dusk to dusk. [31] Issa Marn worked the arrival docks as a culture shock absorber, the city's strangest official job. [47] When mainlanders stumbled off the ferries, dizzy with drumbeats and dyed smoke, she steadied them, translated the masks, taught them which laughter was welcome and which was warning. [75] Lately the arrivals had changed. [80] They came in pressed linen, carrying surveyor's cases instead of luggage, asking about mooring rights instead of music. [98] The Tidewater Consortium meant to buy Velmara whole. [106] In sixty days the Harbor Council would vote on privatization, and the surveyors were mapping which rafts to keep as attractions and which to cut loose. [132] Issa was paid by that same Council, which made her smile official and her grief private. [148] She was carnival-born; her grandmother had helped raise the first raft over the drowned old town. [164] In her pocket she carried a foundation stone chip, pried from the sunken church before the water closed over it, gray and rough as a tooth. [190] Her grandmother had pressed it into her palm and said, We float, but we are not unmoored. [207] Two allegiances pulled at her: the Council that paid her, the carnival that made her. [222] She could not fight openly without losing the badge that let her read the surveyors' maps. [238] So she fought quietly. [242] Issa's mind was orderly and intuitive at once; she could feel a pattern before she could name it, then file it neatly where it belonged. [267] Studying the Consortium's draft charts one night, she felt the pattern arrive like a chill. [282] The maps valued rafts by position. [288] Whatever sat along the central canal would be preserved, polished, priced; whatever drifted at the edges would be severed and towed away. [310] Gentrification with a tide chart, Issa called it privately. [319] But Velmara's rafts were not fixed. [325] They could be unlashed and relashed, and the survey would not be finalized until the vote. [341] If the carnival could reorganize itself, weaving the puppet barges and elder houseboats into the valuable center, the Consortium could not carve the city without gutting its own prize. [370] She could not announce this; the Council monitored the criers and the printing rafts. [384] But nobody monitored the paint. [389] Velmara's hulls had always bloomed with graffiti, murals layered like sediment, and the surveyors saw only decoration. [406] Issa found Pell, a mural painter she had once absorbed into the city when he arrived seasick and friendless, and taught him a cipher built from carnival iconography. [434] A juggler with three moons meant move on the third tide. [445] Rope painted around a dancer's wrist named which raft should bind to which. [458] Night after night, murals appeared, and the city read them the way it read weather. [473] Rafts began to drift, casually, festively, as if the carnival had simply decided to dance in a new formation. [492] The surveyors redrew their maps and found them obsolete by morning. [503] Desperation ran under all of it like cold water under the boards. [515] Issa slept little, briefed newcomers by day with a calm face, and decoded the Council's frustration by night. [533] When a Council clerk asked her, almost idly, whether the murals meant anything, she felt both her allegiances tighten like ropes on a cleat. [557] They mean we are alive, she said, which was true enough to pass. [570] On the fifty-ninth night, a Consortium agent caught Pell mid-mural, brush dripping. [582] Issa arrived flashing her badge, explaining smoothly that mural painting was protected cultural practice, that frightening artists would sour the very authenticity the Consortium hoped to sell. [609] The agent, new to the city and grateful to the woman who had once taught him the customs, let it go. [630] Her two loyalties, for one breath, had pulled in the same direction. [642] On the morning of the vote, the Council convened on the central barge and looked out at a city rearranged. [662] The elder houseboats now lined the golden canal; the puppet barges flanked the grand carousel; there was no clean cut left anywhere, no edge to sever that would not bleed value. [693] The Consortium's offer collapsed into amendments, then into delay, then into a charter clause that no raft could be cut loose without its household's consent. [718] It was not victory; the money would come again, patient as the tide. [731] But it was time, and time was what the carnival had always known how to spend. [747] That night Issa stood with Pell beside his newest mural: a woman holding a small gray stone above the water. [767] She took the foundation chip from her pocket and pressed it into the wet paint, fixing it there like a signature. [788] We float, she said, but we are not unmoored. [797]
