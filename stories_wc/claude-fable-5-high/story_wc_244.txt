Nobody could ever find Mara Quill after a disaster ended. [10] She arrived with the floodwater or the fire, routed trucks and tarps and insulin with eerie precision, and dissolved before anyone could thank her. [34] Thanks, she believed, created debts, and debts created bonds, and bonds were the one cargo she had never learned to route. [55]

In her pocket she carried a fragment of moon rock, given to her by a retired astronaut she had pulled from a collapsed clinic in Valparaiso. [81] He had pressed it into her palm and said, now you owe me a visit, and she had fled the country instead. [103] The rock weighed almost nothing and everything. [110]

Twenty years of elegant vanishing had left her fluent in jokes and bankrupt in friends. [125] She was desperately jocular the way drowning people are desperately buoyant. [136] One night, alone in a depot office, she admitted the diagnosis: loneliness, chronic, self-inflicted. [150] Being a logistician, she decided to engineer the cure the only way she knew: as a supply problem. [168]

She could not invite anyone; invitations from Mara Quill would be studied like ransom notes. [183] But she knew how rumors moved, because she had spent decades moving cargo along exactly those routes. [200] So she seeded one: tell a ferry cook, who tells a customs officer, who tells his sister the radio host. [220] The rumor said that at the disused railway station outside Brennick, the one strangled in ivy, a cure for loneliness would be dispensed, but only to those who brought something to give away. [253] Reciprocity was the active ingredient; she was certain of that much. [264] You cannot catalyze a reaction with only one element in the flask. [276]

The rumor traveled through improbable chains, mutating beautifully. [284] By the time it reached Brennick, the cure was variously a tea, a choir, a dog, and a small gray stone from the moon. [308] That last variant startled her; she had told no one about the rock. [321] Rumors, like rivers, find the truth downhill. [328]

On the appointed evening she arrived early, intending, as always, to watch from the edges. [343] The station was a green ruin, ivy swallowing the ticket windows, the platform clock stopped at some forgotten quarter past. [363] People came anyway, in ones and twos, carrying their tariffs: jars of plum jam, a violin, a folding chair, an argumentative parrot. [385] No one was in charge, so everyone was, courteously. [394] A man directed foot traffic with apologetic bows; two grandmothers annexed the stationmaster's office and declared it a soup embassy. [414] It was anarchy with excellent manners, rules emerging and dissolving like weather, all of it strangely gentle. [431]

Mara stood at the platform's end, rehearsing exits. [439] Then a girl with a skinned knee marched up and demanded the cure, holding out a drawing of a horse as payment. [461] Mara made a joke, her oldest reflex, something about horses being notoriously bad at trains. [476] The girl did not laugh; she waited, hand still out, ferociously patient. [488] And that was the moment the story changed: not when the rumor started, but when its author was finally asked to pay into it. [512]

Mara took out the fragment of moon rock. [520] She told the girl, and the crowd that gathered, the truth: a man had given her this, and she had never gone back, because owing someone is how they keep you. [551] She had spent her life believing that was a trap. [561] Standing in the ivy, watching strangers trade jam for violin lessons and soup for stories, she understood it was the whole machine. [583] We are bound by reciprocity or we are bound by nothing. [594] The cure for loneliness was not a substance; it was a debt you agreed to keep open. [611]

She gave the girl the moon rock. [618] Conditions applied, she said: the girl must give it away someday, to someone who needed proof that the sky can be owed and repaid. [642] The girl nodded with the gravity of treaties and surrendered the horse drawing. [655] Mara folded it into the pocket where the rock had lived for twenty years. [669] It weighed almost nothing and everything. [675]

She stayed past midnight, which she had never done anywhere. [685] She told jokes that landed because she was no longer telling them to escape. [699] The grandmothers fed her; she owed them now, and said so, out loud, like a vow. [715] In the morning she wrote a letter to a retired astronaut in Valparaiso, beginning: I am late, but I am coming, and I am bringing soup. [741] The station kept its ivy and its stopped clock, but people kept coming back, trading and owing, every gathering seeded by a rumor that was, for once, entirely true. [770]
