Rhea Volos counted her steps in rhyme, the way she always did, chanting old powder recipes under her breath as she walked the road to Fort Caldessa. [27] Saltpeter, sulfur, charcoal fine; willow burns the longest line. [36] The chant was not superstition; it was a filter. [45] A wandering fireworks designer learns to let the rhythm sieve the world, so that everything ordinary slides past and only the strange snags in the net of her attention. [74] It was how she heard the fortress before she saw it: a silence too complete, a town holding its breath. [94] Fort Caldessa rose out of the valley at dusk, its broken battlements smothered in climbing roses, every crack stuffed with blossom, as if the stone had decided that if it could not be a fortress anymore it would be a garden. [135] Beautiful, and wrong. [138] No lamps, no cookfires, no children shouting. [145] At the gate an old warden told her why. [154] Three months ago the sky had gone out. [162] Not clouds, not smoke; the stars simply stopped arriving, night after night, a black lid clamped over the valley. [181] People had begun to whisper that the dark was a verdict. [192] Some had stopped planting. [196] Some had stopped speaking. [200] Hopelessness, Rhea knew, has its own gravity; it pulls everything down, slowly, politely, until nothing stands. [216] "You knew Ilan," the warden said, eyeing her scorched satchel. [226] "He was our fire spinner. [231] "He swore he would teach the dark some manners. [240] "Then his heart gave out on the chapel steps. [249] "Nobody has lit so much as a candle since." [258] Rhea had known Ilan on the festival roads, a laughing man who treated flame like a dance partner who might bite. [279] She walked the courtyard chanting her recipes, letting her ambient awareness comb the dimming fortress, and the chant snagged on something at the chapel steps. [304] Ilan's kerosene rag knot still hung from an iron hook, the tight braided fist of cloth every fire spinner ties, stiff with old fuel, smelling faintly of his act. [333] And beneath it, set into the chapel floor, the mosaic was wrong. [345] Wrong in a way only a filtering mind would catch: among the thousand blue tiles, certain ones had been recently relaid, their grout pale, their angles deliberate. [372] Rhea knelt and read them like a score. [380] Ilan had left a note in mosaic, tile by tile, in the road-sign cipher traveling performers use: LIFT CHARGES, BELL TOWER, BURST LOW, MAKE THEM LOOK UP. [407] He had died mid-sentence, mid-plan, his last performance hidden in the floor for whichever fire-handed friend wandered through next. [426] Rhea laughed once, quietly, the way you laugh at a joke told from inside a coffin. [442] "All right, old man," she said. "Manners it is." [451] She worked through the next day with a solemn mischief, requisitioning the bell tower, grinding charcoal, packing lift charges in paper she begged from the despairing baker. [478] "If the sky refuses to make stars," she told the warden, "we are obligated, professionally, to file a complaint." [497] He almost smiled. [500] She untied Ilan's kerosene rag knot last, soaked it fresh, and fixed it to the master fuse; the old showman would light his own finale. [525] Night came down like a lid again, starless, total, the hush so deep Rhea could hear roses shedding petals on the battlements. [547] The townsfolk gathered because she had asked, standing in the courtyard with their faces aimed at their shoes. [565] Down was where their eyes had learned to live. [574] Rhea climbed the bell tower chanting, struck a spark, and touched it to the rag knot. [590] It caught with a soft, familiar whump, Ilan's signature sound, and the fuse ran hissing into the dark. [608] The first shell burst low, as instructed, gold and close, so near that people flinched and, flinching, looked up. [627] The second bloomed higher, silver over the roses. [635] The third opened white at the top of the sky, and for three full seconds Fort Caldessa had stars again, handmade, defiant, falling slowly enough to be believed. [663] Someone cheered. [665] Someone wept. [667] A child asked, loudly, whether the sky was apologizing. [676] "It is negotiating," Rhea called down, grave as a judge, and lit the next fuse. [691] She knew fireworks did not fix a broken sky. [700] But hopelessness pulls downward, and the cure for a pull is a push: necks craned, chins lifted, a whole town's gaze hauled upward against that gravity, one burst at a time. [731] When the last ember faded, the dark returned, but it had been argued with, and everyone had seen it lose. [751] In the morning, the warden found Rhea adding tiles to the chapel mosaic, blue on blue, leaving instructions for the next wanderer: powder stores, fuse lengths, the cipher key. [780] Then she shouldered her satchel and walked out through the roses, chanting. [792]
