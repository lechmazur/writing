They called it the Hush, the basalt plateau where compasses forgot north and birds refused to land. [17] Maren had been exiled there four years ago, after the covenant game went wrong. [31] For three centuries, the valley and mountain peoples had renewed their peace at every eclipse, two emissaries playing a single game of chess that was required, by ancient design, to end in stalemate. [64] Neither side could win; that was the whole covenant, balance enacted in wood and patience. [79] Maren had tutored the valley's emissary, the young heir Ilsa, and he had taught her too well. [96] Under the last eclipse she had seen a winning line and, being seventeen and brilliant, taken it. [113] Checkmate in six, the covenant eclipsed in the very shadow meant to bless it, and the tutor blamed for the breaking. [134]

He kept only one thing from the old life: a worry stone of polished basalt, cut from the original covenant table, its surface dented where three generations of thumbs had pressed their doubts into it. [169] He rubbed the dent each evening and walked the Hush, because the plateau's strangeness was the only thing difficult enough to occupy him. [192]

The Hush was a magnetic nexus, a knot of iron-rich stone where the planet's field bent and sang. [210] At twilight, when the air cooled, the singing became audible, a low pulsing hum that rose and fell. [228] Most exiles would have heard noise. [234] Maren, who had spent a lifetime being thoughtfully precise about patterns, heard something else: the silences between the pulses were not random. [256] He began to chart them. [261] Short gap, long gap, paired gaps, a rest of nine beats. [272] Night after night he tried to correlate the intervals with anything he knew: tide tables, star transits, the calendar of eclipses. [293] Nothing fit until the evening he sketched a chessboard in the dust. [305] Sixty-four squares; the gaps clustered in sixty-four distinct lengths. [314] His thumb went still in the worry stone's dent. [323]

The first builders of the covenant had quarried this plateau for their table; the stone remembered, somehow, what had been played upon it. [346] Or perhaps the founders, knowing memory fails, had encoded the original game into the nexus itself, magnetized move by move into the bedrock. [369] Either way, the message was not in the hum but in its absences. [382] He could only read it by translating silence. [390]

It took him a month of twilights to transcribe the whole game, kneeling in the violet dusk with cold fingers and a charcoal stub. [414] What emerged astonished him. [418] The original covenant game was not a cautious shuffle toward stalemate. [429] It was ferocious. [432] Both founders had attacked with everything, sacrificed queens, burned through brilliancies, and then, at the precipice of victory, each had chosen the harder move, the one that gave the other breath. [463] The stalemate was not the absence of winning. [471] It was winning refused, twice, out of love for the game's continuation. [483] Maren wept, because he understood at last what he had failed to teach Ilsa: not how to draw, but how to discover beauty in the difficult, how the hardest move and the most generous one could be the same. [522]

The next eclipse was coming. [527] The moon had already entered its waxing run; he had until the gibbous turned full, before the next phase, to put the game in the right hands. [554] An exile could not cross the border, but an exile's letter could. [566] He did not have to send it far. [574] Ilsa came to him instead, riding through the demagnetized dusk with no compass, navigating by the hum itself. [592] She had been searching for him for a year, carrying her guilt the way he carried his stone. [610] "The councils will replay the rite at the eclipse," she said, "but nobody remembers how a stalemate is supposed to feel." [631]

He showed her the transcription. [636] They played through the founders' game on a board scratched into basalt, twilight after twilight, until she could feel the shape of it: the courage of the attacks, the greater courage of the refusals. [670] "You taught me to see winning lines," she said on the last evening. [683] "Now teach the part you skipped." [689] "I didn't skip it," he said. [695] "I hadn't learned it yet." [700]

When she rode for the border, she carried the transcription and his worry stone, the dent worn smooth and deep. [720] "So the table can have its piece back," he told her. [731] He stayed on the plateau, as the law required, and watched the moon fatten. [745] On the night of the eclipse he stood at the nexus and listened. [758] Somewhere beyond the ridge, two emissaries were refusing to win. [768] The hum paused, and in that long silence Maren smiled, empty-handed, exiled, and entirely at peace, having finally played his best move from very far away. [794]
