The distress beacon had been pulsing for forty minutes, and Iris no longer believed anyone was coming. [17] She had sent the signal herself, fingers steady on the transmitter even as her dive line snagged somewhere in the dark above her. [40] Now she hung in the nave of a forgotten underwater temple, sixty meters down, watching her air gauge tick toward nothing. [61] The hush here was not empty. [67] It was gathered, deliberate, as though centuries of drowned prayers had pooled beneath the vaulted coral and waited for a listener. [88] Her lamp swept across columns furred with anemones, across an altar where something small caught the light and held it. [108] A crystal perfume vial, no longer than her thumb, sealed with silver and impossibly intact. [123] This was why she had come. [129] The university had laughed at her thesis: that the temple priests of Veyra had learned to harness the ephemeral, distilling scent and memory together into glass, so that the dead could be visited like rooms. [164] Iris had spent six years and her marriage chasing that idea, and the vial on the altar was either vindication or the prettiest way to die she could imagine. [193] She finned toward it, and the snagged line jerked her short like a leashed animal. [208] Panic rose, hot and animal, telling her to thrash, to haul, to fight the rope into surrender. [225] Every nerve she owned screamed pull. [231] Instead she went still. [235] Her instructor's voice surfaced from years ago: a snarl tightens when you fight it; override the instinct, drift back, give it slack. [257] So she breathed slowly, wasting precious air on calm, and let herself float backward into the temple's dark. [275] With gloved fingers she began to unsnarl the line from a broken finial, working each loop loose the way her mother had once worked knots from her hair, patient, humming, gone now eleven years. [309] The last loop slipped free. [314] She drifted down to the altar through water so clear it felt like falling through glass. [330] Up close, the vial was warm. [336] That should have been impossible at this depth, and yet warmth bled through her glove like a held hand. [355] The moment her fingers closed around it, the vial touched her memories. [367] Not metaphorically. [369] Her mother's kitchen unfolded around her in scent: bread, lavender soap, rain on hot pavement, the cigarette her father smoked only once, the day he left. [395] The temple was reading her, and answering. [402] Then came memories that were not hers: a priestess in white kneeling at this altar as the sea came through the doors, choosing not to flee, choosing instead to seal one last breath of the living world, jasmine and woodsmoke and a child's wet hair, into crystal. [449] She had sent her own distress signal, Iris realized. [458] Not a radio cry but this, a message in glass, pulsing across three thousand years: someone was here; someone loved; remember us. [480] Iris's gauge chimed its final warning. [486] Eight minutes of air, and a slow ascent needed twelve. [496] Time was running short in the simplest, cruelest arithmetic she had ever faced. [509] Her instinct, again, screamed at her: bolt for the surface, lungs be damned. [522] Again she overrode it. [526] A panicked ascent would burst her from the inside; a measured one might leave her breathless but alive in the last meters, where her own emergency signal could still summon the rescue boat she no longer trusted to come. [565] She tucked the vial against her heart and rose, slow as falling snow reversed. [579] Beneath her, the temple receded into its lonesome splendor, columns dissolving into blue, the gathered hush closing over the altar like a book gently shut. [604] It had waited millennia to be heard; it could grieve being empty a little longer. [619] At ten meters her tank gave its last thin sip. [629] Her vision tunneled. [632] And then, absurdly, mercifully, the vial warmed against her chest, and a memory that was not hers carried her: the priestess's final breath, full and unafraid, expanding through Iris's body like borrowed courage. [665] She broke the surface into ordinary sunlight. [672] A trawler, drawn by her beacon after all, was already turning toward her. [685] Later, in the noisy brightness of the deck, Iris held the crystal vial up to the sun and did not open it. [707] Some things you harness only by refusing to spend them. [717] She would carry the priestess's signal to every doubting colleague, proof that the ephemeral could be caught, kept, and answered. [737] And some nights, when the world went quiet enough, she pressed the warm glass to her ear and listened to a hush three thousand years deep, no longer lonesome, finally heard. [768]
