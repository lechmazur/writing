The pumps failed at dusk, and for the first time in forty years the library went silent. [17] Sela Marn, page indexer for the Drowned Archive of Veldrass, set down her pen and listened to the blackout. [36] Above her, the sunken city pressed its dark weight against the dome, and seawater light came down in shifting, dappled bands. [57] She had always worked by logic: a page existed, it belonged somewhere, and her index said where. [74] But the index had a wound in it, and tonight the silence let her feel its edges. [91]

For months she had cataloged the founding charters of Veldrass, the documents the surface council cited to claim the harbor rights of every fishing family above. [117] Each charter bore the seal of the First Scriptorium, pressed in oxblood ink, and each seal told her something was wrong. [138] The official explanation said the charters were original, drafted when the city still stood above the waves. [155] Sela, tranquilly perceptive in the way of people who read margins more than headlines, had begun building an alternative hypothesis. [175] If the seals were genuine, their ink should have aged with the paper; instead, the oxblood sat crisp on fibers already softened by a century of damp. [202] The charters had been stamped later, she suspected, by someone who wanted ownership to look ancient. [218]

While the pumps ran, she had never been able to test the idea; the Archive's overseers monitored every reading lamp and logged every drawer she opened. [244] The blackout had cut the monitors along with the lights. [254] She lit a phosphor lantern and walked into the forgotten wing, the shelves no overseer had bothered to index because nothing there served the council's story. [280] Dust hung in the lantern light like sediment. [288] Without the engines' hum, the room held a stillness that felt reclaimed rather than empty, as if the library had been waiting decades to think clearly again. [315] Sela felt it settle into her too. [322]

In a cabinet of scriveners' tools she found what the records claimed had been lost: the antique ink stamp of the First Scriptorium itself, its brass handle green with age, its face worn smooth at one corner. [359] That worn corner was the proof she needed. [367] A stamp wears down through use; every genuine impression it made after the wear would show the same blunted edge. [387] She carried it back to her desk, inked it, and pressed it onto scrap paper beside a council charter. [406] The charter's seal was crisp at every corner. [414] It had not been made by this stamp, or by this stamp's era at all. [429] Someone had cut a copy, sharp and new, and printed history onto old paper. [443]

Sela sat back, and the lantern's light wavered across her hands in pale, dappled patches, like sun through shallow water. [463] She thought of the fishing families above, paying rent on harbors their grandmothers had owned outright. [479] Her motive had never been rebellion; it was simpler and harder than that. [492] She wanted to trace honest origins, to make the index say what was true rather than what was convenient. [511] An index that lies is not an index; it is a wall with the word door painted on it. [530]

Working by lantern light, she began to readjust the catalog. [540] Each forged charter she reclassified from 'founding document' to 'later impression, provenance disputed,' with the evidence noted in her small, exact hand. [562] She cross-referenced the worn stamp, sketched its blunted corner, and filed the true Scriptorium impressions beside the false ones so that any future reader could see the difference at a glance. [593] Logic, she knew, could be erased by power, but only if it lived in one place. [609] So she made three copies of her findings and shelved them under unrelated headings, scattered through the wing no one watched. [630]

Near dawn, the pumps shuddered and resumed, and the dome filled again with its old mechanical drone. [647] The monitors blinked awake; the overseers would return with their logs and their lamps. [661] Sela returned the antique stamp to its cabinet, exactly as she had found it, because evidence is safest when it looks unimportant. [683] She kept the stillness, though. [688] The library had reclaimed it first, but she carried her share away, a quiet interior room where no overseer could log her thoughts. [711] When the council next requested a charter, her index would answer honestly, and honesty, once shelved in three places, is difficult to drown. [734] Outside the dome, the morning current stirred, and the light came down through the water in slow, dappled bands, patient as resolve. [756]
