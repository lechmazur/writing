For thirty years, Mott drove the coach down Gloom Hill, carrying soldiers to the harbor and coffins back up. [19] The war ended the way wars end here: not with treaties, but with poisoned water and a reef gone white as bone. [41] After the reef died, the fish left, then the fishermen, then nearly everyone else. [55] Mott stayed, because the dead do not relocate, and someone must keep their addresses. [69]

He had learned dead languages the way other coachmen learned dice: long nights, captive company. [84] His passengers had been scholars, priests, prisoners; their grammars had rubbed off on him like road dust. [101] So when divers found the drowned stone in the harbor, the magistrates sent for him. [116] It was a treaty pillar, older than the town, carved in a tongue no living mouth had spoken in four centuries. [137] Mott read it by lantern light from a rowboat, water lapping at the words. [151] It recorded a concord between two ancient peoples who had fought on this same shore and then, exhausted, made a vow. [172] Where blood has salted the ground, let green things be planted, until the field forgets it was a field of war. [193]

The magistrates wanted the translation for a museum plaque. [202] Mott wanted to keep the promise. [208]

The battlefield still lay above the harbor, a scorched terrace where the last fighting had churned the earth and nothing had grown since. [231] Mott owned one thing of value: a collection of dried seed pods, gathered over thirty years of routes. [249] Passengers had paid him in seeds when coin ran short: mountain lupine, marsh iris, a vine whose name he knew only in a language no one else remembered. [277] Each pod rattled like a small dry promise. [285] But the ground was ruined, and Mott was no farmer. [295]

The birds taught him. [299] After the reef bleached, the seabirds had changed their speech, and Mott, a man tuned to dying tongues, noticed. [318] Terns cried one note over barren water and another, rounder, where life persisted. [331] He spent a season on the jetty learning their grammar, until he could follow their calls like signposts. [349] The gulls' richest chorus rose over the coves where dead reef matter washed ashore in dark, crumbling drifts. [367] Crushed coral, fish bone, weed rot: the sea's dead were rich in everything a wound of earth might need. [386]

So the coachman became a carter of strange freight. [395] Each dawn he drove his empty coach down Gloom Hill and loaded it where the birds told him to dig. [415] Each dusk he hauled the reef's remains up to the battlefield and worked them into the scorched soil, fertilizing graves with the sea's own losses. [440] One death feeding another into life seemed to him the whole logic of the drowned treaty. [456]

The magistrates called it grave-meddling until the first lupine broke through. [467] Then they called it a memorial and left him alone. [477] He planted his pods in widening rings: iris where the trenches had been, the nameless vine over the powder scars. [497] He spoke to the seedlings in the treaty's language, because a vow should be tended in the tongue it was made in. [519]

Spilled seed rode his cartwheels down the hill, and by the second spring the jetty itself had bloomed. [537] Iris stood in the cracked planking; the vine wrapped the mooring posts and hung its flowers over the water. [556] At twilight Mott would stand at its end, a narrow plank road running out into vastness, flowers behind him, gray water before him. [579] He had spent his life on narrow roads through enormous dark, and this felt the same: one thin line of intention laid across something too large to argue with. [608]

On the last evening of that spring, the terns changed their call again. [621] It was the round note, the living note, cried over the harbor shallows where the dead reef lay. [639] Mott knelt on the blooming jetty and looked down. [648] Petals from the vine had been falling into the water for weeks, and beneath them, on the white skeleton of the reef, something had taken hold: a faint green fur, small anemones opening like buds. [683] The garden he had fed with the sea's dead was now feeding the sea in turn. [699] The old concord had been submerged, not broken; it had only been waiting for someone to read it aloud and act. [720]

Mott said the treaty's last line to the water, in the dead language, which did not feel dead anymore. [739] Then he climbed onto his coach and drove up Gloom Hill in the dark, slowly, the way a man drives when he is no longer carrying coffins, only seeds. [768]
