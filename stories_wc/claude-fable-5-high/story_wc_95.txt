<story>
The glass atrium held its breath the way only glass can, with light. [13] Mirel Audaz, dueling philosopher of the Synod, waited beside the quiet brook that ran along the atrium's glass wall, her mechanical arm ticking through its morning calibrations. [40] She had come to fence Doran Vell at noon, and noon was being slow about arriving. [56] In her guild, arguments were not published; they were fought, blade to blade, until a question became a destination. [75] That was the whole creed she had built her life on: a question left open is a road left unwalked. [95] Today's question was succession: who would speak for the Synod when old Master Hessen died, Mirel's faction or Doran's. [114]

While waiting, she paced the atrium and noticed what she was perhaps meant to notice: the marble sundial at its center stood half-painted. [137] Half the hour lines gleamed gold; the rest were bare chalk sketches, drawn at angles no honest sun would cast. [157] Wrong angles trouble a philosopher the way wrong notes trouble a composer. [169] She knelt. [171] The chalk lines were precise in their wrongness, which meant they were not wrong at all; they were code. [190]

From her coat she drew the Danish tin sewing needle card, a flat little relic her mother had carried out of Copenhagen, its paper sleeve still bristling with slim steel. [220] Her mother had mended sails; Mirel mended arguments; the needles served both trades. [233] She set needles upright along each chalk line, tiny gnomons casting tiny shadows, and her mechanical arm, steadier than flesh, measured the angle each one made against true noon. [262] Seventeen degrees, then nine, then twenty-four: she translated angles into letters by the old surveyor's cipher every Synod student learned and most forgot. [285] The message assembled itself like a melody: I do not want this duel; propose terms; ask me why. [303] It was a hidden overture, an opening movement disguised as decoration, left where only a mind like hers would look. [323] Doran could not retract the challenge publicly; his faction would devour him for cowardice. [337] So he had painted his plea into the architecture and trusted her curiosity to find it. [353]

Noon arrived, and so did Doran, with both factions filing in behind him like opposing weather systems. [370] The atrium filled with robes, wagers, and the particular dignified chaos of scholars pretending not to crave blood. [388] Mirel saluted, and they fenced. [393] But she fenced the way diplomats negotiate: diplomatically fierce, every attack a question, every parry a concession with conditions. [412] Between exchanges, too quiet for the crowd, she said, "Seventeen, nine, twenty-four." [424] Doran's guard faltered for half a heartbeat, which from a duelist is a full confession. [439] "Hessen is not dying," he murmured under the next bind; "he was poisoned, and whoever wins today inherits the poisoner's gratitude." [460]

The duel, Mirel understood, was the trap: a victor would be crowned, blamed, and owned. [475] Her creed turned over in her chest like a key. [485] Here was a question, and her whole life insisted that questions deserved destinations, not winners. [500] So she did the unthinkable with perfect manners: she drove Doran back to the sundial, hooked his blade with her mechanical wrist, and locked both weapons against the marble where everyone could see the chalk lines. [536] "A draw," she announced, "pending an inquiry." [543] "Master Hessen has been poisoned, and this duel was arranged to launder the succession." [557]

The dignified chaos lost its dignity at once. [565] Robes surged, wagers were disputed, and someone fainted with great ceremony against the glass wall. [580] Mirel raised her needle card like a tiny shield of evidence and explained the cipher, angle by angle, while Doran confessed that he had bribed the painter to leave the dial unfinished and to draw his message in chalk. [619] He had found hellebore in the steward's accounts, billed as "garden remedy," three days after Hessen first sickened. [637] The steward, present and suddenly the color of chalk himself, ran for the brook door and was tackled by three logicians who had never agreed on anything before. [665] It was, several witnesses later wrote, the most productive duel in Synod history. [678]

That evening, Hessen, fed an antidote and indignation in equal doses, sat up and demanded the minutes. [695] Mirel and Doran returned to the atrium alone, where the brook outside had recovered its quiet and the light was folding itself away. [718] "You could have simply written me a letter," she said. [728] "Letters are read by stewards," he said; "sundials are read by philosophers." [740] She laughed, took a needle from the tin card, and scratched a final angle into the unpainted half of the dial. [761] Decoded, it read: question answered; destination reached. [768] They left the sundial half-painted, on principle. [775] Some instruments are more honest unfinished. [781]
