After the town went silent, Mirei kept her shop open for nine days, gilding the edges of paper no one would buy. [22] She was the last gilded-leaf stationer on the coast, and habit was the only neighbor she had left. [40] On the tenth morning she packed her battered notepad, a packet of gold leaf, and a burnisher, and climbed the mountain. [61] The notepad had belonged to her grandfather, who claimed every page could hold a century if you pressed gently enough. [81] She had filled half of it with rubbings: the grain of the shop counter, the rust of the harbor chain, the weave of her mother's last apron. [108] She collected the textures of different times because texture, unlike memory, did not argue with you. [124]

The mountaintop held the keeper's garden: a blooming labyrinth of bonsai forests, hundreds of miniature groves arranged in hedged corridors that turned back on themselves. [149] Azalea and quince flowered between the trays, so the paths burned pink and white even in the gray hour before sunrise. [170] The keeper was gone, like everyone, but his shed still had power, and a single fluorescent lamp hummed over his workbench. [191] Mirei had been an earnestly studious child in this shed, copying the keeper's labels while he watered. [208] Each bonsai bore a card: This pine survived the burning of a city that never existed; this maple shaded a treaty no one signed. [232] He invented histories for every tree, and she had once asked him why he lied. [247] "A tree that small needs a past that large," he said, "or no one will believe it is old." [266]

Now she stood in his doorway and listened to the lamp. [277] Its hum was not steady. [282] It wavered, dipped, returned, like a voice rehearsing a sentence it could not finish. [296] On the bench lay the keeper's last notes: a grid of times and pitches, dawn after dawn, the hum charted like a tide table. [320] A final line, underlined twice: When the hum falls silent, the valley follows. [333] The date beside it was the morning the town stopped answering. [344]

She did not weep. [348] She sharpened a pencil instead, sat where he had sat, and took up his charting by the faint hum at dawn, marking each tremor in her battered notepad beside the rubbings of everything she had loved. [384] For six mornings the hum sagged lower. [391] On the seventh, she understood what the keeper had been doing with his lies. [405] The cards were not decoration; they were ballast. [413] Wherever a tree carried a rich invented past, the dew on its needles caught the first light and refracted it, scattering small spectra across the gravel; wherever a card had faded blank, the dew lay dull. [449] The labyrinth was a lens, and story was what it focused. [460] The garden held the valley in place by believing extravagantly in itself, and the hum was the sound of that belief running thin. [483]

So she went to work, because work was the shape her grief knew best. [497] She cut fresh cards from her finest stock and gilded each edge so it would catch the dawn. [515] Then she opened the notepad and gave the trees her town. [526] To a crooked juniper she gave the harbor chain's rust and wrote: Here a fleet once anchored against a winter that lasted four years. [550] To a flowering quince she gave her mother's apron weave: Under this tree a woman fed an army of strangers and was made a saint. [575] The histories were inventions, but the textures beneath them were true, and she suspected the garden could tell the difference between a lie told carelessly and a lie told with everything you had. [608]

Each dawn she charted the hum before lifting a brush. [618] Each dawn it steadied a little, like a pulse returning under a thumb. [631] She did not let herself hope loudly; she let herself hope in pencil. [644] On the nineteenth morning the hum held one clean note from first light to full sun. [660] She walked the blooming corridors and watched dew refract on every needle, the whole labyrinth flickering with small, stubborn rainbows. [680] Far below, in the silent town, a single chimney let out smoke. [692] Then a second. [695]

Mirei pressed her palm flat against the notepad's last filled page and felt the texture of this morning fix itself there: gravel, gold leaf, the warmth of a lamp that no longer needed watching. [729] She did not write what had happened. [736] She wrote what would be believed: On this mountain, a stationer once mended a valley with paper, patience, and the right kind of lie. [760] Then she gilded the edge of the page, so the dawn would have something to hold. [776]
