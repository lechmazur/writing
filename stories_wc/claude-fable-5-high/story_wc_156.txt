Mira Castellan restored murals the way other people solved puzzles: methodically, but with a playfulness that scandalized her colleagues. [19] She would match a pigment by mixing thirty samples in a strict grid, then choose the winner by flicking a coin. [40] The coin mattered. [43] It was a hobo nickel, carved during the Depression, the buffalo reworked into a sleeping man whose hat brim hid his eyes. [65] Her grandfather had carried it; now she did, certain it was only superstition. [78] Certainty was Mira's real trade. [83] She arrived at jobs knowing the answers: this blue is lapis, this crack is water damage, this saint's face was repainted in 1911. [106]

Then the city hired her to restore the mural at the heart of the Resonance Garden, a derelict sound wave garden of parabolic dishes, whispering walls, and curved benches that bent voices into strange returning shapes. [142] The mural covered the garden's central acoustic wall and depicted, impossibly, the garden itself. [156] Painted visitors leaned toward painted dishes, listening. [163] In the mural's corner stood a small painted woman holding a small painted coin. [177] Mira told herself it was coincidence and began her grid of solvent tests. [190]

On the third morning she cleaned grime from the painted woman's face and found her own face underneath. [208] She stepped back into the focal point of the largest dish and asked the empty garden, aloud, "Who painted you?" [228] The question left her mouth, struck the curved wall, and began its journey back. [242] In the pause between question and reply, the garden bewitched her. [253] The reflected sound arrived not as her own voice but as her grandfather's, low and amused: "Wrong question, Mira." [272]

She woke in her studio apartment, heart hammering, nickel clenched in her fist. [285] A dream, obviously. [288] She made coffee, walked to the garden, and found the mural exactly as the dream had left it, her face waiting in the corner. [312] Beneath the woman's painted hand, new letters had surfaced: ASK AGAIN. [323]

Mira did not believe in haunted walls. [330] But she believed in evidence, so she stood at the focal point and asked, "What is the right question?" [349] Again the pause; again the sound returned changed. [357] This time it was her own voice, ten years younger, before the certainty had set like plaster: "What if you don't know?" [379]

She woke again. [382] This time she was eleven, on her grandfather's porch, watching him carve the buffalo into the sleeping man. [400] "He isn't asleep," her grandfather said, tapping the nickel. [409] "He's waking up inside a dream, inside another dream." [418] "Every layer he sheds, he knows a little less and wonders a little more." [432] "That's the trade, Mira: certainty for curiosity." [439] "Most people refuse it." [443]

She woke a third time, kneeling before the mural with a swab in her hand, and understood that she could not tell which waking was the deepest. [470] Each awakening was nested in another, the way the mural held the garden and the garden held the mural. [489] The old Mira would have panicked, would have demanded a diagnosis, a solid floor. [503] Instead she felt something loosen in her chest, like varnish lifting from a color brighter than anyone remembered. [521]

She worked through the afternoon in her old way, methodical and playful at once: grids of careful cleaning, then a coin flip to choose where wonder went next. [549] Beneath decades of soot, the mural revealed its secret: the painted visitors were not listening to the dishes. [567] They were asking questions, mouths open, frozen forever in the moment before the reply. [581] The garden's builders had understood: the pause was the whole point. [592] Answers ended things; the waiting kept you awake. [600]

At dusk she stood at the focal point one last time, holding the nickel up so the low sun caught the sleeping man's hat. [624] "Am I awake now?" she asked. [630] The question crossed the garden, touched the curved wall where her painted self stood, and started back. [647] In the pause, Mira smiled and pressed the hobo nickel into the wet plaster of her final repair, exactly where the painted hand could hold it. [673] Let the next restorer find it. [679] Let them be certain it was only a coin, right up until the garden asked them otherwise. [696]

The reply arrived, soft and folded by the curved stone, in a voice that might have been hers, or her grandfather's, or the wall's: "Does it matter?" [723] She laughed, and did not check. [729] For the first time in years, she walked home without knowing anything for sure, and the not-knowing felt like waking up. [750]
