Emrys Vale had buried four hundred people, and not one of them had stayed quiet in his mind. [18] An undertaker learns to chase stillness the way other men chase fortune, and he was weary of always arriving after it had fled. [41] The mysterious package arrived on a Tuesday, wrapped in salt-stiffened canvas with no sender and no stamp. [58] Inside lay a broken lighthouse lens, a great glass eye cracked clean through its center. [73] That night the crack caught his lamplight and threw it against the wall in pulses: three short, three long, three short. [94] A distress signal, flashed by glass that should have held no light at all. [108]

Emrys told himself he would ignore it. [115] He told himself this each evening for a week, and each evening he found his coat already on and his boots already laced, his hands inadvertently stubborn in ways his intentions never sanctioned. [148] On the eighth night he carried the lens to the harbor, and the cracked glass turned in his arms like a compass needle, pointing down. [173]

Everyone in Greywater knew the old story: a city had drowned out past the shoals two centuries ago, swallowed whole between one tide and the next. [199] No one remembered its name anymore, which was, Emrys thought, the cruelest kind of death. [214] He rowed out, the lens in the bow flashing its plea at the black water, and the black water answered with light of its own. [239]

When he slipped over the side, lens hugged to his chest, the sea did not drown him. [256] It parted around the glass like a curtain, admitting him into a sunken street where lampposts still burned with cold green flame. [278] At the street's end stood the library. [285] Its doors opened into a hall that could not exist. [295] Shelves rose through ceilings into rooms stacked atop rooms, staircases folded back into themselves, and corridors occupied impossible spaces, wider inside than the building was outside, deep as the trench beneath the city. [328] Every shelf held not books but names, etched on slate spines that glowed faintly, each one pulsing the same rhythm the lens had flashed. [352] Three short, three long, three short. [358] Thousands of distress signals, two centuries old, calling to anyone who knew how to listen to the dead. [376] And who listened to the dead more faithfully than an undertaker? [387]

He understood then why the lens had come to him. [397] The drowned had never been buried. [403] Their city had no graves, no rites, no register of endings; the library was their only monument, and it was failing, its lights guttering one by one as the names were forgotten above. [436] Rest, he had always believed, needed a permanent address, a place where grief could deliver itself and be received. [455] These souls had been sending letters for two hundred years to an address that did not exist. [472] He would build it. [476]

He set the broken lens in the library's central well, where a reading lamp must once have hung, and began the only work he knew. [501] He read each name aloud, the way he had spoken over four hundred graves, and as each name was spoken its slate spine dimmed gently, peacefully, the frantic pulse smoothing into stillness. [533] The released light did not vanish; it drifted into the cracked lens and pooled there, and the crack began, impossibly, to seal. [555] Each rest he granted revitalized the glass; each mended inch of glass strengthened the library, its impossible rooms settling into quieter geometries, its folded staircases relaxing like unclenched hands. [584]

He meant to stop after a hundred names. [592] His mouth kept reading. [596] He meant to rest after a thousand. [603] His feet carried him up another folded staircase. [611] His stubbornness had never asked his permission; it simply outvoted him, and for once he was grateful to lose. [630]

When the final name dimmed, the lens blazed whole. [639] Its light rose through the water like a column, and the library rose with it in spirit if not in stone, its address fixed at last in the world's memory. [669] Sailors above would later swear a new lighthouse burned beneath the shoals, steady, never flashing, signaling nothing but safe harbor. [689]

Emrys surfaced at dawn and rowed home through water gone glassy and calm. [702] For the first time in forty years of chasing stillness, he found it had stopped running. [718] It was waiting for him on the shore, patient as a kept appointment. [731] He had spent his life giving the dead their rest; now, finally, the rest had a permanent address, and so did he. [753]
