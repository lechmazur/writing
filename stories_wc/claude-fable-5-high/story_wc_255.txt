In Vael, the phoenix rose every morning from the great kiln at the city's heart, and for one hour afterward, glass could hold a soul. [25] Sefa the glassblower worked only in that hour, shaping vials of bartered awakenings in her walled courtyard. [42] Break one of her vials and something dormant in you would awaken: a forgotten language, a numbed grief, a sense you were born without. [66] The price was always equal: to wake one thing, you had to let her put another thing in you to sleep. [87] Sefa herself had paid years ago, trading her voice for the skill; where her tongue had been, a concertina reed now sat, brass and trembling, so that she could only hum chords instead of words. [122] To bargain, she rearranged the potted plants that lined her courtyard, an old grammar of stems and blooms that careful customers learned to read. [146]

Ilo the painter arrived the morning after his third rejected exhibition, carrying a canvas under his arm like an apology. [166] He had been born colorblind, and the critics had finally said aloud what he feared: that his grays and ochres were a limitation, not a style. [192] He wanted red. [195]

Sefa studied him, then moved a pot of rue beside a pot of thyme: cost, and a steep one. [214] She tapped the rue twice. [219] To wake color in him, she would have to put his painter's hand to sleep for a year. [237] Ilo smiled the smile that had gotten him out of debts and into salons, innocently cunning, all warmth and angles underneath. [258] "Then take the blindness itself," he said. [265] "It is dormant color, isn't it, sleeping in me already?" [275] "You would not be putting anything to sleep; you would simply be waking what is owed to me." [293] Sefa's reed hummed a low, amused chord. [300] No one had ever argued the ledger backward before. [309] She moved a sprig of holly next to a white geranium: agreed, but with a warning. [325] He did not stay to read the warning. [333]

When the vial broke against his palm, red arrived like a shout. [345] Then green, then violet, then a thousand insistent others, each one louder than the last. [360] He ran home and painted for six days. [368] The canvases were garish, obvious, dead. [374] All his life, blindness had forced him to build pictures out of tone and weight and the tender gradations of gray, and now color shouted over everything he knew how to say. [406] His flaw, he understood too late, had been his entire craft. [417]

He returned to the courtyard the morning after the phoenix rose, while the kiln-light still ran gold along the walls. [437] "I want to barter it back," he said. [445] "Wake my gray sight again, and let the colors sleep." [455] Sefa's plants answered slowly. [459] She set a cracked pot of lavender at his feet, then a flawless one beside it, then turned the flawless pot to show the dull, unglazed seam at its back. [489] Nothing is whole; that was the warning he had not stayed to read. [502] Every awakening only trades one imperfection for another. [510]

Ilo knelt among the pots, and for the first time he truly looked at her arrangements: the deliberate asymmetry, the bruised leaves she never pruned, the beauty she made precisely out of what was broken. [545] She had been telling him this from the start, in a language he had been too hungry to read. [564] "Name your price," he said quietly. [570] She hummed, and the reed in her mouth wavered between two notes like a question. [585] Then she moved a fern beside a stalk of fennel: not payment, but a request. [600] Paint what I say. [604] Most customers guessed at her plants and guessed wrong; she had spent years being misread, bartering awakenings for everyone but herself. [625]

Ilo understood. [627] He took back his gray sight that morning, and the colors went down into sleep like coals banked for winter. [647] But he stayed. [650] Each dawn, after the phoenix rose and the courtyard filled with kiln-light, he painted small signboards translating her arrangements: rue and thyme, holly and geranium, every phrase she had ever shaped in soil. [683] He painted them in grays and ochres, in the patient tonal language only his flawed eyes had ever taught him, and customers began to read her fluently for the first time in twenty years. [717]

His fourth exhibition was of those signboards, hung exactly as her pots had stood. [731] The critics called the gray palette a revelation. [739] Ilo only smiled his cunning, innocent smile, because he knew what they did not: that nothing whole had ever been worth waking, and that the reed in a mute woman's mouth could hum, on certain mornings, something that sounded exactly like laughter.
