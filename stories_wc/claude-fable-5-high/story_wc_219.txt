Six weeks after first contact, the signal still played beneath everything, a hum the radios could not unhear. [18] Dr. Imka Sorel had been the first to call its effect a hoax, and the first to start glowing. [37] The light pooled faintly under her skin at night, a soft phosphor that her colleagues called grace and she called an uncharacterized biochemical response. [61] The university had suspended her for saying so on broadcast, for refusing the word miracle in a season that demanded it. [82] She had been rebellious in committee rooms all her career; she was not going to stop for an alien. [101]

What troubled her was not the glow but the walking. [111] Across the continent, people had begun leaving their houses with the calm of sleepwalkers, all of them converging on places they had never been and describing the journey, afterward, as going home. [143] An artificial pilgrimage, she wrote in her notebook, manufactured by a signal that had learned which molecules mean homecoming. [162] If she could isolate that chemistry, she could prove the longing was engineered, and perhaps forgive herself for feeling it. [182]

She carried one instrument she trusted more than her ruined lab: a thumb of fly amber, forty million years old, her grandmother's paperweight. [205] Inside it, a fly hung mid-flight, caught forever on its way to somewhere. [218] Amber preserved chemistry across deep time; if the signal spoke to anything ancient in the body, the amber was her control, her sealed and untouchable sample of the world before. [248]

The nearest convergence point was a rainwashed tower in the hills, a derelict observatory greening with spring. [265] She could not feel its pull the way the walkers did, or she told herself she could not, so she tracked them instead. [288] The old sculpture path behind the tower had gone wild, bronze figures drowning in ivy, but the long stone colonnade above it had kept the rain off, and a fine yellow pollen lay there like flour. [324] Dusty footprints crossed it in dozens, bare and shod together, all pointing one way. [338] She followed them past a verdigris shepherd, past a woman of bronze holding an absent child, and the quiet of the path was bronze too, weighted, patient, the silence of things that had stood still long enough to turn green. [378] Her own feet, she noticed near the end, had stopped following and started leading. [392]

The tower rose out of the wet hillside, dark stone streaked silver by a week of rain. [409] Walkers stood around its base in loose rings, perhaps two hundred of them, glowing faintly in the gray afternoon, saying nothing. [430] Nobody wept or sang. [434] They simply stood the way people stand in a kitchen they grew up in. [448]

Imka took her samples with shaking hands: swabs of skin, breath in vials, the air itself. [464] Every reading agreed with her hypothesis. [470] The signal triggered a cascade of oxytocin and old olfactory memory, a counterfeit of the smell of one's first home. [490] It was a forgery, exactly as she had said on broadcast. [501] Then she took out the amber to log her control, and stopped. [513] The amber was glowing. [517] Not reflecting her skin's light, but glowing from within, from the small dark body of the fly, the same soft phosphor that lived in her own hands. [544]

Nothing artificial could reach through forty million years of fossil resin. [555] Whatever the signal spoke to, the fly had carried it too, mid-flight, on its way to somewhere, before anything human existed to be homesick. [579] The pilgrimage was artificial, yes; the senders had built it the way a key is built. [595] But the lock was older than the species, older than the amber, a homing written into living chemistry from the start. [616] The senders had not invented the longing. [623] They had only proven, from however far away, that they shared it. [635]

Imka sat down among the silent walkers, on stone still cold from the rain. [649] Her skepticism did not leave her; it settled, the way bronze settles into patina, keeping its shape while changing its surface. [670] She would write the paper, and the committee would hate it, because it would say both true things at once: the pilgrimage was a fabrication, and the homecoming was real. [700] She held the amber up against the tower's wet dark stone and watched two lights answer each other. [718] Somewhere overhead, past the spring clouds, the senders were waiting to learn whether anyone down here understood the message. [737] Imka, luminous and doubting, glowing and unconvinced, wrote the only reply her chemistry allowed. [751] We know the way. [755] We have always known the way. [761]
