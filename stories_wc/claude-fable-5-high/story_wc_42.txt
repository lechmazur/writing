Imeko Aranha had repaired cameras for forty years in the lunar observatory that crowned Mount Verassa, a white dome floating above the clouds like a second moon. [27] The astronomers called her a sage, though she only called herself a listener. [40] Every shutter she rebuilt, she calibrated by ear, claiming she could time an exposure by the sound of a leaf falling in a still room. [65] Nobody laughed anymore; her cameras had caught lunar eclipses with a clarity that machines could not explain. [82] Her purpose was simple and changelessly constant: to harness the ephemeral, to hold what time refused to hold. [100]

Then came the season when foundations shake, when the mountain itself began to tremble. [114] Deep tremors rolled up Verassa's spine each night, cracking the dome's old concrete piers. [128] The directors announced they would restructure the entire observatory, dismantling the dome and rebuilding it on seismic floats before winter. [148] Everything would be boxed, surveyed, and possibly discarded, including the brass corridor clock whose second hand had begun, after the first tremor, to spin without stopping. [174]

The young engineers found the clock funny. [181] Imeko found it unbearable. [185] Her daughter Sela had wound that clock the morning she left the mountain, twelve years ago, to ride a launch out of the atmosphere. [209] Escape velocity, Sela had said, laughing, is just the speed at which leaving becomes permanent. [224] Her transport had reached it flawlessly, carried her to the far relay stations, and never once carried her back. [243] Messages came, thin and delayed, then thinner, then not at all. [254] The spinning hand felt like a verdict: time at the observatory had been knocked loose, racing to catch a girl already past all catching. [278]

On the night the demolition crews arrived, a final tremor shook the mountain hard enough to split the eastern pier. [298] While the staff evacuated to the lower terraces, Imeko climbed against the current, into the swaying corridor, and took the clock down from its hook. [323] In her workshop, by lamplight, with dust sifting from the ceiling, she opened its back the way she had opened a thousand camera bodies. [347] The escapement was gone, sheared by the quake; nothing braked the gears, so the hand simply fell forward forever, like an orbit with no world to circle. [374]

She understood it then, with a luminous ache that spread through her chest like dawn through cloud. [391] The clock was not mocking her. [397] It was Sela, exactly: a thing that had reached escape velocity, freed from the gravity meant to govern it, spinning beautifully and uselessly into its own distance. [424] You could not stop such a thing. [431] You could only capture it. [436]

Her hands knew what to do before her grief did. [446] She mounted her oldest field camera on the bench, the one with the shutter she had tuned for silence, and set the runaway clock before it. [472] Through the window behind them, the full moon hung above the cloud sea, steady as it had been every night of her life. [495] One long exposure, she decided: the constant and the fleeting in a single frame. [509] The mountain groaned again; she did not look up. [518] She closed her eyes and listened for the shutter's whisper, that breath softer than a leaf falling, and opened the lens. [539] For ninety seconds the spinning hand poured its motion onto the film, becoming a silver blur, a halo, a small captive galaxy beneath the unmoving moon. [565]

When the crews finally restructured the observatory that winter, lifting the dome onto its floats, they hung Imeko's photograph in the new entrance hall. [589] Visitors always asked about it: the moon sharp and eternal, and below it a clock face wearing a ring of light where its hand had refused to rest. [617] Imeko would tell them it was a portrait of her daughter. [628] Some things leave so fast that no gravity can keep them, she would say. [642] But a patient lens can hold their light, and that is not nothing. [655]

She kept the clock running, unrepaired, on her workshop shelf. [665] Every year, on the anniversary of the launch, she photographed it again beneath the same moon, the blur a little softer as the gears wore down. [691] And one spring, a letter arrived from the far stations, twelve years delayed by failed relays, in Sela's handwriting: I still set my watch by your shutter, Mother. [719] I am decelerating; I am coming home. [726] Imeko read it twice, then walked to the shelf, opened the clock's back, and set a new escapement gently into place. [747] The hand slowed, steadied, and began, at last, to keep ordinary time. [759]
