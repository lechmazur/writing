Mara Voss had been the celestial signal interpreter at Station Kestrel for three years before she understood that the gravitational anomaly outside her window was not breaking the transmissions but folding them. [32] Every signal that passed through the anomaly arrived bent, doubled, smeared across years. [45] Her job was to unbend them. [51] The realization came in her third spring, after planting the wrong seeds. [63] The station's hydroponic locker held her predecessor's packets, hand-labeled in faded ink, and she had sown what she believed was barley. [84] What rose instead was a bitter, blue-stemmed cress she could not name, useless for bread, sharp enough to make her eyes water. [106] Furious at the waste, she went digging through her predecessor's effects for an explanation and found, instead, a dried-up fountain pen with an exotic nib: twin-slit, iridescent, machined for an ink that no longer existed. [141] Beneath the pen lay a notebook, its last entry circled twice: The anomaly does not store events. [158] It stores the relationships between them. [164]

Mara had spent her career assuming history was a sequence: this happened, then this. [178] But the signals she unbent told a different story. [187] A distress call from a freighter lost forty years ago arrived braided with the reply that had never been sent. [207] A colony's last broadcast looped through its own founding speech, each sentence answering the other across decades. [224] The anomaly did not preserve what had happened. [232] It preserved how things touched. [237] And what is preserved through contingent relationships, her predecessor had written, can be edited through them. [253]

Mara had her own history she wanted edited. [261] Eleven years earlier, her sister had sent a final message from a medical transport, and Mara, buried in work, had queued it for later. [285] Later never came; the transport did not arrive anywhere. [294] She had taken this posting at the edge of charted space because grief is easier to carry where no one asks about it. [317] Now she wondered if the anomaly had been the reason all along, waiting in her future like a correction. [336]

The notebook explained the method in her predecessor's confidently uncertain hand, every claim hedged and yet underlined. [353] You could not change an event; events were knots, fixed. [363] But you could rewrite the threads between them, the contingencies, the because and the therefore, and the past would mean differently even though it happened identically. [389] The writing required ink with a particular crystalline structure, something to hold a sentence steady inside curved spacetime. [407] The recipe was simple: the pressed sap of a blue-stemmed cress, bitter enough to make your eyes water. [425] Mara laughed until she cried. [430] The wrong seeds had been the right seeds all along; the mislabeling was itself an edit, a thread laid down for her to follow. [454]

She crushed the cress, filled the pen, and watched the exotic nib drink the blue sap as if waking from a long sleep. [477] At the observation port, where the anomaly's field ran strongest, she set the nib to paper and began with her sister. [498] Her first instinct was to write the message as answered, herself as present, the transport as saved. [515] The ink refused; the nib scratched dry against the knot of the event. [528] So she wrote the threads instead. [534] She wrote that her sister's unanswered message had traveled outward, bent through the anomaly, and become the reason Mara learned to unbend anything at all. [559] She wrote that her silence had not been indifference but the seed of this listening. [574] The ink flowed. [577]

Outside the port, the anomaly shimmered, and in the archive the old signals reorganized: the distress call now arrived alongside every reply it had caused, decades late and somehow on time. [608] Nothing in the record changed; everything in the record meant differently. [619] She understood, then, what her predecessor had been doing, and what she would spend the rest of her posting doing: not fixing the past but threshing it, beating the husks of mere occurrence until the grain of meaning came free. [659] Grief did not vanish; it changed its grammar. [667] Her sister's death stopped being a period and became a comma, a clause that the rest of her life kept extending. [688] It was a strange comfort, and she distrusted it for exactly one evening before deciding that strange comfort was the only honest kind. [711]

On the last page of the notebook she added her own entry, confidently uncertain, hedged and underlined. [728] We cannot be sure any of this works, she wrote, but I am certain it is worth doing. [746] Then she gathered the season's cress seeds, mislabeled them carefully as barley, and returned them to the locker for whoever came next. [768] Some histories, she had learned, are best edited in advance. [778]
