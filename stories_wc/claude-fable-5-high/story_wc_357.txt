Sera Voss flew her orchard skiff the way other pilots flew nothing at all. [14] On Pomarion, the orchard planet, apple groves rooted themselves on floating rock arches, and the only roads were rivers. [33] Water spilled off the arches in long ribbons, and Sera navigated by water flow, reading the braids and droplets the way sailors once read stars. [58] This season, the groves were dying everywhere except one arch, the oldest, called the Gathering. [73] Sera wanted to find the source of its resilience before the blight took the rest. [88] She chose the worst possible hour to go: the inversion. [98] Once a day, Pomarion's gravity inverted for exactly one hour, and everything loose fell upward into the bright nothing. [117] But during inversion, the rivers reversed too, climbing the arches like silver vines, and only then did they flow toward the Gathering. [139] No map showed the way; the water itself was the map. [150]

When the warning bells fell quiet, gravity let go. [159] Sera's stomach turned over; the skiff lifted with the rising rivers. [170] The world entered its whirling silence: leaves spiraled upward without a sound, water spun in slow helixes, and even the wind seemed to hold its breath. [196] She followed the thickest braid of climbing water, banking under arch after arch. [209] Where the current split, she chose the branch that ran fastest, trusting that resilience would draw the most water to itself. [230] Forty minutes in, the braids converged on a single arch wider than a city, crowned with apple trees so old their bark had gone silver. [255] The Gathering. [257]

She moored the skiff to a root and climbed down, or up, depending on how you counted. [274] Here the silence was not empty. [280] The arch was hollow, and the inverted rivers poured into its caverns, and every drop struck stone and returned as sound. [301] Sera realized she was standing inside a gathering of echoes: centuries of falling water, of orchard songs, of harvest bells, layered and circling in the hollow rock. [328] The echoes did not fade here; the inversion stirred them daily, keeping them alive, a storm of sound wrapped in stillness. [349]

At the cavern's heart, embedded in the taproot of the eldest tree, she discovered something small and wrong: a broken arrow tip, green metal, older than any colony record. [378] The root had grown around it in rings of furious health. [389] Where the metal touched living wood, blossoms opened out of season. [400] She understood, slowly, the way you understand a song's ending before it arrives. [413] Someone, long before her people, had fired this arrow; its tip was a seed-tool, a fragment of technology that accelerates plant growth wherever it bleeds into soil. [440] The arrow had shattered against this arch, and the shard had been feeding the Gathering ever since. [457] The echoes told the rest. [462] Among the layered sounds she caught a fragment older than all the others: a hum, a chant, voices in no human language, repeating like ripples from the moment the arrow struck. [493] Whoever they were, they had seeded this world and gone, leaving their work to echo. [508]

Sera reached for the shard, then stopped. [515] Pull it free, and she could carry resilience to every dying grove; pull it free, and the Gathering might wither. [535] The hour was draining away; soon gravity would remember itself, and the rivers would crash back down. [552] She thought of her mother's grove, black-leaved and failing. [561] She thought of the voices in the echo, who had given and then left. [575] Giving was the point; hoarding was the blight. [583] So she did not take the shard. [590] Instead she cut three slivers of root, wood already saturated with the arrow's gift, enough to graft into the dying groves. [611] The tree would not miss them; the shard would keep feeding it for centuries more. [626]

The bells of the far settlements began to toll faintly through the cavern: ten minutes left. [642] She climbed back to the skiff and let the dying inversion carry her, riding the slowing rivers in reverse, racing the hour home. [665] When gravity returned, it came like an exhale; water fell in bright curtains, and sound rushed back into the world. [685] Sera set down in her mother's grove with the slivers wrapped in damp cloth. [699] She grafted the first into a blackened trunk before dusk. [709] By morning, green had broken through the dead bark like a held note finally sung. [724] She would return to the Gathering each inversion, take only slivers, and teach other pilots to read the climbing water. [744] The source of resilience, she decided, was not the shard alone. [755] It was the giving that echoed, hour after inverted hour, outlasting the hands that loosed the arrow. [772]
