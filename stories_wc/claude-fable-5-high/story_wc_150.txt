Yara had been gone nineteen years when the letter from the dig committee found her in Toronto. [17] It said the old orchard above the village was sinking, and that the roots had broken through into the catacombs beneath. [38] They needed hands, and they needed someone who remembered the stories. [49] Yara remembered almost nothing, which was precisely why she came. [59] Her grandmother had died mid-pilgrimage, on a road to a shrine no one could name anymore, and the family's faith had quietly died with her. [84] Yara's own leaving had felt the same: a journey begun in hope and never properly finished, only paused into permanence. [104]

The orchard unsettled the other workers. [110] Plum trees grew straight out of the hillside, but their roots dove down through the limestone vaults, snaking between the stacked bones of villagers eight centuries gone. [137] Where a root cradled a skull, the diggers crossed themselves and looked away. [150] Yara looked closer. [153] She had returned warmly curious rather than afraid, asking the foreman about every niche, every carved initial, every seed husk found in a dead man's hand. [179] The dead here had been buried with fruit, the foreman explained, so the orchard could carry them upward, a little each year, toward the light. [204] It was a slow resurrection, he said, and shrugged like a man apologizing for a beautiful idea. [221]

The official work was the tomb at the orchard's heart, a sealed chamber the roots had finally pried open. [240] Excavating it would take the whole season, and across those slow weeks Yara developed a habit the others called wandering. [260] It was not aimless. [264] Each evening she walked the tunnels with a lamp, following the roots the way one follows a sentence, certain they were spelling something. [287] She mapped where they thickened, where they turned, where they avoided certain alcoves as if out of respect. [305] Her wandering had a purpose she could not yet say aloud: she was looking for the shrine her grandmother had died walking toward. [328]

Three weeks into the dig, in a collapsed gallery the survey had dismissed, the roots converged like spokes around a broken octagonal stone. [351] Yara knelt and brushed it clean. [357] It was a sundial base, its gnomon long gone, its hour lines worn to whispers. [372] A sundial in a place without sun should have been absurd. [383] Then she saw the shaft above it, choked with roots and rubble, and understood: light had once fallen here, straight down at noon, into the country of the dead. [412] This was the shrine. [416] Pilgrims had come underground to watch the dial mark a single golden hour among the bones, proof that even buried things still kept time toward morning. [442] Her grandmother's interrupted journey ended in this room she never reached. [453] Yara felt something move in her chest that she had stopped expecting years ago, careful and bright, like cupping a flame in wind. [476] She did not call it faith yet. [483] She called it a hypothesis. [488]

While the official dig opened the tomb stone by stone, Yara began to rebuild the shrine. [504] She fitted the cracked base back together with mortar the foreman quietly donated. [517] She cleared the light shaft by hand, easing roots aside without cutting them, because they had guarded this place longer than any congregation. [540] She forged a new gnomon from an iron stake and set it at the angle the old socket demanded. [559] The diggers drifted in to help on their breaks, skeptics with trowels, drawn by something none of them named. [578]

On the day the tomb finally opened, it held no treasure, only a pilgrim's staff and a pouch of plum pits, the grave of someone else who had died mid-journey and been planted like seed. [613] The orchard above that chamber was the oldest and heaviest with fruit. [625] Yara laughed and wept at once, and could not have said where one ended. [639]

At noon the next day, the whole crew crowded into the gallery. [651] Light dropped through the cleared shaft like a verdict, and the new gnomon laid its shadow precisely along a worn line in the rebuilt stone. [676] No one spoke. [679] Around them the roots glowed faintly gold where the light grazed them, holding the bones of the village like a slow promise being kept. [703] Yara did not feel certainty, exactly. [709] She felt what her grandmother must have felt setting out: that some journeys are not interrupted, only handed on. [728]

She stayed past the season's end, tending the dial, learning the hours. [740] Pilgrims began to come again, a few at first, walking down among the bones to watch noon arrive underground. [759] Yara greeted each one the way the orchard greeted the dead: patiently, and with every intention of carrying them toward the light. [781]
