After luck became measurable, gardens stopped belonging to gardeners. [9] The Bureau of Fortune issued every citizen a score, and estates hired only hands above seventy, since the new science insisted that plants drank luck the way roots drank rain. [39] Old Sorrel, the withered vine dresser of the Halloway maze, measured at eleven. [52] He should have been dismissed the first season. [60] Instead, twelve years ago, he had quietly traded ledgers with his dead brother, whose score of eighty-one outlived him on paper. [81] It was a deception, and Sorrel never pretended otherwise to himself. [92] He was wisely pragmatic about sin: a lie that kept a garden alive weighed less than the truth that would kill it. [114] Still, a borrowed number is a debt, and he meant to repay it. [127]

His ledger of repayment was a collection of marbles, every one of them cracked. [141] Each marked a small victory, and Sorrel commemorated small victories elaborately, because he believed gardens kept accounts the way men kept grudges. [163] When a graft took against the odds, he held a procession of one down the maze's long axis, candle in hand, and buried a marble at the turning where the graft grew. [195] When the frost spared the muscat row, he sang the only hymn he knew, badly, and laid a marble beneath the trellis like a coin on a tongue. [223] The cracks were the point. [228] Whole marbles were for boys; broken ones were for men who had bargained with weather. [243]

Then the Bureau announced re-audits, and an examiner named Vell arrived at the Halloway gate with a brass luck-gauge and his brother's lie folded in her satchel. [270] Sorrel did not run. [274] He had one night, and he spent it mending the deception the only way a deception can be mended: by growing it true. [297] He dug up all thirty-one marbles and polished them until the cracks shone like lightning trapped in glass. [315] Then he reset them, one at every turning of the maze, a buried constellation of everything he and the garden had survived together. [338] If the Bureau wanted measurement, he would give them one the gauge could not argue with. [354] He would prove that gardens know their gardeners. [362]

In the morning, Vell read his ledger and frowned at the dead man's number wearing a living man's name. [381] Before she could speak, Sorrel confessed everything, plainly, the way a pragmatic man pulls a splinter. [397] Then he asked for one test of his own devising. [407] Walk the maze alone, he said, and then watch me walk it. [419]

Vell entered, gauge ticking. [423] The hedges did not attack her; they simply withdrew. [432] Leaves curled at her shoulder as she passed. [440] A clematis dropped its blossoms in a slow gray rain. [450] At each turning, the buried marble under her boots stayed cold, and the green around it dimmed, the whole maze withering politely, like a host who wants a guest gone. [480] Her gauge read eleven. [484]

Then Sorrel went in. [488] Vell followed at a distance and watched the maze remember him. [499] Where his crooked shadow fell, buds split their seams. [508] The muscat row reached for his sleeve like children at a fence. [520] At every turning where a polished marble slept, the hedge flushed green above it, blooming out of season, blooming out of spite, blooming the way old men laugh, with the wither already in it. [554] The gauge in Vell's hand spun past eighty and kept going. [565] It read ninety-three at the maze's heart, where the very first marble lay, cracked the day his brother died and the graft they planted together took anyway. [592]

Vell lowered the gauge. [596] Your number was never eleven, she said. [603] It was never eighty-one either, Sorrel answered. [610] It was never mine at all; it is the garden's, and the garden lends it to whoever stays. [628]

Vell wrote a finding the Bureau had no form for: fortune garden-bound, nontransferable, score void where the gardener is absent. [648] The forged ledger she burned in the potting shed stove, since a lie that has grown true is just a prophecy filed early. [671]

That evening, Sorrel held the most elaborate commemoration of his life. [682] He lit lanterns in the yew arches, sang the hymn twice, both times badly, and led a procession of two, because Vell stayed for it. [707] At the maze's heart he buried a new marble, freshly cracked against the gate stone, and polished until its flaw caught the lantern light. [731] A small victory, he admitted, just one old man kept in his place. [744] But small victories were the only kind the garden had ever asked of him, and it bloomed over the burial all night, even as the autumn took the leaves, blooming and withering in the same breath, the way every honest thing does. [786]
