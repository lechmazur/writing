The flood had silenced the opera house three weeks ago, but Mirela still came every night, lowering herself by rope ladder into the drowned pit where she had played second cello for eleven years. [34] Lanterns hung from the brass rail above her, flickering against black water that rose to her knees. [51] The pit had always been an in-between place, neither stage nor audience, and now it was between worlds too, half air and half river. [75] She came because of the program. [81] It had surfaced on the first night after the storm: an antique opera program from 1911, Dido and Aeneas, floating against her music stand as though delivered. [108] The water should have destroyed it. [114] Instead, the damage was making it. [120] Each night the soaked paper bloomed with new stains, and the stains were not random. [135] Tonight, under lantern light, the watermarks had sharpened into a picture: a hand, five fingers spread across what was unmistakably a fingerboard. [157] Mirela believed she knew whose hand. [163] Old Anselm Voss had occupied the first cello chair for forty years before dying mid-performance last spring, his bow lifted, the orchestra suspended amid the hush of unstruck chords. [192] He had never spoken much in life; he had conversed in glances, in the tilt of his scroll, in breath taken together before a downbeat. [217] Why should death change his language? [223] Lately, Mirela had been inspired in ways she could not explain. [234] Phrases arrived in her practice that were not hers, fingerings she had never used, an urgency in her wrists. [253] She had begun copying the watermarks into a notebook, convinced they formed a code, and convinced that Anselm wanted it unraveled before the city drained the building and gutted it for renovation. [285] The hand on the page showed a position: third finger, second string. [297] Beneath it, a tide-line of mildew curled into something like a fermata over a rest. [312] Hold the silence. [315] She had brought her cello down on a plank laid across two chairs, the instrument's endpin wrapped in rubber, absurd and necessary. [337] She played the note the watermark named: a D, held, then released into stillness. [351] The lanterns guttered. [354] Across the program, ink that had been dissolved for a century migrated, and a new image gathered: stairs, descending. [373] It was a conversation, she realized, wordless and patient, the only kind Anselm had ever held. [389] She answered each image with sound, and the water answered her with pictures. [402] A door, a low arch, a panel marked with the carved initials A.V. [415] The stairs she knew: they led from the pit down to the instrument lockers, fully submerged now. [432] She waded to the stairwell and went under, lantern light shattering on the surface above her. [448] In the green dark she found the panel, pried it loose, and pulled free an oilskin bundle before her lungs began to burn. [471] She surfaced gasping into lantern glow. [477] Inside the oilskin lay a manuscript: a complete cello concerto in Anselm's narrow hand, unsigned, unperformed, dated the year his daughter died. [499] He had hidden his grief in the building's bones rather than let it be heard. [514] Now the renovation would have buried it forever, and he had needed someone to disrupt that quiet ending. [532] Mirela set the manuscript on her stand, where the water could not reach. [545] She understood, then, why the message had come to her and not to the conductor or the directors above. [564] Pit musicians live their whole lives in between: below the singers, above the silence, unseen by the audience, holding up everything. [585] Only someone fluent in that middle territory could have read a letter written in stains and rests. [602] She tuned, soaked to the shoulders, and began the concerto's first movement. [614] The flooded hall took the sound and carried it strangely, the water turning the pit into a resonating chamber, every phrase doubled by its own reflection. [640] When she reached the long rest at the movement's heart, she lifted her bow and held the hush as the watermark had taught her. [664] In that suspended quiet, the lanterns steadied all at once. [674] On the antique program, the stains loosened, the hand unclenched, and the last image to form was simple: an empty chair, first stand, and an arrow pointing toward it. [703] His chair, offered. [706] Not a haunting, but a handing over. [713] Mirela finished the movement, and by the final note the program had gone blank, its century of ink given entirely to the telling. [736] When the workmen came at dawn to begin pumping out the hall, they found her asleep in the first chair, a dry manuscript in her lap, and they could not explain why the standing water, lantern-lit and still, seemed to hold its breath around her like an audience waiting for the downbeat.
