Mara Venn had been a lost-wax caster for thirty years before she learned that a bell could hold a name. [20] She learned it from Brother Ilo, keeper of the icebound sanctum at Vorne, where the aurora pooled green light across a ceiling of fused ice. [45] The monks there kept a ceremonial rope, centuries old, its braids knotted in patterns that recorded the names of the dead. [66] Each twist was a syllable; each crossing, a breath. [75] The rope was a transcribed memory, Ilo told her, but a memory no one living could read aloud anymore. [94] The last monk who knew the rope's grammar had died with the names locked in his hands. [111]

Mara had come to Vorne once before, twenty years ago, to cast a memorial bell for the sanctum. [129] She had hurried the pour, eager for the commission, and the bell cracked on its first winter night, taking with it the only sounding of three hundred names. [157] She had spent two decades casting other people's bells and refusing to hear her own failure ring in every one of them. [179] Only after forgiving yourself, her teacher had once said, can you cast true; bronze remembers a grudging hand. [197] It took her until this winter to believe him. [206]

She returned to Vorne carrying her crucibles, her wax, and one strange relic: a scrap of punched card torn from a street organ in the southern ports. [233] The organ grinder, an old sailor, had played a hymn he claimed came from Vorne itself, learned from a monk who fled the ice. [257] When his organ broke, Mara bought the ruined card for the price of a meal. [272] Holes punched in paper, knots tied in rope: she suspected they were two scripts for one language. [289]

In the sanctum, under the slow burning of the aurora, she laid the card scrap beside the great coiled rope. [309] Brother Ilo watched her trace the punched holes with one finger and the braids with another. [325] The hymn's rhythm matched the rope's oldest section, knot for note. [336] "It is a key," she whispered. [342] "The sailor's hymn is the first stanza, written twice." [351] But the rope had been damaged over the centuries: braids loosened by frost, recoiled by careless hands, whole passages scrambled. [371] To recover the names, she would have to reconfigure the braids, undoing and relaying each strand in the order the card dictated. [393] Ilo hesitated; the rope was sacred, never to be unmade. [403] "I will be technically gentle," Mara said, "the way a caster breaks a mold: with force, but never with harm to what is inside." [427] He nodded once, and she began. [433]

For eleven nights she worked beneath the aurora, her fingers raw, her breath freezing on the rope's fibers. [451] Where the card showed a hole, she crossed left over right; where it showed unbroken paper, right over left. [470] Slowly the scrambled passages resolved, syllable by syllable, into names: Aldra, Senne, Korviel, hundreds more. [485] Ilo read them as they emerged, voice shaking, the first time those names had been spoken in a hundred years. [505]

But spoken names melt like snow; Mara wanted them preserved in sound that outlives breath. [520] So she carved each name's rhythm into wax, sheathed the wax in clay, and melted it away, the lost shape leaving room for bronze. [544] This time she did not hurry the pour. [552] She let the metal cool for three full days, the way grief should be allowed to cool, without being rushed toward usefulness. [574]

On the solstice, they hung the new bell in the sanctum's ice vault and rang it with a striker wrapped in the rope's spare strand. [599] The tone climbed the frozen walls, and the vibration seemed to illuminate the ice from within, every trapped bubble flaring like a small lantern, the aurora overhead answering in green and violet. [631] Mara stood in the ringing light and felt twenty years of frost in her chest give way, not in a flood, but the way snowmelt comes: ardently, quietly, certain of spring. [662]

"The cracked bell," Ilo said gently, "was never the end of them." [674] "Only a pause." [677] Mara pressed the organ card scrap into his hands as a relic for the sanctum's archive, paper and rope and bronze now one continuous sentence. [702] Somewhere south, she thought, a sailor had cranked a battered organ for coins, never knowing he carried three hundred souls in his hymn. [725] Memory travels like that, she decided: punched into paper, knotted into rope, poured into bronze, passed hand to forgiving hand. [745] When she left Vorne in the spring thaw, the bell was still ringing the names at every dusk, and the ice above the sanctum glowed faintly each time, as if the dead themselves leaned close to listen. [782]
