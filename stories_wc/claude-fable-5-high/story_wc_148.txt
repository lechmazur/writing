They built the lighthouse prison on a jagged reef so that the sea itself would be the warden. [18] Its cells spiraled upward inside the tower like a screw, a labyrinth of one continuous corridor, and the men kept there were liars: forgers, false witnesses, prophets of invented dooms. [48] Aren arrived as the youngest of them, convicted of nothing but his blood. [61] His father was the boy who cried wolf, grown old and dead now, and the village had decided that lying ran in families the way drowning runs in fishermen. [90] When Aren warned the harbor that the ferry's hull was rotten, nobody listened, because his name arrived before his words did. [111]

The ferry broke apart on the reef three nights after they locked him in the tower, close enough that he heard it. [133] He heard the hush more than the wreck: the moment the screaming stopped and the water smoothed itself over like a liar's face. [156] He had been chasing that hush ever since, the way some men chase ghosts. [170] He knew what the morning tide would bring. [178] Bodies wash toward the reef; they always have. [186] And when the supply boat found drowned passengers tangled beneath a prison full of liars, the guards would need a story, and Aren's name was the easiest thread to pull. [216] The wolf-crier's son, signaling ships onto the rocks. [224] He had until dawn, before they found the bodies, to be gone. [236]

He owned one thing the guards had let him keep, mistaking it for piety: a square of carved lattice, pried from the village confessional by the old priest himself. [265] "Your father confessed to me every year," the priest had said, pressing the wood into Aren's hands. [282] "Through this screen, even a liar's truth holds its shape. [292] It sustains impossibilities. [295] A believed liar, a forgiven sin, a way out of a place with no doors." [310]

There was, in fact, one door. [316] At the labyrinth's lowest turn stood a narrow door left ajar for decades, rusted open a hand's width, leading only to a sheer drop and the sea. [343] Generations of prisoners had stood at it and pressed and scratched and given up, and their failures had scuffed the wood into a dense scripture of marks. [370] Aren went down that evening, when the setting sun poured through the gap and filled the corridor with copperlight, a stillness so complete it felt like repose rather than captivity. [400] He raised the confessional lattice and held it against the scuffed door, and the low sun did the rest. [419] Light came through the scuffs and through the lattice together, and the two patterns crossed, and on the corridor wall behind him the random scratches resolved into lines. [447] Not random at all. [451] The marks hovered on the stone, trembling with the light, a glowing map of the tower's spiral, and at intervals along it, words. [474] SAY IT TRUE, the first interval read. [481] ALL OF IT. [484]

Aren understood then what the prison truly punished. [492] Not lying, but silence: every man in the tower was a man whose true story had never once been heard. [512] The labyrinth fed on that silence; the hush he chased lived here, coiled in the stone, and the only thread that could cross it was a story told whole. [541]

So he walked the spiral upward in the failing copper light, lattice held before his mouth like a screen, and he confessed. [563] He told the corridor about his father, who had lied twice as a boy and told the truth for fifty years after and was believed exactly never. [590] He told it about the ferry, the rot he had seen with his own hands, the harbormaster's laugh. [608] He told it the part he had told no one: that when the screaming stopped that night, some shameful sliver of him had felt vindicated, and he had hated himself for it ever since. [642]

With each turn of the spiral, the hush ahead of him thinned. [654] Other prisoners drifted to their cell bars to listen, and their listening was a kind of absolution; no one laughed; no one called him his father's son. [681] When he reached the lamp room and the story ran out, the silence was different: emptied, restful, the hush of something finally heard. [704] Below him, faint through the spiral, came the groan of old hinges. [716] He descended to find the narrow door standing wide for the first time in decades, opening onto a spine of reef bared by the low tide, a wet road to the dark shore. [749] Before he stepped through, Aren dragged the lattice's corner across the door, one more scuff in the scripture, his line of the map for whoever came next. [776] By the time they found the bodies, he had crossed to shore, owning nothing but the lattice and a story finally believed. [798]
