The building on Marrow Street wore its history in carved letters no one could read anymore. [16] I moved in because of those letters, though I told the landlord I liked the light. [32] That was the first of the agreed upon lies. [41] He knew it, and he nodded anyway, and the lease was signed. [53] My neighbor in 4B was Mr. Halvard, a retired tutor of dead languages. [66] I learned this from the brass plate on his door, worn smooth where decades of knuckles had knocked. [84] I went to him with an empty cup and a lie about sugar. [97] He filled the cup without asking my name. [105] The next week I borrowed sugar again, and he set out two chairs as if he had been expecting me. [125] By the fourth week the sugar stayed in its bowl and the tea did the talking. [141] We never named what we were doing. [148] He pretended I came for company; I pretended I believed his pretending. [160] His apartment smelled of ink and old paper, and on his desk sat an antique seal of dark bronze, its handle carved like a column. [185] Its face bore the same looping character that crowned our building's doorway. [197] I saw it the second week and said nothing, and he watched me say nothing, and we both filed the silence away. [219] What I wanted, truly, was to learn the language of old buildings. [231] Not architecture, not history, but the actual words: the inscriptions scored into lintels and cornerstones across the old quarter, a script that appeared in no archive I could find. [260] My grandmother had hummed those shapes to me like lullabies before she died, and I had spent years trying to find anyone who could read them. [286] Halvard could, I was certain. [291] He taught me Latin verbs as cover, his hands patiently nimble as he wrote out conjugations, fingers folding paper, pouring tea, never spilling, never hurrying. [316] Each week he let slip one stray mark of the other script, like crumbs deliberately dropped. [332] I gathered them without comment. [337] That was our second agreed upon lie: that he was not teaching me, and I was not learning. [355] In August the radio promised a meteor shower, and Halvard invited me to the rooftop garden, the first invitation he had ever spoken aloud. [379] The garden was older than the tenants knew, beds of nightflowering vines trained along iron rails, and stones at the borders cut with that same script. [405] He carried the tea things; I carried the sugar, a joke neither of us made out loud. [422] The first meteor crossed and the vines opened, pale as paper. [433] I decided I was done being patient in the wrong direction. [444] So I interrogated him, gently, the way you press an old lock rather than force it. [460] Why does your desk seal match our doorway, Mr. Halvard? [470] Why do you teach me Latin we both know I do not want? [483] Why did you fill my cup before I finished my lie about sugar? [496] He smiled at the sky as if greeting an old colleague. [507] The script is a builders' tongue, he said, written by the guild that raised this quarter. [523] It was never meant to be spoken, only set in stone, a language of dedications, warnings, and promises a building makes to its people. [547] The seal had belonged to the guild's last scribe, his grandfather, and the law of the guild was simple: the tongue could not be sold or volunteered, only given to someone who came asking without asking. [583] Hence the sugar, he said. [588] Hence the tea, the Latin, the crumbs. [595] Agreed upon lies were the only honest way in. [604] And my grandmother, I asked, though I already felt the answer settling like familiar weather. [619] She came to my grandfather with an empty cup, he said. [630] She borrowed sugar for two years. [636] Then she vanished from the quarter before the giving was finished, and the tongue went silent with her. [654] I understood then why everything in his rooms had felt familiar before I could have known it: the smell of ink, the rhythm of his pouring, the shapes she had hummed over my crib. [688] She had carried her unfinished lessons into lullabies. [696] Halvard drew the antique seal from his coat and pressed it into the soft wax he had warmed against the teapot. [717] The mark it left was the doorway's character: it means tenant, he said, but also heir. [733] Above us the meteors kept falling, brief bright letters on a dark page. [746] Next week, he said, bring your cup. [753] We will need more sugar, I agreed, and for once neither of us was lying. [768]
