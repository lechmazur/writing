Three months after receiving the donor heart, I climbed the chain ladders to the floating rock tea house above the valley. [21] The mist below moved like slow water, and the tethered boulder swayed gently under my boots. [37] Strangers had begun telling me their secrets since the surgery, unprompted, on trains and in waiting rooms. [54] My donor, the nurses whispered, had been a dye master's apprentice who magnetized stories the same way. [71] I wanted to know why, so I came to the woman who had trained her. [86]

The dye master sat watchful at the tea house door, her eyes tracking me before her head turned. [104] Indigo stained her knuckles like old weather maps. [112] You carry Sefa's heart, she said, before I spoke a word. [123] It beats the way she walked, like someone always arriving. [133]

She poured smoke-dark tea and let me explain my question badly. [144] Why do strangers open to some people like doors blown by wind? [156] Why did Sefa magnetize stories, and why do they gather around me now? [169]

The dye master did not answer with words at first. [179] She brought out a small lacquered box and lifted from it a single blue morpho wing. [195] It flared electric blue, then dimmed to brown as she tilted it. [207] There is no blue pigment in this wing, she said. [217] Only structure, only ridges that catch light and return it transformed. [228] I have ground a thousand petals seeking this color and never found it in matter. [243]

She called it iridescent caution, the wing's way of warning and beckoning at once. [257] Predators flinch from the flash; mates fly toward it; the wing itself holds nothing but shape. [273] Sefa was like that, she said, and her voice went quietly explosive, grief packed tight beneath calm syllables. [291] People did not give her stories because she demanded them. [301] They gave because her attention had structure, ridges that caught their light and returned it blue. [317]

I asked how anyone could learn such a structure. [326] The dye master rose and led me to the tea house's western rail. [339] Below us, the mist had thinned, and bright seams of light crossed the valley floor. [354] I time every dye bath by tracking sun trails, she said. [365] When the light reaches the river bend, indigo sets; when it touches the cedar grove, madder blooms red. [383] The sun does not hurry, and neither does anyone worth telling things to. [396] Sefa learned her patience here, watching those trails creep across stone. [407]

She made me sit through the whole afternoon, naming each place the light touched. [421] At first I fidgeted, restless with my borrowed heartbeat. [429] Then something slowed in me, and I began to see what watching actually was. [443] Not waiting, but a kind of hospitality offered to whatever arrives. [454]

Near dusk a sun trail crawled up the boulder itself and slid through the tea house window. [471] It struck the morpho wing on the table and seemed to illuminate the whole room with shifting blue. [489] Light scattered across the dye master's face, and I saw she was crying without sound. [504] Sefa caught this wing the day before her accident, she said. [515] She wanted to learn its blue, and I told her some colors cannot be owned, only hosted. [532]

Then the dye master told me her own story, the first she had spoken aloud in a year. [550] How she had refused to take Sefa to market that morning, claiming the vats needed tending. [566] How the cart went without her and never came back up the mountain road. [580]

I understood then that the answer to my question was happening to me as she spoke. [596] I had sat still long enough, structured by patience, and her story had flown toward the flash. [613] The heart in my chest was not magnetic; it had simply taught my body Sefa's stillness. [629] Strangers told me things because something in my new rhythm gave them room. [642]

The dye master pressed the morpho wing into my palm and folded my fingers over it. [658] Carry her caution, she said, the shimmer that warns and welcomes. [669] Listen like the wing holds blue, by shape and not by hunger. [681]

I climbed down through the rising evening mist, the boulder's shadow long across the valley. [696] At the trailhead, a tea porter fell into step beside me and began, unasked, to tell me about his brother. [716] This time I knew what I was doing, and I let the light land. [730]
