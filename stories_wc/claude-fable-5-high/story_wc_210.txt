The wishing well ran dry on the night the last wish was granted, and by morning Serel was the only soul left in Lantren. [24] Everyone else had wished themselves elsewhere: into painless lives, into endless summers, into versions of themselves that never grieved. [43] Serel had refused her turn. [48] An alchemist learns early that anything granted without cost is a counterfeit, and she had spent forty years refusing counterfeits. [68] So the town emptied around her like a tide going out, and the well sealed itself with a sound like a held breath released. [92] What it left behind was an amber bottle with no opening, resting on the well's lip. [108] No cork, no seam, no neck; just smooth honeyed glass with something luminous turning slowly inside. [124]

Serel carried it down to the paper-lantern cove, where the fishermen had once hung lights to call their boats home. [144] The lanterns still burned, though no one tended them, as if the cove itself remembered being needed. [161] She sat among the rocks and studied the bottle for three days. [173] Every alchemical instinct told her to open it: to distill, decant, transmute. [185] But there was nothing to open. [191]

On the fourth night she understood the bottle was not a container but a question. [206] The light inside was everything the town had been: its arguments, its recipes, its funerals, its bad jokes, all the unwished remainder of a thousand departed lives. [233] The well had granted comfort, and comfort had hollowed everyone out; what they shed in leaving had condensed here, sealed in glass. [255] It could not be poured. [260] It could only be carried, the way a mind carries a thought it has not finished thinking. [277]

So Serel began to think the town back into being. [287] Not to restore it; restoration would be another counterfeit, another painless summer. [299] She wanted to conceive a civilization that would never need a wishing well, a town whose people chose their lives instead of escaping them. [323] The work could not be written down. [330] Ink fixed things; ink was a kind of wish. [339] The bottle answered only to a living mind, to whatever she could hold burning in her working memory all at once, unrecorded and unrelieved. [363]

She started small. [366] One street, held entire in her head: the baker's crooked door, the smell of tar, the woman who sang off-key at dusk. [388] When she held the whole street without dropping a single detail, the light in the bottle brightened, and a lantern flared on the far rocks. [413] In the morning, the baker's door stood at the cove's edge, crooked as ever, opening onto a street that had not existed at midnight. [437] But it was not the old street. [444] Where she had remembered the gutter that flooded every spring, she had also imagined a channel that carried the water to gardens. [466] Memory was the ore; imagination was the transmutation. [474] This, she realized, was the true alchemy she had chased her whole life, and it cost exactly what real things cost: effort, attention, grief. [498]

Grief, because to hold the town in mind was to hold its people, and to hold its people was to miss them. [520] Some nights the bottle offered her an easier way. [529] Its glow would soften into the shape of her daughter, gone twenty years to the well's first granting, and the light would whisper that she could simply live inside the memory instead. [561] Serel pressed her palm to the warm glass and said no. [572] She would not take the painless version. [579] She built her daughter a school instead, conceived in such exact detail that the chalk dust was real when it rose from the cove the next morning. [606]

Street by street, season by season, she held more than any mind should hold. [620] She rebuilt the laws so that no one could trade their sorrow away, and the markets so that no bargain hid its cost. [643] And the people came back. [648] Not summoned, not wished; they simply began appearing at the edges of what she had thought into being, drawn home by the gravity of a place that wanted nothing from them but their presence. [682] They came back changed, embarrassed by their endless summers, hungry for weather. [694] The baker found his crooked door and wept at the improvements. [705]

When the last of them returned, the light in the amber bottle had thinned to a single thread. [723] Serel carried it down to the cove one final evening, beneath lanterns now tended by living hands. [740] The bottle had never had an opening because it was never meant to be emptied; it was meant to be exhausted, spent thought by thought into the world. [768] When the last thread of light dissolved, the glass turned clear, and Serel set it on the rocks like a finished experiment. [790] Behind her, the town was singing off-key. [797]
