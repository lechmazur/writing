For thirty years I have kept the chronicle, and for thirty years it has kept me. [16] Every dawn in this mountain ranger cabin I write down what I did in my sleep: the cities I argued with, the rivers I taught to speak, the nights I flew. [47] Lucid dreaming was my one talent, and chronicling it became my one job after the forest service let me keep the cabin and forgot I existed. [73] Yesterday, on the north ridge, I found my grave. [82] A flat stone, my full name chiseled in a hand I recognized as my own, and a death year I could not read because lichen had eaten the numbers. [111] I should have been terrified. [116] Instead I felt the tired astonishment of a woman who has seen too many impossible things to flinch and too few to stop looking. [140]

The trouble is that I am chronologically confused, and I admit this the way other people admit to bad knees. [160] Decades of waking inside dreams have stripped the labels off my years. [172] I cannot always say whether I am sixty-one or thirty-four, whether the fire lookout burned last August or in some August still coming. [195] So a grave with an unreadable date is, for me, a genuine emergency. [208] If I cannot prove which year I am standing in, I cannot prove I have not already died. [226]

That is why I came down from the ridge and took the birch bark scroll from its tin. [244] I started it the winter the paper ran out, peeling bark from the deadfall behind the woodshed, and I have saved it ever since for one purpose: the final chronicle, the one that would distill all this chaos into a single narrative a stranger could follow. [290] Order, beginning to end, birth to grave. [297] But to write the ending I needed the date, and to read the date I needed to dream. [315] In dreams the lichen would peel back if I asked it nicely, and from the air the whole stone would lie open like a page. [340]

The problem: I have not flown in years. [348] Flight requires a wildness I misplaced somewhere around the chronicle's fortieth volume. [360] So last night I did the thing I had always forbidden myself: I read the early volumes before sleeping. [379] There she was, the woman I used to be, twenty-six and feral with appetite, riding thunderheads over a dreamed Montana, kissing strangers made of weather. [404] The nostalgia that rose in me was not the soft kind. [415] It had teeth. [418] It paced the cabin with me, knocking the kettle, wanting that old sky back the way an animal wants out of a trap. [441] Good, I thought. [444] Use it. [446] Jealousy of one's past selves is an ugly fuel, but it burns. [458]

I fell asleep hating her a little, that younger chronicler with her easy wings, and the hating followed me down into the dream like a lit match. [485] I woke inside it standing on the cabin porch, and the ridge was there, and she was there too, twenty-six, already airborne, showing off. [509] The envy went through me like a hot wire, and my heels left the boards. [524] I soared. [526] Not gracefully; I climbed the night the way you climb stairs in the dark, furious and certain, chasing my own youth across the ridgeline until the chase itself became the flying. [557]

Below me the grave waited, and from above, the lichen obeyed and withdrew. [570] The stone read: SHE WROTE IT ALL DOWN. [578] No year at all. [582] Only that, in my own chisel-hand. [588] I hovered there a long time, jaded enough to laugh, awed enough not to. [602] Of course there was no date. [608] I had carved that stone myself, in some dream I had failed to chronicle, a message lobbed forward to a self I knew would lose track of the years. [637] The grave was not a deadline. [643] It was an assignment. [647] My young self landed beside me on the air, the way only dream-people can, and she was not smug after all, only impatient. [670] Stop envying me, she said, you are the part of us that finishes things. [684]

This morning I unrolled the birch bark scroll across the cabin table and began. [698] Not chronologically; I have made my peace with that. [707] I am ordering my life the only honest way left to me: by weight. [721] The heavy nights first, the flying last, so the story rises as it goes. [735] Whoever finds this cabin will find a narrative, not a mess, and a stone on the north ridge that tells them the chronicler kept her word. [761] And if, some night, an old woman is seen soaring above the ranger cabin, jealous of nobody, write it down. [781] That is how chaos becomes a story. [788]
