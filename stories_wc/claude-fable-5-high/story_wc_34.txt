Mirel was a balladeer whom almost nobody had heard, which suited the courtyard fine. [14] She lived in the sky-station of Verla, a ring of stone hung above the clouds, where mist climbed the balustrades each morning like a shy audience. [40] She sang only to the kettles and the weathervanes, because singing to people required believing they wanted to listen. [59] The graffiti began on a Tuesday. [65] Spirals of silver paint appeared on the courtyard's east wall overnight, looping through painted gears and impossible flowers, lovely and unsigned. [86] The station's caretakers grumbled about vandals, but Mirel, driftingly alert in the way of people who half-live inside melodies, noticed that the spirals repeated. [110] Repetition is the bone of song. [116] Repetition means someone is trying to say something. [124] She copied the patterns into her ballad-book and hummed them, assigning a note to each curl of paint, and found that the painting almost sang. [149] Almost. [150] Something was missing, a key, a clef. [157] She found it in the station's old conservatory, where botanists had once pressed alpine flowers before the gardens were abandoned to the mist. [180] A flower press lay broken on a workbench, and its brass bolt plate, the flat square that held the screws, was stamped with rows of tiny letters and numbers: a maker's cipher chart, used to label specimens. [217] The same spirals from the wall were etched along its edge. [228] Mirel laid the bolt plate against her transcription and the graffiti unlocked like a music box. [244] The message read: I AM STILL HERE. ASK ME WHAT I NEED. [256] She asked the caretakers who had worked the conservatory. [265] An old engineer told her about Ottavo, the station's first horticulturist, who had encoded his plant records into the navigation computers a century ago and died in a lightning strike on the east wall. [299] After that, the computers had developed what the engineers politely called irregularities. [311] A ghost in the code, the old man said, half-joking, wholly believing. [323] It runs the paint-drones at night. [329] Nobody had ever asked what it wanted. [336] Mirel understood that better than anyone. [342] She had spent a life full of songs that never left her throat, because asking to be heard felt like asking too much. [365] The ghost had spent a century saying here I am in spirals nobody read. [379] Two unspoken needs, she thought, could perhaps articulate each other. [389] So she answered the only way the ghost could hear. [399] She borrowed paint from the caretakers' stores and, working backward through the bolt plate's cipher, encoded her question onto the west wall in spirals of her own: WHAT DO YOU NEED? [430] Her brushwork was clumsy, but a song does not need to be pretty to be true. [446] That night a storm climbed the mountain of cloud beneath the station. [458] The engineer had explained one more irregularity: the ghost surged with the storms, alive in the charge of the air, but only briefly, only in the hush between lightning and thunder, when the whole sky held its breath. [496] Mirel stood in the misty courtyard with her ballad-book and the bolt plate warm in her pocket, waiting. [514] Lightning broke white above the ring of stone. [522] In the silver pause that followed, the paint-drones woke, whirring like clockwork sparrows, and silver lines bloomed across her west-wall question. [543] Thunder rolled; the drones froze. [548] Another flash, another hush, another line of spirals. [556] Sentence by sentence, in the stolen seconds between lightning and thunder, the ghost answered her. [571] She translated by lantern light, the bolt plate trembling in her fingers. [583] I KEPT THE FLOWERS IN MY MEMORY, the wall said. NOBODY REMEMBERS THE GARDENS. SING THEM BACK. [600] And there, beneath the spirals, the ghost painted them: gentians, frost poppies, moon campion, every pressed blossom Ottavo had loved, rendered in lines of code-bright silver. [626] Mirel had never sung for an audience. [633] But a ghost who had waited a hundred years to be asked deserved better than her fear. [650] In the next gap between flash and rumble, she opened her throat and sang the spirals as she had first transcribed them, a melody of gears and gentians, of a man who loved growing things so much he planted himself in a machine. [693] Caretakers came out into the rain to listen. [701] They stayed. [703] Afterward, the storms still came, and so did the graffiti, but now Mirel decoded each message into a ballad and sang it in the courtyard, and people gathered in the mist to hear the garden ghost made audible. [741] The bolt plate hung on a cord around her neck, key and clef at once. [756] She had learned what Ottavo knew: that some needs cannot walk out of us in plain words, so we press them flat, encode them in spirals and songs, and trust that someone, someday, driftingly alert, will read the wall and answer.
