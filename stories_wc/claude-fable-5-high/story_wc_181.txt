The Curatorium clung to asteroid Veska-9 like a burr, and Imre Hale had curated its impossible orchard for eleven years without once understanding it. [24] The trees grew from no soil, blossoming in slow silver out of the vacuum itself, an astral orchard rooted in nothing but the asteroid's loneliness. [49] Each blossom eventually hardened into a pistachio shell, and inside each shell waited an inheritance. [64] Memories, mostly: a grandmother's recipe, a debt, a grudge, sealed by the dead for the living. [80] Families across the system paid to plant their legacies here, and Imre's job was simply complicated: catalog every shell, deliver each to its rightful heir, and never, ever open one out of order. [113] Order, the Curatorium's charter insisted, was everything, because the orchard did not grow forward in time. [129] Inheritance here was nonlinear: shells ripened from heirs not yet born, bequests arrived before their bequeathers, and a careless reading could tangle a family's whole century into knots. [157]

On a Tuesday that felt like every other Tuesday, Imre found a shell that broke the catalog. [174] It hung alone on the oldest tree, sanded flat on one side, polished into a cameo blank, the kind of oval onto which a jeweler carves a profile. [202] But the blank was empty: no face, no name, no heir listed in the ledger. [217] The orchard had never produced anything unfinished, and Imre, bewildered, turned the little oval over and over under the blossom-light until his hands shook. [241]

The charter offered one remedy for anomalies: a curator could formally inquire of the orchard, once per tenure, and the trees would answer. [264] The charter also warned, in red ink, that the answer was forbidden knowledge, and that curators who pursued it did not stay curators. [287] Imre had never wanted to ask anything in his life. [297] He wanted, suddenly and terribly, to ask this. [305]

He did his best thinking in the station's tiny washroom, the only room shielded from the orchard's hum, where a man could hear his own mind. [331] Sitting on the closed lid with the blank in his palm, he had the first true philosophical breakthrough of his career. [352] If inheritance ran backward as easily as forward, then a blank cameo was not unfinished; it was unstarted, waiting for a face that had to be chosen rather than remembered. [382] Someone downstream in time had sent him not a memory but a question. [395] He laughed once, a brightened disquiet fizzing in his chest like static, half wonder and half dread. [412]

He washed his face, walked into the orchard, and made his inquiry aloud. [425] "Whose face belongs on this?" [430] The blossoms turned toward him like ten thousand small ears. [440] The answer arrived not as a voice but as a book, hardening out of starlight onto the oldest branch, bound in bark. [462] Its first page read: "You carve it." [469] Its last page read: "You carved it." [476] Everything between the first and last page was blank, and Imre understood that his whole remaining life was meant to happen there, in the unwritten middle. [502] This was the forbidden knowledge, then: not some cosmic secret, but the unbearable news that the inheritance he managed had always included him. [525] Curators who learned it did not stay curators because catalogers cannot remain neutral about a catalog that contains their own future. [546]

Imre spent three days bewildered in the old way, and then bewildered in a new way, which felt like the beginning of courage. [569] He retreated to the washroom each evening, where the hum could not reach, and worked through the second breakthrough sentence by sentence on the fogged mirror. [595] If the future could plant questions in the past, then the past owed the future answers, and order was not a sequence but a conversation. [620]

On the fourth day he took the finest tool in the Curatorium and began to carve. [636] Not his own profile, which would have been vanity, and not a stranger's, which would have been a lie. [655] He carved the face he saw whenever he imagined the heir who would someday stand in this orchard, holding the cameo, needing to know that someone before them had chosen carefully. [686] It came out looking a little like him and a little like no one yet. [701]

When the last cut settled, a new blossom opened on the oldest tree, silver and certain, and the bark-bound book grew one more written page. [726] Imre logged the cameo into the ledger under a name he invented and somehow recognized, then sat down on the cold rock among the roots that grew from nothing. [755] The orchard hummed around him, no longer lonely, or at least lonely in company. [769] He was still bewildered, and he suspected he always would be, but it was a brightened bewilderment now, simply complicated, the kind a person can live inside. [796]
