After numbers changed meaning, the town of Lowmarsh stopped trusting clocks. [11] Sevens curdled into omens, fours became apologies, and a price tag reading twelve could start a fistfight or a marriage. [31] So the town appointed Ina Voss its timekeeper, because she alone still felt the hours in her body. [49] Ina was a borrowed-time timekeeper in the literal sense: a fever at nine had stopped her heart for four minutes, and the doctor told her mother those minutes were a loan that would someday be recalled. [85] She had lived ever since as a debtor of duration, exquisitely attentive to every hour she might not get to keep. [106] Her office was the emerald silo at the edge of the grain fields, painted green generations ago to honor a harvest no one could enumerate anymore. [132] Inside, she kept time the only way left: by texture. [142] Morning felt like burlap, noon like warmed brass, dusk like wet wool against the wrist. [157] She rang the silo's bell when the texture turned, and Lowmarsh arranged its life around her hands. [174] What she could not do was explain time to herself. [184] Feeling it was not the same as understanding it, and a debtor wants, above all, to understand the thing she owes. [205]

That spring, workmen digging a drainage ditch rediscovered the town's time capsule, buried before the change, and brought it to her cracked open and weeping groundwater. [231] The capsule was a ruin of intentions. [238] Photographs had dissolved into gray milk; letters had swollen into pulp; a cassette tape had rusted to its reel. [257] Only one object survived: a fragment of a cremation urn, glazed blue, etched with half a sentence. [274] The shard read: "time is not a river but a," and then the break. [288] Ina turned the fragment over and found initials scratched into the glaze: E.V. [301] Elsbeth Voss, her grandmother, the town's last clockmaker, cremated the winter before the capsule was sealed. [317] Someone had broken her urn, kept the ashes, and buried a piece of the vessel with a message meant for the future. [339] The rest of the sentence existed somewhere, on other shards, in other hands. [352]

But how does a timekeeper search a town that fears direct questions about the past, in a world where you cannot even post an address? [377] She used the garage sales. [382] Lowmarsh held them weekly, since barter had replaced arithmetic, and everyone read the handwritten descriptions like scripture. [399] Ina began embedding messages in her item descriptions. [407] A chipped teapot became "blue-glazed, broken on purpose, seeking its sentence." [418] A mirror was listed as "shows you the river you are not." [430] Week after week she seeded her tables with riddles only a fellow shard-keeper would recognize, and week after week she waited, savoring the wait itself, noting how anticipation made the hours grain differently, like wood cut against itself. [468] This, she realized, was data: expectancy thickened time. [476]

At a sale in late summer, an old man named Tobias Marsh lingered over the teapot. [492] "Broken on purpose," he said, and drew from his coat a blue shard reading "fabric; it is felt, not." [511] Elsbeth had given pieces to her three apprentices on her deathbed, he explained, with instructions to scatter them into the future and trust the future to ask. [538] The capsule shard was his fellow apprentice's doing; the third piece had gone to a woman who left for the coast. [559] Tobias had read Ina's descriptions for two seasons before believing them. [570] "Your grandmother said the asking mattered more than the answer," he told her. [583] "She said a mystery you anticipate teaches you what a mystery solved never can." [597]

They fitted the two shards together in the emerald silo's green-glass light, the seam nearly invisible. [613] The joined inscription read, "time is not a river but a fabric; it is felt, not," and then the fracture, the final word still out there, traveling toward some coastal garage sale of its own. [648] Ina laughed, and something in her chest unclenched for the first time since the fever. [663] Standing there, she felt suddenly timeless, not outside of time but woven into it, a thread rather than a debtor. [683] Her borrowed minutes were not a loan being recalled; they were the warp through which her whole life had been woven tighter than anyone else's. [708] She understood, at last, the texture she had been ringing bells about for years: time passing feels like anticipation, like a sentence interrupted, like the hour before a stranger arrives with the missing piece. [742]

That week, her garage sale table held only one item. [752] The description read: "two-thirds of a sentence, blue, patient; will trade for the word you carried to the sea." [771] Then Ina Voss climbed the emerald silo, rang the dusk bell, and settled in to enjoy the waiting. [789]
