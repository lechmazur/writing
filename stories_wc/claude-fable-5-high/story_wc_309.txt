The year the spell-engines learned to dream, Corvan Pell failed his mastery examination for the ninth time. [17] It hardly mattered anymore. [21] The Academy's brass engines could now compose in an afternoon what whole guilds of mages once labored decades to invent, and they did it without sleep, doubt, or wonder. [50] Creativity, the proctors announced, had been automated; everyone else could go home. [62]

Corvan went to his grandmother's garden instead. [69] She had built a folly there long ago, a little ruined arch that ruined nothing, led nowhere, and meant everything to her. [91] Its keystone still glowed faintly at dusk, a lingering luminance left over from the small hand-worked charm she had pressed into it the summer before she died. [118] The engines could counterfeit a thousand brighter lights, but none of them lingered. [131] That was the difference he could not explain to the proctors: machine light arrived and departed on schedule, while hers stayed, the way warmth stays in a stone after the sun has gone. [164]

He pried the keystone loose, wrapped it in oilcloth, and strapped it to his back. [179] His grandmother had told him once where such stones belonged: the Orrery Spire, an icebound needle above the clouds where the first mages had built an arch with one stone missing, waiting. [211] No engine had ever mapped it, because the spire accommodates impossibilities, and engines refuse to compute what cannot be. [230]

Corvan knew only one spell well, the one too humble for examinations: mist walking. [244] It asked nothing clever of its caster, only patience and a willingness to trust ground that was not there. [263] At dawn he stepped off the garden wall onto the low fog and began to climb. [279]

Mist walking is slow. [283] He walked for three days, ascending each bank of cloud like a stair, sleeping in cold hollows of vapor that held his shape until morning. [308] Below him, the cities glittered with engine-made wonders, every spire identical, every light precise, the brilliance of a thousand suns rendered somehow dim by sheer abundance. [334] Brightness without meaning, he thought, is only a kind of darkness that hurts the eyes. [349]

On the fourth morning the clouds broke against something solid: the Orrery Spire, sheathed in blue ice, rising out of the white like a held breath. [375] Its geometry was wrong in gentle ways. [382] Stairs spiraled both up and down at once; doorways opened onto the rooms behind him; an icicle fell upward, unhurried. [402] The spire did not break the rules so much as forgive them. [414] Corvan, who had spent his life failing rules, felt strangely at home. [426]

At the summit stood the unfinished arch, two curves of ancient stone reaching toward each other across a gap exactly the size of his grandmother's keystone. [452] His fingers were nearly too frozen to work, and for a long minute he fumbled at the harness. [470] Then the buckle gave, and he could unstrap the stone at last, and the weight he had carried for days became, in his hands, an offering. [496]

He understood, finally, what the spire was for. [504] It was not a weapon or an archive or an engine. [515] It was a folly, the first and greatest one, a thing built for no purpose except to say that someone had been here and had loved the making. [543] Every garden folly in the world below was a small echo of it, and every keystone charmed by patient hands was a word in a sentence the first mages had left unfinished on purpose, trusting someone would come. [581] Not the cleverest someone. [585] Just one who still believed a thing could be sacred precisely because it was simple. [600]

He set the keystone into the gap. [607] It fit the way the last word of a prayer fits. [618]

What happened next was not a blaze. [625] The arch began to glow the way his grandmother's charm had glowed, faintly, stubbornly, with a light that did not announce itself so much as remain. [651] The luminance ran down the ice of the spire and out along the clouds, and far below, he knew, it was settling into garden walls and doorsteps and the cupped hands of anyone who had ever made something small with care. [692] It would not outshine the engines. [698] It did not need to. [703] Theirs was a brilliance that dimmed everything; this was a dimness that, held close, proved brilliant. [719]

Corvan sat beneath the finished arch until the cold told him to go. [732] He walked home down the mist, an underachiever still by every measure the Academy kept. [747] But his grandmother's empty arch glowed again at dusk that evening, keystone or no, and it lingers yet. [765]
