The station hung at the rim of a forgotten sky, its dishes turned upward like cupped palms still waiting for light. [21] Oru was its janitor, though her broom swept more than floors; she gathered the dust of burned-out meteors, the ash that drifted in through seams no engineer had ever sealed. [51] No one had transmitted orders in three hundred years. [60] The age of star-tracking had ended quietly, unmourned, and Oru worked on through the hush of that overlooked era because someone had to keep the windows clean enough for falling things to be seen. [94] She was not lonely, exactly. [99] She was curious in the way of those who suspect the universe is a question politely waiting to be asked. [119] Each night she climbed to the observation deck carrying her one inheritance: a curve of yellow lantern glass, salvaged from the signal lamp the first trackers had used to flag incoming meteors. [151] Held to her eye, it turned the cold starlight warm, honeyed, almost alive. [164] Through it she watched the stars fall. [171]

The station's old instruments could still plot trajectories, and Oru had taught herself to read them, not as a scientist reads, but as a mourner reads a face. [199] She traced each falling star's path backward, from its bright dying streak to its long dark beginning, and in that tracing she found she could feel what the stone had felt. [230] Eons of drifting. [233] The slow gravity of a world reaching out. [241] Then the fall: terror and beauty fused into one descending line of fire. [254] This was her secret discipline, her unnamed religion: to empathize with what burns. [267]

At first she had pitied the meteors. [274] Poor wanderers, she thought, ending in flame after such patient travel. [285] But pity, she learned, was a kind of distance. [294] The yellow glass taught her better. [300] One night a meteor came in low and slow, skimming the atmosphere of the dead world below, and through the amber lens its trail did not look like destruction at all. [331] It looked like a signature. [336] Like something signing its name in light before letting go. [346] Oru set down the glass and wept, and the weeping did not weaken her. [360] It opened her, the way a seed is opened. [369]

She began to keep a ledger. [375] Not of masses and velocities, as the old trackers had, but of lives. [388] This one was born in the collision of two patient asteroids. [399] This one carried ice older than any sun she had swept beneath. [411] This one fell afraid; she could tell by the way it tumbled. [423] And as her ledger grew, something strange grew with it: a stillness that was not emptiness but abundance, the way a field at dawn is still and yet entirely alive. [453] The station, which had felt like a tomb, began to feel like a garden where light came to be witnessed. [473]

Then came the night she had perhaps always been waiting for. [484] The instruments shrieked: a massive bolide, inbound, its path crossing directly over the station. [498] Protocol, written by hands long dust, demanded she shelter in the deep vaults. [511] Oru climbed to the deck instead. [517] She raised the yellow lantern glass with hands that shook, and she did not hide the shaking, not even from herself. [538] This, she understood at last, was the strength she had been sweeping toward all these silent years: not armor, but openness; not the refusal to break, but the willingness to be breakable in the presence of something vast. [576]

The bolide came down the sky like a god remembering how to walk. [589] Through the amber glass it was not white fire but golden, primal, elegant as a heron's dive, and Oru traced its path with her whole trembling body, backward into the dark where it had waited, forward into the brightness where it would end. [632] I see you, she said. [638] I know the long cold you came from. [646]

The shockwave, when it passed, rattled every window she had ever cleaned, and not one of them cracked. [664] The bolide buried itself in the far plain, and the silence that followed was the fullest silence Oru had ever known, a stillness so rich it seemed to bloom. [693] She walked out to the crater at dawn. [701] At its heart lay the stone, still warm, veined with gold that matched her lantern glass exactly, as though the universe had been signaling in this color all along. [730] She did not take a sample. [736] She sat beside it, vulnerable and unafraid, janitor and witness, and let the morning hush settle over them both. [755] Somewhere above, more stars were falling, and someone would be there to trace them home. [770]
