Mara was a cartomancer who painted her own deck in onyx ink, and the cards never lied to her, only to everyone she loved. [24] On the night her granddaughter was born, she did not go to the hospital. [38] She climbed instead, up the switchback trail behind her dead mother's house, toward the glade that floats above the clouds at midnight. [60] The cards had shown her the way: a saucer, a hand, a white field of folded sky. [77] In her pocket she carried the cracked saucer from her daughter's dolls' tea set, the one she had stepped on the morning she walked out, twenty-six years ago. [105] The crack ran across the painted roses like a road on a map she had refused, for decades, to read. [125]

Her family had a custom, older than anyone could explain: on every birthday, you traced the child's hand on the tablecloth in ink, so the cloth filled year by year with growing hands. [158] Mara had traced her daughter Lis's hand six times. [167] Then she left, and the tablecloth stopped. [174] Tonight she carried it rolled beneath her arm, six small hands and a wilderness of blank linen. [191]

She wanted to know where the lost time had gone. [201] Not metaphorically -- the cards insisted lost time accumulates somewhere, the way rain accumulates in valleys, and the somewhere was up. [221]

The trail ended where the mountain should have, but did not. [233] She stepped off rock onto cloud, and the cloud held. [243] The glade lay before her, moonlit grass growing impossibly from vapor, ringed by birches whose leaves were thin as breath. [263] It was so beautiful her throat closed; beauty like this did not soothe, it strangled. [278]

Others were there. [281] Figures knelt in the silver grass, each fastened to the cloudbank by ropes of mist knotted at the wrists, heads bowed, murmuring apologies into the dark. [307] The cloudbound, her mother had called them once, the repentant who climbed up and could not finish their sorry, so the sky kept them. [331] Mara had thought it a story to frighten children into kindness. [342]

She unrolled the tablecloth in the center of the glade. [352] Six inked hands looked up at her, each larger than the last, like a tide going out. [369] She set the cracked saucer where the seventh hand should have been. [381] The crack began to glow, faintly, the color of onyx ink catching moonlight. [394] And the blank linen began to fill. [401] Hands appeared, tracing themselves: seven years old, ten, thirteen, a slim seventeen-year-old hand, a woman's hand wearing a wedding ring, a hand resting on a pregnant belly. [428] Every birthday she had missed surfaced in ink, because lost time does accumulate, and it had accumulated here, waiting for the cloth that was its rightful home. [455]

Mara wept without understanding and did not want to understand; she was willingly perplexed, because comprehension would have made it smaller. [476] Some mercies you must accept like weather. [483]

Then the mist came for her wrists. [490] It coiled gently, almost lovingly, knotting itself, drawing her down to kneel among the cloudbound. [505] She understood the bargain then: stay, repent forever, and the glade would keep her sorrow beautiful and unfinished, like everything else it kept. [528] It would have been easy. [533] Kneeling is easier than returning. [538]

But tonight, somewhere below the clouds, a child had been born, and her hand was small and untraced, and someone would need to hold the pen. [564] Mara worked her fingers into the misty knots and began to unfasten them, one by one, the way you unfasten a habit, a grief, a name you have called yourself. [594] The glade resisted; the birches sighed; the kneeling figures lifted their faces in something like envy. [610] Repentance, she saw now, was not meant to be a residence. [621] It was meant to be a road back down. [630]

She rolled the tablecloth, its twenty-six new hands still wet with sky. [642] She lifted the saucer last. [647] The crack had not healed -- she was glad of that. [657] Some breaks should stay legible. [662] At the glade's edge she paused, throat aching at the terrible loveliness of it, then stepped off the cloud onto stone. [683]

She walked down through the dark, into the ordinary valley, toward the hospital window that was still lit. [701] When Lis saw her in the doorway, the tablecloth in her arms, neither of them spoke. [717] Lis looked at the twenty-six hands she had never drawn and recognized every one. [731] Then she unfolded her daughter's tiny fist on the white expanse, and Mara uncapped her onyx ink, and together they traced the first hand of the new accumulation. [759]
