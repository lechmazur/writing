The orchards of Veil came with the planet, planted by no one anyone remembered, their roots wrapped around stone and their branches reaching toward the floating rock arches that drifted overhead like patient gray rainbows. [35] We had crashed there, two hundred strangers from a failed convoy, into the soft violet light of the Nebula of Respite. [56] The nebula earned its name; nothing chased us through that glowing dust, and for the first year we simply breathed. [76] Then the orchards began to die. [82] Fruit shriveled on the branch, blossoms opened to no visitors, and we understood, too late, that whatever pollinators this world once held had vanished before we arrived. [109] Hunger makes strangers of family and enemies of strangers, and we were still only strangers. [124]

The chalk messages started that winter. [130] Every dawn, on the deserted sidewalks of the colony we had poured but barely used, someone wrote in a looping, old-fashioned hand: REEXAMINE THE BELL. [155] We posted watches; the watchers saw no one, yet the words returned, white and certain, before every sunrise. [173] It should have frightened us. [178] Instead the handwriting felt strangely trustworthy, the way a grandmother's recipe card feels trustworthy, and arguments at the ration line began ending with someone muttering, well, what does the chalk say today. [210]

The bell, we eventually realized, meant the butterfly garden watering bell, a copper vessel shaped like a church bell with a perforated rim, found in the ruins of a homestead beneath the largest arch. [244] We had catalogued it as a curiosity and shelved it. [254] Now Ines, our botanist, turned it over under lamplight and found what the first survey had missed: rows of dormant chrysalises packed inside the hollow crown, sealed in wax, sleeping. [284] Etched inside the rim were instructions in the same looping hand: WAKE THEM WITH WARM WATER, FEED THEM WITH FLOWERS, AND PLANT THE FLOWERS TOGETHER, ALL OF YOU, OR NOT AT ALL. [316]

That last clause was no poetry. [322] The chrysalises numbered in the thousands, the nectar flowers grew only in seed form scattered through the homestead ruins, and the planting window before the cold season was nine days. [352] No faction could do it alone, and our writer in chalk seemed to know exactly that. [368] Each dawn the sidewalks carried new assignments, patient and precise: NORTH CREW TO THE THIRD ARCH, THE SOIL THERE HOLDS HEAT; MARTA, FORGIVE THE ENGINEER, YOU NEED HIS PUMP. [397] Whoever wrote them knew the planet the way you know a body you have lived in. [413]

On the fifth night I broke the rules and waited on the sidewalk myself, wrapped in a tarp, breathing slow. [433] She came at first light, an old woman made of dawn haze, kneeling with a stub of chalk that never grew shorter. [455] She did not startle when I sat beside her. [464] "I planted these orchards," she said, "and I died before the butterflies hatched, and I have spent a long time wishing someone would come who needed them." [491] "Why the chalk," I asked, "why not simply appear and tell us?" [503] "Because a ghost giving orders makes followers," she said, "but a mystery solved together makes neighbors." [519] Then she pressed the chalk into my hand, cool as river stone, and asked me to keep her secret until the work was done. [543] I did. [545]

On the ninth day we hung the watering bell from the keystone of the great arch and poured warm water through its perforated rim, and the falling drops rang against the copper like a small ceremony. [581] The chrysalises woke over three thorned, anxious weeks, and our hope through that waiting was a brambled thing, snagging on every frost warning and failed seedling, drawing blood and holding on anyway. [613] Then one violet morning the air filled with copper-colored wings. [623] The orchards bloomed and held their fruit. [630] Something else held too: Marta and the engineer married under the third arch; the north crew adopted the orphaned twins who had carried seed trays without complaint; the ration line became a long table. [664]

Only after those strangers had become family, when no message could break us into factions again, did I tell them about the gardener of dawn. [689] She never wrote again after that. [695] I think the telling was what released her, or maybe it was the wings. [709] But every dawn I walk the sidewalks with her chalk in my pocket, and when two neighbors quarrel I write their names side by side beneath a task too big for either one alone. [743] The Nebula of Respite glows violet over the arches, the bell rings softly when we water the butterfly garden, and the chalk never grows shorter. [768] Some inheritances are not objects but appointments. [775]
