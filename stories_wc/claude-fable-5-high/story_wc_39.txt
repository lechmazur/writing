They called him the penny poet, the vagrant who traded verses for copper at the fish market and slept where the wind forgot to look. [25] His home was a feral loft beneath the dunes, the hayloft of a barn the sand had swallowed decades ago, its rafters now veined with beach grass and the burrows of swallows. [57] There, on shelves gleaned from shipwreck planks, he kept his true wealth: glass jars, hundreds of them, each one holding a captured sound. [80] He had learned the trick from his grandmother, who swore that if you opened a jar at the exact moment a sound was born, the glass would keep it the way amber keeps a fly. [115] The young laughed at this; the old wept. [123] That, he understood, was the whole tragedy of the town: two languages, no translator. [137] He had ruined things once, in the ordinary way young men do. [149] He had left his grandmother alone in her last winter, chasing a literary city that never learned his name, and returned to find her cottage sold and her belongings scattered into junk stalls. [182] Redemption, he decided, would not arrive whole; it would have to be gleaned, the way gulls glean a tide line, scrap by shining scrap. [206] So he hunted her life through the market stalls, buying back her thimbles and letters with poem-pennies. [223] On a Tuesday of thin rain, he found the music box. [234] It sat forgotten under a crate of rusted hinges, its rosewood lid warped, its dancer snapped at the waist. [253] He knew it instantly: she had wound it every evening of his childhood, humming the melody her own mother had hummed crossing the sea. [277] The stall keeper wanted a dollar; the poet had sixty cents and a sonnet. [291] The sonnet, mercifully, was good. [296] Back in the loft, he turned the key and heard only a grinding cough, three sour notes, then silence. [315] The comb's teeth were intact; it was the spring that had forgotten its strength, the way the town had forgotten its grandmothers. [337] He worked through the night by lantern light, oiling the gears with fish oil, coaxing the cylinder, splinting the dancer with a gull's quill. [361] And he understood, somewhere past midnight, what the repair was for. [372] The old of the town were dying with their sounds still inside them: lullabies in drowned dialects, the creak of looms, blessings muttered over bread. [397] The young inherited houses but not voices. [404] He could be the bridge; he could become the translator between generations, jar by jar, bottling the elders' sounds before the silence took them. [428] He would begin with the music box, his grandmother's voice once removed. [440] At dawn's earliest whistle, when the wind threads the dune grass and makes the whole hill sing one low note, he carried the box and his cleanest jar to the crest. [471] His grandmother had taught him this too: a sound captured alone is a fact, but a sound captured with the dawn wind inside it is a feeling, and the wind would sweeten the brittle notes into something a child could love. [512] He wound the key. [516] The melody rose, halting at first, then sure, the mended dancer turning her crooked pirouette while the dune whistled its harmony beneath. [538] He opened the jar at the exact breath the two sounds married, and sealed it. [553] Standing alone on the crest, salt-stung and ragged, he felt positively wistful, a sweet ache aimed backward and forward at once, grief and purpose holding hands. [579] That morning he did not sell poems. [586] He walked instead to the almshouse by the harbor and asked the eldest resident, a netmaker with hands like driftwood, to sing whatever she remembered. [611] She wept, then sang; he opened a jar. [619] Word traveled the way word does in small places. [628] By autumn, children climbed the dunes to the feral loft, and the poet, for a penny or a story or nothing at all, would unseal a jar and let a dead dialect lullaby pour over them like warm rain. [667] He wrote each sound a small poem of explanation, a label in two languages: what it was, and what it meant. [688] He never grew rich, and the loneliness never fully left him; it only changed its quality, becoming a kind of lonesome splendor, the solitude of a lighthouse rather than a wreck. [719] On the last shelf, apart from the rest, sat the first jar, the one holding the music box and the dawn wind together. [742] He had promised himself he would open it only once, on the morning he finally believed he had paid his debt. [763] Some dawns, listening to the dune whistle its one low note, he suspected that morning was close. [780]
