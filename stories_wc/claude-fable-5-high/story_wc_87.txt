Elias Marsh had once steered freighters by starlight, and now he could not trust himself to cross a parking lot. [20] Ever since the night his miscalculation put the Coralline on the rocks, the broken navigator had lived in a rented room above the coastal kayak launch, listening to tourists drag plastic hulls across gravel. [54] He kept his astronomical compass on the windowsill, a brass relic etched with constellations, its needle frozen since the wreck. [74] He told people it was broken; the truth was that he had never opened the case to check. [92] Some mornings he hummed sea shanties in a minor key while making coffee, merrily melancholic, a man who had learned to whistle at his own grave. [118] When fog took the coast, as it often did, Elias walked to town by counting utility poles, eleven from his door to the bakery, nineteen to the harbor office. [147] A man who had once read the heavens now navigated by creosote and wire, and he found a bitter comedy in that. [169] Mara, the teenager who ran the kayak rentals, had pestered him all summer for navigation lessons. [185] He always refused, gently, the way a man refuses a drink he wants too much. [200] "Teaching you would mean believing I still know something," he told her once. [213] She had laughed, thinking it a joke. [220] The afternoon the fog came in early, faster than the forecast promised, Mara was out past the point with a rented double and two customers. [245] By four o'clock the water had vanished into white, and the harbor office said the patrol boat was forty minutes out, assisting a trawler. [269] When every expectation collapsed at once, the patrol boat absent, the forecast wrong, there was someone lost and only him to do the math. [293] The regrets came back all at once, a backlash like a snapped towline, the Coralline's hull screaming on basalt, the inquiry, the men who never sailed again. [320] His hands shook so badly he dropped his keys twice on the stairs. [333] Then he heard it: faint, somewhere in the white, Mara's voice calling, distorted by water and distance. [350] He found his feet gravitating toward the launch ramp before his fear could vote. [364] His heart had decided; the rest of him would simply have to catch up. [378] He grabbed the launch's air horn and the astronomical compass from his sill, because if he was going to fail again, he would fail with his whole history in his pocket. [409] There were no stars; there was barely a shoreline. [418] But the utility poles marched along the coast road above the beach, each one carrying a sodium light that burned a smeared orange halo through the fog. [445] Elias knew their spacing the way he had once known declinations: fifty meters apart, the line bending north at the point. [466] He ran the beach beneath them, counting aloud, and at every third pole he blasted the horn and listened. [485] Mara's voice answered, closer, then farther, then closer again as the current pushed them. [499] At pole seventeen, where the road bent, he understood the drift: they were being carried toward the rocks where the Coralline had died. [522] His faith in his own reckoning flickered like the sodium lamps, on, off, on. [536] He opened the compass case for the first time in six years. [548] The needle swung, steadied, and pointed, alive the entire time, waiting only for him to look. [564] He laughed, one short broken sound, and took a bearing off the seventeenth pole. [578] "Paddle toward the horn," he bellowed, "two hundred meters, bearing on my voice, hard strokes!" [593] He walked them in pole by pole, horn blast by horn blast, correcting their angle against the compass each time the fog swallowed their answers. [618] The kayak's yellow nose came out of the white twenty meters from shore, Mara's face gray with effort, the two customers weeping with relief. [642] Elias waded in to his chest and hauled the bow onto sand. [654] Later, wrapped in blankets at the launch, Mara asked how he had found them with no stars and no sea visible. [675] "Utility poles," he said, and showed her the compass, its needle trembling honestly in the lamplight. [691] "It was never broken," she said. [697] "No," he agreed. [700] "I was." [702] He hummed his minor-key shanty while they stacked the kayaks, sad as ever, and somehow lighter than air. [720] In the morning, the fog gone, he met Mara at the ramp with a chart, the compass, and a pencil. [740] "First lesson," he said, "you don't wait for perfect skies." [750] "You take a bearing on whatever the world gives you, and then you move." [764] His hands, for the first time in six years, did not shake at all. [778]
