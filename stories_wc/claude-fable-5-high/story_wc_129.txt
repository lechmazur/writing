Sefa came to the crater town with nothing but a bundle of marsh reeds and hands that knew how to braid them into living shapes. [25] She was a marsh-reed sculptor from the drowned south, and she had buried everyone who once spoke her name correctly. [45] The town clung to a crystalline orchard perched at the edge of a shimmering crater, where the old impact had fused soil into glass and the trees grew translucent fruit that chimed in wind. [79] People here lived beyond the murmur of lost languages; the strike had erased the archives, and three generations later no one could read the stones their grandparents carved. [107] Sefa understood that kind of silence. [113] What she did not understand was how to belong to people who already belonged to each other. [130]

Her chance arrived as a rumor. [136] Someone was painting a tag across the town's glass walls: a knot of seven interlocking loops, appearing overnight on cisterns, doorways, even the orchard fence. [161] No one had ever seen the artist. [168] The council fretted; the children adored it; old Maren, the cider keeper, swore the knot resembled a symbol from the carved stones nobody could read anymore. [194] So a search began, half scolding and half pilgrimage, and Sefa, hungry to graft herself onto this community, volunteered first. [214]

She was paired with Doran, the orchard warden, a man so boldly sympathetic that he apologized to trees before pruning them. [235] He asked about her drowned village on their first night watch and then simply held the silence with her, which was more than anyone had offered in years. [263] Together they mapped the tags. [268] Sefa noticed what the others had not: each knot was painted at the height of a child's reach, and the loops always pointed downslope, toward the crater. [295]

Night after night, the shared search braided strangers together. [304] Maren brought cider to the watchers; the baker's twins drew copies of the knot; Doran taught Sefa the orchard's names while she taught him to weave reed lanterns. [332] She felt it happening, the slow ache of being almost included, luminous and unbearable, like light through unripe fruit. [351]

On the ninth night, following the loops' direction, they descended past the orchard's last row and found it: a glimmering obsidian arbor, an archway of impact glass standing alone on the crater's rim, half swallowed by ash. [388] Every inch of it was painted with knots, hundreds of them, layered over years. [402] Beneath the arbor crouched the artist: Liss, a girl of perhaps twelve, the orphan who slept in the cider house and spoke to almost no one. [428] She did not run. [432] She pointed at the oldest carved stone, set into the arbor's base, where the same seven-looped knot was chiseled beside rows of unreadable script. [456] "I found it after my mother died," Liss said. [465] "I don't know what it means, but it was ours once, so I kept putting it where people would see." [485]

Maren, summoned at dawn, wept over the stone. [493] Her grandmother had drawn that knot on bread loaves; she remembered now, the way the body remembers before the mind does. [514] It was not a word at all but a diagram: seven loops for seven founding families, each strand passing through every other, a map of the webs of kinship that had built the town before the sky fell. [552] The language was gone, but the knot had survived, carried forward by a lonely child with a paint pot. [571]

The council wanted to scrub the walls and scold the girl. [582] Doran argued otherwise, and Sefa stood beside him, her voice shaking, an outsider defending an outsider. [598] "Let me make it permanent," she said. [605]

For a month she worked at the crater's rim with Liss as her apprentice, weaving marsh reeds soaked in mineral wash through the obsidian arbor: seven great loops, each strand threaded through every other, green life braided into black glass. [645] The whole town climbed down to watch the dedication. [654] Maren spoke the only old word anyone still knew, a greeting, and the orchard's fruit chimed behind them like small bells agreeing. [676] "We dedicate this arbor," Sefa said, "to the families who made this place, and to everyone who arrives carrying nothing and asks to be woven in." [702] Then she added an eighth loop, raw reed, unpainted, and threaded it through the other seven. [718]

Liss took her hand. [722] Doran rested his palm on the glass. [729] The crater shimmered below them, holding all the words it had taken, and for once the silence did not feel like loss. [751] It felt like a knot, pulled tight, holding. [759]
