The smog over Caldera City never truly lifted, but on the rooftop of the Mirren Building, behind greenhouse glass gone amber with grime, Una Voss had learned to live without sleep. [31] She was a painter by trade and an insomniac by constitution, and the two facts had braided together until she could no longer say which fed the other. [59] What kept her on the roof under the waxing moon was not the tomatoes or the stubborn orchids, but a brush. [80] It had belonged to Master Hadim, the calligrapher who once lived two floors down, and its handle ended in a small sliver of polished horn shaped like a crescent, so that everyone called it the moon-tipped brush. [117] Hadim had written in Saffi, a script so old that the city archive listed it among the lost languages, kept alive only in his patient hand. [143] When Una was nineteen and starving between commissions, he had left bread and ink at her door without a word, week after week, asking nothing. [168] He died in spring, and the building forgot him by summer, and that forgetting sat in Una's chest like a swallowed stone. [190] She refused to believe it. [195] Strangers remember kindness, she told herself; they only need something to rouse the memory. [209] So she made herself an experiment, and she went about it the way she primed canvas: methodically hopeful, layer over careful layer. [231] Each sleepless night she climbed to the greenhouse, where the glass filtered the smog into a dim gold haze, a saffron quiet that made the whole roof feel like the inside of a lantern. [265] There she waited for the waxing moon to find a seam in the pollution. [279] When its light slid across the panes, the daydreams came: she saw Hadim's wrist turning, heard him name each Saffi character as if introducing old friends. [305] She had never studied the script properly; the moonlit reveries were her only classroom, memory unspooling what waking effort could not reach. [327] With the moon-tipped brush and white gouache, she painted Saffi words on small clay tags from the greenhouse pots. [346] Each tag carried one word Hadim had loved: shelter, bread, again, friend. [358] Beneath the word, in plain letters, she wrote its meaning and a single line: a kindness was done here once. [378] Before dawn she hung the tags around the neighborhood: on the bakery grate, the tram shelter, the door of the shuttered archive. [400] For a week, nothing. [404] She did not despair; method forbade it. [411] On the eighth night, climbing to the roof, she found a loaf of bread wrapped in paper on the greenhouse step, and on the paper, in shaky copied strokes, the Saffi word for again. [445] Una stood very still in the saffron dark, holding warm bread, and laughed once, softly, the way people laugh when something they have bet their heart on pays out. [474] More followed. [476] The baker, it turned out, had been a boy Hadim once taught to write his own name; the tag on his grate had roused the memory whole. [503] A retired archivist left a note begging to know who still wrote Saffi, because she had catalogued it as dead and wept doing so. [527] A tram driver pinned a tag inside his cab and began letting the fare slide for anyone who looked the way Una had once looked at nineteen. [554] By the time the moon had fattened past its half, they were coming up to the roof itself. [572] The baker brought flour sacks to patch drafts in the greenhouse; the archivist brought Hadim's confiscated primers, smuggled out of deep storage under her coat. [597] Strangers sat among the orchids in the gold haze and traced crescents in the air, learning a dead man's living alphabet. [618] Una still did not sleep. [623] But now her nights had shape: moonlight through smog-stained glass, the brush's horn tip catching the glow, a circle of half-known faces repeating words that meant shelter, bread, again, friend. [653] One night the archivist asked her why she had started it all. [665] Una wiped the moon-tipped brush clean and considered the question with a painter's patience. [679] A man fed me once and asked for nothing, she said, and the city pretended it never happened. [697] I needed proof that the pretending was the lie. [706] Above the glass, the waxing moon pushed a pale thumbprint through the smog, and below it the lantern-colored greenhouse hummed with a language that was no longer lost, only sleeping, and finally being roused.
