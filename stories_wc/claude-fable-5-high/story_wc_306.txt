Mara sold doubt the way other people sold rope: by the measured length, knotted to hold. [16] A conscience for hire, the harbor folk called her, and she never argued with the title. [32] Certain men paid her to hesitate on their behalf, and she hesitated beautifully. [45] Magistrate Ellum found her in the tavern with a purse of silver and a face like a slammed door. [64] Three nights ago he had turned away a ship in the storm, certain it carried fever, and it had broken on the southern rocks. [88] He did not want absolution. [93] "I want to know if I was right," he said, "and I want the knowing to cost me something." [112] Mara studied him and saw the rarest kind of client: a man whose certainty had begun to chafe like an iron collar. [134] She named her price and added a condition: a night's vigil on the beach below the old lighthouse, and her ritual at dawn. [157]

They built a campfire on the shingle and fed it driftwood while Ellum talked. [171] He talked the way certain men do, laying out his reasoning in clean stones: the ship's flag, the season of sickness, the lives ashore he was sworn to guard. [200] Mara listened and said nothing, because her trade had taught her that doubt cannot be poured into a vessel still warm with argument. [223] Her work only began, as her contract put it, "after the campfire's last spark dies." [238] So they waited, and near dawn the final ember went grey, and she stood. [252]

She wore her cloak of doubt then, a garment of grey patches sewn from the clothes of people who had changed their minds. [275] It made her look unfinished, and that was the point: nobody trusts a hesitation delivered by someone who seems sure. [295] They climbed the lighthouse stairs as the sky went the color of oyster shell, wind threading through the open gallery at the top. [318] From her satchel Mara drew nine wind chimes, each tuned to a different question. [332] "Was I right hangs here," she said, hooking the first to the rail, "and Was I cruel here, and Could I have known there." [356] The method was old: you did not ask the wind anything; you simply learned to decipher which chimes it chose to ring, and in what order, and let the selection argue with you. [389]

Ellum watched her with a soldier's suspicion. [396] "You believe this?" he asked. [401] "Not a word of it," Mara said pleasantly, "and I mean every word of it." [416] It was her oldest answer, sincerely insincere, and it was the truest thing about her trade: the ritual was theater, but the listening it forced was real. [443]

Dawn broke breezy and raw. [448] The wind came off the water and rang Could I have known, then Was I cruel, then Could I have known again, and never once touched Was I right. [477] Ellum's jaw worked. [480] "The wind is random," he said. [486] "Entirely," Mara agreed. [489] "So why does it hurt that it skipped your question?" [499]

He had no answer, and she watched the first crack open in him, careful as a midwife. [516] From an inner pocket she brought out her last instrument: a hollow mirror shard, glass ground so thin it held no silvering at all, a frame of reflection around nothing. [546] "Look in it and tell me you were right," she said. [557] He looked, and of course there was no face there to agree with him, only the grey sea showing through where his certainty wanted a witness. [583] "I might have been right," he said at last, voice shaking. [594] "I might have drowned forty people for a flag and a season." [606]

"Both," Mara said. [609] "You hired me to carry the second sentence so you could keep living with the first." [625] "And that is the only thing I will not sell you, because it is the only thing worth having." [644] This was the part of her work no contract named: she did not sell doubt to destroy certain men, but to welcome nuance into certainty, the way a window welcomes weather without surrendering the house. [679]

Ellum wept once, briefly, then stood straighter than he had on the beach. [692] Mara reached up to unhook the chimes, then stopped at his raised hand. [705] "Leave them," he said. [709] "I will climb here when I am too sure of anything." [720]

So instead she unhooked the clasp of her cloak and set the grey patchwork on his shoulders, and she stood in the wind without it, feeling the cold honestly for the first time in years. [755] It frightened her, and it did not harm her: this was what she had been selling all along, a way to be open without being undefended. [781] She kept the hollow shard. [786] Empty frames, she had learned, are the only mirrors that never lie. [798]
