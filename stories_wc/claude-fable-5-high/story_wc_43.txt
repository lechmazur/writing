The hospice of Saint Cecilia sat on a ridge so high that the valley's weather happened below it, and on most mornings the windows opened onto an unbroken floor of cloud lit gold by sunrise. [35] The patients called it the radiant haven above the clouds, and Mara Quist, the music therapist, never corrected them. [54] Mara was serene the way deep water is serene, with currents underneath. [66] On Tuesday the blood work came back for Eli Brandt, room nine, and the doctor's careful voice told everyone what the numbers meant: weeks, not months. [92] Eli took the news the way he took his pills, dry and without water. [106] Then he asked Mara to bring him the cigar box from his dresser. [119] Inside lay a jagged piece of a broken vinyl record, black as a crow's wing, one third of an old seventy-eight. [140] "My brother Tomas has the rest," Eli said. [148] "We snapped it between us in 1974, in our mother's kitchen, and we have not spoken since." [165] He turned the shard so the lamplight ran along the grooves. [176] "It was her favorite hymn record," he said, "and we fought over who deserved it, which tells you we deserved nothing." [197] "I want to say goodbye to him without a single wrong note," he said. [211] "A flawless goodbye, Mara, or none at all." [219] Mara understood the request was a wall as much as a wish; flawless was the word men used when they were afraid of weeping. [243] But she was decisively kind, which meant kindness in her was not a mood but a verdict, acted on at once. [264] That evening she found Tomas Brandt's number through the parish in the valley and called him. [280] "He doesn't want apologies," she told the silence on the line. [291] "He wants the record whole one more time." [299] Tomas arrived two days later, climbing out of the cloud bank like a man surfacing from gray water, carrying a shoebox under his arm. [323] The brothers sat across from each other and said nothing, because fifty years of nothing is hard to interrupt. [342] So Mara did what she always did when grief jammed the door: she hymned the mundane. [358] She lifted her guitar and sang, softly and seriously, about the breakfast tray, the burnt toast, the squeak of the orderly's left shoe, the way the kettle in the family kitchen always whistled flat. [392] It was her oldest trick: make the small things holy and the unsayable becomes sayable. [407] Tomas laughed first, a rusty hinge of a laugh, and said the kettle in their mother's kitchen had whistled flat too. [428] Eli said their mother sang over it anyway, on key, out of spite. [441] Then both men were talking, the words coming out in the order they had been swallowed, 1974 first. [459] Tomas opened the shoebox. [463] Inside were the other two pieces of the record, wrapped in a handkerchief that still held a ghost of their mother's orange-blossom water. [486] The scent rose into the room, faint and sweet, and Mara watched both old faces lift toward it the way plants lift toward light. [510] She thought: that is what hope smells like; not the future, but the proof that something tender survived the past. [530] On the bedside table they reunified the record, three shards fitted along their old fractures until the label's circle closed. [550] It would never play again, and it did not need to. [561] Mara read the title off the mended label and played the hymn from memory, slow, while the cloud floor outside burned rose and gold. [585] The brothers sang the parts their mother had taught them, baritone and cracked tenor, missing notes everywhere. [602] Eli stopped midverse and looked at his hands. [610] "It isn't flawless," he said. [615] "No," Mara said. [618] "It's better." [620] Eli died nine days later, in the early morning, with Tomas asleep in the chair beside him and the mended record on the sill. [644] The room afterward held a strange quality Mara had learned to recognize in her years above the clouds: a pulsing stillness, quiet that kept time like a heart, as if the silence itself remembered the song. [680] Tomas asked to keep the record, then changed his mind and asked her to keep it instead, here, where the kettle whistled flat and somebody made it holy anyway. [709] Mara set it on the music room shelf beside her guitar. [720] Some afternoons, when the sun comes through, the black shards shine along their healed seams, and the faintest breath of orange blossom rises, and new patients ask what it is. [750] "Hope," Mara tells them, serenely, and begins to sing about the weather, the toast, the squeaking shoe. [767]
