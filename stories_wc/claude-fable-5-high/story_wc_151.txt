The Aurelian Arcade kept its glass roof from the old century, and at dawn the light came through it like honey poured over the shuttered shopfronts. [26] Mara Voss arrived before the drones woke, carrying her ebony clipboard like a relic. [40] She was, by trade, a pseudonym creator. [47] In a city where every message was scanned, sorted, and predicted before it was sent, surface mail had become the last unread channel, and the people who used it wanted names that could not be traced back to their pulse. [87] Mara built those names. [91] She was carefully inventive about it, never flashy: a "Tomas Reel" for a man who missed rivers, a "June Halloway" for a woman leaving a marriage like a closing door. [121] Each name carried a small, deniable truth. [128] The old post office at the arcade's end still collected letters at seven, and this morning, for the first time in nine years, one of the envelopes in the brass box would be hers. [162] It sat in her coat pocket, addressed in her own handwriting, signed with her own name. [178] But first, the chalk. [182] For three weeks, someone had been drawing on the arcade's tiles before sunrise: looping glyphs in children's sidewalk chalk, pink and yellow and a blue that glowed faintly gold under the roof's morning light. [216] The cleaning units erased them by eight. [223] Mara had begun copying them onto her clipboard, the only object her grandmother had left her, its black wood worn smooth by two generations of hands that distrusted screens. [252] She did it for the same reason she did everything: to discover meaning in the margins, in the places the sorting algorithms never thought to look. [278] This morning the glyphs were fresh, still dusty at the edges. [289] She knelt, clipped a blank page over her older copies, and began to juxtapose the new marks against the old, line by line, dawn by dawn. [315] Set side by side, the pattern surfaced like a photograph in developer fluid. [328] Each glyph was a letterform folded into itself, and the first cluster, unfolded, read: TOMAS REEL. [344] Her breath caught. [347] The second read JUNE HALLOWAY, and the third, the fourth, the ninth, all of them, were names she had invented. [367] Someone was keeping a ledger of her ghosts in sidewalk chalk. [378] She heard the soft scrape of chalk before she saw the girl, maybe ten years old, crouched near the post office step, finishing a glyph with the seriousness of a stonemason. [409] "You're the name lady," the girl said without looking up. [419] "My grandfather sorts the surface mail." [425] "He says your people's letters are the only ones with handwriting that shakes." [438] Mara lowered the clipboard. [442] "Why write the names here?" [447] The girl shrugged, dusting yellow from her palms. [455] "Grandfather says every letter that comes back undeliverable gets burned." [465] "Nobody remembers those people existed, because they never did." [474] "So I write them down where the sun touches first." [484] "A name should get one morning, at least." [492] Mara looked at the glyphs again, the bright childish colors against the arcade's old gold light, and understood she was standing in a cemetery of her own making. [520] Every undelivered letter was a person who had tried to say something true under a borrowed face and failed to find their reader. [543] The meaning had been in the margins all along, in the burn pile, in the chalk. [559] Her own letter felt suddenly heavy. [565] It was addressed to her brother, estranged eleven years, and she had nearly signed it "Ada Marsh," a name soft enough to be refused without pain. [591] At the last moment, at her kitchen table, she had crossed it out and written Mara. [607] Now she knew why. [611] A pseudonym could carry the truth, but only a real name could be grieved, forgiven, or answered. [628] The collection bell chimed seven, antique brass over silent machinery. [638] Mara crossed to the box and held the envelope above the slot. [650] "If it comes back," she said, "don't let him burn it." [661] "Bring it to me." [665] The girl considered this, then held out a stub of pink chalk. [677] "Write your name down anyway," she said. [684] "The real one. [687] So the morning has it either way." [694] Mara let the letter fall, heard it land among strangers' confessions, and knelt on the warm tiles. [711] Beside nine invented names, in shaky pink capitals, she wrote her own. [723] Behind the glass roof, the city's towers blinked their cold blue data into the sky, but down here the light was gold, the chalk was bright, and somewhere a truck with rubber tires was coming to carry paper across actual distance. [764] Mara recorded the morning's glyphs on her ebony clipboard, one margin at a time, and waited to learn whether her name would come back to her. [790]
