Mara Voss had been called blessed exactly once, and pariah ever since. [12] The selection algorithm chose her to keep watch through the shutdown, the lone mind left waking while four hundred colonists slept in cold berths below. [37] Blessed, the captain said, because the algorithm found her temporal sense uniquely stable. [50] Pariah, because everyone else heard the unspoken half: she had no one aboard who would miss her. [67] After the shutdown, the ship's drive coasted at nine-tenths of light, and silence took on a texture. [84] It echoed. [86] Her footsteps on the time dilation observation deck returned to her a half-beat late, as though the corridor itself were rehearsing her absence. [109] The deck had been built for tourists of physics: a long gallery of viewports where the forward stars bunched into a blue-shifted crown and the aft stars smeared red and dim. [140] Plaques explained that one hour here equaled two and a quarter back home. [153] Mara did not need plaques. [158] She felt the stretch in her bones, every minute pulled long like cooling glass. [172] She carried one thing that was not ship issue: her grandmother's sketchbook, its canvas cover fastened with a gilded compass rose applique, the gold thread worn soft at the northern point. [203] In her family, every anniversary was marked the same way: you went outside, whatever the weather, and sketched the night sky exactly as it appeared. [228] Weddings, deaths, departures. [231] Her grandmother said the stars never lied about where you stood; if you drew them honestly, you could always find your way back to who you were that night. [260] Tonight marked one year since launch, one year by the ship's stretched clocks, anyway. [274] Back on Earth, her family had already kept this anniversary twice without her. [287] The arithmetic confused her in a way the plaques could not fix; grief kept arriving from a calendar she no longer shared. [309] So she did the only thing her blood knew how to do with confusion: she opened the sketchbook and turned it into a map. [333] She sketched the warped sky as honestly as her grandmother taught: the blue crown forward, the red smear aft, the strange starless band between where aberration had swept the heavens bare. [364] And there, in the crown, she glimpsed it: a point of light that should not move, moving. [381] A glimpse only, gone when she stared, present when she drew. [392] Her pencil had caught what her eyes kept dismissing, the way her grandmother's sketches once caught a comet the newspapers announced a week later. [416] Protocol said: log anomalies, wake no one, conserve power until the burn. [428] Protocol had been written by people who would sleep through the whole stretched decade and never feel one minute of it. [449] Mara pressed her thumb against the compass rose and felt the worn northern point, the place where four generations of thumbs had asked the same question. [475] Which way is true north, when there is no north, no up, no shared clock to agree on now? [494] Her grandmother's answer surfaced like a star from cloud: true north is not a direction; it is whatever you steer by when nothing else holds still. [520] Night after stretched night, she returned to the deck and sketched, dating each page in both clocks, hers and Earth's. [540] The moving light resolved across her pages into a curve, then a trajectory, then an intention: an object decelerating, on a course that would cross theirs. [566] Not debris. [568] Debris does not brake. [572] Confusion rose in her again, and this time she recognized it for what her family custom had always quietly insisted it was: an invitation. [596] You do not sketch the sky to tame it; you sketch it to follow it somewhere. [612] She walked to the command interface, broke the seal protocol said she must never break, and began the wake sequence for the navigator. [635] Then she returned to the observation deck to wait, the echo of her footsteps following like a second, slower self. [655] When the navigator stumbled onto the deck, frost still in his lashes, he found the pariah they had all been glad to leave awake, holding open a sketchbook. [683] "Something is coming to meet us," she said, and her voice came back off the viewports twice, as if the silence itself agreed. [706] He studied her pages, the honest, dated skies, the curve drawn in pencil, and for the first time since the algorithm blessed her, someone looked at Mara Voss without flinching. [736] "Can we adjust course?" she asked. [742] "Toward it?" [744] "Toward it." [746] She touched the gilded rose. [751] "I finally know which way north is." [758]
