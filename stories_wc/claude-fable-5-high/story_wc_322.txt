Mara called herself a fever dream architect, though the plaque outside the gallery said only Curator. [16] The Halloran Optical Illusion Gallery had been closed to the public for three years, but she still kept the lights on. [37] She worked at night, when the impossible staircases on the walls seemed to breathe. [51] Her craft was rational enchantment: magic with ledgers, spellwork that obeyed arithmetic. [63] Hospitals sent her the fevered, the ones whose delirium had become dangerous, and she built them better dreams. [81] She did this by weaving patterns of sinew thread across wooden frames, each knot a load-bearing joist in someone else's sleeping mind. [103] The thread came from a butcher who never asked questions, and Mara never offered answers. [118]

The gallery suited the work. [123] Illusions are honest lies, she told her clients' families; they show you exactly how your eyes betray you. [141] Dreams built inside the gallery inherited that honesty. [149] They felt realistically imaginary, vivid enough to soothe but structured enough that no sleeper ever mistook them for waking life. [169] That margin of doubt was her safety rail, and she charged extra for it. [183]

Her newest patient was her brother, Tomas, burning at forty degrees in the back room, and for him she charged nothing. [204] They had not spoken in eleven years. [211] The hospital had called her because his chart listed no one else. [223] She told herself she took him in out of professional curiosity. [234] The truth was simpler and stranger: she wanted to discover the texture of connection, to learn whether the thing that bound people together could be held between finger and thumb like thread. [266] She had built dreams for hundreds of strangers and felt nothing pass between them. [280] Perhaps blood would conduct. [284]

She strung the first frame beside his cot and began the standard lattice: a childhood kitchen, a window, rain. [303] But fevered minds edit themselves, and as memories alter, the architecture must alter with them. [318] Each night Tomas's delirium revised the kitchen. [325] The window migrated. [328] The rain turned to ash. [333] A door appeared where their mother used to stand. [342] Mara followed every change, unpicking knots, rethreading sinew, keeping pace with a memory that was rewriting itself in real time. [362] She noticed, with an unsettling calm that frightened her more than panic would have, that the kitchen he remembered was not the one she did. [387] In his version, she was already gone. [394] In hers, he was the one who had left. [403] Both versions could not be true, and the lattice, being rational, refused to hold them simultaneously. [419] The thread began to hum. [424]

On the fourth night, the contradiction reached its limit. [433] The frame did not break; it erupted, knots unraveling outward in a slow geyser of pattern, and the dream spilled into the gallery itself. [457] The painted staircases peeled off the walls and became walkable. [467] The kitchen assembled itself in the rotunda, half hers, half his, the two versions stitched along a seam of trembling sinew. [488] Tomas stood at the seam, awake inside his own fever, looking at her with their father's eyes. [505] You built this, he said. [510] It was not a question. [515] Only half, she said. [519] The rest is yours. [523]

By every rule of her craft, she should have cut the lattice and collapsed the dream before it consumed them both. [544] Instead she stepped across the seam into his half of the kitchen. [556] The ash-rain touched her skin and she understood, at last, why he had remembered her gone: in his memory, her leaving for apprenticeship had happened first, had caused everything after. [586] Eleven years of blame dissolved into mere sequence, two children keeping different clocks. [599] She took his hand. [603] There it was: the texture of connection, neither thread nor knot, but the precise friction of two altered memories agreeing to share a single room. [628] It felt like sinew, warm and faintly resistant, the stuff she had been weaving all along without recognizing it. [647]

The fever broke at dawn. [652] The gallery returned to itself, the staircases flattening back into paint, looking embarrassed about the whole affair. [669] Tomas slept without dreaming, which was the highest compliment her work could receive. [682] Mara sat in the rotunda with the ruined frame in her lap, unhurried, possessed of a calm that no longer unsettled her. [704] She rethreaded the sinew slowly, not to build a dream this time, but to keep her hands honest while she decided what to say when he woke. [731] The plaque outside still said Curator. [737] She thought she might finally have it changed: keeper of what two people see differently, and love anyway. [755]
