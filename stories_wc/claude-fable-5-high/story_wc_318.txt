My grandfather, Aurelio Vance, was the only leaf blower symphony conductor our town ever produced, and he took the title seriously. [21] Every October he arranged his landscaping crew in the old echo chamber tunnel beneath the rail yard and conducted their machines with a willow baton, coaxing chords out of two-stroke engines until the concrete sang. [56] He started it the year my mother died, because our house had gone quiet in a way that no one was willing to name. [80] We were a family that tended to the unspoken the way other families tended gardens, carefully, daily, and without ever calling it grief. [103] Aurelio decided the silence needed an opponent loud enough to be ridiculous, and ridiculous enough to make me laugh again. [123] It almost worked, three autumns running, the tunnel howling his strange music while I stood at his elbow and refused to smile. [145]

Then his heart began to fail, and the doctor gave him a season, maybe two. [160] Aurelio bought an hourglass at the church rummage sale, set it on the kitchen windowsill, and turned it each morning like a man winding a clock he did not trust. [190] He spent his last weeks in the workshop, leaving walnut husks to macerate in jars of rainwater until they broke down into a brown ink, which he used to write out his final score by hand. [226] He called the piece Lullaby for a Quiet House. [235] He died on a Tuesday, in his chair, and when I came downstairs the hourglass on the windowsill had emptied, every grain settled, as if the two of them had agreed to finish together. [269]

After the final grain slips through the hourglass, my grandmother said, a house decides what kind of quiet it will keep. [290] Ours chose the bad kind. [295] We moved through the rooms like museum staff, dusting his absence, never laughing, never daring to. [311] Then, in late October, an envelope surfaced in the workshop with my name on it in walnut ink. [329] Inside was the pull cord handle from his favorite blower, worn smooth as a river stone, and a single line: the crew knows the piece, you know the tunnel, finish what I started. [362]

I went that night, mournfully eager, wanting it over and wanting it never to end, the handle cold in my pocket like a small fossil of him. [389] The crew was already there, eleven men and women with machines slung over their shoulders, his ink-written score taped to the tunnel wall by lantern light. [415] His own blower sat on a crate at the center, its starter rope bare, waiting for the handle only I carried. [436] I threaded it on with shaking fingers. [443]

Before I could pull, the wind came through the tunnel on its own, thin and off-key, and I swear it carried something half-sung, a lullaby with my mother's old melody bent crooked inside it. [477] The crew heard it too; I watched them go still, the way people do in church when they suspect they are not alone. [500] It was not frightening, exactly. [505] It was a true haunting, which I have learned is mostly tenderness with nowhere left to live. [522] He had been singing to that quiet house for years through engines because words would have broken him, and now the tunnel was singing back. [547]

I pulled the cord. [551] The engine caught on the first try, which it never did for anyone but him. [566] Eleven more roared up around me, and I raised his willow baton and followed the score. [582] The echo chamber did what he had always promised it would: it took our crude droning and folded it over itself until it became a single enormous voice, rising and falling like breath. [615] Underneath it, threaded through it, the off-key wind kept its half-sung lullaby going, and I understood the score had been written around it, leaving room for a part only he could play. [647]

Halfway through, the absurdity of it landed on me all at once: a dying man's last wish, performed on lawn equipment, in a drainage tunnel, by weeping landscapers. [675] I laughed. [677] It tore out of me, ugly and bright, my first laugh since my mother died, and the tunnel grabbed it and multiplied it until it sounded like a whole household laughing. [708] That, I realized, was the piece's true final movement, the one he could not write down. [724]

When the engines died, the wind had gone quiet, its work finished. [736] I carried the laughter home like a coal cupped in my hands and set it loose at the kitchen table, where my grandmother caught it and laughed too, and the house, at last, chose the good kind of quiet, the kind with room for sound. [781] The pull cord handle hangs on the windowsill now, beside the hourglass, which we never turn. [797]
