When the last humans sealed themselves into hibernation towers to wait out the burning decades, Mara stayed awake. [18] She was a sensory map maker, the only one her guild had trained before sleep took them all. [36] Her work was simple to describe and impossible to explain: she mapped places by what they felt, heat-mapping emotions the way old cartographers had charted depth. [62] Grief read cold and blue on her instruments; longing burned orange; the unknown registered as a white so bright it hurt to record. [85] After humans hibernated, the coastal cave network north of the towers began producing that white in quantities no one had ever seen. [107] So Mara went. [110]

In her breast pocket she carried an elm leaf fossil, a small slab of stone her mother had pressed into her hand before climbing into her sleep pod. [138] A transitional object, the old psychologists would have called it: something a child clings to when crossing from one world into another. [160] Mara was forty-one and unashamed. [165] Everyone she loved had crossed into sleep; she was crossing into something else, and the fossil was her tether to both. [186]

The caves opened at the tideline, black mouths exhaling air that her sensors flagged as factually mystical, her guild's term for phenomena that measured cleanly but explained nothing. [214] The temperature gradients were real. [219] The emotional signatures were real. [224] What produced them, in caves emptied of every living human, was not anything her instruments could name. [241]

The first chamber was flooded, so she let herself float on her back through the dark, sensor array humming against her chest, the fossil a cool weight over her heart. [271] Floating was the only way through; the passages were too tight to swim and too deep to wade. [289] Above her, the ceiling glowed faintly in her visor's overlay: pale gold, the signature of patience. [305] Something in these caves was waiting. [311]

Deeper in, the heat-maps changed. [316] Whole walls blazed white with the unknown, and threaded through the white were colors she had only ever recorded in humans: grief, wonder, a thin red wire of fear. [345] The caves were feeling things. [350] Or something in them was. [355]

In the deepest chamber, her maps failed entirely. [363] Every wavelength saturated at once, and Mara stood in a cathedral of limestone while her instruments wept static. [381] She understood, then, why she had really come. [389] Not to map. [392] Maps were for the sleepers, for the world that would wake in a century and want everything labeled. [410] She had come because the unknown had no one to speak for it, and she wanted, more than she had ever wanted anything, to become its voice. [437] When humanity rose from hibernation, someone would have to tell them that the world had not waited quietly, that it had grown strange and feeling in their absence, and that strangeness was not a threat to be charted away. [476]

She took the elm leaf fossil from her pocket. [485] Under her thumb, the leaf's veins stood out in stone, a thing that had crossed from living to mineral and survived the crossing. [508] Her mother had known what she was giving her: not a keepsake but a lesson. [523] Transition does not destroy; it translates. [529] The elm had become stone and was still, unmistakably, a leaf. [540] Mara could become the voice of something vast and remain, unmistakably, herself. [552]

She set the fossil on the chamber floor, an offering and an introduction. [565] On her overlay, the white light bent toward it, curious, and a new color bloomed across the stone, one her guild had never assigned a name. [591] She decided to call it recognition. [597]

She stayed in the caves through the long sleeping years, learning the grammar of the white light, recording its moods in maps no one was awake to read. [625] It learned her in return, painting her own emotions back at her in colors on the walls, a conversation conducted entirely in temperature and trust. [650] On the morning the towers were due to open, the fossil lay at the cave mouth, returned, warm as a held hand. [672]

When the towers finally opened and the first groggy humans stumbled into a changed world, they found a woman waiting at the tideline with a stone leaf in her hand and a hundred years of maps at her feet. [711] They asked her what had happened to the world while they slept. [723] Mara held up the fossil so they could see the leaf inside the stone. [737] "It changed," she said, "and it is still itself, and it would like, at last, to be heard." [755]
