Mereth had been called the elusive eel herder long before the world lost its grip. [15] Elusive because nobody ever saw her work, only the silver rivers of eels arriving where she promised they would. [34] She was cursed with precognition, and the curse made her shy of company; she could not bear to greet people while watching their endings flicker behind their eyes. [62] Three winters ago she had foreseen the falling, and told no one, because telling had never once changed a thing. [82] The dust of a collapsed star had sifted down through the atmosphere that autumn, fine as breath, and wherever it settled, friction quietly died. [106] Boots skated on bare stone, ropes refused knots, and the eels, who had always navigated by the texture of currents, scattered into a sea gone frictionless and unreadable. [134] After friction disappeared, the village starved slowly and politely, the way coastal people do. [148]

Mereth's grandmother had herded eels before her, and had died the night the dust came down, gripping Mereth's wrist with the last friction left in the world. [175] Promise me you will learn the sacred patterns, she had said, not memorize, learn, because the sea does not keep secrets, it keeps mathematics. [199] The promise lingered in Mereth like a splinter that worked deeper every season. [212]

So she climbed the tidal bluff each night, where the aurora burned green and violet over the water, ignited by the same star dust drifting still in the upper air. [242] Up there she had noticed something her curse showed her in fragments: the dust was not lawless. [259] Her visions came as geometry now, spirals and ratios, futures arranged like shells. [272] She began painting rocks with crushed shell and ochre, and placing them along the bluff in mathematical sequences, ones and twos and threes and fives, the old spiral her grandmother had traced on eel skins. [307] The rocks were not decoration; they were an argument addressed to the dust. [320] Where the sequence was correct, the painted stones held their places against the slick wind, and friction crept back into the ground between them like a shy animal. [348] Where she erred, even by one stone, everything slid. [357] The dust, she understood, was not a curse but a question, and it would return grip only to those who answered in its language. [381] She did not want to merely fix the world; she wanted to understand the sacred patterns beneath it, as she had promised. [403]

On the last night, the aurora hung low enough to touch, and her curse offered her two futures. [421] In one, she completed the great spiral and the bluff became an anchor, friction radiating outward, the eels returning to water they could read again. [446] In the other, she misplaced the final stone and watched the whole pattern shear loose into the sea. [464] Her curse had never shown her a choice before; it had only ever shown her certainties. [480] This, she realized, was what it meant to learn rather than memorize: the pattern had grown large enough to include her. [501]

She knelt at the spiral's center as the tide pulled back below, holding the last rock, painted with a single white ring. [523] The dust in the air above her shimmered, leaning toward her arrangement the way iron leans toward a magnet. [542] She was not commanding it; she was harnessing it, the way she had once harnessed eels, not by force but by laying down a shape the world wanted to follow. [572] Her hands shook. [575] In both futures she had seen herself place the stone with steady hands, and her hands were not steady, which meant the futures were guesses now, not sentences. [603] She laughed, a sound she had not made in three winters, and set the stone down by feel instead of foresight. [624]

The spiral closed. [627] Friction did not return like thunder; it returned like dawn, a borrowed dawn, light the aurora lent down to the bluff while the true sun was still hours away. [656] Grass gripped soil. [659] Her boots held the rock beneath them. [666] Far below, the dark water wrinkled, and she saw the eels come in their thousands, a silver river bending to follow the spiral's echo through the shallows. [693]

Mereth stayed on the bluff until the real morning came and made the borrowed one unnecessary. [709] Her curse was quiet, perhaps for the first time, or perhaps it had simply learned what she had learned: that a pattern truly understood is not a prophecy but an invitation. [740] She walked down toward the village to teach them the sequences, no longer elusive, her promise finally kept and still, somehow, lingering, the way the best promises do, asking to be kept again tomorrow. [774]
