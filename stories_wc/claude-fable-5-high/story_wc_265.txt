After the sixth sense awakened, everyone could hear their own thoughts arriving before they thought them, and the world grew very tidy. [22] Tidy minds bored Ezra Vane, who had been a philosopher until philosophy became unnecessary, and who was now simply a stray. [43] He went where the unmapped minds were rumored to hide: the mechanical rainforest that engineers had abandoned a century ago and that had kept growing anyway. [69] Brass canopies dripped warm oil instead of rain, copper vines ticked as they climbed, and the humidity smelled of cinnamon and solder. [91] Ezra had one talent the sixth sense could not predict: he translocated during sleep. [105] Every night he lay down beneath one grove and woke beneath another, miles deeper, as though the forest were reading him and turning the page. [130] Most people would have called this a miracle; in those days, miracles were merely probable, and Ezra had learned to budget for them. [153] What he could not budget for was the small gear. [163] He woke on the ninth morning with it pressed into his palm, no larger than a coat button, its teeth worn smooth on one side as if something had chewed on a single idea for too long. [200] When he held it up, the wind moved through the trees, and he heard the forest speak in dialects. [219] Wind through the iron palms made a low argument; wind through the aluminum ferns made laughter; wind through the tall chrome pines made a sound like a question that refuses to finish itself. [252] Ezra understood, with the certainty of a man who had spent his life waiting to be uncertain again, that the forest was thinking, and that each species of tree was a different mode of thought. [287] This was why he had come: to rediscover the wild frontier of his own mind by walking through someone else's. [307] He followed the dialects for days, using the gear as a compass; when he turned it between his fingers, certain winds grew louder, certain groves called. [333] Each night his sleep carried him along the path the gear suggested, and each morning he woke closer to the center, where the argument, the laughter, and the unfinished question all braided together. [366] At the center he found the Loom Tree. [374] It was enormous, woven rather than grown, its trunk a cable of ten thousand smaller cables, and at its heart spun an axle with one socket empty, exactly the size of a coat button. [408] The forest's voice gathered here, and Ezra felt the hum of insight, that vibration just behind the sternum that means a truth is about to land whether you are ready or not. [440] The truth landed: the forest was not thinking. [448] It was stuck on one thought, looping for a century, and the missing gear was the reason. [465] Ezra laughed at the obviousness of it; the gear had not been a gift but a summons. [482] But when he reached toward the socket, the wind through the chrome pines rose into a warning, and he stopped. [502] He listened the way philosophers used to listen, before answers came pre-delivered. [514] The pines were not asking him to complete the loop; they were asking him to unravel it. [531] A finished mind, the forest had learned, becomes a tidy mind, and tidy minds stop growing. [547] So Ezra did the hardest work of his life with his hands instead of his arguments. [563] He pulled the loose cable at the trunk's seam and began to unravel the loop strand by strand, each freed wire whipping upward to become a new branch, a new species, a new dialect waiting for wind. [600] He kept the gear. [604] Not as a tool, but as a reminder that an answer worn smooth is just a question that gave up. [624] That night he slept at the Loom Tree's roots and, for the first time in years, woke exactly where he had lain down. [647] The forest no longer needed to carry him anywhere; he had arrived at the only frontier that mattered. [665] His sixth sense reached for his next thought and, for the first time since it awakened, came back empty-handed. [684] Ezra grinned into the oily rain. [690] Somewhere above him, the wind found a tree it had never met, and made a sound nobody could predict. [709] He sat down to listen, a stray philosopher with a pocketful of obsolete certainty, and began, at last, to wonder. [729] In those days, miracles were probable; wonder was the rare thing, and he had found it growing wild. [747]
