<story>
Mara had been tuning the ferry lounge's piano when the hull tilted past the point of return. [17] She surfaced clutching the one thing her hands had refused to release: her tuning fork case. [33] The cold water took forty-one passengers and, from her, every sound she would ever hear again. [49] The doctors called it barotrauma compounded by hypothermic nerve damage; she called it the silence. [64] Three months later she accepted a winter residency at the Winterglass Conservatory, a music school grown inside a Victorian greenhouse, its panes furred with frost ferns. [90] She took the residency for one reason: she meant to translate silence into music, or admit that both had drowned with the ferry. [113]

On her first night she opened the case. [121] The forks lay in their velvet grooves, salt-stained but true, and when she struck the A and pressed it to her sternum, the pitch bloomed in her bones exactly as it always had. [154] Unshaken marvels, she thought; the sea had taken everything except vibration itself. [166]

The conservatory's other winter resident was Tomas, a botanist who studied how greenhouse orchids survived cold snaps. [183] His tool was a thermal camera, and one evening he aimed it at her by accident and went still. [202] On his screen, her grief was visible: heat pooled at her throat, cold rivered down her forearms, a storm system of feeling she had told no one about. [230] He wrote on his notepad: you are silently expressive; the body speaks in temperature. [244] Mara stared at the screen and felt the residency's vague hope sharpen into method. [258] If emotion had a heat signature, then silence was not empty; it was a score nobody had learned to read. [278]

They worked through January. [282] Tomas filmed faces; Mara assigned pitches. [288] Warmth gathering at the eyes became rising thirds; the cold flush of fear became a falling tritone; the steady ember of calm became a drone on D. [315] She checked every interval against the forks, striking them and reading their truth through her jaw, her wrist, the table's wood. [336] After noise became impossible, the velvet-lined case held her only honest instruments. [348]

In February a letter arrived from the ferry company's memorial committee. [359] The families of the forty-one would gather at the conservatory, the only venue large enough in the harbor town, and they wanted music. [382] Mara almost refused. [385] Then she remembered the lounge piano, the last instrument she had ever touched with hearing ears, salvaged from the wreck and donated to the conservatory's atrium. [411] She had left it half-tuned when the hull rolled; somewhere inside it, her unfinished work was still wrong. [429] She could not bring back the forty-one, but she could rectify that. [441]

For a week she lived inside the piano's body. [450] She struck each fork, pressed its stem to the soundboard, and laid her palm flat beside the strings, feeling for the slow beating where two vibrations disagreed. [477] When the beating stilled under her hand, the note was true. [488] Tomas watched her work and wrote: you tune by touch better than most tune by ear. [504]

On the night of the memorial, frost crawled the winterglass panes and the families filled the atrium in their dark coats. [525] Tomas mounted the thermal camera above the piano and fed its image to a screen only Mara could see. [544] She had written no piece in advance. [551] The composition would be the room itself, heat-mapped and translated as it happened. [564] She sat at the rectified piano and watched the screen. [574] A hundred faces glowed in oranges and blues: grief banked like coals at one woman's jaw, anger burning white across a young man's brow, and somewhere near the back, the cool violet of a child too small to remember whom she had lost. [617]

Mara played what she saw. [622] Rising thirds for the warming eyes, the falling tritone whenever fear flickered, the drone on D wherever calm finally settled. [642] She heard none of it. [647] She felt it instead, through the bench, through her wrists, through the soundboard she had spent a week making honest. [667] On the screen, the room began to change. [675] Heat spread from face to face like sunrise crossing water, and she answered it, chord by chord, until the music and the mourning were the same shape. [702]

When she lifted her hands, the families rose. [710] She could not hear the applause, but on the screen it blazed, a hundred small fires. [726] Afterward she closed the tuning fork case and ran her thumb along its salt-scarred lid. [741] Some things the sea could not detune. [748] She had gone under as a tuner of pianos; she had surfaced, slowly, as a translator of silence, and the silence, it turned out, had always been singing. [776]
