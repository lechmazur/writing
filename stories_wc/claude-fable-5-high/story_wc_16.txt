The archive of extinct languages smelled of cold wax and patient dust, and Mirei had not spoken aloud inside it for three years. [23] She was the last arcane linguist on the institute's payroll, keeper of tongues that no living mouth remembered. [41] Tonight she broke her silence, because tomorrow Aster was leaving. [51] Three years ago, Mirei had promised her mentor she would never speak Veshara, the promise-language, again. [67] Veshara was extinct because it could not lie: every sentence was a vow, and every vow braided itself into every other vow ever spoken. [91] Its speakers had drowned in their own contradictions, strangled by oaths they could not keep. [106] Mirei had spoken it once, recklessly, to prove a theory, and the broken vow had snarled the entire lattice. [125] Aster had taken the blame before the board, and Mirei, ashamed, had answered her mentor's kindness with three years of silence. [146] Now Aster's transfer papers were signed, and at dawn their paths would diverge for good. [161] Mirei could not let her go carrying that snarl, that silence, that debt. [174] On her desk sat the glass ink well, the only surviving artifact of Veshara's scribes. [189] Its ink never dried, because the vows suspended in it had never been resolved. [203] Held to lamplight, the dark liquid showed threads, thousands of them, knotted around the one ugly tangle Mirei had made. [223] Her plan was carefully reckless, which she had decided was the only honest way to work. [239] Reckless, because she would speak the dead language again, in violation of her vow. [253] Careful, because she had spent three silent years mapping every filament of the lattice, learning how each promise anchored its neighbors. [274] You could not cut a knot in Veshara; cutting was lying, and lying collapsed the weave. [290] You could only unsnarl it, vow by vow, by speaking new promises that gave the old ones somewhere true to go. [311] She dipped her pen and began. [317] "I promise to finish what I broke," she said, writing the glyph as she spoke. [332] The ink shivered, and one filament in the tangle loosened, but only if a second promise could hold its weight. [352] "I promise to tell Aster the theory was mine alone." [362] A second thread eased, dragging a third taut, which demanded a third vow, which demanded a fourth. [379] This was the architecture of Veshara: nothing stood alone, and repair happened only via interconnected promises, each one a rafter resting on the next. [403] Hour after hour she spoke, and the archive began, very faintly, to sing. [416] Each kept vow rang a single clear tone; each tone tuned the threads beside it; the old scribes had called these resonances symphonies of truth, the sound a community made when no word in it was false. [453] Mirei had read about the music; she had never believed she would hear it. [467] She laughed once, then pressed her knuckles to her mouth, exuberant and terrified at once, because a false note now would silence everything forever. [491] Near dawn, one knot remained, and it would not move. [501] It was her original vow, the promise never to speak Veshara again, now contradicted by the whole night's work. [520] She could not resolve it alone; it had been made to Aster, and only Aster could release it. [538] The door opened. [541] Aster stood there in traveling clothes, drawn down through the stacks by music no archive should make. [558] "You're speaking it," Aster said. [563] "I'm fixing it," Mirei said, the first words she had given her mentor in three years. [579] "I stayed silent because I thought silence was safer than another broken word." [592] "But silence is just a vow with no one holding the other end." [605] She held out the pen. [610] "Release me from the old promise, and make me a new one, and the lattice closes clean." [627] Aster studied the glass ink well, where the threads now lay almost smooth, one stubborn knot pulsing at the center. [647] "And if I make a promise I can't keep?" [656] "Then make one you can," Mirei said. [663] "That's the whole grammar." [667] Aster dipped the pen. [671] "I release you," she said in careful Veshara, "and I promise to write to you from wherever I land, every month, until one of us stops answering." [698] "I promise to answer," Mirei said. [704] The last knot slid open like a breath finally exhaled. [714] The ink rang, every thread in tune, and for one long moment the extinct language archive was not a tomb but an instrument, sounding a chord no one had heard in four hundred years. [748] Then the music settled into the walls, the ink in the glass well finally began to dry, and dawn came. [768] They walked together to the station, talking the whole way, spending their last shared hour before their paths diverged on the only currency Veshara had ever honored: words meant entirely.
