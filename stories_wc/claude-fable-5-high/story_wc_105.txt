The greenhouse had gone down with the harbor a generation ago, and the apple trees never noticed. [17] They blossomed under thirty feet of green water, ringed by shipwrecks that leaned against the glass like old men listening at a door. [40] Tamsin Wrey climbed down the airlock ladder every morning to work among them, because the drowned orchard was the quietest place left on the coast. [65] She designed hearing aids, and quiet was her raw material. [75] Hers were not ordinary devices. [80] A standard aid amplifies the present; Tamsin's were built to imprint, to press a remembered sound into a damaged ear the way a seal presses wax. [106] People came to her for the voices of dead mothers, the creak of demolished houses, the laugh they had stopped being able to hear years before the hearing went. [135] Memory, she had learned, was not stored in sound but in the shape sound left behind. [151] So she collected absences. [155] Hung from the orchard branches were dozens of small brass bells with their clappers removed, recording every ring they failed to make. [177] From those negatives she could cast almost anything. [185] Almost. [186]

In October a man named Joa Reyes rowed out to her airlock with a folded note, because he could not hear her answer a knock. [211] The note said: I want the sound of trust breaking. [221] Twenty years earlier, his brother had scuttled their fishing boat for the insurance money while Joa slept in the forward berth. [242] The blast took his ears before it took the hull. [252] He had survived everything except the not-knowing, the lifetime spent imagining a moment he had never actually heard. [270] The boat was still down there. [276] It was one of the wrecks ringing her greenhouse, a trawler called the Verity, lying on her side with her name still legible. [299] Tamsin almost refused him. [303] Then she thought of her own ledger of absences and signed the contract. [316]

The work needed living tissue. [321] Imprinting only takes in cells that are still becoming themselves, so she cultured a graft of Joa's own cochlear cells in a warm dish, and everything afterward had to happen while those cells divided, a window of one autumn night. [361] First, the absence. [364] She dove to the Verity at slack tide and hung a clapperless bell in the engine room where the charge had been set. [387] For nine days it recorded everything the wreck refused to say. [398] When she played the negative back, she expected an explosion-shaped hole. [409] Instead the deepest absence was small: a held breath, a hatch eased shut by someone trying not to wake his brother. [430] Trust does not break loudly, the recording said. [438] It breaks at the volume of someone deciding you are not worth waking. [451]

Then, the casting. [454] On the headland above the harbor stood the old shipwrights' forge, cold for forty years, its firebox full of ash from every keel ever bent on that coast. [482] Tamsin only fired it on aurora nights. [489] She had been born hearing colors, faintly, the way you hear a neighbor's radio through a wall, and the aurora gave her a thin green chord to tune against. [518] The resonating shell of an imprinting aid must be forged to a pitch, and pitch, for her, was a matter of light. [540] That night the sky burned green and violet while the cells divided in their dish beside the anvil, and she drew the alloy from the coals again and again, listening to its color. [573] Cherry red sang too high; straw yellow, too forgiving. [582] At the exact dull orange of a hatch hinge in lantern light, the metal hummed the same note as the wreck's silence, and she quenched it in the ash of forty years of other people's labor. [618] She seated the graft, sealed the shell, and pressed the absence into the dividing cells like a thumbprint into bread dough. [639] By dawn the window had closed and the aid was done, a battered little marvel of brass and bone. [658]

Joa wore it for the first time in the orchard, under the glass, with the Verity's shadow lying across the apple trees. [680] Tamsin watched him hear it: the breath, the hinge, the smallness. [691] He had spent twenty years braced for thunder, and the truth weighed less than a closing door. [708] He wept, then laughed, then sat a long while saying nothing. [719] "I thought the memory would sink me," he wrote on his slate. [731] "It is more like ballast." [736] Tamsin nodded and said nothing about her own unheard moment, the one she had been circling for years, though that night she finally hung a silent bell outside her childhood house. [767] Memories do not return to us, she had learned; we are remade, cell by dividing cell, into people who can return to them. [790] Above the drowned orchard, the bells rang nothing, beautifully.
