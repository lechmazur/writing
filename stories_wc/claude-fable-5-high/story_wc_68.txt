They call me a philosopher the way you call a rat a tenant: technically accurate, mostly insult. [17] I live in the dry sewer line beneath what used to be the harbor district, and I am not ashamed of it. [39] The plague ended two winters ago, and the Provisional Council ended honesty about a month later. [55] Their story is simple: the old world poisoned itself with its machines and its sea-charts, the sickness was the bill, and salvation lies in forgetting. [80] It is a tidy narrative, and tidy narratives are how you starve a future politely. [95] I keep a competing one, written in silt. [103]

Below my tunnel, the old city lies sunken, drowned when the seawall failed in the chaos years. [120] At low tide I wade down through the access shafts into its ruins, and I dig. [136] Not like a looter, like a reader. [143] Mud keeps better records than councils do. [150] Each flood season lays down a page, and I cut through the sedimentary layers the way a careful man cuts through an argument: one premise at a time. [178] Gray silt from the drowning year. [184] Ash from the burning year. [189] Pale lime from the plague pits. [195] The layers do not flatter anyone, which is why I trust them. [207]

Last week, beneath the ash line, my trowel rang against brass. [218] A cog, small as a coin, toothed like a saint's halo: the escapement gear of a seafarer's chronometer. [236] I knew it because my grandmother was a harbor horologist, and I spent boyhood evenings watching her hands move like quiet law. [258] A chronometer is how sailors once kept time at sea, and time is how they found longitude, and longitude is how a port city ate. [283]

The Council says navigation is what brought the plague ships. [293] The Council is not wrong about that. [300] But the layers say something the Council omits: the lime sits above the ash, and the ash sits above the silt. [321] We drowned and burned before we ever sickened. [329] The plague did not punish our knowledge; the loss of our knowledge invited the plague. [344] When the seawall engineers were exiled as decadent, the wall failed; when the wall failed, the granaries flooded; when the granaries flooded, ships came begging, carrying fever in their holds. [374] Causation, properly ordered, is the most rebellious thing a man can own. [386] I am not a romantic rebel; I throw no torches. [396] I rebel the way arithmetic rebels against a liar: calmly, abrasively, without raising my voice. [411]

Still, for three nights, I agonized over the cog. [420] The Council pays ration scrip for surrendered relics, then melts them in the Forgetting Furnace while people applaud. [438] I am hungry; philosophy does not season broth. [446] Worse, hiding it is a hanging offense now, dressed up as public hygiene. [459] I held the gear to my lamp and let the two stories fight across its teeth. [475] In the Council's story, it is a fang from the machine that bit us. [489] In mine, it is a seed. [495] A city that cannot keep time cannot keep tide tables; a city without tide tables will drown again, and the next lime layer will be ours. [521]

On the fourth morning, a girl named Soot found my tunnel, sent by the dockworkers who still trade me bread for letters. [543] She wanted to know if it was true that her drowned grandfather had been a navigator, because the Council teacher said navigators were criminals. [567] I watched her face hold both stories at once and start to crack. [580] That ended my agonizing. [584]

I took her down through the shafts at low tide and showed her the layers, naming each one without anger or apology. [606] Silt, ash, lime: read upward, child, because mud cannot be bribed. [617] Then I pressed the cog into her palm and taught her its name, its purpose, and the longitude problem it once solved. [639] We are building a chronometer around it now, from salvaged brass and my grandmother's remembered patience. [655] Soot brings other children; I make them measure twice and recite the old escapement ratios until they dream in gear teeth. [676] When the Council's wardens finally come, and they will, they will find a sewer, a crank-mouthed almost-philosopher, and a dozen small hands that already know how to keep time. [705] Methods are not relics; they are verbs, and you cannot melt a verb. [718] Let the Council keep its tidy story above ground. [727] Down here, layer by layer, tooth by tooth, the truer one is winning. [740]
