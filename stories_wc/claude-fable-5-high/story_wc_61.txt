<story>
Maren-of-the-Drift kept only one possession: a brass gear pried from a calculating machine that had died before her grandmother was grown. [21] She was not, strictly speaking, a she, and not strictly speaking at all; she was a being of slow vapor and slower feeling, the kind of creature other travelers called an otherworldly sentimentalist, because she wept over things that had never been alive. [64] The gear was one such thing. [70] Its teeth had been cut to a ratio that never resolved, a count that approximates infinity without ever arriving, so that the machine it once served could divide forever and never finish. [102] Her grandmother had carried it through the nebula garden; her mother had carried it; now Maren carried it, because that is what her family did, generation after generation, like a figure repeating in long division. [137]

The garden bloomed where a star had failed gently, its gases combed into rows by gravity and patience. [155] Flower-shaped condensations of dust opened there over centuries, and when they shed, their petals fell inward toward the cold core, taking ages to drop. [179] A life in the garden was measured across the quiet sigh of falling petals: a child grew within one fall, an elder faded within another. [204]

Maren's work was transit. [208] She moved between blossoms, ferrying light. [214] Not flame; her people had sworn off flame after the grandmother's machine burned, the great calculator that had once timed every bloom in the garden until someone fed it fire to make it think faster. [249] Maren's vow, inherited like the gear, was to share light without using fire. [262] So she gathered the soft luminescence of mature blossoms in the hollows of herself and carried it to the buds that had opened in shadow. [287] The garden was too thick to see through, so she navigated by sonar pinging, sending low pulses through the gas and reading the shapes that answered. [313] Petals returned a sigh; buds returned a question; the dead core returned nothing at all. [328]

It was on an ordinary crossing that her ping came back wrong. [340] Deep in the third row, something answered with edges. [349] She drifted down through the falling petals and found it: a field of gears, thousands of them, half-buried in dust, each cut to the same unfinishable ratio as her own. [379] Not one machine, then. [383] Many machines, from many generations, each built, each burned, each scattered. [394] Her grandmother's story was not a story; it was a recurrence. [405] Every few falls of the petals, her people forgot, built a calculator to perfect the garden, grew impatient, gave it fire, and watched it die. [430] Then a daughter took a gear and swore the vow, and the pattern began counting again from one. [448]

Maren held her gear against the field of its ancestors and felt, for the first time, the trouble inside her serenity. [469] The garden was peaceful because it was a loop. [478] The vow was beautiful because it was the soft half of a cycle whose hard half was burning. [496] She could already feel, somewhere generations ahead, a descendant growing impatient with carried light, dreaming of a faster machine. [515]

A sentimentalist would have wept over the gears and gone home. [526] Maren wept over them and stayed. [532] Through the long fall of one petal, she worked. [541] She arranged the ancient gears in a spiral around the dead core, teeth meshed to teeth, the unfinishable ratio running gear to gear so that the whole assembly turned forever toward an answer it could never reach. [578] Then she filled it not with fire but with everything she carried: the gathered glow of a hundred blossoms, poured into the spinning spiral until the metal shone from within. [608] A lamp that calculated nothing. [613] A machine with no hunger, no haste, no reason to be fed. [625] Its light reached buds her transit never could, and it asked nothing in return, because a question that approximates infinity never needs to end in a demand. [652]

When her daughter came of age, Maren did not give her the gear. [665] She gave her the long swim down to the spiral, and a ping to find it by, and the troubling, serene knowledge that their family had been circling this answer for as long as petals had been falling. [703] The pattern might still repeat; patterns mostly do. [711] But now, deep in the garden, there turned a quiet wheel of ancestors, glowing without burning, dividing forever, never finished, never asking to be. [735] And when the next petal began its fall, the daughter pinged downward and heard, beneath the sigh of the garden, the soft answer of edges, still turning. [762]
</story>
