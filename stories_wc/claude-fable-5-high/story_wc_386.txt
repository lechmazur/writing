The glacier held its dead the way the sky holds borrowed starlight: nothing in it was its own, and everything in it still shone. [24] Mara Voss had been diving the wreck of the Halcyon for three winters, and the drowned had been whispering to her for two. [47] They could not speak to her directly; the cold had taken their voices the way it takes everything, slowly and without apology. [69] Instead they slipped sideways into the dreams of strangers, brief cameos in other people's sleep: a sailor at the edge of a wedding, a girl in a red coat standing in a kitchen that smelled of smoke. [106] So Mara learned to listen at secondhand. [113]

Each evening the villagers of Skeld climbed to the shrine carved into the glacier's flank, a blue-walled chapel of ice where a hundred candles burned in niches, their light passing through the frozen walls like lamplight through windows. [151] They came to mourn the forty souls the Halcyon had taken, and they stayed to tell Mara their dreams. [170] She wrote everything in a salt-stained ledger, and she wove the truth carefully, thread by thread, because dream-testimony tears if you pull it too hard. [195]

The official inquiry had ruled that ice sank the ship. [205] Aldous Crane, who owned the Halcyon and half of Skeld besides, had wept handsomely at the memorial and collected the insurance the following spring. [229] But the dreams disagreed. [233] In the baker's sleep, the girl in the red coat kept appearing beside an oven that roared too loud. [252] In the schoolmaster's, she stood at a window, holding something round and glittering up to a light that was not the moon. [274] Nine dreamers, nine cameos, and in every one of them: heat. [285] Ice does not burn. [289]

Mara laid the accounts side by side like panes of glass, and through them, faintly, she began to see a single room: the purser's cabin, third deck, where the girl, Liv Crane, the owner's own niece, had slept on the night the ship went down. [334] Every dream was a window, and every window faced the same fire. [346]

She dove the next morning. [351] The Halcyon lay under the glacier's tongue, ninety feet down in water so clear it felt like falling through air. [371] The purser's cabin door had been forced from inside; the bulkheads wore scorch marks like old bruises beneath their frost. [391] And on the shelf above the bunk, fused to the wood, sat a snow globe melted into a sagging teardrop of glass, its tiny village slumped into a glittering drift at the bottom. [424] Glass does not sag in icy water. [431] It sags in fire. [435] Mara surfaced with the globe pressed against her chest like a second heart. [448]

That night the village gathered at the shrine for the winter vigil, Crane among them, distributing candles with a benefactor's smile. [469] Mara did not shout. [473] Accusation, she had learned, is a rope that snaps when jerked. [484] Instead she set the melted globe in the central niche, where candlelight poured through its warped glass and threw a trembling village of light across the ice. [511] Then, one by one, she asked the dreamers to speak. [521] The baker described the roaring oven. [527] The schoolmaster described the girl at the window. [535] The harbormaster's widow, voice shaking, described a man in a fine coat carrying a lantern toward the cargo hold, where the manifest, she now realized, had listed crates of wool that no one in Skeld had ever ordered. [573] Empty crates, insured as treasure. [578] Nine windows, opening onto one room. [584]

Crane's smile cracked the way ice cracks, from somewhere deep. [594] "Dreams prove nothing," he said. [599] "No," Mara agreed, lifting the globe so its melted face caught the light. [612] "But glass remembers fire, and forty families deserve to know which one their dead drowned fleeing." [628] She had wired the maritime board that afternoon; the inspectors would dive at first light, and the scorched bulkheads would say the rest. [651] The dreams had only shown her where to look; it was the wreck itself that would expose him. [669] Crane left the shrine alone, and no candle's light followed him out. [681]

Mara stayed after the others had gone. [688] She had not come for justice, exactly; justice was a thing for courts and inspectors. [703] She had come to salvage something pure from the wreckage, one unburned thing, and she found it in the way the candle flames steadied all at once, as if forty held breaths had finally been let go. [740]

That night, for the first time, Liv came to Mara's own sleep instead of a stranger's. [756] The girl stood at a window in her red coat, holding a snow globe that was whole again, snow drifting down on its tiny, unburned village. [782] She did not speak. [786] She only shook it once, smiled, and let the light through. [797]
