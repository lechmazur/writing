Mirei, craftiest of the valley's lantern makers, worked the way others made confessions: slowly, by hand, at night. [18] Her workshop hung at the edge of the lower city, where the dream-gems came down on copper wires from the floating isle. [40] Every sleeper in the valley wore one: a rough stone at the throat that recorded dreams the way wax records a voice. [62] Mirei's craft was polishing them into lenses, so the dreams could be set inside lanterns and burned as light. [81] It was honest work until her son Tomas died, and his shadow detached. [94] That is what shadows do, three nights after a death: they peel up from the floorboards like wet paper and fly toward the glass basilica on the floating isle, to roost among the panes. [128] Mirei watched her son's shadow go and did not weep. [138] Instead she kept his dream-gem, the one custom said she should surrender, and she began to polish it. [156] Each pass of the cloth bared another night of him. [166] She lit the gem in a lantern and his dreams poured over her walls: Tomas flying over the tea terraces, Tomas with hands made of sparrows, Tomas calling her name in a color she had no word for. [204] It was the most beautiful light she had ever made, and it was unbearable, and she could not stop making it. [225] Every dawn, when the lantern guttered out, she folded the night's anguish into an origami crane. [241] She used the paper from his school notebooks, his sums and misspellings creased into wings. [256] It was the only way she knew to give grief a shape small enough to hold. [272] By winter there were nine hundred cranes and almost nothing left of her. [285] Each polishing thinned the gem, and the dreams grew shorter, blurrier, like a voice through water. [301] She understood, with a craftsman's cold clarity, that she was sanding her son away. [315] So she packed the gem, the cranes, and a cloth of polishing rouge, and bought passage on the tether-car to the floating isle. [338] The isle is a strange place to walk. [346] It rises a little every year, shedding ballast stones, while the path to the basilica winds down through terraced tea gardens carved into its inner hills. [372] A pilgrim descends while ascending: with every downward step through the tea rows, the whole isle lifts her higher above the valley. [394] Mirei walked between hedges of white tea blossom, the leaves silvered by altitude, the air tasting of rain that had not yet decided to fall. [419] An old keeper poured her a cup at the last terrace and looked at the bulge of cranes in her satchel. [440] "You kept a gem," the keeper said, not unkindly. [449] "I polished it," Mirei said. [454] "I am a lantern maker. [459] I made him into light, and the light is eating us both." [471] The keeper led her into the glass basilica. [479] It was not a building so much as a held breath: walls of fused dream-glass rising in ribs and roses, every pane murmuring faintly, and in the spaces between panes the shadows of the dead roosted like folded birds. [518] Mirei recognized Tomas at once, a small dark shape pressed against a colorless window, restless, dimming. [534] "The shadow needs its dreams to settle," the keeper said. [544] "You have been burning them in the valley. [552] He has been roosting on empty glass." [559] Here was the torment, laid out in luminance: every night of comfort she had taken had been theft. [577] "Can it still transform?" she asked. [583] "If there is enough left. [588] The work is yours, lantern maker. [594] We only fuse what is brought to a true polish." [604] So Mirei knelt beneath her son's shadow and did the thing she did best, in reverse. [620] Not polishing to take the dreams out, but polishing to give them wholly over: long, surrendering strokes of rouge and cloth until the gem went clear as held water and weighed nothing in her hand. [655] The keeper set it in the furnace mouth, and the gem transformed: it spread, sighing, into a single pane the color of Tomas's last dream, that color she had no word for. [687] His shadow settled against it like a hand fitting a glove and went still, and through the new glass the evening light came down both gentle and merciless, showing her everything. [718] Mirei opened her satchel on the basilica steps. [726] One by one she set the nine hundred cranes loose into the updraft, his sums and misspellings wheeling out over the tea gardens, and the grief did not leave her, but it flew. [759] Walking home, climbing terraces while the isle settled gently lower, she finally understood: some things must go down to be lifted. [780] That night she made a lantern with only a candle in it, and it was enough. [796]
