The cathedral had been abandoned for forty years, but the orchard in its rafters had not. [16] Apple trees rooted in the rotted beams, their branches threading the broken vaults, their leaves drifting down onto the altar like slow green snow. [40] Tamsin Vell, the quartz-eye mender, climbed the scaffold stairs with a bag she had not opened since her daughter died. [60] Behind her came Pia, eleven years old and endearingly feisty, arguing with gravity itself as she climbed. [77]

"You said the trees were dying," Pia called. [85] "They look fine to me." [90]

"Fine is what things look right before they fall," Tamsin said. [101] She had meant it about the beams, but Pia wrote it down anyway, the way children collect accidental wisdom from grandmothers who never intend to give any. [128]

The orchard had been planted by the vow-keepers, the old order who believed promises should be deciduous. [145] Every autumn the town climbed to the rafters, spoke their vows aloud, and let the falling leaves carry the old ones away. [167] A vow renewed each year stayed alive; a vow assumed forever quietly rotted, like a beam nobody checked. [185] Then the timber company bought the cathedral, signed a contract promising to preserve the orchard, and breached it within a season. [206] Their surveyors had cut into three load-bearing trees before the town's lawyers stopped them, and now the wounds were going black. [227] After the breach of contract, the town had won damages but lost its nerve; nobody had climbed up to speak vows since. [249]

Tamsin's quartz eye, fitted after an accident in her thirties, showed her what her living eye could not: the faint stress-shimmer in wood and stone where things were about to give. [280] She turned it on the wounded trees and saw the shimmer running deep, from cut bark down into the rafters themselves. [301]

"If these three die, the roots let go, and the whole roof comes down," she said. [317]

"So mend them," Pia said, as if it were obvious. [327]

"I mend cups and clocks, girl, not trees." [335]

"Your daughter mended trees." [339]

It was true. [342] Mara had been the last vow-keeper, the one who grafted and pruned and coaxed the orchard through droughts, and she had been Pia's mother. [366] Tamsin had climbed here today to assess, to report, to be useful at a safe distance from grief. [384] But Pia was already kneeling at the worst wound, hands hovering, waiting to be taught. [399]

Tamsin opened the bag. [403] It was Mara's, taken from the hospital and never emptied, and Tamsin had forgotten or refused to remember what it held. [424] Inside: a grafting knife, a tin of pitch-wax, a coil of linen binding, packets of seed, and a curl of green silk. [446] Her daughter's hair ribbon, still smelling faintly of apples. [455] Tamsin's living eye blurred; the quartz one, mercifully, could not. [465]

"Pitch-wax seals the wound," she said, her voice steadier than she felt. [477] "Mara taught me that much, back when I pretended not to listen." [489]

They worked through the morning, the mender's hands learning a new trade from a dead woman's tools. [506] Tamsin's quartz eye found the living tissue beneath the blackened bark; the grafting knife pared away the dead; the wax went on warm. [529] When the linen ran out one binding short, Pia held up the ribbon. [542]

"No," Tamsin said, too fast. [547]

"She'd want it holding something together," Pia said. [555] "Not lying in a bag holding nothing." [562]

Tamsin stared at her granddaughter, who had her mother's stubborn jaw and a habit of being right by accident. [581] She took the ribbon and tied it around the last graft, the green silk bright against the dark bark. [600]

"The company men said it was pointless," Pia said. [609] "They said the orchard's finished." [614]

"Then we persist," Tamsin said. [619] "Mending is mostly refusing to agree that a thing is finished." [630] She had meant it as a grumble; Pia wrote it down as gospel. [643]

They came back the next week, and the next. [652] By autumn, two of the three trees showed new growth beneath the wax, and the quartz eye found the stress-shimmer fading from the rafters. [676] When the leaves began to fall, Pia asked why nobody spoke vows anymore. [689]

"Because the company broke its promise, and people decided promises were worthless." [701]

"That's backwards," Pia said. [705] "If a vow can break, that's why you have to keep saying it." [718]

Tamsin laughed, the first real laugh in two years, because the girl had stumbled into the whole creed of the vow-keepers without ever being taught it. [744] So that October, an old woman and a fierce girl stood under the rafters and spoke their vows aloud, alone but loudly, and let the leaves carry the old grief away. [775] Above them, tied to a mended branch, a green ribbon turned in the wind, holding something together. [792]
