<story>
In the mirror world, dawn was not a time but a fabric, and for a hundred years no one had woven it. [22] Yarrow knew this because she was the last memory keeper, though her memories no longer kept themselves. [39] They had scattered the night the sun on the far side of the glass went out, fleeing her skull like sparrows from a burning eave. [64] Now they roosted in objects: a thimble that remembered her mother's hands, a key that remembered a door long melted into shadow. [86] She gathered them the way other people gathered firewood, because in a realm of endless night, remembering was the only heat left. [108]

Her home was the old alchemical orchard greenhouse, a long hall of iron ribs and clouded panes where the alchemists had once grown light-bearing fruit. [133] The trees still stood, silver and leafless, their branches hung with apples gone dark as snuffed lanterns. [150] At the hall's eastern end was the mica window, a single sheet of layered mineral the alchemists had set facing the place dawn used to rise. [176] Mica splits light into leaves, the old texts said; it could take one thread of morning and shuffle it into many. [197] But there was no morning left to split. [205]

The villagers below the orchard hill had stopped speaking to one another. [217] Darkness does that; without shared mornings, there is nothing to agree on, no hour to meet in. [234] Yarrow watched their lamps drift apart like dying embers and understood that the realm was not merely dark but unraveling. [254]

Then, in a cracked teacup, she found a memory that was not hers. [267] It held a song, a weaver's song, the kind grandmothers had hummed while the alchemists worked: thread of rose, thread of gold, pull the morning through the fold. [295] The forgotten songs were the looms, she realized; dawn had never risen here on its own. [311] In the mirror world, dawns were woven by voices, and when the people forgot the songs, the sun simply ran out of cloth. [334]

She went hunting through her hoard. [340] A copper spoon yielded a lullaby; a boot nail gave up a harvest chant; the thimble, when she finally pried it open, released her mother's voice singing the verse that bound all the others. [374] But each song, once sung, left its object cold and empty, and left her emptier. [389] This was the price the texts had warned of: a memory keeper does not own what she keeps; she only holds it until it must be spent. [416] To rekindle the realm, she would have to release everything she had clutched against the dark. [432]

For three nights, which is to say for one long unmeasured night, she carried the dark apples down from the trees and laid them in a wooden cradle before the mica window. [464] Then she did what her mother's voice told her: she swaddled them. [476] Strip by strip she wrapped each cold fruit in cloth torn from her own keeper's robes, binding them snug as newborns, because light, like an infant, will not stay in a body that is not held. [512] Then she began to sing. [517]

The lullaby first, and a spoon's worth of her childhood went out of her like breath on glass. [535] The harvest chant next, and she forgot the taste of every summer she had ever stored. [551] Last, her mother's verse, and as it left her she could no longer say whose hands the thimble had remembered, only that they had been kind. [577]

Inside their swaddling, the apples began to glow. [585] One thread of light slipped free, thin as a hair, and touched the mica window. [600] The layered mineral did what it was made to do: it split the single thread into a thousand, and the thousand into a weave, rose crossing gold, a whole bolt of morning unrolling across the valley. [636]

Below the hill, the drifting lamps stopped. [643] Doors opened. [645] Neighbors who had not spoken in years stood blinking in shared light, and because dawn gives everyone the same hour, they began, awkwardly, to use it together. [672] The woven morning did what mornings do in the mirror world: it knitted the loose stitches of the community back into one cloth. [695]

They climbed the hill to thank the woman in the torn robes and found she could not remember her own name. [716] So they gave her one, and gave her bread, and an old woman taught her a song to hold it all in. [738] Each evening now, the village climbs to the greenhouse and sings the dawns forward, so the songs are never again forgotten. [759] And Yarrow, lighter than she has ever been, keeps nothing now but the morning, which she releases, every day, by letting it go. [782]
</story>
