The memory came back to Sefa on her ninth birthday in this life, whole and terrible: she had once stood on a cold shore while the last great auks shuffled past her boots. [33] After the reincarnation memory, nothing ordinary fit her anymore. [42] She was a contemporary of extinct species, the only mind alive that remembered the smell of auk feathers and the bellow of aurochs, and the remembering made every crowded room feel like a museum of strangers. [78] So she climbed. [81]

The tidal library stood above the clouds on the peak of Brann Tor, anchored to an ancient stone circle whose granite pillars hummed at a frequency older than language. [110] Twice a day the cloud sea rose like a tide and swallowed the lower stacks, and twice a day it withdrew, leaving the shelves beaded with cold light. [138] The librarians shelved their books according to that rhythm: drowned knowledge below, dry knowledge above. [153]

Sefa came carrying one object from her first life, recovered from a collapsed barrow she had somehow known how to find: a brass sundial, green at the edges, its gnomon bent like a broken wing. [188] She had made it, once, with hands that were not these hands. [200]

The head archivist, a stooped woman named Orla, examined the sundial and then examined Sefa with the same long attention. [220] "Awakenings are brittle," Orla said. [225] "Old memories return like frost flowers: beautiful, and shattered by a careless touch." [238] "Most people who remember a past life crack apart trying to hold two selves at once." [254]

"I don't want to crack," Sefa said. [261] "I want to stop needing anyone to tell me which self is real." [274]

Orla led her down to the deepest stack, where the cloud tide had just receded. [289] There, between two granite pillars of the buried circle, stood the Reading Mirror: a sheet of metal polished until it was atomically reflective, every atom of its surface aligned so perfectly that it returned not just light but attention. [328] "Look into it while the stones hum," Orla said, "and you will see whoever is looking, all of them." [347]

Sefa looked. [349] The hum of the ancient stone circle rose through her boots, through her ribs, steady as a held note, and in the mirror her face doubled, then layered. [377] A weathered woman with salt-cracked lips stood behind her eyes: the shore-dweller, the auk-watcher, the one who had hammered a brass disc to track the sun across a dying season. [407] The awakening was brittle, exactly as warned. [414] When Sefa flinched, the older self splintered into fragments of grief, beaches of broken eggs, the silence after the last bellow, and Sefa staggered back, gasping. [440]

"You cannot fight her out of you," Orla said, "and you cannot drown in her either." [456] "You have to coexist." [460]

For a full turning of seasons, Sefa practiced by the hum of the stone circle, the only sound steady enough to hold two lives in one chest. [487] Each dawn she set the brass sundial on the windowsill above the cloud sea and straightened its gnomon by one careful degree, a slow repair her old hands remembered and her new hands learned. [521] When its shadow finally fell true, both of her selves read the hour together, and neither flinched. [538]

She returned to the Reading Mirror at low tide. [547] This time, when the layered face appeared, she held the hum in her ribs like a keel and let the shore-woman's grief move through her without breaking either of them. [577] The mirror, faithful to the atom, reflected one woman, finally, with two lifetimes standing calm inside her. [594]

She understood, then, what her isolation had actually been: not a wall, but an untranslated language. [610] No one alive shared her memories, and no one needed to; the witness did not require a fellow witness to be true. [632] Loneliness had been dependence wearing a mask, waiting for someone to confirm her. [645] Independence was simpler and harder: she confirmed herself. [653]

Sefa stayed at the tidal library and began to write. [663] She wrote the auks as no fossil could give them: the comedy of their walk, the wet black shine of their backs, the way they trusted. [689] She shelved the volume in the lowest stack, where the cloud tide would touch it twice a day, because some knowledge should be drowned and recovered, drowned and recovered, until it learns to survive its own awakening. [726]

On clear mornings, travelers who climb above the clouds find a woman on the library steps with a mended brass sundial, telling time by the sun and stories by the hum of the stones. [760] She does not need them to believe her. [768] She tells the stories anyway, the way a tide returns: not from loneliness, but from fullness. [784]
