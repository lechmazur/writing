In the third generation after violence became impossible, the harbor town of Saltreach had forgotten how to lock its doors, but not its hearts. [24] Maren worked the docks there, and the stevedores called her graceful the way you call a storm beautiful, meaning it with admiration and a little fear. [50] She could swing a crate the weight of a man across a gangplank without breaking stride, and she could end a conversation with the same economy. [76] What she carried in her coat pocket was older than the Quieting: a pirate's whalebone scrimshaw, etched by her great-grandmother with an island that appeared on no chart. [104] The old woman had been the last raider of the western water, and when raiding ended forever, when fists simply stopped landing and blades turned soft as kelp in human hands, she had carved instead. [139] She carved Vael, the island that detaches from the seafloor and rises only when a whale calls it up from the deep, and she carved it from memory, because she had been born there. [173]

The scrimshaw had a property nobody could explain: whoever held it spoke the truth their heart had guarded longest. [192] Maren had learned this the hard way, handing it to Tomas one evening to admire the carving. [209] "I love you and I am terrified you are leaving," he had said, flat and astonished, and then thrown the bone at her like an accusation. [235] It could not hurt her, of course. [242] Nothing thrown in anger landed anymore; the scrimshaw curved gently in the air and settled into her palm like a gull coming home. [265] That was how they fought, she and Tomas: all the fury of lovers, with none of the harm available, so the fury had nowhere to go but tenderness. [293]

He followed her down to the water on the night of the lowest tide of the year. [310] She walked past the wrack line, past the barnacled posts, out to the sublittoral fringe, that drowned margin where the kelp begins and the land admits it was always only borrowed from the sea. [344] Cold water ringed her thighs. [349] "You don't even know it's real," Tomas shouted from the shallows, furious and gentle at once, the only register left to anyone. [371] "I know what I am," she said. [378] "The Quieting took the war out of us, Tomas, but it did not put anything back." [394] "My grandmother spent forty years on these docks pretending the sea was just her job." [409] "I lift crates so I don't have to feel how easily I could swim instead." [424]

She held the scrimshaw out over the dark water and felt it grow warm. [438] Under her thumb, the etched island seemed to loosen on its carved roots. [451] Then the call came. [455] It rose from the deep beyond the shelf, a whale's voice so low it arrived through her bones before her ears, a sound like a door being unbarred in a house the size of the ocean. [491] Far out, the horizon changed shape. [497] Vael detached from the seafloor the way a held breath finally leaves a chest, and it rose, streaming, green-shouldered, real. [517]

Tomas waded out and gripped her arm, and because his hand could no longer be cruel, it could only be honest. [538] "Then take me with you," he said, and it sounded like a challenge, like a fight he intended to win by surrendering everything. [561] "You'd drown," she said. [565] "Then I'll drown angry and in love, which is the only way anyone does anything now." [581] She pressed the scrimshaw into his fist instead. [589] His guarded heart opened on contact, and what spilled out was not pleading but permission. [604] "Go," he said, hating it, meaning it. [611] "Go be the thing the crates were hiding." [619]

Maren kissed him with all the violence the world no longer permitted, which is to say with none, which is to say with everything else. [644] Then she dove past the kelp, past the fringe where land surrenders, and her body remembered what it had always been: not a worker who moved like water, but water that had been working. [678] The whale called once more, and the island answered, and Maren answered, all three of them loose at last from their moorings. [700] On the shore, Tomas held the scrimshaw and let it tell him his own truths until dawn. [717] When Vael sank again at sunrise, it took her with it, and the carving in his hand changed: a small graceful figure now stood on the etched cliffs, facing the sea, finally home. [750]
