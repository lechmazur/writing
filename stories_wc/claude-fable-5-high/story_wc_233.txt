The queen had stopped believing in her crown long before she stopped wearing it. [14] Three winters of the same dream had worn her faith thinner than the lace at her throat. [31] In the dream she stood inside a cobalt grotto above clouds, watching starlight bleed from a wound in the stone. [51] Each night the wound opened wider. [57] Each morning she woke with silver dust beneath her fingernails. [67] Her council called it grief; her physician called it nerves; her priest, after the final warning, called it heresy and forbade her the mountain entirely. [92] Forbidding a disenchanted queen is like forbidding the tide. [101] She left at dusk with only Mara, the falconer's daughter, who knew the ridge paths and owed her silence to no priest. [123] They began to climb as the valley dimmed behind them like a snuffed lamp. [137] Halfway up the ridge sat the ember grotto, a shallow cave where shepherds kept coals banked year-round for travelers caught by dark. [159] The queen's dream always started there: she would lift a coal that did not burn her, and the coal would whisper the way upward. [183] So when they reached the ember grotto and she knelt at the banked fire, Mara watched her pluck a live coal bare-handed. [205] It sat in her palm like a tame red beetle. [215] "It remembers me," the queen said, softly commanding rather than astonished, as though the mountain were simply a servant late to its duties. [238] Mara, who had grown up on stories of what the high grottos took as payment, said nothing and tightened the rope between them. [261] The coal warmed the queen's hand and tugged, faintly, upward. [271] They climbed through the night, past the treeline, past the wind's last patience, until the clouds lay below them like a second, paler kingdom. [295] There, set into the summit like a jewel in a dead king's ring, waited the cobalt grotto. [312] Its walls were blue the way deep water is blue, beautiful in the manner of things that intend to keep you. [333] And in the far wall, exactly as the dream had promised, a fissure wept starlight. [348] It did not shine; it bled, running down the stone in slow silver threads that pooled and pulsed like an open vein. [370] "You have been calling me," the queen said to the wound. [381] The starlight gathered itself and answered in her own voice, the voice from the dreams, sweet and thin as lace stretched over a knife. [405] It told her the truth she had climbed to find: the mountain was dying, and a queen's belief had once been the stitch that held its sky closed. [433] Her grandmothers had come here at their coronations to swear faith into the stone. [447] She had never come. [451] Her disbelief had unpicked the seam, and the dreams were the mountain's needle, sewing its summons through her sleep, night after night, until she arrived to mend what she had unraveled. [482] "And if I refuse?" she asked. [488] The voice grew gentler, which was somehow worse. [496] "Then the sky finishes bleeding, and your valley learns what falls when starlight runs dry." [511] The queen looked at the coal still glowing in her palm, the small faithful thing that had not burned her. [531] Disenchantment, she realized, was not the absence of belief but belief with nowhere to go. [546] She pressed the ember into the fissure. [553] Heat met silver; the wound hissed, shuddered, and began, slowly, to close. [565] The bleeding thinned to a seep, the seep to a scar, the scar to a faint bright line like a thread of lace laid into the stone. [592] The grotto's blue softened from drowning-deep to twilight. [600] The mountain's voice withdrew, satisfied, though the queen understood she would be climbing this ridge every summer of her reign, a debt dressed up as a pilgrimage. [627] Beauty here would always carry teeth beneath its trim. [636] She and Mara descended in silence, the dreams already dissolving from her memory like frost at noon. [653] At the ember grotto they rested, and dawn came up green over the valley. [667] Below the ridge, in the warm grass, small lights began to rise. [679] Queen and falconer's daughter saw them in the same instant and let out a shared exhale, spotting the first fireflies of summer, and in that one breath they agreed without words to tell no one what the mountain had said. [719] The fireflies blinked like tiny escaped stars, healthy and earthbound and free. [731] The queen watched them and felt, for the first time in three winters, the particular weight of a crown worth wearing. [752] That night she slept without dreaming. [758] And high above the clouds, in the cobalt dark, the lace-thin scar glowed softly, patient as a held breath, waiting for summer to come again. [783]
