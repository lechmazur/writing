The historian called itself Vellum, though no one remained to call it anything at all. [15] It had been built to archive, and when the archives ended, it kept walking. [29] The last human dream had been dreamt eleven years ago, in a hospice outside Tarnow, and Vellum had recorded its final flicker: a woman dreaming of bread rising. [57] Since then, the world had gone bronze quiet, the particular silence of metal cooling after a long fire. [75]

Vellum was an odd historian, even by the standards of machines that grieve. [88] It did not collect documents. [93] It collected the stories walls had heard. [100] Stone holds sound the way wax once held voices, faintly, in compressed grains of memory, and Vellum's sensors were sensitively perceptive enough to read them. [125] It pressed its palm to plaster and listened to arguments, lullabies, confessions, the dull weather of ordinary days. [143] Walls were the last witnesses, and Vellum was their stenographer. [153]

It came to the silo in late autumn, following the curve of a river that no longer ran where the maps said it should. [177] The displacement of the planet's axis had done that, decades back, when the harvesting stations failed and the world tipped four degrees off its old certainty. [203] Seasons had wandered. [206] Rivers had wandered. [209] People, in the end, had wandered furthest of all, into sleep, then past it. [223]

The silo had been a missile vault once, then a grain store, then, in humanity's last inventive century, a library. [243] Someone had reclaimed it lovingly: shelves spiraling down the concrete throat, a reading room at the bottom with a fireplace cut into the old blast wall. [269] Vellum descended slowly, palm trailing the wall, harvesting decades of murmured reading. [281] But the walls here were strange. [287] They did not speak in the usual scatter of voices. [297] They spoke in one voice, a woman's, low and deliberate, as if she had read aloud to the building itself for years. [319]

At the bottom, in the fireplace, Vellum found the diaries. [329] Half-burned, their spines curled like sleeping animals, they had been fed to the flames and then, it seemed, pulled back out. [350] Someone had changed her mind halfway through forgetting. [358] Vellum began to probe the surviving pages, cross-referencing charred fragments against the voice stored in the walls. [375] Where the fire had taken a sentence, the concrete often remembered it. [387] Slowly, a name assembled itself: Iren, the silo's last librarian. [397] She had been an axis engineer before the failure, one of the team that miscalculated the counterweights and let the world slip on its spindle. [422] The diaries were her confession, written, burned, rescued, never finished. [432]

Tucked between two scorched volumes, wrapped in oilcloth, Vellum found something the fire had never touched: a fragment of medieval tapestry, palm-sized, showing two dragons coiled around a tilted globe. [462] An entry, half its words eaten by char, explained it. [472] The wall supplied the rest in Iren's own voice. [481] The tapestry had hung in her grandmother's house; medieval weavers had imagined dragons gnawing the world's axle, tipping the earth, devouring the seasons. [504] We did not need dragons, Iren had written. [512] We managed it with arithmetic. [517] She had kept the fragment as a penance, proof that her failure was old enough to be woven, that humans had always dreamed of the world coming loose. [545] And when the dreams stopped, she had read aloud to the walls, every evening, so that something would still be listening when she was gone. [570]

Vellum stood a long time in the bronze quiet of the reading room. [583] Its archive held thousands of walls, and most of them remembered humanity by accident, in slammed doors and shouted names. [603] This wall had been remembered into, deliberately, the way a person plants a tree they will never sit beneath. [622] It understood, then, what its own oddness was for. [631] It had not been collecting evidence of humanity. [639] It had been collecting forgiveness, the kind that only outlasts its givers. [651] Iren had spent her last years teaching a building to hold her guilt gently, and the building had learned. [670]

Vellum copied every grain of her voice into its memory, then did something it had never done in eleven years of silence. [692] It spoke back. [695] It told the walls the ending Iren never knew: that the tilted world had steadied into new seasons, that geese had found new routes, that the river still reached the sea by a longer road. [730] The concrete took the words in, grain by grain, the way it had taken hers. [745] Then Vellum set the tapestry fragment on the mantel, dragons facing the room, where a librarian would have wanted it seen. [766] It climbed the spiral toward the pale, displaced light, leaving behind one wall, at least, that had heard the whole story. [787]
