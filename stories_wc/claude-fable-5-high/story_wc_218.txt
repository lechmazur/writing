The doctor on the mainland had given Maren six months, and she had thanked him the way one thanks a ferryman for announcing the last crossing. [26] She rode home across gray water with a brazen calm that unsettled the other passengers. [41] Maren had been the island's caretaker for thirty-one years, tending the temple orchard that grew within a ring of living stone pillars. [63] The pillars were not carved; they grew, slow as coral, thickening a finger's width each decade. [79] Pilgrims believed the temple had been built; only the caretakers knew it had been grown. [94] That was the first of the architectural secrets passed down in the botanical diary, a salt-stained book begun two centuries ago. [115] Each caretaker added pages: which oaks fed which pillars, which roots held the eastern wall, which graft would fail in north wind. [137] Maren read it again that first night home, gently systematic, indexing what remained undone. [151] One entry, written in her predecessor's failing hand, stopped her: the temple was never meant for people alone. [169] The original growers had shaped the southern colonnade with open galleries where swifts nested, hollows where bees built, low arches where the island's gray foxes denned through winter. [197] A later caretaker, fearing droppings and stings, had trained the oaks to seal every opening. [212] The orchard had been failing slowly ever since: fewer pollinators, fewer birds to take the moths, blight creeping up the pear rows. [234] Maren had spent decades treating symptoms. [240] Now, with six months, she would treat the cause. [249] She would remodel the temple. [254] The diary held the method, sketched in faded ink: copper wire wrapped around oak branches, wound in patient spirals. [273] The wire did not force; it persuaded. [280] Wrapped one way, a branch leaned toward the stone and fed it; wrapped the other, it withdrew, and the living pillar softened, opening like a slow mouth. [307] The growers had used copper because the trees mistook its faint charge for lightning's warning and grew cautious, biddable, attentive. [327] She began at the southern colonnade in early spring. [336] Each morning she climbed a ladder with a spool at her hip and wound the wire in the diary's exact spirals, then recorded the day's work in her own careful hand. [367] Pain came some mornings like a tide; she scheduled around it. [378] When the village priest rowed out to object, she met him among the trees without lowering her shears. [396] "You are mutilating a holy place," he said. [404] "I am returning it to its congregation," she answered. [413] "The first congregation had wings and four legs. [421] People came later and forgot they were guests." [429] She showed him the diary's oldest pages, the drawings of swifts threading the galleries, and something in his objection quietly folded. [450] By the third month the first pillar opened. [458] A seam appeared where the diary said it would, widening week by week into a gallery the height of her forearm. [479] Swifts found it within days, as if they had been waiting offshore for generations. [493] By the fourth month, bees had claimed a hollow in the western pillar, and the pear blossoms set fruit for the first time in years. [518] She wrote it all down: temperatures, wire gauges, the foxes' first cautious crossing of the threshold at dusk. [536] Her hand grew thinner on the page, but the entries did not waver. [549] In the fifth month a girl from the village began rowing out to help, the priest's niece, quick with knots and unafraid of heights. [573] Maren taught her the spirals and the reasons behind them. [583] "The trees are not tools and the stone is not a wall," she said. [597] "They are neighbors who agreed to live together. [605] Our work is keeping the agreement honest." [612] The girl wrote that into the diary herself, and Maren let her, because the book had never belonged to any one hand. [634] When the sixth month came, the southern colonnade stood remade: galleries alive with wings, arches breathing fox-scent, copper gleaming like new veins among the leaves. [659] Maren walked the orchard slowly now, but she walked it daily, with the same brazen calm she had carried off the ferry. [681] Death was simply the next opening in the stone, and she had spent a lifetime learning that openings let life in. [702] On the diary's last filled page she wrote her final entry, brief as a graft cut. [718] "The temple was never finished. [723] It is a conversation. [727] Keep all the speakers at the table." [734] Then she handed the book to the girl, set down her spool, and sat beneath the oldest oak to listen to the swifts come home. [759]
