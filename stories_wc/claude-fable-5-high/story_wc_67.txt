The Vanishing Point Gallery occupied the narrow end of a converted railway station, its walls angled so that every framed absence seemed to rush toward a single distant door. [29] Mireille kept no finished work there. [35] Only empty frames hung in receding rows, because everything she drew, she burned. [48] She was a charcoal portrait ash scatterer, the last of a strange trade: she drew the dead from the memories of the living, then reduced each likeness to powder and gave the grieving a small tin of what remained. [87] The man arrived at dusk carrying a mask made from shattered porcelain shards, the pieces wired together so the cracks caught the lamplight like lightning frozen mid-strike. [114] "My wife's wedding china," he said. [120] "She broke every piece the night before she died, and I built this so I would not have to remember her face doing it." [144] He had worn it at the funeral, he admitted, behind a veil, and the smooth wrongness of it had felt like shelter. [166] Mireille recognized the mirage of safety when she saw it; half her clients arrived wearing one, though rarely so literally. [186] "Then you want her last face," she said. [194] "I want to know what she felt," he answered. [203] "It lasted one second, and I looked away, and I have been looking away for a year." [220] This was her true work, the thing no photograph could do: to crystallize a fleeting emotion, to catch the single second a face told the truth before the living flinched from it. [252] She led him past the empty frames to her studio, where an old rocking chair waited beside the easel. [271] "I work while it rocks," she explained, setting it in motion with one hand. [285] "When it stops, the portrait is finished, whatever it has become. [296] Grief should not be given time to start lying." [305] He talked while the chair swung its slow pendulum, but he could only give her fragments. [321] Her hair was dark, or had been; she hummed when angry; the plates had not been thrown at him but down, at the floor, at something beneath them both. [350] Mireille worked the way she always worked, through incomplete information, trusting the gaps more than the claims. [367] A mourner's memory is a witness bribed by love; she had learned to weave truth carefully from what people refused to say. [389] He said his wife was never afraid, so Mireille drew fear's first cousin, vigilance, into the set of the jaw. [409] He said the marriage was safe, and the word came out of him like a stone, so she shaded the eyes of a woman who had built a shelter and then discovered she was the only one keeping the rain out. [450] Safety, Mireille thought, was the kindest mirage of all; people walked toward it for whole decades and never noticed the horizon retreating. [472] The chair's arc grew shorter. [477] He clutched the porcelain mask in his lap, thumb riding a crack, and at last said the thing the gaps had been pointing to all evening. [503] "She wasn't angry that night. [508] She looked relieved. [511] She had finally decided to leave me, and the doctors found her heart the next morning, and I will never know if I was the weight she was putting down." [541] Mireille's stick of charcoal moved once more, lightening the mouth, lifting it, letting relief and sorrow share the same lips. [561] As the old rocking chair finally slowed to a stop, she set down the charcoal and turned the easel. [580] He made a sound that was not quite weeping, something closer to recognition, the noise a man makes when a door he believed was a wall turns out to open. [610] "That second," he whispered. [614] "You caught it." [617] "Now watch it go," she said, because this was the contract. [628] She carried the portrait to the iron brazier, lit the corner, and let the fire take the face neither of them had invented and neither of them had wholly known. [658] When the flames finished, she crushed the cooled remains with a marble pestle, powdering them to a gray softness finer than the charcoal they had once been. [685] She funneled the ash into a tin and pressed it into his hands. [698] "Scatter it somewhere she chose," Mireille said. [705] "Not somewhere safe. [708] There is no such place, and she knew it before either of you." [721] He left the porcelain mask on the seat of the rocking chair, face up, its cracks shining. [738] Mireille watched him walk the gallery's long perspective, growing smaller between the empty frames until the vanishing point accepted him, a man carrying one second of truth in a tin. [768] She felt it again, a grave astonishment: nothing could be kept, and that was why anything shone. [785] Behind her, the empty chair held its broken face toward the dark. [797]
