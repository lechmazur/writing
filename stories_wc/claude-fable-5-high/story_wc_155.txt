Imka Vell painted with light, which meant she owned no brushes, only lenses, prisms, and a coat with forty pockets full of colored glass. [24] Her vivid murals lived on walls only while she stood before them, throwing projections that made strangers stop and, briefly, forget to be tired. [48] Lately, though, even her brightest work felt like an apology for something she could not name. [64] Joy had gone out of her the way warmth goes out of stone after sundown: slowly, and then completely. [83] So when a salvage dealer sold her a fresco fragment dug from the edge of the old orchard district, she bought it without haggling. [107] The plaster showed a child's hand reaching for a painted apple, and somehow the apple seemed to be laughing. [126] Everyone knew the orchard district was gone, swallowed by a thick fog that had not lifted in three centuries. [145] Maps drew it as a gray smudge; grandmothers drew it as a warning. [158] Imka took the fragment to the knowledge network instead, the candlelit hall where archivists linked their ledgers, letters, and memories into one murmuring web. [182] An old indexer named Bram traced the fragment's pigment to a festival mural: the Apple Laugh, painted every autumn until the fog came. [205] "People walked into that fog smiling," Bram said, "and the ones who wandered out had forgotten why." [222] He slid a tin across the table: exposed film, pulled decades ago from a camera found at the district's border. [242] "Ruined, of course," he said, "light got in." [250] Imka laughed for the first time in months, a small startled sound. [262] Light getting in was her entire profession. [269] That night she went to the fog's edge, where dead apple trees stood in rows like patient gray ghosts. [288] She set her lanterns, loaded the exposed film into a projector built from scavenged lenses, and shone the ruin of it straight into the mist. [313] The fog, she had guessed, was not weather at all but a vast unfixed emulsion, three centuries of unprocessed light hanging in suspension. [336] The ruined film was its negative, and the fog began to develop. [348] What happened next happened within the fold of a daydream, the way she later told it, because no other grammar fit. [369] She did not walk into the orchard district; she drifted into its remembering. [382] Around her, the mist resolved into ladders and bushel baskets, into lanterns strung between branches heavy with fruit. [400] Translucent harvesters moved through the rows, laughing at jokes three hundred years old, and the jokes were still funny. [419] She found the wall where the Apple Laugh had been painted and saw the gap her fragment had left, a missing tooth in a wide grin. [445] She lifted the plaster piece from her satchel and fitted it home. [457] The mural completed itself like a held breath finally released. [467] A girl made of mist, the same child whose hand reached for the painted apple, turned and looked at Imka with grave, amused eyes. [491] "You forgot how to be glad," the girl said, not unkindly, "so you came to borrow some." [508] "I came to steal it outright," Imka admitted, and the girl's laugh shook apples from a tree that no longer existed. [529] The daydream began to thin at its edges; daydreams always do. [540] But Imka understood, in the pensive way one understands things while half asleep, that joy was never lost, only undeveloped, waiting in suspension like the fog itself. [567] She woke at the orchard's edge at dawn with dew in her hair and the fragment gone from her satchel, given back where it belonged. [592] The fog had thinned to a silver gauze, and through it she could see, for the first time in living memory, the shapes of trees. [617] A month later, Imka mounted her grandest display on the city's eastern wall, facing the orchard district. [634] She projected the Apple Laugh whole: the child, the fruit, the impossible grin, painted in nothing but light through forty pockets of colored glass. [658] Bram came from the knowledge network with his fellow archivists, who wrote down nothing that night and remembered everything. [677] Strangers stopped, and forgot to be tired, and a few of them laughed at a joke three hundred years old. [697] Imka stood among them, thoughtful and grinning at once, holding her smallest lens up to the sky. [714] Somewhere behind the thinning fog, she felt sure, an orchard was developing slowly into morning, and she intended to be there for the harvest. [738]
