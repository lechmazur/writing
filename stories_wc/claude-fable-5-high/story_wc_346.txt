The lighthouse did not appear on any chart, because its light was not for ships. [15] Mara climbed its spiral stairs at dusk, as she had every dusk for forty years, to adjust the doomsday clock. [35] The clock lived in the lamp room where a lens should have been, a tower of gears within gears, each wheel turning a smaller wheel, each smaller wheel turning one smaller still. [67] Its hands measured how much world was left. [75] Tonight they stood one breath from midnight. [82]

In her coat pocket she carried a broken puppet head, cracked down the cheek, one painted eye worn to wood. [102] It had belonged to her daughter, Lis, who had died in the first collapse, when the clock first lurched toward its end. [124] Lis had used it to play a game: the puppet sang a lullaby, and the lullaby put the whole pretend world to sleep, gently, so nothing in it would be afraid of the dark. [158] Mara had not been able to save Lis. [166] She had only been able to save the song. [175]

Her work was simple and terrible: each dusk, she wound the clock back as far as it would allow, buying the world another night. [199] But the allowance had shrunk for years, and tonight the key turned and caught on nothing. [215] The mechanism had reached its last tooth. [222] Time had one ripple remaining, and she stood inside it. [232]

She did not weep. [236] She had expected this dusk her whole adult life, and still it arrived like a hand closing slowly around her heart. [257] Then she noticed what the clockmakers had never documented. [266] The clock was beautifully flawed. [271] One gear, the ninth in the ninth nest of wheels, was cut a half-tooth wrong, so that once per cycle the entire mechanism hesitated. [295] In that hesitation, all sound stopped. [301] Not silence as absence, but silence as a surface: a pane of hush, thin and clear as glass, hanging in the lamp room where the light should have turned. [330] Mara reached toward it and felt the hush hum against her palm, like a window with the whole of time pressed to its other side. [355]

She understood then what the flaw was for. [363] The clock did not only count the world down. [372] It listened. [374] Whatever was given to the pane during the hesitation would be carried inward, through nested patterns, gear into gear, wheel into wheel, scale into smaller scale, until it was written into the grain of time itself. [410] And whatever lay in time's grain would surface again wherever time began again. [423]

She could not stop midnight. [428] No adjuster ever could; that had never been the job, however the job had been named. [444] The job was this: to choose what the ending would carry. [455]

Mara took the broken puppet head from her pocket and set it on the rail facing the pane, its worn eye catching the last red light of dusk. [483] Her voice was rusted. [487] She had not sung since the first collapse. [495] But the song was still there, under everything, the way Lis was still there, under everything. [511] She sang the lullaby into the pane of hush. [520] Sleep now, little world, nothing in the dark will hurt you. [531] The hush took the melody the way still water takes a stone: a circle, then a circle within the circle, then circles all the way down past seeing. [559] The ninth gear caught its half-tooth and turned. [567] She felt the song repeating inside the mechanism at every scale, each gear teaching it to the smaller gear, the pattern nesting itself toward the infinitely small, where endings stop meaning anything. [599]

This was not a rescue. [604] It was something older and humbler. [610] It was what she could vouchsafe to a thousand lifetimes she would never see: not warning, not knowledge, only the assurance that someone, once, had loved them in advance. [639]

Midnight arrived without sound. [643] The last ripple of time moved through the lighthouse the way dusk moves through a room, unhurried, dimming nothing it did not have to dim. [668] Mara stayed where she was, hand on the rail beside the puppet head, and across the hush of time's final ripple she heard her own lullaby returning to her, small and many, from every nested wheel at once. [706] It sounded like a thousand small voices learning a song. [716] It sounded like Lis. [720]

What she felt then had no clean name. [728] It was awe with a bruise in it, grief and wonder pressed into one ache, the feeling of watching the world end and begin in the same breath. [756] Somewhere far ahead, in a lifetime not yet wound, a new world would stir in its first darkness and hear, without knowing why, that it was already safe. [784] Sleep now, little world. [788] Nothing in the dark will hurt you. [795]
