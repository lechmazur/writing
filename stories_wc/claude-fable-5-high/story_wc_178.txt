The ministry had stopped paying salaries in the spring, and by autumn no one could say which ministry would replace it. [21] Tarisa stayed anyway. [24] She was an astronomer of careful habits, mending her own boots and rationing lamp oil, and the observatory cost her nothing but patience. [47] The observatory itself was a strange inheritance: a Babylonian palace rebuilt a century ago by a king who wanted Nebuchadnezzar's glamour and a working telescope in the same building. [76] Glazed lions paced the walls beneath copper domes; cuneiform replicas framed doorways that hid steel beams. [92] Between regimes, the place belonged to no one, which meant it belonged to her. [106]

She found the tile while sweeping the processional hall to save the cost of a caretaker. [122] It sat loose among its blue-glazed neighbors, and where they bore rosettes, it bore a grid of numbers. [140] Four rows, four columns, every line summing to thirty-four: a magic square, fired into ceramic. [155] A frugal woman does not throw away a puzzle that costs nothing to keep. [169] She set it on her desk beside the ephemeris and let it stare at her for a week. [187]

The numbers were too deliberate to be decoration. [195] Sixteen cells, sixteen values, and tucked in the corner a maker's mark she recognized from the observatory's oldest logbooks: the initials of Ferman Aziz, the first astronomer ever posted here, dead sixty years. [228] His logs were famous among the staff for being useless, pages of devotional verse to an unnamed beloved, scattered between observations. [249] Tarisa had always suspected the verses were not what they seemed. [260] Love poems written by a man who recorded cloud cover to the quarter hour were either very sincere or very encrypted. [281]

She worked at night, since daylight was for hauling water and bartering eggs for ink. [296] She drew the square as a schematic, not a poem, not an ornament, but a diagram, mapping each cell to a line of Aziz's verse by its number. [324] Read in the square's order, the love poems shed their disguise. [335] Line seven, line twelve, line one, line fourteen: each opening word was a coordinate, a declination, a date. [353] The beloved had never been a woman. [360] Aziz had been writing to the sky. [367]

It was, she thought, empirically ironic: the one record everyone dismissed as sentiment was the only record that held data. [387] His measurements, decoded, tracked an object the official catalogs never listed, something faint crossing Cygnus over eleven years, moving wrong for a comet, too slow for a planet. [415] He had hidden it because the regime of his day shot men for unauthorized findings, and he trusted no government, only whoever would someday sweep the hall. [442] His devotion had been encrypted not from shame, but from love of the work itself. [457]

Tarisa took his final coordinates to the dome on a night when the city below was dark from rationing and the stars came down close. [482] There was a dusky harmony to the hour, the bruised violet of the horizon humming against the lion-blue tiles, the telescope's gears the only voice in the palace. [510] She extrapolated his arc sixty years forward, by hand, on the backs of unpaid invoices. [525] Then she turned the telescope and waited for the object to uncloak itself from the field stars. [542] Near midnight it did: a dim point exactly where the dead man's love letters said it would be, still traveling, still unexplained. [564]

She did not know what it was. [571] She knew only that two regimes had risen and rotted while it kept its appointment. [586] Whatever government came next would want the discovery stamped, named, owned. [597] Tarisa decided the unknown deserved a better contract than that. [607] So she sealed a covenant the only way an astronomer between regimes can: she answered. [622]

She bought a single blank tile from the kiln-woman by the river, haggling the price down to two candle stubs and a repaired shawl. [646] Into the wet clay she pressed her own magic square, a new grid keyed to her own observations, her own continuation of his arc. [670] Anyone could see numbers; only someone patient enough to draw the schematic would see the promise underneath. [687] She fitted Aziz's tile back into the processional floor and set hers beside it, mortared with ash and water, two squares among ten thousand rosettes. [712] In her logbook she began, deliberately, to write bad love poetry. [723] Let the next regime read sentiment; let the next sweeper read coordinates. [735]

Outside, the dusk settled over the false palace in layered blues, the old empire and the new vacancy holding one quiet note between them. [759] Tarisa trimmed her lamp to half its wick, frugal even in triumph, and looked up. [774] The unknown thing crossed Cygnus, keeping its half of the covenant, and she kept hers. [789]
