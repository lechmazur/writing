The tide gave her ninety minutes. [6] Mara Voss hated enclosed spaces, and had spent twenty years crawling into them anyway, because the only way through fear was deeper. [28] Tonight she stood at the mouth of Hollow Stair Cave, listening to the sea crash and then hush, crash and then hush, as if the water itself were holding its breath for her. [61] Below, in the drowned dark, lay Station Pelagia, a forgotten research facility built where the transatlantic cables came ashore. [80] Forty years ago, a small team had monitored signal decay in the undersea cables, then vanished from every record when the program was quietly buried. [105] Her grandmother had been one of them, a name Mara could not find in any archive, on any plaque, in any obituary. [127] She was returning for the third time, and the last, because the cave only opened between tidal changes, and the window was shrinking with the season. [153] She descended. [155]

The passage narrowed until her shoulders scraped both walls, and her lungs began their old panicked arithmetic. [172] She answered it the way she always did: by tracking invisible things. [184] From her belt she took a vial of warm water and uncapped it, letting vapor bloom into the cold air. [204] Where the mist drifted, condensed, and vanished, she could read the cave's hidden breath, the unseen currents marking open space ahead. [225] Steam to droplet, droplet to film of frost: she navigated through phase transitions, the way her grandmother's notes had taught her. [246] If water changed state, there was airflow; if there was airflow, there was room; if there was room, she could keep moving. [268] The panic loosened its fingers. [273]

After twenty minutes she dropped into the station proper, her lamp sweeping over rusted consoles and the thick black trunks of the cables themselves, still humming faintly where they ran out beneath the sea. [307] Live, after all this time. [312] Somewhere far above, an ocean of voices was passing through this room, and no one alive remembered the people who had made it possible. [336] That was why she had come: not for treasure, not for proof of her own courage, but to stand by the unremembered. [358]

On her first descent she had found the lab; on her second, the flooded dormitory. [373] Tonight she needed the archive room, where the duty rosters would be. [385] The corridor to it had collapsed, leaving a crawlspace barely wider than a coffin. [399] She knelt at its mouth and felt her chest close like a fist. [412] Then her lamp caught something impossible in the rubble: a flake of gold, bright as a struck match. [430] She lifted it free and found a single vellum page, salvaged from some wrecked ship's codex and kept by one of the researchers, its margin illuminated with saints and waves. [460] A fleck of the illuminant gold had broken loose, and it told her what no instrument could: the page was dry, the air beyond was dry, and the archive had survived the flood. [493] In the margin, beside a painted wave, a careful hand had written: We keep what the sea forgets. [511] Her grandmother's handwriting. [514]

Mara breathed mist into the crawlspace and watched it pull steadily inward, condensing on stone, promising air on the far side. [535] She went in on her elbows, eyes on the vapor, letting the physics of water carry her through the fear. [555] The archive room stood intact. [560] Steel cabinets, wax-sealed folders, and there, in a logbook's final pages, eleven names, eleven signatures, eleven people who had kept the world's voices flowing and been erased for the trouble. [590] Elena Voss, third from the top. [596]

Mara photographed every page, then sealed the logbook and the codex leaf inside her dry bag. [612] Outside, the rhythm of the sea was changing, the hush growing shorter, the crash growing long. [628] The tide was turning. [632] She crawled back faster than fear, reading the mist when her lamp failed in the wet, trusting droplet and frost to map the dark. [656] Water rose around her boots, then her knees, cold enough to burn. [668] At the final squeeze, the panic came back whole, and she pressed the gold fleck against her palm like a coin paid for passage. [692] Her grandmother had worked forty years in rooms like this, unthanked, unrecorded. [704] Mara could manage forty more feet. [710] She surfaced into moonlight as the sea closed the cave behind her with a single enormous crash, followed by the deepest hush she had ever heard. [736]

A week later, a small notice ran in the maritime journal: eleven names, eleven photographs of signatures, and a line of medieval script reproduced in gold. [762] We keep what the sea forgets. [768] It was not a monument, but it was a return: the unremembered, standing in the record at last, while the cables beneath the waves carried their names around the world. [798]
