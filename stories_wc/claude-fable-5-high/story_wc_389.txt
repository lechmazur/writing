Wren Calloway lived where the weather went to argue, in a stormlit observatory bolted to a peak above the clouds. [20] The only door worth using opened through a cobalt glass portico, an arch of blue panes salvaged from a drowned cathedral, and on clear mornings it threw ancient shadows across her floor like the bones of a language. [58] She was, by her own grudging admission, a lonely visionary: too strange for the valley towns, too stubborn for the academies, and too fond of her rusted radios to leave. [88] Below the cloud deck, a prophet was rising. [96] His name was Ilo, and Wren had taught him once, years ago, when he was a barefoot boy who asked why shadows kept time better than clocks. [123] Now his sermons crawled up the mountain through fragmented radio frequencies, shredded by storms into stray verses and static. [142] Wren caught them on six soldered receivers, each tuned a hair apart, and pieced the scraps together over bitter tea. [162] The trouble was lovely and terrible at once: the fragments could be assembled more than one way. [179] Stitch them in one order and Ilo sounded lyrically prophetic, all dawn and mercy, promising that "light owes its shape to what it cannot pass through." [205] Stitch them another way and he sounded like a man sharpening a knife on scripture. [220] The valley towns, each catching different shards through their own bad antennas, had split into congregations of variant allegiance: the Dawnsworn heard a healer, the Ashbound heard a general, and both swore loyalty to the same voice. [257] It would have been funny, Wren thought, if it were not also a war learning to walk. [274] Across the rise of the prophet, she kept her ledgers. [284] She logged every fragment, every storm, every drift of frequency, the way other hermits log birds. [300] And one sleepless night, with hail playing the dome like a drunk percussionist, she found the pattern. [317] The storms did not shred Ilo's sermons randomly. [325] They shredded them according to the sun: which fragments survived depended on where the ancient shadows of the ridgeline fell on the relay towers below. [350] The mountain itself was editing the prophet. [357] Wren laughed until she frightened the kettle off its hook. [367] Then she got to work, because if shadow could edit a sermon, then someone who understood shadow could write one. [387] From a cedar chest she took the old eclipse charts, the ones the first astronomers had inked before anyone owned a wire, and unfolded them across the observatory floor until they touched all four walls. [422] In nine days, a total eclipse would drag its ancient shadow across every relay tower in the valley at once. [442] For four minutes, the mountain's accidental censorship would become absolute: only one signal, perfectly placed, would get through. [460] Whoever spoke in that darkness would write the dawn. [469] She spent the nine days splicing. [475] From her archive of fragments she built a sermon Ilo had never preached but had always meant, assembling his own scattered words into the gentlest possible order: the healer's verses, the boy's questions, the mercy with the knife edited out. [515] It felt like forgery. [519] It also felt like teaching, which had always been a kind of forgery she could live with. [536] On the morning of the eclipse, the storm came early and personal, lightning stitching the cloud deck below her like a seam ripping open. [560] Wren hauled her transmitter under the cobalt glass portico, because the arch was the only place where her antenna cleared the dome and because, frankly, she wanted the blue light on her hands while she did it. [597] The moon bit into the sun. [603] The valley's thousand quarreling signals guttered out one by one as the shadow swallowed the towers. [619] Into that hush, through the storm's own fragmented radio frequencies, Wren sent her stitched sermon, and for four minutes every receiver in the valley, Dawnsworn and Ashbound alike, heard exactly the same voice say exactly the same thing. [657] It said that light owes its shape to what it cannot pass through, and that no shape is owed a war. [678] When the sun came back, it came back over a valley gone strangely quiet. [692] Three days later, a single transmission climbed the mountain, clean and unbroken for the first time in years. [710] It was Ilo, laughing. [714] "I know that splice, teacher," he said. [721] "You always did keep time by shadows." [728] Wren poured her tea, watched the blue arch print its old cathedral bones across the eclipse charts, and decided that loneliness, like static, was just a signal waiting for the right dark to pass through.
