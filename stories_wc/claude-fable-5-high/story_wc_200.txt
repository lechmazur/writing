Mara was a lost soul collector, though she preferred the older title: ferrier of the unfinished. [16] Every season she descended into Echo Gorge, where the dead who had died mid-confession still murmured their broken sentences into the stone. [38] The gorge collected sound the way other places collected dust. [48] Spectral confessions drifted there in layers, apologies stacked on regrets stacked on the names of people long buried. [66] Souls could not leave until someone heard them fully, and hearing was Mara's trade. [80] She carried two tools: a harp she had deliberately never tuned, and a crocodile's tooth on a cord around her neck. [101] The harp's misaligned strings made distorted notes that matched no living scale, and only such wrongness could touch the wrong-shaped grief of ghosts. [124] A perfect chord slid off the dead like rain off glass. [135] A flawed one caught. [139] The tooth had belonged to her teacher, who had pried it from a riverbeast that nearly took her arm, then laughed so hard at her own survival that the wound closed faster than any salve could manage. [176] "Laughter is half of healing," the old woman had said, pressing the tooth into Mara's palm. [192] "The dead forget that first." [197] Mara had spent ten years trying to prove her right. [207] Collectors before her had carried souls out of the gorge like sacks of sorrow, delivering them onward still heavy, still hurting. [228] Mara wanted more than delivery; she wanted to integrate laughter into the healing itself, so the dead crossed over lightened rather than merely moved. [252] Tonight she had come for the hardest soul in the gorge. [263] His name was Edrun, a ferryman who had drowned forty years ago with a confession half-spoken, and his unfinished sentence had grown teeth of its own, snapping at any collector who approached. [295] Three had fled the gorge swearing never to return. [304] Mara was not braver than they were, only modestly fearless: she admitted her fear, thanked it, and walked anyway. [323] She began her descent in the hush of a hidden sunrise, when light entered the gorge only as a pale gold seam along the upper rim, never reaching the floor. [353] In that hour the echoes thinned, and a single voice could be followed like a thread. [369] She heard Edrun before she saw him. [376] "I should have told her—" the gorge repeated, over and over, the sentence breaking in the same place each time. [396] She pursued the voice down switchbacks of wet shale, the confession growing louder, angrier, colder. [411] When the cold became cruel, she sat on a flat stone, unwrapped the harp, and dragged her fingers across its warped strings. [433] The distorted notes wobbled out, sour and limping, and the gorge went quiet to listen. [448] Edrun condensed out of the shadow like breath on a window. [459] "Finish it," Mara said gently. [464] "I should have told her," he rasped, "that I sank the boat myself." [477] "I was afraid of crossing in a storm, so I claimed the hull had failed, and she rowed out alone to prove the river safe, and the river took her." [507] The confession hung complete at last, and the gorge shuddered with the weight of it. [522] This was the moment other collectors would have opened the soul-lantern and carried him off, finished but not whole. [541] Mara instead lifted the crocodile's tooth and told him about her teacher: the riverbeast, the bleeding arm, the laughter that had stitched flesh better than thread. [567] "You drowned hiding from water," she said, "and spent forty years haunting a riverbed." [581] "The river got you anyway, you stubborn old coward." [590] A sound came out of Edrun that the gorge had never echoed before: a creaking, astonished laugh, rusty as an unused hinge. [612] "Forty years," he wheezed, "of haunting the one thing I was running from." [625] The laugh moved through him like the sunrise moving along the rim, not erasing the dark but proving it had edges. [646] His guilt did not vanish; it became something he could hold instead of something that held him. [663] When Mara opened her lantern, he stepped in lightly, like a man boarding a boat he finally trusted. [681] She climbed out of Echo Gorge as the hidden sunrise widened its gold seam, the harp on her back still beautifully, usefully wrong. [704] Behind her, the remaining confessions murmured on, but one of them, she thought, sounded almost curious now, as if the dead had heard laughter in their gorge and wondered whether it might someday be for them. [740] Mara touched the crocodile's tooth and smiled. [747] There was an ache in her chest, luminous as that thin seam of morning, and she carried it gladly all the way home. [770]
