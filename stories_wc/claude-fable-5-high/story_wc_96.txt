They called him the penny poet because a poem cost one cent and he refused to charge more, even in winter. [21] He had no other name worth keeping, because he molted personalities monthly, shedding the old self the moment each new moon's first sliver cleared the skyline. [47] In March he was Tobias, gentle and slow; in April, a sharp-tongued drifter called Ash; in May, someone who could not stop laughing. [70] Only two things survived every molt: a coffee tin he carried in his coat, and Marisol. [86]

He had met her sorting books in the prison library, back when the city still bothered with prisons instead of demolitions. [107] She was a guard's clerk then; he was serving eight months for reciting verse in a square where assembly was forbidden. [128] They shelved damaged novels side by side, and she noticed that every month he alphabetized differently, as if a new man had taken over his hands. [154] Instead of reporting it, she asked him what it felt like. [165] "Like a building falling down politely," he said, "one floor a month." [177] She laughed, and the friendship took root between the stacks like a weed nobody had ordered. [193]

When the regime began burning the public libraries, it was Marisol who found him vagrant on the river street and pressed a key into his palm. [219] "The old Carnegie branch," she said. [225] "They torched the roof and left the bones." [233] "Help me rebuild it." [237]

Three years on, the rusted library stood open to the sky, its iron shelving oxidized the color of dried blood, its missing roof replaced by stars. [263] By day it looked dead, which kept the patrols away. [273] By night, people came with books hidden in bread bags and babies' blankets, and he logged each one and shelved it, whoever he happened to be that month. [301] Marisol called it the blooming resistance, because they did not fight with fire; they planted. [316] Every recovered book was a bulb in frozen ground; every poem he sold for a penny carried, folded inside it, the library's location in cipher. [341] The pennies themselves went into the coffee tin. [349] Anyone who shook it heard coins, and that was the point. [360] Beneath the false bottom lay the true ledger: every smuggled title, every borrower, every safehouse shelf in the city, written in his smallest hand. [384] That was the whole project of his life: to forge beauty from anarchy, the city's and his own. [402]

Tonight was his molting night, and for the first time it frightened him. [415] Whoever rose inside him at moonrise might not love this place, might pawn the tin, might forget her face. [434] The library was the only constant he had ever built, and he could lose it from the inside. [452] He climbed the rusted stair to the balcony where Marisol was stringing storm lanterns and put the coffee tin in her hands. [474] "Hold this until I'm someone again," he said. [482] "Eleventh month running," she said. [487] "This one feels different." [491] "The list got long, Marisol." [496] "If the next man is a coward, or a snitch--" [506] "Then I will sit him down where I sat you," she said, "and we will sort books until he understands." [526]

He almost believed her. [530] Below them, the night's borrowers moved among the shelves with shaded candles, light sliding along the rust until the whole ruin glowed like a cracked lantern, luminous at every fracture. [560] The sky paled above the eastern rooftops. [567] As the moon cleared the skyline, the molt took him the way it always did: a long exhale, a name dissolving on his tongue, the scaffolding of a man folding politely, floor by floor. [601]

Marisol watched a stranger open his eyes in her friend's body. [612] "Where am I?" he asked. [617] "A library," she said. [621] "We are rebuilding it." [625] She pried up the tin's false bottom and laid the ledger in the lantern light: hundreds of titles, hundreds of names, a garden mapped in pencil. [651] "All of this grew from pennies," she said. [659] "From a man who fell apart every month and planted anyway." [670] The stranger turned the pages slowly, and something in the handwriting, his own handwriting, snagged in him like a root. [690] "What was he called?" he asked. [696] "Whatever the month required," she said. [702] "What will you be?" [706] He looked up through the missing roof at the stars and the new sliver of moon, the broken building shining at every seam. [729] "Someone who finishes this," he said, and reached for a book to shelve. [742]
