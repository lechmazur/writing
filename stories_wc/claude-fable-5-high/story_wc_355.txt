Maestro Olufemi Brandt had spent forty years refusing every war the world offered him, and he had filled the vacancy with collections. [22] His workshop held Tibetan bowls beside ship bells, a theremin beside a broken church organ, prayer flags strung over a wall of tuning forks arranged like a silver army he would never march. [55] But his truest collection was stranger: a ledger of silences. [65] Entry forty-one: the silence after an apology nobody accepts. [74] Entry sixty-six: the hush inside a hospital elevator, descending. [83] He believed, with the theatrical sincerity of a man who bows to his own teakettle, that every silence had a shape, and that naming the shapes was a kind of mercy. [114] Tonight the town gathered on the hill for the centennial fireworks, and Olufemi stayed below in his shadowed workshop, its skylight open to the stars, hunting the one silence he had never managed to catalog. [149] Entry one hundred would be his sister Mirele's silence, the ten years she had lived in his house and said almost nothing, and then died saying less. [176] All she had left him was her diary, sealed by a clasp of her own making. [192] Mirele had been a locksmith of peculiar genius, and the clasp had no keyhole, only a sliver of spring steel that would release, he suspected, for one precise note and nothing else. [224] He had three years of failure behind him and, tonight, perhaps twenty minutes of usable quiet ahead. [241] Once the fireworks began, the air itself would be too crowded for a whisper of steel to be heard. [260] He set the diary on a felt cushion beneath the skylight and addressed it as if it were an audience. [280] "Madam," he announced, "tonight I intend to recover you." [289] He began with the forks, striking them one by one, holding each against the clasp's tiny hasp. [306] A 440 hummed politely and accomplished nothing. [313] C sharp made the hinge shiver, the way a sleeper shivers without waking. [326] His descriptions arrived before his thoughts did; he could not help narrating the world as it touched him. [344] "You sound," he told the stubborn clasp, "like a held breath in a winter stairwell." [359] Then he stopped, because he had said that exact sentence once before, about Mirele herself, to her face, and she had left the dinner table without a word. [387] The memory rearranged him. [391] He had spent decades describing other people's quiet from the outside, pinning silences to his ledger like moths, and he had never once asked what it cost a person to produce one. [423] Mirele had not been a specimen. [429] She had been a frequency he never tuned himself to receive. [440] Outside, the hillside crowd went quiet, that taut communal hush of a thousand upturned faces in the final stillness before fireworks ignite. [462] Entry one hundred was happening right now, all around him, and he ignored it. [476] Instead he abandoned the forks and switched on the theremin, sweeping its tone slowly upward, listening not for the note he wanted but for the note she had been. [505] He remembered her humming over her workbench, always the same low private pitch, a sound she swallowed whenever he entered the room. [527] He had described that habit in his ledger too, beautifully, uselessly. [538] Now he hunted for the pitch with his whole body, eyes shut, hand trembling in the instrument's field, descending past his own certainty into hers. [563] Lower. [564] Lower still, into the register of a woman keeping herself small. [575] The clasp clicked open like a small mouth deciding, at last, to speak. [588] Starlight through the skylight fell across her handwriting. [596] The first page read: I do not hum for him because he would describe it, and then it would be his. [617] Olufemi sat down on the workshop floor as if the sentence had unstrung his knees. [632] Page after page, she had cataloged him: his performances of gentleness, his pacifism that refused every battle except the quiet ones he waged with adjectives, his way of loving things by labeling them. [665] Two collectors of silence had lived in one house, and only she had known it. [680] The first firework went up and broke the sky into burning chrysanthemums, and the crowd's roar rolled down the hill and through the open skylight. [705] Olufemi did not reach for his ledger. [712] He let the noise pour over him undescribed, and inside it he hummed her pitch, badly, like a beginner, which is how everyone starts hearing another person. [739] Later, he would burn the ledger; entry one hundred, he understood now, could not be written, only kept. [757] Above him the fireworks bloomed and faded, and between their detonations came the small, ordinary silences, no longer specimens, only invitations. [778]
