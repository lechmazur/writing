The search party disbanded at dusk, and Mara let them go without argument. [13] Six days of dragging the river had taught the town what it wanted to know: that her brother Eli was gone, and that grief could be filed away like a closed ledger. [45] Mara was an alchemist, and alchemists do not believe in closed ledgers. [57] They believe in balance. [61] She walked back to the foundry where Eli had last been seen, the old ironworks the rain had been washing for three days straight, as if the sky meant to scrub the place of evidence. [96] Water ran in bright threads down the slag heaps and pooled silver in the casting pits. [112] In her pocket she carried the only thing the searchers had found: Eli's zodiac coin, brass, struck with the twelve signs around its rim, snapped clean in half. [140] He had carried it since childhood, claiming it told him who he was. [153] The foreman had admitted, eyes down, that Eli waited here for her that final night, that he had called her name into the rain before walking toward the river alone. [183] She had been at her bench, perfecting the balance of a stranger's gold, and had not come. [200] Her discipline was a factual one, for all that outsiders called it mystical. [213] Every transmutation obeyed measure: so much given, so much returned, weighed on scales finer than a jeweler's. [230] And yet the measures themselves were mysteries: why a name weighed more than the breath that spoke it, why sorrow registered on brass pans at all. [256] It was a science with a haunted basement. [264] She set up her traveling scale on a cold anvil under the foundry's broken roof, rain ticking through the gaps like a patient clock. [288] On one pan she placed the two halves of the coin. [299] On the other, she placed nothing, and spoke her brother's name. [310] The empty pan dipped. [314] Names had weight; she had always known it as fact, but she had never understood it, and understanding was suddenly the only thing she wanted from the world. [342] She spoke the name again, and again, and the pan dipped lower each time, as if every repetition added what the name had gathered: the night he burned his hand at this very forge, the argument they never finished, the boat he should not have taken. [388] A name, she realized, is a vessel for everything done and left undone in it. [403] Hers was heaviest of all, because it was the name he had called when she did not come. [421] She could not unmake the river. [427] Her art's first law forbade it: nothing returns that has not been paid for, and a life has no equivalent. [447] But she had spent her whole career inside that law, never once pressing her hands against its walls, and tonight she meant to find where it actually ended. [475] From her case she took the small ingot of gold that represented twenty years of careful, balanced work. [493] She would not solder the coin to hide the break. [503] She had read of the eastern menders who fill a bowl's cracks with gold so the damage becomes the most honest, most beautiful part of the vessel. [530] That was her intention now: not to pretend the coin had never broken, but to make the breaking visible and precious. [551] She raked coals in the old forge until the rain hissed off the chimney, melted a thread of gold, and laid it into the fracture with a steady hand. [580] As she worked she spoke both names, his and hers, into the seam. [593] The scale beside her trembled, then did something she had never seen: the empty pan and the laden pan drew level, weight answering weight, as though the gold had agreed to carry half. [626] Here, then, was the limit she had come to find. [636] A name's weight cannot be erased, but it can be shared; regret cannot be refunded, but it can be transmuted into something that shines. [660] The mended coin cooled in her palm, the twelve signs whole again around a bright golden scar. [677] She spun it on the anvil, the old game Eli had loved, and it did not fall to any sign at all; it slowed and stood balanced on its edge, the seam catching the forge light like a sliver of dawn. [718] She wept then, without bitterness, because the regret was still hers but it was luminous now, a crack filled with gold instead of a wound filled with silence. [746] In the morning she walked to the river and did not throw the coin in. [761] She carried it home, half his weight and half hers, redeemed, and the rain finally stopped. [777]
