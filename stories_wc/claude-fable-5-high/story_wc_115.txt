Mara kept the radio on for the liars. [8]
Every night at two, Madame Corvina's psychic hour crackled out of the dead-mill country, and Mara, who had never once prayed and never once believed, listened the way a geologist listens to rockfall. [41]
The callers did not know things, but they remembered things, and memory leaked through their fevered guesses like water through brick. [62]
She had mapped half the county's abandoned mills by interpreting frantic calls to talk-radio psychics: a woman moaning about boilers gave her the Harlan works; a boy babbling about drowned looms gave her the riverside weave shed. [99]
Recklessness was her trade; she went under fences other explorers photographed from outside. [112]
Tonight an old man called in, voice like wet gravel. [122]
He did not ask Corvina for his future. [130]
He said: I was foreman of the ghost shift at the Calder mill, the off-book crew that worked nights so the owners could dodge the inspectors, and in the winter of seventy-one I sent six men through the train tunnel to clear a steam line, and when the warning came I sealed the door instead of opening it. [188]
Then he hung up. [192]
The hush that followed was total; even Corvina, who could fill any silence with spirits, said nothing for nine seconds of paid airtime. [215]
It was inside that hush, drowsy and electric at once, that Mara laced her boots. [230]
She knew the Calder tunnel. [235]
Her grandfather had worked Calder until it closed, and he had died with something stuck in him, some word he chewed at suppers and never spoke. [261]
Mara had spent her whole skeptical life telling herself that swallowed truths weighed nothing, that the dead kept no ledgers. [281]
She wanted, finally, to find the weight of one and hold it in her hands. [296]
The tunnel mouth wore its rust like sleep. [304]
Her headlamp swung across rails gone orange, sleepers soft as bread, the long throat of the dark exhaling cold. [323]
She walked in the drowsy alertness she had trained into herself, the half-dream state where fear stayed quiet and the eyes stayed wide. [346]
Three hundred yards in, the steam line appeared, riding the wall like a vein. [360]
It ended at a junction, and at the junction was the valve wheel. [373]
Someone had beaded it. [377]
Hundreds of glass beads, threaded on wire, wound around every spoke, red and white and a blue gone milky with age. [398]
Mara's job, when she sold salvage to fund her wandering, was to appraise: brass by the pound, gauges by the maker's mark. [420]
Her hands moved by habit, and her habit told her the truth before her head did: nobody beads a valve in a sealed tunnel from the outside. [447]
She counted the strands. [451]
Six. [452]
Six men, six wires, beaded slowly, in the dark, by hands that had time. [466]
They had not died quickly. [471]
They had sat against this junction while the air went stale, and they had made something, the way her grandfather whittled at suppers, the way fingers confess what mouths cannot. [501]
One strand was not beads but bone buttons, cut from a work coat, and Mara knew that coat: it had hung buttonless in her grandfather's hall closet her entire childhood, never worn, never thrown away. [536]
Her grandfather's brother. [539]
The uncle who had supposedly run off west; the empty chair; the word chewed and never spoken. [556]
Her grandfather had known, and had been paid to keep knowing quietly, and the knowing had sat in him like swallowed iron until it killed him slow. [583]
When the company unsealed the tunnel months later, quietly, her grandfather had carried that buttonless coat home. [600]
She unbolted the wheel with her wrench, the way she had stripped a hundred mills, but this time she was not stealing scrap. [623]
She was retrieving evidence. [627]
The wheel was heavy, brass under glass, and she finally had her appraisal: this was the weight of a swallowed truth, eleven pounds, give or take a family. [655]
Walking out, she felt no ghosts, and that was fine. [665]
What the ghost shift left behind was realer than haunting: beads, buttons, a foreman's gravel voice cracking open on cheap radio fifty years late. [689]
At the tunnel mouth, dawn was coming up gray, and her drowsiness finally separated from her alertness, the way it does when a long shift ends. [715]
She called the station from her truck and asked for the old man's number. [729]
They would not give it, of course, so she gave them hers instead, and a message: tell him a Calder granddaughter found what the six men made, and she does not want a confession from him. [765]
She wants names for the strands. [771]
She drove home with the wheel belted into the passenger seat like a rider, beads clicking at every pothole, talking the whole way. [794]
