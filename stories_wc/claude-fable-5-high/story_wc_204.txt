After reality became negotiable, the only fixed point left was the library. [12] It drifted on a swirling sea of clouds, a glass orchard where books grew like fruit from crystalline trees, each volume ripening on its branch until someone reached up to read it. [44] Nothing changed there. [47] That was the point, and that was the problem. [56] Mira had lived in the library for what might have been a decade or an afternoon, tending the trees and avoiding the letterpress operator who worked in the cellar of clouds below. [88] He called himself Verge, answered questions with questions, and printed every page the orchard grew. [103] His press never stopped, and so the library never changed: a perfect system, sealed and eternal. [119]

Then came the rustling. [123] It started in the eastern groves, a sound like pages turning themselves, like something asleep in the books beginning to stir. [144] Mira followed it one morning and found frost on the glass branches, which should have been impossible in a place without weather. [166] The frost had formed in patterns: spirals, ladders, a crude map of the orchard with one tree circled three times. [186] She found the tree at the orchard's heart, and hanging from its lowest branch, not a book but a bottle. [206] Inside it, something moved, faint as breath against a window. [216] A whisper, bottled. [219]

When she lifted it down, Verge was standing behind her, ink-stained to the elbows. [233] "You weren't meant to find that yet," he said. [242] "Or you were meant to find it exactly now. [251] The frost writes; I only read." [257]

"What is it?" she asked. [262]

"The last thing anyone said before reality opened its terms for renegotiation," Verge said. [276] "I caught it. [279] I have been printing around it ever since, building a library so stable that nothing could ever wake it." [298]

Mira turned the bottle and the whisper turned with her, restless. [309] "Why hide it?" [312]

"Because perfect systems cannot survive a true word," he said. [322] "The library is perfect. [326] It is also dead. [330] You have noticed, I think, that nothing here ever grows past ripeness, that you never finish a book, that your mornings repeat." [352]

She had noticed. [355] She had been reading the same chapter of the same green-spined novel for as long as she could remember, and the memory of wanting more had thinned to almost nothing. [385]

"And the frost?" she asked. [390]

"The whisper, dreaming," Verge said. [395] "It writes its patterns on the cold glass every morning, asking to be let out. [410] The rustling you hear is the books answering. [418] They want to wake too." [423]

"And if I open it?" [428]

Verge's face did something complicated. [433] "Then eternity ends. [436] All of this, the endless drifting, the unripening fruit, every repeated morning, collapses into a single moment. [453] One true instant instead of a forever of false ones." [463]

"That sounds like dying." [467]

"It sounds like living," he said. [473] "Briefly. [474] Which may be the only way living is done." [483]

Mira understood, then, why he was cryptic. [490] He had wanted this for years, maybe centuries; he had built the perfect system precisely so that someone else might one day choose to break it, because the choice could not be his. [523] A jailer cannot also be the key. [530] He longed to compress all this eternity into one real moment, but his press only knew how to repeat. [549]

The rustling rose around them, every tree in the orchard trembling with unread pages. [563] Mira thought of the green-spined novel and the chapter she would never finish in a forever of mornings. [581] She broke the seal. [585]

The whisper left the bottle like a held breath finally released, and the library ruptured. [600] Not violently; it ruptured the way ice does in spring, the way a chrysalis does, a clean splitting along lines the frost had drawn all along. [626] The glass trees cracked open and the books took wing, pages beating like starlings, and the swirling clouds below parted to reveal, for the first time, ground. [653] The whisper spoke at last, the sentence reality had been waiting for someone to finish. [668] Mira heard it as her own name, and Verge heard it as his, and possibly both of them were right. [688]

In the single moment that followed, the one true instant Verge had purchased with centuries of patient printing, Mira finished her chapter. [710] It was enough. [713] It was, she understood as the library dissolved into morning light, everything. [725] Somewhere below, on the new ground, frost was already forming on a window, writing the first pattern of a world that would never again pretend to be perfect, or to last forever, or to be anything more than what it was: awake.
