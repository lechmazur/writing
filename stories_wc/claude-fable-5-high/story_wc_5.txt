Yeva Marn was a flash-paper alchemist, which meant her art existed only in the instant of its burning. [18] She wrote sigils on sheets thin as breath, touched them with a coal, and read the truth in the half-second of flame. [40] It was a discipline of patience disguised as fire. [49] Now she had carried that patience north, into the withered orchard at the bottom of a subarctic crater. [67] The apple trees had died standing, black and rimed, their branches holding the shapes of fruit they no longer bore. [87] A century ago, the story went, monks had planted this orchard to guard something that fell from the sky: a measure of safekept starlight, sealed beneath the roots until someone worthy came to ask for it. [123] Yeva did not feel worthy. [128] She felt necessary. [131]

In her satchel she carried a storybook illustration plate, a rectangle of engraved copper her grandmother had salvaged from a shuttered press. [153] It had once printed the final picture of a children's tale: an orchard under impossible stars, and a door in the ground. [175] Her grandmother had insisted the engraver was one of the monks, and that the picture was not fiction but instruction. [195] Yeva had spent twelve years learning to believe her. [204]

Her reason was simple and impossible to say aloud without sounding mad: she wanted to translate heartbeats into maps. [223] Her sister had vanished in the white wastes three winters back, and the searchers had given up. [240] But starlight, the old alchemical texts agreed, remembers every living pulse it has ever touched. [255] Burned through flash-paper, that memory could be read as geography: each heartbeat a coordinate, each rhythm a road. [273] If Mara's heart still beat anywhere under the snow, the starlight would draw Yeva a map to it. [291]

The asking, though, had rules. [296] The plate's border was engraved with a pattern of objects: a cracked cup, a bent nail, a bird's skull, a spoon, a key without a lock. [322] Discarded things, the monks' cipher said, because starlight trusts what the world has thrown away. [337] So Yeva walked the orchard for nine days, gathering its refuse: rusted pruning shears, a frost-split bucket, bottle glass, the wire ribs of an old lantern, a child's boot hard as stone. [369] On the tenth day she began to lay them out, matching the plate's engraved border item for item, angle for angle, in a wide circle around the oldest tree. [398]

The work demanded a stillness that lived in the body. [408] She knelt in the snow for hours, adjusting the bucket's tilt by a finger's width, then waiting, motionless, until her shadow crossed the next mark. [433] Her knees froze; she did not shift them. [441] Her fingers split at the knuckles; she moved them slowly anyway, placing bottle glass with the unhurried care of someone threading a needle in the dark. [467] Patience, for a flash-paper alchemist, was never abstract. [475] It was the long discipline before the brief fire. [484]

On the solstice, the crater changed. [490] The wind, which had scoured the rim for weeks, stopped all at once. [503] No birds, no settling snow, no creak of dead branches: the world held its breath, and Yeva understood this was the asking hour. [526] She set the copper plate at the circle's center, against the oldest tree's roots, and laid a single sheet of flash-paper upon it. [549] On the paper she had written one thing: her sister's name, in ink mixed with her own blood, so the starlight would know whose pulse to remember. [576] Then she touched the coal to the corner. [584] The flame lasted half a second. [590] It was enough. [593]

Light rose out of the ground the way water rises through cloth, slow and silver, gathering in the engraved lines of the plate. [616] The storybook picture filled with it: the impossible stars brightened, and the little door in the ground swung open on the copper. [638] Yeva did not breathe. [642] In the stillness, the light began to move. [650] It threaded out from the plate along her circle of discarded things, touching the shears, the bucket, the bird's skull, the boot, and where it touched, lines appeared in the snow. [681] Not random lines. [684] Ridgelines, river bends, the slope of a valley she half recognized, and at its heart a pulse of light beating in slow rhythm, faint but steady. [710] A heartbeat, rendered as a place. [716] Mara, alive, sixty miles east. [721]

Yeva copied the map onto fresh paper with shaking, careful hands, then did what the cipher's last symbol commanded: she pressed the plate face-down into the snow and sealed the starlight back beneath the roots, returning what she had only borrowed. [762] The light sank; the wind returned; the world breathed again. [772] Yeva rose on frozen knees, folded the map against her heart, and began to climb out of the crater, walking east. [793]
