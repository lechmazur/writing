After the energy beings arrived, the prisons opened themselves, every lock humming apart like a struck chord. [17] Calloway walked out of his cell with nothing but a frayed coil of rope, knotted once for every day of his nine-year sentence. [40] He had braided it from torn sheets, planning an escape he never needed, and the knots had become his calendar instead. [61] Three thousand knots, each one a thumbprint of a vanished day. [72] Now he drifted the roadsides, trading poems for bread, and the rope rode his shoulder like a question he could not put down. [95]

The beings did not speak so much as glow at people, and what they glowed was always a question. [114] They experienced everything at once, past and future folded flat, and they could not understand why humans wept at clocks. [134] They wanted to know what time felt like from inside it. [145] Calloway wanted the same thing, which is why he was a poet, and why he followed the light south. [164]

It led him to a terra-cotta grotto cut into a desert hillside, its clay walls glowing faintly rust under starlight. [184] A being waited inside, a slow ribbon of gold turning in the dark like honey poured through water. [202] You kept the rope, it glowed, and Calloway understood it as words. [214] "Habit," he said. [217] "I keep what keeps me." [222]

The being pulsed, amused, and the grotto walls began to peel. [233] Layer under layer of terra-cotta lifted away like pages, and inside each layer was a year of his life, pressed into the clay the way fossils are pressed into stone. [263] There was his mother's kitchen, the smell of rain on hot asphalt, the courtroom, the cell, the first poem he ever wrote on a wall. [288] We will play a game, the being glowed, and its tone was not cruel but mischievous, a child holding both hands behind its back. [312] Choose, it said. [315] We can return your nine years, smooth and unlived, and you will walk out of here a younger man who was never caged. [338] But the knots will untie themselves, and the poet they made will untie with them. [353] Or keep the years as they were, frayed and locked, and teach us instead what they felt like. [371]

Calloway laughed, because the choice was impossible, and impossibility was the only subject he had ever written about with any honesty. [392] "You don't play fair," he said. [398] We do not understand fair, the being admitted, glowing warmly, which is why we play. [413]

He sat down in the dust and ran his thumb along the rope, knot by knot, the old pattern of counting that had kept him sane. [439] Some knots were tight as fists; those were the bad days. [450] Some were loose, tied carelessly, almost happy. [457] One, near the middle, was double-knotted: the day his mother died and he could not attend the funeral. [475] And there it was, suddenly, the answer to the question he had drifted a thousand miles to ask. [493] Time had a texture, and he was holding it. [502] It was not smooth, not a river, not the clean line the beings imagined humans suffered along. [519] It was fibrous, uneven, frayed where grief had worried it, tight where fear had pulled, soft where it had been handled with love. [542]

"Keep your smooth years," he said quietly. [549] "They'd be rope nobody ever climbed." [555]

He held the coil up into the being's light and began, knot by knot, to read it aloud. [573] Not poems exactly, but the names of days: the iron taste of the first morning, the slow blue boredom of August, the double knot and the silence after it. [602] The being dimmed and brightened with each one, and Calloway realized it was learning to listen the way humans do, one moment at a time, unable to skip ahead. [631] For the first time since arriving, it was inside time instead of above it. [645]

When the last knot was read, the grotto's layers folded gently closed, sealing his years back into the clay where they belonged. [667] You chose badly, the being glowed, and then, with something like a wink, or chose well; we honestly cannot tell the difference yet. [690] "That," Calloway said, shouldering his rope, "is the most human thing you've ever said." [704]

He walked out under the stars, which were only old light, which was only old time, arriving at last at someone able to feel it. [729] On the road the next morning he tied a new knot, the first in nine years, loose and easy, for a day worth keeping. [753]
