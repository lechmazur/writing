Mara Voss had painted four hundred portraits without ever seeing a face. [12] Faces dissolved for her the way reflections break on moving water, so she worked from edges instead: the crease beside a mouth, the asymmetry of an earlobe, the precise geometry of a scar. [45] Her clients called the results fractally precise, true at every scale except the one that mattered to her. [63] On the last morning of the Quillwood Library, she climbed the rope stair into the great hollow trunk one final time. [84] The scholars' guild had carved the dead oak into a library three centuries ago, shelving its rings with ledgers and star charts, and now the city had condemned it: rot in the heartwood, cranes already parked in the meadow below. [124] Demolition began at seven. [128] It was six, and the hush inside the trunk had a weight she could feel on her skin, the stillness of a held breath before a verdict. [155] She had come for one reason: the guild had commissioned her to craft a farewell, a mural on the inner walls that would mirror the sky the scholars had studied here, so the tree would fall full of weather instead of dust. [197] She had finished the clouds the night before; pale cirrus climbed the rings of the wood toward the skylight, exact down to the smallest curl. [222] One panel remained empty: the space where the guild's charter demanded a portrait of the founder, a face no record had preserved. [244] Mara had refused the panel twice. [250] Even with a record, she could not have held a face whole; her limit was absolute, and she had spent a career mapping its borders without ever crossing them. [279] But emptiness in a farewell felt like a lie, so she searched the abandoned shelves one last time. [297] On the highest ring she found a volume the movers had left behind: a treatise on celestial navigation, its spine too heavy for its size. [322] The pages opened onto a hollow rectangle, a secret book safe cut into three centuries of paper. [339] Inside lay a single coin, dark with age, struck with the guild's compass mark on one side and, on the other, a face. [362] To her eyes it was the usual fog, features sliding apart as soon as she gathered them. [379] But her fingers were not blind. [385] She closed her eyes and read the coin the way she read bark and scar tissue, through faint indentations worn shallow by centuries of pockets and palms. [412] A heavy brow rose under her thumb. [419] A broken nose. [422] A jaw set slightly off true, as if the founder had been listening to something far away when the die was cut. [444] For the first time in her life, a face was not arriving through her eyes at all, and so it did not dissolve. [467] It accumulated, ridge by ridge, the way the tree had accumulated rings. [479] She understood then what she had gotten wrong about her limit. [490] It was not a wall but a shoreline; she had only ever stood on it facing the wrong direction. [509] She climbed to the empty panel with the coin pressed in her left hand and a brush in her right. [529] She did not look at what she painted. [537] Her thumb traveled the indentations and her hand translated, stroke by stroke, touch becoming pigment, until the founder's face stood among the painted clouds, gazing up into the skylight as if checking the weather against his charts. [574] When she opened her eyes, the face blurred at once, as every face did. [588] It did not matter. [592] Her fingers knew it was true, and the wood would hold it whole even if she could not. [610] Below, an engine coughed in the meadow. [617] The hush deepened rather than broke, the whole trunk seeming to inhale. [629] Mara returned the coin to its book safe and carried both down the rope stair. [644] Some things deserved to outlast the fall; some deserved to fall finished. [656] At the meadow's edge she stood with the guild's last archivists in the cold early light. [672] She could not tell which gray-coated figure was which, and for once the not-knowing felt less like exile and more like the fog a navigator simply sails through. [700] At seven exactly, the cranes leaned in. [707] The old oak came down with a sound like a closing book, and somewhere inside the falling dark, a painted sky and a true face went under together, intact. [736] In her satchel, the hidden coin kept its indentations, patient as rings in wood, waiting for her hands. [754]
