<story>
Mira Solenne was a luminous glass artist, known for vessels that seemed to keep daylight inside them, and now her glasshouse was ash. [23] She survived the fire by an accident of thirst, having walked to the cistern at midnight while someone barred her studio doors and fed flame through the vents. [51] After surviving the attempt, she did not go to the magistrates. [62] She went into the canyon instead, carrying the one thing the fire had given back: a charcoal smuggler's ledger strip, curled and scorched, found pinned beneath the fallen kiln door. [92] Glassmakers in the valley burned only licensed coke; charcoal was contraband, hotter and cheaper, and whoever torched her studio had brought their own fuel. [116] The strip was a testament written in soot: half a column of deliveries, names burned to ghosts, and gaps where entries should have been. [140]

The quiet canyon held her like a cupped palm under stars so dense they seemed poured rather than scattered. [159] She camped beside a seep of water, and at night the stone hummed faintly with wind, a drifting resonance like the note a goblet makes when a finger circles its rim. [190] She had come to retrace the smuggler's route, because charcoal had to travel this way, between kiln country and the cutwood hills, and the ledger's surviving fragments named landmarks: Split Anvil, Three Pools, the Eye. [225] But the strip's middle column was illegible, fire-glazed to a shine she knew intimately, soot fused into a film thin as breath on a window. [250]

The first morning, she found the answer by accident. [259] She had left the strip weighted under a flat stone overnight, and frost had crept across it. [276] Where the original ink had penetrated the paper, the surface held warmth differently, and the frost refused those lines. [295] Pale script floated up out of white crystal: dates, weights, a buyer's mark shaped like a broken star. [313] She nearly wept, then nearly laughed, because of course it would be this: she had spent her whole life learning how heat leaves its memory in things. [340] Frost was simply glass that had learned patience. [348]

The reading lasted only minutes each dawn before sunlight erased it, so she became steadily adaptive, shifting camp each evening to colder pockets of the canyon, angling the strip toward the open sky, learning which stones bled their warmth slowest. [388] Each morning gave her another inch of testament. [396]

By the fourth dawn she understood what troubled her most was not the entries but the absences. [413] Every tenth delivery was missing; the dates skipped like a stone over water. [426] The smuggler had been paid for loads he never recorded, and the gaps fell always on nights of the new moon, when the valley road went unwatched. [453] Her studio had burned on a new moon. [461]

She sat with that knowledge through a long blue evening, listening to the canyon's hum. [476] All her life she had filled silence with work, kiln roaring, pipe turning, breath pushing light into form, and she had treated every pause as failure. [502] Now the pauses were speaking. [507] The ledger's silences told the truer story, just as the frost's refusals drew the letters. [522] She thought of her own gaps, the year she had stopped blowing glass after her teacher died, the months she called wasted, and wondered if they too had been entries written in invisible ink. [556]

On the sixth morning, frost gave her the final line. [566] The broken-star mark belonged to Coss Avener, the licensing officer who had twice tried to buy her glasshouse and twice been refused. [588] He had purchased the unrecorded loads, charcoal off the books, fire without a paper trail, and the smuggler, perhaps growing a conscience or a price, had begun keeping this hidden column in heat-fast ink. [622] The fire meant to erase her had instead annealed the evidence against its buyer. [636]

She did not rage. [640] She worked. [642] Over two careful days she made a frost-press from canyon clay and river glass, a shallow chilled mold that could coax the patterns out and hold them long enough to copy. [673] She traced every recovered character onto birch bark, then sealed the original strip in a glass vial blown over her campfire, a small luminous thing, light folded around testimony. [702]

On her last night, she lay beneath the stars and let the canyon's slow note move through her. [720] She would walk out tomorrow, retrace the smuggler's road in reverse, and lay the scorched testament before judges who could not unsee it. [743] Then she would build again, and this time she would leave room in her days for stillness, for the cold, for whatever the pauses wished to write. [770] Glass, she finally understood, was not the shaping of light. [780] It was the holding still that let light arrive. [789]
