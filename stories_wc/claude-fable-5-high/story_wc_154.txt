Mara Tevita had spent eleven years translating for peacekeepers in the Pacific, carrying other people's words through Bougainville and the Solomons until the words wore grooves in her. [28] Now she translated for a children's hospital in Auckland, which was supposed to be quieter. [43] It was not quieter the week Iosefa arrived. [51] He was nine, evacuated from an atoll after a storm collapsed a seawall onto his arm, and he was shrewdly innocent in the way island children sometimes are: wide-eyed at elevators, but able to read an adult's fear before the adult could. [93] His cast was already filthy with writing. [100] Before the surgery to reset the bone, he pressed something into Mara's palm: a brain coral fragment, gray and folded like a small fossilized mind. [125] "Hold the worries," he said in his dialect, and grinned at her surprise that she still understood it. [143] "You read my arm while I sleep." [150]

After the anesthetic kicked in and the nurses wheeled him away, Mara carried the coral and a photograph of his cast down to the hospital atrium. [176] It was twilight, and the glass roof had gone the color of wet slate; mist from the harbor pressed against the panes until the ferns inside seemed to float in cloud. [207] She sat beneath them with the photograph and began to work. [218]

The notes on the cast were smudged almost past reading: rain, sweat, the long flight. [233] They were also not in one language. [240] Iosefa's whole village had signed the plaster before the evacuation boat left, and they had written the way frightened people write: in fragments, in borrowed alphabets, in church Samoan and pidgin and a child's phonetic spelling of English. [278] Decrypting it was slow, devotional work. [284] One smear resolved into a grandmother's plea that the doctors not cut off what God had made. [301] Another, in a teenager's slanted hand, asked whether the sea would take the school next. [316] A third, nearly erased, was a question Mara had translated a hundred times in conflict zones, from soldiers and mothers alike: if we leave, who will we be when we come back? [348]

She understood, then, what the boy had handed her. [357] The cast was not a souvenir of signatures. [365] It was an archive of shared anxieties, a whole village's dread compressed onto a child's broken arm, and he had been carrying it across the ocean the way she had once carried ceasefire terms between men who hated each other. [405] He had read her fear in the pre-op room, she realized. [416] He had seen the old translator's terror of words that arrive too late, and he had handed her the coral and the cast the way you hand someone a rope: not to save yourself, but to give them something to hold. [457]

Mara turned the brain coral fragment over in the failing light. [468] Its folded channels looked like the smudged script in the photograph, like every transcript she had never been able to fix. [489] In Bougainville she had once translated an apology a day after a man died waiting for it, and she had carried that knot in her chest for eleven years, certain that words were always too slow for wounds. [527] Now, in the mist-clad atrium, she began to unknot it. [537] Not by forgetting, but by grafting: the surgeons upstairs were setting new bone against old break so the two would grow as one, and she could do the same with meaning. [568] The apology had been late, yes. [574] But the cast in her hands was words arriving exactly on time, because a nine-year-old had been shrewd enough to make his own body the envelope. [600]

She worked until the mist went black against the glass, translating every smear into clean English on hospital letterhead: the grandmother's plea for the surgical team, the teenager's question for the resettlement officer, the unanswerable question for the chaplain, who dealt in unanswerables. [643] Each translation went where it could actually be answered. [652] For the first time in her career, she was not carrying words between enemies but between the frightened and the able. [673]

When Iosefa woke near midnight, groggy and whole, Mara was sitting beside him with the coral on his blanket. [692] "I read your arm," she told him in his dialect. [702] "Everyone's worries got where they needed to go." [710] He studied her face the way he studied everything, finding the thing beneath the thing. [725] "Yours too?" he asked. [729] Mara looked at the coral, that small stone brain that had held a village's fear across an ocean, and felt the old grief settle into something load-bearing, the way grafted bone does. [761] "Mine too," she said, and meant it. [768]
