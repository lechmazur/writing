The toll keeper of the lightning collection tower could not tell a lie, not since the strike that had silvered her left hand and rewired something honest into her tongue. [30] Mara collected coins at the tower's only bridge, and when travelers asked how she was, she answered with the whole truth, which made her famous and a little feared. [59] After the city-wide blackout, fewer travelers came. [66] The tower had failed first, its capacitor coils gone dark, and then the drone beehives that drank its current had dropped from the orchards like spent matches. [93] Without the mechanical swarms, nothing pollinated; the city was eating its last honest harvest. [107] Still Mara kept her booth, because routine without wonder is just waiting, and she refused to merely wait. [125] Each evening she set one small marvel on the toll counter, a moth wing under glass, a stone that smelled of rain, so that anyone crossing would carry a question home. [156]

On the ninth dusk after the blackout, the sky went the color of warmed honey, and an old man in a beekeeper's veil paid his toll with a matchbox. [185] "That isn't legal tender," Mara said, because she had to. [195] "No," he agreed, "but it's worth more than the bridge." [205] Inside the matchbox lay no matches, only a strip of paper bearing a cryptic riddle: I sleep where thunder used to live, in black lacquer I keep my queen; feed me dusk and I will give the gold no wire has ever seen. [248] When she looked up, the man was already crossing, and her truthful mouth surprised her by calling, "I will climb tonight." [269] She could not say a thing unless she meant it; apparently she meant this. [283]

The lightning collection tower rose above the bridge like a dead candle, its rods bare against the nectar-colored sky. [302] Where thunder used to live: the strike chamber at the top, sealed since the blackout. [317] Mara climbed the maintenance stair with her silvered hand on the rail, past the dead coils, past the cradles where the drone hives had been charged and dispatched in tidy, humming routine. [349] She had watched those hives launch every morning for eleven years and never once wondered what they had replaced. [368]

The strike chamber smelled, impossibly, of clover. [375] In its center, on the scorched altar where lightning had once been harvested, sat a lacquer box the size of a bread loaf, black and gleaming, breathing a low, living hum. [406] She lifted the lid and revealed them: real bees, a wild colony, comb hanging in golden sheets, a queen moving through her court like a slow ember. [433]

The beekeeper was waiting on the platform behind her, veil folded back, eyes wet. [447] "I smuggled them up here years ago, when the drone program made living hives illegal as competition," he said. [466] "The chamber stays warm, the wildflowers on the riverbank stay close, and nobody looks inside a dead tower." [484] "Why show me now?" [488] "Because the city needs pollinators and I am old, and because everyone knows the toll keeper cannot lie." [506] He pressed the lacquer box's brass key into her silvered palm. [517] "When you tell the council that living bees survived the blackout, they will have to believe you." [534] "A liar's truth is worthless; yours is currency." [542]

Mara understood then why he had paid her in riddles instead of words. [555] If he had simply told her, she would have been obligated to report a confession; a riddle let her discover, and discovery belonged to her alone. [581] He had wrapped a crime in wonder, and wonder was the one toll she never refused. [597]

She stood before the council the next morning with the lacquer box humming on the table. [613] "The drone hives are dead and cannot be rebuilt before the bloom," she said. [627] "These are alive, they are real, and they ask for nothing but flowers." [640] Because it was Mara saying it, no one argued. [649]

By summer, the orchards held wild hives split from the queen in the lacquer box, and the old beekeeper taught apprentices on the riverbank, pardoned by necessity. [676] The tower's coils were eventually repaired, but the strike chamber was left to the bees, by unanimous vote. [694] Mara still keeps the toll bridge. [700] Each evening, when the light turns to nectar over the water, she sets the empty matchbox on the counter beside the coins. [722] Travelers ask what it once held, and she answers truthfully, as she must: "A question worth climbing for." [740] Some of them look up at the tower then, where bees rise from the strike chamber like slow gold lightning, and they pay their toll without counting the change. [769]
