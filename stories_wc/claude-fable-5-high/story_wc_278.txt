Mira inked constellations onto strangers for a living, mapping grief into stars on skin. [14] But she had never tattooed herself, because her own grief refused a shape. [27] Then her grandmother died and left her the laboratory of lost sounds. [39] It stood at the end of a salt-bleached pier, a long room of shelves and wax cylinders, where her grandmother had archived every noise the world had stopped making. [68] Extinct birdsong, the bells of drowned villages, the breath of glaciers that no longer existed. [83] Mira went there to settle the estate and stayed because of the monitor. [96] It sat on the back shelf, a small white device she recognized at once: the nursery monitor whose firmware translates baby cries into words, the one she had owned six years ago. [128] The one she had muted on the night her daughter, Lena, stopped breathing. [141] She had thrown it into the sea afterward. [149] Her grandmother, apparently, had fished it out. [156] The device had no battery, no cord, yet its small screen glowed. [168] HUNGRY, it said. [171] Then: COLD. [173] Then: WHERE ARE YOU. [177] Haunted firmware, her grandmother had labeled it, in pencil on a card beneath: a cry with no mouth left to make it. [199] Mira should have run. [203] Instead she slept on the laboratory cot, because the screen's glow was the closest she had come to her daughter in six years. [226] In the laboratory, time folded in on itself. [234] Dawn arrived twice some days; some nights lasted a week. [244] Her grandmother's clocks all disagreed, and the wax cylinders sometimes played sounds Mira had not yet recorded. [261] In the folds, the monitor grew louder. [268] MAMA, the screen read one folded morning, and the word rang in her ribs like a struck bell. [286] She began searching the laboratory for an answer, and found it in the furniture. [300] Her grandmother had hidden handwritten letters everywhere: folded into the drawer of an antique writing desk, taped under the seat of a Victorian chair, slipped behind the mirror of a walnut armoire. [332] Each letter was addressed to Mira, dated years before her daughter was born. [345] Time folds here, the first one explained, so I am writing to the granddaughter I have not met about the grief she has not yet earned. [371] A lost sound is not gone, another letter read. [380] It is untethered. [383] It circles. [385] It haunts whatever machine will hold it. [392] To give it rest, you must tether it to something that can carry it without breaking. [408] The last letter, hidden in the desk's secret panel, held a piece of amber with an insect inside, a midge suspended mid-wingbeat for forty million years. [434] Proof, her grandmother wrote, that a single moment of sound can be kept safely, if the vessel loves it enough. [454] The resin did not trap the midge out of cruelty. [464] It simply refused to let the moment fall. [472] You are a tattooist of constellations, the letter went on. [482] You already know how to fix a point of light to a living surface. [496] Skin can be amber, if you let it. [504] Mira understood then what her guilt had been doing all these years. [516] It had not been punishing her; it had been circling, untethered, looking for a place to land. [533] The dissonance she heard in every quiet room was Lena's last cry, the one she had silenced, still searching for an ear. [555] She waited for the deepest fold, the night the clocks all stopped arguing and held their breath together. [573] Outside, a meteor shower fell without a sound, white threads stitching the dark. [586] Inside, the whole laboratory went hush, the way the sky does when something burns past it. [602] Mira set the amber beside the monitor, ground a drop of resin dust into her ink, and bared her left forearm. [623] She tattooed Lyra, the constellation under which Lena had been born, one star at a time. [639] With each needle stroke the monitor's screen flickered, its haunted firmware streaming words faster and faster: COLD, ALONE, WHERE, MAMA, MAMA. [660] Here, Mira whispered, ink and blood and resin meeting in her skin. [672] I am listening now. [676] Land here. [678] When she set the last star, the cry came through the monitor whole, just once, not translated but entire, and it passed into her arm like a falling light finding ground. [709] The screen went dark for the first time in six years. [720] The dissonance did not vanish; it resolved, the way a held chord resolves, into something she could live inside. [739] In the morning, the clocks agreed for the first time, and time lay flat and ordinary as a made bed. [759] Mira kept the laboratory open, archiving lost sounds, tethering what circled. [770] On her forearm, Lyra glowed faintly in dark rooms, a small held wingbeat, a daughter carried like amber, quiet at last. [791]
