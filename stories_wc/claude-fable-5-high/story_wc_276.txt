Maren fished the sound wave garden every night, casting lines of waxed gut between the resonant stalks. [17] The garden had grown from the goddess's last song, centuries back, each note rooting where it fell and rising as a stem of hardened hum. [42] Other fishers had quit when the constellations began sleepwalking. [51] Stars drifted out of their figures after midnight, the Hunter wandering loose-shouldered, the Crown rolling downhill across the dark, and the garden answered their movement with sounds no chart could hold. [82] Maren stayed because she was cool-headed in the way of deep water, and because she wanted one catch only: the echo of the goddess herself. [107] The echo still lived in the garden, a scrap of original voice that zigzagged between the stalks like a minnow refusing the net. [130] Whoever caught it, the old fishers said, could ask the goddess one question and be answered. [146] Maren's question was simple and had her sister's name in it. [157] Lira had been a sleepwalker since childhood, and on the first night the stars learned the habit, she had risen from her bed and walked into the garden, following the drifting lights, and never come back. [193] Her only inheritance from their father was a rail spike, pulled from the abandoned line that once carried pilgrims to the garden's edge. [216] Iron that had borne a thousand hymns holds pitch like nothing else, he had told her, and she used the spike as her sinker, her tuning weight, the cold heart of every cast. [249] The garden transliterates emotions; that was its danger and its gift. [260] Whatever a visitor felt, the stalks spelled aloud: grief came out as long gray vowels, hope as a stutter of bells, fear as a saw against bone. [287] Loud-hearted fishers drowned themselves in their own broadcast feelings, and the echo fled their racket every time. [304] Maren had spent ten years learning to feel quietly, folding her grief small, until the garden read her as little more than weather. [327] Tonight the sky was wrong in the right way. [336] The Weaver constellation had walked clean out of her loom, dragging her stars in a slack line toward the horizon, and as the pattern broke itself, the garden's standing chords began to falter. [369] This was the hour the old fishers whispered about: when patterns break themselves, the echo rises to mend the song, because it cannot help it, because mending was what the goddess made it for. [403] Maren waded between the stalks, spike swinging low on its line, and listened for the unraveling. [419] There: a silver shiver of voice, darting zigzag through the failing chords, stitching one back together, fleeing, doubling, stitching again. [439] She did not chase it. [444] A net of noise would never close on a thing made of song. [457] Instead she planted the rail spike in the soft loam between two stalks, where the broken chord gaped widest, and she did the one thing the garden could not translate. [487] She voiced silence. [490] Not absence, but silence shaped deliberately, mouth open, breath moving, no sound permitted out, the form of a lullaby with all the song removed. [514] It was the lullaby their mother had sung over both daughters, feral and tender, the kind you hum to a child while wolves circle the house. [540] The garden strained to transliterate what she felt and found only the outline of a song, a hole shaped exactly like music. [562] And the echo, born to mend, could not bear a hole. [573] It came zigzagging down the rows, slower now, drawn to the silence the way a tongue returns to a missing tooth, and it poured itself into the iron of the spike, filling Maren's unsung lullaby with the goddess's own voice. [613] The spike rang once, low and old, like a bell under the sea. [626] Ask, said the iron. [630] Maren kept her head cool even now, even with her one question burning in her throat after ten years of folding it small. [653] Where did my sister go, she asked, the night she followed the sleepwalking stars. [667] The answer came not in words but as warmth traveling up the line, and the garden, given a feeling at last that was not Maren's, transliterated it into light. [696] Overhead, the strayed Weaver paused, and one small star at the end of her dragged thread turned, the way a sleepwalker turns when someone hums the right song. [724] Maren did not call out; you cannot shout a sleepwalker safely down. [736] She lifted the ringing spike and walked home in a slow zigzag along the rows, the goddess's voice spilling from the iron as a lullaby gone feral and gentle at once, and far above her, one loosed star began, hesitantly, to follow.
