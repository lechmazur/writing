After the nostalgia ban, the sea was the last criminal. [10] Every tide was an act of remembering, a return to a shore it had loved before, and so the Lunar Office declared the old dances obsolete. [36] Yara had been the most skilled tide dancer on the coast, her body a ledger of every wave the censors wanted unwritten. [58] When they offered her retraining, she chose elective exile instead, signing the form with a steady hand while the clerk avoided her eyes. [81] They crushed her guild moonstone at the border, a cabochon worn smooth by forty years of salt, and swept the powder into the wind. [105] One dust speck escaped the broom of the law and lodged in the whorl of her left thumb, a star too small to confiscate. [129]

She settled in a twilight garden at the inland edge of the dusk line, where the sun never quite finished setting and the stars came out anyway. [156] Black tulips, obsidian-leafed ferns, night phlox: everything there had learned grace from darkness, and so had she. [173] The ban forbade her from dancing what the sea remembered, but it said nothing about what the sky forgot to hide. [194] So she began her last work: to pen a cosmic verse on mortal skin, a poem the censors could never burn because it would walk away on living legs. [223]

She composed it by observing gaps. [229] The censors read words, so the verse could not be made of words; it had to be made of the silences between them. [252] Every night she charted the dark intervals between stars, the pause between cricket calls, the held breath between two waves she could no longer touch but could still hear in the dust speck's faint pulse. [287] The moonstone fragment in her thumb warmed whenever a gap aligned with an old tide, a compass needle the width of nothing. [309] Verse by absent verse, she translated the spaces into a pattern of ink. [322]

In her third year of exile, a courier girl named Senna began stopping at the garden gate, officially to deliver ration chits, unofficially to watch Yara move. [349] Senna had been born after the ban and owned no memories worth outlawing, which made her skin the safest paper in the world. [372] "Teach me," Senna said one evening, and Yara answered, "I will teach you to leave." [387] Fledglings in the garden's black hawthorn taught the method better than she could: they did not study flight, they studied the gaps between branches, and one day the gaps were enough. [418] Yara meant for Senna to fledge the same way, to slip through the spaces in the Lunar Office's attention and carry the verse beyond the dusk line. [445]

Through one autumn she inked the poem along the girl's shoulders, a constellation of deliberate omissions, each mark placed where a star was not. [469] To any inspector it would read as ornament; to anyone who had ever loved the sea, it would read as the sea. [491] On the final night, under stars that seemed to lean in, Yara set the last mark at the nape of Senna's neck and felt the dust speck in her thumb go suddenly cold. [524] The verse was complete, and completion demanded payment. [532] Her feet, which had crossed forty years of moving water, became spontaneously anchored to the garden soil, roots of bone threading down through loam before she could draw a second breath. [563] She understood at once: a poem of pure motion required a counterweight of pure stillness, and the cosmos, ever balanced, had chosen its author. [587] She did not protest. [591] "Run," she said, with the calm of black glass. [600]

Senna ran the way fledglings fly, through the gap between the patrol's lantern sweeps, through the seam where dusk met true night, and the garden closed its dark leaves behind her. [631] The inspectors came at dawn, as inspectors do, and found only a woman standing barefoot among black tulips, smiling at nothing they could cite. [655] "Are you nostalgic?" they asked, instruments out. [662] "I remember nothing," Yara said truthfully, for the verse had taken her memories with it, sealed in ink on a courier's shoulders, walking south. [686] All she retained was the cold dust speck in her thumb, no longer a compass, only a punctuation mark, the period at the end of her moving life. [714] The instruments found nothing, because there was nothing left to find. [725]

Seasons turned, and travelers began detouring through the twilight garden to see the woman rooted there, still as polished stone, graceful as the dark itself. [750] Far south, where the ban's writ thinned, a young woman bared her shoulders on a beach at night, and strangers who read the gaps between her marks began, without knowing why, to weep and then to dance. [787] The tide came in to watch. [793]
