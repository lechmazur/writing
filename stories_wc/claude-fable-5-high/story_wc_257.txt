After hitting rock bottom, Edith Vane stopped making mourning jewelry. [10] For twenty years she had braided the hair of the dead into rings and lockets, giving strangers something to hold when holding was all that remained. [36] Then her sister Rosa died, and Edith discovered she could not braid her own grief. [51] The workshop went dark; the rent went unpaid; the lockets sat unfinished like small shut mouths. [67]

The first origami crane appeared on her doorstep in March, folded from graph paper, with coordinates inked inside its wing. [87] Edith almost threw it away. [92] But Rosa had been a tattoo artist who folded paper while her clients gathered their nerve, and the crane's creases were Rosa's creases, exact and unhurried. [118] The coordinates led to a bridge they had crossed as children. [129] A second figure waited there, a fox tucked into the railing, holding new numbers. [143] Edith followed for eleven days, through gardens and stairwells and a closed library, each fold pulling her a little further out of the dark. [167] She understood, dimly, that someone was carrying out Rosa's last instructions, but she did not let herself ask why. [186]

The final coordinates climbed a hill she had forgotten, to the old municipal observatory, decommissioned before either sister was born. [206] Inside, every clock had stopped at a different hour, so that no hour could claim the place; it felt timeless, a room subtracted from the calendar. [232] Dust hung in the dome like held breath. [240] At the center stood an antique planetarium projector, and beside it, a young woman with Rosa's steadiness in her hands. [260]

"I'm Lien," she said. [264] "Rosa's apprentice. [266] She made me promise." [270]

From a flat leather folder, Lien drew a tattoo stencil on vellum, the skin-thin sheet glowing faintly where lamplight passed through. [291] It was Rosa's final design: a withered stem, drawn in her sister's unmistakable line, opening at its tip into a bloom that had not happened yet. [317]

"She designed it in the hospice," Lien said quietly. [326] "She said you spent your whole life decorating endings, and she wanted to give you a beginning you'd have to wear." [347]

Edith touched the vellum and felt the old pull of craft in her fingers, faint but alive. [364] "Why here?" she asked. [368]

Instead of answering, Lien threw the switch on the projector to illuminate the dome. [382] Stars poured upward, thousands of them, pinholes of electric light wheeling across the plaster sky. [397] They were genuinely artificial, every one a fabrication, and yet Edith's chest tightened exactly as it would have under a real sky. [419]

"Rosa called them honest fakes," Lien said. [426] "Made things that still mean what they say. [434] Like your lockets. [437] Like this." [439]

Lien crossed to the dome's slit and unhooked two long drapes of canvas, paint-stained, hanging where the shutter mechanism had rusted open. [461] "Rosa called these the curtains of intention," she said. [470] "Closed, you see only the stars you make. [478] Open, you let the actual night in. [485] She said grieving people have to choose, every evening, which way to pull them." [499]

Edith stood between the projected constellations and the cold true dark beyond the slit, holding the stencil. [516] She thought of every customer who had wept at her counter, and how she had armored their losses in gold and glass and never once thought to armor her own. [546]

"She wanted to teach me something," Edith said. [554]

"To teach withering to bloom again," Lien answered. [562] "Her words. [564] She made me memorize them." [569]

The ardor in Edith was quiet, banked, more ember than flame, but it was there for the first time in a year. [591] She rolled up her sleeve. [596]

Lien worked by the planetarium's borrowed starlight, pressing the vellum to Edith's forearm, the stencil's purple ghost settling onto her skin like frost. [619] Then the needle, patient as braiding. [625] Edith watched the withered stem appear, line by deliberate line, and watched the bloom open at the end of it, ink where there had been nothing. [651] It hurt the way thawing hurts. [657]

When it was finished, Edith walked to the curtains of intention and pulled them open herself. [673] Real night entered the timeless room and mingled with the made stars, and she could no longer tell, and no longer needed to tell, which light was which. [701]

In her pocket she still carried Rosa's last crane. [710] Before leaving, she folded one of her own, clumsy but sincere, wrote the observatory's coordinates inside its wing, and left it on the projector for whoever hit bottom next. [739] Then she went home, reopened the workshop, and began a locket that held not hair but a pressed paper flower, an honest fake, blooming. [763]
