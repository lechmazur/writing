Mira had worked labyrinth maintenance for eleven years, and in that time she had learned that grief weighs exactly four grams per cubic foot of hedge. [26] The labyrinth absorbed sorrow from everyone who walked it; that was its function, plain as plumbing. [42] Her job was to measure the saturation, log it in the municipal ledger, and deconstruct any wall of trees that had taken in more sadness than it could hold. [71] Deconstruction was delicate work: you could not simply cut an overburdened tree, or its stored sorrow spilled back into the paths and ruined a month of visitors. [98] You had to unbraid it branch by branch, the way you would take apart an argument, and you could only do it at night, because the labyrinth heals in moonlight and forgives whatever you remove. [133]

For measurement she used the broken sextant. [140] It had belonged to the astronomer who built the tower at the labyrinth's center, a woman who believed sorrow could be navigated like open water if you only had the right instrument. [172] The sextant's mirror had cracked the night she died, and ever since, it refused to sight stars. [189] Instead it sighted dreams. [193] Dreams snag in trees the way kites do; visitors shed them while walking, and the fragments hang in the branches, faintly luminous, until rain takes them down. [220] Through the cracked mirror, each fragment showed an angle, and by mapping the scattered dreams across a quadrant Mira could triangulate exactly where sorrow had pooled too deep. [248] It was ordinary work, the way lighthouse keeping is ordinary. [258]

What was not ordinary was the reading she took on the night of the storm. [273] The northeast quadrant measured heavier than anything in her ledger's history, heavier than the year the harbor flooded, and the dreams there had stopped scattering. [298] They had gathered, deliberately, into a single line pointing at the tower. [310]

Mira had always wondered what the trees witnessed. [318] They drank in every confession whispered between the hedge walls, every funeral suit brushing past, every child lost and found; they had to be keeping some account of it all, and eleven years of curiosity had hardened into a need to know. [360] So she followed the line of dreams. [367] The storm broke as she walked, lightning silvering the wet leaves, but the moon kept tearing through the clouds, and wherever its light landed she could see the hedges knitting their small wounds closed, calm as breathing. [404]

The tower door stood open. [409] Inside, the storm went strangely quiet, the way sound dies in deep snow. [422] She climbed the spiral stairs to the observatory, where moonlight and stormlight took turns through the broken dome, and raised the sextant out of habit. [447] The cracked mirror filled, not with dream fragments, but with roots. [458] She saw the labyrinth from below, the whole vast network of it, and understood at once that the trees were not merely absorbing sorrow. [482] They were carrying it here, down through the roots and up through the tower's stones, delivering it grief by quantified grief to the astronomer's old observatory the way rivers deliver themselves to the sea. [516] The trees witnessed everything, and what they did with their witnessing was bring it to her. [532] Because the astronomer was gone, and someone had to read the measurements, and for eleven years that someone, without knowing it, had been Mira. [556]

She lowered the sextant slowly. [561] On the floor of the corridor behind her, in the storm's held breath, something tiny struck stone. [578] A pin, falling in absolute silence, ringing like a struck bell. [589] It was the astronomer's brass calibration pin, shaken loose from the sextant's cracked housing after forty years, and at the moment Mira heard it she understood the instrument had finished its work. [621] It had not been broken at all; it had been recalibrating itself, all this time, from stars to sorrow. [640]

Mira picked up the pin and set it back in its socket, the way you fix anything. [657] Then she went down into the northeast quadrant and began to deconstruct the heaviest wall, unbraiding branch from branch while the moon did its quiet mending behind her hands. [686] Each branch she loosed gave up its measured weight of sadness, four grams at a time, and the storm carried the dreams away clean. [710] In the morning she would log it all in the ledger, in the dry language of maintenance: quadrant cleared, saturation normal, instrument functioning. [733] But she would also write, in the margin, what the trees had finally told her: that nothing wept in the labyrinth went unwitnessed, and someone was always taking the measure of it, and that this, too, was simply part of the job. [775]
