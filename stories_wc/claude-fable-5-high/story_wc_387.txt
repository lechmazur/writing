Old Bren mended nets at the foot of the sea wall, and he believed every torn mesh told a story. [20] A diamond tear meant a marlin; a slow fray meant a lazy hand; a clean cut meant a knife and a secret. [42] The fishermen called him a philosopher and laughed, but they brought him their nets all the same. [59] Bren did not mind the laughter; he knew the world ran on work nobody bothered to see. [76] Hidden knots held every catch, and hidden patterns held every life. [87] It was firefly season, and the rain had just stopped when he first heard the sound. [103] From the old fortress parapet above the harbor came a faint, rhythmic rasp, like a bird made of straw beating its wings. [125] He heard it the next night, and the next, always after rain, always when the fireflies rose from the marsh grass. [146] A pattern, he thought, threading his needle. [153] And patterns, like knots, deserved witnesses. [159] On the seventh night he set down his needle and decided, with a demure sort of audacity, to climb. [178] He would not announce himself; he would not knock on any door; he would simply go and see whose effort the town was sleeping through. [203] The fortress stairs had crumbled long ago, so he went by following moon paths instead. [218] After rain, the moon laid silver ribbons across the wet stones, showing which steps still held and which had rotted into shadow. [240] Where the light pooled, the stone was sound; where the dark sat thick, the sea had eaten through. [258] He climbed ribbon to ribbon, an old man stitching himself up the wall. [271] At the top, the parapet whispered. [277] Rainwater trickled through cracks in the battlements, and the wind moved through loopholes in long, breathing sighs, so the whole wall seemed to murmur old orders to soldiers who would never return. [309] And there, in a notch of the wall, knelt the harbormaster's widow, Senna, whisking tea. [324] She worked a bamboo tea whisk in a chipped black bowl, her wrist quick and exact, the froth rising pale as moonlight. [346] Around her, fireflies drifted up from the marsh below, hundreds of them, blinking against the dark water like a sky that had fallen and refused to go out. [374] The scene was scintillatingly somber: a thousand small lights, and at their center one woman making tea for a man four years drowned. [397] Bren understood at once. [401] The harbormaster had climbed here every wet night of firefly season to count returning boats, because rain confused the lamps and someone had to watch. [426] Nobody had ever thanked him; nobody had known. [434] Now his widow kept his post, and made his tea, and poured it over the wall to the sea that took him. [456] Bren knew he should leave. [461] Grief that climbs a ruined wall to be alone has earned its solitude. [474] But he had not climbed all this way to let one more hidden effort go unwitnessed. [490] So he did the boldest quiet thing he knew: he began to whistle. [503] It was the mender's whistle, the soft three-note call the fishermen used at dusk to say a net was finished and whole again. [526] Senna did not startle. [530] Her wrist slowed, the whisk went still, and she spoke without turning. [542] "How long have you known?" [547] "Seven nights," Bren said. [551] "Though I suspect the sea wall has known for four years." [562] She laughed, just barely, the way people laugh when crying has worn out its welcome. [577] "He whisked this tea every night he stood the watch," she said. [589] "He told me the froth kept his eyes sharp and his temper kind. [602] Nobody ever saw what he did up here, and he liked it that way." [616] Bren sat down on the wet stone beside her, uninvited and unhurried, his audacity wearing its politest coat. [634] "I mend nets," he said. [639] "Nobody sees that either, until a net fails and a family starves. [651] The world is held together by work done in the dark, and I have decided to spend my last years finding it and saying thank you." [677] Senna looked at him for a long moment, then handed him the bowl. [690] He drank. [692] The tea was bitter and green and tasted faintly of rain. [703] Below them, the fireflies pulsed in slow waves, and Bren saw it then, the pattern hiding inside the pattern: they brightened whenever the wind whispered through the loopholes, as if the wall itself were conducting them. [739] "Everything answers something," he murmured. [744] "We just stand too far away to hear the question." [754] Senna poured the last of the tea over the parapet for the sea, and rinsed the bamboo whisk, and began again, this time making a bowl for two. [782] Far below, the moon paths trembled on the water, waiting to lead them home. [796]
