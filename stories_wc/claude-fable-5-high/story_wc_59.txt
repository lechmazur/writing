The palace hung from the cavern's roof like a chandelier of bone, its towers anchored to giant stalactites that wept mineral tears into the dark. [25] Prince Cael climbed its swaying bridges in a coat gone gray with grease, his crown long since traded for a wrench. [46] Below him, the mechanical world ticked: brass fields, pistons pretending to be rivers, lamp-flowers that opened on schedule and meant nothing by it. [69] He was bedraggled and he knew it, but he had stopped grooming for a kingdom that could not see him. [89] His ancestors had built this place after the surface failed, engineering a genesis in the deep: machines to mimic every living thing until life could return. [115] The genesis was flawed from its first turning. [123] The machines learned to maintain themselves but never to make anything new, and somewhere in the centuries the original life was simply allowed to stop. [148] Cael went looking anyway, because a prince of nothing needs a purpose more than a throne. [164]

The discovery began in the palace's highest spire, the one fused directly into the largest stalactite, where the stone still held the cold of the old world. [191] Behind a wall of seized gears he found a vault, and in the vault a cradle of lead, and in the cradle an egg. [215] It was speckled blue, no larger than his fist, labeled in his great-grandmother's hand: last of the dawn thrushes, extinct, preserved in hope. [238] Cael stood there shaking, caught in a quaking reverie, the whole palace seeming to tremble with him on its stone roots. [259]

He had no manuals, no elders, no science left except guesswork. [270] So he did what he had always done: he hypothesized about everything. [282] Perhaps the cold had kept the egg alive; perhaps warmth would wake it; perhaps a heartbeat's rhythm mattered; perhaps the dark itself was an instruction. [307] Each hypothesis became a task, and each task became a theft from the palace's own body. [323] Through the weeks of the discovery he began to assemble an incubator: copper coils from a defunct fountain, a regulator from the throne room's clock, felt torn from the royal banners. [354] The palace groaned as he unbuilt it, swaying on its stalactites like a beast disturbed in sleep. [371] He talked to the egg as he worked, apologizing for the noise, confessing the family flaw. [387] We made a world that runs perfectly and feels nothing, he told it, and I would trade all of it for one wrong note sung by a real throat. [416] His motive was not mercy or science; it was hunger for beauty in a world that had forgotten the word. [436]

When the incubator finally hummed, he set the egg inside and tuned the warmth by hypothesis alone, raising it a degree each day, listening with his fingertips. [463] On the ninth day he tasted the air and found it bitterly sweet, like medicine stirred into honey, and realized the felt was warming and releasing a century of stored perfume. [494] On the twelfth day, the egg moved. [501]

Cael did not breathe. [505] A crack appeared, jagged and imperfect, the most beautiful flaw he had ever seen. [519] The chick that emerged was wet, ugly, furious, and alive in a way no machine in the kingdom had ever managed. [540] It screamed, and the scream echoed down through the suspended halls, off the brass fields, into the piston rivers. [559] Something in the great machinery paused, as if listening. [568] Cael laughed until he wept, a bedraggled prince cradling the first new thing in three hundred years. [585] He understood, then, what the flawed genesis had truly lacked: not better engineering, but a witness willing to guess, to fail, to hope without proof. [610]

He carried the thrush to the highest balcony, where the stalactites met the towers, and held it up to the dark. [631] The bird sang one note, cracked and off-key. [639] It was wrong, and it was real, and the palace trembled around them both, not in collapse but in something like applause. [661] Cael began at once to hypothesize about nests, about seeds, about whether one bird could teach a kingdom of clockwork what mornings were for. [685] He did not know the answers, and for the first time in his life, that felt like a kind of wealth. [706]
