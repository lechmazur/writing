Mara Voss had inspected craters for the Agency since the first satellites went up, and she knew this one the way you know a face. [25] It was her report that had condemned it. [33] The volcanic summit, she had written, offered stable rock, thin air, and a clear horizon: ideal ground for the new deep-space tracking dish. [56] What the report did not say was that the crater cupped a lens of ancient ice, older than any calendar, and that the excavation crews would grind it out by August. [87] So she climbed back up alone, the night before the machines arrived, carrying a camera and a satchel of small glass vials. [109] She wanted one photograph of the ice under first light, the last of it anyone would ever take. [127]

The summit at midnight was a bowl of moonlit stone, pale as bone china, the ice at its center glowing faintly blue. [149] Somewhere overhead, a satellite ticked across the stars, a man-made spark among the old fires. [164] Mara set her tripod on the crater rim and began the long business of waiting. [179] She could not use a flashlight to check her watch; even a brief glow would ruin her dark-adapted eyes and risk fogging the film. [203] So she used chemistry instead, a trick from her laboratory years. [214] Each vial held a slow clock reaction she had calibrated at home: when the mixture turned over, it triggered a faint chemiluminescent shimmer, a whisper of cold light timed to the minute. [246] One vial would glow at two o'clock, telling her to start the star-trail exposure. [260] Another at four, telling her to close the shutter. [269] The last would wake her, if the cold lulled her, twenty minutes before dawn. [283]

Her grandfather would have liked the vials. [290] He had been the nightwatch at the old mountain observatory, back when astronomers timed their plates with an hourglass because clockwork froze at altitude. [314] When the observatory was decommissioned to make room for radar, the hourglass had shattered in the move. [331] Mara, eight years old and purposefully curious even then, had crawled under the packing crates and rescued a single grain of its sand. [354] She carried it still, sealed in a sliver of glass on a chain, one grain of measured time against the wide unmeasured dark. [377] It was that curiosity, never idle, always aimed, that had carried her from his lap to a survey post in the space age. [400] It was also what had betrayed the ice. [408] She had been the one curious enough to drill the core, to date the layers, to prove the crater floor was sound beneath them. [432] Knowledge had a price, and tonight she had come to pay it. [444]

The cold pressed in after two. [450] The first vial glowed; she opened the shutter and let the stars write their slow arcs above the dying ice. [470] Her toes went numb, then her fingers; she walked tight circles on the rim and recited core samples like a rosary. [491] She had outlasted worse nights than this, in Iceland and the Atacama, and she meant to outlast this one too. [511] The night could not be argued with, only endured, one calibrated minute at a time. [526] The second vial glowed at four, and she closed the shutter. [537] Then she sat against a boulder still holding the day's old warmth, and somewhere in there she slept. [555]

The third vial woke her, a green pulse at the edge of vision, faithful as her grandfather's rounds. [573] And then came the hush. [578] It arrived before the light did: the wind lay down, the rocks stopped their faint settling, and the whole summit seemed to hold its breath between the last star and the first color. [611] In that dawning hush, the ice turned rose, then gold at its broken edge. [625] Mara made the photograph slowly, deliberately, the way her grandfather had turned his hourglass: one exposure, one chance, no second mornings. [646] The shutter fell like a held breath released. [654]

Afterward she walked down onto the crater floor and knelt at the ice. [667] She unclasped the chain, cracked the sliver of glass, and pressed the single grain of the nightwatch's sand into a meltwater seam. [689] Time given back to time, she thought; let it lie with something older than counting. [704] Far below, she heard the first diesel engines climbing the access road. [716] The dish they built would listen to probes beyond the Moon, and that was a kind of wonder too; she would not pretend otherwise. [740] But wonder spends things, and someone has to keep the receipts. [752] She capped the lens, the morning's melancholy light pooling gold in the doomed crater, and started down to meet the machines, carrying the last of the ice safe inside a small black box. [785]
