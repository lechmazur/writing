Maren had pruned the orchard on the verdant parapet for sixty years, ever since the waterfall first taught her to listen. [21] The trees grew where two worlds overlapped, their roots in one earth and their crowns in another. [38] Each cut she made was a stitch, and the orchard was her loom, weaving between worlds so neither could drift away from the other. [62] The waterfall beside the parapet fell from the bright world into the fading one, carrying light downward like thread. [81] Lately the thread had thinned. [86] Below the falls, the lower world was going gray. [95] Its people still ate and worked and slept, but their children had stopped asking why the moon followed them home. [115] Wonder was draining out of them like color from old cloth. [126]

On the first morning of spring, upon the hush of budding hope, a boy climbed the parapet steps. [144] He came from the gray world below, sent by a mother who remembered rumors of a gardener who could mend what doctors could not. [168] His eyes were the color of ash. [175] "I do not feel anything anymore," he said, as though reporting the weather. [188] Maren set down her shears and studied him the way she studied a sick branch, looking for the place where life still moved. [211] She had given succor to many travelers, but never to one so young and so emptied. [227] "Sit," she said, "and watch me work." [234]

Maren was insatiably creative in her craft. [241] She had never made the same graft twice. [249] She joined pear to lightning-struck oak, spliced winter branches onto summer trunks, and trained one tree to bloom in two worlds at once, its blossoms white above the falls and gold below. [281] Every invention was an experiment in the same question: how do you keep wonder alive in a thing that is dying of ordinariness? [304] The boy watched without interest, which was its own kind of answer. [316]

That evening she brought out the heirloom seed. [324] It had passed through eleven generations of pruners, never planted, because it was not yet finished. [340] Each keeper added to it by capturing essence: the held breath of a child seeing snow, the gasp of a sailor sighting land, the silence after a perfect note. [369] The seed held them all, a hoard of astonishment waiting for soil worthy of it. [384] "My task," Maren told the boy, "has always been to preserve the color of wonder until the world needed it back." [405] "I believe the world is asking now." [412]

But the seed was incomplete. [417] It needed one final essence, and she was too old to feel astonishment freshly; her wonder had ripened into knowledge long ago. [439] She needed someone who could be astonished for the first time. [450] She needed, she realized, someone exactly like a boy who had forgotten everything, because forgetting and never-knowing open the same door. [471]

So she gave him the most ordinary work she had. [481] For seven days he carried water, raked leaves, and held branches while she cut. [495] She never told him what to feel. [502] She only positioned him, again and again, where the orchard's strangeness could find him: beside the tree that bloomed in two worlds, beneath the spray where the waterfall braided light from both skies. [535] On the eighth dawn, a blossom opened against his cheek as he slept under the double-blooming tree. [552] He woke into the falling petals of two worlds at once, white drifting down through gold, and he made a small sound he had never made before. [579]

Maren was ready. [582] She held the seed near his lips and caught that sound, that first unguarded gasp, the way her grandmother had taught her, and the seed grew warm as a held hand. [613] Together they planted it at the parapet's edge, where the soil of both worlds mixed. [628]

The tree that rose by evening was unlike any graft she had ever imagined. [642] Its roots drank from the waterfall and sent color down the falls like dye through water. [658] Below, in the gray world, the river began to shine. [668] Children gathered at its banks and asked, for the first time in years, what was upstream. [684]

The boy stayed. [687] His eyes had gone the brown of turned earth, and his hands had learned the shears. [703] Maren watched him prune his first branch, badly, and felt something she had not felt in decades: surprise at what might grow. [725] A radiant hush settled over the parapet, the kind of quiet that is not emptiness but fullness waiting to speak. [745] Somewhere in the new tree's branches, a twelfth seed was already forming, gathering room for wonders not yet felt. [764] Maren handed the boy her shears and began to teach him how to weave.
