In the city of Veil, snow fell without sound, and the people smiled without reason. [15] Each dusk the Choir sang the lullaby of conformity from the towers, and whatever a citizen had felt that day rose out of them like steam and drifted away. [44] The Choir called this the harvest of echoes, and called it mercy. [56] Liss was the city's most celebrated courtesan, and she had never once been touched. [70] Her trade was listening. [74] Magistrates and masons paid to sit across from her and confess what the lullaby had not yet taken, because confession, in Veil, was the last private pleasure. [101] She answered them not with words but with movement, a bodily poetic the Choir could not transcribe: a grief rendered as a slow turn of the wrist, a hope spelled in the arch of her spine. [137] Her chastity was not modesty; it was strategy. [145] What the body never surrendered, the harvest could not claim. [155] So she remained whole while the city emptied, a full vessel among the drained. [169] It was an old client, a dying weaver of the Choir, who told her where the echoes went. [187] Beneath the silver courtyard at the palace's heart stood a prism sanctuary, a vault of cut glass that woke only under starlight. [209] Inside it, every stolen feeling hung knotted in a great loom of luminous threads, each strand a voice, each tangle a life. [231] The weaver pressed a key of braided silk into her palm and said the lock was not the difficulty. [250] The difficulty was that the loom recognized intention, and any heart that came in anger would be harvested on the spot. [271] Only a perfect calm could enter, and only between sigh and smile, in the breath where a person belongs to neither sorrow nor delight, could the threads be touched at all. [302] Liss had spent twenty years rehearsing exactly that breath. [311] She crossed the silver courtyard on the longest night of winter, snow settling on her bare shoulders like applause that had forgotten how to make noise. [337] The guards let her pass; everyone let Liss pass; that was the terrible gift of being decorative. [354] The courtyard's mirrored flagstones doubled the stars beneath her feet, so that she seemed to walk through the sky in both directions. [376] At its center, a hatch of frost-rimed glass. [384] The silk key turned without resistance, which frightened her more than resistance would have. [398] Below, the prism sanctuary blazed with borrowed starlight, every facet throwing pale fire across the loom, and the loom was vast: a thicket of glowing threads snarled into a million small captivities. [430] She could hear them faintly, the echoes: a child's laugh, a widow's curse, a thousand abandoned songs. [447] Rage rose in her, and the nearest threads turned toward her like hungry stems. [461] So she did what she had always done with feeling too dangerous to speak: she gave it to her body and let her body translate. [486] She exhaled a long sigh. [491] In the suspended instant before her smile arrived, in that unclaimed country between the two, her hands found the first knot. [512] She worked the way she danced, by untangling threads as though they were verses, each loosened strand a line returned to its author. [535] A freed echo did not scream or shine; it simply went home, slipping upward through the glass like warmth remembering a window. [557] Hours passed, or none. [561] She held the breath between sigh and smile the way a dancer holds a balance, renewing it endlessly, her serenity so absolute it would have alarmed anyone watching, because no living face should be that still while doing treason. [600] When the final knot opened, the loom hung empty as a winter branch. [613] Above her, Veil began to make noise. [620] Weeping first, then laughter, then arguments decades overdue, the whole city unlocked at once, flooded by everything it had been politely deprived of. [643] The Choir tried to sing the lullaby of conformity, but a lullaby requires sleepers, and no one in Veil would ever be that tired again. [668] Liss climbed back into the silver courtyard as dawn grayed the snow. [680] She had defied the lullaby not with a shout but with a pause, and the pause had won. [698] For the first time in twenty years, she let the smile arrive. [710] It was hers; no one had paid for it; nothing came to harvest it. [724] The snow kept falling, and at last it had something to fall on: a city loud enough to deserve its silence. [745]
