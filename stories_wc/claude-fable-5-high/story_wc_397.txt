Mara crossed three borders to reach Hesketh Sound, where dying on schedule was legal. [14] The clinic called its mandatory forty days of waiting a fold in patience, a pleat in time where the decision could breathe before it became permanent. [40] She had not planned to spend that fold doing anything but counting. [52] Then the October storm broke the drift-ice marina apart, and the town needed hands. [66] The marina had been the town's strange pride: floes lashed between pilings, fishing boats moored in channels cut through pack ice, blue light everywhere. [90] Now it was wreckage, and the relief coordinator did not ask why Mara was in town, only whether she could hold a net. [113] She could. [115] Every morning the volunteers went out in flat-bottomed boats to trawl the channels for what the storm had taken: ropes, oars, photographs, once a wedding ring frozen into a shelf of ice. [147] Mara worked the dragnet with a fisherman named Olen, who never asked questions either. [161] In her coat pocket she kept the tip of her grandmother's lace parasol, a finger of carved whalebone, the only piece left after a century had eaten the silk and rusted the ribs. [194] Her grandmother had carried that parasol onto a sinking ferry and lived; she had died decades later, slowly, screaming, in a hospital that would not let her go. [222] Mara had a diagnosis with the same shape, and she had decided to refuse the shape. [238] What she wanted, in the fold, was simpler than courage: she wanted to learn the sound of approaching endings, so that hers would not arrive as a stranger. [266] On the ninth day the ice taught her the first lesson. [277] A floe shelf groaned, a long low note like a door in a sleeping house, and Olen pulled her backward a full second before the shelf calved into the channel where she had been standing. [312] "Ice always says goodbye first," he said. [319] "You just have to learn its language." [326] She wrote that down. [330] On the seventeenth day the net snagged, the boat tipped, and Mara went into the black water. [347] The cold was not a temperature; it was an argument that her body lost instantly. [362] She heard, beneath the surface, a sound she would never be able to describe in clinic paperwork: a hush gathering itself, patient and total, like snow about to slide from a roof. [394] Then Olen's gaff hooked her collar and dragged her back into the noise of living. [409] She woke in the relief station changed. [416] Not cured, and not converted; changed the way a bell is changed after it has been struck. [433] From that morning on she gripped reality loosely, the way Olen had taught her to grip a line in freezing weather, firm enough to hold, slack enough that the sea could not take her hand with it. [470] Rooms felt provisional; days felt borrowed; none of this frightened her anymore. [482] She went back out on the boats. [489] Now she could hear it everywhere, the grammar of endings: the marina's pilings ticking before they shed their ice, the wind dropping a half-tone before it died, the particular silence in a recovered photograph of someone's flooded kitchen. [527] Endings announced themselves, she learned, if you stopped bracing against them long enough to listen. [542] On the thirty-ninth day the trawl brought up something pale: a ruined parasol, lace gone to gray ghost-cloth, ribs bent like a bird's wing. [566] Not her grandmother's, of course; some other woman's sunlight, some other century. [578] But its tip was missing, snapped clean off, and Mara laughed until Olen looked worried. [593] She took the whalebone tip from her pocket and fitted it to the broken shaft. [608] It did not fit, naturally, and that was the lesson too: nothing ends where another thing conveniently begins, but the pieces still rhyme. [631] On the fortieth day she walked to the clinic through wind that came off the floes smelling of cold iron and salt. [653] The counselor asked the required question: did she wish to proceed? [664] Mara listened to herself the way she had learned to listen to ice. [677] No groan yet. [680] No gathering hush. [683] Only the small ticking of a body still shedding its days at an ordinary speed. [698] "Not yet," she said. [702] "I will know the sound when it comes, and I will come back when I hear it." [719] She kept the parasol tip in her fist as she walked back toward the marina, where the volunteers were rebuilding the channels for winter. [743] The fold in her patience had not been a delay after all; it had been a pocket, and she had filled it. [765] The wind came up off the water and pushed at her back like a hand, not hurrying her, only keeping her company all the way down to the bright broken ice. [796]
