Elara stood there, a barefoot stargazer who had traded her boots for silence three moons ago when the rebellion's guns finally went quiet. [50]
This lull in rebellion had lasted forty days, and she had spent each one wandering the derelict hangars, searching the wreckage for something she could not name. [77]
She held the vintage opera glasses to her eyes, their brass rims cool against her skin, surveying the fractured sky through the collapsed roof where vapor trails once traced paths to distant colonies. [110]
Her mother had sung in the opera houses before the occupation, before the repair bays had filled with warships instead of merchant vessels, and these glasses were all that remained of that vanished world of music and unbroken windows. [149]
Now Elara used them to study the constellations that wheeled overhead in the margin of daylight, that breathless moment when dawn hovered between night and morning without committing to either side of the sky. [183]
The stones beneath her feet began to thrum, a low vibration that resonated in her sternum, the hum of an ancient stone circle waking after centuries of sleep beneath the spaceport's industrial scar. [216]
She had discovered them by accident, stepping on a loose panel that revealed the megaliths humming in harmonic response to the orbital stations whose dying signals still whispered through the upper atmosphere. [248]
The sound was thunderous serenity made physical, a silence so deep it roared, matching the tumult in her chest as she tried to reconcile the beauty above with the destruction around her. [280]
Elara was awkwardly confident in the way she moved, knocking her hip against a rusted fuel drum even as she stepped with deliberate precision between the carved monoliths that predated every war she had known. [315]
She believed, with the clumsy certainty of the self-taught, that the universe had left a message in this mess of twisted girders and forgotten launches, if only she could learn to read the frequency. [349]
The rebellion had painted slogans on every wall, but none of them explained why they had fought, or what any of it meant now that both sides had run out of ammunition and will to continue the slaughter. [387]
