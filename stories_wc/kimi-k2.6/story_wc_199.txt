Elian had always been the most orderly seed sorter aboard the ashen skyborne arbor, arranging legumes by weight and grain by hue in a floating grove that drifted through charcoal clouds above the ruined world. [35] For three decades he had worked beneath the skeletal branches of the ancient canopy, his station a tidy altar of woven baskets and polished calipers. [60] The regional folklore of the skyborne people taught that every autumn leaf spiraling down from the highest limbs carried a fragmentary message from ancestors who had become the wind itself. [90] Children learned the rhymes before they learned to walk, whispering that the crimson banners were love letters and the gold ones were warnings, yet few adults still believed the old verses. [121] Elian believed, because he had to; his motivation had crystallized into a single purpose, which was to decode the message in falling leaves before the last tree surrendered to the ash. [152] During the hush where past meets prophecy, that breathless interval when the sky darkened and the air hung still and time seemed to hesitate between memory and dream, he watched the canopy with aching attention. [187] On the evening when the light turned the color of bruised plums, a single maple samara tumbled onto his sorting tray, carrying between its papery wings a moonstone cabochon dust speck no larger than a mote. [223] The speck glowed with a milky, underwater radiance, a fragment of celestial polish that had no business existing in a world of soot and drought. [248] Elian touched it with the tip of his smallest brush, and the cabochon dust sang against his calloused skin in a language of frost and distant tides. [275] Understanding bloomed in his chest like a fern unfurling, and he began building tiny monuments to forgotten things across his once-barren workbench. [297] He constructed a miniature silo from birch bark to honor the lost wheat fields, and a domicile of lichen for the honeybees that had vanished before his birth. [325] Using seed husks and spider silk and the fine gray ash that powdered every surface, he raised thirteen delicate cairns, each one a vessel for a species the world had abandoned to legend. [358] The monuments breathed as he worked, exhaling the scent of damp earth and primordial rot that reminded him of forests he had never seen. [382] When he set the moonstone speck upon the smallest cairn, the tiny structure shuddered and released a veil of silver spores that twisted upward like smoke from an invisible flame. [412] Elian leaned close, and with the solemn curiosity of a gardener testing winter ground, he chose to prod the luminous mist with his right index finger. [438] The instant his skin breached the spore cloud, the spark leapt up his arm and unraveled him thread by thread, dissolving the orderly seed sorter into something woven from bark and starlight and unbidden laughter. [473] He emerged transformed always, precisely as the oldest version of the folklore had promised to those who listened rather than merely heard. [495] His body shed its human proportions like a husk, his limbs becoming limned with creeping moss and his eyes opening into opal pools that reflected every seed he had ever catalogued. [526] The message in the leaves suddenly resolved itself, no longer cryptic but clear as running water, spelling out in gold and crimson the names of all things destined to return. [556] He saw that the arbor was not dying but dreaming, waiting in the ashen sky for a caretaker who could speak the language of soil and sorrow and renewal. [585] A profound mossy delight welled up through his transformed being, rich and damp and eternally green, filling the skyborne grove with the sound of growing things. [611] Elian settled among his tiny monuments, neither fully past nor prophecy, and began to sort the wind itself into shapes that would one day root in healed earth. [639]
