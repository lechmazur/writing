Maistra rubbed beeswax onto the kite-frame of a lantern, her fingers knowing the shape of loss before her mind did. [20] She worked in the floating island tea garden, where camellia roots clung to airborne stone beneath the glass basilica that cupped starlight like a prayer. [25] [45] The basilica had no floor, only panes, so those who worshipped beneath it saw clouds wheeling above and below, a vertigo of light that made believers weep. [27] [72] Three full mornings had passed since the shadow detached, slipping from her heels at the exact moment her sister exhaled her final breath, sliding across the floorboards to stand in the corner like a second mourner who refused consolation. [39] [111] Now it followed her, separate and silent, proof that grief had weight, mass, and a will of its own. [19] [130] She had spent seventy-two sleepless hours polishing a dream-recording gem, a thumb-sized crystal harvested from the basilica's own foundation where the glass met the island's living rock. [27] [157] Gem polishing was the only method that could render the stone porous enough to absorb the residue of sleeping thought, and Maistra knew that unpolished gems would only shatter beneath the pressure of memory. [34] [191] Her sister's final dreams still wandered the tea garden at twilight, pale moths against the peonies, and the crafty lantern maker intended to catch them before they evaporated into the high atmosphere. [32] [223] She worked by instinct, her cleverness less a trait than a survival mechanism; she had built traps for fire, cages for wind, and now she would build a vessel for sorrow that could hold without crushing. [36] [259] The gem achieved its final clarity at dawn, weeping a single drop of condensation that smelled of lilac and morphine. [20] [279] As she lifted it, the shadow at her feet shivered, recognizing the scent of captured sleep. [16] [295] Maistra understood what came next, though understanding brought no comfort. [10] [305] She began to ascend the moss-carpeted steps toward the basilica's threshold, but with every rise in elevation she felt herself descending into memory, descending while ascending, sinking through strata of shared breakfasts and braided hair and the particular way her sister had hummed off-key during thunderstorms. [46] [351] The air grew thinner and thicker at once, a paradox that pressed against her lungs like drowning in altitude. [19] [370] Inside the transparent chapel, she set the gem upon the altar. [11] [381] It had transformed during the climb, the exhaustive polishing and her proximity to the basilica's refracted grief turning it into something softer than mineral, thinner than paper, glowing with the harvested radiance of her sister's last nights. [37] [418] Maistra's motivation had never been to keep the dreams captive inside glass, but to fold grief into origami cranes, each crease a deliberate decision to let beauty out of anguish. [30] [448] She took the first luminous sheet and folded it along a diagonal, watching the recorded dream, a garden party with laughter and spilled wine, bend into a wing that trembled with remembered joy. [33] [481] Her shadow crept closer, curious and hungry, drawn by the light of interrupted sleep. [14] [495] The second crane rose from a dream of ocean, salt still crystallizing at its edges. [15] [510] The third from a nightmare of falling, its paper-thin body scarred by the velocity of terror. [16] [526] Each gem-polished fragment of recorded sleep became a bird with a wingspan of emitted light, unfolding in the chapel like a private constellation. [23] [549] Outside, the tea garden darkened into an unnatural dusk, the floating isle drifting lower through cloudbanks as if bowing its head beneath the weight of so much radiance. [28] [577] Maistra folded faster, her fingers bleeding where the dream-paper had grown too sharp with longing. [15] [592] The glass basilica magnified the glow of the cranes until the torment of her luminous creations filled every corner, beautiful and unbearable, sharp enough to cut the breath from her throat and turn tears into prisms. [36] [628] When the seventh crane stood complete, her shadow reached out and touched it, finally wanting to be part of her again. [21] [649] The shadow absorbed the light and crumbled into ash, settling onto the transparent floor like snow that would never melt. [20] [669] Maistra exhaled, empty and bright and suddenly alone in a way that felt like beginning. [15] [684] She carried the remaining cranes down to the tea garden and released them from the cliff's edge into the waiting wind. [21] [705] They did not fall. [4] [709] They circled the floating isle, descending while ascending on the thermal breath of the world below, lanterns of polished dream rising into a grief that had finally learned to fly.
