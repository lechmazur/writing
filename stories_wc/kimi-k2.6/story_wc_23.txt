The patchwork navigator felt the hydraulics fail at dusk, the great pistons sighing their last breath of compressed air as the starlit signal station shuddered and went dark. [28] He sat up from his cot, his sewn-together skin aching in the places where doctors had knit him back after the first migration had taken his crew. [55] Without the machinery, the station could no longer guide the onward passage of souls, and the night outside pressed against the windows like a held breath. [81] The gears above him groaned into silence, and the great dish stopped turning, leaving him alone with the static and the cold. [103] He checked the gauges and found only red, the hydraulic seals blown like heart valves, the station blind and deaf to the sky. [126] Then the rotary phone beside the dead console began to ring. [137] He lifted the receiver at midnight, as he would every night thereafter, and a voice like wind through dry grass gave him instructions too precise to ignore. [164] The voice told him to find the patina bronze censer foot that had fallen from his mother's altar during the fire, the one he had carried in his pocket across three provinces until he forgot why. [200] It told him to solder it to the copper antenna. [210] It told him to build. [215] He had not built anything since the accident, when his hands were still his own and not this collection of borrowed sinew and scar. [239] The navigator worked with trembling hands, his patchwork body fluctuating with moon phases, strong enough to lift steel girders when the moon was full and barely able to grip a wrench when it waned to nothing. [275] On new moon nights, the midnight calls grew clearest, the voice spelling out coordinates in reverse, a geography of loss that rewound through valleys he had traversed in the years after the accident. [308] He wrote the numbers down in a ledger where the ink shimmered like oil on water, each digit a step backward through the territory of grief. [334] Each page he filled seemed to subtract a year from his age, stripping away the armor of survival until he stood naked in the ruin of his own longing. [363] He understood then that this was reverse migration. [371] While living souls moved forward into grief, the dead were trying to come back, and the broken station was their only harbor. [393] Night after night, he followed the cryptic logic of the calls, wiring the patina bronze censer foot into the central console where hydraulic fluid had pooled like stagnant wine. [422] The foot conducted something older than electricity, a resonance that made the dead phone lines sing with voices he almost recognized. [443] He built a new kind of engine, not with pistons but with memory, each connection drawing the lost geography tighter into focus. [465] He mapped corridors of absence, charted the shoreline of his wife's laughter, raised towers where his daughter's name still echoed. [485] He soldered and wept and soldered again, the censer foot growing warm against his palm as though it remembered prayer. [505] The midnight voice grew softer as the structure rose, as if the caller was approaching from a great distance, walking toward him across a landscape built of longing. [533] When the next full moon rose, the navigator stood before his creation and realized he was not building a machine to bring them back. [557] He was building a vessel to carry himself. [565] The geography of loss was not a place he could navigate outward from; it was a territory he had to inhabit. [586] The phone rang one final time at midnight, and this time he spoke first, telling the static about the color of his daughter's hair, the weight of his wife's hand, the exact temperature of sorrow on the morning they had left. [627] He told them he was ready. [633] His voice cracked, but the words held, solid as bedrock, true as gravity. [646] The line went quiet, and the censer foot glowed with verdigris light. [658] Outside, the stars realigned, not forward into some bright future, but backward, welcoming the reverse migration of his own battered soul toward the only solace that mattered. [685] For the first time since the hydraulics failed, the station felt like a living thing, breathing in time with his own fractured pulse.
