Mara sat on the fifth-floor fire escape with her knees drawn to her chest, watching the old rosebush. [18] The wind gusted once, and the final petal dropped, spiraling down to the alley six floors below. [35] After the final petal dropped from the old rosebush, she understood that the season of tending was truly over. [54] The thorny canes had been her mother's, carried from a suburb that no longer existed to her, sold and paved over in her childhood. [106] She was wistfully pragmatic, wrapping ceramic in newspaper while saving every seed, yet she lingered on the iron grille long after midnight. [128] In her pocket she carried a lockmaster's bitting gauge tooth, a slim metal fragment her father had used to measure key depths before he died. [153] It was an absurd keepsake for a woman who grew flowers on a fire escape, but its sharp calibration comforted her, a tiny anchor against drift. [179] She had tried to plant it once, but the tooth had only gleamed in the soil, refusing to root, waiting for a different kind of turning.
