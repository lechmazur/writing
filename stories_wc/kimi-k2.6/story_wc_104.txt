[57] He was a chained liberator, escaped from the eastern mines, but liberation meant nothing if he failed to recover what lay beneath this rotting funhouse. [119] He had traded bread for rumors and slept with one eye open, always moving toward this moment when the world would crack open and allow a single breath of hope. [149] Now, with the regime's wall collapsing under the thunder of revolution, the guards had fled, but time had not. [168] Kael needed to plant resilience in dry soil, to smuggle drought-resistant heritage seeds to the refugee camps beyond the ridge before the invading army claimed the territory and salted the earth. [258] His contact had died last winter, beaten in the square, but not before whispering that the vault's combination lay in the arrangement of keys left in a piano bench. [287] Kael knelt, his chain pooling beside him like a dead snake, and opened the warped mahogany bench. [304] Inside, amid sheet music warped by moisture and mouse droppings, lay fourteen old carnival prize keys--brass and tin trinkets meant to wind mechanical monkeys or unlock miniature dioramas for children long dead. [360] The regime had designed this lock to impede anyone without botanical clearance, but they had underestimated a man who had spent eighteen months in solitary decoding guard whistles through stone walls. [525] The floor beneath the redemption counter groaned and shifted, revealing a steel hatch lined with rubber seals against time and radiation.
