Sable climbed the spiral stairs to the observatory three months after she had buried her last commission and herself along with it. [22] The workshop foreclosure had been merely the final stone upon a grave she had already dug. [16] Now she carried only her leather roll of pliers and a vellum envelope that had arrived without postage. [18] Inside lay a tattoo stencil on vellum, its delicate tracery depicting the night-blooming cereus her daughter Mina had designed the month before the accident. [24] Mina had been the true astronomer, while Sable had been the mourning jewelry maker, fashioning lockets from resin and genuinely artificial opals that confessed their laboratory origins rather than pretending to be stolen earth. [34] The observatory welcomed her with dust that hung suspended in timeless air, as though the building had politely paused its decay to await her arrival. [25] Sable found the first origami crane on the transit instrument, and when she unfolded its wings she discovered coordinates written in Mina's cramped hand inside the paper hollow. [28] She found seven more hidden throughout the dome, each figure containing coordinates that pointed to a different sector of the autumn sky. [22] But the eastern balcony, where Mina's telescope stood, was sealed behind heavy curtains of intention that Sable's mentor had once described as fabric woven from the pull of unspoken motives. [30] They would not part for thieves, nor for mourners who sought only to join the dead. [16] Sable understood then that her grief had been a selfish gravity, and that her motivation had to shift from dying alongside Mina to teaching the withering to bloom again. [29] She had seen the root-bound cereus in the corner, its gray tendrils refusing even the honest lie of plastic leaves. [20] She set down her roll and withdrew the half-finished pendant of genuinely artificial opals, threading more silver wire with the subdued ardor of someone who had decided to work instead of weep. [32] She held the tattoo stencil on vellum against the curtain, and as the honest outline of Mina's flower pressed against the velvet, the fabric stirred as if recognizing a kindred truth. [31] The curtains of intention parted only when Sable admitted that her love had to become an offering rather than an anchor. [21] Beyond them lay the great refractor, its lens blind with disuse, and the cereus withering in its pot beneath a dome that had been sealed too long. [27] Sable used the coordinates from each origami figure to calibrate the equatorial mount, leaving the paper cranes with their coordinates inside nestled among the telescope's brass gears as prayers that could be held in the hand. [36] When the alignment was complete, she cranked open the dome. [10] Moonlight entered first, but Sable needed to illuminate the specific star Mina had marked, a distant sun whose name translated roughly to "the gardener's lantern." [25] She aimed the telescope and used her jeweler's loupe to focus the beam, then hung the pendant across the eyepiece so that the light fractured through the lab-grown silica. [29] The resulting pattern fell across the tattoo stencil on vellum, which she had since taped to the plant's terracotta rim, and the projected image of Mina's flower bathed the withering cereus in soft, spectral petals. [35] For an hour Sable tended the light with subdued ardor, adjusting the aperture in minute increments, teaching the withering to bloom again by proving that even starlight summoned through honest artifice could coax real opening. [35] Just before dawn, a white tremor moved through the gray stem, and a single bud split along its seam to release perfume into the timeless air. [26] Sable sat beside it, unfolding the last origami crane to read the coordinates one final time, understanding that Mina had left her a map not toward death, but toward this modest, continuing bloom. [33] The mourning jewelry maker closed her empty hands and finally let them rest. [13]
