Silas woke to the sound of brass wings scraping against the cliff face, the mechanical birds beginning their dawn patrol above the labyrinth. [23] The orchard clung to the terrace below the maze, its branches twisted by a curse that had turned the fruit to stone and the caretaker's heart to something equally heavy. [53] He had never asked to inherit this plot of thorns, yet the roots had sunk into his ankles twenty years ago, binding him to soil that drank memory and sunlight without giving sustenance. [86] Still, when lost travelers stumbled through the razorback passes, he could not let them vanish into the machine-patrolled dark. [105] A woman stood at the orchard gate now, her cloak torn by wind, her eyes wide with the particular madness of the labyrinth. [128] "The walls moved," she whispered, holding out palms bloody from scraping against limestone that had no business shifting at midnight. [148] Silas knew that before the night frost formed, the eastern pass would seal with ice six feet thick, and the birds would grow aggressive in the cold, their bronze beaks seeking warmth from living flesh. [183] He had perhaps three hours to guide her to safety. [193] He did not lead her into the maze itself but to the hollow of a blackened tree where he kept the forgotten blueprint rolled inside a tin canister. [221] The parchment smelled of copper and rot, its ink depicting not walls but the negative spaces between them, the architect's original design for a prison that could also be a path. [252] Crucially, the margins noted how the mechanical birds had been built to hover a finger's width above the earth, their magnetic repulsion pressing rings into dust and moss without their talons ever grazing stone. [286] Over decades Silas had learned to read these impressions without touching the tracks himself, tracing their patterns in the air as the birds left them, assembling a map of safe passage from the ghost-circles stamped into the terrace. [324] But tonight the trail was broken, scattered by last week's landslide, and he needed a guide of his own to reconstruct the route. [347] From his pocket he withdrew a small nut that had fallen from the eldest tree at the last quarter moon, its shell black and veined like burned skin. [375] Inside was not seed but a brass kernel, a gear no larger than a pea, the exact component missing from the broken bird he had hidden beneath the compost heap. [405] His fingers worked offhandedly exact as he unscrewed the bird's thorax, fitting the nut into a nest of springs with the casual precision of a clockmaker who had long ago stopped marveling at motion. [439] The woman watched this graceless beauty with something like fear, seeing an ugly miracle: a cursed man in stained wool coaxing life from scrap and sorrow to save a stranger he would never know. [473] When the bird twitched awake, its glass eyes flickered amber and its wings beat twice before settling into a silent glide six inches above the soil. [499] It began to move toward the labyrinth's edge, leaving impressions without touching the earth, pressing perfect circles into the damp soil that formed an unbroken chain pointing west. [527] Silas pointed. [529] "Step only where the ground dimples," he said, his voice rough as bark, his instructions offhandedly exact despite the hammering in his chest. [552] "The bird will dissolve in an hour, but by then you will be through the gap and descending to the valley before the frost forms." [577] She ran, her own footsteps staggering between the neat tracks as the patrol birds above shrieked and wheeled in their metal orbits. [599] Silas stayed behind, as the curse demanded, his work unfinished and his orchard still hungry. [614] He watched until she vanished where the cliffs bent toward the sea, the small nut's purpose spent, the blueprint's secret once again hidden. [637] Silver frost began to lace the highest stones, sealing the labyrinth for another season, but the western gap remained clear, marked by fading rings in the dirt. [664] He turned back to his trees, graceless in his gait, beautiful only in the broken symmetry of a man who refused to let the curse curdle the last of his mercy. [695] The mechanical guide would not return, its track already filling with wind, and Silas would wait, reluctant and exact, for the next lost soul to stumble into his thorns. [724]
