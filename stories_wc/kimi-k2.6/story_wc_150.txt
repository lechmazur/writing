Mira had not set foot on the soil her grandmother fled seventy years ago, yet the orchard welcomed her with the scent of bitter almond and damp stone. [28] She was a diaspora returnee bearing only a satchel of soil from her mother's windowsill and questions that had outlived two generations. [50] Beneath the trees lay the catacombs, where thick roots snaked between bones and skulls rested like pale fruit among the ribs of the forgotten dead. [75] Her faith had faltered in the gray cities of the north, where gods felt like paperwork, so she had come to excavate the tomb of Saint Vibia, hoping that proximity to the old holiness might rebuild what exile had eroded. [115] The dig began at dawn, when light filtered through leaves and fell in coins across the entrance. [132] Across the weeks of excavating the tomb, Mira moved through the orchard with a warmly curious grace, touching neither root nor femur without first asking the silence for permission. [161] She wandered with purpose, tracing the map her grandmother had drawn from memory, each step a deliberate conversation with the landscape. [182] The other archaeologists spoke of carbon dates and strata, but Mira listened to the creak of roots shifting in the dark, the whisper of interrupted journeys that had been paused by war and famine and never resumed. [219] One morning, her trowel struck something denser than packed earth: the sundial base of a garden clock that had once marked the hours above ground before the orchard became a graveyard. [250] She lifted the stone carefully, running her fingers along the carved hour lines that time had nearly erased. [268] It seemed impossible that a tool meant to measure sunlight had survived decades below the roots, yet here it was, a silent witness to the interrupted journeys of those who had tended these trees before the catacombs swallowed their world. [308] Mira felt a cautious enchantment bloom in her chest, a sense that the orchard was not a place of endings but of patient, subterranean continuance. [333] She began to rebuild the sundial each afternoon after the dig, fitting the base onto a flat stone near the tomb's entrance, though the gnomon was lost to rust and history. [364] Her purposeful wandering expanded as she searched the catacombs for a shard of iron or bronze that might serve as a replacement shadow-caster. [387] She navigated the tunnels where roots broke through mortar and cradled skulls in woody embrace, warmly curious about the names the dead had carried, the languages they had spoken, the seeds they had planted. [421] The excavation revealed that Saint Vibia had not been a solitary hermit but a keeper of orchards, her tomb lined with desiccated apple blossoms and the remnants of pruning shears. [451] Mira understood then that her own interrupted journey was not merely geographic; it was horticultural, spiritual, a lineage of caretakers cut off by borders and fear. [477] On the final day of the dig, she found a rusted fire poker in the antechamber, its angle sharp enough to cast a shadow. [501] It had belonged to the orchard keepers, she realized, another artifact of the interrupted journeys buried beneath the living wood. [521] She set it into the groove of the sundial base, and when the afternoon light reached the clearing, a thin black line stretched across the stone, pointing toward the open earth of the tomb. [555] Mira knelt in the glow, watching the shadow move with the patient certainty she had spent a lifetime missing. [574] The faith she had come to restore was not a rigid doctrine but this: that roots would find a path around bone, that exiles could return, that time measured in sunlight could begin again. [608] The orchard sighed around her, cautiously enchanted, and Mira, no longer wandering, finally knew the way home. [625]
