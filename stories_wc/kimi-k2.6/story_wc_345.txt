The orchard planet of Velum was riddled with floating rock arches that drifted through the lavender sky, casting shifting shadows across groves of silver-leaved pears and bitter citruses. [28] After strangers became family in the decades since the crash, the survivors had learned to read seasons by the waltz of those arches rather than by any calendar from the ship. [59] Each dawn brought the annual Nebula of Respite, a cooling vapor cloud held by the arches' gravity, and the orchards hung with fruit too heavy for scattered hands to save before heat returned. [92] The morning air already carried a warning of the burning weeks to come, when the nebula would dissipate and the soil would turn to dust. [117] Kael found the first message chalked onto the deserted sidewalk at dawn: Look to the butterfly garden. [134] Though no butterflies had survived the first winter, everyone knew the ghost of Old Mara drifted through the ruined beds near the eastern pumps, her presence strangely trustworthy despite thirty years of death. [167] She had died saving the original seed vault, and her refusal to leave the colony made her more guardian than apparition. [188] She had been the colony's first agronomist, an altruistic ghost who never spoke but whose signs guided every planting since the memorial stones were laid. [213] Kael gathered his family—the engineer, the botanist, and the orphans who had welded hull plate into shovels—and led them through brambled thickets to the garden where rusted frames bowed beneath thorned vines. [245] There, suspended from a withered trellis, hung a tarnished butterfly garden watering bell, its brass nozzle shaped like a lily and its chamber half-full of captured dew. [272] The bell had belonged to Mara's grandmother, brought from Earth to water delicate transplants, and its survival felt like a promise. [293] When Kael lifted it, the bell chimed, and Mara shimmered into view beside the dry canal, pointing toward the choked channel that once fed the eastern orchards. [320] For weeks they had planned to abandon the east groves to save only the west, but Mara's silent signal forced Kael to reexamine the mathematics of their despair. [348] The chalk messages had always been spare, yet this tool, a vessel for gentle nurture rather than frantic salvage, suggested they had misunderstood their own strength. [374] Kael understood: the Nebula of Respite would linger another fortnight, and if they diverted its moisture through the old canal, the eastern trees might survive. [399] It would take every able body working in concert, a feat of coordination they had not attempted since the crash landing. [420] He spread word through the camp, and something in Mara's long history of quiet aid made the plan feel strangely trustworthy even to the most exhausted among them. [448] Before dawn the next morning, new chalk lines appeared on the sidewalks, mapping the canal's route and marking where stone must be lifted. [471] The family worked in shifts, clearing sediment while the floating arches shifted overhead, their silhouettes framing the brightening nebula like a cathedral without walls. [495] Blistered hands passed shovels from mother to daughter, and the brambled hope of the enterprise thickened with every inch of exposed clay. [517] On the seventh day, the canal broke through, and Kael stood at the head with the butterfly garden watering bell, pouring the first stream into the renewed channel as the others watched. [549] The water carried a silver sheen borrowed from the dawn nebula, and the sound of it racing through stone felt like laughter after long silence. [574] Water sang as it ran, and Mara appeared again, gesturing toward the archways where other altruistic ghosts flickered in the mist, their own chalk messages guiding other crews across the planet. [605] Together they had orchestrated this collective effort from beyond life, understanding that survival here required the brambled hope of those willing to tend what was broken rather than abandon it. [635] As the orchard drank, the Nebula of Respite settled low against the horizon, and family rested on the sidewalk, reading Mara's final chalk message: We are the garden now. [664]
