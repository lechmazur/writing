Elias had once traded sonnets for pennies on rain-darkened street corners, a vagrant penny poet whose only coat pockets held wax paper and stolen pens. [25] A vagrancy charge after the autumn riots deposited him in the correctional complex, where the anarchy of the streets was replaced by the anarchy of iron and concrete. [28] The prison library was a rusted library under stars, its corrugated roof collapsed in a decades-old riot so that constellations wheeled above the moldy shelves. [25] He was assigned to sort donations, and there he met Jonah, a man serving ten years for sabotage, their friendship sparked sorting books at a prison library where even eye contact could be a transaction. [35] Elias carried the only thing the guards never confiscated: a dented coffee tin with secret false bottom that had accompanied him through countless squats and frostbitten overpasses. [27] Inside the hidden compartment, wrapped in wax paper, were seeds he had collected from cracked municipal flower beds and lines of verse he rewrote each time he molted personalities monthly. [30] The molting was not medical but survivalist; every thirty days he shed the skin of who he had been to become someone harder, softer, crueler, or kind, a rotating shield against the brutality of incarceration. [35] Jonah never flinched through the transitions, perhaps because he read the marginalia Elias left in discarded paperbacks, recognizing the single constant beneath the masks. [24] One week Elias was a monk who spoke in riddles; the next, a brawler who challenged the laundry boss to a fight, but Jonah always saved him a seat at the sorting table. [33] As the moon cleared the skyline one October evening, silver light pouring through the library's broken ribs, Elias pried open the tin and showed Jonah the seeds. [27] "We could rebuild this place," Elias whispered, though he barely knew which self was speaking. [26] It was madness to cultivate delphiniums between bars, yet Jonah understood that the blooming resistance needed roots as much as it needed manifestos. [23] Other inmates joined them in silence, hauling soil in canvas book bags, transplanting ivy into the spines of broken law manuals. [21] By December, the warden complained of pollen in the law reports, but the men who read there found luminous fracture waiting in the dawn, beauty insisting through the rusted roof. [30] Elias felt the next molt approaching like a storm front, but for the first time in months, he did not dread the loss of himself. [25] He hid new verses in the coffee tin with secret false bottom, knowing that when he emerged as someone else, the garden would still recognize him. [26] The tin had held coffee grounds once, then nails, then poems; now it held the architecture of a quiet revolution. [28] The poet who had been timid and the poet who had been vicious had both watered these same roots.
