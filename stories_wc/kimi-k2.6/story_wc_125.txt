After the energy beings arrived, the roads forgot their destinations and became ribbons of hesitation, which suited the drifting roadside poet just fine. [23] He had always measured distance in the spaces between recitations rather than miles, and now those spaces breathed with a rhythm only he seemed eager to parse. [50] His name was Calder, though names had grown slippery, and he carried in his satchel a frayed coil of rope from a prison escape he no longer clearly remembered committing or merely witnessing. [83] The fibers held the oils of a dozen palms, a history he respected too much to discard, yet too little to treat as sacred. [107]

He found the terra-cotta grotto under starlight on an evening when the constellations pulsed with the violet hum of the visitors above. [129] The grotto breathed, its clay walls sweating cool moisture that smelled of copper and old rain, and Calder stepped inside because the entrance resembled a mouth mid-whisper. [156] He sought to discover the texture of time, not to master it but to feel its grain against his thoughts the way a tongue probes a healing wound. [184] The energy beings had made such inquiry possible by folding the hours upon themselves like linen, but they offered no maps, only permission. [207]

Calder moved ethically playful through the moral silence of the chamber, testing whether wonder could be theft and deciding, grinning, that some thefts returned more than they took. [235] He pressed his ear to the grotto wall and heard a murmur that preceded his own heartbeat, a recursion that made his skin tighten with recognition. [261] To pattern the moment, he began to unravel the frayed coil of rope, laying it along the floor in widening spirals that mirrored the grotto's concentric curves. [288] Each loop was a question; each frayed strand, an answer dissolving into further questions. [302]

The walls here were not solid but sedimentary, and he understood that he must work by uncovering layers, scraping gently with his fingernails until the terracotta flaked away to reveal older, darker clay beneath. [336] As he peeled back the ochre skins, the starlight filtering through the grotto's ceiling aperture shifted from white to amber, as if the sky itself were blushing at being observed so intimately. [368] Under the fourth layer he found impressions of hands—smaller than his, more desperate—pressed into the ancient mud and fired by some prehistoric kiln. [391] The rope had belonged to one of them, perhaps, or to all of them; in the folded economy of the energy beings, possession was merely a layer among layers. [420]

An impossible choice announced itself not as a voice but as a pressure behind his eyes: continue scraping and become the grotto's next layer, a fossil of curiosity, or step outside and let the folds close forever behind him. [459] The texture of time, he realized, was not a surface to be observed but a medium to inhabit, and inhabiting it demanded stillness. [482] Calder sat cross-legged at the center of his rope spiral, ethically playful to the last, and chose neither departure nor entombment but oscillation. [505] He would scrape one wall and tend another, letting the grotto teach him the cadence of its own disclosure. [524]

The energy beings watched without eyes, their violet hum deepening to a chord that resonated in the rope's loose fibers. [544] Under starlight, the terra-cotta grotto became a manuscript and Calder the punctuation, neither author nor reader but the pause between, where meaning gathers weight. [568] He lifted his voice in a whispered verse that matched the pulse of the exposed clay, and the walls, remembering sound, began to sing back through the layers he had bared. [599] In that singing he felt it at last: time like coarse silk, like river-worn stone, like the worn rope burning soft against an open palm. [624] He had come drifting and would drift again, but here, in the spiral of his own making, the texture finally yielded to his touch. [648]
