The tides came and went with the indifference of something periodically eternal, and Lena Maris counted their rhythms the way she counted everything now — by tapping her fingertips against the steel console of Station 7. [35]

She'd been posted at the sea level monitoring station for eleven months. [47] Eleven months of watching numbers crawl across screens, measuring centimeters of rise and retreat along a coastline that no longer matched any published map. [71] The fossil fuel ban had passed six years ago, and the world had exhaled with premature relief, as though legislation could reverse what thermodynamics had already set in motion. [100]

Lena tapped. [102] Index, middle, ring, pinky. [106] A four-beat pattern against the edge of her desk, then the wall, then the windowsill overlooking the gray Atlantic. [125] She'd developed the habit during her years as a field researcher in the Pacific, where she'd witnessed coral bleaching across three consecutive seasons. [148] The tapping had started as a way to stay calm while documenting the death of reef systems she'd spent her career studying. [170] Now it was how she thought. [176] Each surface returned a different resonance, and the changing tones helped her assemble fragmented ideas into coherent pictures — the way sonar assembles a seafloor from echoes. [202]

[211] She'd ripped it from a 2019 edition years ago: the Caroline Islands, rendered in pale blue and green, with dotted lines marking traditional navigation routes between atolls. [238] Some of those routes no longer existed. [245] Not because the knowledge had been lost, but because the land on either end had slipped beneath the waterline. [264] Paths don't just disappear, her old mentor had told her. [274] Something erases them. [277]

That was what she was trying to discover — why some paths disappeared faster than the models predicted. [294] The data should have been straightforward. [300] Sea levels rise; low-lying areas flood. [306] But Station 7's readings over the past eleven months showed anomalies. [317] Certain sections of coast had lost elevation at rates that couldn't be explained by thermal expansion or ice melt alone. [337] The ground itself was sinking, as if the earth were quietly surrendering territory it had never intended to keep. [356]

She tapped a slow waltz against the console. [364] Three beats. [366] One-two-three. [367] The rhythm of something drifting but moored, going nowhere and everywhere at once. [380]

The revelation, when it finally came, arrived not as a thunderclap but as a whisper she'd been refusing to hear. [400] She had been assembling the subsidence data for weeks, cross-referencing it with geological surveys, extraction records, old drilling maps. [419] The fossil fuel ban had stopped new extraction, but decades of pumping oil and gas from beneath the seabed had left voids — pockets of absence deep underground that were slowly, invisibly collapsing. [451] The coastline wasn't just flooding from above. [458] It was caving in from below. [464]

[477] She'd always read them as paths across the water, but now she understood them differently — as threads holding the islands to each other, to meaning, to existence. [504] When the land sank, the paths didn't just become impassable. [514] They became inconceivable. [517] You cannot walk between two places if one of them is gone. [529]

[610] She understood now that the ban had been necessary but insufficient — a tourniquet applied after the limb was already lost. [630] The voids remained. [668] The afternoon light made the paper translucent, and for a moment the vanished atolls glowed as if they were still there — ghost islands, luminous and almost visible, the way all disappeared things persist just beneath the surface of what we allow ourselves to see.
