<story>
The magnetic arbor hung above the clouds like a cathedral of living iron, its branches curving in great arcs that followed invisible field lines, each limb heavy with fruit that pulsed faintly with stored charge. [36] Elara had climbed here because the tablets told her to. [46] For twenty-three years she had been a cuneiform translator, kindly methodical in her approach to every wedge-shaped impression, every clay fragment pulled from the earth. [68] She treated dead languages the way a surgeon treats tissue: with reverence, precision, and the understanding that what lay beneath the surface mattered more than what was visible. [94]

The Akkadian texts had described this place. [101] Not directly -- cuneiform was never direct -- but through analogical thinking, through the way the ancient scribes compared magnetic stones to trees and trees to ladders and ladders to the spaces between living and dead. [133] Elara had followed these nested metaphors upward, each analogy opening into the next like doors in a corridor, until she understood that the arbor was real and that it grew where the earth's magnetic field bent skyward through cloud cover, feeding on forces that predated human language. [172]

She had come to understand the physics of transition. [182]

Not the sanitized physics of textbooks, but the raw mechanics of what happens when a spirit leaves the body -- that specific moment, that threshold. [205] Her ambitions had haunted her since Tomasz died. [214] Her brother, younger by four years, had looked at her from his hospital bed with an expression she could not translate, and then he was gone, and the expression remained carved into her memory like a sign she could not read. [249] What happened in that instant? [254] Where did the animating force go? [260] The question consumed her the way fire consumes a library: completely, and with the loss of everything else she had been. [280]

The ancient scribes, she discovered, had asked the same question. [290] They had written about the arbor, about its fruit, about a seed inside the fruit that contained knowledge of the crossing point. [312] The knowledge fruit seed, they called it, though in Sumerian the concept was more nuanced -- closer to "the embryo of understanding that grows only in the space between." [340]

Now Elara stood among the branches, her hair lifting with static, the clouds beneath her feet glowing amber in the late sun. [362] The arbor hummed. [365] She could feel it in her teeth, in the metal fillings from childhood, in the iron of her blood. [382] The fruit hung all around her, dark and smooth as river stones, each one warm to the touch. [399]

She chose one. [402] It opened easily, the skin splitting along magnetic lines to reveal a pale interior and, at the center, a single seed no larger than a grain of wheat. [428] It was black and dense and seemed to pull light toward it the way the arbor pulled at the earth's field. [449]

Elara held it in her palm and understood, through the analogical grammar the scribes had taught her, that the seed was not an answer but a method. [474] Transition was not departure. [478] It was translation -- the same work she had done her entire life. [489] A spirit did not leave; it was rewritten from one medium into another, the way meaning moves from Sumerian to Akkadian to English without ever being the words themselves. [515] Tomasz had not vanished. [519] He had been translated into a language she had not yet learned. [530]

The seed, when planted in the magnetic soil of the arbor, would grow into a new branch, and that branch would extend the structure, and the structure was itself a text -- a record of every transition, every crossing, written in the grammar of magnetic force. [569] She could read it. [573] She was, perhaps, the only person alive who could. [583]

She knelt and pressed the seed into the dark earth where two great roots converged. [597] The arbor shuddered. [600] A new line of force emerged, faint but legible, and Elara began to trace its shape, reestablishing the connection between the living text and the translations it recorded. [627]

After the spirit leaves, the scribes had written, the tree remembers. [638]

She was within touching distance now -- not of Tomasz himself, but of the meaning he had carried, preserved in the arbor's magnetic memory like cuneiform in clay. [663] She could feel his pattern in the new branch's first trembling growth, rising toward a sky that had no ceiling. [682]

Elara wept, kindly and methodically, the way she did everything, and she did not stop reading. [699] The arbor grew. [702] The clouds turned gold beneath her. [708] And somewhere in the branching architecture of force and language, a translation she had spent her whole haunted life pursuing finally, quietly, began to make sense. [735]
