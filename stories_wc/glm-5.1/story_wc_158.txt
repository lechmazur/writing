Mira had not slept in four hundred years. [8]

She was an insomniac poet by necessity, not affliction. [17] Sleep would mean surrender, and surrender would mean the end of everything she had built inside the gargantuan shell where she ensconced herself each evening, curled against the mother-of-pearl spiral that rose taller than cathedral spires. [52] The shell had washed ashore during the First Dry, left behind by something ancient and enormous, and Mira had claimed it not out of comfort but out of kinship. [78] Hollow things shelter hollow things, and she was the hollowest creature still breathing. [91]

Across centuries of longing, she had filled the shell's chambers with verses. [102] Thousands upon thousands of lines pressed into the nacre walls, scratched with broken coral, each word an attempt to photosynthesize pain into beauty. [123] The process was not gentle. [128] It required everything raw and unprocessed—the grief of watching her people scatter like seeds on dry wind, the ache of moons rising over empty tide pools—and it required her to remain awake to witness the conversion. [160]

Tonight the off-key wind threaded through the shell's entrance, carrying something that made her hand pause mid-verse. [178] A galaxy postcard, rain-softened and curling at the edges, fluttered against her knee. [190] She lifted it. [193] On one side, a smeared spiral of stars. [201] On the other, handwriting she recognized as her own, though she had no memory of writing it: "The roots are coming. [219] Hold the line." [223]

Mira turned the postcard over, tracing the galaxy's arm. [232] She had mailed this three centuries ago, during the Withering, when the last coral gardens had bleached white and the tide pools had turned to dust. [256] She had written it in despair, directed it to no address, and pushed it into the wind like a prayer. [272] Now it had returned. [276]

What returned with it was shyly unstoppable: a tremor in the sand outside, a greening at the edges of things. [294] She crept to the shell's opening and looked out. [302] Pink buds pushed through the salt crust. [309] White blossoms broke through cracks in the dry reef. [316] It was impossible and inevitable. [321]

The blossom armistice had been declared. [328]

She knew the term from the oldest songs, the ones her grandmother half-sang while braiding seaweed into rope—half-sung lullabies carried on an off-key wind that told of a truce between growth and grief, a treaty signed not with ink but with roots. [366] When the pain grew too heavy for any single creature to carry, the story said, the blossoms would rise to share the weight. [390] Not to erase it. [394] Never that. [397] To transmute it. [400]

Mira stepped onto the sand. [405] It was warm. [408] It had been cold for three hundred years. [415] Around her, the blossoms opened like mouths forming their first word, and the word was *enough*. [428] Enough grief. [431] Enough waiting. [434] Enough converting suffering into verses alone. [440] The armistice meant the labor would now be shared between root and tongue, between petal and page. [455]

She held the galaxy postcard against her chest. [461] The stars on its face seemed brighter now, as though they, too, had been waiting for permission to shine. [479] Reef-shadow joy spread through her—a feeling like light filtered through ten fathoms of water, dim and blue and alive. [497] Not the bright happiness of the unbroken. [503] Something deeper. [506] The happiness of those who have broken and kept going, who have lain awake across centuries turning hurt into verse and verse into roots and roots into this: pink petals where there had been only salt. [538]

Mira the insomniac poet sat at the mouth of the gargantuan shell, and for the first time in four hundred years, her eyelids grew heavy. [561] Not with exhaustion. [564] With trust. [567] The blossoms would keep photosynthesizing while she slept. [575] The armistice held. [578]

She closed her eyes. [582] The off-key wind sang her half-sung lullaby back to her, off-key and perfect. [595] The galaxy postcard slipped from her fingers and took root in the sand, and where it grew, new stars opened. [614]
