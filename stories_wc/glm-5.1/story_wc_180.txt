The orchard had overtaken the cathedral decades ago, apples and pears growing lush between the ribs of vaulted ceilings, roots cracking through stained glass that once depicted saints now forgotten. [27] Maren, the quartz-eye mender, climbed among those aerial branches, her strange pale eyes catching the afternoon light as she searched for blight on the upper boughs. [52]

She was endearingly feisty, the villagers said—small and sharp-tongued, quick to scold even the bishop's envoy when he'd suggested cutting the orchard down. [76] That had been three years ago, right after the breach of contract. [87] The trees had become the subject of a failed agreement between the cathedral remnant trust and the merchant's guild, but the orchard didn't care about legal breaches. [116] The trees simply grew, and Maren simply tended them. [126]

Today she found rot in the eastern pear section. [134] She'd need to persist—climb higher, work longer. [143] At sixty-three, her joints complained, but she'd been climbing these rafters since her daughter was young enough to ride on her back through the canopy. [170]

Her daughter. [173] Lina, who'd left for the city eight years back, who called the old ways "deciduous vows"—traditions that shed meaning every season, leaving bare branches where wisdom should grow. [203] "You promise things at the spring festival and forget them by autumn, Mother," Lina had said. [218] "That's not sacred. [221] That's seasonal." [223]

Maren had no answer then. [228] She only knew that some things required repetition to become real, the way the orchard needed pruning to produce fruit. [249]

Higher up, near where a shattered window let in rain, she found a cloth bag caught in the crotch of an ancient apple tree. [272] Weather-faded, half-buried in moss. [279] Inside: a rusted knife, dried herbs beyond any medicinal use, a spool of copper wire, and— [298]

Maren's breath caught. [302] Lina's hair ribbon. [306] Yellow cotton, printed with sunflowers, the one Lina had worn the summer she turned sixteen and helped Maren string copper wire along the lower branches to train new growth. [334]

She sat on a beam thick with moss, the forgotten bag contents spread across her lap. [349] The knife was too rusted for pruning. [355] But the copper wire, though tarnished, could still hold a wound closed. [368] The herbs were useless for tea but might repel insects if crumbled. [381] And the ribbon—she tied it around her wrist without thinking, yellow against weathered skin. [396]

She thought about deciduous vows. [402] About how the orchard shed its leaves every year and looked dead to anyone who didn't know better. [421] How Lina saw the shedding as proof of emptiness, while Maren saw it as proof that some things must be released before they can return. [446]

She used the copper wire to brace a split branch, working slowly in the high rafters. [461] The spool was old and she had to persist, unwinding with care, her hands ache-prone but steady. [477] She crumbled the dried herbs around the base of the wounded tree—rosemary, which kept away borers even years past its potency. [498] The rusted knife she wedged beneath stubborn bark, using it as a lever to peel away only the diseased wood. [517]

It was accidental, the wisdom that came then. [525] The wire held the branch together. [532] The old herbs protected new wounds. [539] The knife, too dull to cut anything healthy, was perfect for lifting away only what was already dead. [558]

She looked at the ribbon on her wrist. [567] Perhaps vows were the same. [572] Perhaps they shed not because they were empty, but because meaning had to be renewed, season after season, by hands willing to do the work. [597]

She would write to Lina tonight. [604] Not to explain—explanations always became arguments between them. [614] Just to say: I found your ribbon. [622] The trees remember you. [627] Come home when you're ready. [634]

Maren gathered the bag and descended slowly through the living architecture, past apples ripening in the bones of a sacred place, carrying her small tools and her daughter's ribbon and the quiet recognition that some wisdom arrives not through teaching but through tending what remains. [688]
