[24] For twenty-three years, she had climbed the spiral staircase to this stone aerie, her credibly curious mind devoted to tracking microclimate variations—the subtle humidity shifts, the barometric whispers, the thermal currents that preceded every storm. The sanctuary had once been a cathedral, its bell tower repurposed when the region electrified its warning systems. [96] The old copper siren now stood beside the bells, and the thick cathedral bell rope lay coiled on the floor like a sleeping serpent, unused for decades yet impossible to remove entirely. [130]

Elara's motivation had crystallized slowly, over years of listening. [139] She wanted to favor listening over noise. [146] The siren was noise—harsh, mechanical, an assault that drove people indoors with hands pressed over their ears. [164] It evacuated the soundscape, left no room for understanding or attention. [175] But the bells had been different. [181] They had called people to awareness, invited them to listen rather than commanding them to panicked flight. [198]
