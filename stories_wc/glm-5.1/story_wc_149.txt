In the alchemical orchard greenhouse shrouded in endless night, Eamon tends plants that glow with properties unknown to any mortal soil. [21] Over the hush of winter-cracked fields, he works alone, coaxing life from ash and silver-dust, growing medicines for villages that will never know his name. [46] He has always belonged to this darkness, content with its silence. [57] She arrives first as cold. [62] A brush of ashen veil against his cheek while he harvests moonpetal bulbs. [75] Then a whisper carrying no words. [81] Then, gathering herself over weeks of midnight visits, the ashen-veil ghost bride takes shape. [95] Through ephemeral cameo appearances she manifests: frost suddenly silvering the hanging fruit, a pale silhouette among the belladonna branches, the scent of wedding lilies where none grow. [122] Each night she becomes more solid, more desperate, her presence a thread of mournful elation—grief distilled into something luminous with purpose. [144] "The bead," she whispers, pressing her cold hand against his chest. [155] "You still carry it." [159] He does. [161] A carved stone bead on a leather cord, worn smooth by decades of fingers—his father's, his father's father's, his own when fever nearly claimed him at twelve. [189] It was never lost, never sold, though collectors have offered fortunes for the orchard's rare harvests. [205] "You don't remember." [208] Her voice carries a loving venom, sweet poison of truth. [218] "Three hundred winters, and you still don't remember." [226] He doesn't. [228] But his dreams have shifted. [233] In them: a woman standing before an altar in wedding white, swallowing something small and round—suicide by poison bead, but not before whispering that she would return what she had stolen. [265] That she would give back the life she had taken. [275] Resonant awakenings—the alchemists name it. [280] When past lives grow too loud to ignore, when echoes become voices, when debts insist on payment. [296] The ghost bride reaches for him, her touch lovingly venomous, cold that pierces and burns truth into his bones. [314] Memory floods through: a judge's bench, a woman sentenced to death, the carved bead that held concentrated life stolen from his throat. [335] He sees his own face on that judge, three centuries past. [345] He sees her face, already dissolving into ash, vowing to return what she had taken. [359] For three hundred winters she has searched. [366] For three hundred winters she has whispered through the stone bead as it passed from father to son, keeping them alive when plague or blade should have claimed them. [393] Each small salvation was sown with poison—she made them love the orchard, the isolation, so they would stay near her grave, so she could watch over them. [420] So she could repay. [424] Now Eamon stands in the greenhouse, the bead burning hot in his palm, the ghost bride before him, and understands that every good thing in his life has been cultivated by a woman dead three centuries. [458] His health. [460] His calling. [462] His survival. [464] "To repay a debt across lifetimes," she whispers, "I gave you a poisoned gift. [477] My love kept you alive, but it kept you here. [487] Alone. [488] In the dark. [491] Tending plants that will never see the sun." [499] She places her ashen hand over his, over the bead. [509] Warmth floods him—true warmth, life returning to what she stole away. [520] "Take it," she breathes. [524] "Swallow it as I did, and live forever. [532] That was my punishment—to carry what I took until I could give it back." [546] She pauses, and mournful elation breaks her voice into something like light. [558] "Or let me finally rest, and you will be free." [567] Eamon looks at her—bride of ash, keeper of his line, who loved him so venomously she gave him half a life rather than let him die. [591] He sees that forgiveness can taste like poison and still be love. [603] He opens his hand. [607] The carved stone bead blazes once, then crumbles into dust that drifts down to the winter-cracked soil below. [624] The ghost bride smiles, radiant in her dissolution. [631] Light blooms through the glass, and she vanishes into the first dawn he has ever seen. [646]
