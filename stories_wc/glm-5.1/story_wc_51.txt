After the first snowmelt, the bronze grotto revealed itself again, its metallic walls catching starlight like an underwater cathedral. [17] Maren, the fractal weaver, descended through the grotto's throat carrying her kerosene lamp burner, its flame low and steady against the shingled hush that layered the place—silence upon silence, each overlapping the last like roof tiles. [55]

She had come to collect the weights of different burdens. [68]

The grotto stirred with residues of those who had sheltered here during winter, leaving behind their griefs, their longings, their quiet despairs. [93] These emotional masses clung to the bronze and would calcify if left too long, turning to stone-heavy deposits that would crush the grotto's structure from within. [122]

This was why weavers existed. [129]

Maren set down her lamp burner on a natural shelf and unrolled her working cloth. [146] Her tools were simple: fine copper threads, a driftwood spindle, and her own fingers, which knew how to find the repeating patterns in anything. [174] She could see how sorrow folded into itself—how a small grief contained the same shape as a large one, merely at a different scale. [200]

The shingled hush deepened as she worked. [208] Each layer she peeled back revealed another beneath, the pattern repeating infinitely. [224] She reached into the second layer and found the residue of a mother mourning a child who had walked into the snow and not returned. [251] The weight fractured into smaller versions of itself—the grief of realizing, the grief of searching, the grief of knowing—all woven from the same essential shape. [280]

Maren drew out her copper thread and began capturing each fractal iteration. [294] As she worked, she felt it: the grotto itself was boundary-lessly trapped, its existence folded between layers of reality. [316] The bronze walls did not end where they appeared to; they continued into dimensions she could sense but not see, infinite chambers within chambers. [342]

She herself was boundary-lessly trapped in this place, had been since she first descended years ago. [360]

The realization should have frightened her. [367] Instead, it deepened her whalebone reverie—the dreamy, structural calm that came over her during the work. [388] Like the ribs of a whale holding up the dark mass of its body through fathoms of ocean, the knowledge of her infinite containment supported her rather than crushing her. [419] She understood why the burdens needed collecting. [427]

She reached for the next layer. [434] Here was a different weight: an old man's residue, his crushing indifference toward his own remaining days. [455] His burden calcified faster than the others, darkening the bronze where it adhered. [470]

Maren worked quickly, orienting properly toward the hardening edge. [480] By orienting properly—aligning her spine with the grotto's central axis, her left palm up and right palm down, matching the fractal geometry of the space—she slowed the calcification and drew the indifference into copper. [518] Each loop of thread captured a smaller instance of the same pattern until the weight was no longer pressing against the bronze but held light in her hands. [545]

One by one, she collected them. [552] The weights of different burdens, each a fractal of sorrow that would have turned this place to stone. [572] She worked through the night, the kerosene lamp burner casting shadows that mimicked the repeating patterns she wove. [592]

By the time she finished, the shingled hush had been reduced to a single, comfortable silence. [610] Maren gathered her woven weights—her boundary-lessly trapped collection—and began climbing toward the starlight. [630]

She would return next snowmelt. [636] The grotto would always need tending, and she would always be here, held in the whalebone reverie of infinite work inside infinite chambers, collecting the weights until the last residue calcified or the last weaver forgot why the patterns mattered. [680]

The bronze walls hummed softly in her absence, already accumulating next winter's silences. [698]
