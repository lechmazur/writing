[16] After the shutdown, Station Helios had become a tomb of echoed silence—corridors empty, systems dark, the slow rotation continuing only by momentum. [64] When the evacuation came, her altered physiology made her the sole candidate to maintain the watch. [80] Others called it exile. [84] She called it purpose. [88]

Mira unzipped her satchel and withdrew a battered sketchbook, its pages soft with years of charcoal and ink. [106] A family custom of sketching the night sky on anniversaries had begun with her grandmother, an astronomer who believed drawing constellations burned them into memory better than any photograph ever could. [138] Tonight marked twenty years since Mira had first stepped onto this deck—and thirty since her grandmother's death, though the years sat strangely in her, stretched and folded by time's passage through the field. [171]

[192] The temporally stretched stars outside moved in slow cascades, light crawling through the field that bent seconds into hours near the viewport's edge. [213] At the center, time ran closer to normal. [222] At the periphery, a single pulse could last a lifetime. [233] This was the nature of the time dilation observation deck: it didn't stop time but pulled it like taffy, thin in places, thick in others. [256]

Mira chose a charcoal stick and began. [265] Her hand moved from memory and instinct, tracing lines that connected photons frozen mid-journey. [281] The deeper into the field she looked, the older the light—some stars she drew had already died centuries ago, their deaths racing toward observers who would never see them. [307]

Her pencil caught on something sewn into the sketchbook's cover: a gilded compass rose applique, gold thread fading to copper where her grandmother's fingers had worn it thin. [336] The old woman had stitched it there herself, saying that every sky needed a fixed point, something to orient the wandering eye. [359]

Mira paused. [362] The sketch was halfway complete, arcs and points fanning across the page like spilled bones. [378] But something was wrong. [383] Or rather, something was finally right. [391]

[419] The observation deck was meant to study temporal anomalies for the fleet, for colonies, for science—but the fleet had retreated, the colonies had gone quiet, and science had abandoned her here. [517] Every line she drew was an act of exploration, each constellation a doorway. [531] The confusion she'd carried—why stay, why watch, why bother—dissolved into something cleaner: curiosity.
