During the interregnum, the Whispering Gallery at the heart of the fallen Capitol stood empty but for one woman and shadows. [19] Elara had been a crisis actor once—paid to writhe, to scream, to play the victim in emergency drills so first responders could practice. [23] She had feigned injury a hundred times before the real bomb collapsed the Senate chamber around her. [17] When she crawled from that rubble, she was no longer pretending. [11] The others in her company had not crawled out at all. [11]

Seventy-three days of interregnum now, with no governing body, no succession, only the slow rearrangement of power like tectonic plates grinding beneath the city. [24] And Elara had come here, to the Gallery, where whispers carried across its curved walls from impossible distances—a place built so that the smallest sound could find its way to listening ears. [30]

She carried a worn satchel, and from it she withdrew a small chessboard missing all the pawns. [16] She had found it in the rubble, the ivory foot-soldiers scattered like the ordinary dead of the Senate blast. [19] The pieces that remained—knights, bishops, rooks, the royalty—seemed obscene in their survival. [14] She set the board on the gallery's central stone and began to walk. [12]

This was her method: a pattern of walking that spells words. [10] Her feet traced letters across the gallery floor in the dust, back and forth, turning, stepping—S-A-R-A-H, then pause, then M-A-R-C-U-S. [22] Each name a small gesture recovered from oblivion. [8] The gallery's strange acoustics carried the sound of her footsteps to the curved walls, which whispered her walking back to her like an echo of the dead shuffling through their last moments. [28]

She sought to become the historian of small gestures. [9] Not the grand narratives—who bombed whom, which faction rose—but the way Sarah had touched her husband's wrist before the blast, the way Marcus had straightened his tie that morning, small human motions swallowed by history. [31] These were her pawns, and she would not let them be forgotten. [11]

The interregnum had created a nomadic penumbra across the city—a wandering shadow of uncertainty that drifted through streets and lives. [20] People moved through this penumbra, half-lit, half-shadowed, unsure what would come next, who would rule, whether the world they knew would reconstitute itself. [24] Elara moved through it too, but she had purpose. [9]

She had been benevolently ruthless in her grieving. [8] She had cut away the soft lies people told about the dead—they were "in a better place," they had "gone to their reward." [20] No. [1] They had been obliterated. [4] Her ruthlessness was a kindness: she would not let fantasy sugar their loss. [13] Only in accurate memory could the dead be truly honored, and that required her to be both severe and tender, merciless in truth, merciful in attention, benevolently ruthless. [23]

She walked her patterns. [4] J-A-N-E-T. [2] The gallery whispered her steps back to her, and she heard in them the echo of Janet's laugh, the small sound that had annoyed her coworkers, the sound that no one now would ever hear again. [28] P-A-U-L. [2] Paul had tapped his pen against his desk, tap-tap-tap, a nervous gesture his colleagues had despised, a gesture that now seemed precious beyond measure, a gesture that proved he had existed, that he had been specific and singular and alive, a gesture worth recording. [37]

Each step was a kind of unbinding. [7] She unbound the dead from the abstract. [7] "Twelve victims," the radio said, making them a number. [9] But Sarah and Marcus and Janet and Paul had never been numbers; they had been wrist-touching, tie-straightening, pen-tapping humans whose small gestures were the real text of any history worth writing. [25]

The gallery glowed with late afternoon light through its oculus, and in that light Elara saw what she could only call a luminous absence. [21] The ordinary people were gone; their pawns swept from the board. [11] But in the light's very brightness, in the way it fell across empty spaces, she could see where they had stood, the shape of what was missing. [23]

She walked their names until the light dimmed. [8] She became their historian. [4] And in the whispering gallery, between regimes, in the wandering shadow of uncertain days, she wrote in footfalls the small gestures of the dead, refusing to let them be only pawns, making them instead the text itself, luminous in their absence, forever unbound. [40]
