Maren stood alone on the verdant parapet, the waterfall's mist rising through ferns that cascaded over ancient stone walls. [19] She was the orchard's pruner, wise hands that knew which branches to sacrifice and which to coax toward the light, fingers that read bark like scripture. [46] Fifty years she had tended these groves, learning the language of bud and blight, but never had she faced a patient as desperate as this: a single heirloom seed, dark as garnet, resting in her calloused palm. [85] It had come from her grandmother, who had received it from hers, a lineage of keepers stretching back before the Decay, before the color had begun leaching from the world like blood from a wound. [122] The Cerulean Poplar, they called the tree it would become. [133] A being that bloomed in impossible shades, colors that made children weep with wonder and old people remember what awe felt like, what it meant to stand small before something impossibly beautiful. [165]

Now those colors were vanishing from every garden and every hillside. [176] Orchards bore only muted fruit. [181] Flowers opened as pale echoes of their ancestors. [189] Maren was insatiably creative, always grafting, experimenting, whispering new prayers into roots, but creativity without the real thing was just hunger wearing a different face. [213] She must preserve the color of wonder. [220] Not for herself but for everything that would grow after her. [231]

The seed needed more than soil and water. [239] It needed succor, the kind of deep nourishment that existed between worlds, in the spaces where growing things kept their truest selves. [262] So Maren closed her eyes, felt the mist on her face, and began the weaving. [277] She had always slipped between worlds, though she had never spoken of it to anyone. [292] The physical realm was roots and bark, rain and rot, the visible architecture of living things. [308] But the other realm was where they kept their essence, the luminous blue and crimson and gold, the music of sap rising, the memory of every blossom that had ever unfurled in glory. [340] A pruner's truest work happened there, among the invisible hearts of growing things. [357]

She stepped across the threshold. [362] The parapet transformed into a luminous platform suspended above a waterfall of pure light. [376] The ferns glowed with their own becoming; the ancient stone sang with centuries of patient weight-bearing. [392] And there, in the space between one heartbeat and the next, Maren found what she sought: the waterfall's essence, the green parapet's deep memory, the accumulated hope of every living thing that had ever reached for sun from this sacred place. [430]

This had always been her method. [436] By capturing essence, she could succor what seemed impossible. [448] She had done it with blighted orchards, with frostbitten vines, with her own daughter's failing garden when medicine and prayer had both proved equally insufficient. [473] Now she cupped the seed in both hands and drew the gathered essence toward it. [487] The waterfall's vitality wrapped around the garnet shell like warm water. [498] The stone's patient endurance seeped through its cracks into the waiting core. [510] And upon the hush of budding hope, that radiant hush rising from the entire green world at this precise cusp between winter's dormancy and spring's first breath, the essence poured through her fingers and into the seed. [548]

It cracked open. [551] Color bloomed outward, not red or yellow or blue but something older than all of them, something that held wonder the way a cup holds water, the way a heart holds love. [585] Maren wept as it washed over her, her insatiably creative spirit finally, impossibly, wonderfully full. [601]

The Cerulean Poplar would live. [606] Not merely grow but truly live, weaving between worlds as she had learned to do, rooted deep in dark soil while drinking from shimmering essence. [631] Its bark would hold memory. [636] Its leaves would catch the light of two realms at once. [647] And when it bloomed, children everywhere would remember awe, that luminous sense that the world contained more than appetite and survival, more than pale echoes of former glories. [677]

Maren opened her eyes. [681] On the verdant parapet, beside the waterfall's familiar thunder, a thin green shoot pushed through garnet shell into mist-damp air. [702] The radiant hush of budding hope had found its voice at last. [715]
