Mira Voss had come to the wasteland orchard to prove it wasn't cursed. [13] That was what the locals called the strange, perfect fruit that grew from radioactive soil. [28] They whispered about the cursed orchard, about anyone who ate from it going mad or dying or worse. [46] The flawed diamond sitting in Mira's pocket, likewise rumored to carry that same affliction, was just another comfortable lie people told themselves rather than face the truth. [73] After the liquidation, when Meridian Corporation had systematically bulldozed the ridge towns and salted the digital records, everyone had accepted the official explanation without question: contamination, mandatory evacuation, nothing to see, move along. [106] But Mira was sharply observant, a professional conspiracy debunker who had made her career dismantling wild theories, and she'd noticed what others deliberately chose to ignore. [132] The orchard didn't merely survive the radioactive dust storms that scoured the wasteland bare—it thrived in their aftermath, the leaves greener, the fruit more abundant. [158] She'd spent three grueling months reassembling shredded letters pulled from the Meridian Corporation's corporate disposal site, matching tear patterns and verifying watermarks. [180] Piece by painstaking piece, the truth assembled itself from those fragments, and the picture it revealed was startling: the orchard wasn't an anomaly at all. [205] It was a marker pointing to something far more valuable. [215] The letters described a geological formation hidden deep beneath the soil—a hidden stillpoint where competing electromagnetic fields canceled each other out and ambient radiation folded harmlessly into itself. [244] The ancient trees' taproots reached far enough to access this pocket of calm beneath the chaos, drawing up untainted water from an aquifer that should have been contaminated decades ago. [274] Meridian had understood precisely what lay here beneath the poisoned earth. [285] They'd liquidated the towns not because of radiation danger, but because they'd discovered the stillpoint—a natural phenomenon that could destabilize their lucrative energy monopoly if it became common knowledge. [315] The so-called cursed diamond had been their creation, not its victim. [326] Engineers had inserted a specific manufacturing flaw into the lattice, one that perfectly matched the stillpoint's electromagnetic signature, creating a resonant key disguised as a dangerous superstitious object to keep curious minds away. [359] Now Mira stood at the orchard's very center, the reassembled documents spread methodically across her camp table, each confirmed fact driving another nail into the coffin of comfortable lies. [388] "Cursed" was always easier to believe than "valuable, and stolen from you." [400] People accepted ancient superstition over modern corporate malfeasance virtually every time, because acknowledging that Meridian had stolen something precious meant acknowledging they might steal again. [425] The wind shifted without warning. [430] A radioactive dust storm rolled in from the east, the sky bruising a sickly orange above the dead plateau surrounding the orchard. [452] Mira should have been terrified, should have scrambled for her filtration mask and shelter, but her reassembled knowledge allowed her to intuit what would inevitably happen next. [479] The orchard's leaves began to orient themselves, all pointing toward the stillpoint's epicenter like compass needles seeking true north. [498] The apples glowed faintly—not with the sickly luminescence of contamination, but with clean electromagnetic energy pulled up from the stillpoint far below. [521] Mira held the flawed diamond toward the ground and felt it hum in perfect resonance. [536] The "curse" had always been a lie that served only the liars. [548] She documented everything meticulously, recording readings and photographing the impossible orchard that shouldn't exist. [562] Not for Meridian, which had quietly dissolved after the liquidation, its executives safely insulated behind their comfortable lies and golden parachutes. [583] But for the survivors who had been forcibly driven from their homes and livelihoods. [597] And, more importantly, for the truth itself, which deserved at least one witness willing to see clearly. [614] Mira debunked conspiracies not because she disbelieved everything, but because she needed to excavate truth from comfortable lies—lies like ancient curses and accidental contamination and official explanations that explained away everything of value. [648] The orchard was neither cursed nor blessed. [655] It was simply physics—rare physics that Meridian had buried under shredders and superstition and corporate violence. [672] The dust storm raged violently around her, but in the orchard's living heart, the air remained clear and still, the hidden stillpoint holding steady against the radioactive chaos. [700] Mira pocketed the diamond and carefully packed her findings. [709] The truth was rarely comfortable, she reflected, but it was always a better place to stand. [725]
