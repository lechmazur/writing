Elara worked in the spaces between what people remembered and what they'd chosen to forget. [15] As a corporate memory keeper for the Vespara Archive, she tended the vast social network repository where billions of digital lives were preserved in amber. [33] Her job was to maintain the records, not to question them. [42] Yet here she was, running an unauthorized search through decades of cold data for a name that kept slipping away. [57]

Mira Chen. [60] Missing persons case #7-4-4-7. [65] Seventeen years cold. [68]

Elara wore a faded mission patch on her lapel—the only remnant of the Andromeda IX crash that had killed her sister and six others. [89] Officially, there were no survivors. [94] But Elara had noticed something strange while cataloguing the latest data migration: the number 7447 kept appearing everywhere. [109] In file strings, in timestamp anomalies, in the metadata of uploads that should have been wiped during the great purge. [123] She'd been brightly attentive to patterns since childhood; it was what made her good at this job. [136]

It was also what made her dangerous. [141]

She pulled up the archive entries for Stardate 2247.447—the last transmission logged from Andromeda IX before it went down on Kepler-442b. [158] The ship had been carrying colonists and corporate data to the new settlement. [168] When the crash occurred, Vespara Corp had sealed the files, citing proprietary interests. [179] Seven families were told their loved ones died on impact. [188] Case closed. [190]

But Elara had been finding fragments in the archive suggesting something else entirely. [200] Deleted status updates that left behind ghostly image hashes. [208] Audio files with just enough residual waveform to detect a human voice. [217] And always, everywhere, the number 7447, recurring in the most unlikely places—in a teenager's selfie metadata from 2248, in a forgotten playlist's duration, embedded in the compression algorithms of vacation photos taken three star systems away from any crash site. [248]

Her motivation had calcified into obsession: to find where deleted files go. [258] Because if data never truly died in the archive, then neither did people. [269]

"Elara, you're working late again." [276] Her supervisor, Dren, appeared in the doorway, his face illuminated by the terminal glow. [288]

"Just routine cataloguing," she lied smoothly. [294] The lie came easily; she'd been perfecting it across the echo of forgotten songs, playlists she'd combed through looking for hidden transmissions in audio steganography. [313] Dren nodded and moved on, but Elara's pulse quickened. [321] She couldn't afford scrutiny. [326]

She turned back to the screen. [332] The social network archive hummed around her—trillions of posts, shares, and memories, each one a small proof of existence. [348] She'd spent months cross-referencing the 7447 pattern, building a map of its appearances across the archive. [361] And tonight, she'd found the keystone: a private message thread from a user who shouldn't exist. [376] The profile was sterile, no photo, no connections, but the account had been active for sixteen of the seventeen years since the crash. [395]

The messages were addressed to no one. [401] But they contained coordinates. [405]

Elara touched the patch on her lapel. [412] "Where are you, Mira?" [417] she whispered to the sensed absence that filled every corner of the archive. [426]

She'd always assumed the recurring numbers were an artifact of the deletion process—fragments of erased data that had bled into the archive's architecture like a stain. [446] But as she traced the thread deeper, she realized the truth: 7447 wasn't an error. [457] It was a signal. [460] Someone had encoded their existence into the digital foundations of the archive itself, using the very deletion algorithms Vespara Corp employed to erase them. [479]

The missing weren't gone. [483] They had woven themselves into the fabric of memory itself. [491]

Elara's terminal chimed. [494] A new message from the impossible account: "We are what you preserve. [502] The files that are deleted are never lost. [509] We are in the numbers. [514] Find us." [517]

She thought of the patch, of her sister's face, of all the empty chairs at all the memorial services held across the echo of forgotten songs. [535] The archive was supposed to be a graveyard of moments. [543] But it had become something else—a garden where the dead could bloom in code. [556]

Elara began typing a response, her fingers moving quickly across the interface. [567] For the first time in seventeen years, the sensed absence didn't feel like grief. [577] It felt like a doorway. [581] And she was ready to walk through it. [589]

In the depths of the social network archive, the number 7447 pulsed once more—patient, persistent, waiting for the memory keeper to finally remember what she'd never been allowed to know. [614]
