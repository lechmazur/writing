Elara had spent eleven years chasing the Greenway Chronicles, medieval manuscripts that scholars called the most elaborate forgery of the Atlantic coast. [22] As an errant legend seeker, she had followed their trail from university archives to fishing villages, always arriving after the documents had been moved or destroyed. [48] Her colleagues called her obsessive. [53] Her funding called her finished. [58] But tonight, on this twilight barge canal, dragging a net through water black as ink, she understood what she had refused to see for a decade. [84] The antique pocket sundial had belonged to Dr. Aldric Marsh. [94] Her mentor. [96] Her betrayer. [98] She kept it in her vest pocket because its weight reminded her why she had started, how she had wanted to understand the architecture of lies, not to expose them, but to comprehend their construction. [133] How a good lie resembled a cathedral: load-bearing facades, flying buttresses of half-truth, rose windows of plausible deniability. [151] Marsh had built such structures, and she had worshipped in them. [162] Now thoughtfully clear, she saw the pattern. [169] The Greenway Chronicles were not some medieval forgery. [177] They were Marsh's own creation. [182] The discoveries she had chased had been planted by him, each one leading to the next, each one keeping her busy while he positioned himself as the premier scholar of texts he had written himself. [217] Her eleven years of devotion had been maintenance work for his cathedral of deception. [231] She had figured it out three days ago, rereading his early papers and seeing the anachronisms, the telltale phrases. [250] But she needed proof. [254] So she had come to salvage what she could before the canal was dredged tomorrow, before the silt that buried Marsh's original workshop was scattered forever. [280] The barge engine churned. [284] She hauled. [286] Her arms burned. [289] By training instincts, she knew where he would have worked. [299] He had been obsessed with water clocks as timing mechanisms for forgeries; the canal house had been his laboratory. [318] When it sank in 1987, he had wept genuinely, then built his career on translating documents he claimed to have rescued from its vaults. [342] Documents he had actually written in that workshop and aged with chemicals he had developed. [356] The net snagged. [359] She pulled harder, and something heavy shifted in the murk. [369] A steel lockbox rose dripping from the channel, its padlock corroded but intact. [381] She did not need to open it, though she would. [391] She did not need the chemical stains and reagent bottles inside to prove what she now knew. [408] Hindsight clarity had already given her the map: Marsh's poses of sorrow when evidence was lost, his convenient friendships with carbon-dating technicians, the way he had always positioned her just far enough away to stay useful and just close enough to stay loyal. [451] She set the lockbox on the deck. [458] The twilight was long gone; stars reflected in the black water. [469] By the silence of morning radio, she would have the lockbox secured and Marsh's reputation dismantled. [485] The silence that came before the five a.m. broadcast, when the night shift ended and the world held its breath, that would be her deadline. [510] By then, everything would be sent to the university, the journal, the forensic lab. [524] Serene upheaval. [526] That was the only phrase for it. [533] She felt no rage, no grief, only a strange, luminous calm, as though she had been waiting for this clearance for years and had finally stopped resisting it. [561] The barge drifted. [564] The antique pocket sundial pressed against her chest, useless for telling time in darkness but perfect for what it actually measured: the distance between belief and understanding. [591] Elara steered toward the dock. [596] The architecture of Marsh's lies had been beautiful, she would give him that. [609] But she had spent eleven years learning its blueprints. [618] Now she would take it apart. [625]
