Margaux Ashworth, last heiress of the Ashworth Newsprint Empire, stood in the echoing observatory above the clouds, watching starlight that had traveled millennia to reach her eyes. [26] The brass telescope tracked its silent revolutions beneath the great dome, mechanism whirring in faithful monotony. [41] The wind outside was a constant scripture read against the glass. [51] She did not move. [55] She only listened and watched, as she had been taught—devotedly observant since childhood, cataloguing details the way other children collected stamps. [76] It was both inheritance and curse, this attention that revealed what the dynasty preferred hidden. [90]

The journalist had called forty minutes ago, his voice a thread of static patched through three satellites. [106] "Your grandfather didn't just report the Voss disappearance," he'd said. [116] "He orchestrated it." [119]

She'd come straight here, to the observatory her grandfather built after the war—his monument to looking upward instead of backward, to naming stars instead of answering questions. [146] What better place to commit a cover-up than a place that literally looks away from Earth? [161] The dome hummed with mountain wind. [167] Below, cloud cover erased the world of men—cities and mortgages, scandals and schedules—all wiped clean. [182]

Her grandfather's telescope still tracked celestial bodies in their eternal patterns—planets circling back, comets returning, orbits of regret repeating across the void. [203] That was his phrase, actually. [208] She'd found it under the brass lens cap, handwritten on yellowed paper: "The heavens show us what we deny on Earth. [227] Everything returns." [229] Like the rosewood pen nib she now turned between her fingers, taken from his desk drawer after the call. [246] Rosewood, once cut, darkens with age.
