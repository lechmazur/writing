Vessren had always been a secretive conjurer, not out of shame, but because the work demanded privacy. [14] The mathematics of emotion could not be parsed with an audience breathing nearby. [26]

In the cavern orchard where she worked, luminescent fruit hung from branches that twisted toward the ceiling, and moisture dripped from stalactites in rhythms older than memory. [55] The roots drank deep from the mineral-rich pools below, and the trees bore fruit that tasted of stone and sweetness intertwined. [78] It was a place where things grew in the in-between. [88]

After the orchard grew silent beneath the moon - after the last drip of water echoed and the pale blooms folded shut - Vessren would come. [114] She carried only the taxidermist's glass eye pair, cool and heavy in her pocket. [128] They had belonged to her mother, though "belonged" was not quite right. [140] Her mother had never worn them. [147] They had been fashioned for a woman already gone, commissioned by a grief-stricken husband who couldn't accept absence when he could have appearance instead. [170] A conjurer himself, he'd thought he could fix what was hollow. [181]

But her mother hadn't been dead. [188] She'd simply left. [192] Stepped out for water one morning and kept walking, her tracks dissolving in the dust long before anyone thought to follow. [213] Ambiguous loss - that's what the village called it, with their careful, pitying tones. [226] But Vessren called it an equation without a solution. [235] A proof that couldn't close. [241]

She knelt on the mosaic stepping stone at the orchard's heart, a circle of fitted tiles worn by time and touch. [263] The chipped patterns caught the lunar light filtering through cracks in the cave ceiling. [277] To anyone else, they were decoration. [284] To Vessren, they were notation. [290]

She placed the glass eyes on the stone, one on each side of the central groove. [306] Then she pressed her fingertips to the tile and began to read. [318]

The mathematics of emotion lived in fractures. [325] In the way joy splintered when you held it too tightly. [337] In how grief could dovetail with gratitude, two edges fitting so cleanly you couldn't tell where one ended and the other began. [358] The chipped patterns told her this. [365] The stone remembered what living hearts forgot: that feeling was not simple arithmetic but something more like water - shaped by its container, changed by its movement, finding level in unexpected places. [397]

She felt the cool insistence of the glass eyes humming beside her wrists. [410] Her mother had never needed them, but Vessren did. [421] Not to see, but to see differently. [428] Through them, the orchard fissured into layers: the pale fruit became equations, the roots became graphs, the stalactite drips became the decimal points in long, long numbers. [455]

And there, in the dovetailing patterns, she found what she'd been looking for. [468]

Her mother hadn't left because of deficiency. [475] She'd left because her equation had balanced elsewhere. [484] The same way love could accumulate quietly in one person and overflow in another, the same way a cavern orchard could bloom in darkness because the darkness was what it knew. [514] There was no betrayal in the math. [521] Only transition. [524]

Vessren picked up the glass eyes. [531] They were warm now, and when she held them to the faint light, she saw that the irises had changed color - from grey to something closer to amber, like honey held against flame. [562]

She had been gently inventive in her seeking, casting spells not to summon or bind but to understand. [580] And understanding, she realized, was its own kind of conjuring. [590] It didn't bring back what was gone. [597] It didn't resolve the ambiguous into certainty. [604] But it made the hollow places habitable. [611]

Above her, the orchard exhaled. [616] The stalactites dripped their slow mineral rain, and the roots drank, and the fruit shivered on their branches like small, pale hearts learning to beat on their own. [642]

Vessren placed the eyes back in her pocket and rose. [652] She would return tomorrow. [657] She would read the patterns again. [663] Because the mathematics of emotion didn't conclude - it merely accumulated, each variable shifting the whole into something new and still true. [684]

She walked home through the silent cavern, the glass eyes warm against her thigh, and the feeling that accompanied her was not closure. [707]

It was a kind of faith. [713]
