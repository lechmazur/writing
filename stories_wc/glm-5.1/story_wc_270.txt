Maren had spent forty years as an insurgent, first against the occupation, then against the calcified thinking of her own people. [21] The volcanic hot springs hissed and bubbled around her, steam rising through the reconstruction scaffolding where the temple once stood. [42] Her granddaughter Yara found her there, sitting at the edge of the boiling pool where the sacred moss grew thick and whispered constantly into Maren's unwilling mind. [71] That was her curse and her gift: accidental telepathy with plants, their root-thoughts pushing into her consciousness like water through cracked stone. [94]

"Grandmother, they're voting tonight," Yara said, settling beside her on the warm rock. [108] "To seal the temple exactly as it was, to pretend the war never happened." [123]

Maren closed her eyes against the moss's insistent murmur—old memories of fire and destruction encoded in its rhizomes. [146] "Tradition isn't preservation," Maren said. [153] "It's the living root." [158] "We evolve it with intent, or it strangles us." [168]

She reached into her pocket and withdrew the embryo in amber, ancient and warm from the heat of the springs. [189] The Council of Elders kept it sealed in the temple vault, insisting it must remain hidden until the prophesied time—implanted destiny, they called it, frozen potential waiting for predetermined revelation. [221] But Maren had stolen it. [227]

The embryo was a seed-creature from before the volcanoes rose, preserved in tree resin when the world was different. [249] Its purpose had been written over generations into sacred texts, a fixed fate that Maren refused to accept. [270]

"Why now?" Yara asked, watching steam curl around the amber in her grandmother's palm. [286]

"The moss told me the ground is shifting." [296] "The hot springs are rerouting." [303] "By the time the Elders' prophecy unfolds, the temple will be underwater." [318]

Yara's eyes widened. [322] "Then what do we do?" [328]

Maren stood, joints aching, and pointed toward the tattered remains of a kite caught in the dead branches of a half-burned manuka tree at the pool's edge. [355] It had been there since before the war, a child's toy from another time. [371] The tattered tail fluttered in the sulfurous breeze, covered in faded markings that the children had dismissed as decoration. [393]

But Maren, who heard the long memory of roots, recognized the script—pre-war conservation code, a language of seeds and seasons. [418] She waded into the shallows, heat soaking through her boots, and carefully freed the tail from the branches. [438]

The cryptic symbols swam before her eyes until her plant-touched mind translated their meaning: instructions for breaking the amber's seal without destroying the embryo within. [468] The code spoke of warmth and intent, of deliberate hands guiding natural processes rather than waiting for chance to unfold them. [494]

"Grandmother..." Yara's voice was sharp. [501] "The Elders will exile you for this." [511]

"They tried to exile me forty years ago," Maren said, lowering the amber into the hottest part of the spring where mineral water could seep through its ancient cracks. [543] "I was the insurgent grandmother before you were born." [555] "We don't follow prophesied paths." [562] "We choose them." [567]

The amber softened, its golden prison becoming permeable, and Maren felt the embryo's first pulse of awareness—not as a voice, but as a pressure, a reaching. [598] Around them, the moss sang with recognition, its telepathic whispers weaving between plant and seed-creature in a conversation older than words. [623]

The embryo was unsealed. [628] Destiny, once implanted and immutable, now curled into possibility—flexible, chosen, alive. [645]

"Tell the Council," Maren said, lifting the transformed thing from the water, "that tradition has evolved." [665] "The springs showed us how." [672]

Yara took her grandmother's hand, and together they walked through steam and whispers toward the vote that would reshape their future. [697] Behind them, new shoots pushed through ash at the water's edge, already dreaming of what they might become. [718]
