Maren had been gone for twenty years before the catacomb orchard called her home. [14] The trees here were ancient, their roots thick as arms, snaking between the bones of her ancestors as if the living and dead had made quiet peace beneath the soil. [44] She had returned to excavate her grandmother's tomb, sealed since the evacuation, and to find what remained of the faith she had lost in foreign cities whose names she had learned to pronounce while forgetting her own. [80]

The digging was slow, careful work. [86] Each handful of earth might contain a fragment of history, and Maren approached each layer with the warm curiosity of someone relearning a language once spoken in childhood. [112] She recognized ceramic shards and corroded bronze, felt the weight of years pressing against her palms like an uncertain handshake from someone who might not remember her. [140]

On the third morning, her trowel struck stone. [148] She brushed away the dirt to find a sundial base, carved from local limestone, its surface marked with hours but missing the gnomon that would have cast shadows to tell the time. [179] The base rested at the head of an incomplete grave marker. [191] Someone had begun to place it here, perhaps as a monument, and then abandoned the work when the evacuation order came through and interrupted every journey that mattered. [220] The sundial's incompleteness mirrored her own. [226] She had been twelve when they fled, old enough to remember the taste of her grandmother's bread but not old enough to keep her faith from crumbling in the refugee camps where hope was measured in rations and no one looked at the sky for meaning anymore. [270] Her journey, too, had been interrupted. [277]

"Show me what else is here," she whispered to the roots, to the bones, to whatever still listened in this place between worlds. [300] And then she began to wander. [305]

Not aimlessly. [307] Purposeful wandering, the kind her grandmother had taught her before the leaving: walk without destination, and the land will tell you where you belong. [333] The old women had practiced this too, circling the orchard at dawn, letting their feet find what their minds could not. [356] Maren moved between the trees, letting her attention catch on whatever stirred something half-remembered in her chest. [375] A particular root formation that curved like a question. [384] A pattern in the bark that reminded her of her grandmother's tattoos, the ones that had told stories she was too young to read. [408] Bones that had been shaped by the growing wood into something almost tender, the dead embraced like seeds in the living palm. [430]

She wandered for hours across the days of excavation, always returning to dig but carrying with her fragments of understanding. [451] The orchard was not a graveyard in the way she had feared. [462] It was a conversation across time. [468] The trees drew sustenance from what had come before, and the dead were held close, not consumed. [486] Her cautious heart, so long armored against wonder in lands where she was never quite welcome, began to feel something like enchantment pressing against its edges, asking gently to be let in. [518]

On the seventh day, as the tomb's capstone finally yielded and revealed her grandmother's remains arranged with surprising care, Maren found the gnomon. [543] It had fallen between two roots that had grown around it like fingers cupping a flame. [560] She carried it back to the grave site. [567]

Maren set the gnomon into the sundial base. [575] The fit was imperfect, the angle slightly wrong from years of settling and displacement. [590] Time would not be measured precisely here. [597] But as the sun struck the stone, a shadow stretched across its face, and the sundial spoke its first hour in twenty years. [621]

She knelt beside her grandmother's bones and felt something shift in her chest. [637] Not the dramatic breaking she had anticipated, but a quieter settling, like roots finding purchase in welcoming soil. [657] Her faith had never been truly destroyed. [666] It had been interrupted, like the sundial, like her own journey, waiting to be completed by hands willing to do the slow work of return. [691]

The orchard rustled around her, living and dead intertwined, and Maren stayed until the shadow crawled past the final hour marked on stone. [716] She would rebuild what had been broken, not by force, but through the patience to wander and the courage to let the interrupted journey finally reach its destination. [750]
