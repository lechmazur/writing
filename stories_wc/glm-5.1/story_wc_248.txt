Miriam had burned seventeen lending libraries before she understood what she was really searching for. [15]

She'd gone off the grid three years ago, severing every tie to her former life as an archivist at the Meridian Collegium. [37] They'd told her the project was about preservation: consolidating scattered texts into safe, climate-controlled vaults. [55] They'd promised no knowledge would be lost. [63]

The faded tapestry fragment in her coat pocket told a different story. [75] Woven by hands from the lost Oscaran civilization three thousand years dead, it depicted a woman with flames cupped tenderly in her palms and grief written in the angle of her shoulders. [105] The Oscaran had understood something the Collegium had forgotten: that fire could create as well as consume. [122] Volcanic tenderness, the ancient texts called it—ferocity forged in love, heat that preserves rather than destroys. [138]

Miriam was analytically intuitive. [143] She read patterns in the spaces between things, heard the questions silences were asking. [157] So when the Collegium began consolidating lending libraries across the provinces, she noticed what they burned: marginalia, annotations, the scribbled thoughts of working-class readers who had no other voice. [184] The grand treatises survived. [188] The quiet voices did not. [194]

The Oscaran had made a promise. [200] Before their civilization vanished into myth, their elders had embedded vows into the architecture of their repositories: no voice, however small, would ever be silenced by time. [226] And when Miriam recognized that this ancient promise was being broken—systematically, deliberately—something volcanic had cracked open in her chest. [245]

She became the arsonist they never suspected. [253] Seventeen lending libraries, emptied in the dark of their true treasures—the journals and letters and hand-annotated texts that chronicled ordinary lives—before she put the torch to what remained. [282] Let the Collegium find only ash where they'd expected to find their sanitized archives. [297]

Her method was artistic, precise: at every library, she portrayed a scene in ochre and charcoal on the surviving walls before the flames took them. [323] She painted a woman's face, over and over—seventeen times, each one deliberately vandalized, scratched through, fragmented. [340] The authorities would describe them as a vandalized self-portrait, the ravings of a madwoman destroying her own image in fury and grief. [362] And in a way, they were right—just not about whose self she was depicting. [378]

Every scratched-away eye, every torn mouth was both a portrait of the forgotten Oscaran woman and a portrait of Miriam herself—an arsonist who burned to preserve, a woman whose tenderness had gone volcanic. [411] Together, the seventeen vandalized portraits formed a trail of artistic creations leading to a hidden message—a map, a key, a way home. [435] Each fragment of destroyed face aligned with the next. [444] The scratched-away eyes in one mural completed the torn-away mouth in another. [457] Vandalism was, itself, the key. [463]

She'd found the tapestry fragment beneath the floorboards of the first library she'd saved, tucked inside a bricklayer's journal from three millennia past. [485] The bricklayer had written: She came to me in the fire and showed me where the promise is kept. [504]

And now Miriam stood in the mouth of a cave that her final mural had pointed toward, the volcanic tenderness in her chest threatening to consume her entirely. [532]

The lost civilization archive stretched before her, impossible and luminous. [543] Crystallized language caught her torchlight, every surface inscribed with Oscaran script. [556] The walls held hundreds of portraits, each deliberately damaged—faces half-erased, hands broken away, bodies fragmented across stone. [575] But when her analytically intuitive mind traced the paths of destruction, she understood what she was seeing. [592] The Oscaran had hidden their promise in the very act of appearing to destroy it. [609] The damage was a lock; the vandalism, the key. [619]

She pressed her tapestry fragment against the final portrait. [629] It completed the image: a woman with tenderness in her eyes, holding not a weapon but an open book. [648] And the forgotten promise blazed to life across the crystallized walls: We will not let any voice be silenced. [668] We have broken our own portraits so that you might remember. [680] The knowledge belongs to everyone. [686]

Miriam sank to her knees in the archive. [695] Ten thousand rescued texts waited in hidden caches across the provinces, their locations encoded in the very art the authorities dismissed as vandalism. [719] She had gone off the grid to rescue a forgotten promise. [732] She had become an arsonist, an artist, a criminal, a keeper of vows—and the volcanic tenderness that had driven her into the flames now softened into something gentler, a warmth that held rather than burned. [768]

She would portray this promise to anyone who would follow her seventeen painted maps and find what she had found. [790] The archives were open. [794] The knowledge would flow again. [800]
