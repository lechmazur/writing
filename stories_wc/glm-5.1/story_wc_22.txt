The reef died on a Tuesday, and by Friday the gloom-hill coachman stood at the blooming jetty, listening to gulls. [21]

Not listening as tourists listen, squinting against twilight and remarking on the sound. [35] He listened as one who speaks dead languages listens: parsing meaning from structures others had abandoned centuries ago. [54] The gulls were arguing about the coffee-colored tide, about the sulfur taste in the current, about how the reef's collapse had sent residuals through the food chain that even the birds could feel turning in their stomachs. [87]

"South corner," the coachman muttered, translating the gulls' coarse syntax into a dialect that had not been spoken by human lips since the harbor was a fishing village. [115] "The current carries nutrients there. [121] The good soil will collect." [127]

He knelt on the jetty's weathered planks, the setting sun painting everything in shades of copper and shadow. [145] From his coat he withdrew a collection of dried seed pods, each one wrinkled and light as paper, each one holding the memory of a flower that had once grown along this coast before the wars, before the ships, before men decided the jetty was a strategic position worth bleeding for. [188]

The battlefield spread behind him, a narrow strip of contested shore that had swallowed generations. [204] Between the sea and the gloom-hills, this thin ribbon of land had seen more bodies than raindrops, or so the old songs claimed in languages no one sang anymore. [231] Languages the coachman still remembered, still spoke to the darkening air when no one else was listening. [248]

He crushed the first pod between his fingers. [256] Dust and tiny black seeds fell onto the jetty's edge, settling into cracks where salt had eaten the wood soft. [275] The gulls called again. [280] He tilted his head, eyes distant. [286] "Not yet," he replied to them. [292] "The tide needs another hour. [297] The sulfur must settle first." [303]

The blooming jetty earned its name each evening at this hour, when the last light caught the wildflowers that had colonized the battlefield's edges—hardy, stubborn things that pushed through compacted soil and shrapnel alike. [338] The coachman had learned to read the flowers' progress by listening to the birds who fed on their seeds. [355] They told him where roots had taken hold, where the earth remained hostile, where his next handful of crushed pods might find purchase. [375]

He moved along the jetty with the patience of someone who spoke words that no longer had native speakers. [394] When you carry languages whose peoples had vanished into the soil, you learn that all work is slow work. [413] Roots take seasons. [416] Understanding takes longer. [419] The submerged concord between land and seed, between intention and outcome, between the dead and those who remember them—such agreements cannot be rushed. [447] They can only be honored. [452]

The gulls wheeled and screamed. [457] He closed his eyes and heard not noise but news: the southern marsh was ready, the western slope needed another season, and somewhere beyond the gloom-hills a hawk reported that the mainland's gardens were failing. [491] The reef's death rippled outward in ways the newspapers could not articulate. [503]

The coachman fertilized methodically, crushing pod after pod, letting the seeds fall where the birds directed. [521] His hands were brown with dust, his knees damp from the planks, his breath forming small clouds in the cooling air. [540] Twilight deepened. [543] The flowers along the jetty's edges seemed to glow with their own faint luminescence, white and yellow catching the last blue of the sky. [567]

Tomorrow the tide would carry nutrients to the places he could not reach. [578] Next season, the flowers would push further into the battlefield. [587] In a decade, perhaps, a garden would stand where soldiers once fell, and the only artillery would be seed pods bursting in the autumn wind. [611]

The gulls called one final time. [618] The coachman answered in a language that had died with the fishermen who first named this harbor, speaking words that meant: I understand. [638] I will continue. [641] The narrowness of the work never troubled him, for its vastness was measured in centuries, and he had always preferred the company of the dead to the urgencies of the living. [671]

He emptied the last pod, and the jetty bloomed in the fading light. [684]
