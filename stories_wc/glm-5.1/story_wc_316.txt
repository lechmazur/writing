Mira watched the two figures circle each other across the training floor. Both had her face. Both had her voice. Neither had her permission to exist. [20]

The floating rock dojo hung suspended over the abyss, ancient stone murmuring with the weight of practitioners past. Outside the open walls, the sky flickered—yesterday becoming tomorrow becoming a version of last week that had never happened. History was rewriting itself, and no one seemed to notice except her. [48]

She should know. Three months ago, she had dissolved. Ego death, the monks called it. Complete surrender of the self. She had crawled back together piece by piece, but something had changed. She could see through people now—their masks, their lies, their careful performances. Her gaze sliced through pretense like a blade through silk. [46]

That was how she knew the clones were both fakes. [9]

"It is my right," said the first clone. "I was made first. I have seniority." [14]

"Seniority does not make you original," said the second. "I have more of her memories. I am more complete." [17]

The custody battle had raged for weeks. Each wanted control of the original's assets, the original's relationships, the original's life. Each claimed authenticity. Meanwhile, the real prize sat on the altar behind them: a glass jar containing a brain, its label reading DUPLICATE in clinical letters. [43]

Mira had learned the truth from the monks. The brain was not a duplicate—it was the original. "Duplicate" was the label the cloning facility used for sources, a bureaucratic lie to maintain plausible deniability. The clones were fighting over custody of the wrong thing entirely. They were fighting for ownership when what they needed was belonging. [52]

Mira felt nothing. That was the cruelest gift of ego death—the numbness. Colors were muted. Food was ash. Touch was static. To defeat this emotional void, she needed to vivify something real. She needed to make something live again, even if that something was herself. [44]

She stepped onto the training floor. The clones turned. [9]

"This does not concern you," said the first. [8]

"Everything here concerns me now." Mira walked toward the jar. "You are fighting over ownership, but ownership requires authenticity. Neither of you is authentic." [25]

"Who are you to judge?" asked the second. [6]

"Someone who died and returned." Mira touched the cold glass. The brain inside was preserved, dormant, waiting. "You both came from this. You are fragments—reflections. You do not need custody. You need each other." [34]

The clones exchanged hostile glances. [5]

"Complementary opposites," Mira continued. "One holds her discipline. The other holds her passion. Apart, you are incomplete. Together, you could become something whole. Something alive." [27]

She lifted the jar. The sky flickered again—a timeline where the cloning had never happened, a timeline where it had happened twice, a timeline where no one had survived ego death at all. They were all collapsing into one another, history desperate to resolve its contradictions. [42]

"I want to feel again," Mira told them honestly. "And so do you, even if you do not know it yet." [20]

She cracked the jar's seal. What remained of the original pulsed weakly—neural pathways firing for the first time in months. [20]

"This is not custody," Mira said. "It is integration." [9]

The clones hesitated. Then slowly, understanding passed between them. Not through words—through the shared ache of incompleteness, the phantom sensation of missing halves. The first clone extended her hand. The second took it. [35]

Light gathered between their joined palms. Mira pushed through—slicing through the pretense of separation, the lie that they were ever truly distinct. The discipline and the passion. The structure and the chaos. Complementary opposites, finding synthesis at last. [39]

The numbness in her chest cracked. Not warmth yet, but the possibility of warmth. A beginning. [15]

Mira watched the clones merge. History settled around them like sediment finding its natural level. The floating rock held steady beneath her feet. Somewhere in the new timeline being written, fragments became whole. [30]

And Mira, the ego death survivor, felt the first true emotion she had known in months. Hope. [15]
