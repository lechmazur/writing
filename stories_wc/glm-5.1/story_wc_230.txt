Maren's fingers traced the ravine wall as she descended toward the hanging orchard. Below, the storm-swollen river roared, and above, the ancient plum trees clung to impossible angles, their roots threading through stone like veins through flesh. The empathy virus had taken so much, but it had not taken the orchards. [56]

She found her first truffle near the base of a gnarled trunk, the earth still damp from yesterday's storm. Logic dictated location: the oldest trees, the deepest root systems, the ravine's most sheltered corners. Reason had served her well in the six years since the virus rewired human speech, since truth began bleeding from every mouth like water from a cracked cup. [105]

The antique glass eye sat heavy in her pocket, smooth surface warm from her body heat. A smuggled relic from the Prentiss Collection, stolen the night the museums closed forever. She had not meant to take it. "I am stealing this," she had announced to the curator, the words erupting before she could stop them, "because I believe it might help me occlude my involuntary honesty." The curator had simply nodded, tears streaming down her face - everyone cried now, feeling everything, unable to filter. [164]

In the hanging orchard, Maren worked the soil with practiced rationality. The truffles here grew deep, near the roots that anchored the trees to the ravine's steep walls. Every specimen she uncovered could be the one - the specific fungus mentioned in Dr. Atherton's discredited textbook, the 2047 edition that mainstream medicine had dismissed. "Rumored cures," they had called her research, "dangerous folklore." But Maren had read the data. Certain fungal compounds could temporarily suppress the neural pathways the empathy virus had hijacked. [258]

She had eaten seven varieties already. Each time, she had felt the compulsion weaken, then return. Each time, she had held the glass eye to her right socket, the empty socket where her own eye had been before the accident, and whispered: "I feel nothing." The words had always emerged wrong. "I feel everything," her mouth would correct, truth forcing its way past her teeth like vomit. [328]

Today's storm approached from the west, dark clouds banking against the ravine walls. Maren worked faster, her rational mind cataloging each specimen: size, color, proximity to root mass, depth of excavation. The methodology mattered. Hope was not a methodology. [380]

But hope persisted, fractured and graceless, like the orchard itself surviving despite all reason. [396]

She found the eighth specimen near a tree that had split in last week's storm, its trunk exposing root matter the color of bruises. The truffle was different - when she cut it open, the interior bled blue. Atherton's textbook had mentioned blue interior bleeding. Page 247, Chapter 9: "Compound Interactions in Post-Viral Neural Environments." The chapter everyone said was fantasy. [459]

Maren ate a thin slice. The taste was bitter, metallic, ancient. She waited, the glass eye cool against her palm. [481]

In the silence between heartbeats, something shifted. The truth pressure behind her sternum lessened. She held the glass eye up, light catching its antique surface. [506]

"I am cultivating psychological safety," she whispered. [514]

The words came out exactly as formed. No correction. No involuntary revelation of deeper fears. [532]

The storm broke. Rain hammered the orchard, the ravine, the hanging trees that refused to fall. Maren remained still, holding the smuggled eye, tasting freedom for the first time since the virus. [564]

She would need to harvest more. She would need to verify the compound's stability. She would need to return to the discredited textbook and refine the dosage. Logic. Method. Reason. The same tools that had always served her. [605]

But now she also had this: a fractured grace granted by a bitter fungus, an ancient glass eye, and the knowledge that somewhere in those discredited pages lay the key to occluding truth long enough to be human again. [648]

The storm raged. The orchard held. Maren pocketed her specimens and began the long climb back up the ravine wall, each step earned, each breath a small victory against a world that demanded she reveal everything. [691]

She would not. She could not. But now, at last, she could choose what to hide and what to show, and that choice - that precious, human choice - was worth every discredited rumor, every smuggled relic, every storm-battered descent into the hanging orchard's indifferent grace. [738]
