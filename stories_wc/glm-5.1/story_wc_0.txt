Dr. Lena Vasik had spent fourteen years observing how neutron stars cool, how their unimaginable fury settles into something quieter over eons. [22] She understood gradual change the way most people understood weather—you didn't fight it, you mapped it, honored its patience. [43] So when the flood took her greenhouse and half her research notes, she refused to call it catastrophe. [61] Just change. [63] Fast, violent change, but still just change. [70]

She had built the greenhouse with her own hands twelve years ago, a place to grow orchids and cycads and sometimes just to sit and think. [96] Now she stood outside it, three days after the waters receded, looking at what remained. [111] The glass walls had shattered inward; mud and debris coated the floor where titan arums had once stretched toward the humid air. [133] A hummingbird feeder hung crooked from a bent support beam, its sugar water long drained or spilled. [150] Lena waded through the wreckage with a bucket and a pair of gloves, at first just picking things up to see if they could be saved or cleaned. [177]

The butterfly had been a Blue Morpho—a research specimen she'd kept years ago, its iridescent wings mounted in a glass case on the top shelf beside her pride and joy, a dying maidenhair fern. [210] The flood had knocked the case down, and now it lay face-down in silt. [224] When Lena lifted the case, she saw the upper wing surface had already begun to decay, its scales lifting and dispersing like tiny planets losing orbit. [250] Dust from the butterfly wing had settled across the interior of the case in intricate, swirling patterns—following micro-currents in the air, tracing pathways she recognized from her physics textbooks. [279] Dust that had obeyed invisible forces, accumulated where forces gathered, mapped decay in the very shape of physics itself. [298]

She carried the case outside and sat on an overturned ceramic pot to look at it in sunlight. [315] Each scaled particle had left its position and gathered elsewhere—along the case's seams, in the faint grooves where the glass met the wooden frame. [339] The decay was happening through accumulated dust patterns, gradual and inevitable. [351] Not dramatic. [353] Not violent. [355] Just the slow redistribution of matter from organization into something else, something that still held form, still obeyed law. [376]

Something about it cracked open in her chest. [385] She had spent a career preprocessing reality—translating light curves into masses, pulses into magnetic fields, always converting raw experience into something measurable before she let herself feel it. [413] It was how she functioned. [419] Her mind processed first, felt second. [425] But the flood had preprocessed nothing. [432] It had simply come, destroyed, and receded, leaving only the rawest form of loss. [447] She had no framework ready for it. [455] No equation. [457]

But here, in a storm-damaged greenhouse after the flood, a butterfly's wing was falling apart through patterns so finely gradual they might have been designed. [483] And she had almost missed them, because she had come here to salvage, not to witness. [501] Because her mind had classified this place as "aftermath" before she even stepped inside, another layer of preprocessing that kept the world at a safe analytical distance. [528]

Lena set the case gently on her knee and just looked. [540] The dust was beautiful. [544] The decay was beautiful. [548] Not because ruin could be pretty—that was the kind of needless drama she wanted to strip from herself, the need to dress up loss in metaphor just to tolerate it. [577] But because gradual change, even when it was decay, followed structure. [589] You could trust it. [593] It didn't demand your grief or your performance of it. [603] It simply proceeded. [606]

She thought of the neutron stars cooling in the deep dark. [617] How they would continue long after everyone who remembered this flood was gone, and the next flood, and the one after that. [639] She thought of how small this made her, and how that smallness didn't diminish her but simply placed her. [658] A woman in a ruined greenhouse, watching a butterfly's wing return itself to the world one scale at a time. [678] No more and no less than that. [686] It was enough. [689] It was more than enough. [694] Lena picked up her bucket and went back to work. [704]
