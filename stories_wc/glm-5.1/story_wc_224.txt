Elara was a mercury-glass seer, her skin translucent and reflective like quicksilver, her eyes windows into worlds that others couldn't perceive. [20] She dwelt in Argolis, the labyrinthine bookstore occupying an entire mountain, where miles of shelves spiraled through caverns and climbed through secret passages toward the clouds. [46] After the exodus, after her people had scattered across a hundred distant lands, remembering had become painful. [64] So each night Elara substituted reality. [70] She would place her hands against the mercury-glass panels that lined her alcove high in the mountain's throat, and the glass would ripple, showing her not what was, but what might have been. [103] Her village whole. [106] Her mother alive. [109] Home intact. [111] But visions were not understanding. [116]

The lost language stone rested on a pedestal beside her sleeping mat. [128] Its surface was carved with words in the old tongue, the language of her people before they'd buried their talents and fled. [149] The elders had hidden their gifts—their seersight, their wayfinding, their deep-singing—beneath shame and survival. [164] They had become exiles who could no longer read their own history. [176] Elara traced the stone's carvings with her fingertips. [184] Pressed her reflection into the words. [190] The glass in her skin caught the carvings and fractured them into nonsense. [203] "You cannot see what you will not know," whispered the stone, which spoke only in truth it found within her. [223] Elara's jaw tightened. [226] Hidden thunder rolled through her chest—not anger, but the kind of pressure that precedes a breaking. [242]

When dawn grayed the caves, she descended through the bookstore's winding stacks. [254] Past the geography of a thousand forgotten empires. [262] Past the poetry section where moths browsed between pages. [271] Past the war histories, which always smelled faintly of copper. [280] She sought Old Ysengrin, the feather-keeper. [286] His alcove was deep in the mountain's belly, where the air tasted of dust and old wisdom. [302] Feathers lined his walls—eagle, owl, wren, heron, crow—each one a catalog of a different kind of knowing. [319] "I need to seed what was buried," Elara said. [328] Ysengrin's dark eyes studied her mercurial skin. [335] "To seed requires soil. [340] What soil do you offer?" [346] "My pain. [349] The memories that cut like glass. [356] The shape of home that I cannot hold." [365]

He drew a single feather from his collection—a small thing, mottled brown and grey, from a bird that made its nest only in places it remembered. [390] "Understanding comes via the texture of a feather," he said. [400] "Not its appearance. [404] Not its name. [408] The way it feels against what you truly are." [419]

Elara took the feather. [423] Its barbs were soft, yielding, except at the rachis where strength hid. [436] She ran it along the mercury-glass of her forearm. [446] And remembered. [449] Not the pain this time, but the texture of home. [460] The way her mother's voice had caught on certain syllables. [472] The groove in the threshold from generations of feet. [482] The particular spice of festival bread rising through their small kitchen. [495] The glyph for "return" carved above their door; its curves had been the first shape she learned to draw. [515]

The lost language stone blazed in her memory. [524] She understood now—her people had not buried their talents. [535] They had planted them. [540] Seeds need darkness before they grow. [548] A crack appeared in her mercury-glass skin. [557] Light—not reflected but generated—spilled through. [565]

Each night after that, Elara did not substitute reality. [575] Instead, she went to the deepest cave, where the oldest shelves stood, and placed her glowing hands upon the lost language stone. [597] The words rearranged themselves into something she could read. [608] Messages from everyone who had gone before, who had planted themselves in distant places, waiting to be found. [627] The mercury-glass seer became the signal fire. [636] Her cracked skin broadcast the old tongue outward through the mountain's labyrinth, a frequency that called to buried talents. [656] One by one, they found their way back. [665] Not to the home that was, but to the home they were making. [679]

And when remembering became painful—as it still did, as it always would—Elara did not flinch from it. [697] She held the feather's texture against her cracked glass and let the pain seed deeper. [714] Because home was not a shape she understood. [724] It was the thing she was building, one remembered word at a time, in the spiral heart of Argolis. [746] The thunder was not hidden anymore. [753] It was the sound of words taking root, of buried gifts breaking surface, of a people learning again to speak. [773]
