The mist rolled over Zephyr Bluff like a silver tide, softening every edge of the world. [16] Margaret stood at the precipice where the rock shelf dropped into nothing, her scarred hands buried in her coat pockets, one finger tracing the engraved pillbox she'd carried for eleven years. [47] The burns on her left cheek pulled tight in the cold, a map of the fire that had ended her career and begun her exile. [70]

Spring was tilting toward them now, the earth beginning its slow lean into warmth, and the accumulated snow had finally started to speak. [92] She'd been watching it for three weeks, noting where it melted first, where it lingered in the shadows of stones. [112] The patterns meant nothing to anyone else. [119] But Margaret had spent twenty years reading codes in things that weren't supposed to be messages, and the old handler who'd burned her—who'd left her for dead in a safehouse turned crematorium—had always loved this kind of game. [157]

Viktor Aasen believed in hiding truth in plain sight, in the poetry of natural systems. [172] Before he'd betrayed her, before he'd sold their network for his daughter's medical bills, he'd taught her that language could live anywhere. [194] Now his coordinates were melting into the bluff, snow retreating in careful sequences that matched the cipher they'd once shared. [214]

She pulled out the pillbox and opened it. [222] Inside, no pills—just a slip of paper with his last transmission, delivered anonymously six months ago: "Some things need fixing before they break completely." [244] The engraving on the pillbox itself was older, a gift from her father: "Courage unused is courage lost." [262]

Margaret had spent her burned years searching for where unused courage waited. [273] She'd found it in small moments—defending a stranger on a subway platform, testifying against corruption despite the cost, returning to the intelligence world as a consultant who refused to let the game be played by the old rules. [309] But this was different. [313] This required something prudently daring: measured risk wrapped in boldness. [323]

[392] Some needed mending. [395] And some—the hardest category—needed to be forgiven before they could be whole. [407]

Margaret smiled without warmth. [446] She'd been watched her whole life. [451] The scar tissue on her hands was proof of what happened when watchers became executioners. [464] But prudently daring meant accepting risk while refusing recklessness, and she'd prepared for this moment with documents, contacts, and three separate extraction plans. [486]

"I didn't think you'd come," he said. [532]

"I don't forgive you," she said quietly. [632]

"I know." [634] His eyes closed. [637] "But you came anyway. That's where unused courage waits—in the doing, not the feeling." [652]
