The velvet library held autumn like a secret, its burgundy drapes absorbing the failing October light while Margaret Chen sat expectantly resigned before the instrument she could no longer play. [32]

Forty years as a choir teacher had taught her that some silences were fuller than others. [47] This one pressed against the library's oak shelves like accumulated grief, like the weight of students who had stopped coming, of traditions that had stopped mattering. [73] She traced the zither's soundhole rosette with her fingertip, the carved rosewood pattern that her grandmother had brought from Suzhou before the wars, before the forgetting. [97]

The rosette was meant to denote something—ancestry, craft, the hours of a master carver's devotion—but its language had grown foreign even to Margaret. [120] She belonged to a generation caught between, fluent in neither her grandmother's tongue nor her grandchildren's indifference. [136] The vanishing arts did not announce their departure; they simply grew quieter until one morning you realized the last person who understood was gone. [159]

Margaret had spent her career trying to braid diverse voices into harmony, teaching teenagers that their differences could become counterpoint rather than cacophony. [180] She had conducted Christmas concerts where Muslim students sang alongside Christians, where atheists harmonized with the devout, each voice distinct yet woven. [200] The method had always been the same: by subtracting noise—the judgment, the assumption, the fear—she revealed the music already present. [219]

Now the after-school choir had three members. [227] The principal spoke of budget cuts with apologetic efficiency. [236] Parents enrolled children in coding camps instead of music programs, preparing them for futures that had no room for beauty they could not monetize. [258]
