The bell ringer arrived at the Laboratory of Lost Sounds three days after the power grid stabilized, carrying nothing but a miner's carbide lamp lens wrapped in velvet. [31]

Mira had spent seventeen years pulling the ropes of the Prism Cathedral's bells, learning how each strike fractured into harmonics that painted the air with color only she could perceive. [58] The congregation called her the prism-song bell ringer, though they never understood what the title truly meant. [73] She heard the world differently, each vibration a thread she could almost touch, almost hold. [87] But holding required connection, and connection had become the texture she sought most desperately to understand. [101]

The laboratory existed in the cathedral's forgotten basement, where acoustic researchers had once tried to bottle the resonance of dying languages. [120] Their equipment remained: phonograph horns pointing at walls, oscilloscopes dark and dreaming, glass tubes arranged like organ pipes. [136] Golden light filtered through dust-caked windows, casting shadows that seemed to breathe with warmth rather than darkness. [152] The blackout had silenced everything for forty-one days, and in that silence, Mira had heard something she couldn't name. [169]

Now she placed the carbide lamp lens on the central workbench. [180] Her grandmother had worn it in the mines, had used its focused beam to find veins of copper running through stone. [199] Mira believed it could focus something else entirely: the beam of a question into the heart of an answer. [218]

[240] Mira had designed a journey without movement, a sacred route mapped through the laboratory's collection of captured frequencies. [256] She would travel by listening, each recording a station of the cross toward some understanding she couldn't yet articulate. [272]
