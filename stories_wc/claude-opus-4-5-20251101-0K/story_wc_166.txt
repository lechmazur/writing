The tide dancer moved through the twilight garden with obsidian grace, her bare feet pressing silent against the cold stones where starlight pooled like spilled mercury. [28]

Selene had chosen this exile seven seasons ago, after the nostalgia ban swept through the coastal provinces and memory itself became contraband. [48] The Magisters had decreed that longing for the past was a disease, that remembrance weakened the present, and so they burned the tide temples where dancers once wrote history in salt and motion. [79] But Selene remembered everything. [83] She remembered because her art demanded it—each wave pattern, each lunar phase, each grandmother who had danced before her. [99]

Now she tended this garden at the edge of the world, where the ocean's breath reached inland to kiss the night-blooming jasmine. [120] The stars wheeled overhead in their ancient patterns, indifferent to mortal laws. [132] Here, beyond the Magisters' reach, she practiced the forbidden choreography of tide dancing, waiting for the single student brave enough to find her. [153]

Tonight, that student arrived. [157]

The girl emerged from between the obsidian pillars at the garden's entrance, her movements hesitant but precise—a bird testing new wings. [175] Selene observed the gaps in her posture: the spaces between certainty and fear, between training and instinct, between the child she had been and the dancer she might become. [202] By observing gaps, Selene had always found truth. [211]

"You've come to fledge," Selene said, not a question. [220] "To leave whatever nest the provinces offered and become something they cannot name." [233]

The girl—Kira, she would learn—nodded. [239] "I heard you write on skin," she whispered. [247] "That you can pen cosmic verses that change what a person is." [259]

Selene studied her. [262] The girl carried hunger in her eyes, the particular starvation of someone forbidden from her own purpose. [278] The tide dancer recognized it because she had carried it once, before her elective exile became her liberation. [294]

"I do not write lightly," Selene said. [301] "The cosmic verse marks you permanently. [307] It anchors something infinite into your mortal frame." [315] She gestured toward her own arms, where silver script spiraled like captured tides. [327] "Some cannot bear the weight." [332]

"I have already borne the weight of emptiness," Kira replied. [342] "The ban took everything—my mother's songs, my grandmother's dances, the way the sea looked on my naming day. [411] This fragment of lunar stone had traveled from the high temples before their burning, preserved across the years of exile. [429] It held the last essence of the tide dancers' sacred materials. [440]

[457] You will become spontaneously anchored to the cosmic rhythm—connected permanently to the tides, the moons, every pull that governs the deep waters." [477] She met Kira's eyes. [481] "You will never forget again. [486] But you will never belong to the provinces again either." [496]

"You are fledged," Selene said softly. [633] "The tide lives in you now. [639] They can ban memories, but they cannot ban the ocean. [650] They cannot ban the moons." [655]
