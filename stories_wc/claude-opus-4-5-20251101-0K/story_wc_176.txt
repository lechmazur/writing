[204] When the organ burned in the fire that took her grandmother, this scrap was all that remained. [221]

She had tried everything to reconstruct the melody. [229] Instruments could approximate the notes, but they could not capture the specific resonance of those forgotten names. [244] A transcribed memory, she learned, required more than notation—it required context, vessel, and intention. [258]

In the center of the sanctum hung the ceremonial rope, thick as her forearm and braided from fibers that predated the written word. [281] Generations had added their own strands, each braid representing a lineage, a prayer, a name that deserved to persist beyond the speaker's death. [304] The rope had hung here for four hundred years, frozen and preserved, a monument to the technically gentle art of remembrance. [324] Technically gentle because one wrong movement would shatter the ice-rigid fibers; one careless touch would erase what others had entrusted to permanence. [345]

Elin warmed her hands with her breath and began to work. [356]

By reconfiguring the braids of a centuries-old ceremonial rope, she could create a pattern that matched the punched card's holes.
