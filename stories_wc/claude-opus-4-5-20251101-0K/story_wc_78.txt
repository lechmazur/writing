The Leeward Tide Prophet stood at the edge of her floating orchard, watching the sky bleed into that particular shade of blue that existed only in the minutes between day and night. [32]

She had no name that the waves remembered, only the title given to her by the fishermen who sometimes passed her drifting sanctuary—a circle of lashed driftwood logs supporting twelve apple trees, their roots tangled into the salt-worn wood like desperate fingers. [71]

The blue hour had arrived, and with it, the clouds began their nightly sermon. [85]

[108] A branching pattern westward, fractals within fractals, each smaller iteration carrying the same message amplified. [122] She had spent forty years learning to read these celestial signatures, understanding that clouds were merely the sky's rough draft of tomorrow. [141]
