She was a clean slate vendor now, though the term felt strange against her tongue, like a word borrowed from a language she was still learning. [54] Her parole conditions glimmered on the thin bracelet circling her wrist—a band of light that pulsed faintly, reminding her that freedom came with arithmetic attached. [79] Glimmering parole, they called it: rehabilitation measured by the quality of what you offered the world, quantified absence calculated by what you no longer took. [103]

[128] Everything here was glass—the trees, the walls, the tables where visitors would eventually sit and read. [145] But it was the lull of her very first opening day, that strange pocket of time between unlocking the doors and welcoming the world, and Vera found herself alone with her merchandise. [176]

Clean slates. [178] She sold them in varying sizes, from palm-sized tablets to full manuscript pages, each one capable of holding words that could be rewritten infinitely. [201] The technology had emerged from the same labs that built her bracelet, the same ones that believed in the mathematics of redemption. [222]

[241] The liquid inside shifted like mercury mixed with pearl, captured from the night sky above the Northern Reaches where moons stacked three-deep on the horizon. [266] Vera had bartered for it during her transit here, trading away the last poems she'd written in prison—verses about walls and their inevitable cracks. [291]

[308] The luminescence spread mindfully scattered across the surface, each droplet finding its own purpose, creating a constellation of glowing points that refused to form a pattern. [337] This was how she prepared her wares: not by imposing meaning, but by providing the possibility of it. [356]

Her bracelet pulsed again. [360] The authorities measured her rehabilitation by quantifying absence—counting what she no longer stole, cataloguing the lies she didn't tell, weighing the silence where her schemes used to breed.
