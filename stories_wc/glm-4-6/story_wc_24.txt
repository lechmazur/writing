[13] After blowing the whistle that marked the beginning of the measurement cycle, he moved with practiced hands to the horizon measurement station's main console. [142] As he inserted the key into his favorite clock—the one with the glass face showing the intricate dance of blue fluids within—he found himself wondering about his motivation for this work. [169] After all these years, he still sought to understand what clocks really measure. [179]

[204] His supervisor had dismissed these variations as mere mechanical imprecision, but Elias suspected something more profound was at play. [227]

[246] The liquid inside rose and fell with metronomic regularity, yet Elias knew this regularity was an illusion—a simplification of something more complex. [269]

[290] Starting from one hundred, he counted down slowly, watching the fluid levels with each number pronounced. [303] By seventy-three, he noticed it—a subtle stutter in the flow, a momentary hesitation that occurred only when he counted backwards. [321]
