[21] Her loft, once shared with Tomas the lion tamer, now held only his absence and the autumnal rust-hued peace she had deliberately cultivated since his passing. [65] The ash wood carving blank sat squarely before her, pale and waiting, devoid of the story it would soon contain. [85]

[111] She would work while it boiled, a measured timeframe that disciplined what might otherwise become endless meandering in sorrow. [132] Her fingers had grown calloused over these months of determined creation, a hauntingly practical response to loss that surprised everyone who expected her to dissolve, not build. [158] But this was how she harvested lessons from grief - not through contemplation alone, but through intentional transformation. [181] 

Shavings fell like soft snow around the workbench as she began shaping the ash, her movements economical and sure. [202] With each curve and cut, Tomas emerged from the wood - not as an exact likeness, but in the way he carried himself, the tension in his shoulders, the subtle tilt of his head when listening. [234] She worked to contain not merely his image but the essence of his presence, both here and gone like the raindrops racing across the skylight above. [259] 

The kettle's whistle pierced her concentration, and Elara rose to make her tea, leaving the partially carved figure bathed in the diffused light filtering through the wet glass. [283] When she returned, steam curling from her mug, the figure seemed almost alive in the shifting light, capturing through crystallized time what death could not take entirely. [312] Tomas had always said that time was both circus ring and audience, and her carving was proving him right - creating moments that performed their truth again and again. [340]

[372] The lion tamer's widow turned carver, finding in wood what her husband had found in animals: a language without words, a communication of essential things. [399] 

Elara traced the nascent mane with her thumb, feeling the grain like life-lines mapping possibilities she must shape. [420] Her method was not to forget but to re-member, to physically gather what scattered in shock and to give it form through this sacred act of attention. [448] Each completed figure created an herbarium of moments, crystallized time against the entropy of loss. [469]

[502] She and Tomas had met in that liminal space when the lights came up and fairy dust still lingered in the air, between the spectacle and its dismantling. [528] Now she created her own threshold moments in ash, artifacts of the here and gone, standing at the border between "was" and "is." [552]

[650] The ash wood carving, now complete, sat in the changing light through the rain-beaded skylight - both a record and a resurrection, hauntingly practical in its ability to hold what couldn't be held. [686] Through crystallized time, she had harvested another lesson from loss, another truth carved from the unchanging core of grief: love transforms but does not end, and the work of hands can make space for heart to heal around its hollows.
