The portrait artist pressed her fingertips against the canvas, feeling for texture rather than seeing color. [15] She had learned long ago that her eyes betrayed her—every face a stranger, even those she'd drawn a thousand times. [35] Touch had become her true sight. [42]

[67] The city had condemned this ancient oak, declaring it structurally unsound despite its heartwood rooms still holding strong. [84] She had one night left to finish her commission: a portrait of the guild's founder, commissioned by descendants who wanted something preserved before the wrecking began. [109]

The problem was simple and impossible. [115] She couldn't recognize faces, yet she'd built a career painting them with fractally precise detail—each pore, each hair, each microscopic variance in skin rendered so exactly that viewers swore her subjects might breathe. [145] Her method was touch and pattern, mathematics and memory, but never recognition. [157] She could craft a face perfectly without ever knowing whose face it was. [170]

[215] "You'll understand him through the indentations." [222]

The coin rested on her palm, warm from hours of handling. [251] Her fingertips traced the ghost-profile again: a subtle rise here, a depression there, the metal's surface telling a story her eyes couldn't read and her mind couldn't process into meaning. Yet the portrait remained wrong. [303]

[326] This canvas showed a face technically flawless but spiritually absent. [336] She had crafted features without capturing a person. [345]

[539] Perhaps the portrait's truth wasn't in recognition but in the attempt itself—the fractally precise documentation of touch across centuries, one artist feeling what another had pressed into silver, both working in darkness of different kinds.
