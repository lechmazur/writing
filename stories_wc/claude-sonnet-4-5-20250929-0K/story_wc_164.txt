The sea-foam recollectionist descended into the iridescent cave, her weathered fingers trailing along crystalline walls that pulsed with dim, cheerful light. [22] She had perhaps three hours before memory fades—before the tide returned and washed away the pathway through the collapsed cliff face. [45] Time enough, she hoped, to discover why some paths disappear while others remain etched in consciousness forever. [63]

[89] The foam held everything: joy-soaked laughter from beach weddings, the salt-bitter grief of fishermen's widows, the electric anticipation of first kisses at sunset. [111] But lately, she'd noticed gaps in her collections, missing pieces that left her records incomplete, as if someone had taken scissors to the fabric of recollection itself. [137]

[160] The seeds had taken root in an impossible pattern, their roots cracking open the earth to reveal this hollow wonder beneath. [182] Local children claimed the bird returned each spring, always dropping seeds in the same strange spiral. [199] Marina suspected the pattern meant something more. [206]

Inside the geode, light refracted through a thousand facets, painting everything in shifting rainbows that made her feel simultaneously hopeful and melancholic—a state she'd come to think of as cheerful gloom. [236] At the cave's heart, she found what she'd been searching for: a pool of crater glass, formed when lightning had struck the crystalline floor centuries ago. [263]

She knelt beside the glassy surface, smooth as memory itself. [273] In her satchel, she carried vials of sea-foam from the missing periods, the gaps in human experience she couldn't transliterate no matter how carefully she tried. [348] Not forgotten, Marina realized with a gasp, but unmade. [357] The emotions she'd been unable to transliterate weren't missing because they'd faded; they were missing because those moments had ceased to exist once different choices were made. [384]

[479] The missing pieces weren't lost—they were deliberately removed, scattered like those seeds the albatross dropped, ensuring the weight of infinite possibilities didn't crush the single thread of lived experience. [522] The emotions of these unmade moments felt different than ordinary memories—lighter, more wistful, tinged with the peculiar sadness of roads that existed only in the shadow of choice.
