The bloodhound's name was Mercy, and she had found her last body three years before the world ended. [18]

Now she lived with Calder in the scrapyard settlement that sprawled across the cracked wasteland like a iron cancer, breathing steam and rust. [40] The old man had adopted her after the resource wars scorched everything green, when tracking corpses no longer mattered because corpses were everywhere. [61] She slept beneath his workbench while he performed his strange archeology, pulling phonograph cylinders from the debris and coaxing secrets from their grooves. [83]

The settlement existed in a wild hush, that peculiar silence of wind moving through metal bones and distant fires crackling. [102] People spoke in whispers here, as if afraid to wake something sleeping beneath the fractured earth. [118]

Calder had shown Mercy the autumn leaf on her first day with him. [131] Preserved in wax, amber and crimson veins frozen mid-curl, it was the last leaf he'd seen before the burning. [150] He kept it on the shelf beside his cylinder reader, a talisman of a world that had breathed differently. [169]

"Distance," he would tell Mercy, scratching behind her graying ears, "is what killed us." [183] His voice carried that intuitively descriptive quality of someone who had learned to read landscapes of loss. [200] "We forgot how to translate distance into closeness, how to see each other across the gaps we created." [218]

The cylinders he collected contained more than music. [226] By reading secret texts in the grooves of phonograph cylinders, Calder had discovered the final transmissions, the last words encoded by engineers who understood the end was near. [253] Some were confessions, others warnings. [259] Many were riddles, puzzles meant to outlast the collapse. [268]

One morning, Mercy's nose caught something old and urgent in the wind. [280] Her body remembered the work even as her spirit had retired from it. [293] She led Calder three miles out into the wasteland to a half-buried bunker he'd never seen. [309]

Inside, they found a cylinder unlike the others, its surface marked with symbols that seemed to shift in the dim light of Calder's lamp. [332]

He brought it home with trembling hands. [339]

That evening, as tinted revelations poured from his speaker in waves of static and voice, Mercy watched her master weep. [358] The recording was a woman explaining how distance had always been an illusion, how the space between people was just fear given measurement. "We were trackers once, weren't we, girl?" [482] He knelt, pressing his forehead to hers. [490] "You found bodies, and I found voices in the dead grooves of history." Mercy watched them organize into small groups, measuring distances that had seemed infinite yesterday and finding them suddenly crossable. [580]

Calder clipped the preserved leaf to his pack. [614]

"Come on, Mercy," he said, and the old bloodhound rose, her nose already seeking the next discovery, the next translation of distance into something resembling home. [638]
