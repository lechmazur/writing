The potter found the beekeeping oasis at dusk, when chimney smoke from the distant settlements flattened against the burnt orange sky. [21] She had followed the sun patterns for three days across the scorched wasteland, noting how shadows pooled differently in certain places, how light bent around invisible seams in the air. [50] The oasis appeared suddenly, a cluster of weathered hives and a crumbling stone cottage that shouldn't exist in this lifeless expanse. [71]

[97] A kettle steamed perpetually on a cold stove. [105] And on the windowsill sat a faded lantern glass, its surface etched with honeycomb patterns, casting fragments of light that didn't quite synchronize with the present moment. [131]

She picked up the glass carefully, feeling its warmth. [140] Through it, she could see the oasis as it had been: hives thriving with golden bodies, a beekeepers hands moving through frames, flowers blooming impossibly in the wasteland. [167] Through it, she could also see the oasis as it would be: buried under sand, then excavated, then cultivated again by people wearing strange clothes. [193] The timelines fractured and overlapped like cracks in twice-fired clay. [204]

"You're drawn here too," a voice said. [211] An old beekeeper materialized in the doorway, then flickered, then solidified. [223] He existed in all the timelines simultaneously, she realized, anchored by something stronger than linear time. [239]

The beekeeper smiled, and the room thrummed louder. [280] "You already know. [283] You feel it in your clay." [289]

"This oasis grew around acts of gentleness," the beekeeper explained, his form stabilizing as he spoke. [333] "Every bee I tended without fear. [339] Every traveler I offered water. "Burns because it forgot this," he said with sharp gentleness, the tone of someone who has witnessed collapse without succumbing to bitterness. [446] "You potters understand. "You already have," the beekeeper said, fading. [546] "Everyone who understands carries it forward." [552]

[571] Behind her, the oasis thrummed and shimmered, existing in all its timelines at once. [585] She would return to her pottery wheel and her patient shaping, understanding now why she had always worked this way—slowly, gently, with attention to the small kindnesses between hands and clay.
