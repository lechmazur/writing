[23] She played wistfully each evening in the tidal arbor terminal, suspended above clouds that rolled like ocean waves beneath the glass floor. [47] The arbor existed in that strange altitude where compasses flickered uselessly, their needles spinning between magnetic poles that refused to anchor. [68] Travelers passed through with luggage tagged for destinations she would never see, their faces illuminated by the perpetual twilight that ruled this in-between place. [93]

On the piano's corner sat a broken anniversary clock, its mechanism frozen at seven-fifteen—the exact moment her husband had boarded the last dirigible before the routes changed. [118] Twenty years now, and she still wound the useless key each night, a ritual of steely longing that kept time honest even when the clock could not. [146]

Tonight felt different. [149] The compass above the departure board spun wildly, and the station master whispered about temporal distortions in the cloud banks. [168] An old film projector hummed in the corner—a relic the station kept for nostalgic travelers who remembered when entertainment meant celluloid dreams. [190] She had watched those black-and-white reels countless times during slow shifts, studying the hidden sections that projectionist lore claimed existed between frames, images spliced in so briefly that only the desperate could perceive them.
