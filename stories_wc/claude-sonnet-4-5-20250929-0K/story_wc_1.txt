The canyon held its breath under a tapestry of stars, and Vera traced her fingers along the rock face, feeling for the patterns she'd missed before. [28] She had survived the attempt—the explosion meant to bury her kiln and her reputation forever—and now everything demanded retracing. [49]

Her workshop lay three miles behind, its windows still dark though dawn approached. [61] She was a luminous glass artist, known throughout the territory for sculptures that seemed to hold light prisoner, but the blast had taught her that beauty made enemies. [90] Someone wanted her work silenced, and the only clue was a charred strip of paper she'd found in the rubble: a smuggler's ledger documenting charcoal shipments, names reduced to ash except for one—Galloway. [122]

Vera had been steadily adaptive her whole life, learning to reshape molten glass as it cooled, adjusting to every shift in temperature and viscosity. [145] Survival required the same fluid thinking. [151] She'd left the canyon town years ago to study in the capital, then returned when the noise there made her hands shake. [172] Here, in the quiet beneath ancient stone walls, she'd discovered purpose in the pauses—those moments between breaths when glass transformed from liquid fire to solid light. [199]

Now those pauses held questions. [204]

She moved deeper into the canyon as the eastern rim began to glow. [217] Galloway ran the charcoal operation that supplied every forge and kiln in three counties. [231] His fuel had fired her best work, those pieces that galleries in distant cities fought over. [247] But the ledger strip suggested something else entirely: quantities too large, destinations too vague, payments in currencies that didn't circulate legally. [268]

The morning frost appeared as she reached the narrow gorge where Galloway stored his inventory. [283] Vera knelt, studying the delicate crystal formations on the rocks. [294] Patterns in morning frost had always spoken to her—they were nature's glass, ephemeral and honest. [310] These patterns, though, revealed disturbance. [315] Boot prints, recent. [318] Wagon ruts cutting through places where frost should have formed undisturbed lacework. [330]

She followed the traces to a cave mouth she'd never noticed, hidden behind a rockfall that looked natural but wasn't. [349] Inside, crates stood stacked to the ceiling. [356] Vera struck a match. [360]

Not charcoal. [362]

Gunpowder. [363]

The scorched testament in her pocket suddenly made terrible sense. [373] Galloway wasn't just supplying fuel for creation—he was arming someone for destruction. [386] And she'd seen something, hadn't she? [393] Three weeks ago, supervising a delivery, she'd noticed irregular packing, had mentioned it to Galloway's foreman. Not forgotten by them. [417]

"You shouldn't have come back." [422]

"The explosion was meant as a warning," he said. [460] "Leave the territory. [464] Forget what you saw." [468]

Vera stood slowly, her artist's hands steady despite the weapon. [479] "I spent years learning that nothing beautiful comes from shortcuts or force," she said. [494] "Glass demands patience. [497] It requires you to understand transformation, to work with the material's nature, not against it." [513] She gestured at the crates. [518] "Whatever you're building here will shatter. [524] It's too brittle, too rushed." [529]

"You think your sculptures matter more than revolution?" [537] Galloway's voice cracked. [540] "The territorial government bleeds us dry. [546] Someone has to fight back." [551]

"With powder stolen under ledgers marked as charcoal?" [559] Vera shook her head. [563] "You're not fighting. [566] You're just burning without purpose." [571]

The stars had faded to grey when she walked back through the canyon, Galloway bound and following, his ledgers and location traded for clemency. [593] The quiet canyon had witnessed everything—the plotting, the violence, the reckoning. [606]

At her workshop, Vera lit her kiln. [614] There would be questions, trials, complications. [621] But first, in the pause before the world intruded, she would create something luminous from the heat that had tried to destroy her. [643] She would work with what the fire had taught her, steadily adaptive, transforming fury into light. [659]

The morning frost melted on her windows as the furnace roared to life. [673]
