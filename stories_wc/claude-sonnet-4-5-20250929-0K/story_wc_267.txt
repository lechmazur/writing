The cobalt fortress stood beneath snow that never stopped falling, its turrets like frozen fingers clawing at a grey sky. [20] Captain Rielle climbed the spiral staircase once more, her left hand trailing the iron banister though she couldn't yet feel it. [40] Three months since the battle—three months of nerve regeneration, of pins and needles blossoming into crude sensation. [57] The surgeons promised full recovery, but she needed to understand what that safety had cost. [72]

The staircase dust showed the slightest discoloration where others had walked before her, a path worn by those who remained when she'd been carried away unconscious. [97] She followed it upward, each step an act of frostbitten hope against the weight of what she'd learn. [116]

At the landing stood the bellhop they called Silent-Choir, arranging luggage with seraphically mundane precision. [130] He'd earned his name during the siege—when the fortress bells rang alarm, he alone moved through the chaos without sound, ferrying ammunition and water to the walls while others screamed orders. [159] He never spoke, but his presence had saved dozens. [168] Now he performed his duties as if war and peace were the same, folding blankets, polishing brass. [184]

"I need to see the music room," Rielle said. [193]

The music room door hung crooked on its hinges. [242] Inside, morning light struggled through arrow-slitted windows, illuminating the grand piano that had survived everything. [257] Rielle's fingers still remembered playing it, before. [264] Before the enemy breach, before she'd held the eastern wall alone for seven minutes while others evacuated. [281] Before the shell that had severed nerves and consciousness both. [291]

The unpardonable silence pressed against her ears. [341] This was where Lieutenant Kovacs had hidden with sixteen children during the assault, playing folk songs to mask their crying while Rielle bled on the battlements. "You were with them," Rielle whispered. [437]

He nodded once, then did something unexpected—he touched middle C. [448] The note rang out, pure and singular in the frozen air. [524] It was Silent-Choir's voice, sacrificed to some trauma she'd never know. [536] It was this broken bench bolt, this damaged room still serving its purpose. Silent-Choir remained beside her, humming without sound, his lips shaping the words everyone in the cobalt fortress knew: "We stand where we're needed, while snow falls on stone." [623]

Outside, the snow continued its eternal descent, burying yesterday's footprints while new ones were made. [638] Inside, two survivors played music in a damaged room, their frostbitten hope a small warmth against the winter of what they'd witnessed. [661]

The cost of safety, Rielle finally understood, was that someone always paid it. [674] The grace was in not paying it alone. [683]

She played until full sensation returned to her fingertips, each note a small resurrection. [697]
