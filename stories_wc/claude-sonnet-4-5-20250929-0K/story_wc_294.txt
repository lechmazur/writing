The abandoned circus train had been Marco's home for three years, ever since the tech startup collapsed and his father disappeared into the hills. [25] The rusted carriages sat tilted on an overgrown siding, their faded paint still proclaiming wonders that would never tour again. [47] Marco kept the central car pristine, hanging his father's collection of foreign paper money along the curved walls like gallery art—rupees, pesos, kroner, and dinar, each bill a story his father never told. [78]

His father had been a highway robber, though Marco learned this only after finding the money hidden in their apartment, the same week the investors pulled out and two hundred jobs vanished. [109] The bills were crisp, organized by continent, clearly important enough that his father had preserved them through years of pretending to be someone else. [133]

Marco understood why some paths circle back. [140] His father had tried to escape his past by joining the startup as a security consultant, rising through the company's explosive growth, earning legitimate money for the first time. [168] But when everything crumbled, he'd returned to the only life he knew, robbing the same mountain roads his own father had worked. [191]

The circus train offered perspective. [196] Marco worked as a ranger now, monitoring the forest preserve that had swallowed the old rail line. [213] He'd marked the territory carefully—installing motion sensors disguised as birdhouses, posting weathered signs about endangered species, creating the impression of constant surveillance. [236] The markers worked; hikers avoided the area, and his father's old associates never ventured close enough to discover Marco's sanctuary. [257]

Through the curved windows, dusk settled over the pines with a quality Marco had learned to cherish—a dusky harmony between day and night, between what was and what might be. [288] He maintained a polished calm in everything: the way he arranged his books, brewed his coffee, catalogued the forest's subtle changes. [309] Visitors to the ranger station often commented on how perceptively calm he seemed, able to read trouble in a broken branch or worry in a stranger's gait. [335]
