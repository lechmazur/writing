The sound wave garden existed in the margins of perception, where frequencies became visible as trembling ribbons of light and silence pooled in shallow depressions like liquid shadow. [29] Dr. Marquez had brought Theo here three weeks ago, after the fourth injection of the experimental treatment that was supposed to untangle the memories bleeding between them. [57] Instead, the neural entanglement had deepened, and now Theo couldn't remember which childhood belonged to whom—the scrape on a knee from falling off a bicycle, was that his or hers? [88]

"You're drumming again," Marquez said, her voice soft in the crystalline air. [99]

Theo's fingers stilled against his thigh, though the rhythm continued in his head, tapping out the pattern he'd unconsciously adopted from her—her nervous habit, her way of thinking through problems. [130] This was the quietly rebellious act he'd perfected: accepting what the treatment was doing to them while refusing to pretend it wasn't happening. [153]

They stood at the edge of the auric lagoon, its waters shimmering gold and green under starlight that seemed closer here, as if the garden existed in some pocket dimension where the cosmos pressed nearer to earth. [189] The lagoon reflected not their faces but their intertwined neural patterns, rendered visible by whatever physics governed this place. [208]

"Do you remember why we volunteered?" [214] Marquez asked. [216]

Theo did, but he wasn't sure if the memory was his own or hers, downloaded through their connected synapses. [236] They had both lost someone—a daughter for her, a sister for him—and both ghosts had loved music, had believed sound could reshape reality. [261] The experimental treatment was supposed to map consciousness, to prove that minds could harmonize, could create something larger than individual existence. [282] Instead, it had made them into guinea pigs in a study about the dangers of merged identity. [300]

"Stop it," she said, but without conviction. [340]

"You're the one who taught me," Theo replied, then realized he might be wrong. [354] Perhaps he'd taught her. [359] Their memories had become so entangled that causality itself seemed negotiable. [370]

The garden responded to their confusion, its cultivated sound waves growing discordant. [382] Trees with bark that hummed in minor keys began to warble. [394] Flowers that chimed with each breeze fell silent. [403]

Marquez moved to perch on the railing, her legs dangling over the auric water. [418] "I brought you here because this place exists at the hush of the universe inhaling," she said. [436] "That moment between breaths when anything is possible, when change can still be chosen." [451]

[463] They could resist, could claw back their separate identities and retreat into the fortresses of their individual minds. [482] Or they could lean into the entanglement, could try to breathe life into the shared dream that had drawn them both to this experiment—the dream that consciousness was meant to be collaborative, that isolation was the real disease. [539] The rhythm was complex now, containing both their patterns, creating something neither could produce alone. [554] The auric lagoon responded, its waters rising in impossible spirals of light.
