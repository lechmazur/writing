Maren had carved over four hundred gravestones in her life, and not one of them had taught her what she truly needed to know: the shape of collective grief. [29]
She was fiercely meditative in her work, each chisel stroke a breath held for minutes, her hands moving with the slow certainty of tide against rock. [55]
But the stones she made were individual monuments, and she had begun to suspect that grief did not live in individuals at all. [78]
It lived in the space between them, in the shared silence of a family that had lost too much to name each loss separately. [102]
The ancient greenhouse at the edge of her property had become her sanctuary over the past three years. [120]
Its glass panels were fogged with a century of condensation, and its iron frame groaned in the wind like an old man's bones. [144]
Vines had claimed the eastern wall entirely, and the floor was a mosaic of cracked tile and wild moss. [163]
At dusk, when the light turned the glass to amber and the shadows stretched like fingers across the stone floor, she would come here to think, her chisel bag heavy on her hip. [196]
It was across the butterfly migration that she first felt the presence. [208]
Monarchs passed through in waves, their wings like stained glass, and with them came her grandmother's ghost. [226]
Not a frightening ghost—a patient one, spectral and warm, sitting on the rusted potting bench with her hands folded, waiting. [247]
Elin had died seven years ago, and Maren had never fully processed the loss, had never been able to retrace the path of their last conversation the way she retraced chisel marks on stone to find the perfect line. [286]
But now Elin was here, and the mentorship began without words, without explanation, as though death had simply removed the walls between them. [309]
Each dusk, Elin would appear, and Maren would carve, and between them on the bench lay a woven ribbon roll—a family heirloom, dyed in deep indigo and crimson, its surface embroidered with the spines of every poem Elin had ever loved. [351]
This was their method: a family custom of trading favorite poems at dinner, carried forward even into death. [369]
Elin would unroll a few inches of the ribbon and recite a line, and Maren would respond with one of her own, and in that exchange the greenhouse would fill with a thundering calm—a silence so heavy it pressed against the glass. [412]
The monarchs would pause in their flight, as if listening. [422]
Through the migration, week after week, Maren learned to see grief not as a single stone but as a landscape, vast and layered, with ridgelines and valleys she had never imagined. [453]
Elin showed her how to retrace not just her own losses but the losses of every family who had stood before her, ordering stones, their faces tight with the effort of choosing words for the unchosen. [489]
The ribbon became a map, each poem a coordinate on the terrain of shared sorrow, and Maren began to understand that the shape she had been searching for was not a shape at all but a space—the space between one heart and another, where grief lives when it is carried together. [541]
By the time the last monarchs disappeared southward, their wings like closing doors, Maren understood what she had been searching for all along. [564]
Collective grief had no single shape. [570]
It was the greenhouse itself—vast, broken, beautiful, letting light through its cracks in ways that no intact wall ever could. [591]
She carved one final stone that autumn, not for any grave but for the center of the greenhouse floor. [610]
It bore no name, only a single line from Elin's favorite poem, and beside it she laid the woven ribbon roll, now unspooled to its full length, every poem visible at once like a skeleton of love. [648]
The ghost smiled and was gone. [654]
The butterflies would return, and so would the poems, and so would the silence between them, heavier than stone, lighter than light. [676]
