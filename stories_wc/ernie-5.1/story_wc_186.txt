Maren had not slept in eleven days, but the stars did not care about her exhaustion, and neither did the glass humming pavilion. [23]
The pavilion rose from the scorched plain like a breath frozen mid-exhale, its walls of curved glass vibrating with a low, constant hum that the old engineers had once tuned to match the frequency of the beacon tower above it. [57]
Those engineers were gone now, along with the cities, the grids, and every certainty that had held the world together before the Collapse. [86]
Maren was the custodian—the last one—and she carried the broken crystal prism against her chest like a second heart, its jagged edges pressing through the oilcloth into skin already mapped with old cuts. [121]
The prism had been the beacon's core, the lens that focused starlight into signal, but it had fractured during the Collapse along with everything else, except for one piece: a shard of hush, the texts called it, a fragment so saturated with stillness that it could fold time backward like paper. [172]
Maren was retrocognitively blessed, though some nights she wondered if the word should be cursed. [187]
When she pressed the shard to her temple, the past unspooled behind her eyes—not as memory but as vision, vivid and involuntary. [210]
She had seen the Collapse from the inside, every tower falling, every voice screaming, and threaded through that destruction she had found a pattern too deliberate to ignore. [238]
Coincidences. [239]
Too many of them, stacked like stones across a river she could not cross without stepping on meaning. [257]
A distress message received the day before the first city burned. [268]
A star aligning with the tower's axis on the exact night the power failed worldwide. [283]
A child born under a rare constellation who would grow up to engineer the very prism now broken in Maren's hands. [304]
She believed it all meant something—that was her motivation, the stubborn engine that drove her up the tower every night to polish dead mirrors and adjust dead lenses, performing rituals for a congregation of none. [340]
Tonight she would rekindle the beacon. [346]
Not with electricity, which no longer existed, but with the shard of hush and the star paths she had spent months tracking across the pavilion floor in chalk that the rain kept washing away. [380]
The method was elegant in its simplicity: align the shard with the precise constellation that had shone on the night the first message arrived, let the hush amplify through the prism's remaining facets, and push silence backward through time until the beacon sang again. [424]
Not to call anyone. [428]
To prove that the coincidences were not coincidences at all. [438]
She climbed. [440]

She closed her eyes and let the retrocognition take her. [517]
The past rushed backward—she saw the star paths folding, saw the message, saw the child, saw every thread stitching chaos into design, and in that vision she understood that the hush was not empty silence but a space where meaning lived, waiting to be heard. [563]
Then the hush spread. [567]
Silence, vast and absolute, poured from the shard into the dead tower, and for one breathtaking moment the beacon flared—not with light but with meaning, a signal that whispered: this all happened on purpose. [602]
