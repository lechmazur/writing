The mercury-glass seer had not slept in what felt like years, though sleep itself was not the problem that kept her wandering. [22] The problem was that every night, without choosing to, she substituted reality with something else—a different sky, a different name for the rain, a version of the mountain where the labyrinthine bookstore had only three floors instead of nine hundred. [64] Each morning she woke in the world she had left, but the ache of the substituted one lingered like a bruise pressed too deep to show. [90] This had been happening since the remembering became painful. [99]

She could not recall when it started, only that at some point the act of remembering had turned into something that scraped against her bones, and so she had buried her talents the way one buries a corpse in shallow ground—not to hide it, but to stop it from surfacing. [150] The talents were there, waiting: the sight that read mercury in glass, the voice that could speak in languages that predated speech, the hands that could reshape what was real. [180] But she had pressed them down so deep that even she forgot what they were. [195]

Now she wandered the labyrinthine bookstore that occupied the entire mountain, its corridors folding into themselves, shelves stacked so high they touched the ceiling like the ribs of some ancient beast that had calcified into paper and ink. [233] She was searching for the lost language stone—a smooth, dark thing no larger than a fist, covered in inscriptions that no living scholar could decode, because the language it was written in had died with the people who spoke it. [274] She had found it three nights ago in a section of the bookstore she had never visited before, though she had lived there her entire life, and the stone hummed when she held it, a low vibration that traveled through her palms and into her chest, and in that hum she heard the shape of something she could not name. [334] Home, maybe. [336] Or the memory of home before it was buried beneath the weight of all she had forgotten. [353]

She wanted to understand the shape of home—not a place on any map, but a geometry, a way that things fit together that she had once known and then lost when the remembering became too much to bear. [392] The stone was the key, she was certain of that, but the stone required a method she had forgotten, a way of reading it that lived in the body rather than the mind. [425] Then she found the feather. [430] It lay on a shelf between two volumes of poetry that had never been opened, white and impossibly soft, and when she touched it, the texture unlocked something deep in her mind—not a memory exactly, but the sensation of a memory, the way a seed knows the shape of the tree it will become. [485] She pressed the feather to the stone. [492] The inscriptions shifted. [495] The mountain shuddered, just once, a hidden thunder rolling through the stone and the shelves and the nine hundred floors of books that had been waiting, it seemed, for this exact moment. [527] And the seer understood: her buried talent was not sight or voice but the ability to substitute reality, to replace what was with what could be, and she had been doing it every night not because she couldn't stop, but because she was trying to seed a new world, one where home had the shape she remembered before the remembering became painful. [589] The feather fell from her hand. [595] The stone went dark. [599] And somewhere in the mountain, a door opened that had never been there before, leading to a room that smelled like rain and old paper, and she knew—with the certainty of mercury reflecting light—that this was the shape she had been searching for. [644] She stepped through the doorway. [649] The mountain held its breath around her, and for the first time in longer than she could remember, the seer was not afraid of what she would find. [677]
