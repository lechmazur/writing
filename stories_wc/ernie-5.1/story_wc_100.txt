<story>The package arrived on a Tuesday, which was wrong, because undertakers do not receive packages on Tuesdays. [22] They receive bodies on Tuesdays, and silence on every other day, and Marcus had long since stopped distinguishing between the two. [44] He set the box on his kitchen table and stared at it the way he stared at caskets—measuring what fit inside, calculating the weight of what he could not refuse. [69] The wrapping was salt-stained, the tape yellowed and peeling, and when he peeled it back with hands that had washed too many dead men clean, he found a lighthouse lens. [97] Broken. [99] A spiderweb of cracks ran through the glass like veins in a hand that had stopped praying, and the brass frame was green with corrosion, as though it had been pulled from the bottom of the sea. [136] He should have thrown it away. [142] Instead, he held it up to the window and watched the afternoon light fracture into something that looked almost like a signal. [168] That night, the lens pulsed. [173] Not with light exactly, but with a rhythm—faint, insistent, the kind of pattern that meant distress. [193] Not his distress. [196] Something older. [198] Something calling from a place that had forgotten how to ask for help. [210] Marcus was weary in the way that sleep could not fix, a tiredness that had settled into his marrow over thirty years of laying the dead to rest. [238] He gave them temporary addresses—plots, headstones, names in registers—but rest never held. [252] The dead wandered, and he felt them in that particular stillness that chased him home each night, settling into his empty chairs as if they had always belonged there, as if his house were a waystation and he were the last one who had forgotten. [294] This was the thing nobody told you about the profession: the dead did not stay buried. [308] They lingered in the quiet, in the pause between heartbeats, in the space where breath used to be. [324] And Marcus, who had spent three decades arranging their temporary peace, was running out of places to hide from their restlessness. [346] The lens led him to the coast, then beneath the waves, to a city that should not exist—a library with shelves descending into dark water, books bloated and illegible, the air thick with the weight of drowned knowledge. [382] The signal grew stronger here. [387] Every instinct told him to surface, but that stubbornness which had carried him through thirty years of other people's grief moved him forward instead, deeper into the impossible space. [414] He understood now what he was meant to do: revitalize this place, make it breathe again, give every restless soul he had ever laid to rest a permanent address where the stillness would not chase them anymore. [457] He set the broken lens in the center of the library floor, and the cracks caught what little light filtered through the water, scattering it in every direction like a star that had learned how to forgive. [503] The signal changed. [506] It was no longer distress. [511] It was arrival. [514] Somewhere above, the dead stopped wandering. [521] And for the first time in thirty years, the stillness did not chase Marcus home. [536] It sat beside him, finally caught, finally still. [545] He closed his eyes. [549] The water pressed against the windows like palms. [557] For thirty years he had carried the dead from one temporary address to another, never once stopping to ask whether they wanted to stop. [579] Now the lens answered for them. [585] The library hummed with a frequency that felt like permission. [593] Marcus exhaled, and the breath left him slow, the way it does when you finally set down something you did not know you were holding. [617] The stillness stayed. [621] It did not chase. [625] It simply was. [629] And in that forgetting, he found the one thing he had never been able to give any of them: a permanent address. [649]</story>
