<story>Maren had spent thirty years translating cuneiform, and the habit of careful hands had never left her, even here, suspended in the magnetic arbor above the clouds where the branches hummed with trapped lightning and the sky was just a rumor somewhere far below. [44]

She could still feel the absence like a word missing from a sentence she had almost decoded, the gap more informative than any sign she had ever pressed into clay. [101]
On the table before her sat the knowledge fruit seed, the one the spirit had pressed into her palm as it dissolved into light, its surface etched with patterns that resembled no script she knew but reminded her, impossibly, of home. [142]
It was warm, and it pulsed faintly, like a heart that had forgotten its rhythm but refused to stop. [161]
Maren was a kindly woman, methodical in all things, and she did not rush to plant it. [178]
Instead, she sat with it, turning it between her fingers the way she once turned clay tablets, reading the wear patterns for meaning the way others read faces. [206]
Her ambition had always been to understand transition—not metaphorically, but physically, the actual mechanics of how one state became another, how a solid became a liquid, how a living thing became a memory. [240]
This was the question that had haunted her since her first tablet, since she first realized that cuneiform wasn't just record-keeping but a technology of transformation, a way to make thought permanent across the gap between minds. [277]
The spirit's departure was the ultimate transition, the one she had spent her whole career preparing to witness, and now that it had happened, she meant to understand it completely. [307]
Through analogical thinking, she approached the seed the way she approached every untranslatable text: by finding the nearest known thing and stretching the comparison until it held, until the analogy became a bridge. [340]
The seed was like a cuneiform sign—a wedge pressed into clay that held meaning only in context, only in relation to the signs around it. [366]
The arbor was like a tablet—a surface waiting to receive inscription, its magnetic branches the stylus. [383]
And the spirit's leaving was like a verb changing tense, the same action viewed from a different moment, the subject still present but transformed. [407]
She placed the seed in the magnetic soil and watched as the arbor's field guided it downward, the branches swaying in response as if acknowledging something ancient and long-expected. [436]
What grew was not a tree but a lattice of light, each branch a cuneiform stroke, each leaf a phoneme, the entire structure a language she could almost read but never quite master. [469]

That was the distance the tone of her life had always carried: touching, never grasped, close enough to feel the warmth but never close enough to hold. [545]
Her haunted ambition had not been to possess knowledge but to stand at the exact point where knowing and not-knowing met, to reestablish the bridge between states even if she could never cross it herself. [580]

She picked up her stylus—she had brought one, of course; a translator never travels without her tools—and began to inscribe the lattice's pattern onto a fresh tablet of cloud-clay, each stroke an act of reestablishment, each sign a promise that the transition, though invisible, was real. [667]
The seed had not given her answers. [674]
It had given her a better question, and for Maren, that had always been enough. [689]
The arbor swayed. [692]
