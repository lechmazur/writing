Maren had not spoken above a whisper in eleven years, not since the night she learned that silence could be accumulated like debt, compounding in the dark until it owed something back. [32]
The prism sanctuary stood on the cliff's edge where the land gave up and became sea, its walls made of sea glass that caught starlight and scattered it into long, trembling bands of color across the stone floor. [70]
She sat cross-legged in the center of that fractured light, the tapestry needle balanced between her thumb and forefinger, its eye so small it seemed to swallow the light rather than thread it. [103]
Before her lay the net—centuries old, stiff with salt and time, its knots tight as fists, each one a small mouth that had never learned to speak. [131]
This was how she exhumed them: not with shovels or prayer, but by unraveling knots in a fisherman's net that had drowned with its crew in 1643, pulling the dead back into the world one thread at a time. [170]
Each knot she pulled loose released a sound—not a scream, but the absence of one, a hollow that pressed against the ears like deep water, a silence so dense it had weight. [203]
The interlaced hush accumulated silence the way the net had once accumulated drowned men, layer upon layer, knot upon knot, until the air in the sanctuary grew thick and tasted of brine and old grief. [238]
She called this work restoration, though she restored nothing visible, nothing that could be hung on a wall or worn on a face. [261]
The masks she repaired by day were broken—cracked along the jaw, chipped at the brow—but those were just her trade, the work that kept her fed while she did the real work at night. [297]
Tonight she was not alone. [302]
Lúca sat across from her, knees drawn up, watching with the particular stillness of someone who had been told a secret and was still deciding whether to believe it or run. [333]
"You're going to tell me again," Lúca said, and the words came out flat, stripped of wonder, the way a thing sounds when you've heard it too many times. [364]
"I'm going to show you," Maren corrected, and pushed the needle into the first knot. [380]
The thread gave way with a sound like a breath held too long finally released, and the air between them thickened. [401]
Lúca flinched. [403]
"That's one of them," Maren said, her voice low and lucid, carrying the unrest of someone who understood exactly what she was doing and why it terrified her. [432]
She worked through the net knot by knot, each one a small exhumation, each release adding to the silence until it filled the sanctuary like water filling a hull, rising past her ankles, her knees, her chest. [469]
The restless spirits didn't appear as ghosts—they never did. [480]
They appeared as pressure, as the feeling of being watched from a direction that had no walls, as the sudden coldness that had no source and no season. [508]
Maren grounded them by giving the silence a place to go: the net, which held it the way it had once held fish, the way it now held the dead, each knot a small coffin for a sound that should never have been swallowed. [552]
"Why do you do this?" Lúca asked as the last knot came free and the net lay flat, empty, its purpose fulfilled for another century. [577]
"Because someone has to," Maren said, and for the first time in eleven years, she let her voice rise above a whisper, let it fill the space the spirits had vacated. [608]
The starlight through the prism walls caught the sound and scattered it into colors, and for a moment, the sanctuary was not silent at all. [633]
It was full. [636]
