Maren had always been the island's most unreliable mystic. [12]
She measured magic with a wooden caliper and recorded dream patterns in a leather journal, which made her either the island's greatest skeptic or its most devoted believer—she could never decide which, and the uncertainty itself had become a kind of practice. [63]
Tonight, standing on the ancient ridge in bloom, she leaned toward neither. [71]
The ridge stretched like a spine above the village, its basalt cracked open by centuries of roots, now smothered in phosphorescent blossoms that pulsed in time with something no instrument could capture. [106]
Before monsoon arrives, the elders said, the ridge would bloom one final time, and the island's collective memory would either hold or scatter like pollen in wind. [137]
Maren didn't believe in collective memory any more than she believed in ghosts, but she believed in patterns, and the patterns were unmistakable. [163]
For three generations, the islanders had dreamed the same dream—a vast orchard where fruit hung like lanterns, each one containing a story someone had forgotten. [192]
When the last elder stopped dreaming, the orchard faded, and with it the songs, the navigation charts, the names of the dead. [219]
Maren had catalogued every dream report with empirical precision, cross-referencing symbols, emotions, and the exact hour each dreamer woke. [242]
The data was whimsical in its consistency: everyone dreamed of the same tree, the same fruit, the same sense of something sleeping beneath the roots. [271]
In her palm, she held the object the dreamers described—a knowledge fruit seed, no larger than a thumbnail, warm to the touch, humming at a frequency she could feel but not measure. [308]
She had found it in the ridge's highest crevice, wrapped in moss that shouldn't have been growing there. [328]
The seed was real; she had tested it. [338]
It grew in darkness, it responded to spoken word, it emitted light when held near other seeds. [358]
But none of that explained why the dreams had stopped. [369]
The monsoon was nine days away. [376]
If the collective memory didn't waken before the rains came, it would dissolve into the flood, and the island would forget itself entirely. [402]
Maren didn't know how to waken a memory, but she knew how to interpret dreams, and the dreams had left instructions buried in their own symbolism. [431]
The tree wanted to be spoken to. [439]
The fruit wanted to be eaten. [446]
The root wanted to be planted in the place where the first story had been told. [463]
She climbed to the ridge's peak, where the blossoms were brightest and the air tasted of ozone and jasmine. [484]
She pressed the seed into the basalt and she spoke—not in any language the island recognized but in the language of the dreams themselves, the one she had decoded from a hundred sleeping minds. [521]
The ridge shuddered. [525]
The blossoms flared white. [530]
And somewhere beneath the stone, something vast and ancient opened its eyes. [543]
Maren stood there, heart pounding, notebook in hand, and wrote a single line: "Empirically, the impossible just happened." [567]
Then she smiled, because for the first time the doubt felt luminous—not like darkness but like the space where light hasn't decided what to become yet. [596]
The blossoms continued to pulse and Maren realized the ridge itself was dreaming, its roots shifting, its stones breathing in rhythm with the seed she'd planted. [625]
She understood now that the empirical and the whimsical weren't opposites; they were the same thing seen from different angles, the way a dream could be both data and mystery. [658]
The monsoon would come in nine days and the island would either remember or forget, and Maren would be there to record it all, not because she believed but because the act of witnessing was its own kind of faith. [699]
She closed her journal and looked out at the village below, where lanterns flickered in windows and someone was singing a song she almost recognized. [725]
The seed pulsed once more, warm against the stone, and Maren thought maybe that's what collective memory is—not a thing you protect but a thing you keep showing up for. [757]
The ridge bloomed brighter. [762]
The doubt remained luminous. [767]
