The coachman from Gloom Hill had not spoken to a living soul in eleven days, and he preferred it that way. [21]
Silence was the only honest language left, and he had driven through enough of it to know its weight. [40]
The road down to the coast was narrow, cut into basalt, and his horses knew every rut by heart, their hooves finding the path in the dark the way water finds its way to the sea. [76]
He guided them to the blooming jetty at twilight, where the lantana had swallowed the old war posts and the air smelled of salt and something sweeter, something that should not have been growing here — not on ground that remembered gunfire. [117]
The reef was dead. [121]
He had seen it from the cliff road three mornings ago — a pale scar stretching to the horizon where color used to live, where fish had once moved in clouds so dense they darkened the water. [157]
Now he stood on the jetty with his collection of dried seed pods pressed against his chest, feeling their brittle weight like a second heartbeat, and he understood that he was the last person who remembered what this place was supposed to be. [187]
The pods were all he had left of the Concord. [194]
Not the treaty kind. The submerged kind — the old agreement between the reef and the shore that had held this coast in balance for longer than any living memory, a pact written not in ink but in the slow chemistry of coral and root and tide. [226]
When the reef died, the concord drowned with it, and the land began to remember what it had been before: a battlefield. [244]
Beneath the lantana, he could feel the old scars — craters where mortars had fallen, trenches where men had waited to die, the compressed silence of a hundred years of violence sleeping just below the flowers. [269]
The garden was beautiful, but it was a lie laid over a wound. [297]
He set the seed pods on the jetty planks and opened his mouth, and what came out was not any language the living would recognize. [336]
It was Old Tidal, the tongue the reef-keepers had spoken before the first ship broke the horizon, a language built on the sounds of water moving over stone. [348]
He spoke it because he was the last one who could, because his grandmother had pressed the syllables into him like seeds into soil when he was small, and because the dead languages still held power that the living ones had forgotten — power that lived in the mouth and the ear, not on paper. [350]
The words were not a prayer. They were an invitation. [380]
He listened. [383]
A single bird called from the lantana — a sharp, descending note, then silence, the kind of silence that is not empty but full, the way a held breath is full. [412]
He understood it. [453]
The bird was saying the soil was ready, that the mycelium threads below had been waiting, patient as anything that lives underground, as anything that knows the surface is temporary. [510]
He picked up the seed pods one by one and pressed them into the gaps between the jetty planks, into the earth beneath, and he fertilized not with chemicals or compost but with understanding — with the knowledge that the bird's call meant the roots of the lantana had opened pathways for something older, something the war had buried but never killed. [520]
This was his method: he did not force growth. [560]
He listened to what the birds remembered and gave the seeds exactly what they asked for, and in return the land gave him back what it had stolen — the memory of what grew here before the guns, before the trenches, before the long century of men trying to kill each other on this narrow strip of coast. [618]
The first pod split open in his hands, and something dark and rich poured out — not soil, but memory, thick and black and alive with the ghost of every root that had ever held this cliff together. [672]
The submerged concord was not a thing you could find. [675]
It was a thing you had to grow back, thread by thread, in the narrow space between what the sea had taken and what the land refused to forget — a vast thing happening in a narrow place, the way all true healings happen. [687]
By the time the last light failed, the jetty was covered in green, and the coachman from Gloom Hill sat among his horses and listened to the birds singing in a language older than war, and he thought that this — this small, vast, stubborn act of planting — was the only victory that mattered, the only concord worth keeping. [690]
