<story>Maren had not led a revolution in forty years, but the asteroid still remembered her voice echoing through its baroque corridors, and she could not forget it either. [28]
She sat in the Archives of Vellum and Dust, turning a crystal vase shard between her fingers, its edges catching the light that poured through windows carved with angelic faces long since eroded by cosmic wind. [64]
The archaeological dispute had consumed the last decade of her life, though consume was the wrong word — it had hollowed her, the way rivers hollow stone, slowly and with a patience that felt almost tender. [99]
The excavation team from the outer colonies claimed the Vessari civilization had been theocratic, built on worship of the Visible Time that had once flowed freely through this sector of space. [130]
Maren and her small faction argued otherwise — they had been poets, dreamers who wove time into art rather than bowing before it. [152]
After time became visible, everyone wanted to claim they understood it, but Maren knew that understanding was just another word for domination. [174]
She was poetically inclined even now, her thoughts moving in meters and rhymes she could not suppress, and she composed elegies for a people she had never met while the dispute raged around her like a storm that would not pass. [215]
The asteroid itself was baroque in the truest sense — every surface ornamented, every hallway a spiral of gold leaf and cracked marble, as though the Vessari had believed beauty was the only argument worth making. [250]
But beauty had not saved them, and Maren could not decide if that was tragedy or mercy. [267]
During the storms — and there were storms now that time was visible, temporal squalls that ripped through the corridors and shattered anything reflective — the team had learned to cover their mirrors with heavy cloth. [301]
This was the method that had saved them: covering mirrors during storms, because the Visible Time, when it struck a reflective surface, did not bounce away but accumulated, pooling like water, and anyone who looked into a mirror during a squall would see not their own face but every moment that had ever existed in that space, all at once. [361]
It drove people mad, or worse, it made them stop wanting to be anyone else. [376]
Maren had seen it happen to Kessel, who had looked into a mirror during the Great Squall of '19 and had not spoken since, sitting now in a corner of the Archives, humming a melody that might have been Vessari. [416]
The crystal vase shard in her hand was the only artifact she had kept from the original dig, and she held it not as evidence but as a vow. [445]
She needed to devise something — not a theory, not a rebuttal, but an entirely new framework for understanding the Vessari, one that would reimagine an entire civilization not as it had been excavated but as it might have dreamed itself into being. [487]
The dispute would never end; the colonies wanted religion because religion justified extraction, and Maren's faction wanted art because art demanded nothing. [509]
But she was tired of arguing about the dead. [518]
What she wanted was to build, in the space between their interpretations, a civilization that could breathe — a Vessari reimagined not through shards and dust but through the ache of what might have been. [552]
She set the crystal shard on the table, and in the baroque light it threw a prism across her hands, painting her skin in colors that had no names in any living language. [585]
Outside, another storm was gathering, and somewhere in the corridors, someone was forgetting to cover a mirror. [602]
Maren stood, her sorrow burnished now, not dull but bright, the way old gold is bright — and she went to find them, because that was what revolutionaries did, even defunct ones, even when the revolution was only against forgetting. [641]</story>
