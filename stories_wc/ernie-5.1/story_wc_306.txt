<story>The last spark of the campfire surrendered to the dark, and Maren climbed the lighthouse stairs with the hollow mirror shard pressed against her chest like a second heartbeat. [28] She was a conscience for hire, the kind of woman people paid to tell them what they already knew but refused to say aloud. [49] At the top, the wind caught her hair and pulled it toward the sea, and she set the shard on the windowsill where dawn was just beginning to bruise the horizon in shades of violet and ash. [79] Her client, a fisherman named Aldric, sat against the railing with his eyes closed, listening to the wind chimes she had strung along the lighthouse gallery that morning. [104] This was her method: deciphering wind chime selections. [114] Each chime represented a moral position, and the ones Aldric chose revealed where his conscience actually lived. [135] He'd picked the low brass one for loyalty, the glass one for transparency, and the wooden one for mercy—but he'd left the iron chime untouched, the one that represented doubt. [161] That was the cloak of doubt, and he refused to wear it. [174] Maren understood this better than anyone because she was sincerely insincere—her entire profession required her to be false while being completely genuine about it. [198] She believed in the work even though the work was a lie. [212] "You didn't pick the iron chime," she said, settling beside him on the cold stone. [228] "Doubt's for people who can't decide." [238] "Doubt's for people who care enough to hesitate." [248] She reached into her coat and pulled out the hollow mirror shard, holding it up so the first light of dawn filled its concave surface and threw a distorted reflection across both their faces. [279] "Look at yourself in this," she said. [287] He looked, and what he saw was not himself but a version of himself warped by the curve—someone recognizable but not certain. [310] "That's what doubt does," Maren said. [318] "It doesn't destroy the image." [324] "It just refuses to let you mistake the reflection for the truth." [337] The breeze shifted, and the chimes sang a dissonant chord that hung in the air like a question nobody had asked yet. [361] Aldric opened his eyes. [365] "I hired you to tell me I was right about leaving her." [378] "And I will," Maren said. [384] "But first I need you to unhook yourself from the certainty that rightness is the same as goodness." [403] He stared at her. [407] "That's not what I paid for." [415] "No," she agreed. [419] "But it's what you need." [425] She was sincerely insincere in that moment—genuinely committed to a profession built on performance, offering him truth through the very artifice he'd hired her for. [453] The dawn light strengthened, and the hollow mirror shard caught it fully now, projecting a warped circle of gold across the weathered lighthouse floor. [479] Maren waited. [483] This was her real motivation, the one she never put on invoices: to welcome nuance into certainty, to crack open the sealed rooms where people locked their doubts and let the light get in. [516] Aldric reached out and touched the iron chime. [526] It rang—a single, low note that cut through the wind and the other chimes and the sound of the sea. [547] He didn't speak. [551] He didn't need to. [555] The note hung in the air between them, a protected space where vulnerability could exist without being named. [576] Maren smiled, and for once her smile was not a performance. [590] She gathered the hollow mirror shard and slipped it back into her coat, where it would wait for the next person who needed to see themselves warped but not destroyed. [619] The lighthouse stood against the dawn, holding its light for no one in particular, and that was enough. [643]</story>
