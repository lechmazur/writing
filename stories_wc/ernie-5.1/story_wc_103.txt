<story>Maren had been designing hedge mazes for longer than she could remember, though the photographs she kept in her coat pocket proved otherwise. [23] Each one was fading, the faces bleaching into white, the dates dissolving like sugar in rain. [39] She carried them not as memories but as evidence that she had once existed in a different configuration. [57]

The living bridge garden stretched before her in impossible spirals, its hedges breathing with a slow, vegetative rhythm. [75] Every path curved inward, every turn folded back on itself, and she understood with a quaking certainty that she had walked this maze before. [99] Not in the way one revisits a place, but in the way a thought revisits itself—retrocognitively trapped, looping backward through her own history. [123]

She was not here by choice. [131] Or rather, she had been here so many times that choice had eroded, like the photographs, like everything. [147]

The halo fragment sat in her palm, warm and impossibly light, a shard of something that had no business existing in a garden of growing things. [173] She had found it at the maze's center on her first walk—or her last—and it had shown her what she craved: the texture of time itself. [201] Not the smooth flow people imagined, but something ridged and granular, something you could press your thumb against and feel the grain of. [224] That was her motivation, the only thing that had survived the fading: to touch time's surface and know it was real. [245]

But the garden was disintegrating. [250] The hedges didn't wither or die—they unraveled through hyperbolic geometry, each branch splitting into infinite smaller branches that curled away from themselves at angles that shouldn't exist in three-dimensional space. [281] Where the geometry took hold, matter simply stopped cohering. [290] Leaves became dust that became light that became nothing. [299] She watched a bridge ahead of her lose its railings one recursive curve at a time, the wood peeling into fractal spirals that vanished before they hit the ground. [328]

Maren clutched the fragment tighter. [333] The photographs in her pocket were almost blank now, just smears of silver where faces had been. [350] She realized she couldn't remember her own face. [358] The retrocognitive trap had eaten that too, pulling her backward through every version of herself until the original was gone. [378] She was not a person anymore. [384] She was a pattern repeating. [389]

Yet the fragment pulsed against her skin, and in its glow she could almost see the texture she sought—not with her eyes but with some deeper sense, the way a maze feels its own walls. [425] Time here was not a line. [431] It was a surface, curved and infinite, and she was walking along it the way water walks along a leaf. [451] The hyperbolic geometry wasn't destroying the garden. [458] It was revealing what the garden had always been: a map of time's topology, drawn in living wood. [476]

She understood now why the photographs faded. [483] They were pictures of a time that no longer existed, and in a place where time curved back on itself, the past couldn't hold its shape. [509] Nothing could. [511] That was the texture—the instability, the trembling of everything against its own dissolution. [525] She was quaking with it, her whole body a low hum of recognition. [538]

Maren stepped forward into the spiraling dark of the maze, the fragment bright in her hand, and let the hyperbolic curves take her. [561] She did not disintegrate with fear. [567] She disintegrated with understanding, each particle of her scattering along the infinite geometry of a time she had finally, impossibly, touched. [588]

The living bridge garden breathed on without her, its hedges curling and uncurling in rhythms that no one would ever read. [609] The photographs in the pocket of the coat she had left behind were white now, perfectly white, and the fragment was gone, and the maze waited for the next version of her to walk it backward into the light.
