<story>Maren had not spoken in eleven years, not since the library collapsed through the membrane and left her stranded in this frozen garden where the mist never lifted and the frost grew in patterns that resembled text she could almost read. [41] She was the guardian still, feral and true, though her books had turned to ice and her shelves to skeletons of frost that creaked like old ships in a wind she could not feel. [75] Her fur, thick, gray, matted with years of solitude, kept her alive when nothing else could. [91] Every night she waited. [95] Not for rescue, not for return, but for the moment when the boundaries of light would soften enough to let her slip through the veil of sleep back to where she belonged. [127] She was dimensionally homesick in a way that had no name in any language she remembered, a longing so deep it had become geological, layered into her bones like sediment. [157] The other dimension, the one with warm light and open stacks and the smell of old paper, existed only as a pressure behind her eyes, a gravity she could feel but never reach, no matter how far she stretched her hands into the mist. [201] So she had learned to interiorize exteriors, to take the frozen garden and swallow it whole, letting the mist become a thought, the ice become a memory, the silence become a room she could live inside without drowning. [239] It was the only method that kept her sane, and sanity was not something she valued; she valued something stranger, something she had no word for yet. [266] Through the veil of sleep each night, she crossed back, not physically, but as a sensation, a warmth against the cold logic of this world, a brief and tender trespass. [296] In those dreams the garden thawed and the light had edges she could touch, and she understood that light itself was a border, a membrane between what was known and what refused to be known, and that the truest thing she had ever learned was that borders were not walls but invitations. [348] The cultist's pendant was the only thing she'd found that didn't freeze. [360] It hung around her neck on a cord of braided hair, warm against her sternum, and it pulsed with a light that didn't obey the rules of this garden, a light that moved inward, that consumed itself, that knew its own edges and chose to blur them. [407] The cultist who'd worn it had come through the same membrane she had, and had died trying to map the boundaries of light with blood and geometry, believing that if she could just chart the edge, she could hold it open forever. [449] Maren kept the pendant not for its power but for its certainty; it was the one object in this world that refused to be uncertain, and she needed that refusal like an anchor, even as she resented it. [487] But her true motivation ran deeper than any artifact could satisfy, deeper even than homesickness. [502] She wanted to make friends with uncertainty itself, to stop waiting for the light to show her clear edges and instead learn to love the fog, to let the not-knowing become a companion rather than an enemy. [540] The foggy lucidity of her waking state was not confusion but a kind of clarity that only came from accepting that some boundaries were meant to be blurred, that the sharpest sight comes not from light but from learning to see in its absence. [584] So she waited, and she swallowed the garden, and she slept, and each morning she opened her eyes to the mist and chose, again, not to know, and in that choosing she found something that felt almost like peace. [623] That was the friendship. [627] That was the only kind that lasted in a place where light itself was a liar and the truest warmth came from what you carried inside you, not from what you could see. [660]</story>
