Maren had tended the glass orchids for longer than she could remember, which was precisely the point, because memory itself was one of the extinct feelings catalogued in the museum. [30]
Every morning she moved through the opal library above the clouds, her footsteps barely disturbing the silence that had settled like snow over deserted marvels no one visited anymore. [59]
The shelves rose in spirals of pale fire, each one holding jars of extinct feelings—sorrows that had no living carriers, joys that had outlived their owners, wonders that had simply evaporated into the mist between one heartbeat and the next. [100]
She was their caretaker now, gently systematic in her rounds, checking each specimen the way a nurse checks a pulse, her fingers brushing the cold glass petals to confirm they still held their shape. [134]
The wind chime hung near the eastern window, its bamboo tube cracked down the middle, a fracture she had discovered three seasons ago and never repaired. [160]
She believed the crack was essential, that it allowed the chime to sing in two voices at once, and sometimes when the light shifted just right, she would strike it with her fingertip and listen. [195]
Not for the sound itself, but for what the sound carried: the echo of a long-forgotten song that lived in the resonance, a melody that belonged to names no one spoke anymore. [227]
That was her work, her quiet obsession—to preserve the sound of forgotten names before they vanished entirely from the world. [248]
She moved across the museum of extinct feelings, gently systematic, lighting each glass orchid with a small flame so they would glow faintly in the opal dark, then recording what she heard in leather journals no one would read. [287]
The regret she carried was not sharp or sudden but deep and flowering, the kind that blooms slowly and poisons softly—foxglove regret, her mother had called it, the kind that grows in gardens you cannot leave. [324]
She had left her mother's garden long ago, left the world below with its noise and its names, and come here to this library where everything beautiful had been abandoned. [355]
Sometimes she wondered if she was preserving something or merely performing a ritual for an audience of glass and cloud, but the cracked chime answered her doubt each time: its broken note held the name she could not quite reach, and she kept striking it, kept listening, kept writing down what the light revealed. [409]
The orchids needed her the way the dead need a witness, and so she stayed, gently systematic, tending deserted marvels until the clouds themselves forgot what they were carrying. [438]
She trimmed the frost from their stems each dawn and whispered to them in a language she had invented from the echoes of old songs, hoping that somewhere in the resonance a name would answer back. [474]
The opal walls hummed faintly in response, and Maren smiled—a small, foxglove smile, beautiful and poisoned—because the light was shifting again, and the chime was singing. [502]
It sang for the name her mother had whispered on her last evening, a name that had no jar, no shelf, no place in any catalogue, only this cracked bamboo tube and the faith that sound, unlike feeling, could outlast its maker. [544]
So Maren lit another flame, and another, until the opal library blazed with quiet fire, and the deserted marvels glowed like lanterns in a sky that had forgotten it was supposed to rain. [577]
She would tend them forever, she thought, or until the last light left the clouds, whichever came first—gently systematic, as the foxglove requires, beautiful and poisonous and utterly alone. [607]
