The rain had stopped an hour ago, but the library still smelled like wet stone and old clover, as if midsummer itself had leaned against the building and left its scent behind, green and persistent and impossible to shake. [39]
Maren sat at the reading table nearest the window, her father's candle lantern beside her, its mica pane casting a pale fractured light across the open pages she wasn't reading. [69]
Three weeks since the heart attack, and she still carried the lantern everywhere, though she knew it was irrational, the way her father had always known the odds and sat down at the table anyway. [104]
She was her father's daughter in every way that mattered: a compulsive gambler's probability professor daughter, trained to calculate odds but cursed to feel them in her bones like a second heartbeat. [136]
Theoretically rebellious, she'd once told her department chair that all probability was just grief wearing a lab coat, that the numbers were a way of not crying. [163]
In practice, she showed up to every lecture, graded every paper, and never once deviated from the syllabus, because the lucid contradiction of her life was that she understood everything clearly and changed nothing. [197]
After the heart attack, her mother had called and said, "Widen your perspectives, Maren, but do it responsibly," as if perspective were a substance that could be measured and dosed, as if you could prescribe someone a broader worldview the way you'd prescribe a statin. [242]
So Maren had begun to traverse the city not by map but by interpreting the cryptic scrawls left on subway seats—numbers, initials, arrows, half-finished prayers—reading them like a language she'd inherited from her father, who'd always seen patterns where others saw chaos. [287]
Today the scrawl on the A train had read: *Go where the light bends but doesn't break.* [304]
She'd gotten off at the rainwashed library, which sat at the edge of the neighborhood like a sentence no one had finished, its brick walls dark with moisture, its windows fogged with the breath of old air conditioning. [342]
Inside, the shelves rose in tall green rows, and the silence had the texture of clover—soft, three-lobed, melancholy in a way that didn't demand explanation, just presence. [371]
She opened her father's notebook, the one he'd kept during his worst years of gambling, filled not with odds but with contradictions he'd written down like prayers. [398]
*I know the house always wins, and I sit down anyway.* [409]
*I understand probability, and I cannot stop.* [416]
Maren traced the mica pane of the lantern, feeling the cool ridge of it, and thought about how her father had been the most lucid man she'd ever known—fully aware of every contradiction he lived inside, and still choosing to live inside them. [460]
That was the thing she couldn't calculate: the odds of a man who sees clearly and still bets everything, who knows the math and still reaches for the chips. [489]
She closed the notebook and stood, carrying the lantern through the library's back corridor where the rain had seeped in and warped the floorboards into something that looked like a map of nowhere, every plank curving toward a destination that didn't exist. [531]
Outside, the midsummer air was thick and green, and she thought about her mother's words—*widen your perspectives responsibly*—and wondered if that meant she was supposed to find a contradiction she could live with, one that didn't end in a hospital bed, one that didn't end with a phone call at three in the morning. [587]
The lantern's mica pane caught the last of the daylight and threw it in soft broken pieces across the wet sidewalk, and Maren walked on, reading the city like her father had taught her—not for answers, but for the beautiful terrible questions that kept you moving even when you knew better. [639]
She was theoretically rebellious, after all. [645]
Maybe today she'd practice. [649]
The rain began again, soft as clover, and she let it fall on her shoulders like a benediction she hadn't earned but accepted anyway, the way her father had accepted every hand, every loss, every small mercy disguised as a bad beat. [691]
She walked toward the subway, where the next scrawl would be waiting, and she would read it, and she would go. [712]
That was the only responsible way to widen your perspectives: to keep moving, keep reading, keep carrying the lantern even when the light would bend and break. [739]
