<story>Maren walked backwards through the forgotten library, her heels clicking against stone in reverse rhythm, because the girl had just asked the question that made her chest tighten. [28]
"Can I go home?" [32]
"Home is a door, not a place." [39] Maren turned forward this time and set down her cup, its dregs forming patterns she had spent twenty years learning to read. [61] The geometry of tea leaves: that was her method, her only real credential as shard-mirror archivist. [77] She could annotate a broken reflection's story the way scholars annotated manuscripts, tracing meaning through fracture lines. [94]
The forgotten library held ten thousand shards, each one a mirror that had remembered something before it shattered. [112] Maren's job was to catalog them, to annotate each fragment with what it had last reflected. [133] But today she was not cataloging. [142] Today she was teaching. [148]
The girl—Linn, maybe fourteen, exiled from the eastern district for a crime Maren suspected was invented—sat cross-legged on the floor, her knees pulled to her chest like a fortification. [179] "It is reversible exile," Maren had told her on the first day, three weeks ago. [197] "You leave, but you can come back." [206] "When?" [209] "When you are ready." [215] Linn had snorted. [221] "That is not an answer." [228] "It is the only one that matters." [238]

[271] She picked up a shard—small, triangular, still warm from the afternoon sun that had pushed through the gap in the roof.
