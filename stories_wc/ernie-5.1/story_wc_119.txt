The lighthouse keeper had not existed before Tuesday, but by Wednesday the whole island knew her name was Maren, and by Thursday they knew she wrote fortunes. [27]

Her tools were simple: a pot of ink, a stack of rice paper, and a pair of tweezers fine enough to handle the spycraft microdot film specs she'd carried out of the service in her coat lining, pressed flat against her ribs for eleven years. [45] [109]
The specs detailed exactly how to shrink a page of intelligence into a dot smaller than a period—a technique she'd used a dozen times for the Agency, always under orders, never by choice, and always with the quiet certainty that someone would eventually read what she'd hidden. [48] [157]
Now, after last orders, she used them differently. [8] [165]
Maren wrote fortunes the way other people breathed: constantly, automatically, and with a precision that unnerved people who thought they understood her. [22] [187]
"You will find what you lost in a place you never looked." [12] [199]

She didn't plan these things. [5] [243]
The words simply knew their way out of her, and they knew where to land, like birds that had memorized a coast they'd never been taught. [26] [269]
This was her method: not force, but direction—by directing the flow of language, she could move people, objects, and secrets into configurations that served a larger design she could only half-see. [32] [301]
The islanders called it luck. [6] [307]
Maren called it open secrets—the kind that hide in plain sight because everyone assumes they're just fortunes, because nobody suspects that a cookie can be a cipher. [35] [342]
"A door you thought was locked has been open all along." [11] [353]
