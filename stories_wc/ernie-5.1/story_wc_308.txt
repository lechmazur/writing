The harmonic stone skimmer had not moved in three hundred years, and she had made peace with that. [16]

The canyon stretched in every direction, its walls rising hundreds of feet high, composed of bones fused together by mineral deposits and time into something that resembled architecture but was really just the accumulated remains of every creature that had ever lived and died here. [96]
Every surface hummed with the memory of sound. [105]
Every hollow still spoke in voices that belonged to no one living. [116]
Her name, if it had ever been spoken aloud, had been ground to silence long ago, worn away by the same relentless winds that had sculpted the canyon walls into shapes resembling frozen screams. [137]
But she remembered. [140]
That was the burden of being historically reverent—she remembered everything, every voice, every footstep, every pulse of sound that had ever traveled through these towers, and the remembering weighed more than the bone itself, more than the dust, more than the centuries. [170]
She had been young and foolish once, when the pulse was sent. [183]

That certainty had died with the others. [219]
The pulse had been left wandering centuries ago, sent out by the last of her kind to find a way home through the labyrinth of echoes that made up this canyon. [240]
It never returned. [244]
She had spent every year since then trying to learn to navigate without moving, to find the path the pulse had taken by reading the world instead of walking it, by listening instead of searching with her feet. [274]
This had become her devotion, her practice, her prayer. [286]
Today, a flock of birds cut across the canyon in a sweeping arc, their bodies moving in patterns that shifted and reformed like living script written against the pale sky. [306]
She watched them the way she always watched—not with her eyes, but with the harmonic sense in her palms, the ability to feel the vibrations of stone and air and bone as though they were all one instrument playing a single, endless note. [337]
This remained her method of appraisal, the only one she had left after the centuries had stripped away everything else but patience and dust. [359]
