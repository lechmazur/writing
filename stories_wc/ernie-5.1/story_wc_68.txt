<story>Maren did not scream when the dead stopped rising. [9] She simply noted the absence and found it insufficient. [18] The plague had ended three weeks ago, leaving the sunken city of Vethara half-submerged in brackish water and full of people who could not agree on what had killed them. [39] Some said it was divine punishment. [44] Others said it was the old machinery, the pumps that had kept the city dry for centuries finally seizing. [58] Maren said nothing, because she lived in the sewers now, in a dry alcove carved from stone that smelled of brine and rot, and she had decided that competing narratives were the truest form of lying. [87]

She was not a philosopher, not yet, but she kept a journal and she questioned things, which in Vethara amounted to the same crime. [106] Her alcove held three things: a candle stub, a clay pot of clean water, and a seafarer's chronometer cog she had pulled from the sedimentary layers beneath the old harbor district. [130] The cog was brass, green with age, its teeth worn smooth by decades of measuring time at sea. [146] It was useless now, broken from its mechanism, but Maren kept it because it represented a method of knowing that did not depend on who was telling the story. [168] Time was time, whether a priest or a pump-tender measured it. [177]

This was her rebellion, quiet and rational: she would preserve the ancient methods, the ones that worked regardless of narrative. [197] The pump-tenders had maintained Vethara for six hundred years using sedimentary analysis to predict water flow, reading the layers of silt like a text. [222] When the plague came and the pump-tenders died, no one else could read the layers. [236] The city flooded. [239] Now people argued about causes while the water rose. [248]

Maren agonized over this, not loudly, not with tears, but with the grinding patience of someone who understood that grief without action was just another narrative. [277] She spent her days waist-deep in the muck, reading the sedimentary layers the way the old pump-tenders had taught her before they were gone. [302] Clay, then sand, then a dark band of ash from the plague fires. [313] Above that, fresh silt, still settling. [318] Each layer was a record, and the records contradicted each other. [328] The priests said the ash layer proved divine wrath. [334] The engineers said it proved mechanical failure. [340] Maren said it proved that both groups were idiots who had never bothered to look down. [357]

She held the chronometer cog in her hand while she worked, turning it over, feeling its teeth with her thumb. [377] A tool that measured time without needing a story attached. [385] She would write her own account, one based on layers, on evidence, on the stubborn refusal to let the dead define the living. [410]

The candle was burning low. [415] She did not rush. [417] Rushing was for people who believed in narratives. [423]

A boy named Soll came to her alcove that evening, mud-streaked and shaking. [434] He wanted to know what had really happened. [440] Maren looked at him with the flat, abrasive calm she had perfected over months of solitude. [456] "What do you want me to say?" she asked. [462] "That the gods were angry? That the machines failed? That someone is to blame?" [478] Soll opened his mouth. [481] "Save your breath," she said. [487] "The layers do not care about blame." [494]

She showed him the cog. [499] "This measured longitude when Vethara was still above water," she said. [511] "It did not ask who built the ship or who prayed for fair winds. [520] It just worked." [522] She pressed it into his palm. [528] "That is what we lost. [532] Not the pumps. [534] Not the priests. [536] The methods that did not need a story to function." [545]

Soll stared at the cog. [550] "Will you teach me to read the layers?" he asked. [558] Maren almost smiled. [561] Almost. [563] "I will teach you to stop listening to everyone else first," she said. [576] "That is the harder part." [581]

She blew out the candle. [585] The dark was total, and the water dripped its steady, indifferent rhythm against the stone. [602] Maren sat in the silence and let the competing narratives of the world above her wash against the walls without entering. [628] She had her layers. [632] She had her cog. [634] She had her stubborn, rational refusal to let the dead write the future. [652] It was not enough. [655] It was never enough. [657] But it was hers, and she would preserve it the way the pump-tenders had preserved this city: one layer at a time, without apology. [688]</story>
