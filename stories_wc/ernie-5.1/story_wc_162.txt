<story>The terrace drifted like a thought no one finished. [9]
Maren sat cross-legged on the prism floor, her fingers tracing the grain of a weathered chair someone had left behind. [29]
She was featherly determined — that was what her mother used to say, back when her mother still said things. [48]
Back when Maren was the lost child of the greatest corn maze architect in three counties, back when getting lost was the point. [71]
Her father had built mazes that breathed, corridors that shifted with the wind, walls of corn that whispered directions if you listened long enough. [95]
But that was before the butterfly effect. [102]
Before a single decision — a wrong turn, a missed flight, a logbook left open on a table — had rearranged everything into this drifting prism terrace, a place that existed in the aftermath of small causes becoming enormous consequences. [141]
The terrace floated above nothing, its surface made of light bent into walkable planes, and Maren had been here long enough to stop being afraid of the drop. [169]
What she couldn't stop doing was touching the furniture. [178]
The chairs, the tables, the benches — all made of wood that shouldn't exist in a place like this. [196]
She analyzed the grain the way her father had taught her to analyze a maze: not with her eyes but with her fingertips, reading the direction of the fibers like a language she was only beginning to speak. [234]
The grain told her which way the wood wanted to split, which way it remembered growing, which way it still wanted to go even now, dead and shaped into something useful. [265]
It was the same intuition she needed for the pilot logbook. [276]
She'd found it on the third day, wedged between two prism panels, its leather cover cracked and salt-stained. [294]

Code 7-7 meant clear air with turbulence below. [328]
Code 3-1 meant fog so thick you couldn't trust your instruments. [339]
But the last page held a code she'd never seen: 0-0. [350]
Zero visibility, zero altitude, zero everything. [356]
It didn't make sense as a weather report. [364]
It made sense as something else. [370]

She'd come here to discover why some thresholds felt sacred, why standing at the edge of a maze corridor felt different from standing in the middle of one, why her father had always said the most important part of any maze was the entrance. [463]

The space between your hand and the wood. [476]
The space between who you were and who the grain said you could become. [491]

It was a threshold. [535]
Her father hadn't been lost. [540]
He'd been standing at the edge, reading the grain, writing the codes, building mazes that were really just exercises in necessary distance — the sacred space between touching something and letting it be. [573]
