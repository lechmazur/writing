<story>The stars were going out one by one, and Maren was counting them the way she counted everything—by feel, by pressure, by the weight of ink on paper. [29]
She stood in the topaz workshop with its walls of amber glass that caught the last light and held it like a breath, and the air inside smelled of linseed oil and ozone and something older, something that had no name in any language she currently wore. [67]
Between vanishing stars, the sky turned the color of a bruise healing, violet and gold and the particular darkness that exists only in the gap between one light dying and the next one remembering to exist. [108]
Maren worked the letterpress with hands that did not belong to her. [121]
They belonged to everyone she had ever been, or wished to be, a rotating cast of selves she cycled through like ink colors, each one precise, each one temporary. [155]

This was her habit: borrowing personalities like type fonts, swapping them out mid-sentence until even she forgot which voice was hers, which laugh was real, which silence. [188]
Tonight she was someone sharp and angular, a woman who understood angles and refused to round them, who spoke in declarative statements and never asked questions she already knew the answers to. [226]
The halo fragment sat on the press bed where type should have been, a shard of iridescent bone no larger than a thumbnail, humming at a frequency that made her teeth ache and her vision swim at the edges. [267]

She had found it three nights ago in the rubble of a collapsed cathedral, half-buried in dust and the ghost of chant, and it had changed the way she felt other people's feelings—not as empathy, exactly, but as transposed empathy, where their emotion arrived in her chest already translated into her own grammar, their sorrow becoming her sorrow but rewritten in a syntax only she could parse. [324]
A stranger's grief became her grief, but restructured, re-set in her own typographic logic, every curve of sorrow mapped to a letter she could print, every scream of joy kerned to fit. [365]

This was what she sought: the mathematics of emotion, the proof that love and loss could be reduced to equations as elegant as the spacing between letters, that every human ache had a solution waiting in the proper arrangement of characters. [406]
She had spent eleven years chasing this, abandoning cities and names and the slow erosion of anyone who tried to love a woman who kept becoming someone else. [440]
The halo fragment was the closest she had ever come. [452]

She rolled it under the press and pulled the lever, and the sound it made was not a sound but a disorientation—a deliberate ambiguity that scrambled her sense of self so completely that she could not tell if she was the one pressing or the one being pressed, the printer or the page. [499]
The topaz walls blurred into liquid amber. [509]
The stars above vanished entirely, swallowed by the interval between their deaths, and in that space between vanishing stars, Maren felt everything and nothing at once, a lucid turbulence where every borrowed personality collided and canceled and resolved into a single unbearable clarity that burned behind her eyes like a letter pressed too deep. [564]

She understood, in that moment, that the mathematics of emotion was not a proof but a process—an endless press, an endless translation, an endless borrowing of selves to get closer to a truth that receded with every impression. [607]
The fragment had shown her this: that empathy was not a bridge but a mirror, and every reflection was already a distortion, and the only honest thing she could do was keep printing, keep translating, keep becoming. [653]

She wiped her hands on her apron and began setting type again, borrowing someone else's patience, someone else's steadiness, because that was what she did, that was the only constant in a life made of variables. [692]
She became whoever she needed to be in order to keep working, to keep pressing the fragment into language, to keep translating the untranslatable into letters that anyone could read and no one could quite decode, because clarity was not the same as truth, and she had learned that difference the hard way, in every city she had left behind. [748]

The last star winked out, and the workshop went dark except for the faint iridescent glow of the fragment on the press bed, pulsing like a second heartbeat. [777]
Maren did not look up. [781]
She was already someone else. [786]</story>
