<story>Maren had always been a poor liar, which in Veridia was a death sentence. [17] The greenhouse city stretched in every direction, its glass panels yellowed by the toxic sky above, and every resident knew that candor in small moments was the only currency that didn't depreciate. [42] She was a gilded-leaf calligrapher, her fingers permanently stained gold from pressing leaf-thin sheets onto parchment, and she had spent thirty years writing other people's honest words onto leaves that would never rot. [68]

After the oasis dried, the city began to forget. [76] Not memories exactly, but the conversations themselves, the ones spoken aloud between people, started evaporating like mist off hot glass. [97] You would say something true and important, and by the time you turned around, the words were gone, absorbed into the toxic atmosphere above. [120] Maren had watched it happen to her neighbor, to her sister, to herself. [138]

That was why she needed to find where lost conversations go. [148]

The map came to her by accident. [152] She had been lifting floorboards to check the root systems of her callinga lilies when she found it, shredded into dozens of pieces and stuffed into the dark space beneath. [175] Someone had torn it apart deliberately, but not well enough. [189] She carried every fragment to her worktable and began to piece them together by hand, her gilded fingers trembling. [213]

It took three days. [217] The map depicted the old postal exchange at the city's center, a building most people had forgotten existed. [235] And there, pressed into the corner of the largest fragment, was a zinc-etched postal plate fragment, its surface corroded but still legible. [259] It read: ALL WORDS SPOKEN WITH TRUTH ARE STORED HERE. [277]

Maren picked up the fragment and held it close. [287] She did not wilt. [290] That meant it was telling the truth, or close enough. [305]

She reread the map carefully, tracing each torn edge with her gold-stained fingertip. [325] The path required passing through three checkpoints, each marked with a question. [341] Not riddles, but honest questions, the kind people avoid answering. [357] The first asked: What have you never said to the person you love most? [376] The second: What do you actually fear, not what you claim to fear? [401] The third: Why are you really doing this? [422]

Maren sat with the map for a long time. [431] Her leaves were beginning to curl at the edges, a sign of dishonesty creeping in, but she hadn't lied yet. [454] She was just avoiding. [460]

She started small. [464] "I'm afraid the city will die and no one will remember us." [483] Her leaves straightened slightly. [491] "I never told my sister I forgave her for leaving." [512] The curl retreated further. [520] "I'm not doing this for Veridia. I'm doing it because I miss my mother's voice and the silence is killing me." [553]

She wilted then, visibly, her skin going pale and papery like the leaves she worked with. [578] But the map glowed faintly where her tears had fallen on it, and a new path appeared, leading deeper into the postal exchange. [604]

She found them there, all the lost conversations, stored in glass bottles on endless shelves, each one labeled with a name. [627] Her mother's voice was in a bottle near the back, preserved perfectly. [646] "I love you," it said. [653] "I always did, even when I was wrong." [667]

Maren held the zinc-etched fragment against her chest and did not wilt. [688] The toxic sky pressed down on the glass above, but inside the postal exchange, every small honest word she had ever spoken was waiting for her, and she began to write them all down again, one gilded leaf at a time. [727]

The city outside could forget. [735] She would not. [739]</story>
