Maren had watched stars from the earth her entire life, never climbing high enough to touch them, only crouching low enough to catch what they left behind. [27]

She had come here as memories resurfaced, the way they always did when the autumn equinox pressed the sky close to the horizon and the stars seemed low enough to whisper to, close enough to hear. [132]
Her grandmother had taught her gem polishing in this very grotto, showing her how to turn rough glass into something that could hold light and return it gently, without burning anything in the process. [166]
The method required patience, not force, and Maren had learned that patience was the only language the stars understood from the ground, the only offering they would accept from someone who could not fly. [204]
She weighed the sea glass in her palm, feeling its coolness, its small heaviness that meant nothing in pounds but everything in years, in every tide that had smoothed its edges. [237]
Each piece she polished was an offering, a way to share light without using fire, because fire consumed and Maren had watched enough things burn to know that some light should only be given, never taken, never hoarded. [278]
The grotto wound around her in serpentine curves, and the mist thickened as she worked, until she could not tell where the cave ended and her memories began, where the stone stopped and the past started. [316]
She remembered her grandmother's hands, rough and sure, pressing a raw stone into hers and saying, "Stillness is not empty, child. [343]
It is a pearl of stillness, and it holds everything the noise cannot." [361]
Maren had not understood then, standing in this same grotto with sand in her shoes and the taste of salt on her lips. [388]
Now, with her own hands shaping glass into something that could catch a star's reflection and hold it, she understood completely. [418]
The sea glass was almost ready, its surface smooth as a held breath, its edges so gentle they would never cut. [444]
She held it up and the mist parted just enough for a single beam of starlight to fall through the grotto's mouth and strike the glass with perfect aim. [480]
The green crescent blazed reflectively vibrant, throwing soft emerald light across the wet stone walls, and for a moment the grotto was not dark at all but alive with borrowed radiance. [517]
This was her gift: not fire, not flame, but borrowed light, returned gently to anyone who wandered close enough to find it. [545]
She placed the finished piece on the stone shelf where dozens of others sat, each one a small lantern that needed no wick, no fuel, no flame. [579]
Visitors sometimes found their way here, lost hikers or wandering souls drawn by something they could not name, and they would stand in the mist and see the grotto glowing with a light that had no source they could identify. [621]

Maren watched them from the shadows, content in her earthbound way, knowing she would never reach the stars but could still bring their light down to those who needed it. [700]
The mist closed behind the last visitor, and Maren picked up another piece of sea glass, already worn smooth by the ocean's long patience, and began to polish it with the same careful hands her grandmother had taught her. [750]
