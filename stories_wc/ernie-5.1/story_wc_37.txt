Maren had forgotten her own name three times before what should have been sunset, but she remembered the songs. [19]
In the mirror world, the alchemical orchard greenhouse sat shrouded in endless night, and memory was the only currency that held any weight. [42]
She was the scattered memory keeper of this place, her mind a sieve that lost and found things in equal, maddening measure. [64]
The greenhouse stretched around her in dark, humid rows of fruit trees that bore no fruit—only light, trapped in glass orbs hanging from branches like frozen moons. [92]
Through the mica window, the outside world was invisible, but Maren could feel it pressing against the thin translucent sheets, hungry and cold. [115]
The community had come to her for years, drawn by the one thing she could still do: she knits communities together through forgotten songs. [139]
They gathered in the greenhouse at what should have been dawn, though here dawn never came, and the darkness was a living thing that breathed against the glass. [167]
Old Thessa came with her trembling hands, young Corin with his silence, and the twins who shared one name because their parents could not decide. [192]
Maren would stand before them, hands trembling worse than theirs, and sing melodies she barely recognized—songs from the old realm, the one that was dying. [218]
Each note was a thread, and together they wove dawns that existed only in the space between breathing and remembering. [238]
The woven dawns were not light exactly, but a warmth that settled into bones, a promise that the night was not permanent. [260]
Tonight, the last of the community had come, and Maren could feel the weight of their need pressing against her scattered thoughts. [282]
They sat on the mossy ground, wrapped in blankets stitched from memories of better seasons, waiting for Maren to swaddle them in sound. [305]
That was the word she used, though she could not remember where she had learned it: to swaddle was to hold so tightly that the thing inside could finally let go. [336]
Maren opened her mouth, and the first song came out broken, a fragment of something about rivers and fire and a woman who danced at the edge of the world. [366]
The greenhouse hummed in response, a low vibration that traveled through the roots of the dead trees and into the bones of everyone present. [390]
The glass orbs began to glow, not with light but with memory—faces, places, the taste of fruit that no longer existed anywhere but here. [415]

They always held, because the songs were not hers—they belonged to everyone who had ever gathered in this place and needed to be reminded that they were not alone. [529]
