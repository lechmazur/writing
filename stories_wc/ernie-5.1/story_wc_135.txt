Maren, the ocean-spray archivist, stood at the edge of the main pier with her boots in a puddle she hadn't noticed. [38]

The nets were different. [77]
Even the gulls seemed to circle in unfamiliar patterns, as though the sky itself had shifted while she was away. [97]
Her work was not glamorous, and she had never claimed it was. [110]

People called her obsessed, but Maren understood herself differently. [142]
She was reactively visionary — not the kind who dreamed of futures, but the kind who, when confronted with a gap, suddenly saw the shape of what had been there. [164]
It happened again now, standing in the wet harbor light with the smell of brine and wet stone filling her lungs. [184]

It meant nothing to him. [205]
To Maren, it meant everything. [210]

All gone. [234]
But she could navigate those absences. [240]
That was what she did, and it was the only thing she had ever been good at. [255]
Over the following weeks, Maren returned to the harbor at staggered intervals, never on the same day twice, never at the same hour. [273]
She let the rhythm of her visits follow the rhythm of the tides. [287]
Each visit she added one more fragment to her collection: a rusted bolt, a shard of painted wood, a coil of rope with the knot still tight. [309]
She didn't know what she was building yet, but she knew it mattered. [323]
The valve sat at the center of her worktable, and every time she looked at it, she saw not what it was but what it had been. [346]

The trumpet was gone. [372]
The herald was gone. [376]
The fleet had scattered. [382]
