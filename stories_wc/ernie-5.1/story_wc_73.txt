Maren climbed the infinite staircase the way she did everything: with the certainty that she would fail and the stubborn refusal to stop. [24] She was a grim optimist by trade and by temperament, a woman who measured hope in negatives and still kept walking. [46] The stairs had no top, no bottom, no railing, and no end, and she had long since stopped finding this cruel. [68] It was simply the shape of her life now, boundless confinement stretching in every direction she chose not to look. [90]

In her coat pocket, a pair of dice clinked against each other with every step. [101] One was smooth, ivory, perfect. [107] The other was chipped along one edge, a flaw she had earned the night she lost everything. [125] She never used the perfect die. [131] The chipped one was her compass, her variable, the imperfection she trusted more than any whole thing. [149]

She was not climbing to arrive anywhere. [155] She was climbing to prove perpetual motion, to demonstrate that a system could sustain itself forever without external input, that energy need not decay, that motion could be eternal if the geometry was right. [185] This was her obsession, her religion, her way of refusing the stillness that had taken everything from her. [205] The proofs lived in her head, reconstructed from memory each time she climbed, derived through mathematical rigor from first principles. [231] She had written them down once in notebooks she no longer had, but the logic was carved into her mind like the staircase was carved into nothing. [259] Each step was a theorem. [265] Each landing was a lemma. [270] The infinite ascent was her proof, and she was both the mathematician and the equation. [290]

Her motivation was not abstract, though she would never have admitted it aloud. [294] She wanted to capture the texture of a missed opportunity, the specific grain of a door she should have walked through years ago. [318] Not the door itself, but the feeling of it, the weight of the handle, the light that would have fallen differently if she had turned it. [344] She wanted to hold that texture in her hands the way one holds a chipped die, knowing it is flawed and precious because of it. [370]

So she climbed with balanced daring, never rushing, never hesitating, throwing the dice at each landing to decide her next move. [396] The staircase did not care about her proofs, but she cared enough for both of them, and that was the only engine perpetual motion required: a system that fed on its own refusal to stop. [428]

The rupture came not in the math but in her, and it arrived without warning. [434] On a landing that felt no different from any other, she threw the chipped die and it landed on its edge, balancing impossibly, and the staircase shuddered. [460] Not physically, but in the logic of her mind, a crack opened in the proof, and through that crack she saw it—the texture, the grain, the light. [486] It was not a door. [490] It was the space between steps, the motion itself, the thing she had been trying to prove could last forever. [510] She caught it in her hands like the chipped die, imperfect and whole, and she kept climbing. [530]

The staircase stretched on, boundless and confining, and Maren climbed it with her grim optimism, her mathematical proofs, her chipped die, her balanced daring, her rupture, her texture—all of it feeding the perpetual motion of a woman who refused to stop. [578] She was the engine. [583] She was the proof. [586] She kept walking. [590] The dice clinked in her pocket, and the staircase went on forever, and that was exactly the point. [608]
