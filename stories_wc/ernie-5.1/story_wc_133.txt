Maren hadn't slept in six days, which meant she'd painted the same stretch of coastline forty-seven times, each canvas a little more unhinged than the last, and she was starting to suspect the island was painting her back. [38]
The tidal island of Sker had survived the water wars only by becoming something else entirely—a place where the sea remembered where it had been and kept returning to argue, reshaping the shoreline with every storm like a sculptor who couldn't stop touching the clay. [82]
She stood at the edge of her studio, a collapsed lighthouse she'd claimed three years ago when the last refugees fled and the silence became a kind of permission, and watched the rain come in long silver threads across the flats. [115]
This was her method, such as it was: follow the rain trails. [125]
Where the water pooled and diverted, it revealed the island's hidden geology, the bones of a world that had drowned and resurfaced and drowned again, and Maren believed—with the wry earnestness of someone who had stopped pretending to be sane—that the land was trying to tell her something if she could only learn to listen with her eyes. [172]
Maren was, by her own admission, a woman threshing meaning from experience like grain from chaff, except the grain kept turning to dust in her hands, and she had begun to wonder if the dust was the point. [199]
Insomnia had taken her sleep but left her something worse: a clarity so sharp it felt like a wound that wouldn't close. [222]
Tonight the rain pulled her south, past the rusted hulls of war-barges half-swallowed by mud, their gun turrets still pointing at a horizon that no longer held an enemy, toward a place she'd never walked. [261]
The trail led to a depression in the rock where seawater had collected into a shallow pool, and there, half-buried in the silt, something caught the last of the light—an amber brooch, oval, the size of her thumb, its surface clouded with age but still holding that deep honey glow that meant it had once been part of something living, something warm, something that had worn it close to a heartbeat. [314]
She picked it up and felt the weight of it—not just physical, but the weight of every hand that had held it before, every story it had absorbed like a small golden sponge. [345]
A voice behind her made her turn. An old woman stood there, wrapped in oilskin, her face a map of the same erosion that had shaped the island, and her eyes held the particular calm of someone who had made peace with loss long ago. [381]
You found it, the woman said, not as a question. [392]
I follow the rain, Maren replied, because that was the truest thing she knew how to say. [409]
That's why you found it. The rain knows where things end up. It carries everything downhill, eventually, to the lowest place where it can rest. [440]
The woman extended her hand, and Maren understood—this was an exchange, not of objects but of recognition, the kind that happens between two people who have both been broken open by the same weather. [474]
She placed the brooch in the woman's palm, and the woman pressed something into Maren's hand in return: a small, smooth stone, warm despite the cold, as if it had been holding heat for a very long time. [508]
What is this? Maren asked, her voice rougher than she intended. [520]
Proof that you were here. That you saw it. That you didn't look away. [538]
The woman turned and walked back the way she'd come, disappearing into the rain as if she'd never been there at all, and Maren stood in the depression, holding the stone, and felt something shift inside her—not sleep, not peace, but the quiet certainty that she had, for once, threshed something real from the chaff, something that would not turn to dust. [589]
She walked back to the lighthouse, and for the first time in six days, she did not paint. [608]
She sat instead, and watched the rain, and let it mean what it meant. [622]
