<story>The storms came every third day now, shaking the hanging orchard until the apple trees groaned against their iron cables like prisoners testing bars. [24]Maren knelt in the mud between the rows, her fingers working the roots of a young truffle oak with the precision of a surgeon, and she tried not to feel what the woman behind her was feeling. [61]It didn't work. [64]After the empathy virus, nothing worked the way it used to. [75]You felt everything—everyone's grief, everyone's hunger, everyone's quiet desperate hope—and it crushed you slowly from the inside, like water filling a boot. [99]Maren was rational. [102]That was the only thing that kept her alive. [111]She foraged truffles the way her grandmother had taught her, reading soil pH and root density, trusting data over instinct, ignoring the emotional noise that rolled through her like a tide she could not outswim. [146]But the woman behind her—Luce, newly arrived from the lowlands with blood under her nails and terror in her chest—was afraid, and Maren could feel it like a hand closing around her own throat. [182]"You're scared," Maren said, because she couldn't help it. [191]The involuntary truthfulness was the virus's cruelest gift. [199]You couldn't lie. [202]You couldn't even soften the edges of what you felt or fashion a kindness from the raw material of someone else's pain. [224]Luce flinched as though struck. [229]"I know," she whispered, and the words tasted like ash. [239]Maren reached into her coat and pulled out the antique glass eye. [251]Its surface was clouded with age, the iris a deep amber that caught the gray light and held it, and it was warm in her palm in a way that glass should not be. [285]This was what she had smuggled out of the quarantine zone—not drugs, not weapons, but this single relic from a world before the virus, before empathy was weaponized and turned into a tool of control. [321]The discredited textbooks said it could occlude the signal. [330]Not all of it—never all of it—but enough to build a pocket of silence where a person could breathe without drowning in someone else's sorrow. [357]The textbooks were dismissed by the new medical establishment as dangerous nonsense, published before the collapse and full of rumored cures and half-baked theories they had buried. [384]But Maren had read them all, cover to cover, in the long nights when the storms wouldn't stop and the orchard swayed like a chandelier in an earthquake. [412]She had found the method in a water-damaged chapter: hold the eye against the sternum, press the glass to the bone, and let the old frequencies do what modern science had forgotten how to do. [447]Occlude. [448]Block the signal. [451]Create a room where truth could exist without pain. [460]"Give me your hand," Maren said. [466]Luce hesitated, but the fear was already too much, and Maren could feel it—could feel her own fear too, reflected back, a mirror of mirrors stretching into infinity. [495]Maren placed the glass eye in Luce's palm and pressed it gently against her chest, right over the sternum, and the orchard went quiet. [519]Not silent—the wind still moved through the branches, the rain still fell—but the empathy receded, pulled back like a tide, leaving Luce gasping on the shore of her own small, private self. [553]"Oh," Luce breathed, and for the first time in months, the word was just a word. [569]"It's not permanent," Maren said, because honesty was the only currency she had left. [583]"But it's enough." [586]Luce looked at her with eyes that were finally, blessedly, just her own. [599]The storm rolled on. [603]The orchard held. [606]And in the ravine battered by wind and rain, two women sat together in the fractured grace of being, for one moment, alone. [629]</story>
