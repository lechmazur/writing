<story>The cartomancer sat cross-legged in the midnight glade, the clouds below her so thick they looked like a floor of hammered silver, and she was willingly perplexed by the silence. [28] No wind stirred here. [31] No birds sang. [33] The air tasted of ozone and something older, something that reminded her of the space between heartbeats, of all the moments she had let slip through her fingers like water. [61] She had climbed here after the birth, after the baby's first cry had torn through the house like a seam splitting, and she had not returned. [85] That had been three days ago. [90] Three days since she had held her daughter and felt the terrible weight of every hour she had wasted before that moment. [108]

Her name was Maren, and she read futures in onyx ink, the dark liquid pooling in the creases of her palms like something alive and waiting to be born. [132] She had read ten thousand cards in her life, and not once had they shown her this: a glade above the clouds, a cracked saucer, and the suffocating beauty of a truth she could not outrun. [159] But tonight she was not reading. [163] Tonight she was repenting, and the clouds were her confessional, vast and indifferent and chokingly beautiful in their luminous dark. [181]

Before her sat the dolls' tea set, a thing she had carried up from the nursery where it had lived on the highest shelf, untouched for years, its porcelain skin gone yellow with neglect. [206] The saucer was cracked—a single fracture running from rim to rim like a river on a map of nowhere, and Maren had stared at that crack for so long it had begun to stare back. [232] She had been searching for where lost time accumulates, and the answer had brought her here, to this glade suspended between earth and heaven, where the stars were close enough to bruise. [261]

The cartomancer reached forward and began to unfasten the cloth wound around the tea set, her fingers moving with the practiced slowness of ritual, each knot a small surrender. [285] The cloth was the birthday tablecloth, white and embroidered with small stars, the one her mother had used every year since Maren was born, washing it carefully, folding it away, bringing it out again the next year as though time itself could be laundered and reused. [318] The family custom was simple: on each birthday, everyone at the table would press their hand into wet ink and trace it on the cloth, so that by the time Maren was twenty-three, the tablecloth was a palimpsest of hands, every person who had ever loved her pressed into the fabric like a fossil record of touch, layers upon layers of palms and fingers and the ghosts of embraces she could still feel if she closed her eyes. [370]

Maren unfastened the last knot and laid the cloth flat on the cloud-stone, its surface cool and impossibly smooth beneath her fingers. [390] She uncapped her onyx ink and traced her own hand again, pressing it beside all the others, and the crack in the saucer caught the moonlight and threw a thin line of silver across her palm like a scar she had always carried but never named. [424] She understood then what the tea set had been trying to tell her across all those silent years on the shelf: the lost time wasn't gone. [445] It was pressed into everything she touched, accumulating in the spaces between fingers, in the creases of cloth, in the fractures of porcelain, in the pause before a mother reaches for her child. [475]

She was not here to find where time went. [481] She was here to confess that she had been the one holding it all along, hoarding it like ink in a bottle, afraid to spend it, afraid that spending it meant admitting it could run out. [511] And the clouds, in their choking beauty, were the only witnesses vast enough to hold her guilt without breaking. [533]

Maren pressed her inked hand to the cloth one final time, and the glade held its breath around her, the stars leaning closer as if to hear. [556] Somewhere below, her daughter slept without knowing that her mother had climbed to the edge of the world to set down what she could no longer carry. [583] The ink would dry. [587] The hands would remain. [589] And the crack in the saucer would stay, because some things are not meant to be mended—only witnessed. [604]</story>
