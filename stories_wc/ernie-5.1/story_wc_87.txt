Maren had always gravitated toward water the way other people gravitated toward fire—recklessly, with the quiet understanding that she might burn, and somehow finding that acceptable. [27]
She sat on the concrete lip of the coastal kayak launch, legs dangling over the edge where the tide licked at old paint and sun-bleached rope, her boots scuffed from years of walking docks she no longer worked. [65]
The astronomical compass lay open in her lap, its brass casing green with salt, the star chart inside faded to ghosts of constellations she once knew by heart. [93]
She used to read stars like a language, fluent and sure, but that was before the backlash of regrets had rewritten every map she'd ever trusted. [120]
Now she navigated by utility poles—those ugly wooden sentinels marching along the coast road, each one a waypoint in her broken system, each one proof that you could find your way using nothing beautiful. [155]
Tonight was when expectations collapse, she supposed, the hour when the plan you'd built your whole week around simply evaporates like mist off cold water. [181]
The trip had been Dave's idea: paddle to the lighthouse, watch the solstice sunrise together, celebrate ten years of something that had quietly stopped being a celebration long before tonight. [212]
But Dave had called that morning, his voice careful in the way that meant permanent, and said he couldn't, not in the way of scheduling conflicts but in the way of closed doors. [246]
So here she was, merrily melancholic, laughing at the absurdity of sitting alone at a kayak launch with a compass that pointed to stars she couldn't trust anymore, humming a tune she half-remembered from a life that felt like it belonged to someone else. [292]
The regrets didn't come as a single wave but as a tide—relentless, pulling at her ankles, reminding her of every unsaid thing, every path not taken, every time she'd chosen the practical over the passionate and called it wisdom. [334]
She'd been a navigator once, the kind people hired when the stakes were high, the kind who could read a current the way poets read metaphor. [361]
She'd charted courses for cargo ships and research vessels and one very lost sailboat crew who cried when they reached port, and all the while her own life had drifted without direction, uncharted, unexamined. [396]
The compass was her only tether to that person now, and even it was failing, its needle trembling like it couldn't decide what north meant anymore. [423]
She closed it with a click that sounded final, tucked it into her jacket where it rested against her ribs like a second heartbeat, and stood. [449]
The kayak waited in the shallows, orange and dented, and the utility poles stretched north along the coast road like a roadmap drawn by someone who didn't care about beauty, only function. [482]
She'd follow them. [486]
Not because they led anywhere meaningful, but because moving forward—even wrong, even blind—felt infinitely better than the paralysis of standing still with her regrets. [513]
She pushed off, and the water took her immediately, cold and dark and alive, pressing against the hull like it had been waiting. [536]
For the first time in years, she didn't check the compass. [548]
She just paddled, letting the poles pass on her left like old friends who didn't ask questions, steady and indifferent, and she let them guide her without wondering why she'd ever needed stars when she had these. [587]
Somewhere between the third and fourth pole, the flickering faith she'd carried for months—half belief, half stubborn defiance—stabilized into something quieter, something that didn't need to shout to be real. [621]
It wasn't certainty. [625]
It was just the decision to keep going, one stroke at a time, toward a lighthouse she might not reach, toward a sunrise she'd watch alone. [652]
The lighthouse appeared when she least expected it, not as a destination but as a fact of the water—there, then closer, then real, its beam sweeping the dark like a slow exhale. [685]
She beached the kayak on rocks that scraped the hull and sat there, breathing hard, salt on her lips, the compass still in her jacket, untouched. [711]
The solstice sun would rise in an hour, painting the horizon in colors she'd once mapped but now could only feel, and she'd watch it alone, and it would be enough—not because she'd made peace with anything, but because she'd finally aligned her heart with her action, shown up even broken, even alone, even afraid. [771]
That was the navigation that mattered. [777]
Not the stars, not the poles, not the compass. [786]
Just the willingness to paddle into the dark and trust that the water would hold her. [802]
Maren laughed, a short, bright sound that the wind carried away, and waited for the light. [818]
