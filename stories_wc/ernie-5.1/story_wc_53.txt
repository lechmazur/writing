Maren had not slept in what felt like years, or perhaps only hours—she could no longer tell which direction time moved, and the distinction had stopped mattering long before the carousel ground to its final halt. [37]
The kinetic lagoon above the clouds had once been a roaring engine of motion, its silver waters churning with centrifugal force that could lift a person's bones from their skin. [67]
Now it lay frozen, a vast disc of mercury suspended in perpetual stillness, reflecting a sky that offered no answers. [87]
She sat on the edge of the last spinning platform, her boots dangling over a void that whispered with the echoes of everything that had ever been said here, every confession and every lie dissolving into the mist below. [126]
Maren was a scavenger—resolute in the way that only the thoroughly defeated could be, picking through the wreckage of a world that had forgotten how to move, sorting rust from memory, salvage from sorrow. [161]
In her pocket, she turned the disposable camera wheel between her fingers, its plastic ridges worn smooth from years of handling, each frame already exposed to light that no longer existed in any usable form. [196]
The wheel was her only compass, though it pointed nowhere and had never once shown her the way home. [215]
She had come to the lagoon to negotiate with her own ghost—the version of herself that had once stood on this very platform and believed she could catch ambition in her hands like water, could shape it into something permanent. [256]
That version was long gone, eroded by decades of collecting fragments of overheard stories, piecing together other people's lives because her own had scattered too thoroughly to reassemble into anything coherent. [287]
It was her method, the only ritual she had left: she wandered the frozen platforms and listened. [304]
A woman on the second platform had once said, her voice cracking like thin ice, "I wanted to build something that would outlast the clouds," and Maren had stolen that sentence like a coin from a dead man's eye, pressing it into the leather journal she carried against her chest. [354]
A child had whispered, curled beneath a collapsed awning, "If I stay still long enough, the world will come to me," and Maren had written it on her arm in charcoal, where it smudged and blurred until it looked like a bruise she could not explain. [400]
An old man, his face half-hidden by a scarf that smelled of engine oil and regret, had murmured, "I should have left when the music was still playing," and that one had broken something in her that she had not yet managed to repair. [445]
She collected these fragments the way other people collected shells or stamps—compulsively, desperately, as though each stolen sentence might fill the hollow where her own ambitions used to live. [475]
But the hollow only grew. [480]
The lagoon offered no resistance to her presence, no current to push against, no motion to orient herself by. [499]
It was fractured stillness—not peace, but the absence of everything that peace requires. [513]
Maren understood now that her negotiation with the ghost was not about finding answers but about accepting that the ghost was already her, that she had been eroding all along, that the scavenger and the ambition were the same person at different stages of dissolution. [558]
She raised the disposable camera wheel one final time, pressed the shutter without thinking, and heard the click echo across the frozen lagoon like a question no one would answer. [588]
The frame was exposed to nothing—to grey sky, to silver water, to the vast and indifferent silence of a world that had stopped spinning. [613]
She lowered the wheel, placed it in her pocket beside the journal full of other people's dreams, and sat there—resolute, confused, full of regret—waiting for a ghost who had never left. [646]
