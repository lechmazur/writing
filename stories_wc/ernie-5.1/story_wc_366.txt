<story>The lamp had gone out at 3:47 a.m., and Maren didn't rush to fix it. [16] She sat in the haunted lantern room with her back against the cold iron and let the dark settle over her like water filling a hull. [35] Maren was the supply skipper for Harlan Point Lighthouse, a woman of few words who knew every crack in the stairs and every gull's name. [24] She had been coming up here for eleven years, delivering kerosene and canned goods and silence in equal measure, and she had never once explained why the lantern room felt less empty than any room on land. [36] Perhaps it was because the room held the shape of every person who had ever stood watch inside it, their ghosts folded into the iron like creases in an old letter. [28] But tonight was different. [4] Tonight, there were letters. [4] They had started appearing three weeks ago, tucked into the supply crates she delivered—anonymous, unsigned, written on paper that smelled like salt and someone else's kitchen. [13] Maren had read each one in the lantern room by the last glow of the lamp, and each time she had felt something crack open in her chest, something warm and aching, like light trying to push through fog. [36] She was not a woman who spoke easily, but she was warmly observant—she noticed the way the fog curled against the glass like a living thing, the way the beam had once cut a golden road across the water, the way a single stone on the sill could mean more than a paragraph ever could. [44] She had started answering. [4] Not with words—she didn't trust words enough for this—but with stones. [13] There was a method to it, a way of stacking stones on the windowsill that the old keepers had used before her, a language of balance and weight passed down through generations of lonely watchers. [33] One stone meant: I hear you. [6] Two stones meant: You are not alone. [6] Three stones meant: Stay. [4] She had left three stones on the sill the night the lamp first flickered, and by morning, someone had added a fourth—a smooth, green piece of sea glass, worn to softness by years of the ocean's patient hands. [40] Maren had held it up to the dark and felt her throat tighten. [13] This was what she wanted to protect—not the lighthouse, not the lamp, but the fragile, glowing thing that had grown between strangers who would never meet, a chain of trust built from anonymous words and stacked stones. [38] The lamp had died tonight, and the room was truly dark, but Maren could feel the presence of everyone who had ever left a letter here, every person who had needed to say something to no one in particular. [36] She placed the sea glass in her pocket, stood, and began to stack stones on the sill—one, two, three, four—and this time she added a fifth, because the ache in her chest had become something luminous, something she wanted to hold, to bottle, to keep safe. [44] The dark didn't scare her anymore. [5] She understood now that safety wasn't something you found—it was something you built, stone by stone, letter by letter, in the haunted spaces between people.
