<story>The oracle woke on the third day after the fever broke, and the mountain weather station hummed with a silence that felt borrowed from somewhere else. [26]

She was fault-tolerant by nature—had swallowed truths that would have shattered lesser seers, held them in her chest like river stones, and kept walking without stumbling. [53]

But this one, the one she'd swallowed during the delirium when her temperature spiked past the point of coherence, had a weight she couldn't locate anywhere in her body. [84]

She needed to find it before the next storm. [93]

The station sat at the spine of the ridge, where the wind changed direction every hour and the barometric readings told stories no one had ever asked for. [121]

She moved through it slowly, still thin from the fever, her skin translucent enough to show the blue rivers of her veins. [143]

Her awareness was stretched wide but focused only at the edges—peripherally aware of everything, directly certain of nothing. [162]

The certainties she'd once held as iron were swaying now, bending like the aurora that sometimes brushed the peaks in curtains of green and violet sorrow, beautiful and untrustworthy. [192]

On the desk beside the anemometer lay a botanical illustration she'd been saving for months—a species of alpine flower that grew only in the crevices where the rock met the sky, its petals arranged in a pattern that looked less like a bloom and more like a question mark. [243]

She'd drawn it herself during the fever, her hand moving with a purpose her conscious mind hadn't authorized. [263]

Now, looking at it in the pale light that filtered through the station's frosted windows, she understood that the flower was the truth she'd swallowed. [290]

Not the words of it, but the shape—the way it occupied the margins of the page, refusing to stay centered, smudging the borders of everything else she'd tried to draw. [322]

She picked up the charcoal and began to smudge the edges of the illustration, not to erase it but to let it breathe, to occupy the margins the way the truth itself had occupied hers—quietly, completely, without permission. [361]

The smudge spread like fog across the paper, and in that spreading, she felt the weight settle at last, not in her chest this time, but in her hands, in the graphite dust on her fingers, in the slow understanding that some truths are not meant to be held but to be lived at the edges of sight. [419]

The fever had taken her certainty and left her with only the peripheral—the way light moves across a mountain face at dawn, the way a flower grows in a crack it was never meant to fill, the way sorrow can be beautiful if you let it sway instead of harden. [470]

She set the illustration down and walked to the window, where the aurora was beginning its slow dance across the sky, and for the first time since the fever broke, she didn't try to name what she saw. [509]

She just let it be. [514]

The oracle understood now that the truth she'd swallowed wasn't a fact but a question—one that lived in the margins, that refused to be pinned down, that swayed with every breath of wind across the ridge. [553]

She picked up the charcoal again, not to answer but to trace the edges of the unknown, to smudge the borders between what she knew and what she could only sense at the periphery of her awareness. [590]

The botanical illustration, with its alpine flower growing in the cracks between certainty and doubt, became the map of her recovery—not a destination but a direction, always shifting, always beautiful in its uncertainty. [624]

She left the smudged illustration on the desk beside the anemometer, where the wind would eventually carry it away, and stepped outside into the auroral sorrow of the mountain night, finally light enough to carry the weight of not knowing. [664]
