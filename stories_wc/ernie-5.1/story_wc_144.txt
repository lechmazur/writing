The mist clung to the rails like breath on glass, and old Sable sat behind his workbench in the depot's eastern alcove, listening to the city exhale. [30] He could not see the fog, but he did not need to. [42] The water in the long stone trough before him caught every shifting light, bending it into patterns his fingers could read like braille. [65] Each ripple told him something: the grey-blue of early dawn, the copper edge of a lantern still burning somewhere on the platform, the silver thread of moisture crawling up the iron beams. [97] This was his shop, though no sign hung above it. [107] Travelers found him by word of mouth, by the quiet reputation that spread through the depot like steam through vents. [130] They brought him broken things, and he gave them back whole, or something better than whole. [154]

On the bench before him lay the hummingbird. [163] It was not alive, but it had been made to seem so, and that distinction mattered to Sable more than most people understood. [192] The client—a young woman with salt in her voice—had carried it across three districts, wrapped in cloth, because it was the only thing she had left of her mother. [226] Its left wing would not beat. [235] The gear train had seized, and the delicate feather-lattice on the primary flight surface had cracked along a hairline fracture that only someone looking closely would find. [268] Sable was looking closely. [275] He tilted the trough's surface with his palm, sending a slow wave across the water, and watched the light split and reform. [301] The fracture showed as a dark thread in the refracted pattern, a shadow where light refused to bend correctly. [330] He picked up his finest tool—a needle no thicker than a whisker—and began to patch. [350] Not the wing itself, but the signal path. [360] The hummingbird's flight was governed by a feedback loop: the drone sensed air resistance, adjusted wing angle, sensed the new resistance, adjusted again. [397] When the gear seized, the loop broke, and the bird forgot how to fly. [415] Sable's job was not to fix the wing but to restore the conversation between the wing and the wind. [445] He traced the broken pathway with his needle, bypassing the seized gear with a hair-thin wire that would carry the signal around the damage. [479] His hands moved with the magnetic calm of a man who had done this ten thousand times, who trusted his water-light more than most people trusted their eyes. [514]

Through the depot walls, he heard the deep groan of a ship's anchor chain rising from the harbor—the maiden voyage, the great vessel pulling free from the dock for the first time. [554] The sound rolled through the stone like a heartbeat, and Sable felt it in his chest. [572] He used that feedback too—the vibration in the floor, the shift in air pressure, the way the water in his trough trembled and sent new patterns dancing across the stone. [608] Each signal told him something, and he decoded it all into direction. [628] The anchor's lift meant the tide was turning, which meant the humidity would shift, which meant the water's refraction would change in exactly the way he needed it to for the final calibration. [665] He dipped the needle one last time, aligned the wire, and pressed. [682] The hummingbird's left wing twitched. [689] Then it beat—once, twice—a blur of silver and green that caught the lantern light and threw it in every direction. [715] The young woman, who had been standing silently by the door, made a sound like a sob swallowed whole. [739] Sable smiled and turned back to his water. [749] The mist was thinning. [754] The ship was gone. [759] But the bird was flying. [764]
