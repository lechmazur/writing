Maren had always been the kind of woman who noticed dusk before anyone else. [16]

Not the dramatic kind—the smaller dusk, the way a shadow lengthened beneath a chair, the way light thinned from a wall like a slow exhale. [54] She called herself a dusk noticer, though no one else used the term, because no one else saw the way light thinned at the edges of things the way she did. [98]

In the blooming library above the clouds, where jasmine crawled across the spines of books that had never been written yet, Maren kept her workstation quiet and softly deliberate. [138] She was comparing silent video footage from decades apart—the only method that still worked for tracking what she called a shadow unbound. [178]

After dreams became shareable, people had stopped watching the real world. [201] They uploaded their visions instead, streaming them into the collective like water into the sea, and the shadows that once anchored them to the earth began to drift, untethered, searching for something they could no longer name. [257] It started with children, whose shadows would lag behind them, reaching for door handles they couldn't touch. [295] Then adults, whose shadows would stand up from chairs while they sat still, walking toward windows that faced the wrong direction. [337] Doctors called it dissociation. Maren called it grief. [354]

Maren's river stone sat on her desk, smooth and dark, worn by a current that no longer existed. [386] She had found the stone in a riverbed that dried up decades ago, back when rivers still ran and shadows still obeyed. [430] She held it when the footage blurred, when the differences between decades became too subtle for even her sharp eyes. [468] The stone reminded her what it meant to be bound—to something solid, something that didn't float away. [502]

The footage showed the same courtyard, the same bench, the same tree. [526] But the shadows had changed. [532] In the older footage, they lay flat and faithful. [548] In the newer footage, they moved on their own, stretching toward things that weren't there, reaching for a shape that had been lost. [592]

That was the shadow unbound—not a shadow without an owner, but a shadow that had outgrown its owner. [630] It had metamorphosed from something passive into something searching. [655] And Maren understood, with a luminous regret that glowed in her chest like light trapped in amber, that she was watching hope change form. [703] Hope had always lived in shadows, she realized. It lived in the dark shape a tree cast on a sunny day, in the silhouette of a person you loved standing in a doorway. But when dreams became shareable, hope stopped needing shadows. It floated free, unbound, and the shadows it left behind began to move on their own, searching for the hope that had abandoned them. [825]

She wasn't trying to stop it. [831] She was trying to preserve the shape of it—the way it had looked before it learned to move on its own. [865] Because once hope became unbound, it could go anywhere, and it would never look the same way twice. [903]

The library breathed around her, its shelves expanding with every dream uploaded, every vision shared into the endless collective. [947] Flowers opened in slow motion, petals catching light that existed only in the space between cloud and sky. [981] New books appeared on the shelves, their pages blank, waiting for dreams that would never be written down. [1019] Maren set down the stone, picked up the next reel of footage, and began again—softly deliberate, sharp-eyed, noticing the dusk even here, even now, even in a place where the sun never fully set. [1083]

She knew what she was preserving wasn't the past. [1107] It was the shape of what the past had meant, before it metamorphosed into something she could only watch, never hold. [1151] And that was enough. [1157] That had to be enough. [1163]
