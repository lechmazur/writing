Maren had been a celestial signal interpreter for eleven years, long enough to know that certainty was a luxury the stars never afforded. [23]
She sat at her console in Outpost Limin, where the gravitational anomaly bent light into slow spirals and made her coffee cup drift an inch to the left every morning. [47]
The station hummed with a low, tidal thrum that lived somewhere between sound and memory, a vibration she had stopped noticing and started relying on. [74]
Three cycles ago, she had planted the wrong seeds. [83]
Not in any garden, though the hydroponics bay still bore the scar of her miscalculation, rows of kelp-substitute choking the vents in a green, suffocating tangle. [108]
She had planted the wrong seeds in the archive. [119]
She had submitted an edited version of the first-contact signals, smoothing out the ambiguities that later proved to be the entire point. [143]
Now the historical record was wrong, and she was the only one who remembered what it had said before. [164]
Maren reached for the dried-up fountain pen on her desk, its exotic nib carved from meteorite iron, the reservoir empty for months. [190]
She turned it over in her fingers anyway, a habit born from years of needing something solid when the signals contradicted themselves. [216]
She was confidently uncertain about her work. [224]
She trusted her instincts absolutely while knowing they could be completely wrong, and that paradox had become the only stable ground she had. [254]
The anomaly distorted time itself, stretching seconds near the core into what felt like hours, and Maren had learned to navigate that distortion the way sailors learn to read a corrupted sea. [290]
Her motivation had always been to thresh meaning from experience, to separate the signal from the noise, to find the grain hidden in the chaff of raw data. [320]
But the wrong seeds she had planted made that work feel impossible now, because she was no longer sure the chaff and the grain were where she had left them. [354]
She could only do this through contingent relationships, the delicate dependencies between one signal and another, between what was recorded and what was lost, between her interpretation and whatever the truth actually was. [397]
Each relationship was a thread, and pulling one meant everything shifted, which meant she could never be sure of anything, which meant she had to keep pulling anyway. [432]
There was a strange comfort in this, a quiet solace in knowing that meaning was not fixed but negotiated, that the universe itself was a draft waiting to be revised. [468]
Maren picked up the pen again, pressing the exotic nib against her thumb even though it could not write. [493]
The weight of it, the memory of ink, the sharp point that once carved truth from starlight, these were enough to remind her why she had become an interpreter in the first place. [527]
She needed to unbend, to release the rigid posture she had held since the editing, since she realized what she had done to the record and could not undo it. [560]
The anomaly pulsed outside, light spiraling in slow, impossible curves, and Maren let herself breathe with it, letting her shoulders drop, letting her spine soften against the chair. [593]
She opened the archive and began to work, not to restore what was, but to find what could still be true, threshing meaning from the wreckage of her own mistakes one careful edit at a time. [632]
The pen sat beside her, useless and perfect, a reminder that some tools outlive their purpose, and that confidence and uncertainty could coexist in the same hand. [666]
The strange comfort settled over her like the anomaly's light, distorted and slow but undeniably real, and she was confidently uncertain, and that was enough. [702]
