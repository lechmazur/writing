[14] Her job was to catalog what people chose to delete—not to restore it, but to understand patterns of erasure. [33] The archive existed in a strange temporal space—across the echo of forgotten songs, as the old-timers called it. [52] Every deleted post, every removed photo, every erased message left a ghost impression in the data architecture. [68]

She was brightly attentive to anomalies, and one kept appearing: the numbers 7-3-1-9. [82] These numbers showed up everywhere. [87] In timestamps of deleted posts. [92] In user IDs of purged accounts. [98] In the metadata of vanished images. [104] 7319. [105] Over and over. [108]

Mara knew about missing persons cold cases—Nexus had an entire department dedicated to cooperating with authorities on digital forensics. [126] But this wasn't an open investigation. [132] This was something buried. [136]

The patch bore an emblem: a sun with seven rays, three stars, one moon, nine planets. [191] 7-3-1-9. [192]

Her mother had been declared a missing person. [200] The case went cold after two years. [207] But Mara had never stopped looking, never stopped wondering where deleted files go—both digital and human. [223]

She began digging. [226] The Helios-7 mission had crashed on reentry, officially killing all six crew members. [238] But the social network archive told a different story.
