Maren swept the observation deck of Station Vela-9, her broom moving in slow arcs across the cold metal floor while the universe bled light above her. [28]

She had been the cosmic janitor for eleven years now, through the hush of an overlooked era when humanity had turned its gaze inward, abandoning the old star-watching posts one by one. [58] Vela-9 remained operational only because decommissioning it would cost more than forgetting it, and so Maren stayed, the sole keeper of a cathedral no one prayed in anymore. [87]

The shooting star tracking station hung in geosynchronous orbit above the Pacific, its sensors still faithfully recording every meteor that scratched the darkness. [109] Once, astronomers had filled these halls, their voices echoing with discovery. [120] Now there was only the hum of machines and the soft whisper of Maren's footsteps. [134]

She paused by the great window, resting her weathered hands on the sill. [147] Tonight, the Perseid shower painted the void in silver streaks, and Maren watched each falling star with the attention of a mourner counting the departed. [172] She had become spiritually curious in her solitude, asking questions she never would have voiced when others could hear. [190] What did a star feel, she wondered, in that final brilliant plunge? [202] Did it know terror, or release? [208]

[237] Her grandmother had given it to her before the launch, pressing it into Maren's hands with instructions to remember that even small lights matter. [259] The glass had turned amber with age, warm as captured sunlight, and Maren held it up to the window, watching the meteors refract through its surface into something softer, something she could almost understand. [293]

[335] It stripped them, burned them, demanded they give everything they were to the brief act of becoming visible. [352] And yet, in that destruction, they achieved a beauty they could never have known while drifting safe and unseen. [370]

[398] She had wanted strength, the kind that felt like armor. [451] There was a flourishing stillness here, she had come to realize, a paradox that defied the frantic pace of the world she had left behind. [551] The tracking sensors hummed, recording data that no one would analyze, measurements that mattered only to her now. [568]

[585] There was primal elegance in maintenance, in the quiet devotion of keeping something alive that the world had deemed expendable. [604] Every stroke of the broom was an act of faith.
