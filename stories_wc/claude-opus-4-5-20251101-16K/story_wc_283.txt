[28] She was the last hourglass turner--a keeper who tended vessels measuring not hours but lifetimes, prophecies, and shared wisdom accumulated across centuries. [109] Mira had come because of the prophecy. [116] The Seers proclaimed her village would burn before winter solstice. [126] She watched neighbors begin their exodus, saw her mother pack belongings with trembling hands. [140] But Mira refused to accept fate as fixed stone. [180] The method lay in cataloging lost buttons. [187] For centuries, hourglass turners collected buttons shed throughout time--fallen from coats during pivotal moments, dropped from shirts during desperate prayers, released by trembling fingers making terrible choices. [215] Each was cataloged by material, era, and the nature of the moment from which it came. [232] Her grandmother's ledger lay open on the amber desk, its pages mapping connections between buttons and the futures they had touched. [254] Mira began her work in unearthed silence, the warehouse's hush broken only by her breathing and the click of buttons against sorting trays. [278] She was quietly inspired by the patterns emerging--a bone button from a soldier's last stand, a pearl from a wedding postponed by flood, a copper disc from a fire that never spread because one woman chose to stay. [318] Each button marked a moment where prophecy had bent. [327] As the truth unraveled through her cataloging, Mira discovered fate's grammar. [338] Prophecies were written in certainty's language but could be translated into possibility's tongue. [351] The predicted fire was not inevitable--it was one outcome crystallized by collective belief, hardened by fear. [367] She uncorked the canyon echo jar. [677] Mira returned to the amber warehouse and turned every frozen hourglass once, freeing their sand to fall again. [695] In that unearthed silence, time resumed its breathing.
