<story>
Mira held the river stone her grandmother had given her, its surface worn smooth by ancient waters that no longer flowed on the surface world. [24] Above the clouds, in the Blooming Library where paper flowers grew from spine and margin, she worked as the institution's only dusk noticer, her sharp eyes trained to catch the exact moment light surrendered to shadow. [58]

This was the era after dreams became shareable, when humanity's sleeping visions could be recorded, catalogued, and revisited like films in an archive. [80] The library housed millions of them, dreams suspended in crystalline spheres that lined walls stretching toward a ceiling lost in perpetual golden haze. [100] Mira's task was to monitor the collection at twilight, when the boundary between real and remembered grew thin enough for things to slip through. [122]

She had noticed it first three months ago: a shadow unbound, drifting through the stacks with no dreamer attached. [140] It moved with softly deliberate intention, browsing the shelves as if searching for something it had lost. [156] Since then, she had documented seventeen more, each one a darkness that had peeled away from its source and gained strange autonomy. [176]

Tonight, she initiated the comparison protocol, pulling silent video footage from the library's surveillance archives spanning four decades. [194] The method was painstaking but revealing. [200] She watched the same corner of the Remembrance Wing in accelerated silence, 2047 flowing into 2067, then into 2087. [217] The flowers along the shelves bloomed and wilted in cycles. [227] Librarians came and went like ghosts. [234] And there, barely visible unless you knew to look, the shadows began their slow migration. [249]

In the oldest footage, every shadow remained faithfully tethered to its origin. [261] By the middle decades, she could see the first separations, shadows stretching like pulled taffy before snapping free. [278] In recent years, the unbound shadows had multiplied, gathering in corners, congregating near the oldest dream spheres. [295]

Mira paused the footage and pressed the river stone to her forehead, seeking the cool certainty it always provided. [314] She understood now what was happening. [321] The shadows weren't escaping randomly. [326] They were metamorphosing, transforming from passive byproducts of light into something active, something hungry. [340]

And what they hungered for was hope. [348]

She had suspected it when she analyzed which dream spheres attracted them most. [361] Not the nightmares, not the mundane, but the dreams people had shared most fervently, the ones containing visions of better futures, of love returned, of wounds healed. [388] The shadows fed on these, leaving the spheres dimmer, their contents fading to gray. [403]

Her grandmother had sent her to this place for one reason: to preserve the shape of hope. [419] The old woman had lived through the Sharing Wars, when factions fought to control humanity's collective dreaming. [434] She knew that hope was not an abstraction but a structure, a geometry that could be damaged, distorted, unmade. [452]

Mira walked through the library at twilight, her sharp eyes tracking three unbound shadows that drifted between the rose-tinted shelves of the Children's Wing. [475] They moved beautifully, she had to admit, their forms flowing like dark water. [489] There was something luminous about them even in their darkness, something that made her chest ache with a regret she couldn't name. [510]

She could destroy them. [514] The library's founders had left protocols for such contingencies. [523] But watching them now, she wondered if they too were a form of hope, transformed beyond recognition. [540] Perhaps they were the shadows of dreamers long dead, seeking connection in the only way left to them. [558]

The river stone grew warm in her palm. [566] Her grandmother had found it in the last flowing river before the waters retreated underground, had polished it with her own hands as a talisman against forgetting. [591] Some things must be preserved even as they change, she had said. [604] The shape matters, but so does the light that casts it. [616]

Mira made her decision with softly deliberate care. [624] She would not destroy the shadows but guide them, give them dreams no one had claimed, hopes that had been abandoned but not extinguished. [647] Let them metamorphose into guardians rather than parasites. [656]

Above the clouds, as dusk painted the library in amber and violet, she opened the first unclaimed sphere and watched a shadow drink deep of forgotten hope. [681] It shimmered, seemed almost to smile, and settled beside her like a faithful hound. [696]

The regret remained, luminous and persistent, for all the hope already faded. [709] But Mira understood now that preservation didn't mean keeping things frozen. [721] It meant tending the shape of hope as it grew, changed, and found new forms to fill. [738]

She placed the river stone on her desk, a reminder of waters that still flowed somewhere beneath the world, and began the patient work of transformation. [762]
