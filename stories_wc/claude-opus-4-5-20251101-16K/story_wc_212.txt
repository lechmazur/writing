Mira had been an heirloom seed saver for three hundred borrowed years. [11] She worked in the icebound atrium under starlight, where frost crystallized on glass walls and constellations wheeled overhead like ancient witnesses. [30] The cold preserved everything here: seeds, memories, the slow decay of time itself. [42]

The egg had hatched twelve hours ago. [49]

She stood now before the central vortex, her breath misting in silver clouds, holding the oak acorn cap in her weathered palm. [71] It was such a small thing, rough-textured and humble, yet it contained the inheritance of every seed saver who had come before her. [95] The vortex inheritance passed through objects like this, spiraling down through generations like DNA written in wood and stone. [114]

[126] It resembled a bird but was made of light, its wings trailing photons that crystallized into snowflakes the moment they touched air. [148] It perched on the frozen fountain's rim, watching her with eyes that contained galaxies. [163]

"You're the reason the atrium froze," she said. [172] It was not an accusation. [177] Her voice carried the furiously tranquil quality that came from centuries of working with life on the edge of death: intense calm, passionate stillness. [201]

[244] In its presence, broken things mended and scattered particles gathered. [254] Time ran backward toward perfect order. [260]

But such power came with cost. [266] The borrowed time she had lived on, the extra centuries granted to seed savers so they could preserve the world's vanishing plants, would unravel if the creature remained unchecked. She had to reopen the vortex. [321]

[344] She could feel them now, their patience, their grief for lost species, their wild hope for what might yet be saved. She smiled at the stars through the clearing ice. [692]

The texture of borrowed time, she decided, was exactly like hope: fragile, finite, and infinitely worth saving. [710]
