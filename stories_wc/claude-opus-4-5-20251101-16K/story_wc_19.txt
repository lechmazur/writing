Mira was born in the dark, in one of her father's bunkers, and she inherited his skill for building walls between the world and herself—but not his love for enclosed spaces. [32]

Every surface below ground pressed against her skin like a held breath. [44] Her father, the renowned bunker builder, could not understand why his daughter clawed at reinforced doors or wept in the magnificent vaults he constructed to save humanity from surface storms. [72]

But once each day, when gravity inverted for an hour, Mira found her freedom. [86]

The inversion came like a sigh—sudden and soft. [95] Objects lifted. [97] Dust rose. [100] And Mira, clutching her tiny porcelain doll, would release her grip on the bunker's floor grates and ascend through the emergency shaft her father had designed to seal, not open. [131]

She had modified it. [135] By limiting exposure of the lock mechanism to the moisture in her breath each day—a patient corrosion—she had weakened the seal until it gave. [160]

Above, impossibly far above where bunkers could reach, the xenial trellis waited. [173]

It was a structure of ancient hospitality, woven from materials no one remembered making, latticed across the upper atmosphere where clouds became floor and sky became ceiling.
