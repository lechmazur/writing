The barge drifted through the twilight canal, its engine silenced for the night. [12] Elara stood at the bow, watching the amber lights of abandoned warehouses reflect off water that had become thoughtfully clear in the stillness. [33] She had been an errant legend seeker for three years now—errant because she had stopped believing the legends she was meant to chronicle, and seeker because she couldn't stop searching for what lay beneath them. [68]

The antique pocket sundial in her coat pocket had belonged to her father. [81] He had been the official chronicler of the maritime guild, a man whose words had shaped the history of these canals. [101] She had idolized him until the silence of morning radio—that strange hour before dawn when static between stations felt like the world holding its breath—and she had discovered his journals. [132] The truth had unraveled everything. [137]

Now she was here to salvage what remained. [145] Not artifacts. [147] Not reputation. [149] Understanding. [150]

The barge pilot, old Marek, had agreed to take her down the forgotten stretch of canal where the mythical shipwreck was supposed to rest. [173] The Starling's Descent, her father had called it—a vessel carrying humanitarian supplies, sunk by storms, mourned by generations. [192] The legend had justified decades of policy, of taxation, of control. [203] But Elara had trained her instincts over years of doubt, learning to hear the dissonance in stories that fit too perfectly. [223]

There had been no storm that night. [230] No humanitarian mission. [233] The Starling had carried something else entirely, and her father had known. [245]

"You're sure about this?" Marek asked from behind her. [254] His voice carried no judgment, only the weariness of a man who had seen many seekers come and go. [272]

"I'm sure I need to see." [278]

The canal narrowed. [281] Trees leaned over the water like mourners. [288] In the deepening twilight, Elara felt the serene upheaval that had become her constant companion—that strange peace that comes from accepting that everything you believed has crumbled, that the architecture of lies is as intricate as any cathedral, and that dismantling it requires the same patience masons use to build. [341]

Her father's sundial caught the last light as she turned it over in her hands. [356] Useless for telling time in this darkness, but she carried it anyway. [368] It was proof, she supposed, that he had once believed in things that needed the sun. [385] Before he learned to work in shadows. [392]

The barge stopped. [395] Marek pointed to where the water darkened—a depth that shouldn't exist in these shallow canals. [410] Man-made, then. [413] Deliberate. [414]

Elara pulled out the diving lamp and the waterproof case containing her recording equipment. [427] She would document what she found below. [434] Not for revenge, not for vindication, but for the hindsight clarity that others deserved. [448] The kind her father had denied an entire generation. [457]

"Two hours," Marek said. [461] "Then we leave, with or without answers." [468]

The water was cold and clear as glass. [483] Her lamp cut through the darkness, illuminating the silted bones of the Starling. [495] And there, in the hold that should have contained medicine and grain, she found the crates. [511] Still sealed. [513] Still bearing the military insignias her father's legend had erased from history. [524]

Weapons. [525] The guild had been running weapons, and when someone threatened to expose them, the Starling had been scuttled—its crew left to drown with the evidence. [550]

She photographed everything. [553] Her instincts, trained through years of questioning, kept her hands steady even as her heart ached. [568] Her father had built beautiful lies. [574] Thoughtful lies. [576] Lies with load-bearing walls and elegant arches. [583]

And now she would salvage the truth they had crushed beneath them. [594]

Elara sat dripping on the deck, the sundial pressed against her chest. [628] She understood now. [631] Not just what her father had done, but why—the fear, the loyalty, the slow corruption of compromise. [648] In hindsight, it was all so clear. [655]

The barge drifted homeward through dawn's first light, carrying secrets that would finally see the sun. [670]
