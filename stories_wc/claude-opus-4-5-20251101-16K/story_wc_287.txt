She had counted the blooms since April, measured their decline with the wistfully pragmatic attention of someone who understood that beauty required witnessing even in its ending. [54] Now her fire escape garden stood transformed—the rosebush skeletal, her tomatoes harvested, only the hardy herbs remaining in their mismatched pots. [72] Beyond the iron railing, the harbor stretched luminous beneath the moon, its waters holding light like a secret worth keeping. [91]

[122] She reached into her cardigan pocket and withdrew the small metal object she always carried: a single tooth from her father's bitting gauge, worn smooth from decades of her handling. [150]

Her father had been the lockmaster for the harbor district, trusted with every door, every vault, every mechanism that stood between possession and loss. [172] He had taught her that locks weren't about keeping people out—they were about choosing who to let in. [191] When he died, Marta had inherited his tools, but she kept only this one piece, this tiny component that had helped him read the language of keys. [217]

[247] The metal caught the moonlight and threw it against the brick wall behind her, a small dancing reflection that reminded her why she had started this garden in the first place. [276]

Marta's motivation had always been simple: to reflect light into dark corners. [288] Her building housed seventeen apartments, most occupied by people who worked nights or feared them, people whose windows faced walls instead of sky. [310] Her fire escape sat at the building's only harbor-facing corner, and years ago she had realized that the moon's path made her little platform a collector of something precious. [339]

So she had positioned mirrors among her plants—small ones salvaged from compacts, medium ones rescued from thrift stores, one large fragment from a bathroom renovation three floors down. [364] Each night, the arrangement caught whatever light the sky offered and scattered it: into Mrs. Chen's kitchen window, across the bedroom wall of the insomniac in 4B, down the lightwell where the Okonkwo children slept. [441] The suggestion that brightness could find you even in the narrowest spaces. [453]
