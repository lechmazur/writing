<story>
Elena Voss restored murals for a living, but she called herself a "resurrectionist of color." [15] Her vivid imagination saw stories where others saw only peeling paint. [26] Her approach was methodically playful - she documented every crack with scientific precision, then spent hours imagining what stories the original artist had whispered to the wall while working. [54]

She came to the Sound Wave Garden on a commission unlike any other. [67] The installation sprawled across an abandoned amphitheater, where parabolic dishes and acoustic mirrors had been arranged to catch, bend, and release sound in unpredictable ways. [92] At its center stood a crumbling mural - a spiral of figures that seemed to be either falling asleep or waking up, nested within each other like matryoshka dolls. [120]

In her pocket, she carried her grandmother's hobo nickel - a 1913 Liberty Head that some Depression-era wanderer had carved into a woman's face with closed eyes. [146] Elena rubbed it before every restoration, a talisman of transformation, of things becoming other things. [161]

The garden was empty when she arrived at dusk. [170] She set up her equipment methodically: UV light, spectroscopy scanner, brushes arranged smallest to largest. [185] Then she allowed herself to play - clapping once, then listening as the sound bounced between mirrors, returning changed, layered, strange. [205]

"Who made you?" she asked the mural aloud. [213]

In the pause between question and reply, the garden answered. [223]

The reflected sound didn't just return to her ears. [232] It moved through her, rearranging something she couldn't name. [241] The carved figures in the mural seemed to shift. [250] The outermost figure opened its eyes. [256]

Elena understood, with sudden clarity, that she was being bewitched through reflected sound itself - but the understanding didn't frighten her. [276] She had spent her life trading certainty for curiosity, choosing questions over answers, wondering over knowing. [292] If the mural wanted to show her something, she would look. [303]

The first awakening came softly. [308] She found herself inside the mural, standing beside the outermost figure - a woman who looked like her but wasn't. [327] "You restore," the figure said, "but do you remember what restoration means? [340] To return to an original state?" [346]

Before Elena could answer, the sound shifted again, reflecting off another mirror, and she tumbled deeper into the second awakening. [366] Here, she stood smaller, nested inside the spiral, facing a child version of herself who clutched a hobo nickel and asked, "What if the cracks are the art?" [394]

Each awakening nested within the last like those spiral figures. [404] In the third, she was her grandmother, carving the nickel by lamplight, turning presidents into wanderers. [420] In the fourth, she was the original muralist, painting figures who painted figures who painted figures, all the way down. [440] In the fifth, she was the sound itself, bouncing eternally between mirrors, never quite arriving, never quite leaving. [458]

She felt the garden's magic working through reflected echoes - each sound she had ever made returning to teach her that nothing was truly lost, only transformed. [484]

When Elena returned to the amphitheater - to her body, her equipment, the purple dusk - she found she had been gone only seconds. [506] The pause between question and reply had contained multitudes. [515]

She looked at the mural differently now. [522] The cracks weren't damage to be erased. [529] They were evidence of time's conversation with the paint, a collaboration spanning decades. [542] Her restoration would honor them, make them sing alongside the original colors. [554]

She took out the hobo nickel and placed it at the mural's base - an offering, a thank-you, a promise. [573] Then she picked up her smallest brush and began to work, methodically playful as always, but changed. [590]

The sound wave garden hummed around her, holding its secrets, waiting for the next visitor who might trade what they knew for what they could discover. [616] Elena didn't need certainty anymore. [621] She had something better: the endless, nested awakening of wonder. [631]
