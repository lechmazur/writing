The lighthouse had stood for two hundred years, its beam orchestrating safe passage by directing flow of ships around the treacherous rocks. [23] Now it served a different purpose, and so did its keeper. [34]

After last orders at the Anchor & Bell, when the village emptied and the coastal road fell silent, the real work began. [55] Mei Lin climbed the spiral stairs to her lamp room, where fortunes waited to be written. [70]

She had been many things in her sixty-three years: translator, analyst, courier. [82] But the cover that stuck was fortune cookie writer. [91] It was supposed to be temporary, a way station while assets were repositioned and operations concluded. [106] That was twelve years ago. [111] Now the fortunes were all she had left, and they had become something she never intended. [127]

[144] Beside them, sealed in a waterproof case, lay the spycraft microdot film specs she had received that morning from a fisherman who didn't fish. [169] The specifications detailed surveillance equipment that three nations were quietly selling to each other—open secrets, known to all intelligence services but never acknowledged, circulating in shadow economies while diplomats smiled for cameras. Her job was simple: encode the relevant details into fortune cookie messages, which would travel to a restaurant in San Francisco, where the right person would know which cookie to open. [232]

[295] A message about "patience at the crossroads"—intended to signal a delayed extraction—had convinced a suicidal man to wait one more day, and in that day, his daughter had called. [326]

[351] Every fortune she wrote entered two currents: the cold river of espionage and the warm tide of ordinary hope. [369] Both flowed from her pen, indistinguishable. [376]

[393] She had wanted to transpose life into a new key, to leave behind the minor chords of betrayal and surveillance. [520] And people she would never meet would crack them open, hoping for guidance, never suspecting that the cryptic message had traveled through a mysterious lighthouse on a rocky island, carrying secrets inside secrets. "The keeper of lights casts no shadow," she wrote, then folded it into her pocket. [692]

Tomorrow it would mean nothing. [697] Or everything. [699] That was the only certainty her cryptic trade had ever offered. [710]
