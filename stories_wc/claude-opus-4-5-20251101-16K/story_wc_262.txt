[27] She was a crystal gazer by trade, though she had never owned a crystal ball. [84] Madeline moved through the cramped space with practiced ease, her spatially imaginative mind mapping every shadow, every angle where light had once conspired with celluloid to create dreams. [128] All those feelings never spoken, all those declarations that died in throats and dissolved in cowardice—they had to go somewhere. [147] Energy could not be destroyed, only transformed. [155] Love unexpressed was love incubated, she believed, held in the dark until it found release. [169]

[328] But oh, the love she had carried—for a studio executive's wife, forbidden and fierce. [381] This was not merely where films had been projected; this was where they had been incubated, where the unexpressed emotions of performers had seeped into celluloid and waited, patient as seeds in winter soil. [438] These goodbye letters were the same. [444] They had been waiting decades to be read, to be acknowledged, to finally reach their intended recipients even though those recipients were long dead. [476] Her own goodbye letter remained unwritten—to her mother, gone three years now, taken by an aneurysm before Madeline could say what daughters always assume they have time to say. [535] The words came slowly at first, then rushing, a cataract of accumulated tenderness she had been too afraid to offer while her mother lived. [557] The booth absorbed it, as it had absorbed so many others, but this time Madeline understood the exchange. [574]

[627] The goodbye letters would continue to incubate in the booth above, but they were no longer trapped.
