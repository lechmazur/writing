Maren pressed her palm against the frosted glass of the winterglass conservatory, feeling the cold seep through her skin like a familiar grief. [24] Six months since the ferry capsized in the North Atlantic, and she was still learning to live in the aftermath. [42] The hypothermia had stolen her hearing—nerve damage, the doctors said, irreversible—and now the world existed behind thick, impenetrable glass of its own. [63]

She was a capsize survivor, though some days she wondered what exactly had survived. [76] The woman who had composed symphonies, who had lived for the crash of orchestral crescendos, seemed to have drowned that night even as her body floated toward rescue. [101]

[115] Her grandmother had left it to her, along with a worn leather tuning fork case containing twelve precision instruments that Maren could no longer hear. [138] She'd almost thrown them into the sea. [145] Instead, she brought them here, to this frozen sanctuary, determined to rectify what the ocean had broken. [161]

Her motivation was madness, perhaps: to translate silence into music. [171] But Maren had discovered something in the quiet months of recovery, a method born from desperation and wonder. [226] Later, pressing her fingers to a friend's wrist during laughter, she'd felt a different heat entirely—brighter, pulsing. [242] The body spoke in temperatures, she realized, in gradients of feeling that required no sound to understand. [258]

[278] She couldn't hear the tone, but she felt its vibration travel through the pane, through the frozen earth, into the roots of the rosebushes. Unshaken marvels. [332] That's what she'd begun calling these discoveries—the wonders that remained constant even after noise became impossible, after the world fell silent and stayed that way. [653] Tomorrow, she would begin arranging the heat-maps into sequences, creating visual scores that others could feel against their skin.
