The elders called it the Crisis Slow, a divine mercy that stretched moments so choices could be weighed. [119]
Iren did not believe in mercy. [125]
He believed in tracks, in patterns, in causes scraped into the earth. [137]

He had built Kesh from scavenged optics and prayer-bells, giving it one directive: watch the way time moved when fear spiked. [178]
Now Kesh circled him in consciously wild loops, almost frantic, as if the eclipse had bruised its clockwork heart. [197]

There, they said, the secret of the Slow would reveal itself, if he had the patience to watch ink die. [260]
