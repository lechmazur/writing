<story>The frugal astronomer arrived at the reimagined Babylonian palace at dusk, carrying nothing but a worn notebook and a small wrapped bundle. [22]
Between regimes, the city had turned its ancient ruins into a speculative museum, half reconstruction, half prophecy of empires yet to come. [44]
The air held a dusky harmony of incense, dust, and low electrical hum, as if history were vibrating just beneath hearing. [65]
He had come not for the exhibits, but to seal a covenant with the unknown, using numbers instead of prayers. [85]
Museum curators called him Tammuz, though he insisted names were merely coordinates, provisional labels on a sky that kept rearranging itself. [106]
He lived frugally in a rented room above a spice shop, spending more on paper and lenses than on food or company. [128]
His colleagues joked that his devotion to measurement was empirically ironic, because he chased unquantifiable longings with painfully precise instruments. [148]
He preferred to call it encrypted devotion, a love letter to the cosmos written in symbols only the patient could decode. [169]
The wrapped bundle in his hand contained the last piece of that letter, a clay magic square tile salvaged from a black-market dig. [192]
Each side of the tile bore cuneiform numerals that summed to the same sacred constant, a balanced whisper of order inside chaos. [214]
He believed the arrangement encoded a map of stellar positions during a forgotten conjunction, a schematic representation of whatever the ancients feared or loved most. [239]
Tonight, the palace-museum had been rented by nobody, claimed by silence; the guards dozed in doorways while floodlights painted the brick reliefs with restless shadows. [264]
Tammuz moved past replicas of lion gates and throne rooms, into a lesser gallery where unfinished reconstructions lay under tarps like sleeping colossi. [287]
This wing, still between regimes of curator and patron, was his chosen chapel, a place not yet claimed by official narratives. [308]
He set his notebook on an overturned crate and unwrapped the magic square tile, its surface matte and cool, smelling faintly of baked river mud. [333]
With slow fingers he traced the border numerals, whispering their sums as if they were names, asking the tile to uncloak its intention. [356]
He opened the notebook, where years of careful observation reduced the night sky to lines and angles, devotion forced through schematic representations into something the eye could test. [384]
His plan was simple in structure, impossible in implication: adjust the palace floodlights to project the magic square as a gigantic grid onto the courtyard. [409]
If the encoded pattern truly matched a lost conjunction, then the stars overhead would answer by aligning with the grid's empty intersections. [431]
It would be an experiment, not a sacrifice, though he admitted the difference felt thin when one gambled their remaining years on a hunch. [455]
He flicked breakers in the utility alcove until the outer lights obeyed him, dimming, brightening, finally arranging themselves into a controllable array. [477]

The tile in his hand grew warm, or so he would write later, uncertain whether the sensation came from fired clay or fevered expectation. [665]
