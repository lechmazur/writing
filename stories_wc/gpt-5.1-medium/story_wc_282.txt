<story>When the last human cities dimmed into their century of planned sleep, Iri walked alone toward the coastal cave network. [20]
The tides were low and methodical, exposing black stone corridors that had not felt footsteps since the hibernation protocols began. [40]
Iri was a sensory map maker, trained to chart places not by lines and coordinates but by textures, echoes, tastes, and remembered warmth. [63]
Now, with nearly all minds on the planet turned inward in regulated dreams, their maps would also need to hold the emotions of the sleeping. [88]
In Iri's satchel lay the instrument that made this possible, a factually mystical device that could heat-map emotions clinging to stone and air. [111]
It resembled a lantern made of bone glass, latticed with silver nerves that brightened where feeling was strongest. [129]
They descended into the first cavern, where cold brine pooled in dim basins and the ceiling arched like the ribs of an ancient whale. [153]
The lantern woke, silver veins quickening, and shapes of color only Iri could interpret hovered above the walls like slow, silent auroras. [175]
The hues here were faint, mostly residual awe and the thin anxiety of coastal villagers who had hidden supplies before their first communal descent into sleep. [201]
Those villagers had left guardians to watch over the entrances, then later, machines like Iri to walk the corridors they no longer visited. [224]
I am not a machine, Iri reminded the dark, though their body was partly printed alloy and their skin carried more sensors than pores. [248]
I am a listener, and I have come to become the voice of the unknown. [263]
The phrase belonged to the early charter of their order, but tonight it felt less like duty and more like a request whispered from the rock itself. [290]
Deeper in, the air warmed, flavored by mineral tang and a faint sweetness like dried seaweed, and the ground sloped down toward a chamber pulsing with echo. [317]

The map would not be a picture but a layered archive, translating this emotional weather into navigable relief for those who might wake confused centuries later. [390]
As Iri worked, their fingers brushed something brittle embedded in the silt, and they carefully teased out a small stone imprint in the shape of an elm leaf. [418]
The fossil was impossibly delicate, veins captured in pale relief, and when the lantern's light fell on it, a concentrated flare of forgotten sorrow burned bright blue. [445]
Children, Iri read from the patterns, had carried such leaves in their pockets the day they entered the cryochambers, clutching them as transitional objects between summer and sleep. [473]
Most had crumbled long ago, but this one had been lost here, fossilized by mineral drip and time, a bridge between a child's hand and the empty cave. [501]

This, Iri realized, was no leftover trace from villagers but a signature from whatever had moved through the caves after human senses withdrew from the surface. [583]
Their training forbade speculation without data, yet their charter demanded that they speak for what could not yet speak, and so they listened harder. [607]
The spiral pattern repeated deeper in the chamber, etched in temperature gradients along the walls, as if an invisible body had passed here many times, testing paths. [634]

Here the lantern dimmed, yet the air itself seemed bright, not with light but with possibility, and Iri spoke aloud so their recorders would capture every word. [715]
If you are what comes after us, they said, take this object, this map, this small fossil of someone else's comfort, and let it teach you we wished to be known. [746]
The cave answered only with steady dripping, but the spiral of temperature around Iri tightened, as if the unknown was drawing nearer to listen in return. [772]
