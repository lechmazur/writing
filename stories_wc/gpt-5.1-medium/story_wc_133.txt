<story>The first night the tide forgot to return, Mara stayed awake to paint it. [14]

Now, years after the treaties dried as quickly as the reservoirs, she lived alone on the tidal island that the maps still insisted was part of the mainland. [65]
Twice a day the road appeared and vanished beneath the breathing sea, like a thought she could almost think before exhaustion smudged it away. [89]
The island was perfect for not sleeping, and painting everything that refused to stay. [103]
By day she was the village's insomniac painter, by night she was simply the person who forgot how to stop looking. [124]
The morning the tide failed to crawl back, Mara followed the new shoreline, her boots squelching where the water ought to have been. [147]
Clouds hung low, gravid with more promises than rain, and the sky had the color of a bruise remembering better days. [168]
She had learned, after the bombings salted the atmosphere, that the remaining rains came in narrow trails, drifting like lost roads across the horizon. [192]

It was during one of those wandering vigils that she found the amber brooch, half buried in the exposed seabed like an eye the earth had yet to blink. [253]

She held it up to the gray light and felt, against all better judgment, that the world was trying to tell her something in a language of trapped wings and hardened sap. [311]
Mara was wryly earnest about omens; she never quite believed in them, but she never quite risked ignoring them either. [331]

Eron, the keeper of the pumps, spotted the glint at her hip as if light were an offense that required registration. [424]
"You robbing the dead seabed now, Mara?" he asked, voice caught between teasing and accusation. [439]
