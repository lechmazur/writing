<story>On nights when the volcano glowed like a banked coal, Niko walked the black sand beach carrying his bottled lightning bug collection. [22]
He was a destitute philanthropist whose pockets were empty but whose routes between ruined villages were lined with people quietly depending on his impossible kindness. [47]
The jars at his belt clinked like tame constellations, each lightning bug a trembling echo of the stars above. [66]
Tonight, during the slow dance of celestial bodies, the sky bent around an eclipse and a wandering comet, and the whole island seemed to hold its breath. [93]
A whispering hush rose between the surf and the distant magma, a soft insistence that something old was about to molt into something new. [117]
Niko had begun walking these detoured routes years ago because he wanted to understand the texture of transformation, not just witness it from afar. [141]
When he had coins, they disappeared into other hands; when he had nothing, he orchestrated coincidences instead, slipping people into one another's paths like notes in a hidden song. [170]
A fisherman seeking rope would meet a widow trying to clear her collapsed roof, and by nightfall both roof and boat would stand repaired. [194]
The bottled lightning bugs were his latest project, captured gently over rice paddies and gifted to families whose oil lamps had shattered in the last tremor. [220]
The volcano had been restless all season, grumbling like an old god caught between anger and confession, and the elders muttered of prophecies they had not meant to remember. [249]
Niko ignored the word prophecy as much as he could, which was difficult for a man who kept accidentally fulfilling prophecies simply by allowing his errands to tangle. [277]
Last year he had told a frightened child to plant a single bean in volcanic ash just to keep her busy, only to find an entire hillside of food sprouting months later, exactly as some cracked temple mural had predicted. [317]
Tonight his route should have curved inland toward the camps, but the whispering hush stiffened into a tug, and he felt himself veer onto a narrow path down to the tide. [348]
It was a detour without reason, which to Niko usually meant a reason waited just beyond the reach of his understanding. [369]
On the black sand beach at the foot of the active volcano a woman stood alone, her telescope propped on driftwood, her bare feet rooted. [394]
She introduced herself as Lila, a sky-watcher from the mountain observatory, here to witness how the moon and comet braided their light over the volcano. [419]
A vibrant hush lived between her words, as if she had been speaking mostly to the cosmos lately and found human conversation almost too loud. [444]
They watched the sky deepen until the comet's tail brushed the dark like a question mark, and Lila began telling him the island's oldest story. [469]
It spoke of a night when the heavens would move in slow choreography, when a stranger bearing bottled stars would stand at the volcano's feet and teach it mercy. [498]
Niko glanced at his lightning bugs, which smoldered green-gold in their glass prisons, and felt the familiar irritation of being drafted into someone else's myth. [523]
I am only trying to see how things change, he said, how fear becomes courage, how ash becomes soil; I am not here to teach volcanoes ethics. [550]
Maybe that is what makes you dangerous to prophecies, Lila replied, because you are the only person I have met who fulfills them by mistake. [575]
The volcano answered with a shudder that sent small stones skittering down its flanks, a reminder that the story, like the mountain, was not yet finished. [601]

Niko closed his eyes, tracing the island's veins in his mind: goat trails, dry streambeds, the stubborn roads of habit, all the routes where coincidence might be convinced to occur. [657]
If he could draw people sideways for just an hour, a collective detour, the lava might pass harmlessly into the sea while the camps stood empty and bewildered but alive. [687]

As Niko ran, the ground hot beneath his soles, he felt that texture of transformation at last, rough and immediate, like pumice scraped directly against the heart. [778]
