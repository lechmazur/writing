<story>The clocks liked to pretend they were quiet, but Elias knew better. [12]
He was the introverted clock scribe of the soundless sanctuary, hired not to ring the hours but to write them down. [33]
Each morning, in the hush of a hidden sunrise, he sat alone among pendulums and dust, ink soaking slowly into his ledger. [55]
Today the sanctuary smelled of petrichor nostalgia, rain just fled from the stone ribs of the cloister and the crystal whispering forecourt after rain still dripping outside his door. [84]
The world beyond the walls murmured with waking carts and crows, but inside a glacial hush held every breath on the tip of breaking. [108]
Elias had asked for this posting to make peace with an abandoned dream, though he had never told the abbess that was what he sought. [133]
Before the sanctuary, he had wanted to design clocks that did not count down lives but opened them, timepieces of music and color for a world that hurried past ticking faces. [164]
Investors had smiled, patted his shoulder, and chosen cheaper gears, and the dream had sunk into him like water into cold stone. [186]
It was easier, he thought, to bear other people's hours than to ask the world to wear his. [204]
So he became a scribe of seconds, cataloging each bell that never rang, each noon that passed with only a line of ink to prove it had existed. [232]
His handwriting was excruciatingly gentle, every serif a small apology to the page, as if bold letters might wake something he was not ready to face. [258]
On this morning, just as a veiled light seeped through the high window, one of the oldest clocks cleared its throat. [279]
The sound was not a chime but a scrape, a gear slipping past its accustomed tooth, a tired refusal. [298]
Elias stood, the bench whispering across stone, and approached the clock that had once told the hours of an entire city before the sanctuary swallowed it. [324]
Through the open door beside it, he could see the crystal whispering forecourt after rain, its tessellated glass stones catching the newborn light in trembling colors. [350]
Water still streaked down the carved arches, tracing old fractures like erased names. [363]
