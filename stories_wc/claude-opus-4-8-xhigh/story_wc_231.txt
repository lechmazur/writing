<story>The lighthouse keeper named Orrin collected things other people forgot. [10] He was eccentric in the way that solitary men become, talking to gulls and arranging seashells by the year they were lost. [32] Every evening at nautical twilight, when the sun had drowned but the sky still bruised purple, he walked the opal dock that the mist made into a corridor of pale milk. [63] It was there, three nights ago, that a stranger had stepped from a ferry, pressed something into Orrin's palm, and walked back into the fog without a word. [91] The gift was a dried Edelweiss boutonniere, its white star flattened and brittle, the kind of flower that grows only on far mountains no harbor could reach. [118] The next morning the stranger was found cold at the dock's end, and the boutonniere became the last gesture of a life Orrin had never known. [144] Orrin resolved to investigate, not the death, which the constable filed as exhaustion, but the meaning. [160] He wanted to decode the stranger's final gesture, certain that a man does not carry a mountain flower to the sea for nothing. [183] Orrin possessed a strange gift he rarely confessed: by touching an object long held by another, he could borrow fragments of their memories. [206] The borrowed memories never came whole; they arrived like the dock in mist, more absence than image. [223] So he learned to read them by observing gaps, studying the shape of what had been omitted rather than what remained. [244] Each twilight he held the boutonniere and let the dead man's recollections wash through him, softly persistent, returning whether he willed it or not. [268] He saw an alpine meadow, a woman laughing, a wedding where this very flower had been pinned to a young groom's lapel. [290] But the memories were riddled with holes, and Orrin noticed the same emptiness recurred: a chair always vacant, a name never spoken, a side of every scene gone dark. [319] By observing those gaps, he understood that the stranger had spent decades waiting for someone who had promised to return and never had. [342] The vacant chair was hers; the unspoken name was the wound the whole life had been built around. [360] The Edelweiss, Orrin realized, was the boutonniere from that abandoned wedding, kept fifty years against the hope of a ceremony never held. [382] The stranger had come to this harbor because someone, long ago, had said she would wait for him at the last dock at the edge of the world. [410] Finding only mist and an old keeper, he had chosen to hand his sole proof of love to the one witness left, then lie down where the waiting had finally ended. [441] Orrin felt the witnessed isolation of it settle over him like the fog over the opal stones. [458] He, too, had been a man alone at the end of the world, keeping a light for ships that no longer came. [480] The stranger had not given him a riddle so much as a mirror. [493] The final gesture had been a passing of the torch, one watcher entrusting his vigil to another before he could rest. [514] So Orrin stopped trying to decode and simply understood. [523] At the next nautical twilight he carried the dried Edelweiss to the dock's furthest plank, where the opal stones vanished into water and mist. [547] He set it down gently, a witness honoring the witnessed, then spoke the name he had read in the gaps. [567] The mist did not answer, but it did not need to. [578] Some vigils are kept simply so that one lonely life is not erased completely. [592] Orrin climbed back to his lamp and lit it, softly persistent against the dark, a borrowed memory now safe inside the only man left to hold it. [619]</story>
