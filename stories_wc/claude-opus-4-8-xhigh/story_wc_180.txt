<story>The quartz-eye mender climbed the cathedral rafters where apple boughs grew thick as sermons. [14] Her eyes, two polished stones, caught the broken light and scattered it across the orchard. [29] Below, the abandoned nave had become a meadow, but the true marvel hung above: a lush orchard spanning the rafters. [49] She had tended these trees since the village signed its contract: protect the orchard, and the orchard would feed every winter table. [71] But after the breach of contract, the villagers stopped climbing, stopped pruning, stopped believing leaves could keep promises. [89] For these were no ordinary trees; they made deciduous vows. [99] Each spring a leaf unfurled bearing a written promise, and each autumn that leaf fell, the vow either kept or forgotten. [120] Now the branches dropped their pledges unread, and the mender feared the orchard would die with the old understanding. [139] Her granddaughter, Pell, scrambled up behind her, endearingly feisty, scolding the abandoned saints in their dusty alcoves. [156] "They quit on us," Pell announced, "so we quit on them." [167] "We persist," the mender corrected, "because the trees never breached anything." [178] She meant to bridge generational wisdom, to hand Pell the patience her own mother had handed her. [195] But patience is hard to teach a child who wants the world repaired by supper. [210] The largest tree, the covenant oak, had split where its great vow-branch tore loose in the spring storm. [228] If that wound stayed open, every promise stored in its rings would rot. [241] The mender opened her satchel and frowned; she had forgotten her grafting wax and twine at home. [258] All that remained were a bag's forgotten contents: a heel of beeswax candle, a packet of last year's seeds, and a faded hair ribbon. [282] The ribbon had been her daughter's, Pell's mother, who had died trusting these trees to remember her. [299] "That's Mama's," Pell whispered, the feistiness draining from her face. [309] The mender hesitated, then decided that wisdom is mostly making good use of what you happen to be carrying. [328] She softened the candle wax between her palms and pressed it into the oak's split like a poultice. [346] Then she bound the torn branch back against the trunk, lashing it tight with her daughter's hair ribbon. [364] "You're tying her up there forever," Pell protested, though her hands were already helping. [378] "I'm giving her back to them," the mender said, surprising herself with the truth of it. [394] A vow is only deciduous if someone gathers the fallen leaves and plants them again. [409] So she gave Pell the packet of forgotten seeds and pointed to the meadow below. [424] "The village broke its word, so we grow a new orchard that doesn't need their word at all." [442] Pell scrambled down and flung the seeds across the old stone floor, still scolding, still feisty, but planting. [460] By autumn the bound branch had knit itself whole, the ribbon swallowed gently into healing bark. [476] When the leaves fell that year, the mender and Pell gathered every single one. [490] They read the old promises aloud, then pressed the leaves into the soil among the sprouting seedlings. [507] "So vows don't end," Pell said slowly, "they just change addresses." [518] The mender laughed, because the child had stumbled into the very wisdom she had failed to teach directly. [536] That, she understood, was how generations truly passed things down: not by lecture, but by mending side by side. [555] Her quartz eyes blurred, which surprised her, since glass was not supposed to weep. [569] Years later, when the village came begging for fruit, having starved on their own broken bargain, the new orchard was ready. [590] Pell, grown and endearingly stubborn still, fed them anyway. [599] "We persist," she explained to her own wide-eyed daughter, "because the trees never breached anything." [614] And high in the rafters, where one old ribbon had become a ring inside the wood, the covenant oak kept every promise it had ever been told. [641]</story>
