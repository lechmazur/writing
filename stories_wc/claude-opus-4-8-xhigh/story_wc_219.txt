After making first contact, the council prescribed what they called an artificial pilgrimage. [13] Naila went anyway, though she trusted ceremonies far less than she trusted her own stubborn data. [29] The visitors had asked humanity a single question: what is the chemistry of homecoming? [43] They claimed to wander without origin, and they wanted to learn the reaction that pulled a creature back toward one fixed place. [65] So the institute built a route, an engineered journey meant to manufacture longing on demand. [80] Naila, a luminous skeptic who still glowed faintly along her wrists from the contact, called the whole scheme sentimental theater. [100] She had always been academically rebellious, the sort who published footnotes that demolished her own advisors. [116] But the assignment was hers, and she meant to prove the pilgrimage produced nothing measurable. [131] The path began past the city, where an overgrown sculpture path wound between rusted figures swallowed by ivy. [149] The designers had seeded the route with dusty footprints, pressed into the soft clay months earlier to suggest that countless others had already passed. [173] Pilgrims were supposed to converge along these prints, drawn by the herd feeling of borrowed company. [189] Naila noticed at once that the footprints were too uniform, too evenly spaced, a fiction of belonging. [206] Still, she followed them, because they led somewhere, and her instruments needed a destination. [220] In her pocket she carried a knot of fly amber, an old gift from her mother, a single insect suspended in golden resin. [243] She had kept it through every move, through dormitories and laboratories and the long quarantine after the ships arrived. [262] As she walked, the air smelled of wet stone and green growth, for it was spring and the rains had only just stopped. [285] Other travelers appeared between the statues, quiet strangers matching their steps to the planted prints. [300] By dusk they all reached the rainwashed tower, its bronze flank still beaded and dripping, glowing dully in the last light. [321] Inside, the visitors waited, patient shapes who measured the humans' breathing and the salt rising in their eyes. [339] A bronze quiet settled over the chamber, a hush so heavy it seemed to weigh on the tongue. [357] Naila set up her sensors, determined to record only absence. [367] But as the pilgrims gathered, something shifted that her skepticism had not predicted. [380] One by one, they began describing places they had never seen yet somehow recognized. [394] A man wept at the smell of bread he had never tasted; a girl named a river she had never visited. [415] The manufactured route, the false footprints, the staged tower, all of it had assembled a counterfeit memory so complete that it ached like the real thing. [441] Naila gripped the fly amber, watching the trapped insect that had flown home to nothing millions of years ago. [460] She understood, suddenly, what the visitors were after. [468] Homecoming was not a place; it was a reaction, a binding of expectation to relief, and it could be triggered by ingredients as artificial as resin around a fly. [497] Her instruments confirmed it: oxytocin, dopamine, a flood of recognition firing for a home that had never existed. [515] The chemistry was real even when the home was not. [525] The visitors leaned close, luminous in their attention, and Naila felt her own wrists brighten in answer. [542] She had come to debunk longing and instead had measured it precisely. [554] "It works," she admitted into the bronze quiet, and the word tasted like surrender and discovery at once. [572] The skeptic in her wanted to resent the trick. [581] The scientist in her could not look away from the data. [592] When she finally stepped back onto the sculpture path, the dusty footprints had filled with rainwater, mirroring the sky. [611] She walked among the rusted statues feeling, against every argument she owned, that she was being welcomed somewhere. [629] The amber warmed in her fist, a small dead traveler held forever inside its golden return. [645] Naila did not believe in pilgrimages. [651] But she believed, now, in the chemistry that built one, and in the strange grace of a home that could be engineered from footprints, resin, and rain. [678]
