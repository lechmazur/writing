<story>The orchard planet Caldera hung beneath a sky of floating rock arches, each one drifting like a forgotten doorway above the brambled groves. [23] We had arrived as strangers, eleven families fleeing a dying station, and somehow the orchard had stitched us into kin. [43] It was after strangers became family that the chalk began appearing. [54] Each dawn, messages chalked onto the deserted sidewalks guided our labor: where to dig, which roots to spare, when the floating arches would eclipse the suns. [80] No one ever saw the writer, yet the instructions proved strangely trustworthy, never once leading us astray. [97] Old Marisel claimed the hand belonged to a ghost. [106] She was right. [109] His name had been Tobias, a gardener who died long before our ships landed, and he lingered out of pure altruism, unwilling to let new arrivals perish as his own crew once had. [142] I learned his story the morning I followed the chalk past the last grove to a ruined glasshouse. [160] There he waited, faint as breath on cold glass, holding a butterfly garden watering bell. [175] Ring it, his chalk read, and the orchard will answer. [185] When I shook the small brass bell, its chime drifted upward and clung to one of the floating arches, and the stone began to glow. [210] Tobias wrote his purpose plainly across the dusty floor: he meant to orchestrate a collective effort, one only the whole settlement could complete. [233] Above us, hidden behind the arches, hung a nebula of respite, a slow tide of light that could heal soil and lungs alike, but it answered only to many bells rung at once. [266] For decades Tobias had rung alone and failed; a single chime could not summon the tide. [282] He needed us, all of us, standing beneath the arches together. [293] I carried his message home, and that night we reexamined everything we thought we knew about the strange chalk and the silent gardener. [316] Some families resisted, fearing a trick. [322] But Marisel reminded them that the ghost had warned us of the bramble blight, the flooding, the falling arch that nearly crushed our children. [346] A spirit who asks nothing for himself, she said, has earned a single morning of our trust. [363] So we forged bells through the long week, melting down hinges, buckles, and the brass fittings of our broken ships. [383] Each family copied the shape of Tobias's butterfly garden watering bell, until forty chimes lay waiting in our hands. [402] At the next dawn his final message stretched the length of the deserted main road: Stand beneath the seven arches and ring as one. [426] We gathered, brambles tearing our sleeves, hope tangled but unbroken in our chests. [439] Tobias hovered above the central arch, raising his spectral hand like a conductor. [452] On his signal, forty bells rang together, and the chime climbed the floating arches like water finding a path uphill. [472] The sky split with soft color. [478] The nebula of respite poured down at last, a curtain of pale light that settled over every grove. [496] Where it touched, the brambles softened into blossom, and the choking air turned sweet enough to drink. [513] Children who had wheezed since landing breathed freely, laughing beneath the glowing arches. [526] I looked for Tobias and found him fading, his work finally done. [538] He smiled, and where his chalk had always scrawled instruction, three last words formed in the dew on the road. [558] Now you remember. [561] We do. [563] Each dawn now, it is we who chalk the sidewalks, leaving guidance for whatever lost strangers the arches might deliver next. [584] And on the orchard's brightest mornings, when the bells hang silent, we ring Tobias's own, just once, so the nebula remembers the family a kind ghost built from the lost. [614]</story>
