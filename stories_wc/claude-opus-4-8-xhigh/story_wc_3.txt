<story>Mira had been a cuneiform translator for thirty years, kindly and methodical, before grief made her climb past the weather. [20] The magnetic arbor floated above the clouds, its iron branches humming, holding her boots to the bark so she would not fall. [42] She had come here because her brother Davi died, and because somewhere in the old tablets she had decoded a rumor of this place. [66] The tablets called it the orchard where transition could be studied, where the living might understand the physics of how a spirit crosses out of a body. [93] Mira wanted that understanding more than she wanted comfort. [102]

For weeks she worked alone among the metal trees, methodical as ever, recording how the magnetism tugged at her pulse like a tide. [125] She suspected, through analogical thinking, that a soul might leave the body the way iron filings leap to a lodestone. [145] If she could measure the pull, she reasoned, she could map the moment of departure. [160] So she carried her instruments from branch to branch, kindly noting each flutter, each strange weight. [176]

Then, on the ninth night, Davi came. [183] He was not solid. [187] He stood at touching distance, close enough that she could see the freckles she had memorized as a child, yet she knew her hand would pass through him. [215] "You decoded the wrong tablet," he said gently. [223] "This is not where you understand. [229] This is where you let go." [235] Mira's haunted ambition flared anyway; she begged him to stay while she finished her work. [250]

For three nights the spirit lingered, and she questioned him as a translator questions a broken text, comparing each answer to the next, building meaning through analogical thinking. [278] He told her that dying felt like a word being lifted from clay, the mark remaining, the breath gone. [297] He told her the arbor was magnetic because longing itself had a polarity. [310] He told her she could not follow. [317] On the fourth night he reached toward the highest branch, where a single fruit hung, dark and ringing like a struck bell. [339] "Take the seed," he said. [344] "Not to bring me back. [349] To begin again." [352]

Then the spirit left. [356] The departure was exactly as he had described: not a tearing but a lifting, the mark of him still warm on the air, the breath of him gone. [384] Mira stood alone in the silence and understood, at last, that her instruments had measured nothing important. [401] After the spirit leaves, grief becomes a kind of physics anyway. [412] She climbed to the high branch and plucked the fruit, and inside it she found the knowledge fruit seed, small and faceted like a cuneiform stylus tip. [439] She held it in her palm, methodical even now, and thought of all the tablets she had translated, how each had been someone's attempt to reestablish a voice after death. [469]

She decided to plant it. [474] Not in the magnetic arbor, but down below the clouds, in the ordinary soil of her own garden, where Davi had once helped her dig. [499] To reestablish the living instead of the dead. [507] The understanding she had wanted was not a formula for transition but the courage to undergo her own. [525]

Descending the iron tree, she felt the magnetism release her finger by finger, the pull of longing finally giving way. [545] She carried the seed in her closed hand, kindly, carefully, the way she had once carried fragile clay. [563] At touching distance from the world again, she let the clouds close over the arbor behind her. [580]

In spring the seed sprouted, and its first leaf bore faint wedge-shaped marks she could almost read. [597] She translated them slowly, methodically: a single line, repeated, that said the dead are not recovered but continued. [615] Mira understood the physics of transition then, not as motion across a boundary, but as the way a meaning survives a vanished hand. [638] She smiled, and went on living, which was the harder language. [649]
</story>
