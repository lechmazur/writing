<story>Mira restored vivid murals for a living, coaxing color back into walls everyone else had abandoned. [16] She worked in a way that was methodically playful, taping off sections like puzzle pieces, then humming nonsense as she filled them. [38] Her newest commission stood at the heart of the sound wave garden, a courtyard where curved bronze panels caught every noise and folded it into shapes you could almost see. [68] The mural on its far wall showed a sleeping woman, and beneath the grime someone had painted a tiny coin pressed into her palm. [92] When Mira cleaned it, the coin resolved into a hobo nickel, its buffalo recarved long ago into a winking face. [112] An identical nickel, real and cold, waited in her pocket, willed to her by a grandfather who swore it could bewitch anyone too certain of the world. [139] Mira had never believed him. [144] She believed in solvents, in pigment ratios, in the right answer arriving the instant a question was asked. [162] So when the garden's caretaker asked, 'Do you ever wonder what the painted woman is dreaming?', Mira opened her mouth to say no. [185] But in the pause between question and reply, the bronze panels gathered his voice, bent it, and threw it back changed. [206] Through that reflected sound the garden bewitched her, and the courtyard dissolved. [218] Mira woke on a scaffold, restoring the very mural she had been cleaning, the hobo nickel warm in the painted woman's hand. [240] She finished a brushstroke, and the woman in the mural awoke, turned, and asked, 'What are you dreaming?' [258] In the pause before Mira could answer, the panels reflected the painted voice, and she woke again. [275] These were nested awakenings, each one cradled inside the last like coins stacked in a sleeve. [291] In one she was the grandfather, carving the winking face into a worn buffalo nickel. [306] In another she was the caretaker, asking the question that started everything. [318] Each layer asked her the same thing, and each time she hunted for the certain reply she had always trusted. [338] But every answer only sank her deeper, one awakening folding into the next. [351] Methodically, she began to map the descent, marking each dream like a section of plaster awaiting paint. [368] Playfully, she started answering wrong on purpose, just to see what the garden would do. [383] And there, in the play, she understood her grandfather's gift at last. [395] The nickel did not bewitch by trapping the certain; it freed those willing to wonder. [410] The next time the painted woman turned and asked what she was dreaming, Mira did not reach for an answer. [430] Instead, in the pause between question and reply, she let her certainty go and offered her curiosity in its place. [450] 'I don't know,' she said, smiling, 'so tell me.' [459] The reflected sound returned softer this time, no longer a trap but an open door. [474] The nested awakenings began to unfold in reverse, layer lifting from layer. [486] She rose through the grandfather, through the caretaker, through the sleeping woman, until the bronze panels stilled. [503] Mira stood in the sound wave garden again, brush in hand, the real hobo nickel cool against her fingers. [522] The caretaker still waited for her answer, the original question hanging in the air. [536] Only a heartbeat had passed; the whole descent had lived inside that single pause. [550] 'Yes,' Mira finally said, 'I wonder about her constantly now.' [560] She turned back to the mural and changed one detail, painting the woman's eyes half open, caught forever in her own waking. [582] Then she signed the corner with a tiny winking face, methodically playful, a question left open for whoever cleaned it next. [603]</story>
