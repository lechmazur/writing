<story>Mira climbed because stopping was not permitted by the geometry. [10] The staircase had no top and no bottom, only the rhythm of risers under her feet. [26] She called herself a grim optimist, certain the place would kill her, equally certain she would find the seam where it failed. [48] In her pocket she carried a pair of dice, one chipped at the corner from some forgotten throw. [66] She rolled them against the steps when despair crept close, watching probability behave. [79] Numbers, she believed, were the only true exits. [87]

The architects of this prison had bet on perpetual motion. [97] They had built a machine of stairs that fed on the energy of whoever climbed it, never tiring, never slowing, a loop disguised as ascent. [122] Each step she gained was returned to the structure as fuel. [133] She understood the cruelty: boundless space, yet absolute confinement. [142]

She had not come here by accident. [149] Years ago she had stood before a door and chosen the safe room behind it, and the braver door had closed forever. [171] That missed opportunity had a texture she could still feel, rough and cold like unfinished stone, and she had spent her life trying to capture it, to hold it long enough to understand. [204] The staircase, she suspected, was that texture made architecture. [213]

So she worked. [216] She covered the walls with chalk, deriving proofs. [224] A perpetual motion machine, she reasoned, must violate conservation somewhere; no system returns more than it receives. [241] If the stairs harvested her motion perfectly, the loop was closed and seamless. [254] But perfection was a claim, and claims could be tested. [264]

She rolled the chipped die a thousand times and logged the results. [276] The chip biased it, infinitesimally, toward the four. [284] That bias was a flaw, and a flaw was a confession: even this place obeyed imperfection. [300] If the dice could lean, so could the staircase. [309]

She climbed and measured. [313] She counted risers between identical scuffs and found the sequence repeated every 1,597 steps, a Fibonacci number, too elegant to be coincidence. [335] The designers had used the golden ratio to fold the infinite into a loop, splicing the staircase's end to its beginning so smoothly the seam felt like more stairs. [364] Perpetual motion required that splice. [369] And a splice was a wound that had been closed. [379]

Here she had to be balancedly daring: reckless enough to attack the structure, careful enough not to be devoured by her own error. [402] A single misplaced proof and she would climb forever, a clean equation feeding the machine. [417] So she balanced. [420] She derived the exact point where the recurrence reset, where step 1,597 became step zero, where the curve pretended to be straight. [442]

At that seam, the geometry was thinnest. [449] The proof told her the loop conserved energy only if it remained unbroken; introduce a contradiction at the splice and conservation would fail. [472] The machine would have to spend more than it took. [482] It would, in a word, rupture. [488]

She reached the seam after a climb she could not measure in time, because time here was a circle and meant nothing. [510] She set the chalk to the stone and wrote her contradiction: a statement that the step both was and was not the first. [533] She fed the paradox into the recurrence the way the chip fed bias into the die. [549]

For a moment nothing changed, and her grim half smiled, expecting failure. [561] Then the rhythm stuttered. [565] A riser that had always returned her energy gave back a little less. [578] The Fibonacci hum dropped a note. [584] Somewhere below, or above, the splice strained against its own arithmetic, and a hairline of darkness opened along the wall like a held breath finally let go. [611]

The rupture spread. [614] Stairs that had eaten her motion now released it, all at once, the harvested centuries of climbing flooding back. [633] The infinite collapsed into the finite. [639] Through the tear she saw not light but choice: the braver door, reopened. [652]

She did not hesitate this time. [658] She rolled the dice once, for luck, for the texture of the moment, and let them fall onto the breaking steps. [679] The chipped one landed on four. [685] She stepped through the wound in the world, optimist and grim both, into the opportunity she had finally caught with her own hands. [708] Behind her the staircase, no longer perpetual, fell still. [717]</story>
