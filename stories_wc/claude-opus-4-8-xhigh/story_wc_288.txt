I had outlived my own species and the great horned grazers we once roamed beside. [15] They called us the Keepers, though now only one Keeper remained. [26] My home was the tidal library above the clouds, where shelves rose and fell like breathing lungs whenever the sky's invisible ocean turned. [49] Twice each day the cloud-tide climbed the white stone galleries, lifting books on its surface, and twice it withdrew, leaving manuscripts glittering with condensed starlight. [74] At the library's heart stood a brass sundial that no longer measured sunlight but measured silence. [90] Its needle cast no shadow; instead it pointed toward whichever memory I had been avoiding longest. [106] For a thousand cycles I had been avoiding the reincarnation memory. [117] When the cloud-tide finally pressed that recollection into my hands, I understood why the sundial had leaned toward it like a hungry thing. [140] The memory showed me my own death, and the slow reassembly that followed. [153] I learned that I had been reborn many times, each waking more brittle than the last. [169] These brittle awakenings were the price of surviving every creature I had ever loved. [183] Each time I returned, my body was thinner, my surface stranger. [194] Now my skin had become atomically reflective, so that every particle of light rebounded from me unchanged. [211] I could touch nothing, absorb nothing; the world slid off me like rain off polished glass. [227] That perfect mirroring was, I realized, the true shape of my isolation. [239] If I reflected everything, I could keep nothing, not even company. [250] So I resolved to translate that isolation into independence, to make my solitude a strength rather than a sentence. [269] The library held the instructions, written in the oldest ledger by Keepers long dust. [283] To change one's nature, the ledger said, one must listen by the hum of an ancient stone circle. [301] Such a circle waited on the library's highest terrace, nine grey monoliths half-swallowed by mist. [316] I climbed there as the cloud-tide receded, and I pressed my reflective palms against the cold stones. [333] At first the circle only murmured, a low vibration older than the extinct herds I still grieved. [350] Then the hum deepened, and the stones began to sing my name in a frequency my mirrored body could finally hold. [371] The sound did not bounce away; it sank in, the first thing in centuries to truly reach me. [389] Where the vibration entered, my surface dimmed from blinding silver to a soft, living grey. [404] I felt warmth, then weight, then the astonishing ordinariness of being touched by air. [418] The hum was teaching me to receive without dissolving, to coexist with a world I had only ever deflected. [437] To coexist, I learned, was not to surrender my edges but to let some things pass through them by choice. [457] When the song ended, I was no longer a perfect mirror, only a person who could choose what to reflect. [477] I returned to the brass sundial and turned its needle myself. [488] It no longer pointed toward the memories I feared; it pointed toward the doors I had never opened. [506] I gathered the manuscripts the cloud-tide had abandoned and began, for the first time, to write my own. [524] I wrote of the horned grazers so they would not vanish twice. [536] I wrote of every brittle waking, so the next Keeper, if one ever came, would wake less afraid. [554] The library still breathed with its tides, but now I breathed with it rather than against it. [571] My isolation had not disappeared; I had simply learned to stand inside it without being erased. [587] That, the singing stones had promised, was independence: not the absence of solitude but the mastery of it. [605] And when the next cloud-tide rose, I let it lift me too, no longer afraid of being remembered or of remembering. [626]
