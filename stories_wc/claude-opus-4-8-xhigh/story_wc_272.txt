<story>Mara climbed the lighthouse with charcoal still under her fingernails. [10] She had stopped painting faces the day her own reflection cracked into mismatched pieces. [24] A crestfallen portrait artist, she no longer trusted that any single likeness could hold a person whole. [41] Behind the lantern room she found the forgotten library, its shelves curving with the tower's round wall. [58] Dust hung like suspended snow, and a single window threw a luminescent ache of dusk across the spines. [76] On a reading table sat a broken tarot card box, its lid splintered, its hinges weeping rust. [93] Inside, the cards had been cut apart and sewn back together with colored thread, each one a fragmented reality. [112] Half a Sun joined half a Tower; a King's mouth opened above a stranger's stitched eyes. [128] Mara understood at once, the way grief understands grief. [137] Someone had tried to redesign the whole deck into faces that no longer matched their bodies. [153] A note, pinned beneath the box, asked in faded ink: who are we when we forget our own outline? [172] She did not need an answer; the question was rhetorical, the kind that only deepens the room it is spoken in. [193] Then the lamp above failed, and the walls began to close in. [205] Not metaphor, but stone, the curved shelves sliding inward with a low grinding breath. [219] Mara realized the lighthouse itself was a fragmented reality, holding together only while someone remembered its true shape. [237] The thread in the cards was the same thread that bound the room; cut wrong, it unraveled the world. [256] To save herself she would have to do the one thing she feared: descend into necessary darkness, beneath the floor where the lantern's power was rooted. [282] A trapdoor opened to a spiral stair, and she went down with the broken box clutched to her chest. [301] Below, the threads of the world hung loose in the air like a cut loom. [316] Faces drifted apart from their owners, voices from their mouths, hours from their afternoons. [330] The keeper who had made these cards, she now saw, had been a portrait artist too, undone by the same despair. [351] He had stopped believing a face could stay whole, and so the threads had obeyed his doubt. [368] Mara knelt and began the slow work, by untangling threads one strand at a time. [383] She matched a laugh to its throat, a shadow to its noon, a daughter to the mother who'd mislaid her. [403] Each knot she loosened, the closing walls above relaxed a finger's width. [415] Her own cracked reflection, she finally admitted, had never been a flaw but an honest map of how broken things still cohere. [437] She did not need every line perfect; she needed only to choose which threads to keep. [453] From the loose strands she began to redesign the deck herself, not toward sameness but toward a truer joining. [472] A face could be many pieces and still be one person; could a world not be the same? [490] She asked it gently, knowing the answer lived not in words but in her moving hands. [506] Hours or minutes passed, time itself one of the threads she was mending. [519] When the last knot came free, the darkness around her softened into a low, living glow. [535] The lighthouse remembered its shape, and the lantern far above flared back to a steady gold. [551] Mara climbed back into the forgotten library, the broken box now mended with her own bright thread. [568] The walls stood open and patient, no longer closing, the room breathing as a room should. [584] She set a fresh card on the table and drew, for the first time in years, a face. [602] It was her own, gathered from many pieces, and it held. [613]</story>
