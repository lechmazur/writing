<story>The anesthesia wore off slowly, the way frost retreats from a window, and Maren woke into a forest lit in bruised ultraviolet. [22] She should not have seen that color, but the interrogators had enhanced her, splicing impossible wavelengths into eyes that once knew only ordinary green. [46] They had enhanced more than her sight. [53] She was proprioceptively enhanced now, aware of every joint and tendon as a glowing coordinate, so that even lying still she felt the precise architecture of her own body. [82] It was the gift they gave their survivors, a body that could never again be lost to itself. [100] In her fist she found a pewter mask strap, cold and notched, the only thing she had carried out of the white rooms. [123] The mask itself was gone; only the strap remained, the buckle she had bitten through during the worst of it. [143] When her fingers traced the notches, her jaw clenched on its own, and she understood she would have to remember through her trauma responses, since the surgeons had stolen the orderly thread of memory. [177] Her body knew what her mind could not hold. [186] She stood, and the forest told her where she was: a place called the ultraviolet penance, where the regime sent both its broken prisoners and its repentant torturers to walk the same glowing paths. [220] The light was meant to be both punishment and absolution, a sterile radiance under which nothing could be hidden. [239] Maren walked, displaced and rooted at once, a stranger in a body that knew the ground better than she did. [259] Each step landed with uncanny certainty, every root and stone already counted by nerves that no longer trusted her. [278] She had come here with one purpose, the only thought that had survived the anesthesia intact: to discover the mathematics of mercy. [300] Her interrogators had spoken of it constantly, claiming pain could be measured, that suffering and forgiveness obeyed equations, that there existed a number at which a person finally broke and a number at which they were spared. [337] She wanted that number, not to wield it, but to prove that mercy had a logic, that someone might have shown her some. [360] Deeper in, the violet glow brightened around a clearing where a man knelt, weeping, his hands pressed flat against the luminous moss. [382] She knew him instantly, not by his face but by her flesh. [394] Her shoulders seized, her breath shortened, her hands rose to brace against a strap that was no longer there. [413] Her trauma response had named him: this was the one who had fastened the pewter mask each morning. [431] He looked up, recognized the strap in her hand, and did not run. [444] He only braced himself, palms flat, offering his throat the way the penance demanded. [458] Here, she realized, was her equation made flesh, the variable she had crossed a glowing forest to solve. [476] Mercy, the regime taught, was the difference between what you could do and what you chose to do, a subtraction performed in real time. [500] She felt how easily her enhanced body could find his arteries, every coordinate already lit. [515] Her hands knew the geometry of harm with terrible fluency. [525] Then she knelt instead, pressing her own palms into the moss beside his. [538] The mathematics resolved itself there, simpler than she had feared. [548] Mercy was not a number to be discovered but a remainder you chose to leave behind. [564] She set the pewter mask strap on the moss between them, no longer a buckle for a mask, only a length of cold metal returning to the ground. [592] The ultraviolet light fell on both of them equally, punishing nothing, hiding nothing. [605] Her body, for the first time since the anesthesia, stopped bracing. [616] And in that stillness, displaced yet finally rooted, she let the forest hold the coordinates she could at last release. [636]</story>
