<story>Mara edited atlases for a living, which mostly meant correcting other people's certainty. [13] She had come to the rusted outpost by the frozen lake to settle one stubborn question. [29] Every map she had ever revised lied about where the rivers begin. [41] The old cartographers drew confident blue threads sprouting from tidy springs, as if water obeyed ink. [57] Mara suspected the truth was messier, and she meant to prove it. [69] The outpost was a single iron room, frost-rimed and humming with a quiet that felt almost too peaceful to trust. [89] She waited for the falcon, because the falcon was her clock. [100] It hunted the surrounding valleys each dawn and returned only when the air pressure shifted, announcing the day's true weather. [120] After the falcon returns, she had decided, she would walk the upstream ridges and find the source for herself. [139] To pass the cold hours she read a polyglot's phrasebook she had inherited, its spine cracked, one corner folded down. [159] That single dog-ear marked a page of words for water in nine languages. [172] The previous owner, a wandering linguist, had pressed the fold there for a reason Mara only now began to grasp. [192] Each word, scribbled with a translation, traced back to the same root: a sound meaning to gather. [209] Rivers did not begin at points, the linguist's note implied; they began wherever scattered water agreed to gather. [227] Mara smiled, subtly confident now that her hunch had company. [237] Outside, the frozen lake lay flat as a held breath. [247] She studied the window, where her warmth had bloomed against the cold. [259] The pattern of condensation on the glass had drawn its own map overnight, fine rivulets braiding downward. [276] Dozens of separate droplets had crept, met, and merged into a single trembling stream. [290] There it was, plain on the pane: a singular convergence, many beginnings collapsing into one. [305] No spring, no tidy origin, only the moment countless small flows chose the same path. [320] The window had answered the question her instruments could not. [330] She traced the largest rivulet with one fingertip, feeling foolish and certain at once. [344] The peace of the place pressed in, lovely and faintly wrong, the way silence sounds before something arrives. [362] Then a shadow crossed the glass, and the falcon dropped onto its perch outside, feathers slick with frost. [380] Its arrival heralded a change Mara had not expected: the lake was beginning to groan. [395] Below the ice, water that had gathered all winter was searching, again, for somewhere to begin. [411] She understood then that the rivers did not start in the mountains at all. [425] They started here, in convergence, the instant separate waters agreed to move together. [438] Mara closed the phrasebook, smoothing the dog-ear flat, no longer needing the reminder. [451] She would not climb the ridges after all. [459] The source she had traveled to find was not a place on any map; it was a behavior of water she would have to draw differently. [485] In the next edition, she decided, springs would become symbols of meeting, not birth. [499] The atlas had been wrong for three centuries, and she found that quietly thrilling. [513] The lake groaned again, a long patient note, and the falcon watched the glass beside her. [529] On the cooling window, the single stream had already begun to split apart, returning to many. [545] Convergence, she noted in her dry, editorial hand, was never permanent; it only had to happen once to make a river. [566] The phrasebook's dog-ear, she thought, had pointed her here better than any compass. [579] She owed the dead linguist a corrected map, and she intended to deliver it. [593] Outside, the tranquil ice held its enormous secret, and Mara, warm and unhurried, began to write the truth at last. [613]</story>
