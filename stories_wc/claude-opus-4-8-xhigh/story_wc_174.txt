<story>Idris, an urbane calligrapher, had traded his gold-leaf commissions for something stranger. [12] Each midnight he carried a battered satchel into the decommissioned radar dome above the harbor, where the old dish had long since stopped listening for war. [38] Inside, the curved walls amplified every whisper into a meteor hush, that breathless quiet a sky keeps just before something falls. [59] There he rehearsed his real work: subversive puppet shows that he performed afterward in the seedy bars along the docks. [79] He chose puppets because their painted faces could mock the harbor's corrupt magistrates without naming them, slipping past the censors who would have jailed a calligrapher's honest pen. [107] His motivation was simple and stubborn; he wanted to explore ethical storytelling, to learn whether a lie made of cloth could still teach the truth. [132] The puppets hung from his fingers like confessions, and the drinkers leaned close, half-lit by guttering candles. [149] In that uncertain glow, half-lit epiphanies arrived: a thief recognized his own father in a wooden tyrant, a smuggler wept at a marionette's mercy. [173] Idris remained uncommonly serene through it all, his voice steady even when the room turned dangerous. [189] Tonight, though, the dome was not quiet. [196] His partner, Vesna, stood among the dismantled puppets, accusing him of cowardice. [208] "You hide behind these dolls," she said, "and call it ethics, but you never risk telling your own story." [227] They argued until the words ran out, sharp things flung and dulled against the curved metal. [243] Then she left, and in the hush left behind by their heated argument, Idris finally heard himself. [260] He sat alone beneath the dead radar, holding the one prop he never used on stage: a charred campfire story stick. [281] It had belonged to his grandfather, who once gathered children around flames and turned the stick as he spoke, each notch a chapter. [304] The old man's rule was that you must never reverse the stick, never tell a story backward, because endings spoken first poison the listener with despair. [330] Idris had spent years obeying that rule, hiding his own griefs behind merry puppets so that audiences always felt hope before sorrow. [352] But Vesna's accusation rang in the meteor hush, and he understood her point at last. [367] To withhold his own ending was its own quiet dishonesty. [377] So, alone in the dome, he chose to reverse the stick. [388] He spoke first of the fire that had killed his grandfather, the night the old storyteller's tent burned. [406] Then he worked backward, notch by notch, toward the laughter that had come before. [420] Saying the grief aloud did not poison him; it cleansed the years of practiced cheer. [435] He realized the ethical story was not the comforting one, but the honest one, even when told out of order. [455] At dawn he gathered the puppets and the story stick together into the satchel. [469] That evening, in the smokiest bar on the waterfront, he set the marionettes aside. [483] He lifted the charred stick instead, turning it the forbidden way, and told the drinkers about his grandfather, about Vesna, about the dome that now listened only to him. [512] The crowd held its breath in that same meteor hush, and within it the half-lit epiphanies bloomed brighter than any puppet had ever conjured. [536] No painted face had moved them; only a man and a turning stick. [549] When he finished, Vesna stood at the back of the room. [560] She had heard the whole reversed tale. [567] "That," she said softly, "was your story." [574] Idris, still uncommonly serene, only nodded and turned the stick once more, beginning again. [588] This time he would tell it forward, the truth intact, and trust the listeners to carry it into the harbor's waking streets. [610]</story>
