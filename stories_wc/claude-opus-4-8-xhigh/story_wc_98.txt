<story>On the island of Sariel, I held a title that no one envied: reality checker. [15] My work was to walk the ancient ridge in bloom, where flowers older than language opened before the monsoon arrives, and decide which of our memories were true. [43] The elders called me empirically whimsical, a phrase they meant as both compliment and warning. [58] I measured wonders with calipers and weighed legends on a scale, yet I never stopped half-believing them. [75] Our people kept no books. [80] Instead we kept a collective memory, a shared dream we entered each night, tending it like a garden. [98] But the dream was fading. [103] Faces blurred, songs lost their endings, and the names of the dead slipped beneath a gray tide. [120] If we forgot ourselves before the rains came, we would waken as strangers in our own bodies. [137] So I climbed to the ridge with a single knowledge fruit seed cupped in my palm. [153] Legend held that the seed, if planted in true soil, would grow a tree whose fruit restored everything a people had ever known. [176] As reality checker, I doubted it. [182] Seeds do not hold centuries; soil does not remember. [191] And yet the seed grew warm against my skin whenever I dreamed, as if it listened. [207] The old woman who taught me the craft had explained the only method that could work: the seed must be planted not in earth, but through interpreted dreams. [235] I had to sleep on the ridge, gather the village's scattered dreams, read their hidden grammar, and feed the truest of them to the seed. [260] My motivation was plain enough: to protect cultural memory before it dissolved entirely. [273] What unsettled me was the cost of certainty itself. [282] That night I lay among the blossoms and let sleep take me. [294] The dreams arrived like fishing boats returning at dusk, each carrying a fragment. [307] A boy dreamed of his grandfather mending a net that had never existed. [320] A widow dreamed a lullaby in a language she had never been taught. [333] Here was my task and my doubt entwined: which fragments were memory, and which were merely longing wearing memory's clothes? [353] I interpreted each one, holding it to the light, testing its weight. [365] The grandfather's net was invention, so I let it drift away. [376] But the lullaby carried the cadence of a tongue our ancestors had truly spoken, and I fed it to the seed. [397] It swallowed the song and pulsed brighter. [404] Night after night, while clouds thickened on the horizon, I sorted true from false. [418] I worried I might discard a real memory simply because it seemed too strange, or keep a beautiful lie because it comforted me. [441] Doubt, I learned, is the only honest light. [449] On the seventh night the first thunder rolled, and I knew the monsoon would break by dawn. [466] The seed had grown heavy, swollen with a hundred salvaged dreams. [477] I pressed it into the dream-soil of the shared night and waited. [489] Nothing happened, and my old doubt surged: I had wasted a dying island's last hope on a children's tale. [508] Then the ridge in bloom began to glow. [516] Every blossom opened wider, and within each one I saw a sleeping face from the village. [532] One by one, they began to waken, gasping the names of grandparents, humming the recovered lullaby. [548] The collective memory was not restored from outside; the seed had only returned what they themselves still carried, buried beneath fear. [569] Rain fell at last, warm and certain, washing the ridge in silver. [581] I stood among the waking dreamers, still a reality checker, still uncertain whether I had performed a miracle or merely believed in one hard enough to make it real. [610] Perhaps both are the same thing. [616] Either way, our memory survived the rains, and that was true enough for me. [630]</story>
