<story>Mira had recorded storms for thirty years, each gust and pressure drop entered in ledgers no one else would ever read. [21] The rogue quarantine zone had swallowed her district years ago, when fevers came and the authorities simply drew a line, locked the gates, and walked away. [47] Those who remained governed themselves, and Mira governed her instruments. [57] She had stayed not from fear but from a quieter motive: to nurture integrity in solitude, to keep one honest record while the world outside forgot them. [84] On this evening she sat in the quiet courtyard at dusk, barometer cooling beside her, ink drying on the day's final line. [106] A layered tranquility settled over the stones: swifts overhead, a distant bell, the smell of rain that had not yet decided to fall. [129] Into that calm dropped a carrier pigeon, exhausted, its message tube glinting against the failing light. [145] Mira, credibly curious by temperament, did not lunge for it; she had learned that haste corrupts observation. [162] She offered water first, then unscrewed the message tube with the same care she gave a falling needle. [180] Inside lay a strip of paper bearing a single sentence: They watched you reach the courtyard. [196] The words arrived only after being followed home, and that timing unsettled her more than the message itself. [214] That afternoon, returning from the eastern cistern, she had sensed a second set of footsteps matching her pace. [232] She had told herself it was wind in the dead arcades, the zone's usual ventriloquism. [247] Now, holding the note, she began to intuit the shape of what she had refused to name. [264] Someone else lived in the quarantine zone, someone organized enough to keep pigeons and patient enough to follow a woman who only ever charted clouds. [289] A blunter mind might have barricaded the door; Mira instead reasoned by progressing laterally, the way she traced a storm front edge by edge rather than charging its center. [318] If a watcher wanted her harmed, the note would have been a knock, not a warning. [334] A warning meant the sender feared something larger than her, and wished her forearmed. [348] She turned the strip over and found, faint in pencil, a barometric figure and a time. [364] Only a fellow recorder would speak to her in pressures. [374] The figure was impossibly low, a reading that promised the worst storm the basin had seen in decades. [392] The time was tonight. [396] Mira understood then that she had been followed not to be threatened but to be located, so the warning could reach the one person in the zone who would believe a number. [428] Her own instruments, she realized, had been falling all afternoon while she dismissed the footsteps. [443] The courtyard's tranquility was not peace but the held breath before collapse. [455] She rose, gathered her ledgers, and looked toward the low arcades where someone unseen still waited. [471] For thirty years she had nurtured integrity in solitude, believing an honest record needed no reader. [487] Now a stranger had read it, trusted it, and staked lives on her diligence. [501] She wrote a reply in the same code, confirming the pressure and adding the wind's bearing. [517] She rolled it into the message tube, fastened it to the pigeon, and lifted the bird into the dimming air. [537] It climbed, circled once, and flew toward the arcades, answering her unspoken question about where the watcher kept his loft. [557] Mira followed it with her eyes, then with her feet, no longer recording the storm alone but moving to meet it. [578] Behind her the barometer kept falling, faithful as ever, while the courtyard dimmed into a deeper, knowing quiet. [596] For the first time, her solitude had a direction, and her integrity, a witness. [610]</story>
