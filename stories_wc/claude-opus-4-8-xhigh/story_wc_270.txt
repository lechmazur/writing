<story>During the reconstruction, my grandmother led us back to the volcanic hot springs where the old village had drowned in ash. [21] She had been an insurgent once, a saboteur of pipelines and decrees, and the new council distrusted her gentleness. [40] I distrusted it too, until I learned what she carried. [50] In her satchel lay an embryo sealed in amber, a relic our family had guarded for nine generations. [68] Tradition said it held an implanted destiny, a child engineered before the wars to inherit a peaceful world. [86] But no one knew how to unseal it without killing what slept inside. [99] Grandmother believed the answer waited here, in the steaming pools where our ancestors had first written their codes. [117] I was the only one who could help, because of the accident. [129] Since the day a lightning storm fused me to the orchard, I had been accidentally telepathic with plants. [147] Ferns murmured their thirst to me; the steam-loving mosses sang in slow, sulfurous syllables. [161] It was the mosses that led us to the abandoned kite. [172] It hung snagged in a dead pine above the hottest spring, its paper bleached, its tail trailing into the water. [192] Along that tail ran a cryptic script, faded characters stitched by some long-dead engineer. [206] Grandmother's eyes lit the way they must have during the rebellion. [217] "That is the unsealing key," she said. [224] "They hid it where only patience could find it." [233] We freed the kite, and I asked the pine what it remembered. [245] The tree's dying thoughts came in fragments: a woman, a flight, a promise to the future. [261] Grandmother spread the tail across a flat warm stone and began decoding the cryptic script line by line. [279] The symbols were instructions: temperatures, mineral baths, the exact rhythm of heat that would soften ancient amber. [296] But the final line troubled her. [302] "It says the destiny is not fixed," she translated. [311] "The child becomes whatever the unsealer intends." [318] I understood then why she had fought so hard to lead this journey. [331] The old council wanted to wake a ruler shaped by the past's grievances, a weapon dressed as a savior. [350] Grandmother wanted something else, and now I knew her true motivation. [361] She meant to evolve tradition with intent, to keep the ritual but change its purpose. [376] We lowered the amber into the spring the script prescribed, where minerals stung my fingers. [391] The mosses crowded the rim, their voices rising in anticipation I could feel in my teeth. [407] As the resin clouded and yielded, Grandmother leaned close and whispered her intention to the waking thing. [424] "Not a conqueror," she breathed. [429] "A gardener. [431] Tend what others would burn." [436] The implanted destiny, I realized, was nothing until someone chose its shape. [448] The amber dissolved like honey in tea, and a small form unfolded into Grandmother's waiting hands. [464] The child breathed, blinked, and the mosses fell silent in reverence. [475] Through my strange gift I felt every plant around the springs turn toward the newborn like sunflowers. [492] They recognized one of their own keepers, just as the script had promised. [505] When the council's riders arrived days later, they expected a soldier already grown by prophecy. [520] Instead they found an old insurgent rocking an infant beside healing waters. [532] Grandmother handed them the decoded tail of the abandoned kite as proof the ritual was fulfilled. [548] "You have your inheritor," she said. [554] "I simply chose what to plant." [560] They could not argue with tradition observed to its final symbol. [571] During the reconstruction, every faction hungered for a destiny to wield. [582] My grandmother taught me that destinies are seeds, and the hand that sows them decides the harvest. [599] I listened to the mosses celebrate, and for the first time the future sounded green.
