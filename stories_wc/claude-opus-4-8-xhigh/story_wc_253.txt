<story>After the happiness mandate, silence became contraband. [7] So the State sent a blank-faced performer to every grieving household, including the farmhouse overshadowed by giant wind turbines. [26] I watched him climb from the dust, his face a smooth mask the regulations required, and I understood the visit was both mercy and sentence. [51] He had come to remake the hush in our home into something approved. [64] My daughter had died beneath those slow blades, and grief, under the new law, was a crime against the public mood. [85] The performer's task was to cultivate lightness in grief, to teach me joy by force of theater. [102] He began at once, dramatically narrating mundane tasks as though they were epics. [115] "Behold," he intoned, lifting the kettle, "the great waters rise, and the morning is conquered!" [130] His voice filled the kitchen, but his face stayed empty, an emotional void wearing borrowed wonder. [146] I said nothing, clutching the dried flower bouquet she had picked the summer before, its petals now brittle as old paper. [167] For three days he performed our chores like crusades. [176] He narrated the sweeping of floors, the milking, the mending, until the mundane swelled into legend. [192] At night he would whittle small wooden birds on the porch beneath the humming turbines. [207] The shavings curled around his boots while he described each cut as a destiny. [221] I began to wonder whether he believed his own performance, or whether nothing remained beneath the mask at all. [240] On the fourth evening, I finally spoke, my voice cracked from disuse. [252] "Does it work," I asked, "this manufactured cheer?" [260] He set down his knife and the half-formed bird, and for a long moment said nothing. [276] "It is not meant to work," he admitted, his trained brightness gone flat. [289] "It is meant to hide you, to remake your hush into the State's silence before they notice your sorrow." [308] Here was the morally ambiguous heart of him: a man who smuggled grief under the disguise of joy. [326] His theater was a cage, yet also a hiding place where mourning could survive. [340] "The narration," he explained, "lets me speak loudly so the monitors hear approved words, while the small carvings carry what cannot be said." [363] He handed me the unfinished bird, and I saw he had whittled my daughter's name along its wing. [381] Something loosened in my chest, not happiness, but a lightness I had not chosen and could not refuse. [399] So I joined his strange ritual. [405] Together we narrated the washing of dishes like a coronation, our voices grand and obedient. [420] Beneath the table, I held the dried flower bouquet, and let the grief move quietly through me. [437] The turbines turned overhead, vast and indifferent, grinding the wind into the power that lit our forced smiles. [455] When the inspectors came, they heard only a cheerful household and ticked their boxes. [469] They never saw the shelf of carved birds, each one a tombstone too small to be a crime. [487] The performer stayed a week, then walked back into the dust toward the next silent house. [503] His face never changed, but at the gate he left a single carved bird in my palm. [520] I keep it now beside the bouquet, two relics of a sorrow I am forbidden to name aloud. [538] Each morning I lift the kettle and announce the conquering of the waters, loud enough for any listener. [556] And in the spaces between my booming words, I have built my own remade hush, a private quiet where I am still allowed to miss her. [582] It is not joy, and it is not obedience, but something stranger living in the gap between. [599] The blades keep turning, the law keeps watching, and I keep whittling lightness from the heavy wood of grief. [618]</story>
