<story>Mara had inherited two things from her father: a fear of fire and the stubborn will to walk straight into it. [21] He had been an arsonist who loved the bright ruin of barns; she had become a firefighter who loved the people trapped inside them. [45] Now she knelt at the obsidian lagoon beneath the dunes, where the water lay black and patient as poured glass. [65] She came here only during the migration of clouds, when the sky drove its slow herds north and the coast settled into a kelp-drift peace. [90] A foreign coin hung at her throat, one her father had drilled and strung on red thread, the only gift of his that never burned. [115] She turned it over slowly, listening to the lagoon as though it might finally answer her. [131] The obsidian lagoon was a drowned crater, glass-walled and bottomless, fed by tides that slipped in beneath the dunes. [150] Fishermen avoided it, calling it a place where the drowned came up to breathe. [164] In her other hand she cradled a jar of black sand from the volcanic beach a mile south, still warm with the memory of the earth. [190] Last year her father had set the ridge forest alight and then walked into his own blaze, and she had carried him out too late. [215] She had hated him for one full season, and then the hatred had burned down to something quieter and harder to name. [237] What remained was an unyielding compassion she could not put down, not for the dead and not for the living. [257] She had pulled strangers from smoke a hundred times, and never once asked whether they deserved it. [274] That was the discipline her grief had hardened into. [283] From the burned ridge she had taken a single cutting, a charred branch of mountain ash that had somehow leafed again. [304] Tonight she meant to graft it onto a young sapling she had rooted here in the black sand at the lagoon's edge. [326] She drew her knife and split the green stem, then shaved the scarred branch to a clean wedge. [344] Her hands, webbed with old burn scars of their own, worked with a surgeon's patience. [359] She fit the wounded wood into the living wound and lashed the join with the red thread, the foreign coin still knotted at its end. [384] The coin pressed against the bark like a small, cool heart. [395] She packed black sand around the union, sealing the graft against the salt wind. [409] She breathed in the iron smell of the wet grains and let the work steady her. [425] She had read that mountain ash, hollowed by fire, sometimes whistled when the sea wind threaded its scars. [443] That was why she had come: to prove that scars can sing, that what fire breaks can be tuned instead of mourned. [465] When the graft took and the tree grew tall, the wind would pour through its burned hollows and make music over the water. [488] She sat back on her heels and watched the clouds drift their long migration across the dark mirror of the lagoon. [509] Somewhere out at the burned ridge, new green was unrolling over old ash, and she thought her father might have understood that. [531] He had loved fire for its beauty; she would teach the burned thing to be beautiful without it. [549] The lagoon held the moving sky so still that the migrating clouds seemed to pass through the earth itself. [568] Mara stayed there, gracefully alert to every shift of air, half expecting a note already. [583] But the night gave only the hush of water and the soft collapse of distant surf. [599] It was enough. [602] The song would come later, in a year she would live long enough to hear, and she would call it by her father's name. [626]</story>
