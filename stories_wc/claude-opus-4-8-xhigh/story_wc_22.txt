<story>The gloom-hill coachman arrived at the blooming jetty as twilight bruised the water purple. [14] He had driven down from the dark ridges where no flowers grew, and the salt air felt strange against his weathered face. [36] His coach carried no passengers tonight, only a battered chest filled with a collection of dried seed pods. [54] Each pod rattled with a memory of some country that had vanished before his birth. [69] This was the place they once called the battlefield of the Two Banners, where men had drowned arguing over a strip of useless coast. [93] Now that the reef had died, the bones of ships and soldiers showed themselves between the tides like dark teeth. [113] The coachman knelt at the jetty's edge and listened, for he understood the language of birds as well as the dead languages he muttered in his sleep. [140] A grey gull cried twice from the pilings, and in its call he heard an old instruction: plant where the salt has not yet reached. [165] He had come to let a garden reclaim this battlefield, to bury the quarrel of the Two Banners under roots and petals. [187] The dried seed pods were his only inheritance, gathered from gardens that no living tongue could name. [204] Among the wrecks below lay the submerged concord, a treaty of peace the two armies had sealed in a brass casket and sunk so neither side could betray it. [233] But the men drowned before they could honor it, and the concord had slept under the reef for a hundred years. [254] The coachman whispered its forgotten words now, the dead languages rising in his throat like tide-water. [270] He scattered the first pods across the wet sand and broken hulls, then asked the gulls how to feed them. [290] Their answer wheeled overhead in shrieks and short notes, telling him to fertilize the soil with the very ash of the burned banners he carried in a pouch. [318] So he did, mixing ash with seed, war with growth, until the dead earth drank both. [334] The jetty had begun to bloom long before, planted by other hands in other seasons, and its night-flowers opened as the light failed. [357] He felt the strange smallness of his task against the enormous dark, a narrow vastness that pressed on him from every side. [379] One coachman, one chest of pods, against a sea that had swallowed an army and was now swallowing its reef. [399] Yet the birds kept calling, and he kept answering, translating their songs into the labor of his hands. [417] A heron's low note told him where the freshwater seeped, and there he pressed the rarest pods, the ones whose country he could only name in a dead tongue. [446] By full twilight the sand wore a faint green fuzz, fragile as breath, brave as a flag. [463] He understood then why the reef had to die: its death had unsealed the concord, and the concord asked at last to be kept. [487] Peace, he realized, was not a paper sunk in brass but a thing you grew, season by season, over the place where men had killed each other. [514] The gulls settled on the pilings, satisfied, and their silence was its own old word for done. [531] The coachman emptied the last of his collection into the rising dark and bowed to the water. [548] He spoke the closing line of the concord in the language no one else remembered, and the tide carried it down to the casket below. [573] Then he climbed back onto his coach for the long road home to the gloom-hill, leaving the battlefield to its new and patient garden. [597] Behind him, in the last violet light, the first true blossom opened on the drowned coast. [613]</story>
