<story>Maren sorted seeds the way other people breathed, by instinct and without complaint. [13] She was the arbor's orderly seed sorter, keeper of a thousand drawers carved into living wood. [29] Her home was an ashen skyborne arbor, a great pale tree that drifted above the clouds, its bark the color of cooled cinders. [52] Each dawn she climbed the spiraling limbs, dividing acorns from samaras, naming every kind she touched. [68] She loved the work, the quiet certainty of every seed finding its proper drawer. [82] The regional folklore of the sky-villages below held that the arbor spoke only once an age. [98] It spoke, the old songs insisted, during the hush where past meets prophecy, when its leaves let go all at once. [119] Maren had waited her whole life for that hush, certain her true task was to decode the message in falling leaves. [140] While she waited, she practiced patience by building tiny monuments to forgotten things. [153] A pebble cairn for a vanished river; a twig arch for a beast no one remembered; a thimble shrine for her grandmother's lullaby. [176] These little works gave her a mossy delight, soft and green and entirely her own. [191] Then, one grey morning, the wind held its breath and the air thickened to glass. [206] The hush had come. [210] Leaves loosed themselves by the thousand, spinning down in slow ashen spirals. [222] Maren scrambled along the branches, catching them, laying them in rows, hunting for the pattern her ancestors swore was there. [242] But the leaves told her nothing; they were only leaves, beautiful and mute. [255] Frantic, she sorted faster, until a single speck drifted onto the back of her hand. [270] It was a moonstone cabochon dust speck, no larger than a grain of pollen, glowing with a blue inner tide. [290] She knew at once it had fallen from no leaf, but from the heart of the arbor itself. [308] The folklore returned to her then, a line she had always misread. [320] The message was never written on the leaves; it was carried inside the smallest forgotten thing. [336] Heart pounding, she lifted a blade of grass and gave the glowing speck a gentle prod. [352] The dust speck split, unfolded, and emerged transformed, as such seeds always do. [365] Where it had lain now stood a sapling no taller than her thumb, silver-leaved and humming. [381] In its hum she finally heard the arbor's once-an-age voice. [391] It thanked her for every monument she had raised to forgotten things. [403] Each small shrine, the voice explained, had kept a memory alive long enough for this hour. [419] The arbor was dying, its ash slowly cooling, and it had needed someone who honored what others let slip away. [439] The silver sapling held its last living seed, and only an orderly heart could carry it down to new soil. [459] Maren understood the message at last, and it was not a prophecy to read but a duty to perform. [478] She cupped the sapling, descended the spiraling limbs, and rode a broad falling leaf down toward the waiting sky-villages. [497] Behind her the ashen arbor exhaled and went still, its long story finished. [510] Below, in soil dark and ready, she planted the seed and built around it one final monument. [527] Not to a forgotten thing this time, but to a remembered one, the sky-tree that taught her to cherish small careful acts. [549] By morning a new arbor uncurled there, green where the old one had been ash. [564] Maren laughed, that same mossy delight blooming in her chest, and began, of course, to sort its first fallen seeds. [584] And somewhere in each tiny seed, she trusted, a forgotten message waited patiently to emerge transformed once more. [602]</story>
