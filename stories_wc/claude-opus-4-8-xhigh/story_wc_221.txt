<story>After the shutdown, the time dilation observation deck held nothing but echoed silence. [13] I was the only one left aboard, the blessed pariah they had sealed inside before fleeing. [29] Blessed, because the radiation had spared me; pariah, because the crew believed my survival was a curse. [46] The great engines that bent time around our hull had gone dark, and now the universe outside crawled by at its honest speed. [69] For years we had lived temporally stretched, a single heartbeat aboard equaling decades among the distant stars. [86] Now that the field had collapsed, every clock agreed again, and the agreement felt unbearably loud in its quiet. [105] I drifted to the observation glass, where my grandmother's gift still clung to the bulkhead. [120] It was a gilded compass rose applique, stitched onto canvas, its golden threads catching the starlight. [136] She had pressed it into my hands the night I left Earth, telling me to always find true north. [155] Out here, of course, no compass works, for there is no magnetic pole to pull the needle home. [173] Yet she insisted that true north was never a place but a direction the heart chose. [189] The crew had blamed me when the reactor faltered, because I had argued to push the dilation higher. [207] When the shutdown came, they took the shuttle and left me behind to drift with the ruined machine. [225] I should have felt only despair, abandoned in a tomb of slowing time. [238] Instead, I remembered the family custom of sketching the night sky on anniversaries. [251] Every year, my mother, her mother, and I would each draw the heavens above us, marking how the patterns shifted. [271] Today, by the ship's lonely calendar, was such an anniversary. [281] So I took a stylus and a sheet, and I began to sketch the stars beyond the deck. [299] As I drew, something extraordinary happened: the constellations were no longer the ones I knew. [314] While we had been temporally stretched, the stars themselves had aged and wandered across the void. [330] I had sailed so far into the future that the heavens had rewritten themselves while I slept in stalled hours. [350] My pencil trembled, and for one breathless glimpse I saw a new arrangement of light I could not name. [369] There, near the upper edge of my sketch, one steady star burned brighter than the rest. [385] I pressed my grandmother's applique against the glass and aligned its golden point with that beacon. [401] True north, I realized, was simply the way forward I was brave enough to face. [416] The confusion that had paralyzed me began, slowly, to turn into something far better. [430] If the crew had condemned me to drift, then I would steer this drifting toward that distant light. [448] The dead engines could not bend time anymore, but the auxiliary thrusters still answered my touch. [464] I would not waste this strange gift of survival mourning the silence of an empty ship. [480] My motivation crystallized clean and bright: to turn confusion into exploration. [491] I finished the sketch, dating it as my family always had, and tucked it beside the compass rose. [509] Someday another traveler might find both, and understand that a pariah had charted a course from despair. [526] I fired the thrusters, and the deck hummed with the first purpose it had known after the shutdown. [544] The echoed silence broke, replaced by the patient roar of a journey beginning. [557] Through the glass, the unfamiliar stars wheeled toward me like an invitation. [569] I was no longer a man abandoned by his time but a pilgrim sailing into a future only I had glimpsed. [590] And in that endless dark, with my grandmother's gold pointing the way, I finally felt at home. [607]</story>
