<story>Mara skippered the supply dory to the outer lights, and she spoke only when weather forced her to. [18] The keepers called her reticent; she preferred the word careful. [28] Still, she was warmly observant, reading faces the way she read tide charts. [41] So she noticed, that autumn, how the keeper at Cormorant Rock had stopped waving from his gallery. [58] Old Halloran's lamp had flickered out for good, and after the lamp flickers out a lighthouse becomes only a tall cold room. [80] He had died alone up there, they said, and now the lantern room was haunted. [95] Mara did not believe in ghosts, but she believed in grief, which is much the same shape. [112] The new keeper was a girl of nineteen named Ines, sent to mind a dark tower until the engineers came. [132] On her first delivery, Mara found the girl pale and sleepless, flinching at the wind in the broken lens. [151] Ines whispered that footsteps climbed the stairs each night, that the dead lamp glowed when no one watched. [169] Mara wanted, more than anything, to protect her from a fear that could hollow a person out. [186] But Mara had never been good with comforting words, so she used the only language the coast had taught her. [206] Along these shores, the keepers shared a way of stacking stones as messages, small cairns that spoke without speaking. [225] Three flat stones meant supplies received; a leaning stone meant come quickly; a stone capped with white meant all is well. [246] Before she left, Mara built a careful tower on the gallery rail and topped it with a piece of sea glass. [267] The glass was the green of a calm dawn, worn smooth by years the way fear is worn smooth by company. [288] It means you are not alone, Mara said, the most words she had spoken all month. [304] When Mara returned a week later, the stone tower had grown. [315] Ines had added her own stones, and beside them lay a folded note with no signature. [331] The footsteps were only the cooling iron, it read; thank you for the light you left. [347] Mara left her own unsigned reply weighted beneath the glass, and so an anonymous letter chain began between the towers. [367] Through the winter the notes traveled the coast in Mara's supply hold, passing from keeper to keeper, never named. [386] Each one carried some small mercy: a recipe, a memory, a reassurance that the long dark would end. [404] Mara understood, at last, what she had been trying to do all her quiet life. [419] She wanted to bottle the feeling of safety and ferry it to anyone keeping watch in the cold. [437] The sea glass was that bottle, in its way, a fragment of something broken turned gentle and clear. [455] When the engineers finally came to Cormorant Rock, they rebuilt the lens and relit the dead lamp. [472] Ines was reassigned, and the haunted lantern room became only a working light again. [486] But on her last night she climbed to the gallery and built a final stone tower for Mara. [504] She crowned it with the green glass and a note that needed no name to be understood. [521] You taught me the dark is only a room you share with others, it said. [536] Mara read it by the new beam sweeping the water, feeling that luminescent ache that is part sorrow, part gratitude. [556] She pocketed the glass, that worn green eye, and carried it onward like a lantern of her own. [574] For the rest of her days she kept the chain alive, sailing from light to light. [590] She stayed reticent as ever, letting stacked stones and unsigned letters say what her mouth could not. [607] And every keeper who received the sea glass learned the same warm secret: no watch is ever truly kept alone. [627]</story>
