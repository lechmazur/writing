<story>A hands-on folklorist named Mira had spent her life prying open the seams of old stories. [16] She believed every legend hid a working mechanism, and she was chronically curious enough to test each one with her own fingers. [38] Decades later, that same hunger dragged her back to the place she had sworn never to revisit. [55] The sunken orchard greenhouse waited where it always had, a glass cathedral drowned in a tidal basin and ringed by the splintered hulls of old shipwrecks. [81] As a girl she had played here, and as a girl she had lost something she could not name. [100] Her summers here had ended when her memories were stolen, sipped away like dew, leaving a hollow shaped exactly like joy. [121] Now she had come to recover them. [128] Local lore said the orchard sprite hoarded what it took, and that you could detect its hiding places only by studying broken constellations. [151] The greenhouse roof, half-shattered, scattered the night sky into fractured panes, so that each surviving shard held a piece of a separate star map. [175] A true folklorist did not merely record such claims; she climbed inside them. [188] Mira waded through the cold shallows, past barnacled ribs of ships that had run aground generations ago, and ducked beneath the broken lintel. [211] Inside, the drowned apple trees still grew, pale and patient, their roots drinking salt. [225] At the base of the oldest trunk she found it: a perfect fairy ring mushroom, ivory-capped, growing in a flawless circle that no tide had ever disturbed. [252] She remembered, dimly, that as a child she had always stepped around such rings, obeying the careful rules of safe play. [273] The legends agreed on one thing: such doors opened only when habits break. [286] So this time, against every instinct of caution she had nursed for a lifetime, she stepped in. [303] The air thickened with the scent of windfall apples, and the broken constellations overhead realigned, sliding their fragments toward a single point above her head. [328] She read them the way she read everything, hands first, tracing the seams of light, and the scattered stars resolved into a map of all she had forgotten. [356] There: a summer afternoon, her grandmother's laugh, the taste of a stolen apple, the precise green of light through leaves. [376] Each memory returned crisp and clean, not soft with longing but sharp as the first cold morning of autumn. [395] The sprite, she finally understood, had never been a thief at all. [407] It was a keeper, holding her brightest hours safe inside the ring until she grew curious enough, and brave enough, to break the one habit that had kept her out. [437] She knelt in the salt water and wept, but the weeping was a kind of homecoming. [453] When she finally stepped back out of the ring, the fairy ring mushroom had crumbled to spores, its work done. [473] Decades later, the curious girl had become a woman who knew, at last, what she had lost and what she had found. [495] She left the greenhouse with her notebook unopened, because some legends are not meant to be recorded, only lived. [514] Outside, the shipwrecks held their patient vigil around the drowned glass, keepers in their own right of older, saltier stories. [534] She thought of all the years she had wandered other shores, chasing other people's myths, when her own had been waiting here the whole time. [559] The habit she had broken was not merely caution; it was the long habit of looking everywhere except home. [578] And so, with the salt cooling around her ankles and the constellations settling whole again, she walked toward the tide, lighter by a lifetime of grief and richer by a single recovered summer.
