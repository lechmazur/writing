<story>In the abandoned spaceport repair bay, where rust climbed the gantries like frozen ivy, Sela worked as a sensory map maker. [21] Her craft was not to chart streets, but to record how places felt: the cold of a doorway, the hush of a forgotten hall, the ache that lingered where machines once roared. [53] Clients hired her to remember sensations that ordinary maps ignored. [63] She had come to this dead port chasing a rumor that lived only in the murmur of half-remembered dreams. [82] Each night for a week, sleep delivered the same image: a green glass shard, pulsing, asking to be found. [101] And each night the dream ended with her late mother's voice withholding something Sela could not name. [118] That refusal became her motivation, a need to discover the weight of a withheld truth her mother had carried to the grave. [140] So she crawled beneath a collapsed fuel cradle, where her fingers closed on something warm and sharp. [157] The green glass shard was hot against her skin, and when she pressed it to her palm, the bay dissolved into light. [179] She understood, in that suspended instant, what the shard truly was: a loom for weaving between worlds. [196] Its power reached through parallel universes, threading her consciousness from one version of the repair bay to the next. [215] But the shard was cyclically spontaneous, surging and stalling on a rhythm she could feel yet never predict. [233] In the first world, her mother sat mending a sensor net, alive and entirely unbothered. [248] Sela asked her question, but the cycle snapped shut before any answer arrived. [261] In the second world, the bay had burned, and a stranger who wore her mother's face wept over a child Sela almost recognized as herself. [286] In the third, there was no port at all, only a desert humming beneath two pale moons. [303] Each leap added a layer to her sensory map, grief stacked upon grief, until the chart inside her mind grew unbearably heavy. [325] She began to suspect that the withheld truth was not a single fact but a choice, repeated across every world. [345] In the seventh leap, her mother finally spoke. [353] "I sent you away through the shard," she said, "because in my world you died in this bay, and I could not bear to lose you twice." [380] The truth possessed a weight after all, and Sela felt it settle into her bones like ballast. [397] Her mother had not hidden a crime, only a love too large to fold into words a frightened child could hold. [418] For years that silence had felt like rejection; now it revealed itself as a desperate, clumsy mercy. [435] The cycle surged once more, threatening to redirect her into yet another universe before she was ready to leave. [454] This time Sela fought the pull, gripping the shard until its green light bit into her skin. [471] She chose to redirect the weave herself, steering not outward into the infinite but back, toward the single bay she had abandoned. [493] If she kept leaping, she would dissolve among countless mothers and countless goodbyes, a map with no territory to anchor it. [514] The repair bay reassembled around her, rust and silence and the faint, oily smell of decades-old fuel. [531] She was home, or the closest thing a woman woven between worlds could honestly claim. [546] Sela sat among the wreckage and let her breathing slow, the shard cooling now to a dull, ordinary green. [565] Then she opened her map and added one final entry, the precise texture of a truth received. [582] It felt like warm glass, like a mother's hand, like the murmur of half-remembered dreams at last going quiet. [601]</story>
