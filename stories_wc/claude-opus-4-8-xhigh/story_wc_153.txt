<story>The mangrove labyrinth sang before I saw it, its voices braided through the roots like wet silver. [17] My grandmother had drowned here, and her apologies had drowned with her, never reaching my mother. [33] I came carrying a satchel of unsent letters, the ink smeared by salt, each one addressed and none delivered. [52] The cryptic medium waited on a sunken dock, her eyes the color of low tide. [67] "You want the moment before she decided," she said, though I had told no one. [82] "Everyone who rows in here hunts that hinge of time, the space where an apology waits unspoken." [99] She opened her palm, and there lay a gargoyle's rainspout shard, gray stone worn smooth as a tongue. [117] "This will evoke the dead," she said, "but only the words they truly meant." [131] I asked why a gargoyle, and she smiled. [139] "Gargoyles swallow every storm and spit it gently away from the people below," she said. [154] "Your grandmother did the same with her grief, and it nearly hollowed her out." [168] The shard, she warned, heals in moonlight alone, so I would have to wait for the moon to find a gap in the canopy. [192] We drifted deeper, the spirits singing in a tongue I half remembered from childhood lullabies. [207] At the heart of the maze stood my grandmother's drowned houseboat, its one window furred with mist. [224] The medium left me there, saying the dead speak best to those they wounded. [238] I waited through the long, breathing dark, the shard dull and silent in my fist. [253] When at last the moon slid through the leaves, the stone warmed and began to glow like a banked coal. [273] I pressed it to the fogged glass and whispered her name, asking for the moment before she chose silence. [292] Slowly, letters began to write themselves in the condensation on the window, drawn by an unseen finger. [309] "I am sorry," the mist spelled, and my throat closed. [319] More words bloomed and dripped: she had quarreled with my mother over a single cruel sentence, then frozen in the space where an apology waits unspoken. [345] She had written letters every night and burned none, mailed none, hoarding them like coals she dared not touch. [364] Those were the unsent letters I now carried, found in her flooded desk after the funeral. [380] I understood then that I had come not to forgive her but to find the moment before deciding, the instant she could still have spoken. [405] If I could stand inside that instant, I might break my own inheritance of swallowed words. [421] The condensation kept flowing, and her hand drew my mother's face, young and angry and loved. [437] "Tell her," the glass wrote, "what I could not." [446] The fierce tenderness in those dripping letters undid me; I wept against the cold pane. [461] The singing spirits gathered close, lending their voices to her silent sentence until the whole labyrinth hummed with apology. [480] I took my grandmother's unsent letters and read each one aloud to the water, finishing the words she had abandoned. [500] As I spoke, the glow in the shard dimmed, its work done, the moonlight spent. [515] By dawn the medium returned, poling her flat boat through the parting roots. [528] "Did you find your moment?" she asked. [535] "I found it isn't a moment at all," I said, "but a choice we keep making." [551] I kept the gargoyle's rainspout shard as a reminder to spit my storms gently, never to hoard them. [569] When I rowed home, I drove straight to my mother's house with the letters and my own unspoken words. [588] She opened the door, and before fear could decide for me, I said the apology that had waited in our family for thirty years. [612] Behind her, the kitchen window fogged in the morning chill, and three words wrote themselves in the condensation: thank you, both. [633]</story>
