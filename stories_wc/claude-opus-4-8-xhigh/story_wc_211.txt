<story>Theodora, the venerable oath scribe, climbed nightly to the ancient orchard garden atop the skyscraper. [15] She came after the foghorn fell silent, when the harbor below surrendered its voice to mist. [31] Her task was to archive emotional patterns, the slow griefs and joys that citizens whispered into her brass machines. [50] She did this because she alone still spoke the dead languages, and only the dead could be trusted to keep a secret. [72] Among the gnarled apple trees she kept a single wax cylinder phonograph, its grooves recording even the hush of leaves. [92] Tonight she carried a wax cylinder phonograph shaving, a curl of recorded wax peeled from a much older cylinder. [111] She had found it tucked inside a sealed oath, brittle as autumn skin and warm with someone's old fear. [130] To read such a fragment she would have to immerse the shaving in warm oil and coax the machine to breathe its forgotten sound. [154] The lamp hissed; the apples glistened; the whole sleeping city held its breath. [167] This garden, transplanted soil by soil, was the oldest living thing the city still possessed. [182] As the shaving softened, a voice rose from the horn, speaking a tongue three thousand years extinct. [199] Theodora translated under her breath, her pen scratching velvet disquiet into the brittle page. [213] "When the foghorn falls silent," the voice murmured, "the orchard will outlive the harbor." [227] She froze, for the foghorn had indeed gone quiet that very week, dismantled by an indifferent council. [244] The recording had been pressed decades ago by a man who could not possibly have known. [260] It was an accidental prophecy, an emotion archived so precisely that it had bent toward the future. [277] To understand its source, her mind reached backward through decades of memory. [289] She remembered, suddenly, a curious third-grade field trip, when her teacher had brought the class up to this very orchard. [309] They had pressed their small hands to the bark and whispered their wishes into a brass horn. [326] The man on the cylinder had been that teacher, young then, frightened of something he could not name. [344] He had wished aloud that the children would remember these trees long after the sea forgot them. [361] His sorrow had been so pure that the wax caught more than sound; it caught tomorrow. [377] Theodora set down her pen, her hands trembling among the low, fruit-heavy branches. [390] She understood at last why she archived emotional patterns: grief, repeated often enough, becomes foresight. [405] The orchard rustled, and a single apple dropped into her lap like a quiet verdict. [420] She had recorded ten thousand farewells here, yet never once recognized her own among them. [435] Far below, the harbor lay swallowed in fog, its lighthouses blind and its bells dumb. [450] She fed the shaving fully into the phonograph and let it play through to its end. [466] The teacher's voice cracked, then steadied into a plea: "Archive us, so we are not undone." [482] She wept, recognizing the instruction she had spent an entire life obeying without ever knowing its source. [499] The dead language settled into her bones, warm and final, like oil sinking into wax. [514] She lifted a fresh cylinder and recorded her own oath in answer to his. [528] "I will keep the orchard," she said, "until the foghorn sounds once more." [541] But the foghorn would never sound again, and so her vow quietly became eternal. [555] That, she realized, was how prophecies were truly made: not foretold, but faithfully promised. [569] The trees leaned closer, as though listening to the shape of their own future. [583] Theodora pressed the warm shaving against her heart and let the velvet disquiet soften into something like peace. [601] Then she descended, carrying the future in oil and wax, content to be its keeper. [616]</story>
