<story>After the plague ended, the city above stopped pretending it had answers. [12] I went down instead, into the sewer beneath the sunken city ruins, where the floods of two centuries ago had buried everything in patient silt. [37] They name me a wannabe philosopher, and I do not argue, because argument is a luxury for people with audiences. [57] I have only the rats, and the rats prefer demonstrations to theories. [69] Every morning I dig through sedimentary layers, scraping down past the plague-years ash, past the festival tokens, past the drowned market floor. [91] Each band of mud is a sentence the city wrote about itself, and I am reading backward. [108] Yesterday I found what I had been agonizing over for a season: a seafarer's chronometer cog, brass, no larger than my thumbnail, its teeth still sharp. [134] It is the proof I needed, though proof of an unfashionable thing. [146] The priests above tell one story: that the old city drowned because its sailors trusted machines instead of gods, and the sea took back the arrogant. [172] The new council tells another: that the ancients knew nothing worth keeping, and we must build fresh from clean ignorance. [192] Both narratives compete for the same survivors, and both are lies told to make grief obedient. [208] I hold the cog to my lamp and see the truth they both omit. [222] The ancients measured time at sea with this, calculating longitude by the steady tick of a wound spring, and that method is what let them sail home alive. [250] It was not arrogance that drowned them; it was a flood no instrument forbids. [264] The method was sound, and the method is what I mean to preserve. [277] I am not loud about it. [283] Being rationally rebellious means I do not shout at the priests or burn the council's edicts. [299] I simply refuse their conclusions and keep the older instructions alive in the one place no one inspects. [317] My calm is abrasive to them, I am told. [326] A boy came to the grate this week, sent to mock the madman in the drains. [342] I gave him the cog and made him hold it under the light until he understood what it once did. [362] Then I taught him how the spring drives the wheel, how the wheel divides the hour, how the hour finds your place on a featureless ocean. [388] He stopped laughing. [391] The priests want him to fear the past, and the council wants him to forget it, but the cog wants only to be turned correctly. [416] That is a third story, and it does not require belief, only practice. [429] He asked why I bury myself here when I could teach in the open square. [444] I told him the square belongs to whoever tells the loudest tale, but the sediment belongs to whoever reads it patiently. [465] Down here the layers cannot be revised by a frightened majority. [476] A flood wrote these records, and a flood does not flatter anyone. [488] The boy returned the next day with two friends, then four, each clutching some salvaged scrap they wanted explained. [507] I do not call this a school. [514] I call it maintenance. [518] Civilizations rot from the method outward, not the monument inward, and a single working cog can reseed a craft long after the cathedral falls. [542] Still I agonize, each night, over whether memory is a kindness. [553] The plague taught everyone that the past kills as easily as it instructs. [566] Perhaps the council is right that some knowledge is contagion. [576] But I weigh the cog in my palm and feel its honest, indifferent weight. [590] It cured no one and condemned no one; it only kept time. [602] Good methods make no promises, which is exactly why they survive their prophets. [615] So I keep digging through the sediment, keep teaching the children to wind the spring, and keep letting the louder stories rule the daylight. [639] Let the city above quarrel about why it drowned. [648] Down here, beneath the ruins, I am simply making sure the next sailors can find their way home. [666]</story>
