<story>The reclaimed silo library stood at the edge of a world that no longer slept, its concrete ribs humming with the cold after the last human dream was dreamt. [29] Imran the historian was odd in the ways that mattered, sensitively perceptive to the breath of old rooms, and he had come to collect the stories walls had heard. [58] He believed buildings remembered, that mortar kept the echo of every confession spoken beneath it. [73] The silo had once held grain, then ammunition, then nothing; now it held shelves spiraling upward into darkness like the inside of a shell. [97] When the last dreamer closed her eyes for good, the planet's stories had stopped arriving from the future and could only be gathered from the past. [123] Imran came to probe the silence the way a surgeon probes a wound, gently, listening for what still lived underneath. [143] At the silo's base he found a fireplace someone had built against regulation, its bricks scorched, and inside it lay a heap of half-burned diaries. [168] He knelt and lifted the brittle pages, reading through the half-burned diaries found in the fireplace as if turning the leaves of a dying tree. [193] The entries spoke of a keeper named Sela who had hidden here during the long unraveling, recording everything she heard the silo absorb. [216] One charred page described a fragment of a medieval tapestry showing dragons, a scrap Sela had nailed above the hearth for company. [238] Imran looked up and the fragment was still there, faded threads of crimson and gold where two dragons circled a tower that leaned at an impossible angle. [265] The diaries explained the tilt: Sela had believed the world suffered a displaced axis, that some ancient grief had knocked existence slightly off its center. [290] Every story leaned the same way, she wrote, every dream tilted toward the same forgotten point, and the dragons in the tapestry pointed there too. [315] Imran, sensitively perceptive, felt the floor's subtle slant beneath his knees and understood she had been right. [332] The whole silo tilted toward the hearth, toward the tapestry, as if the building itself wanted him to read what the dragons were guarding. [356] He continued to probe the unburned margins, piecing together Sela's final purpose. [368] She had been doing exactly what he now did, collecting the stories the walls had heard, hoping that if enough were gathered the axis might right itself. [395] A balanced world might dream again; that was the bronze-quiet faith stitched between her sentences. [410] The last legible page ended mid-word, scorched where she must have burned the rest to stay warm through a final winter. [431] Imran sat in the bronze quiet of the dying afternoon and felt the weight of every unfinished sentence pressing on the leaning shelves. [454] He took out his own notebook and began to copy her words, then the words the walls offered him in their slow, mineral voice. [478] They told of the grain that fed a starving town, the ammunition that ended a war nobody won, the keeper who refused to leave. [502] With each story he transcribed, the slant seemed to ease, the leaning tower in the tapestry straightening a hair's breadth. [522] He understood that the work would not finish in his lifetime, that no single historian could collect every story a world had buried. [545] But the displaced axis did not need righting all at once; it needed only the patient gathering, sentence by sentence, the way silt slowly fills a wound. [572] When the moon rose through the silo's broken cap, Imran nailed the dragon fragment to a higher shelf where the next keeper would find it. [597] He left the half-burned diaries open in the cold fireplace, an invitation to probe further. [612] Then he slept, and for the first time since the last human dream, something stirred behind his eyes, leaning gently back toward center. [635]</story>
