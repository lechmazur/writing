<story>In the prism sanctuary, starlight fractured through glass walls and scattered colors across the floor. [15] Mira knelt there, a broken mask restorer who had long ago lost the ability to mend her own scarred face. [35] Before her hung a centuries-old fisherman's net, its rope gone gray, each knot tied by hands the sea had taken. [55] The villagers swore the net accumulates silence, drawing the hush from every mouth that passed near it. [72] Mira had come to believe them. [78] Within those knots, she was certain, the drowned still lingered, restless, unable to lie still in their unmarked graves. [97] Her motivation was simple and stubborn: to ground these restless spirits before they pulled the living down with them. [116] She lifted her only tool, a slender tapestry needle her grandmother had used to weave funeral shrouds. [133] The method demanded patience: by unraveling the knots in the net, one by one, she would release what each had bound. [154] To do it, she needed a secret—a true one, spoken aloud. [165] The interlaced hush of the sanctuary, she had learned, only loosened when met with honesty offered freely. [182] So she began to speak, and as she spoke, her needle began to pick. [196] "My father drowned in this bay," she told the net, easing the first knot apart. [211] The rope sighed, and a thread of starlight slid through the gap like water finding a crack. [228] "I was meant to be with him that night," she continued, threading the needle beneath the second knot. [246] It gave way, and the air thinned, as though the room exhaled a breath held for decades. [263] Her hands stayed lucid and sure even as unrest climbed her spine; this was the bargain she had chosen. [282] "I broke his mask the morning he sailed," she whispered, and the confession trembled out of her. [299] He had worn a carved fisherman's mask for luck, and in a child's tantrum she had let it fall and shatter. [320] He sailed without it, and the sea kept him. [329] That was why she mended masks now—every restored face an apology she could never deliver. [344] The third knot opened beneath her needle, and the net shuddered along its whole length. [359] Now the prism sanctuary brightened, each pane catching starlight and throwing it back as soft, moving figures. [376] The spirits were rising, called up by her words, ready to be exhumed from the rope that held them. [395] She worked faster, untying knot after knot, naming her guilt with each one she loosened. [410] The hush that the net had hoarded for centuries unwound with the fibers, releasing voices long pressed flat. [428] She heard her father among them, not accusing, only tired. [438] "I forgive the morning," his voice seemed to say, "if you forgive yourself." [451] The last knot was the hardest, swollen with salt and time, and her needle bent against it. [468] She spoke her final truth: "I have been waiting my whole life to be allowed to grieve." [485] The knot dissolved, and the entire net came apart in her lap like spent ash. [500] All at once the figures of light bowed, settling downward through the floor toward the ground at last. [518] The restless spirits were grounded, and the sanctuary fell into a silence that was no longer heavy. [535] Mira sat among the loosened threads, her face wet, the tapestry needle still warm in her fingers. [552] She understood now that she had never been here to fix masks. [564] She had come to exhume herself, to lift her own buried sorrow into the open and let it breathe. [583] Outside, dawn had not yet come, but the starlight no longer trembled. [595] The interlaced hush remained, yet now it felt woven rather than imposed, a quiet she had earned. [612] She gathered the ruined net, broken at last like the masks she loved, and carried it toward the morning shore. [632] Her grief, finally shared, had become something she could lay down.
