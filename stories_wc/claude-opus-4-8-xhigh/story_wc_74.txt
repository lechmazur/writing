<story>Mira had not truly slept in nine years. [8] She painted instead, in a rooftop greenhouse perched above a smog-choked city, where the air tasted of rust and the stars had long since drowned. [33] Among the ferns and dust-coated glass, she worked under a waxing moon's glow, the only light the poisoned city could not extinguish. [55] Her insomnia had a purpose now, and the purpose was strange and patient: she was reconstructing lost languages. [73] Years ago, a calligrapher named Osei had pressed his moon-tipped brush into her hand at a crowded train station, asking only that she keep it moving. [99] He was a stranger; she never saw him again. [108] But that small kindness had rooted in her like a seed, and she had carried it through every sleepless night since. [129] The brush's tip, pale as bone, seemed to drink the moonlight. [140] When the silver light touched it, Mira's mind would rouse into daydreams sparked by moonlight, and the dead scripts would unfurl behind her eyes. [164] She saw the curling alphabet of a coastal people swallowed by the sea, the knotted glyphs of mountain shepherds, the brushwork of a city that no longer had a name. [194] She painted them onto the greenhouse panes, letter by letter, methodically hopeful that meaning could be coaxed back from oblivion. [214] Osei had told her, in his single soft sentence, that every language was a chain of remembered kindnesses, each word handed down by someone who chose to teach rather than forget. [245] That was her true motivation: to prove that strangers remember kindness, that nothing given freely is ever wholly lost. [264] The work moved slowly, and the city below never noticed the glow on its highest roof. [280] A saffron quiet filled the greenhouse, the hush of warm lamplight on cold glass, the scent of marigolds she grew to honor the dead tongues. [305] Some nights doubt crept in with the smog. [313] What good were words no living mouth could speak? [322] Then she would touch the moon-tipped brush to the glass, and the daydreams would return, gentle and certain. [340] On the night the moon swelled nearest to full, a knock came at the greenhouse door. [356] A young woman stood on the rooftop, shivering, holding a scrap of paper covered in symbols Mira recognized at once. [376] They were the coastal letters, the drowned alphabet. [384] The woman's grandmother had written the note before she died, and no one alive could read it. [401] She had heard, through the building's whispering tenants, of a sleepless painter who studied dead words on a roof. [420] Mira's hands trembled as she laid the scrap beside her painted panes and began, slowly, to translate. [437] The grandmother had written instructions for making marigold dye, and, at the very end, a line of love for a grandchild not yet born. [461] The young woman wept, reading her grandmother's tenderness for the first time. [473] Here was the proof Mira had hunted through countless sleepless nights: a stranger's gift, the calligrapher's brush, returning across decades to deliver another stranger's love. [498] Kindness, she understood at last, was the one language that never went extinct. [511] It needed no grammar and feared no sea; it simply passed from hand to hand. [526] She pressed the moon-tipped brush into the young woman's palm, just as Osei once had. [541] "Keep it moving," she said, and the woman nodded as though she understood the weight of it. [558] Outside, the waxing moon climbed higher over the smog, and for the first time in years, Mira felt the deep, honest pull of sleep. [582] She lay among the marigolds, methodically hopeful no longer, simply hopeful, and closed her eyes beneath the glowing glass. [601]</story>
