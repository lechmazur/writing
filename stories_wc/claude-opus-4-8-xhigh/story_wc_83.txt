<story>Mara was the only one who walked away from her own wedding. [12] The drone came at dusk, mistaking strings of paper lanterns for something worth erasing, and when the smoke thinned she stood alone among overturned chairs. [37] Survivors are supposed to feel grief; she felt instead an unfiltered awakening, a raw clarity that burned away every comfortable lie she had told herself. [62] She climbed, eventually, to the floating observatory, a brass-and-glass gondola that drifted above the cloud deck on hot ribbons of dawn. [83] There she became a calculator of accidents, a hunter of the moment when a thing might have gone otherwise. [102] Her obsession had a name: she wanted to find where unused potential pools. [115] Every life, she believed, leaked possibilities like water from cupped hands, and somewhere those abandoned futures had to collect. [134] The old astronomer who kept the observatory had left her one instrument worth the climb: a geode crystal the size of a skull, its outer rind dull as a stone, its inner cavity packed with violet teeth. [171] When she held it, the crystal felt intangibly tangible, present in her palm yet humming with a weight that no scale could measure. [194] It responded to nearness, brightening when she stood close to outcomes that had almost happened. [209] She learned to read it the only way she trusted: through perturbation theory. [222] She treated each ruined day as a stable equation and added tiny disturbances, solving for the small corrections that revealed what reality had nearly been. [247] The wedding became her favorite calculation, the strike a perturbation she ran a thousand times, watching the geode flicker toward the world where the drone banked left. [274] In that branch the lanterns burned harmlessly, the dancing went on, and her husband laughed with a mouth that still existed. [295] The hunt consumed her nights. [300] She tracked the pooled potential across the sky like a fisher reading current, the crystal pulling her gondola toward thunderheads where unspent lightning gathered. [324] There, she reasoned, the abandoned could be touched, the never-was made briefly real. [337] But the observatory ran on temperamental machinery, and her instruments drew power that the ancient panels could barely supply. [356] She could only work while the circuit breakers cool, those brief intervals after she tripped the load and the relays ticked, surrendering their heat to the thin air. [384] In those windows the geode opened fully, and the unused futures poured into the cabin like cold honey. [402] Tonight the breakers had snapped, and as the metal sighed she pressed the crystal to the glass. [419] The violet teeth flared, and the cabin filled with a thousand weddings that had not died. [435] She felt the bleak exhilaration of it, the joy of seeing the lost and the despair of knowing it was only arithmetic. [457] Her husband turned toward her, half-formed, a man assembled from corrected error. [469] "You keep solving me," he said, or the equation said. [479] "I'm bringing you back," she answered. [485] "You're hunting a place that only exists because I'm gone," he told her, and the truth of it stung worse than the original blast. [509] The crystal grew heavier, that impossible weight, as if all the pooled potential wanted finally to be spent. [527] She understood, then, the danger of the awakening she had welcomed: clear sight showed her she was not rescuing the dead but building a museum of her refusal to grieve. [557] The breakers ticked their last; the heat was nearly bled away; the window was closing. [572] She had one perturbation left to run. [579] She could solve for the branch where she let the lanterns stay only lanterns, where the wedding stayed in the past, where she climbed down from the observatory and lived. [609] The geode dimmed at the thought, mourning its own emptying. [619] Mara held the crystal against her chest and chose to spend the last of the pooled futures not on resurrection but on release. [642] The phantom husband smiled, complete for one heartbeat, and let himself dissolve. [654] The breakers warmed again as power returned, the moment gone, the equation closed. [667] In her hands the geode was just a stone, hollow and ordinary, its violet teeth dark. [683] For the first time since the dusk that erased her guests, Mara wept, and the weeping felt like the only solution that had ever balanced. [708] She opened the gondola's hatch to the cold blue morning and began the long, exhilarating descent toward a life that no theory could correct. [732]</story>
