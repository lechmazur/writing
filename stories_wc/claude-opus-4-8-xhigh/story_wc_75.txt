<story>Sera had guided strangers through the grotto city of Veyle for thirty years, her voice echoing off walls the monumental waterfall kept forever wet. [24] Behind that thundering curtain, lantern-lit galleries held the only museum a drowned people had left. [39] She was a cultured woman, historically oriented to her marrow, able to date a stitch or a varnish by candle-smell alone. [60] After the beekeeper's last visit, the honey ran out, and so did the city's small sweetness. [76] He had been the final tradesman willing to cross the spray, and his absence felt like a door quietly closing. [96] Into that silence came the displaced persons, two hundred souls fleeing a flooded valley upriver. [111] They arrived shivering, clutching bundles, their faces grey as the stone. [122] The council wanted them housed in the empty east galleries, among the cases of dying arts Sera had sworn to protect. [143] She feared their cold hands on her fragile looms and inkstones. [154] Yet she watched a child press her nose to a display and breathe a small fog of wonder, and something in Sera began to thaw. [179] The girl was staring at Sera's proudest relic: an emerald-tinted opera glass lens, the last survivor of Veyle's vanished glassblowers. [199] "Through that," Sera said, surprising herself, "the old singers watched operas no one performs anymore." [214] The lens threw a green coin of light across the child's cheek, soft as rosewater, and Sera felt the dread beneath her pride. [237] Her arts were not merely fragile; they were already half-ghost, kept alive by one aging woman's hands. [254] If she guarded them behind glass, they would simply die more slowly. [266] That night she could not sleep for the waterfall's roar and the cough of strangers in the dark. [284] She remembered her grandmother's kitchen, the one warm thing the flood had not reached in memory. [300] Her family had a custom of baking bread shaped like the eater's initials, so that no guest ever ate anonymous bread. [321] By dawn Sera had lit the museum's old ceremonial oven, the one she had only ever shown tourists as a curiosity. [342] She kneaded until her shoulders ached, then twisted the dough into letters she did not yet know. [359] She carried the steaming loaves into the east gallery and asked each frightened newcomer their name. [375] A man named Tomas received a crusted T; a woman named Ilse, a coiled I. [390] The displaced persons stared, then wept, because someone had bothered to learn the shape of them. [406] The thaw spread faster than Sera had dared hope. [415] She realized her dying arts had been starving not for visitors but for hands. [429] So she opened the cases. [434] She set the looms before the refugees and confessed she had forgotten how to thread them. [450] An old fisherman's wife laughed and showed her; weaving, it turned out, was her drowned village's craft. [467] A boy lifted the emerald-tinted opera glass lens and asked what songs it had watched. [482] Sera realized she could not answer, and that this was the true catastrophe. [495] So they decided to make new songs worth the watching. [505] Through the long wet winter the grotto filled with looms clacking, with bread shaped like every alphabet the valley had ever spoken. [527] The historically oriented guide began, for the first time, to record history being made rather than mourned. [544] She kept the green lens on her desk, no longer a relic but an instrument. [559] When spring came and the waterfall thinned with the mountain thaw, the council asked when the displaced persons would move on. [580] Sera answered that they had stopped being displaced the day they were given their initials in bread. [597] The museum, she said, was no longer a tomb for dying arts but a workshop, and a workshop needs people. [617] On the anniversary of the beekeeper's last visit, a girl climbed to the high gallery with the emerald lens. [636] She trained it on the waterfall, where evening light fractured green and gold. [649] Below her, two hundred voices rehearsed an opera nobody had ever heard, composed from flood-songs and kitchen rhymes. [667] Sera watched the rosewater dread of those first weeks dissolve into something braver. [680] She had set out to rescue the past and had, almost by accident, rescued the future. [696] The bread cooled on the windowsills, spelling the names of the living. [708] And the waterfall, ancient and indifferent, kept its silver curtain drawn around the only home that needed it.
