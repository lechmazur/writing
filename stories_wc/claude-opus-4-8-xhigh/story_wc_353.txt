<story>The silence came to the siskin alder grove the way frost comes, without announcement. [14] Years before, the cities had emptied and the great machines had wound down, until even the radios forgot their hum. [34] Mira waited in the grove each dawn, a tree-song sculptor in a world that had forgotten how to ring. [53] She had been symphonically minded since childhood, hearing chords in the way light fell across bark. [69] She had come here to create music in a silent world, the only inheritance her grandmother had left her. [88] Years ago, the old woman had carved living alders so that wind would draw melodies from their hollows. [106] Now those sculptures stood mute, and Mira knelt before the largest, a long-forgotten figure cracked along its spine. [124] She pressed her palm to it and read the stress cracks the way others read sheet music. [141] Each fracture told her where the wood had once flexed, where tension had sung, where the grain had held its breath. [162] By analyzing those cracks, she learned the sculpture's lost tuning, the intervals her grandmother had buried in the timber. [181] The work was slow, and grief sat with her, patient as the cold. [194] She had buried her grandmother under these very roots and had not heard a single song since. [211] A hedgehog had curled in the roots through the long afternoon, a tight bristled fist against the quiet. [229] Mira would not begin until it stirred; the grove demanded that kind of patience. [243] She believed that song was empathy in motion, a feeling carried outward until another creature felt it move through them. [263] To play before the small animal woke would be to speak over someone sleeping. [277] So she worked in intensely quiet hours, stringing wire across the alder's hollow throat. [291] For an anchor she used a porcelain insulator salvaged from the dead power lines beyond the grove. [308] The smooth white knob had once held current through screaming wires; now it would hold a single trembling string. [327] She fixed it into the crack her grandmother's hands had shaped, and the fit was perfect, as though the wood had been waiting. [350] Dusk thickened, and the siskins settled into the alders without a note. [362] Then, after the hedgehog uncurled and unfolded its small dark face toward the cooling air, Mira finally breathed. [380] She set her fingers to the wire and began to strum. [391] The first sound was barely a sound, more a pressure against the silence than a break in it. [409] But the insulator gathered the vibration, and the cracked alder answered, releasing a low chord her grandmother had hidden there decades before. [431] The stress cracks she had studied now opened like tiny mouths, and the wind moved through them, harmonizing. [449] It was not loud; nothing in that grove was ever loud again. [461] Yet the hedgehog lifted its nose, and the siskins turned their heads, and Mira understood that they felt it the way she did. [484] The music traveled not as noise but as motion, a shiver passing creature to creature, empathy made audible. [502] Somewhere beyond the trees, a thrush that had been mute for years tried a single answering note. [519] She played the tuning she had recovered, completing the symphony her grandmother had only begun. [534] Her symphonically minded heart arranged each gust into counterpoint, the alders as strings, the porcelain as the keystone of it all. [555] For the first time since the world fell quiet, she was not alone in her listening. [571] The silent world did not end that evening, but it cracked, gently, like the sculpture had. [587] And through that crack, song returned, soft enough that you had to love it to hear it. [604]</story>
