<story>The bell tuner arrived in Hollow Marrow before the collapse, when the town was already a corpse of itself. [19] No one had walked its cobblestones in forty years, and the silence there was a wild hush, vast and listening. [39] He was a cryptic man, speaking in half-riddles, and he carried only an ebony tuning fork wrapped in gray cloth. [59] I followed him because my grandmother had been born here, beneath the ancient archway that still framed the dead market square. [80] She used to say the town had a heartbeat once, a rhythm shared by every soul, until grief knocked it out of time. [103] The tuner said he had come to restore that shared balance, to rebuild what was broken not with stone but with sound. [125] I asked him how, and he only smiled and struck the ebony tuning fork against the cold archway. [143] A low note bloomed, darker than any bell, and the empty windows seemed to lean toward it. [160] Listen, he whispered, the town forgot how to walk together. [170] He explained that a living place is rebuilt via the rhythm of footsteps on cobblestones, not by hammers or coins. [190] So we began to walk. [195] Each dawn he struck the fork, took its pitch into his bones, and matched his pace to it across the square. [216] He was infinitely patient, never hurrying, repeating the same slow steps day after day after day. [232] My feet learned the cadence until I no longer counted it; it simply lived in me. [248] On the ninth morning, an old fisherman came down from the hills, drawn by the steady tapping of our heels. [268] He fell into step without a word, and the rhythm grew louder. [280] The next day two sisters arrived, then a widower, then a family with three small children. [296] They had all once belonged to Hollow Marrow, scattered after the grief, and the sound called them home. [314] Soon a hundred feet pressed the cobblestones in one shared beat, and I understood the tuner at last. [332] He had not come to fill the town with bodies, but to give the survivors back their common pulse. [351] With each matched footstep, the wild hush softened into something almost like song. [364] Doors that had been shut for decades creaked open, and lamps were lit in the long evenings. [381] The square smelled of bread again, and the market stalls rose plank by plank beneath the ancient archway. [399] Still the tuner walked his patient circle, holding the ebony tuning fork against his heart like a compass. [417] I asked why he labored so long for strangers, and for the first time he answered plainly. [434] Before the collapse, he said, I lived in a town like this, and I let its rhythm fall apart. [453] I have been walking ever since, tuning the silence of dead places back toward life. [468] His penance was patience, and his instrument the simple music of belonging. [480] When Hollow Marrow finally hummed on its own, he wrapped the fork in gray cloth and turned to leave. [499] I begged him to stay, but he shook his head and pointed at our feet. [514] The rhythm is yours now, he said; guard it, or you will need me again. [529] He walked out beneath the ancient archway, his steps fading into the hills. [542] We did not let the beat die. [549] Every morning we still gather on the cobblestones and walk together, to restore the shared balance he taught us to keep. [570] And sometimes, in the deep quiet, I imagine I hear an ebony note answering from far away, patient and waiting. [590] The town breathes once more, kept alive by nothing but the rhythm of footsteps on old stone. [607]</story>
