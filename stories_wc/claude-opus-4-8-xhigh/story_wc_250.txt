<story>The canal guardian had never spoken a word, and the village had long stopped expecting one. [16] Each dusk she walked the cobalt dock after rain, where the wet stones glowed like spilled ink under a bruised sky. [37] In her palm she kept a dental mirror, a small circle of silver scavenged from a drowned dentist's bag, and it was the only treasure she owned. [64] The town feared the dark that pooled in the canals, certain it swallowed children whole. [79] She had taken it upon herself to teach the darkness the value of stars. [93] When the first true star pricked the sky, she lifted the dental mirror and angled it just so. [111] A thread of light leapt from the silver and landed in the black water, and there it trembled, alive. [130] Behind her the old harbor house waited with its many shutters, and these were her loom. [146] She moved among them with spiralingly calm hands, opening one, closing another, creating patterns with shutters that broke and braided the starlight. [168] Slat by slat she began to weave the light across the dock, until the cobalt stones wore a quilt of pale constellations. [190] It was within the edge of a half-spoken secret that a boy crept onto the planks. [206] His mouth had opened on something and then closed, the words half born, half drowned. [221] He was the dredger's son, and everyone whispered that he had seen what dragged the missing under. [238] The guardian did not turn to him with questions, because she had none to give. [253] Instead she pressed the dental mirror into his hand and folded his fingers over it. [268] That was her whole language, this silent empathy passed palm to palm like a shared warmth. [284] The boy understood that he need not say the thing he could not say. [298] A stillwater tension held the dock, the kind that hums just before something finally breaks gently. [314] He lifted the small mirror toward the deepest, darkest pool, where the canal seemed to have no floor. [332] He caught a single star and dropped it into that throat of black. [345] The darkness, the guardian had always believed, was not cruel but merely uninstructed. [358] It had never been shown that it could hold light instead of swallowing it. [372] Together they worked the shutters now, the boy clumsy, the guardian sure, both weaving. [386] Stripes of brightness laddered down the cobalt dock and slid into the water like spilled coins. [402] And the darkness, gathering all those reflections, began at last to glitter rather than gape. [417] The boy's shoulders loosened, and the half-spoken secret loosened with them, no longer a stone in his chest. [435] He did not need to confess what he had seen, only to learn it could be outshone. [452] The guardian watched his face and felt the old ache she had carried since her own silence began. [470] Years ago a flood had taken her voice and her family in one cold breath. [485] She had stayed to guard the canal so no other child would meet the dark unprepared. [501] Now, watching the boy's wonder, she knew her long vigil had finally found its purpose. [516] When the boy finally tried to return the dental mirror, she shook her head once. [531] The work of teaching darkness was too large for one pair of spiralingly calm hands. [546] He would need it for the nights she could no longer keep. [558] The boy nodded, and in that nod a whole conversation passed between them, wordless and complete. [574] The rain returned softly, dimpling the canal, but the woven stars held fast on the surface. [590] And the dark, for the first time in the boy's memory, looked almost like a sky. [606]</story>
