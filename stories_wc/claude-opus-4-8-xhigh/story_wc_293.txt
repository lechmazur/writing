[102] Now she tried to recount his words exactly, because she sensed they were all she would keep of him. [173] He had accepted it to escape a sentence of 'never' - never trusted, never employed, never home. [223] So he had made this flake the old way, wet-plate collodion poured and fixed by hand, because pixels could be erased but glass remembered. [334] Vanished, leaving only the glass and the song still warm in her hands. [347] Now, alone above the valley, she tilted the flake until the painted light moved across its scaffold folds. [365] The flutters were not random; they rose and fell in a rhythm she already knew. [380] It was the bridge-song itself, the melody she had played him, the cadence of crossings, mapped into fabric and frozen in silver. [402] He had built the secret from her own music, trusting that she, of all people, would recognize the tune and follow it. [424] Each long flutter was a held note; each ripple, a footbridge between cities; the whole map a song she could play and thereby walk. [448] The disbelief in her chest turned luminous, almost unbearable, a brightness that felt like grief and hope wearing the same face. [469] She could keep driving and forget him, as the amnesty wanted everyone to forget. [483] Or she could recount his vanishing to the archive keepers, play them the fluttering bridge-song, and demand they return one blurred man his name. [507] The pullout wind lifted the loose canvas of a roadwork barrier nearby, and it fluttered exactly like the scaffold in the glass, as if the world were repeating his instruction. [537] She set the flake on the dashboard where it would catch the morning, and lifted her fiddle to her chin. [557] Watching the wrap flutter, dynamically observant, she translated each fold into a note until the map became a melody she could carry across any border. [582] The bridge-song rose over the mountain scenic pullout, no longer a tune for crossing gorges but for crossing erasure itself. [602] She would find the archive, recount what the amnesty had taken, and play until a face surfaced from the pixels like a swimmer breaking water. [627] From the ashes of 'never' she meant to sculpt one stubborn possibility: that no one need vanish to be forgiven.
