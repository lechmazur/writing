<story>After the first snowmelt, the Fractal Weaver descended into the bronze grotto, where starlight pooled like cold honey on hammered walls. [21] Her name was Sela, and her hands had learned long ago to thread silence the way others threaded wool. [40] She had come to collect the weights of different burdens, for that was the only craft her people had ever trusted her to keep. [64] In the grotto's center stood a single kerosene lamp burner, unlit, its brass mouth waiting like a held breath. [83] Sela knelt before it and listened to the shingled hush, that layered silence which lay over the chamber in overlapping rows, each quiet sliding beneath the next like roof tiles against rain. [115] To begin the work, she had to light the burner, but flame here obeyed no match. [131] The grotto demanded a stranger logic: the burner kindled only by orienting properly, its wick angled toward the faint northern star that bled through a seam in the bronze. [160] She turned the small wheel, tilting the burner a hair's width at a time, until the wick caught the starlight and bloomed into a thin blue thread of fire. [189] The flame did not warm the air; instead it drew the burdens out of the stone. [205] They rose as threads, gray and shimmering, each one humming with the weight of a life that had set its sorrow down in this place. [230] Sela wove them on a loom of her own ribs, breathing the strands into a fractal lace that branched and branched again, every knot a smaller copy of the whole. [260] The first burden was a fisherman's grief, heavy as a wet net, and it sang to her in a whalebone reverie of tides and the long dead. [287] She let the song move through her without keeping it, for a weaver who holds a sorrow becomes the sorrow. [307] The second was lighter, a child's unspoken apology, and it threaded easily into the lace beside the fisherman's salt. [326] But the third burden resisted. [331] It was the weight of someone who had felt boundary-lessly trapped, a soul caged not by walls but by the absence of any edge to push against. [358] That formlessness was the worst prison of all, Sela knew, for you cannot climb out of a cell that has no shape. [380] The thread of it writhed against her fingers, refusing to take a knot, threatening to calcify into a hard white bead that would lock the whole weave still. [408] If even one strand calcified, the loom of her ribs would harden too, and she would become another statue in the grotto's growing congregation of the petrified. [435] Sela understood then why the burner had to be oriented by the star and not by any human hand. [454] The trapped soul needed a direction, a single fixed point outside its boundless cage, the way the flame needed the north. [475] So she lifted the writhing thread toward the seam of starlight and let it see the one far light it could orient against. [498] The strand shuddered, then softened, and at last it bent into a knot, finding its edge in the only boundary that mattered: a distance to long for. [525] The shingled hush thickened, layer upon layer, as the last weights settled into the lace. [540] When the weaving was done, Sela held a shawl of fractal silence, every burden present and none of them hers. [560] She draped it over the cold burner, and the blue flame guttered low, satisfied. [574] Outside, the thawed streams carried the season forward, and the grotto returned to its patient dark. [590] Sela climbed back toward the surface, lighter for having carried so much, leaving behind a place where sorrows could finally rest without turning to stone. [615]</story>
