<story>Sael was the last harmonic stone skimmer, and she revered every grain of the past. [15] Her home was the canyon of endless echoes, where towers of bone rose like the ribs of forgotten giants. [34] Through centuries of layered dust, her people had skimmed flat stones across the still pools below, listening for the chords each throw released. [57] The sound would climb the bone towers, doubling and folding, until a single skip became a hymn. [74] But Sael had stopped throwing. [79] Her legs had failed her years ago, and the pools lay far beneath her ledge. [94] So she set herself a harder craft: to learn to navigate the canyon without moving at all. [111] The elders had spoken of a pulse left wandering, the first chord ever skimmed, loosed long before the dust began to settle. [133] It had never found a wall to die against, and so it still traveled, threading the towers, a heartbeat without a body. [155] If she could hear it, she could map the entire canyon from her ledge. [169] To find it, she watched the birds. [176] Each dawn a flock rose from the bone spires, and Sael read the canyon's secrets through the patterns in their flight. [197] The pulse bent the air, and the birds bent with it, curving where no wind blew, scattering where the old chord crossed itself. [220] By their turning she learned the shape of sound she could not yet hear. [234] For this she needed an instrument worthy of the task, and she had only one. [249] Beneath her hand lay a stone arrowhead, chipped by an ancestor whose name the dust had eaten. [266] It was the oldest thing she owned, and she would appraise it now not for its edge but for its voice. [287] She held it to the light, weighing its grain, its fractures, the way it hummed when she breathed across its face. [308] A skimmer's truest stone was never round; it was sharp enough to cut the air into notes. [325] She set the arrowhead on her knee and waited, watching the flock wheel against the pale sky. [342] They folded inward, then snapped sideways, three times in the same place above the third tower. [358] There, she understood, the wandering pulse still struck the bone and rebounded. [370] She raised the arrowhead and turned its point until its hum matched the birds' silent arithmetic. [386] The stone trembled, drinking the distant chord, and at last she heard it: faint, patient, older than memory. [404] The pulse moved through her like a tide finding an old channel. [416] She did not throw the arrowhead. [422] She did not need to. [427] By holding it steady against the canyon's breath, she had become the throw, the still point the sound passed through. [447] Below her, the pools shivered, though no stone had touched them. [458] The bone towers answered, ringing softly, as if the giants remembered being alive. [471] Sael wept without sorrow, the way the weathered cliffs weep dust. [482] She had honored every skimmer before her, and now she had carried their craft somewhere none had gone. [500] Movement had always been their gift; stillness would be hers. [510] For days she sat, reading the birds, tuning the arrowhead, letting the wandering pulse pass and return. [527] Each circuit taught her another corner of the canyon, another tower she would never climb. [542] She learned the depth of the pools, the cracks in the spires, the hollow where the wind slept. [560] The map grew inside her, drawn in sound, needing no single step. [572] When the cold came and her breath thinned, she did not fear it. [585] She had already traveled the whole canyon a thousand times without rising. [597] She pressed the arrowhead to her chest and let her own heartbeat join the wandering chord. [613] When it stopped, the pulse did not. [620] It gathered her last small skip into its endless round, carried it up the towers of bone, and set it loose among the centuries of dust. [646] Somewhere, a flock turned in a way it never had before. [657] And the canyon, reverent and patient, kept her voice wandering with all the rest. [671]</story>
