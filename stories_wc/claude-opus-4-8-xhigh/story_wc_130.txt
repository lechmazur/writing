<story>The cavern orchard slept beneath the moon, its apple trees nourished by stalactites that dripped patient silver onto the roots. [20] Mira waited until the orchard grew silent, until the last leaf stopped trembling, before she began her work. [38] She was a secretive conjurer, gently inventive, who kept her purposes folded like letters she would never send. [56] In her palm rested a taxidermist's glass eye pair, two amber spheres that once stared from some museum's stuffed owl. [76] They had belonged to her brother, Tomas, the taxidermist, who had vanished three winters ago. [91] Vanished was the wrong word, perhaps, for no one knew if he had died or simply walked elsewhere. [109] That ambiguous loss lived inside her like a held breath, neither grief nor hope, only an aching arithmetic. [127] Mira's motivation was strange and stubborn: to understand the mathematics of emotion, to weigh longing as one weighs flour. [146] She believed that if she could measure her sorrow exactly, she could finally set it down. [162] At the orchard's center lay a mosaic stepping stone, ancient and cracked, its tiles forming spirals no architect had planned. [182] Mira had learned that the conjuring worked only via the chipped patterns on the mosaic stepping stone, where the breaks made a language older than grief. [208] She knelt and set the two glass eyes into a pair of chips that seemed shaped to receive them. [227] They settled with a soft click, beginning to dovetail into the fractures as if the stone had been waiting decades for exactly these pieces. [251] Light bloomed upward through the cracks, drawing the dripping stalactites into faint music. [264] The glass eyes glowed, and in their amber depths Mira saw not a ghost but a graph. [281] Lines of light arced from tile to tile, plotting her love for Tomas against the years of his absence. [300] The curve did not fall to zero, as grief should; it leveled into a gentle, stubborn plateau. [317] Mira traced the shape with one finger and understood its meaning at last. [330] The mathematics of emotion did not resolve into an answer; it resolved into a constant. [345] Love, the graph insisted, was not a quantity that emptied but one that simply remained. [360] She had wanted a number to subtract, a way to be free of the weight. [375] Instead the stone offered something kinder and harder: the weight was not punishment but proof. [390] Whether Tomas lived or had become only these two glass eyes, the constant did not change. [406] The ambiguity she had feared became, somehow, a place she could stand. [418] Around her the orchard answered, apples brightening as though the dripping water had carried the light into their skins. [437] Mira gathered the glass eyes back into her palm, and the graph dimmed to memory. [452] She would keep them, not as relics of mourning but as instruments of a quiet, ongoing arithmetic. [469] Perhaps Tomas would return through some door she could not see, and perhaps he never would. [485] Either way, the secretive conjurer had what she came for, though it was not the relief she had imagined. [504] She had imagined an ending; she had been given a continuation. [515] The stalactites dripped their slow blessing, marking time the way her heart did, steadily, without conclusion. [531] She rose, tucked the eyes against her chest, and walked the spiraling tiles toward the cavern's mouth. [548] Behind her the orchard settled again into its moonlit hush, patient as ever. [561] Mira smiled, a small and unexpected thing, the buried optimism of someone who had stopped waiting for proof and learned to live inside the question. [586] The chipped patterns would be there tomorrow, and the night after, holding their gentle equation. [601] And whatever the answer, she would keep counting, because the counting itself was a kind of love that refused to end. [622]</story>
