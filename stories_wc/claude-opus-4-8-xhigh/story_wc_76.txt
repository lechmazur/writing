Mirella built her clocks to run counterclockwise, hands sweeping the wrong way like memories rewinding. [15] Townsfolk called her cursed; she called herself neatly inventive, each gear filed and seated with a surgeon's patience. [33] When her mother died, leaving only a riddle scratched on slate, Mirella followed it to the mysterious lighthouse on a rocky island the fishermen refused to name. [60] They warned that a hunger stone lay at its base, an old drought-marker carved with the line: if you see me, weep. [82] Mirella reached it under the slow exhale of low tide, when the sea drew back its gray sheets and bared the stone's grim promise. [106] She did not weep. [110] Instead she climbed the spiral stair, lantern hissing, to a room where her mother had clearly lived and worked. [129] On the workbench rested an ancient yoyo, its lacquer cracked, its string knotted in a pattern too deliberate to be accident. [150] A note beneath it read: this is the lock, and you, my backward girl, are the key. [167] Mirella understood at once that she must solve it through reverse-engineering the puzzle of the ancient yoyo, unwinding what her mother had wound. [190] She studied each loop, mapped each axle, and counted the hidden gears nested inside the wooden halves. [207] The toy, she realized, was a clock disguised, and like all her own creations it was meant to turn the wrong way. [229] Working backward, she retied the string counterclockwise, reversed the escapement, and let the yoyo fall. [244] It spun at the string's end, humming, and a latent radiance woke inside the lamp room, a glow that had slept in the glass for decades. [270] The lighthouse, she finally saw, had never guided ships. [279] It was an engine for bending time, and her mother had built it to initiate one who could run against the clock. [301] The note's last lines explained the price: the radiance could carry a single soul backward, but the carrier could never return forward again. [324] Her mother had died because she had tested it once, and the going-back had unmade her, year by year, until nothing remained but slate and string. [350] Mirella felt the old bleakness settle, yet beneath it bloomed a fierce exhilaration she could not deny. [367] All her life she had run counter to everyone, and now the chance had come to step beyond fate's design entirely. [388] Below, the hunger stone waited for the tide's return, ready to drown its warning once more. [404] Mirella had perhaps an hour before the sea swallowed the path and the choice with it. [420] She could leave, marry the future the village had measured out for her, and let the stone keep its grief. [440] Or she could ride the radiance back to the morning her mother left, and warn her, and unmake the loss before it began. [463] The cost would be herself, dissolved slowly into a past she could touch but never keep. [479] Mirella wound the yoyo backward one final time, her hands steady, her breath matching the slow exhale of the retreating sea. [500] The radiance flared white, and the room filled with the smell of brine and burning lacquer. [516] She thought of every clock she had ever set against the grain of time, and smiled. [532] Fate had drawn a straight line for her; she intended to spool it the other direction. [548] As the light took her, Mirella stepped off the edge of her own story and into the unwritten current beneath it. [569] Outside, the tide turned, the hunger stone vanished beneath the water, and the lighthouse went dark, its purpose at last fulfilled. [590] Somewhere in a vanishing past, a backward clockmaker reached for her mother's hand and began the long, bright unwinding of her fate. [612]
