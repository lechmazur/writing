<story>After time became visible, Anselm walked the baroque asteroid alone. [10] Once a revolutionary, now defunct, he wore his old sash like a bandage over a wound that had stopped bleeding decades ago. [32] The revolution he had led was rubble now, scattered across the carved cornices and gilded ruins that floated in the asteroid's thin, perpetual dusk. [56] Since time turned visible, it hung in the corridors as slow amber threads, and one could watch an afternoon thicken like resin. [78] Anselm, poetically inclined even in defeat, called these threads "the handwriting of the dead." [92] In his palm he carried a single crystal vase shard, the last relic of the civilization his uprising had toppled. [112] Now the new historians arrived, and with them an archaeological dispute that gnawed at him. [127] They argued whether the shard belonged to a temple or a tyrant's banquet hall, whether the old society had been cruel or merely misremembered. [151] Each faction wanted to claim the shard, to bend its meaning toward their thesis. [165] Anselm listened with burnished sorrow, the way a man listens to strangers quarrel over the body of someone he once loved. [186] He had burned that civilization to free it, and now he feared he had erased it instead. [203] So he resolved to devise a different kind of record, one no committee could win. [218] His plan grew from an old custom of the toppled people: covering mirrors during storms. [233] They had believed that when ion storms lashed the asteroid, an uncovered mirror could trap a soul and hold it forever in the glass. [257] Anselm had mocked the ritual as superstition in his fiery youth. [268] Now, with time made visible, he understood it differently. [277] The next storm came, electric and howling, and the amber threads of time tangled into bright knots. [294] Anselm moved through the palace covering every mirror, exactly as the lost people once had. [309] But he left a single mirror bare, and against it he pressed the crystal vase shard. [325] Where the storm light struck the glass and the shard together, the trapped time unspooled. [340] In the mirror he saw not himself but them: potters singing, children chasing light, lovers arguing gently in a language he had tried to silence. [365] The vase had not belonged to a temple or a tyrant; it had held wildflowers on a kitchen sill. [384] The historians were both wrong, because both had asked the smaller question. [396] Anselm wept, and his weeping was the first honest history written about that world. [410] When the storm passed, he did not give the historians their answer. [422] Instead, using the bare mirror and the shard, he began to reimagine an entire civilization. [437] Storm after storm, he summoned its faces into the glass and copied them into a great book of verse. [456] He was poetically inclined, and so he gave the dead back their mornings, their quarrels, their small festivals of rain. [476] The archaeologists raged that this was invention, not evidence. [485] Anselm only answered that a people deserved to be remembered whole, not catalogued in fragments. [500] His verses spread through the gilded corridors, and travelers began to come, not to dispute, but to grieve. [518] The defunct revolutionary became, at last, a keeper of the very thing he had once destroyed. [534] On his final storm, Anselm covered every mirror but the bare one, then pressed the shard to the glass a last time. [556] He stepped into the unspooling light and let it take him among the singing potters. [571] When the historians found the room, the mirror was empty, the shard was whole again, and a finished book lay open on the floor. [595] They read it in the visible amber of falling time, and for once, no one argued. [611]</story>
