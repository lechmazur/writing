Marsh loved the 6:14 commuter ferry the way other men loved sunrise. [12] As an ardent ferry deckhand, he greeted every passenger by name, certain that kindness mattered even on a vessel that never truly stopped moving. [36] His friends called him resiliently positive; he called himself permanently in transit, never arriving, never quite gone. [53] He kept an empty hourglass in his coat, a gift from a passenger who vanished one foggy autumn. [71] She had whispered that when the sand was gone, the clocks would run sideways, and he should not be afraid. [91] On the night it happened, the ferry slid into a fog bank that should not have existed. [108] The harbor lights smeared, then dissolved, and the commuter ferry simply vanished from the world it knew. [125] When the mist parted, Marsh found the boat resting inside a spectral grotto under starlight, water glowing pale as memory. [145] Above the cavern, stalactites hung like frozen pendulums, and every clock on board spun sideways, hands sweeping toward horizons instead of hours. [167] The passengers slept, suspended between heartbeats, but Marsh stayed awake because the empty hourglass in his pocket had finally turned warm. [188] A voice crackled through the pilothouse radio, and he recognized the vanished passenger, speaking from somewhere folded just beside the present. [209] She explained that the grotto was a seam between parallel timelines, and that every commuter who vanished here belonged to one half of a broken pair. [235] To free them, Marsh would have to merge the parallel timelines before the sideways clocks completed a single impossible turn. [255] The method, she said, was hidden in interconnected patterns: the rhythm of the dripping water, the constellations overhead, the names he had memorized. [278] Marsh, ever resiliently positive, decided that a deckhand who knew every passenger could surely learn the song the universe was humming. [299] He listened to the water dripping in threes, matched it to a triangle of stars, and called those stars by passenger names through the radio. [324] With each correct pairing, a stalactite chimed, and a sleeping commuter exhaled, as though one timeline reached across to clasp its twin. [346] The interconnected patterns tightened, sound answering light, name answering name, until the grotto rang like an instrument tuned by longing. [366] But the sideways clocks were nearly closed, their wandering hands curling back toward a center that would seal both worlds apart forever. [388] One pattern remained unsolved, and the empty hourglass blazed, demanding the final pairing he feared to make. [405] The last star had no passenger name, because the missing half was the deckhand himself. [420] Marsh understood then that he existed in both timelines, a man permanently transient, belonging fully to neither. [437] To merge the worlds, he had to speak his own name into the radio and let the two halves fall together. [458] He smiled, because surrendering had never frightened a man who lived between arrivals. [471] "Marsh," he said, and the empty hourglass filled instantly with light instead of sand. [485] The grotto folded; the sideways clocks lurched upright; the two ferries became one. [498] Passengers woke in the ordinary harbor, certain they had only dozed, while the radio fell silent and the starlight drained back into morning. [521] They never knew the commuter ferry had vanished, nor that a single deckhand had stitched two skies together via interconnected patterns of sound and light. [546] Marsh remained on deck, resiliently positive, greeting each face by name as though no merging had ever cost him anything. [566] Only the hourglass had changed, no longer empty, its light pulsing softly in his pocket like a second heart. [585] He understood he would never fully arrive and never fully leave, forever a man of the in-between. [602] And as the 6:14 ferry pushed out beneath an ordinary sun, Marsh laughed, content to be permanently transient, sailing between worlds he alone remembered. [626]
