<story>Mara turned the great hourglass at dawn, as she had every dawn for forty years. [15] She was the hourglass turner of the amber warehouse beneath the cliffs, keeper of its honeyed, unearthed silence. [33] The warehouse held forgotten things in resin-colored light: crates of yellowed letters, broken clocks, and a single jar that contained a canyon echo. [56] When she pressed her ear to its lid, she heard the long, returning voice of the gorge where her grandmother once shouted prophecies into the wind. [82] Her grandmother's final prophecy had arrived scrambled, its words tumbled like dice. [94] Mara had spent decades trying to unscramble it, certain that within the jumble lay something worth saving. [111] Her method was strange but stubborn: she worked by cataloging lost buttons. [123] Travelers who lost their way among the cliffs always left a button behind, snagged on splintered crates, and Mara believed each one carried a syllable of the broken prophecy. [152] Bone buttons, brass buttons, buttons of pearl and horn, each labeled, sorted, and entered into her ledger. [169] She arranged them by weight, then color, then by the sound they made when dropped, listening for the order the prophecy demanded. [191] She had never wanted certainty; she wanted to spin the prophecy into raw possibility, to set its fixed doom loose into open air. [214] One evening a boy stumbled into the warehouse, soaked from the cliffside rain, missing a button from his coat. [233] He watched Mara turn the hourglass and asked why she kept time for an empty building. [249] She told him about the jar, the gorge, and the scrambled words she could not finish alone. [266] The boy, quietly inspired, knelt beside her ledger and began reading the buttons aloud. [280] His voice was young and unhurried, and it filled the warehouse's deep, unearthed silence with something almost like music. [299] Where Mara heard only fragments, the boy heard pattern, and where she saw doom, he saw doors. [316] Together they laid the buttons in a new sequence across the amber floor. [329] She turned the hourglass once more, and as the last sand fell, the jar's echo rose to meet their voices. [349] The gorge spoke back, and the syllables aligned, and the prophecy began to settle into shape as the truth unraveled before them. [371] It was not a sentence of fate after all, but an invitation: the future was unfinished, waiting to be chosen. [391] Mara understood then why no single turner could ever read it; the prophecy was built for shared wisdom. [409] One voice held the question, and another the answer, and only together did they unscramble the whole. [426] Her grandmother had not left a warning; she had left a door, sealed until two strangers learned to listen at once. [447] The boy looked up, eyes bright in the amber haze. [457] He asked what they should do with such raw possibility now that it lay open between them. [474] Mara smiled, the first smile in many turns of sand. [484] She lifted the jar and carried it to the warehouse mouth, where the cliffs leaned toward the sky. [502] She unsealed the lid and let the trapped echo pour out into the living canyon. [517] The old prophecy returned to the wind, no longer hoarded, no longer fixed, free to be answered by anyone willing to listen. [539] The boy added his missing button to her ledger, the final entry, the syllable that had completed the sequence. [558] Mara realized the lost travelers had never failed to find their way; each had carried a piece of the door's key. [579] She had simply been gathering, across forty patient years, the scattered wisdom of everyone who had ever passed through. [598] Now, at last, the warehouse beneath the cliffs would no longer keep its silence. [612] Mara turned the hourglass one final time and set it on its side, letting the sand rest. [629] Time, like the prophecy, belonged to no one alone. [638] Together they walked out into the bright, unwritten morning. [647]</story>
