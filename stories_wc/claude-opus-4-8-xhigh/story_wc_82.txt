<story>Sennel was a crafty lantern maker, and grief had taught her to fold light into paper. [16] On the floating island tea garden where she now lived, she folded origami cranes from sheets that glowed. [34] Each crane held a recorded dream, captured the way her dead son had taught her before the fever took him. [54] He had loved the dream recording lanterns most of all, the ones that drank sleeping minds and replayed them as drifting color. [76] Now she folded his last recorded dream into a thousand cranes, hoping the act would transform her sorrow into something she could finally release. [100] The tea garden floated beneath the glass basilica on a floating isle, a cathedral of polished panes that hung above her like a frozen sunrise. [125] The monks there practiced gem polishing, grinding sorrow-stones until grief became light enough to set free. [141] They had promised Sennel that if she climbed the basilica's spiral, she could pour her son's final dream into their oldest gem and let it shine forever. [168] But the climb was strange, for the spiral descends while ascending, looping inward so that each upward step sank her deeper into memory. [191] She began at dusk, just after the shadow detaches. [200] On these isles, every evening, each person's shadow detaches and wanders freely until dawn, leaving the living temporarily unburdened. [219] Sennel always climbed in that grace, when her grief stood apart from her like a dark companion. [236] Carrying her lantern of cranes, she stepped onto the spiral and felt the floor tilt the wrong way. [254] Up she walked, yet downward she fell, the glass walls bending her reflection into the boy she had lost. [273] This was the basilica's luminous torment: it offered radiance only by drowning the climber in everything they wished to keep. [293] Each crane's recorded dream flickered against the panes, showing her son laughing, sleeping, asking for one more lantern. [311] She wept, but she did not stop, because stopping would mean folding nothing more, and the cranes were all that kept him near. [334] At the summit, which was also the lowest point, the old gem rested on its pedestal, dull and hungry to be filled. [356] A monk handed her a polishing cloth and explained the final price. [368] To set the dream into the gem, she must polish it with her own grief, and once polished, the dream would shine for strangers but fade from her. [396] She would lose her last memory of him to give it to the world. [410] Sennel understood then why the spiral descends while ascending; to rise into light, one must sink past everything precious. [429] She pressed the cloth to the gem and began to polish. [440] With each stroke, a crane in her lantern unfolded itself, releasing its recorded dream into the stone. [457] The gem brightened, and her memories thinned, his voice growing distant, his face softening into glow. [473] But Sennel was crafty, as all good lantern makers must be. [484] She had folded one final crane she had not told the monks about, a tiny one tucked against her heart. [504] As the gem swallowed the rest, she kept that last crane closed, preserving a single dream of him for herself. [524] The basilica blazed, sending her son's recorded dream across the floating isles so every grieving sleeper might borrow his peace. [544] When dawn came and the shadows returned to their owners, Sennel descended the spiral, which now, of course, ascended as she went down. [567] Her shadow rejoined her, lighter now, the grief within it polished smooth. [579] Back in the tea garden, she opened her last crane and let its small dream replay against the steam of her cup. [601] Her son laughed once, briefly, and she smiled through tears that finally felt like light. [616]</story>
