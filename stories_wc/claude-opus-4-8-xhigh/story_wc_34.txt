<story>Above the clouds, where the old airship gardens moored, a misty courtyard waited for someone to listen. [17] Mara, a seldom-heard balladeer, climbed its brass stairs each dusk to play for the fog. [32] Her tinkersong wonder spilled from a fiddle strung with copper wire and stubborn patience. [46] The courtyard hung between weather and sky, its fountains dry, its lanterns blinking like patient, curious eyes. [63] No audience ever came, yet the walls remembered her, for they bloomed with graffiti each morning. [79] Spirals, gears, and painted petals appeared overnight, though no painter ever showed a hand. [93] Mara began to suspect a ghost in code, the courtyard's dead caretaker, somehow uploaded into its lights. [110] He had built this place years ago, and his mind still flickered through wires he could no longer touch. [129] She found him driftingly alert, attentive one moment, scattering into static the next. [142] The graffiti, she realized, was his only mouth, a desperate method to articulate unspoken needs. [157] Each mural spelled a yearning he could not say aloud, encoded in color, angle, and repeated petal. [174] For a week she answered his paintings with small tunes, coaxing him to trust the woman with the fiddle. [193] Then one night a storm crowded the heights, and lightning split the gauzy dark. [207] In that taut hush between lightning and thunder, his face shimmered upon the misted stone. [222] Only then, charged by the strike, could he hold a shape long enough to truly be seen. [239] He raised a flickering arm and pointed to a rusted thing half-buried in the courtyard ivy. [255] It was a flower press bolt plate, the clasp from his late wife's old flower press. [271] That small plate was the one keepsake he had lost the day his heart had stopped. [287] Through years of graffiti he had begged for it, but no listener had ever learned to read him. [305] His petals had always been pleas, his gears the turning of a grief no one decoded. [321] Mara knelt, pried the plate free from the ivy, and wiped its tarnished face clean with her sleeve. [339] The ghost flared with such gladness that the courtyard lamps trembled like softly struck bells. [354] But he wanted more than the relic; he wanted his whole story carried beyond these lonely heights. [371] So Mara struck a bargain with the flickering caretaker, balladeer to ghost, craft to craft. [386] She would encode his longing into a song, note by note, the way he painted line by line. [404] All night they worked together, mural answering melody, melody answering mural. [415] He spilled fresh colors across the stone; she spun them into chords on her humming copper strings. [432] Where the graffiti grieved, her fiddle wept; where it dared to hope, her fiddle climbed. [447] The storm circled them like an audience, and for once the balladeer felt truly, fully heard. [463] Slowly the courtyard filled with both paint and sound, a duet between the living and the lost. [480] By dawn the ballad held him whole: the builder, the widower, the patient mind asleep in the wires. [498] The mist thinned, and the courtyard glowed pale gold above the gently sleeping clouds. [512] Mara tucked the flower press bolt plate into her coat, beside her tuning tools and rosin. [528] She would carry both the keepsake and the finished song down to the lower towns. [543] Below, the lower towns rarely listened to balladeers, but they could not ignore a song stitched from a true ghost's heart. [564] There she would sing him into a hundred listening ears, until his unspoken needs were spoken at last. [582] She promised to return each storm, to add new verses whenever the lightning lent him form. [598] The ghost in code watched her descend, driftingly alert, but for the first time, no longer alone. [615]</story>
