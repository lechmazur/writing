<story>After the city-wide blackout, the only light left in Brume was the slow amber of nectar dusk pooling over the lightning collection tower. [23] Wren the toll keeper sat at its base, where she had always sat, counting the coins of those who climbed to charge their lanterns from stored sky. [50] She was a candid woman, candid past her own choosing, for a childhood fever had left her involuntarily truthful. [69] Ask her anything, and the answer fell out whole, unvarnished, sometimes cruel. [81] The blackout, everyone agreed, had begun with the drone beehive failure. [92] The city's clockwork bees had once pollinated the orchards and, in their humming flight, churned the static the tower harvested. [112] When the hive went silent, the orchards stilled, the tower starved, and Brume went dark. [127] Wren minded the dark less than the others did, because the dark had not changed her routine, and her routine was the thing she loved. [152] On the third evening of nectar dusk, a traveler came carrying a small lacquer box. [167] He set it on her toll stone and asked, plainly, whether it held anything worth saving. [183] Wren opened the lid because she could not refuse a question, and inside lay a single matchbox. [200] On its paper drawer, in faded ink, a cryptic riddle had been written. [213] "I am the keeper's twin who cannot lie," it read, "and I sleep where the false bees forgot to fly." [233] The traveler watched her, and asked if she understood it. [243] "I do," she said, and her truthful tongue gave her no room to pretend otherwise. [258] The keeper's twin was the queen bee, the original, hand-built, the one that lied to no one because it had no programming to deceive. [282] When the engineers replaced it with cheaper drones that faked their pollen counts, the hive's lies compounded until the whole swarm collapsed. [304] The true queen had been shut inside the tower's top chamber, the one place the false bees never flew, and forgotten. [325] Wren told him all of this without deciding to, the way water tells the shape of a slope. [343] The traveler's hands shook, and he confessed he was the engineer who had ordered the cheaper bees. [360] He had spent the blackout searching for a way to undo his shortcut, and the riddle was the last clue he could not crack. [384] Wren climbed the tower for the first time in years, because he asked her to, and because she wanted to see. [405] Step by step the dusk thickened to honey-colored gloom, and at the summit they found a sealed brass cell. [424] Inside, the true queen waited, wings folded, its tiny lamp still warm with the last charge it had ever stored. [444] The engineer wanted to seize it at once, but Wren made him wait. [457] He asked her why, and her answer was the truest thing she had ever said. [472] She wanted to wake it slowly, to watch one honest machine open its eyes, because she had spent her life preserving wonder inside routine and would not let this moment become only a repair. [506] So they sat in the nectar dusk and let the queen warm, and when its wings finally stirred, the sound was a single clean note. [531] The queen rose, circled the chamber, and began to hum the rhythm the dead hive had forgotten. [548] Far below, a single lamp in Brume flickered awake, then another, as the tower drank the new motion and gave it back as light. [572] The engineer wept and asked whether the city would forgive him. [583] "No," Wren said, honestly, "but it will forget, and that is nearly the same and far less kind." [601] Then she did the kindest thing her honesty allowed, which was to keep the queen's secret rhythm where only the tower could hear it. [625] She placed the matchbox back in the lacquer box, sealed the riddle inside, and returned it to him as both a gift and a verdict. [650] Every dusk after, Wren returned to her toll stone, counting coins, watching lanterns climb to drink the queen's quiet hum. [670] Travelers still asked her to reveal whether the new light would last, and she always answered truthfully that she did not know. [692] But she knew the wonder had returned to her routine, and that, for a candid woman, was a truth worth keeping the gate for. [716]</story>
