<story>After the last ship sailed, the island closed like a hand, and I unpacked my pens. [16] I am a traveler who writes obituaries for the living, a strange trade I learned in stranger ports. [34] Families summon me when they cannot mourn someone who still breathes, when an absence sits at the table and refuses to be named. [57] The Dowager of the cliff palace had summoned me with a letter folded into a dry leaf. [74] She received me in her shadowed garden, where autumn had laid its slow fire among the hedges. [91] Copper leaves drifted onto black water, and the marble paths wore a lonesome splendor, as if grandeur had outlived its guests. [112] I write vividly, so that those I describe recognize themselves before I hand them the page. [128] The Dowager wanted an obituary for her son, who was not dead but gone. [142] He had boarded the last ship years ago and never written, and she had stopped explaining his absence even to herself. [163] My work, I told her, was to furnish explained absences, to give the missing a reason we could finally lay to rest. [185] She led me indoors to the throne hall, where a vast carpet bled its old reds across the floor. [204] One palace carpet weft thread had worked loose, a single crimson strand she tugged whenever grief found her. [222] Pull it, she said, and the whole pattern shivers; that is how I know the weave is still alive. [241] I had come to understand the geography of healing, the way sorrow maps itself onto rooms and threads and gardens. [261] My method is simple and frightening: I heal by projecting presence. [272] I gather a person so completely in words that, reading them aloud, the listener feels the absent one enter the room. [293] For three nights I interviewed her in the shadowed garden, and she gave me her son in fragments. [311] His laugh that cracked on high notes, his habit of naming the stars wrongly on purpose, his fear of still water. [332] On the fourth evening I read the obituary I had built, and my voice carried across the cold pond. [351] I described how he must have died, somewhere far, choosing the sea over a mother he could not face. [370] As the words filled the air, the Dowager's hand rose, intimate and trembling, as if to touch a cheek that was suddenly there. [393] She wept the way the leaves fell, without struggle, and the garden seemed to lean toward her. [410] Then she rose, walked into the hall, and tied off the loose weft thread for the first time in years. [430] A finished absence, she whispered, is kinder than an open one. [441] I have learned that healing has a geography, and its capital is the moment we let presence and parting share one breath. [463] But the work always turns back on its maker. [472] I write the living because I cannot write myself, because somewhere a harbor holds a name I abandoned. [490] The last ship would not return until spring, so I stayed on as the Dowager's guest, two strangers wintering in a splendid, empty place. [514] Each dusk we returned to the shadowed garden, where autumn surrendered slowly to a thin and ringing frost. [532] She asked me, at last, to write her own obituary while she still lived, so she might read it and decide what to mend. [556] I described her vividly: a woman who guarded a palace and a single crimson thread, who mistook waiting for love. [576] When I read it, I projected her own presence back to her, and she met herself the way one meets a long-absent friend. [599] That intimate recognition, I think, is the only true homecoming, and the geography of healing had finally given me its map. [620] In spring, when the first ship answers the last, I will carry these explained absences across the water, my pages warm with borrowed presence. [644] And perhaps, at some farther harbor, I will dare to write my own. [657]</story>
