<story>Mira held a title that frightened the court: ignorance cartographer. [10] Her duty was to map what the kingdom did not know, the blank provinces of human ignorance. [27] Each dawn she climbed the obsidian spire that stood beside the frozen lake. [40] The ice below mirrored a colorless sky, smooth and waiting like an unwritten page. [54] She carried one instrument, a kingfisher feather quill nib whose blue barb hoarded what little light survived. [71] The Council had bound her with a single constraint. [80] She could chart the unknown using only last winter's ruined harvest ledgers, nothing more. [94] Within that narrow limit she was expected to seed innovation, to grow new fields from old failures. [111] They believed scarcity sharpened the mind, and Mira, groundedly imaginative, did not disagree. [124] She loved invention, but she never let it drift from the hard floor of evidence. [139] Atop the spire waited a vast vellum sheet, the kingdom's only blank page. [152] On it, by ancient law, the cartographer wrote the future the realm would obey. [166] Whatever she inked became policy: which valleys to sow, which mouths to feed, which hopes to ration. [183] So she did not guess. [188] She worked through predictive modeling, folding the ruined ledgers into patterns that whispered of seasons to come. [205] Frost depths, seed-rot curves, the slow arithmetic of hunger all bent under her counting. [219] Where the data ended, she drew careful borders, naming each gap as honest ignorance rather than false certainty. [237] For three days the model resolved into a single, fragile conclusion. [248] If the kingdom planted the failed grain again, but along the warm springs feeding the frozen lake, it would prevail against the coming famine. [272] The very crop they had cursed held the answer, if only its placement changed. [286] It was innovation born entirely within the constraint, exactly as the Council had dreamed. [300] Now came the hardest moment, the one every cartographer feared. [310] Before the pen meets the blank page, the future remains soft, reversible, kind. [323] After the first stroke, it hardens into fact, and people live or starve by the line. [339] Mira dipped the kingfisher nib and held it above the vellum, trembling. [351] Doubt rose like cold from the lake. [358] What if her model had misread a single frost figure? [368] She steadied herself with the discipline that defined her craft. [378] Imagination without grounding was prophecy; grounding without imagination was decay. [388] She had checked the warm springs against the ledgers four times, and the numbers held. [403] The blue barb of the quill seemed to remember its bird, a creature that dove into freezing water and rose with silver life. [426] That was the wager of any future worth writing: to plunge, and to surface changed. [441] Mira let the nib descend. [446] It touched the vellum, and the soft future became a firm one. [458] She wrote the new planting in clean, unhurried lines, marking the springs, the depths, the dates. [474] Beside the plan she left her honest borders of ignorance intact, so future cartographers would know where her certainty stopped. [494] That humility, she believed, was what let a kingdom keep transforming instead of merely surviving once. [510] When spring came, the cursed grain rose green along the warm springs, exactly as the model had promised. [528] The frozen lake thawed at its edges, and the people ate. [539] They did not call it a miracle, for Mira had taught them better. [552] It was the slow craft of mapping ignorance until invention had nowhere to hide. [566] Each year afterward she climbed the obsidian spire again, dipped the kingfisher nib, and paused in that fertile silence before the pen meets the blank page. [592] In that pause lived every possible kingdom. [599] And with grounded imagination, she chose the one that would prevail, and wrote it true. [614]</story>
