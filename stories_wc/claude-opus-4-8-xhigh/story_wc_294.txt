<story>My father robbed the high road for thirty years, and he left me only a tin of foreign paper money. [20] He called it his retirement, though he never lived to spend a single bill. [34] I kept the collection in my jacket while I built my company, a startup that promised to map the world's forgotten roads. [56] We launched from an abandoned circus train parked on a dead spur outside the city, because the rent was nothing and the romance was everything. [81] Investors loved the story: the robber's son charting paths nobody else would walk. [94] In those first months our painted cars filled with engineers, and the old lion banners swayed above their monitors like dusky harmony made visible. [118] I learned my father's true trade was not theft but reading land, the way he marked his territory with stacked stones and scratched fences so rivals knew where his road began. [149] I built the same instinct into our software, and the money poured in. [162] For two years we rose. [167] Our valuation doubled, then doubled again, and journalists called my stillness a polished calm, mistaking grief for genius. [185] Only my partner, Della, saw deeper; she was perceptively calm herself, and she watched me the way my father watched horizons. [206] One evening she found me in the caboose, sorting the foreign bills under a lantern. [221] "Why do you keep counting money you'll never use?" she asked. [232] I told her the truth: I was trying to discover why some paths circle back. [247] Every bill came from a traveler my father had stopped, and every traveler, he swore, eventually returned along the same stretch of road. [270] He believed people, like rivers, bend toward what they already know. [281] Then the market turned, and our investors began to circle back too, demanding we sell their forgotten roads to the highest advertiser. [303] A rival firm offered to buy our maps and bury them, paying in suitcases of cash. [319] Della wanted to take the deal and survive; I wanted to dissuade her, and the board, from killing the thing my father had died beside. [344] So I did it the only way I trusted: through territorial markers. [356] I walked the board members down the spur at dusk and showed them my father's stacked stones, still standing after twenty years. [378] I showed them the scratched fence posts, the way a single mark could tell a stranger, this far and no farther. [399] Our maps were such markers, I argued, the last honest borders in a world being paved over by ads. [418] For a moment, the dusky harmony of that ruined train held them, and they hesitated. [433] But hesitation is not rescue. [438] The funding dried, the engineers drifted away, and within a season the startup that had risen on a dead spur died there as well. [462] Della left last, pressing a single foreign note into my hand, one she had carried for luck. [479] It matched a bill in my father's tin exactly. [488] I sat alone in the caboose then, in the polished calm I had worn so long it had become my actual face. [510] And I finally understood my father's answer. [517] Paths circle back not because the world is small, but because we keep building our lives along the same hidden markers, the griefs and hopes we cannot stop returning to. [547] My father robbed travelers to feel he owned the road; I built a company to feel I had escaped it. [567] Both of us only walked the same circle, marking territory we could never truly hold. [582] I stepped down from the silent train with the tin under my arm, the foreign money worthless everywhere but here. [602] Above me the faded lion still leapt across the wood, frozen mid-circle, going nowhere, going home. [618]</story>
