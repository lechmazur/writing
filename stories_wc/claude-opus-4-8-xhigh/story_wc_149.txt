<story>Beyond the winter-cracked fields, an alchemical orchard greenhouse glowed under a sky fixed in endless night. [16] Tomas tended its copper trees alone, as he had each winter since his bride died. [31] He came not for the quicksilver fruit but for her. [41] The ashen-veil ghost bride returned to him through ephemeral cameo appearances, each visit briefer than the last. [58] Tonight she flickered between the alembic-vines, pale as a candle remembered rather than lit. [72] "You should stop waiting for me," she said, her voice lovingly venomous, tender and cutting in a single breath. [91] Tomas only smiled and pressed a carved stone bead into his palm until its grooves bit his skin. [109] The bead had been hers, the last solid thing she owned, and through it he could summon these fragile minutes. [129] "I owe you a death," she whispered. [136] At her words the orchard hummed with resonant awakenings, every copper leaf chiming as though her sorrow had plucked them. [156] He had never understood the debt; she had never explained it. [167] But tonight the hush over the winter-cracked fields seemed to lean inward, listening. [180] "In a life before this," she began, "you drowned pulling me from a frozen river, and I lived on, married, grew old, while your name dissolved beneath the ice." [209] "I swore I would repay you, though it cost me every future I was given." [224] Tomas felt the bead grow warm. [230] "So when we met again, here, in this orchard your hands built, I loved you the only way a borrowed soul can—fiercely, and with poison." [256] He remembered then: the wedding, the wine she had brewed from nightshade blossoms, the way she had wept while he drank. [277] "You poisoned me," he said, and the realization carried no anger, only a mournful elation, the strange joy of a riddle solved at last. [301] "To repay a debt across lifetimes," she answered, "I had to give you the death you gave for me—so your soul could finally rest instead of mine." [329] The greenhouse trembled, its glass throat ringing with that resonant awakening, and Tomas understood that he had been the ghost all along. [351] He had died on their wedding night, and she had stayed, a living woman aging in the dark, tending his copper trees, waiting to confess. [376] The ashen veil was not hers but his, woven from his lingering frost. [389] "You are not the bride," he whispered. [396] "I am the ghost." [400] She nodded, weeping, ancient now beneath the orchard light, her hands knotted like the oldest vine. [416] "I have grown old guarding your bead," she said, "so that one night you would remember, and forgive, and go." [436] Tomas looked at the carved stone in his fading hand and felt the debt finally balance between them. [454] "There is nothing to forgive," he said. [461] "You loved me across deaths. [466] That is repayment enough." [470] The orchard sang its last resonant note, and the winter-cracked fields exhaled their long-held hush. [485] Her venom, he saw now, had been mercy—a poison brewed from love to free him. [501] The ephemeral cameo of his own haunting flickered toward its end. [512] "Plant the bead," he told her, "where the copper roots drink deepest, and let me become an orchard instead of a ghost." [534] She kissed the carved stone and pressed it into the warm alchemical soil. [547] Light bloomed—quicksilver and gold—and Tomas felt himself unspooling into the trees, his sorrow turning sweet, his elation aching and bright. [569] "Goodbye," she breathed, and for once her voice held no venom, only love grown gentle with release. [586] The endless night above the greenhouse softened toward a gray that might, at last, become a dawn. [603] In the hush over the winter-cracked fields, a single copper bud opened, humming his name. [618] And the ashen veil drifted free, dissolving like breath upon the cold, finally, mournfully, joyfully gone. [634]</story>
