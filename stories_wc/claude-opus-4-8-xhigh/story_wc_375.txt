<story>The last echo of laughter faded from the upper gallery, and only then did Mira descend. [16] Charcoal still blackened her fingertips, a permanent stain from a craft she had abandoned for grief. [32] Her brother had been the one who laughed; his death had silenced the studio and dried her ink. [50] Below the city lay a subterranean crystal cave, where as children they had hidden treasures behind a painted door. [69] She remembered every facet of that place, for she was mnemonically gifted, unable to forget a single line she had ever seen or drawn. [93] The walls glittered with auroral light, pale greens and mournful violets refracting through the quartz like a sorrow turned luminous. [113] At the cave's heart hung a painting, the very one her brother had made the summer before he fell ill. [133] He had painted her hands gripping charcoal, as if begging her never to set the medium down. [150] Beside it, a small iron box waited, sealed by a combination lock that had long since broken. [167] Its dial spun uselessly, the numbers worn smooth, yet Mira recalled the code he had whispered: their birthdays, joined. [186] But the broken lock answered to no memory now, refusing to release what it guarded. [201] She had come to rectify her long silence, to honor the promise she had made over his hospital bed. [220] He had told her that art was an inescapable calling, that one could flee it but never truly escape. [239] For two years she had tried, and for two years her fingers ached for the charcoal she despised. [257] Now she pried at the lock with trembling hands, but the rusted mechanism would not yield. [273] Then she understood; the box was a riddle, and the answer was the painting itself. [288] A painting with something behind it, her brother's favorite trick, learned from the magicians they once adored. [305] She lifted the canvas from its hook, and behind it the crystal wall held a shallow alcove. [322] Inside lay a leather sketchbook, its pages blank, and a fresh stick of willow charcoal. [337] A note in his looping hand read: 'The lock was always broken, Mira; the real seal was your fear.' [356] She wept, and the auroral crystals scattered her tears into a thousand grieving colors. [370] He had foreseen this moment, had prepared it to uncork the creativity she would surely suppress. [386] Kneeling on the cold stone, she opened the sketchbook and pressed the charcoal to the first page. [403] From memory she drew his face, every crease and dimple recalled with merciless precision. [417] Her gift, once a curse that replayed his suffering, now became the instrument of his resurrection on paper. [435] Line by line, the silence of the cave filled with the soft scratch of an artist obeying her calling. [454] She understood at last that one cannot rectify loss, only render it into something that endures. [470] When the portrait was finished, his laughter seemed to echo once more between the glowing stones. [486] Then it faded, as all echoes must, leaving her alone with the work it had birthed. [502] Mira climbed back toward daylight, charcoal-fingered once again, the sketchbook held against her heart. [516] Behind her, the broken lock lay open in spirit if not in mechanism, its purpose finally served. [533] The crystal cave dimmed behind her, its auroral sorrow folding gently into the dark. [547] She would never abandon the calling again, for she had learned it was inescapable and, at last, beloved. [565] Each portrait she would draw henceforth carried a fragment of him, smuggled past the door of death. [582] The world above had grown gray without her hand, and she meant to color it again. [598] In every stranger's face she would now seek the spark her brother had refused to let her bury. [616] And so the sorrow that had once silenced her became, instead, the dawn from which she drew. [633]</story>
