<story>Mireveil had outlived empires, and boredom had become his only weather. [11] He lived inside a prismatic escondite, a hideaway of refracting glass buried beneath the dunes, where every wall split light into ribbons no mortal eye could name. [38] Here he waited, as he had waited for nine thousand years, for something that might finally feel like an ending. [58] The walls answered his moods in hushed thunder, a rumble so soft it lived under the skin rather than in the ears. [80] On the night the sun convulsed, the escondite trembled with it. [91] A solar storm rolled across the planet, and the magnetic fields that braided the world together began to fray. [110] Mireveil pressed the cursed amulet fragment to his chest, the single shard he had never been able to destroy. [129] It had promised him many deaths and delivered none; immortality was its curse, and he wore it like a stone in his shoe. [152] "Tonight," he whispered, "perhaps you will let me leave." [161] He had grown dimensionally aware over the centuries, able to perceive the seams where one space pressed against another. [180] Most nights those seams lay still, indifferent walls of geometry. [190] But the storm changed them. [195] Through the magnetic fields, the disrupted lines of force poured into the escondite and woke the angles themselves. [213] Mireveil felt it first as intrigue, a flicker of attention from corners that had never noticed him before. [231] The room was thinking. [235] Then the corners began to plot. [241] This was the spatial mutiny the old texts had warned of: when charged fields agitate a dimensionally folded place, the space rebels against the laws that bind it. [269] Walls peeled from their right angles like men deserting a doomed ship. [281] The floor questioned why it should remain beneath him. [290] Distances renegotiated their terms, and the far prism slid suddenly near while the near one fled. [306] Mireveil laughed, a sound he had not made in a thousand years. [318] He understood at last what he had been hoping for. [328] Every immortal hour he had wondered the same thing, the one question the amulet refused to answer: what happens after goodbye? [349] Not death, which he could not have, but the after of it, the country past the last word. [367] If space itself could mutiny, perhaps it could carry him across that border. [380] "Take me," he said to the rebelling room. [388] The escondite heard him in its hushed thunder, and the prisms leaned in like conspirators. [403] The amulet fragment grew cold, then colder, the curse finally meeting a force it could not outlast. [420] Magnetic light braided around his wrists. [426] The mutinous angles opened a seam he had never seen, a doorway with no other side, only a held breath where the after of everything waited. [452] Mireveil stepped toward it without fear. [458] The fragment shattered. [461] Nine thousand years of weight fell from his shoulders as the curse broke at the threshold it had built. [480] For one instant he stood between two states, neither living nor ended, watching the storm-fed dimensions fold gently shut behind him. [501] He felt the goodbye arrive, soft and enormous, the hush before the thunder that never came. [517] And then he learned the answer he had crossed nine millennia to find. [530] The prismatic walls went dark and ordinary, their rebellion spent with the dying storm. [544] The escondite was empty now, the magnetic fields settling back into their patient hum. [558] On the silent floor lay the shards of the amulet, dull and harmless, a curse with no one left to carry it. [580] The dunes outside kept their ancient silence, unaware that one of their immortals had finally gone home. [597] Somewhere beyond the last seam, for the first time in his endless life, Mireveil was no longer bored. [615]</story>
