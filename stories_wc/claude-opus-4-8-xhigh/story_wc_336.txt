<story>Maren had pruned the orchards of two worlds for sixty seasons, and the wise woman knew every branch by the sound it made in wind. [25] She lived on a verdant parapet beside a waterfall, where the mist rose like breath and the moss glowed faintly green at dusk. [48] There, upon the hush of budding hope, the first spring after a long grey winter, she felt the change before she saw it. [71] The color was leaving. [75] Across the gorge, in the orchard she tended on the far side, the blossoms opened pale and bloodless, white where there should have been rose, ash where there should have been gold. [107] Maren understood at once, for she was the last who remembered how the two worlds had once been woven together, thread through thread, like the warp and weft of a single cloth. [139] When that weaving frayed, wonder drained from both. [147] In her pocket she carried the only remedy, an heirloom seed passed down through the pruners of her line, each of whom had been, like her, insatiably creative. [175] The seed was small and dark, ridged like a walnut, and it held within it the captured essence of every spring her ancestors had ever loved. [201] They had made it the way she made everything, by capturing essence, by gathering what was fleeting before it fled. [221] A child's first laughter, the exact red of a ripe apple, the smell of rain on warm stone, all of it pressed and folded inward until the seed hummed with stored light. [253] Her grandmother had warned her: plant it only when the worlds begin to forget their own colors, for once planted it cannot be reclaimed. [277] Now that hour had come. [282] Maren knelt at the lip of the parapet, where the waterfall poured down into mist and the two worlds met in spray. [304] This was the seam, the place where she could still pass a hand from one realm into the other and feel the difference in the air. [330] If she planted the seed here, its roots would reach into both soils at once, weaving the worlds anew. [349] But she hesitated, because creating something meant letting it leave her hands, and she had spent her whole life keeping wonder close. [371] Below her, the dying orchard sighed. [377] A single blossom, the last to hold any blush of pink, trembled and went white as she watched. [395] That decided her. [398] To preserve the color of wonder, she could not hoard it; she had to give it away. [415] Maren pressed the heirloom seed into the wet green moss of the parapet, at the precise point where the waterfall's mist touched both worlds. [439] She covered it, whispered to it the way she whispered to her trees, and offered it her oldest succor, the patient tending that asks nothing back. [465] For a long moment nothing happened, and the radiant hush of the gorge held its breath. [481] Then a thread of green pushed up, thin as a needle, and split the air into two faint shimmering halves. [501] Roots dove left and right, into the soil of her world and into the soil of the other, and where they passed the colors came rushing home. [528] Across the gorge the pale blossoms flushed rose and gold again, drinking back the hues they had nearly lost. [547] The new sapling rose between the worlds, its leaves catching light from two skies at once, and on every leaf shimmered a fragment of the essence her family had saved. [577] Maren laughed, the sound her grandmother had once captured, returned to her now from the tree itself. [594] She would prune this one too, in time, shaping it so the weaving held strong, so the seam between worlds never tore again. [617] She had given away the seed, yet wonder had not left her; it had only grown larger than her two hands. [638] Upon the verdant parapet, beside the falling water, the wise pruner sat in the gathering green light and understood the last secret of her craft. [663] Creation was not capture but release. [669] To keep the color of wonder, you had to let it bloom in worlds beyond your own. [686] And so she stayed, insatiably creative to her final season, tending the tree that wove two springs into one. [705]</story>
