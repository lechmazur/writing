<story>Vance Orrery built clinics with one hand and emptied rivers with the other. [13] He had engineered the drought himself, diverting the highland dams until the valley cracked open like old porcelain. [31] The salt flat that remained was his cathedral, white and endless, humming under a sky bruised with thunder that never arrived. [52] People called him a philanthropist, and he did not correct them. [63] He fed the orphans his diversions had starved, and felt nothing he could name. [77] Yet beneath the steel of him lay a chamber he showed no one. [90] There he kept the egg, the last egg of the extinct marsh-warbler, a bird whose song had once filled this basin before the water learned to obey him. [118] He had not drained the valley for profit, though profit had followed. [130] He had drained it to read the ground. [138] For the cracks were a map. [144] As the mud dried, it split into channels and forks, a script only thirst could write, and Vance had spent his fortune learning to read it. [170] The pattern pointed inward, toward the salt flat's blind center, where the old hatchery had drowned a century before. [189] He arrived during the lull of distant thunder, when the storm held its breath at the horizon. [206] The egg rode in his coat, wrapped in wool, warm as a confession. [219] His instruments said the acoustics here were perfect, the salt a vast white drum that could hold a sound for ages. [240] This was the truth he hid even from himself: he was sentimental, hopelessly, secretly so. [255] He remembered his mother humming the warbler's song while the river still ran, and he had razed a watershed to keep that echo from dying. [280] The plan was simple and monstrous. [286] He would release the recorded song into the basin, broadcast it across the salt until the flats themselves remembered, until the mineral lattice rang with the dead bird's voice. [315] The egg he would bury at the center, the cracks all aiming there like the spokes of a stilled wheel. [335] He knelt where the map insisted. [341] The salt was cool and the air tasted of metal and rain that would not fall. [357] He pressed the egg into a hollow, the shell pale as the moon's underside, and he understood that he was burying his mother too, and the river, and the man he might have been. [391] Thunder murmured, far off, almost kind. [397] Vance keyed the transmitter, and the warbler's song rose from a hundred buried speakers, climbing the white silence in a fractured, broken grace. [420] The notes cracked and stuttered where the salt warped them, yet they were unmistakably the song, the very song, alive again across the dead lake. [445] He wept, which surprised him. [450] He had not wept when the valley emptied, nor when the children came. [463] The amoral man, the engineer of thirst, knelt in his cathedral and shook. [476] But the salt had its own intentions. [483] The vibration he loosed traveled deeper than his instruments foresaw, into the brine pockets sealed below, and the ground began to weep with him. [507] Water, ancient and trapped, rose through the cracks his drought had carved, following the same map, filling the channels he had read. [529] The basin was answering. [533] The thunder broke its lull at last, and rain came down in sheets, and the manufactured drought dissolved into the very flood it had hidden. [558] Vance did not run. [562] He watched the egg vanish beneath rising water, the song still playing, warping now into something like birdsong drowning and birdsong being born. [585] He had wanted to preserve an echo. [592] Instead he had released a resurrection he could not control, and he laughed, because it was the most honest thing he had ever built. [616] The water took his knees, his chest, the recorder still singing in the storm. [630] Somewhere in the noise, he was certain, a marsh-warbler answered from a throat that should not exist. [647] Perhaps it was the salt. [652] Perhaps it was the wool unraveling, the shell breaking in the new tide. [665] Perhaps the dead simply forgive us when we finally mean it. [676] Vance closed his eyes and let the song and the flood carry him, an amoral man undone by the one tenderness he could not engineer away. [702] The valley would be a lake again. [709] The birds, he thought, might follow the water home. [718]</story>
