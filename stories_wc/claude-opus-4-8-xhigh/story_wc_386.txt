<story>Mira had spent her life among drowned ships, a wreck-diver who learned that the dead linger longest in cold water. [20] She was a ghost whisperer too, though she rarely used the word, preferring to say she listened where others only saw rust. [42] Years ago a passenger liner went down beneath the polar shelf, and one soul refused to surface with the rest. [62] That soul was her sister, Elin, lost before they could mend the silence that had grown between them. [80] To find her, Mira built a candle-lit shrine carved into glacier ice, deep enough that the daylight never reached it. [100] There, surrounded by walls that glowed like borrowed starlight, she practiced a strange salvage. [114] She had discovered that the drowned visit the living through windows of connection, brief crossings between sleep and grief. [133] So she gathered strangers who had sailed near the wreck, and she listened to their dreams. [149] Her method was patient: she searched for cameo appearances, the small uninvited figures who wandered the edges of other people's nightmares. [170] Elin, she believed, was hiding inside someone else's sleep. [179] Each night she lit the candles and let the travelers rest on furs against the ice. [195] A fisherman dreamed of a girl combing kelp from her hair. [206] A widow dreamed of a child laughing at a door that opened onto deep blue. [221] Mira wrote everything down, weaving truth carefully, knowing that one wrong thread could unravel a fragile crossing. [238] She would not lie to the dead, and she would not lie to the living, even when the truth was a blade. [260] The only thing she had salvaged from the wreck was a melted snow globe, its glass warped by some fire that should have been impossible underwater. [286] Inside, a tiny frozen village sagged like a candle left too long in the sun. [301] It had been Elin's, a gift Mira once mocked as childish, and now it was the last pure thing left from a sisterhood she had wrecked with her own pride. [331] She kept it on the shrine's altar, where the candlelight pooled inside the bent glass and threw crooked stars across the walls. [353] One night a young sailor named Tomas came to her, haunted by a recurring dream. [368] In it he stood in a sinking corridor while a woman pressed her palm against a porthole from the other side. [389] Each time, she mouthed a word he could not hear. [399] Mira recognized the gesture at once, for it was how Elin used to say goodnight through their childhood window. [418] She guided Tomas back into the dream, holding the melted globe so its light could anchor him. [435] There in the borrowed dark, Elin appeared at the edge, a cameo flickering against the green water. [452] Mira spoke through the window of connection, asking the question she had crossed an entire glacier to ask: why she stayed. [473] Elin's answer was simple and terrible. [479] She stayed because Mira had never said she was sorry, and the dead cannot leave on a silence. [497] Mira understood then that her salvage was not Elin at all, but the apology she had buried under years of pride. [518] To free her sister she had to expose the wound she had hidden even from herself. [534] So she spoke it, plainly, the cruelty she had thrown the night before the voyage, the words that sent Elin onto the doomed ship alone. [559] The confession moved through the ice like warmth, and the candles bent toward it. [573] Tomas wept though the grief was not his own. [582] Elin's cameo brightened, no longer a trespasser in another's sleep but a guest finally welcomed home. [598] She pressed her palm one last time against the imagined glass, and this time Mira heard the word: forgiven. [617] The melted globe grew cold in Mira's hands, its crooked stars steadying into a clean, quiet light. [634] When Tomas woke, his nightmare was gone, replaced by the memory of a stranger's gratitude. [649] Mira blew out the candles one by one, and the shrine returned to plain blue ice. [665] She had come to salvage something pure from the wreckage, and at last she had: not a ghost, not a globe, but the truth she had been too proud to carry. [696] She climbed toward the surface, where real starlight waited, no longer borrowed, and let the dead drift gently out to sea. [717]</story>
