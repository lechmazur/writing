<story>Mara called herself a forgiving skeptic, the kind who pardoned belief in others while withholding it from her own heart. [20] As the village bone setter, she trusted what her hands could feel: the fracture, the alignment, the patient knitting of what was broken. [43] Magic, she liked to say, was only medicine no one had bothered to measure. [57] That certainty held until the morning the substitute body was found. [68] For half a year the villagers had whispered that little Tomas in the miller's cradle was not Tomas at all, but a thing left in his place. [95] Now the proof lay on the table: a small shape of bound twigs and candle-tallow that had once breathed and cried, collapsed at last into bark and ash. [123] They called Mara to examine it, because she alone refused to flinch. [135] Sifting the debris, she found something her skepticism could not explain - a single baby tooth, pearled and perfect, far too real for a doll of sticks. [161] She held the changeling's baby tooth to the light, and felt a cleft in certainty open inside her like a seam splitting along old bone. [186] If the false child had teeth, then somewhere the true child wore another mouth, in another country. [203] The miller's wife, hollow-eyed, pressed Mara's wrist and confessed where the swap had happened. [217] Beyond the manor stood a hedge maze, and at its heart, she whispered, was a secret garden where she had once left Tomas sleeping in the warm grass. [245] Mara had played in that maze as a girl; she remembered yearning to reach its green center and never quite managing. [266] Now she walked it with the tooth clenched in her fist, taking each turn by the feel of the moss beneath her boots. [289] The hedges parted at last, and the secret garden opened before her, walled in living leaf and humming with a light that did not come from the sun. [317] A child sat among the roses, and she knew at once it was not Tomas, though it wore his crooked smile. [338] It looked up at her, shrewdly innocent, an old patience folded into a small face. [353] "You found my tooth," it said, and offered its palm, where the gap in its grin proved the loss. [372] "I am the one who lived in your village while your Tomas grew strong here in our garden." [390] Mara, who trusted only bones, knelt and saw that the child's arm had healed wrong, the small wrist bent like a green branch. [413] "It broke when I fell from your world into this one," the changeling said. [427] "No one in the garden knows the setting of bones." [437] So she did what she had always done: she felt the bend, the wrongness, and with a steady, practiced pull she returned the bone to its honest line. [465] The child gasped, then laughed, and the garden brightened as if it had drawn its first easy breath. [483] "Why take him at all?" Mara asked, because forgiveness still needed reasons. [495] The changeling looked toward the sky, where the dome of the garden was darkening into evening. [511] "We do not steal," it said. [517] "We borrow the ones who are nearly lost, and we mend what your world breaks - and then we turn our mistakes into constellations, so the harm becomes a map instead of a wound." [550] As it spoke, the healed wrist began to glow, and tiny lights rose from the garden's beds, the old sorrows of swapped children lifting into points of fixed, forgiving fire. [580] Mara watched her own disbelief join them, a small dim star among the brighter griefs. [595] Then Tomas himself came running from the far hedge, whole and laughing, and the changeling pressed its baby tooth back into Mara's hand. [618] "Keep it," it said, "as the seed of a doubt worth tending." [630] She led the true child home through the maze, no longer certain of anything - and gladder for the gap. [649] That night she set the tooth on her windowsill, where it caught the new stars, and she let herself yearn, at last, for the wonders she had spent a lifetime refusing. [680] A forgiving skeptic still, she had simply forgiven the world for being larger than her hands could measure. [698]</story>
