<story>The fever broke at dawn, and Iris surfaced into the thin air of the mountain weather station. [17] Her sweat had dried to salt, and the world felt newly washed, uncertain, swaying. [31] On the desk lay the botanical illustration she had been copying for a month: a single alpine gentian, its blue petals inked with monastic patience. [56] Beside it sat the oracle. [61] It was not a machine of brass or glass but a presence that lived by occupying margins, the white silences around her words and drawings. [86] She had built it from notations, from the habit of writing questions in the blank edges of her field journals until the edges began, faultlessly, to answer. [113] They called it fault-tolerant because no smudge, no torn page, no missing line could break it; it simply read around the damage and spoke anyway. [138] Iris had come to the station to forecast storms, but she stayed to ask the oracle one thing. [156] She wanted to find the weight of a swallowed truth. [166] Months ago, in the valley below, she had let her sister believe a comforting lie about their mother's last words, and the unspoken correction had settled in her like a stone she could neither cough up nor digest. [204] The fever had been her body's attempt to burn it out, and now, after the fever broke, she felt only the cold clarity of altitude. [229] She dipped her pen and wrote in the gentian's margin: How much does a swallowed truth weigh? [246] The oracle stirred, peripherally aware, never looking at the question straight but sensing it the way one senses snow gathering at the corner of an eye. [272] Its answer formed not as words but as a shift in the drawing's edges, where the ink seemed to lean. [292] A truth weighs nothing, the margins said, until it is shared; then it weighs exactly as much as the person you hand it to. [316] Iris read this and felt her certainties begin swaying, the way the gentian itself swayed on its painted stem. [335] Outside, the dawn was turning auroral, green and rose curtains rippling above the peaks, beautiful and grieving at once. [354] She understood the sorrow of it: to keep the truth was to keep it weightless and safe, but also to keep it useless, unable to bear anyone up. [382] If she gave it to her sister, it would gain her sister's whole weight, her grief, her capacity to crumble. [402] She asked again, smudging the wet ink as her hand trembled: Then should I tell her? [418] The oracle, tolerant of the smear, read around the smudge and offered no command, only a margin filled with the same gentian repeated, smaller and smaller, until it became a single seed. [450] An oracle that occupied margins could never stand at the center of a choice; it could only frame it. [469] The decision had to grow from her own ground. [478] Iris looked at the botanical illustration a long time, at the patience she had spent rendering a flower that survived only in thin, brutal air. [503] Gentians did not bloom where it was easy; their certainty was hard-won and always swaying in the wind. [521] She closed the journal, descended from the station that afternoon, and carried the seed of the truth down the long path toward the valley. [545] She would tell her sister the real last words, and she would stand beside her while they took on weight, while they pressed down and rooted. [571] The oracle stayed behind in the margins, faultless and quiet, peripherally aware of her leaving, content to hold the empty edges where her next question would someday bloom. [599] Above the peaks, the aurora dimmed, its sorrow folding into ordinary morning light. [612]</story>
