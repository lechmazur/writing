<story>Mira lit the last pier lamp as the town began its sky lantern release, each flame loosed into a hush so deep it felt like a meteor holding its breath. [30] She was the late-shift lamplighter, and for years she had chased a single private question: what was the color of sound? [51] Most people called the want foolish, but Mira hid behind that truth, letting strangers laugh so they never guessed how seriously she meant it. [75] Tonight the answer arrived as an object: a brass hotel skeleton key tag, washed onto the boards, stamped with a room number that no inn in town had ever used. [105] When she pressed it to her ear, it carried a low ringing, and the ringing, impossibly, looked faintly blue. [124] She understood at once that the tag wanted to signify a place, not a room. [139] So she went to find it the only way she trusted, by wandering purposefully along the tideline until the water decided to answer. [162] The lanterns above thinned, and the blue ringing thickened, pulling her toward the black breakwater where an old ship lay drowned. [183] She breathed in, stepped off the rocks, and let the cold take her down through a hatch furred with weeds. [203] Inside, the pressure eased into an impossible pocket of air, and her lamp caught what the sea had been hiding. [223] It was a forgotten library, shelves of swollen books standing in a sunken ship, their spines glowing where the brass tag's ringing touched them. [247] Here every word that had ever been spoken aloud at sea had drifted to settle, and each waited in its own quiet color. [270] Sailors' prayers burned amber; warnings shone a hard, warning red; lullabies pooled in soft greens against the keel. [288] Mira walked the aisles in the meteor hush, learning at last that sound did have color, and that color was simply memory made visible. [312] At the heart of the library hung a chandelier of unspoken light, and beneath it lay two rings on a velvet card. [334] The skeleton key tag fit a tiny lock there, and when she turned it, the rings rose and spoke. [353] They were luminous vows, the marriage promises of a couple who had drowned with this ship a century ago. [372] Their voices glowed a blue she now recognized, the exact blue the tag had carried to her on the pier. [392] They had vowed to find each other again, and they had been waiting for someone willing to carry the promise upward. [413] Mira cupped the blue light against her chest, and it settled into her like a lantern remembering its flame. [432] She climbed back through the hatch, broke the surface, and rose into a sky still trembling with drifting lanterns. [451] On the pier she opened her hands, and the blue vow lifted free, joining the paper flames as a single brighter point. [473] The whole town watched it climb, not knowing they were witnessing two old promises finally keeping themselves. [490] Mira stood among her glowing pier lamps and understood her question had been a vow of its own all along. [510] She had wanted the color of sound, and sound, it turned out, had wanted to be heard by someone like her. [531] She pocketed the empty key tag, its ringing now silent, its work done. [544] Then she lit one final lamp, the brightest, to signify the place where the drowned voices had been answered. [563] Around her, the meteor hush held, soft and blue, as the last lanterns thinned into stars. [579] Mira smiled, certain now that every flame she had ever raised was a small, patient promise to the dark. [598] And the dark, for once, glowed back. [605]</story>
