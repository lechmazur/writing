<story>The stray philosopher arrived in the mechanical rainforest the morning after his sixth sense awakened. [15] He had no name anyone remembered, only a coat full of questions and a small gear he carried like a relic. [36] The gear had fallen from his own mind, he believed, the day his thoughts began to translocate during sleep. [55] Each night his body stayed still while his consciousness wandered, waking him in places he had never walked to. [74] He had come here, to this forest of copper ferns and brass vines, to rediscover the wild frontier of his own mind. [96] The trees were machines, each one a tower of gears and pistons grown like wood. [111] Their leaves were thin sheets of beaten tin that caught the light and rang softly. [126] When wind moved through them, the sound differed from tree to tree, and the philosopher learned to listen. [144] An oak of iron groaned low; a willow of silver chimed high; a fern of bronze whispered in fractions. [163] He understood, suddenly, that the sound of wind through different trees was a language. [177] This was the hum of insight he had chased his whole life, the vibration that meant a true thought was near. [198] He held the small gear to his ear and felt it tremble in time with a distant copper palm. [217] "You are a key," he told it, "or perhaps a tooth missing from some greater wheel." [233] By day he walked the humming paths, fitting the gear against trunks, listening for the note that matched. [251] By night he lay beneath the loudest tree and let sleep carry him elsewhere. [265] He woke once in the canopy, balanced on a turning branch, with no memory of the climb. [282] He woke again knee-deep in a stream of warm oil, the gear still clutched in his fist. [299] The translocations frightened him less each time, for he began to suspect they were guiding him. [315] The forest, he reasoned, was answering the same question he carried, only it answered in motion. [331] On the sixth night, after his sixth sense had ripened into something steadier, the hum grew loud enough to ache. [351] He slept, and his mind translocated to the forest's center, where a single great tree stood unfinished. [368] It was a vast clockwork trunk with one empty socket at its heart, a gap shaped exactly like his gear. [388] The wind passed through it and made no sound at all, a silence that felt like a held breath. [407] He woke standing before that very tree, his feet muddy, the gear warm against his palm. [423] He understood that placing the gear would either complete the forest or unravel it entirely. [438] To complete it was safe; to unravel it was to free every thought the machines had been holding still. [457] The philosopher hesitated, because completion was certainty and he had spent his life distrusting certainty. [472] He had come to rediscover the wild frontier, not to bolt another fence across it. [487] So he pressed the small gear into the socket and turned it the wrong way on purpose. [504] The great tree shuddered, and the hum of insight rose into a single clear chord. [519] Then the forest began to unravel, gear by gear, leaf by tin leaf, into a glittering cloud. [536] The wind that had spoken through iron and silver and bronze now blew through nothing, and that nothing sang. [555] In the unraveling he heard every probable miracle the machines had been too rigid to allow. [571] Copper ferns became birds; pistons became rivers; the willow's chime became laughter he half recognized as his own. [589] The philosopher felt his sleeping mind and waking mind fold together at last into one wandering thing. [606] He did not translocate that night, because for the first time there was nowhere he was not already. [624] When morning came there was no forest, only an open plain humming faintly under a wide and ordinary sky. [643] In his hand lay the small gear, still warm, its teeth worn smooth as a river stone. [660] He pocketed it, smiling, for the frontier was no longer out there to be found. [675] The stray philosopher walked on into the quiet, carrying the whole wild country of his mind. [691]</story>
