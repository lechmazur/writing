<story>Mara never wanted the orchard. [5] The trees grew on a cliffside labyrinth, their roots clutching stone like frightened hands. [19] She was its reluctant caretaker, bound by a curse she had not chosen—a bargain her grandmother made and left unpaid. [40] Each tree bore fruit that whispered, and the mechanical birds patrolled the hedges above, brass wings clicking, eyes pinpricks of cold light. [62] They watched for travelers who wandered the maze, and they reported every misstep to no one, faithfully. [79] Mara's only tool was a small nut, smooth and dark, that her grandmother had pressed into her palm before dying. [99] "When the frost comes," the old woman had said, offhandedly exact, "you will know what to assemble." [116] Mara had no idea what that meant. [123] For years the nut sat in her pocket, warm against her hip, useless as a riddle. [139] Then the travelers came—three of them, ragged and lost, drawn into the labyrinth by a road that lied. [158] They had entered at dusk, and now the air sharpened. [168] Before the night frost formed, they would freeze among the cursed trees, and the birds would simply log their stillness. [188] Mara found them shivering near the orchard's heart. [196] She wanted to turn away. [201] The curse had taught her that caring cost more than it returned. [213] But something in the smallest traveler—a girl clutching her father's sleeve—undid her resolve. [228] "Follow me," Mara said, though she did not know the way out herself. [241] That was the truth of the labyrinth: it had no fixed path. [253] The hedges rearranged when the birds wheeled overhead. [261] To guide lost travelers to safety, one needed a map that did not exist. [275] Or one that had been forgotten. [281] The nut grew hot. [285] Mara remembered, suddenly, a forgotten blueprint her grandmother had drawn in the frost on a winter window long ago—lines of an engine, a lantern, something that could read the maze and remake it. [319] "The frost," Mara breathed. [323] The cold was already gilding the leaves. [330] She knelt and cracked the nut against a stone. [339] Inside lay not a kernel but a coil of impossibly thin wire and a sliver of mirrored glass—components, waiting. [359] She had to assemble them. [364] But how, with no instructions? [369] The blueprint, she realized, was not on paper. [377] Her grandmother had taught her a stranger art: leaving impressions without touching. [389] Mara hovered her fingers above the frost gathering on a flat stone and let her breath and warmth carve the design—lines appearing as the frost retreated from her nearness, drawn but never smudged. [423] Slowly, in negative, the blueprint emerged: a small lantern-machine that bent the maze's logic. [437] The travelers watched, baffled, as Mara fit wire to glass, following the ghost-lines she had impressed into the ice. [456] Her hands were clumsy; she had never built anything graceful in her life. [469] The thing she made was ugly—a lopsided cage of wire around a shard of mirror, knots where there should be welds. [491] But when she breathed on it, it woke. [499] The mirror caught the cold birdlight and threw it back, and the mechanical birds froze mid-wheel, confused by their own reflected signals. [521] The hedges, no longer rearranged by their patrol, settled into a single honest path. [535] It was the most graceless beautiful thing Mara had ever seen—an awkward little engine glowing in the dark, drawing a line of safety through a place built to lose people. [566] "Go," she told the travelers. [571] "Quickly, before the frost finishes." [576] The father hesitated. [579] "Come with us." [582] For the first time, Mara considered leaving the orchard. [591] But the curse held her roots as surely as the trees clutched the cliff. [605] "I can't," she said. [609] "Someone has to keep the path open." [616] The girl pressed something into Mara's hand as she passed—a single seed from her own pocket, an ordinary acorn, warm as the nut had been. [642] "For next time," the girl said. [648] Then they were gone down the honest path, and the frost came, and the birds slept, and Mara stood alone among the whispering trees. [672] She closed her fingers around the acorn. [679] Reluctant still, but no longer only that. [686] When the next travelers came lost into the dark, she would be ready to assemble the way again. [704]</story>
