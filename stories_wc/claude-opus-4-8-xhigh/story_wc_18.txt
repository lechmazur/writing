<story>The mirror maker named Sela worked where the orchard district had drowned in centuries of thick fog. [17] Frost shimmered upon the twilight, lacing the dead apple boughs with the cold brightness of glass mountains. [34] She had come here for one reason: to capture a runaway dream that had slipped from her own sleep and now wandered the rows like a pale lantern. [62] In her satchel lay an astronomer's broken sextant, its mirror cracked, its arc bent, a gift from her dead father who had measured stars until the fog ate the sky. [92] Mirrors were her trade, and she believed a dream, like starlight, could be caught only in glass. [109] The dream had been hers since childhood: an orchard in bloom, boundaries gone between earth and air, where she walked unafraid. [130] Losing it, she had felt the world flatten, so she resolved to recreate that vision and live inside it. [149] Yet a dream is slippery, and Sela was a woman firmly grounded, more comfortable with silvered tin than with phantoms. [169] She planted her largest mirror among the broken trees and waited. [180] The dream drifted near, curious at its own reflection, and Sela understood that to hold it she must erase the boundary between dreamer and dream. [205] Her father had once taught her how sailors and stargazers steadied their instruments through synchronized breathing, matching the rhythm of lungs to the sway of the sea. [232] So she breathed with the dream. [238] She inhaled when its light swelled, exhaled when it dimmed, until their two pulses became a single tide. [256] The frost on the branches rang like struck crystal, the whole district trembling with the chill music of glass mountains. [276] Slowly the mirror clouded, then cleared into the orchard of her childhood, blossoms opening where no fruit had grown in lifetimes. [297] But the broken sextant in her satchel grew warm, reminding her that her father had lost himself the same way, chasing the sky until he forgot the ground. [325] The dream, sensing the dissolving boundary, surged toward the glass, eager to swallow her whole and keep her dreaming forever. [345] For one breath Sela wanted to step through, to abandon the fog and live among the blossoms. [362] But she was firmly grounded, and she remembered the cold honest weight of tools in her hands. [379] She seized the broken sextant and pressed its cracked mirror against the great glass. [393] Two reflections met, and in the seam between them she broke the rhythm of their shared breath. [410] She exhaled while the dream inhaled, and the synchrony shattered. [420] The boundary snapped back into place like a drawn string. [430] The orchard in the mirror froze mid-bloom, captured at last, a single shimmering image she could carry and never enter. [450] The runaway dream, robbed of its open door, thinned into the fog and was gone. [465] Sela knelt in the frost, breathing alone now, her heart steadying to its own rhythm. [480] She had wanted to recreate the dream and live inside it; instead she had recreated it as a thing she could hold without being held. [505] The mirror, now warm and bright, held her childhood orchard like a star pinned behind glass. [521] She wrapped it carefully and tucked the broken sextant beside it, two instruments that had finally measured the right thing. [541] The twilight frost still shimmered, and the ruined orchard district lay silent beneath its endless fog. [557] Sela rose, firmly grounded, and walked back the way she had come. [569] Her father had erased the boundary between himself and the sky and vanished into it. [584] She had touched that same edge and chosen the ground, and the dream she carried home would keep, unbroken, in its mountain of clear and silent glass. [611]</story>
