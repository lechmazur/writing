<story>After receiving the donor heart, Wen climbed to the floating rock tea house to begin asking her quiet question. [19] She was a watchful dye master, and the new pulse in her chest felt borrowed and urgent. [36] Her motivation was simple and impossible: to learn why some people magnetize stories while others pass unseen. [53] The tea house drifted above the valley on a single weathered boulder, tethered by nothing but old prayer rope and habit. [74] Mist rose around it each morning like cool breath, wrapping the railings in valley-mist wonder. [89] Wen brought her dyes, her threads, and a single blue morpho wing pressed beneath glass. [104] The wing had belonged to her donor, a girl named Liang who had collected impossible colors before she died. [123] Liang's mother had given it to Wen with a warning: "Tilt it slowly, or its blue will frighten you." [142] That was iridescent caution, the way the wing flared sapphire then vanished depending on the angle of light. [160] Wen understood color, but she did not understand why Liang's name still drew listeners across three villages. [177] People spoke of the dead girl as though she were a song they could not stop humming. [194] So Wen decided to investigate the only way a dye master knew how: by tracking sun trails. [211] Each clear day, she marked where light crossed the tea house floor and laid threads along its path. [229] She believed that if she could illuminate the exact route the sun took, she might map how attention moved. [248] Stories, she reasoned, traveled like light, choosing some surfaces to ignite and leaving others dim. [263] The tea master, an old man named Bo, watched her stretch yarn across his floorboards and said nothing for a week. [284] On the eighth morning he set down two cups and finally asked what she was chasing. [300] "I want to know why Liang's heart found me," Wen said, "and why her story keeps finding strangers." [318] Bo lifted the blue morpho wing to the window and tilted it the way a careless person never would. [337] The blue erupted, then dissolved, then erupted again, quietly explosive in his patient fingers. [351] "You think the wing is blue," he said, "but there is no blue pigment in it at all." [369] He explained that the color came from structure, not dye, from tiny scales bending sunlight into illusion. [386] "The wing does not hold blue," Bo said; "it teaches light how to become blue." [401] Wen sat very still, her borrowed heart knocking against the idea. [412] Some people, she realized, were not full of stories the way a jar is full of water. [429] They were shaped, like the morpho's scales, to bend whatever passed through them into something luminous. [445] Liang had magnetized stories not by hoarding them but by giving ordinary light a reason to dazzle. [462] That night Wen worked by lamplight, dyeing a long banner the precise restless blue of the wing. [479] She did not use one pigment; she layered many, so the cloth would shift and breathe as people walked past. [499] At dawn she hung it from the tea house railing, where the rising sun could find it first. [517] Travelers on the valley path stopped, pointed, and began to ask who had made such a living color. [535] Bo smiled and told them about a dye master with a borrowed heart, and the listeners leaned closer. [553] Wen watched the story leap from mouth to mouth, catching light, changing with each angle of telling. [570] She finally understood the iridescent caution in Liang's mother's words. [580] A story, like the morpho's blue, was not a possession but an event between a surface and the sun. [599] Tilt it carelessly and it blinded; tilt it with attention and it revealed. [612] People magnetized stories, she saw, when they let themselves be structured for wonder rather than stuffed with facts. [630] Wen pressed her palm to her chest, thanking the heart that had carried her this high. [646] Each morning after, she kept tracking sun trails, not to capture the light but to follow where it wished to go. [667] And the floating rock tea house, wrapped in mist, became a place where ordinary travelers learned to illuminate one another. [687] Wen, the watchful dye master, had her answer at last, shimmering and impossible to hold. [702]</story>
