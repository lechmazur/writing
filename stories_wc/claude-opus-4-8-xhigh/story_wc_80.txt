<story>Edda served as a firm shore surveyor, hired to certify the submerged pavilion before the reservoir claimed it forever. [19] Each evening, after the drawbridge rose and the sluice gates clamped shut, the basin drew back just enough to bare the pavilion's flooded floor. [43] She waded in then, lantern raised, into a silence that somehow blazed. [55] Stoically sensitive, she had learned to feel everything and show nothing, the only posture that let her work among ruins without drowning herself. [78] The town had been emptied for the dam thirty years ago, its people relocated, its futures abandoned mid-sentence. [96] Edda's true commission, the one no firm had signed, was to become the archeologist of abandoned dreams. [113] She carried her grandfather's antique traveling inkwell, a brass-hinged thing that had crossed three wars without spilling. [130] With it she wrote small notes on waterproof vellum and slid them into the cracks while she logged the foundation repairs. [151] Placing notes in foundation repairs was her quiet rebellion: each crack received the name of someone who had once meant to return. [173] The pavilion had been a dance hall, then a wedding venue, then nothing. [186] Her own mother had been married here, in a dress photographed once and never worn again. [202] That photograph, and the man in it, formed the interlaced absence Edda had measured all her life: a father gone, a town gone, the two losses woven so tightly she could not pull one free. [237] Tonight her lantern found the bandstand, and beneath a slab of fallen plaster, a tin box wedged into the original mortar. [258] She had to wrest it loose, prying with her survey rod until the mortar surrendered. [273] Inside lay notes in her father's hand, slipped into these same foundations decades before, when he had worked the dam's first repairs. [295] He had not vanished into another woman's town, as the family story went; he had stayed to seal the leaks, refusing to leave the pavilion unguarded. [321] The last note read: When the bridge lifts, someone will come; tell her I kept it dry. [338] Edda sat in the rising water, stoic face wet from more than the basin. [352] The inkwell trembled in her grip, the same brass he must have held when he wrote his vellum confessions into the dark. [374] She understood then that he, too, had wanted to be the archeologist of abandoned dreams, and had simply started before her. [395] The absences uncrossed; the loss of him no longer interlaced with the loss of the town, because both had been here all along. [418] She uncapped the inkwell and wrote a final note on her own vellum. [431] "Found you," it said, nothing more. [437] She tucked it into the crack she had just emptied, sealing the conversation across thirty silent years. [454] The firm would receive its proper report, accurate to the millimeter, certifying the pavilion as sound enough to remember. [473] But this last entry belonged to no client. [481] Above her, the drawbridge held its iron posture, and the basin began its slow return. [496] Water climbed the bandstand steps with the patience of a tide that knows it owns everything eventually. [513] Edda gathered her instruments, the antique inkwell warm now from her hands, and climbed toward the failing light. [531] The hush around her was not empty; it blazed with everything that had refused to disappear. [547] Her grandfather had carried the inkwell through wars; her father had carried his grief through floods; she carried both upward now. [568] The pavilion would sink again, but it would sink remembered, and that, she decided, was a kind of rescue. [587] She would return tomorrow, after the drawbridge rises again, to read whatever the water had not yet wrested away.
