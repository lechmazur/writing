<story>Mara had witnessed the reef bleach twice, and both times she stayed until the color drained completely. [17] Now she lived alone at the Halden sea-level monitoring station, a steel pod moored to a drowned headland. [35] The fossil fuel ban had come a decade too late for the corals, but the sea kept rising regardless, patient and certain. [57] Her job was simple: record the water's climb, and report which footpaths along the coast had vanished beneath it. [76] She had taken the post for one reason: to discover why some paths disappear while others, somehow, endure. [94] Above her bunk she had pinned a single atlas page, torn out years ago from a book she no longer owned. [115] It showed her childhood shoreline, every cove and trail inked in confident black, none of which existed anymore. [133] Each evening, restless, she tapped rhythms on the station's metal walls, a practice she had learned underwater. [150] Divers once spoke through such taps, signaling presence when words drowned, and the habit had outlived its purpose. [168] Her days drifted, moored, each identical to the last, the way a buoy strains against a chain it cannot break. [188] Then one storm-grey morning, a survey drone returned with sonar maps of the seabed below her window. [205] She began to assemble them on the floor, fitting fragment to fragment, until the drowned land took shape. [223] The sonar showed the old trails perfectly preserved beneath the waves, ridges and hollows the water had simply covered. [242] Nothing had been erased; the paths had only changed elements, becoming roads for fish where once they carried feet. [261] She held the torn atlas page against the assembled sonar map, and the two aligned exactly, line for line. [280] That was when the delayed revelation finally surfaced, a truth she had carried unknowingly since the reef first paled. [299] Paths do not disappear; they become periodically eternal, surrendered at high tide and revealed again when the water withdraws. [318] She remembered then that the corals had not simply died; their skeletons remained, white roads waiting for new life. [337] Her tapping had never been a habit at all, but a question she kept asking the dark: are you still there? [358] That night she descended in the station's dive cage, palming the cold rails as the floodlights bled into the murk. [378] Below her stretched the bleached reef, ghostly and still, exactly where the map had always promised it would be. [397] She laid her glove against a dead coral spine and tapped the old diver's rhythm, slow and deliberate. [415] For a long moment nothing answered, only the drifting silence of a moored world holding its breath. [432] Then, faint against her palm, came a tapping back, irregular and alive, the click of something newly settled. [450] A young polyp, no wider than a coin, had taken root on the skeleton of the reef she had mourned. [470] The paths had not been lost; they had only gone quiet, waiting beneath for the conditions to return. [488] Surfacing, Mara understood her true task at last, the reason the monitoring station had drawn her like a tide. [507] She would not merely record which footpaths the rising water swallowed each season. [520] She would keep tapping, year after year, mapping the slow return of every drowned and patient road. [537] She took the torn atlas page from the wall and pressed it flat beside the assembled sonar map. [555] Together they formed a single record: what the land had been, and what the water now quietly kept. [573] Outside, the sea rose another hair, eternal in its rhythm, periodic in its mercy. [587] And Mara, no longer simply a witness to ruin, began to tap a slow welcome into the dark. [605]</story>
