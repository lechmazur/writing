<story>Mira returned to the village her grandmother had fled forty years before. [12] She came not as a tourist but as a diaspora returnee, hungry to rebuild what war had scattered. [30] The elders received her warmly, curious about the foreign girl with such familiar eyes. [44] They directed her to the old catacomb orchard at the edge of the hills. [58] There, fig and pomegranate trees grew over the dead, their roots snaking between bones like patient fingers. [75] Her grandmother had described this place every night, a story interrupted by exile and never finished. [91] Now Mira meant to finish it. [97] She had arrived through purposeful wandering, drifting across three countries, begging strangers for fragments of a faith she had nearly lost. [118] Somewhere along the road, between checkpoints and crowded ferries, she had stopped believing in anything but distance. [135] Each journey had been interrupted by closed borders, sickness, or grief, yet every detour had carried her closer. [153] The orchard met her with a cautious enchantment, sunlight trembling through leaves onto soil that seemed to hum. [171] An old caretaker named Yusuf handed her a trowel and pointed to a sunken slab. [186] Beneath it, he said, lay the tomb of the family's first believer, sealed when the wars began. [203] So began the slow work of excavating a tomb that had waited three generations. [217] On the first day she cleared the roots, apologizing softly each time her blade nicked an ancestor's rib. [235] On the second she struck stone, round and grooved, etched with hours she could not yet read. [252] By the third day she had freed it entirely: a sundial base, its bronze needle long stolen. [269] Yusuf wept when he saw it. [275] Her grandmother, he explained, had once been the keeper of this very dial before she ran. [291] Each morning the village had gathered around it to mark the hours of prayer. [305] When the dial fell silent, so did the people's faith, scattering like the family itself. [320] Mira understood then that her purpose had never been mere homecoming. [331] Her true motivation was to restore lost faith, hers and theirs alike. [343] She studied the grooves through the long afternoons, tracing every weathered numeral with her thumb. [358] The dead lay quiet beneath her, their bones threaded with roots that bore sweet fruit each summer. [375] It struck her that the orchard itself was a kind of rebuilt promise, life feeding patiently on loss. [393] Every fig she tasted seemed grown from the very faith her people had buried here. [408] From a market in the capital she had carried home a length of bronze, almost as if she had known. [428] Together she and Yusuf forged a new gnomon and seated it within the ancient stone. [443] They lifted the rebuilt sundial onto a pedestal among the fig roots and the gentle dead. [459] Word spread, and the villagers came warily, half afraid the old magic might mock them again. [475] They had learned to distrust beautiful things, for too many had been taken. [488] At dawn the new needle threw a clean shadow across the dial, and the first hour was named aloud. [507] An old woman began to pray, and others, hesitant, joined her long-interrupted words. [520] The journey her grandmother had broken off was, at last, continued by the granddaughter. [534] Mira knelt where roots cradled bone and felt the whole orchard exhale. [546] The enchantment was no longer cautious; it had ripened into something like belief. [559] By harvest the orchard's fruit was shared again at the foot of the dial, where strangers became neighbors. [577] Each day after, the shadow walked its faithful circle, and the village wandered back toward itself. [593] And Mira, no longer a returnee but simply a daughter, set her watch by the sun. [609]</story>
