<story>Mara had chronicled tidal pools for forty years, and she knew the grotto better than her own hands. [18] Tonight the cave breathed salt and starlight, its ancient mouth open to a sky scattered with cold fire. [36] Her granddaughter Lin sat across the largest pool, arms folded, waiting for an answer Mara did not want to give. [56] Lin had asked whether she should stay and inherit the chronicle, or leave for the mainland and a different life. [76] The question hung between them, and the pause that followed stretched long enough to hold a whole tide. [94] Mara did not fill the silence with persuasion. [102] Instead, warmly observant as ever, she let her eyes drift to the small green thing resting on the rock. [121] It was a jade cicada, carved by Mara's own grandmother, polished by a century of thumbs. [137] The old women of the island had always carried it through their own transition periods, turning it over when a decision refused to come. [161] Mara had learned long ago that she could not foresee a person's future by staring at it directly. [179] Truth, like a tidepool crab, fled the obvious gaze and revealed itself only through a tangential approach. [196] So she spoke not of Lin, but of the cicada. [206] "A cicada spends years underground," Mara said, lifting it to the starlight. [218] "It molts in darkness, sheds the only shell it has ever known, and then it climbs." [234] Lin's jaw stayed tight, but her eyes followed the green glint. [245] Mara set the carving beside a tidal pool and watched its reflection tremble. [258] "I have spent my life recording these pools," she went on, "not because the water matters, but because each pool is a small clock." [282] "The tide leaves, the creatures wait, the tide returns; the texture of time is written in those waitings." [300] That phrase was the heart of her work, the reason she had knelt on cold stone for four decades: to discover the texture of time, grain by grain. [328] Lin finally spoke, her voice low. [334] "You're telling me to leave." [339] "I am telling you what I see in a shell," Mara answered. [351] By circling the subject, she had let her own foresight surface. [362] She could feel, the way she felt an incoming tide in her knees, that Lin was already gone in spirit. [382] The girl belonged to a transition period, that trembling membrane between one life and the next. [398] To hold her here would be to keep a cicada in its broken husk. [412] Yet Mara also foresaw something gentler in the carving's trembling reflection. [423] The cicada always returns to the same trees its ancestors had sung in. [436] "Go," she said. [439] "But take the cicada." [443] Lin's tightness cracked, the way a husk splits along its seam. [454] "I can't take the chronicle's heart and then abandon you," she said. [466] "You aren't abandoning it," Mara said, warmly. [473] "You are carrying it into a new ground, where it will wait underground for years before it climbs." [491] The stars wheeled slowly over the grotto's ancient mouth, and the tide began its long return. [507] Lin picked up the jade cicada and pressed it to her palm, learning its century of warmth. [524] In that gesture Mara read the rest of the unspoken conversation, the whole of it folded into the pause they had just crossed. [547] Lin would leave, would struggle, would return one starlit year with a child of her own to teach the pools. [567] The chronicle would survive the way all living things survive, by molting. [579] Mara dipped her fingers into the pool one last time that night, feeling time pass through them like cool water. [599] She had her texture at last: not a single grain, but the soft seam where one era yields to another. [619] Above the grotto, the cold fires kept their slow watch, patient as the women who had counted them. [637]</story>
