<story>Ilse, a provenance researcher, climbed to the velvet observatory at midsummer, where the dome's plush walls swallowed every echo of her doubt. [22] Her assignment was simple on paper: authenticate a transparent violin made of ice crystal, an instrument the empire wished to claim. [43] It rested on a cushion, exhaling a faint chill, its strings catching the slow violet light of the longest evening. [63] The empire's agents believed the violin's lineage could justify a war, a borrowed heritage to prove their thrones were destined. [83] Ilse had spent her life tracing objects to their truths, but she carried a secret that no archive could explain. [103] She was accidentally telepathic with plants, a condition that began the morning a fern whispered her mother's grief into her wrists. [124] The observatory's old gardener had let ivy propagate across the brass instruments, and now the vines murmured to her constantly. [144] They told her the violin was not carved but grown, frozen from the tears of a single mulberry tree. [163] That tree, the ivy insisted, had ached for a child who once promised to return before the promise lost its glow. [184] Ilse felt the mulberry ache settle in her chest like cold syrup, sweet and unbearable. [199] To verify the story, she did what she always did in private, the method she never wrote in reports. [218] She brewed bitter coffee, tilted the cup, and read the future in the coffee stains spreading across porcelain. [236] The stains formed a map of perforated destiny, a path riddled with holes where choices had been punched out and discarded. [257] Through one of those holes she saw the empire melting the violin to mint its image onto coins. [275] Through another she saw herself refusing, the instrument surviving, a single seed of music allowed to propagate into a forest of song. [297] The vines pressed closer, begging her to choose the living future over the lucrative ruin. [312] Yet the empire paid her wages, and a researcher with debts learns to weigh a dream against an empire each dawn. [333] She lifted the ice violin and felt the mulberry tree's memory thrum through her telepathic skin. [349] The tree had wept this instrument so its song might travel where its roots could not, so its love might propagate. [370] If she signed the provenance the empire wanted, the violin would be unmade before midnight, its glow extinguished forever. [389] The coffee stains had shown her the perforation, the gap in destiny just wide enough for a different note. [408] Ilse understood now why the plants had chosen her, an accidental listener who could still translate sap into testimony. [427] She set down her pen and drew the bow across the crystal strings instead. [441] The violin sang a sound like thawing rivers, and the ivy shivered, propagating the melody leaf to leaf through the velvet dark. [463] In the resonance she heard the lost child's voice, finally arriving before the promise lost its glow. [480] The empire's agents would come at dawn, expecting paper, but Ilse had already weighed the dream. [496] She wrote a provenance no empire could spend: this object belongs to the tree, and the tree belongs to everyone. [516] Then she carried the ice violin into the garden, where it would not melt but root. [532] By midsummer's last light it had begun to grow downward, threading frozen veins into warm soil. [548] The mulberry ache eased as new shoots rose, each one humming the perforated destiny she had chosen to leave open. [568] Provenance, she finally understood, was never about where a thing came from, but what it was permitted to become. [587] And in that permission, the dream finally outweighed the empire, note by fragile note. [601]</story>
