<story>Mara climbed the moonlit summit alone, her boots crunching frost that no satellite had yet learned to melt. [18] As the crater inspector for the old observatory, she had walked this rim a hundred times, but never with such purpose. [39] Tonight she meant to photograph the last of something precious: a dawn unmarred by orbiting mirrors. [55] By morning, the space age would hang its newest constellation overhead, a lattice of reflectors built to banish night from the cities below. [78] After that, no true darkness would survive, and no honest dawn either. [90] Mara was purposefully curious, the kind of woman who measured a thing precisely because she feared losing it. [108] In her glove she carried the old nightwatch's hourglass sand grain, a single amber speck pressed into a glass locket. [128] The watchman had given it to her before the electronic clocks replaced him. [141] "One grain," he had said, "is enough to outlast a whole hourglass, if you know which moment to keep." [160] She set her camera on its tripod and began her ritual of timekeeping, for radios were forbidden near the great mirror lest static blur the exposures. [186] Instead, Mara worked using chemical reactions for timed messages, the way the night crews always had. [202] She uncorked a slender vial and tipped powder into clear fluid, and a pale green glow bloomed, counting down its own slow fade. [225] When the green died, she would open the shutter; when the second vial flared amber, she would close it. [244] Down the mountain, her colleague Reyes watched for these glows, reading her progress like a slow alphabet of light. [263] Each fading color told him she was alive, patient, and still set on her stubborn vigil. [279] The hours thinned. [282] Satellites stitched the dark with silent threads, and Mara photographed them too, cataloguing the very thing that would soon erase her sky. [304] She did not hate them; she only wanted to remember what they were replacing. [318] Near the end, the cold deepened, and her chemicals slowed, their reactions sluggish in the frost. [334] She cupped each vial against her chest, willing the heat of her body to keep the small lights honest a little longer. [356] She had only to outlast the night, and the watchman's words steadied her: one moment, kept whole. [373] Then it came, the dawning hush she had climbed for. [383] The wind dropped, the stars dimmed one by one, and the whole summit seemed to hold its breath as the first grey light crept over the crater's far wall. [412] In that silence, color bled slowly into the sky, rose over ash, gold over rose. [427] Mara tipped the last vial, and as its glow guttered out, she opened the shutter to the dying night. [446] She held it open through the long, breathless minute, the grain in her locket catching the new light like a captured spark. [468] When the amber vial finally flared, she closed the shutter, and far below, Reyes saw the signal and understood it was done. [490] Within the hour, the mirrors rose in the east, and the sky brightened past any star, past any dawn it had ever known. [513] Mara packed her camera with steady hands, though her eyes stung. [524] She had not stopped the future; no inspector could. [533] But on a single frame of film she had kept one moment whole, exactly as the watchman promised: the last honest dawn, preserved in silver and silence. [560] She pressed the locket once, the lone grain warm against her palm, and began the long walk down into a brighter, lesser world. [583] Behind her, the summit fell quiet again, but it was a different quiet now, a hush without darkness to give it meaning. [605] Mara carried her photograph and her single grain into the morning, the only soul who would remember what the sky had been before the lights outlasted the stars. [633]</story>
