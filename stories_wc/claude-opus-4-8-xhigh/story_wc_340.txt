<story>Mirel rode the brass elevator alone, an ascending servant in a spire of ice that pierced the clouds. [18] She carried the late airship captain's goggles, cradled like a sleeping bird against her apron. [33] Her mistress had died on the tenth floor, and Mirel had been summoned to dress the body. [50] The elevator climbed slowly, one floor at a time, and Mirel counted them the way other people counted prayers. [69] On the first floor, the cage shuddered, and frost flowers bloomed across the glass. [83] Mirel had served the household for nine years, and she had loved her mistress with the affectionate detachment of someone who knew she would always be the one left behind. [113] By the third floor, she had opened the captain's satchel, warmly inquisitive even now, unable to stop herself from wondering. [133] Inside lay a small book, its pages crowded with symbols that meant nothing she could read. [149] It was an encrypted diary, she realized, the captain's secret kept even from the woman who had loved him. [168] The captain had been Mirel's mistress's husband, lost three winters ago when his vessel struck the spire's frozen mast. [187] By the fifth floor, Mirel held the goggles to her eyes, the same lenses he had worn against the wind. [207] Through the tinted glass, the elevator's lamp split into a hundred fragments. [219] And there, through that scattered light, the diary's symbols began to unfold. [231] The captain had not written in code at all; he had written in a script readable only when seen through his own prismatic lenses. [255] By the seventh floor, Mirel was reading. [262] She had always wanted to seek out the joy beneath anguish, to find some warmth at the cold center of grief, and here at last the words gave it to her. [293] The captain wrote of his wife's laughter, of the small servant girl who hummed in the stairwells, of mornings when the clouds glowed pink below the windows. [320] He had hidden his happiness so no storm or rival could ever steal it, encoding delight as though it were treasure. [341] By the ninth floor, Mirel was weeping and smiling at once, holding both feelings the way she held the goggles, gently and without keeping them. [366] The last entry named her: the girl Mirel, who he hoped would one day climb high enough to read this. [386] He had trusted that an ascending servant, curious and kind, would finish what his death had interrupted. [403] The elevator reached the tenth floor and stopped. [411] Beyond the doors lay her mistress, still and white in a room walled with ice. [426] Mirel stepped out, the goggles in one hand, the diary in the other. [439] She set the lenses upon the dead woman's brow, so that even in the dark she might see the way her husband had written her into joy. [466] Then she opened the diary on the still chest and read aloud, slowly, letting the buried gladness rise into the freezing air. [488] She did not grieve so much as marvel, holding her sorrow at a tender arm's length. [504] Outside the high windows, the clouds turned the color of warm bread, and the spire's ice caught the dawn and threw it everywhere. [527] In that scattered light, the whole frozen room seemed to unfold like a flower long held shut. [544] Mirel finished the final page and closed the book softly. [554] She would carry these secrets down all ten floors and keep them, not as a wound, but as an inheritance of light. [576] A servant ascends, she thought, even when she is only going home. [588] And smiling still, Mirel pressed the call button and began, gently, her long descent. [602]</story>
