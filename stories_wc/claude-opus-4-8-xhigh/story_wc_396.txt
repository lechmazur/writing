<story>Vesna was the calmly intuitive peacekeeper of the lighthouse orchard, a grove of apple trees perched above the shifting ice floes. [21] Dark filaments laced her skin, a courageous anomaly she had inherited from a grandmother no one dared name. [39] Those shadows let her sense the fear hiding under every threat, the grief curled inside every shout. [56] The settlement on the ice trusted her to soften their quarrels before they froze into feuds. [72] For years a crystal ball had helped her, showing the consequences of words not yet spoken. [88] Then a brawler hurled it against the lamp-room stone, and it burst into a hundred glittering pieces. [105] Now only one broken crystal ball fragment lay in her palm, cold and clouded and silent. [121] Without it she could feel emotions but never trace where they would lead, and her gift felt half-blind. [139] Yet her purpose remained simple: to map empathy onto action, to turn what she felt into choices that kept people whole. [160] She had buried too many neighbors to believe that good intentions alone could stop a blade. [176] One night, polishing the fragment, she noticed its surface reflected not the lamp but a dim, unfamiliar room. [194] She recognized it as the old vintage store on the mainland quay, its walls hung with tarnished mirrors. [212] Somehow the shattered visions had scattered there, surviving only as glimpses in vintage store mirrors. [227] If she could gather those glimpses, she might reconstitute the crystal ball's sight inside the lone fragment. [244] She crossed the groaning floes at dawn and entered the cramped, dust-smelling shop. [257] The owner, an old woman wrapped in furs, only nodded, as if she had expected the peacekeeper for years. [276] Vesna did not ask how the old woman knew her; intuition was a language they both spoke. [293] Mirror after mirror showed Vesna fractured scenes: a raised fist, a weeping child, a harbor swallowed by flames. [311] Calmly, intuitively, she held the fragment to each glass and let the images pour into the crystal. [328] Piece by reflected piece, the fragment warmed, its cloud clearing into a single living eye. [343] The old woman pressed a hand to the glass and warned that sight without feeling was only another kind of blindness. [364] She returned to the orchard just as the season's worst dispute erupted beneath the trees. [379] Two families stood among the apple boughs, blaming each other for a boy who had fallen through the thinning ice. [399] A father raised his harpoon, demanding an answer, and the whole orchard went still. [413] During the weighted breath before reply, Vesna lifted the reconstituted fragment to her eye. [427] Within it she saw two futures branching from this single instant. [438] In one the harpoon flew and both families bled into the black water below. [452] In the other a single honest word turned their grief into shared mourning. [465] The shadows in her veins flared, and she felt the father's terror as though it were her own. [483] She stepped between them and named his fear aloud, saying no hand had pushed the boy, that the ice itself was the only enemy here. [508] The harpoon lowered, the held breath released, and the two families wept instead of warring. [523] In that moment Vesna understood that the crystal had never made her wise. [536] It only showed the consequences; the courage to feel them and still act gently was always hers. [553] She set the mended fragment on the windowsill of the lamp-room, where it caught the orchard light. [570] Below, the ice floes shifted and groaned, but the settlement upon them held together for one more fragile season. [589] And Vesna stood among the apple trees, mapping each felt sorrow into one more steady, courageous act. [606]</story>
