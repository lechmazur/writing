<story>The wandering fireworks designer arrived at the blooming fortress at dusk, when its ivy-throttled walls unfurled white blossoms that opened only as the light failed. [25] His name was Pell, and he chants while walking, a low rhythm of half-remembered names that kept his feet honest on long roads. [48] He had come because of a letter that was not a letter at all but a note in mosaic, tiles set into the fortress courtyard that spelled a plea only a craftsman of color could read. [84] "Come and unmake the dark," it said, each syllable rendered in chips of cobalt and bone. [100] Inside, the garrison had given up. [106] The captain met him with hollow eyes and a confession: this was during the hush of a night with no stars, the night the enemy had promised to scale the walls and end them. [140] "We have no banners left, no hope," she said. [149] Pell smiled, serious and playful at once. [156] "Hope is only the refusal to fall," he answered. [165] "I came to defy the gravity of hopelessness, and that, madam, is my trade." [179] He asked for the strangest things. [185] He wanted a fire spinner's kerosene rag knot, the dense oily fist that street performers soaked and lit, and the soldiers brought him one from a dead juggler's pack. [214] He weighed it in his palm, breathing its sharp perfume. [224] Then he walked the ramparts, chanting, and the men watched him filter the night. [238] He did not filter with sieves or cloth. [246] He filtered through ambient awareness, listening to the way wind curled over the merlons, tasting where the air went still, learning which shadows held grappling hooks and which held only fear. [277] By this he mapped the assault before it came. [286] "Color is a language," he told the captain, packing powder into paper shells. [299] "And tonight we will say something they cannot answer." [308] He set his charges along the blooming wall, tucking shells among the night-flowers so each blossom cradled a seed of light. [329] The kerosene knot he saved for last, threading it onto a long pole as a torch and a fuse-bearer both. [349] When the ladders kissed the stone, he lit it. [358] Pell ran the rampart spinning the burning knot, a wheel of flame that turned his chant into a war-song, and where he touched a wick a flower of fire answered. [388] The fortress bloomed twice now, once in petals and once in sparks. [400] Crimson chrysanthemums burst above the gate; green willows of fire wept down the curtain wall; and a single great blue peony opened where the mosaic note lay, lighting its tiles so the whole courtyard read its plea aloud in color. [440] The attackers, expecting silence and slaughter, found instead a sky that laughed at them. [454] Their nerve, which is only borrowed gravity, let go. [463] Ladders fell. [465] Men fled the strange exuberance of a stronghold that refused to die quietly. [478] By the time the last ember sank, the night with no stars had been given a hundred temporary ones. [497] The captain found Pell sitting on the parapet, the spent rag knot smoking at his feet, his face streaked with soot and something close to joy. [523] "You defeated them with a celebration," she said, half accusing. [533] "I reminded them, and you, that despair is a choice dressed as a fact," he said. [549] "Anyone can be afraid in the dark; it takes craft to be glad in it." [564] He stood, gathering his packs, already chanting the next name on the next road. [578] "Keep the mosaic," he called back. [584] "When the stars hide again, read it, and remember what color can do." [597] Then the wandering designer walked out through the blooming gate, his low song fading into the dawn, leaving a fortress that had learned how to bloom against the dark. [626]</story>
