<story>After luck became measurable, the city issued everyone a brass gauge. [11] Mine read low. [14] So I went where unlucky people go, the garden maze on the hill, hoping its hedges might lend me what numbers could not. [37] There I met the withered vine dresser. [44] He was wisely pragmatic, the sort who pruned hope and disease with the same calm shears. [60] "Your gauge means nothing here," he said, not unkindly. [69] "Gardens know their gardeners, and they will know you soon enough." [80] I asked what he meant. [85] He led me along a corridor of green, where roses opened and rotted in the same breath, a blooming, withering riot that smelled of both honey and decay. [113] "Watch," he said. [116] He drew from his coat a collection of marbles, every one cracked, fine seams running through their glass like frozen lightning. [137] "These were my luck, before luck had a number," he said. [148] "Each marble marks a small victory I once celebrated far too elaborately." [160] He smiled at the memory. [165] "A seedling survived frost, so I held a feast for one. [176] A vine fruited, so I lit lanterns and danced until dawn." [187] Then he told me his secret. [193] For forty years he had deceived the maze. [201] "When a plant died, I replaced it before the owners noticed, and I mended the gap so cleanly that none could tell the dead from the living." [228] "That was my deception, and I tended it like any other bloom." [240] I did not understand why he confessed. [247] He knelt and began to polish each cracked marble against his sleeve, until the seams caught light and glowed. [266] "The deception broke the day the gauges arrived," he said. [276] "The owners measured their garden's luck and found it high, higher than my honest neighbors' plots." [292] "They credited the numbers, not the hands. [299] So I let the lie stand, mended and shining, to prove a quieter thing." [313] He pressed a marble into my palm. [320] "Gardens know their gardeners," he repeated. [326] "Watch where you walk, and the maze will answer." [335] I wandered the green corridors, and the dying roses turned toward me as I passed, leaning the way a face turns toward a familiar voice. [360] Where my low-luck shadow fell, buds I had never touched began to swell and open. [375] I knelt to a struggling lily and only wished it well, and by my next breath its petals had unfurled, white as an apology. [399] The gauge in my pocket did not move, but the garden did. [411] When I found him again, the old man was fading, his skin grey as the dusk-bound ivy. [428] "Luck can be counted," he whispered, "but care cannot, and the maze counts only care." [443] "My deception was kindness wearing a false face, and the plants forgave it because my hands were honest." [461] He closed my fingers over the cracked marble. [469] "Keep this. [471] Polish it when you win something small, and celebrate that nothing as if it were everything." [487] By morning the vine dresser was gone, folded back into the soil he had mended so long. [504] I buried his marbles at the maze's heart, all but the one he gave me. [519] Now I tend the corridors myself, replacing what dies, mending each gap with patient, dishonest grace. [535] The owners read their high gauges and praise their luck. [545] I say nothing. [548] I only polish my single cracked marble each evening, marking the day's small mercies with a private, elaborate joy. [567] It is a small victory, and I honor it as he taught me, grandly, for no one but the hedges to see. [589] The roses bloom and wither in the same hour, and they lean toward me when I pass. [606] That is proof enough. [610] The garden knows its gardener. [615]</story>
