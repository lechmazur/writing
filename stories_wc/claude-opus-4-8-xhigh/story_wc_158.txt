<story>In the hollow of a gargantuan shell that the tide had abandoned upon a forgotten reef, there lived an insomniac poet named Wren. [23] She could not sleep, and so she wrote. [31] Her only companion was a galaxy postcard, its glossy spiral of stars curling at the edges from centuries of being held. [52] The postcard had no sender and no message, only a single line of faded ink: Wait for me across centuries of longing. [74] Wren did not know who had written it, but she had decided, long ago, that the words were a promise meant for her. [97] Sleepless, she had endured one war after another, watching reef-dwellers fight over coral territory until the water ran bitter. [116] Her gift, the only one she trusted, was to photosynthesize pain into beauty. [129] She took the grief of others as sunlight and turned it, leaf by leaf, into poems. [145] Each night she would ensconce herself deeper within the shell's pearled chambers, where her voice echoed and multiplied. [163] The reef knew her as the shy one, the small voice that never raised itself in anger. [180] Yet she was shyly unstoppable, returning each dusk with a new verse no tide could silence. [196] The warring clans had ignored her for generations, dismissing the poet in the shell as harmless. [212] But beauty, she believed, was the slowest and surest weapon. [222] So Wren began to sing. [227] She sent her poems out via half-sung lullabies carried on an off-key wind, the strange thin gale that wound through the reef at midnight. [251] The wind warbled and cracked, but it carried her words to the cribs of every clan's children. [268] The young ones learned her lullabies before they learned to hate. [279] Years bled into decades, and the children grew, humming verses that praised the enemy's gardens as their own. [297] When at last the clans gathered to negotiate, they found they could not remember the original insult. [314] They remembered only the songs. [319] And so was born the blossom armistice, signed not with ink but with the planting of a single anemone between the rival reefs. [342] It bloomed, and where one flower stood, a thousand followed, until the warring water filled with quiet color. [360] Old soldiers wept into the current, unable to say whether they grieved the lost years or rejoiced at the gardens, and the difference no longer seemed to matter. [388] Wren watched from her shell, feeling something she had no word for, something like reef-shadow joy. [404] It was the dim, dappled gladness of one who labors in darkness and sees, finally, the light fall sideways through the water. [426] Still, she did not sleep. [431] The promise on the galaxy postcard kept her awake, its faded line whispering across centuries of longing. [448] One night, the off-key wind returned, but it carried no song of hers. [461] Instead it bore a melody she had never written, half-sung and trembling, the missing harmony to every lullaby she had ever released. [483] Someone, somewhere across the dark reefs, had been listening for centuries, learning her voice, answering at last. [500] Wren rose, the postcard pressed to her chest, and understood at last who had written the promise. [517] It was the reef itself, singing back the love she had spent centuries planting. [531] Every grief she had photosynthesized had grown into a voice, and now those voices returned to cradle her. [549] For the first time in her long, sleepless life, Wren felt her eyelids grow heavy. [564] She laid the galaxy postcard against the pearl wall, where it caught the bioluminescent glow and seemed, briefly, a real sky. [585] Then she let the half-sung lullaby of the whole reef wrap around her like a tide. [601] And the insomniac poet, who had turned every wound into a garden, finally slept. [615]</story>
