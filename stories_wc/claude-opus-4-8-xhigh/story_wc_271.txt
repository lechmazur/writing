<story>Naila tended the arboretum perched on a seaside cliff, where salt wind bent the cypress into prayers. [17] The grounds had passed to her after the last lighthouse keeper retired, leaving the coast without a single human watcher but the trees. [40] She was quietly talented with growing things, coaxing fig and myrtle from soil that should have refused them. [58] Few visitors knew what she had survived. [65] Years before, a drone strike had erased her wedding in a single white flash, taking the groom, the guests, the music. [86] She had walked out of the rubble carrying only a crumpled photograph, its corner scorched, the faces inside still laughing. [106] She kept it in her breast pocket the way other people kept hearts. [119] The strike had left her with a body that froze. [129] A door slamming, a high engine overhead, and she would lock rigid, breath sealed, the world narrowing to a needle. [149] Doctors named it and shrugged. [154] It was the arboretum that taught her otherwise. [162] She had learned to read herself through somatic markers - the cold creeping up her wrists, the tightening behind the sternum, the way her tongue pressed the roof of her mouth before the freeze arrived. [196] When she felt them gathering, she would kneel and put both palms flat against the earth between the roots. [215] There, in the soil's patience, she found what she came to call a pearl of stillness: a small round calm she could hold against the panic, dense and cool, until the storm passed through and left her standing. [253] She did not pretend the calm undid anything. [261] The dead stayed dead. [265] That was the discipline she practiced and, eventually, taught - to anchor optimism in realism, never to lie about the wreck, only to insist that something green could still be grown beside it. [297] People began to come. [301] Other survivors, mostly, who had heard of the woman on the cliff who could teach the body to stop betraying itself. [322] They arrived hollow-eyed, flinching at gulls. [328] Naila walked them along the gravel paths and named the trees. [339] She showed them where to press their hands. [347] She taught them their own somatic markers, the private alphabet of dread that each body wrote differently. [364] One autumn a young man named Idris came, mute with grief. [375] He had lost a brother and would not speak of how. [386] When the foghorn sounded - the automated one that had replaced the keeper's hand - he froze so completely that Naila thought his heart had stopped. [410] She knelt with him and pressed his palm to the dirt. [421] "Find the pearl," she said. [426] "It is not hope that lies. [432] It is the small round quiet that tells the truth and holds anyway." [445] He stayed a season. [449] He learned the figs, the myrtle, the cold rising in his wrists before the freeze. [464] On the morning he finally wept instead of locking, Naila took out her crumpled photograph and showed him the laughing faces. [485] "I do not keep this to mourn," she said. [494] "I keep it so I never decorate the loss. [503] They were real. [506] The flash was real. [510] And the trees are real too." [516] Idris touched the scorched corner and nodded. [523] When he left, he carried a fig cutting and his own pearl, wherever he had buried it inside himself. [542] Naila stayed on the cliff. [547] The last lighthouse keeper had once kept ships from the rocks; she had inherited a smaller, stranger watch - keeping people from the rocks inside them. [572] Each dawn she walked the bent rows and pressed her palms to the soil out of habit now, not need. [592] The freeze still came sometimes, when a low jet crossed the sea. [604] But she had her marker, her pearl, her honest green answer to the white flash. [619] She did not promise her visitors that the world was kind. [630] She promised only that stillness could be grown, like a tree, in poor ground, by quietly talented hands, after everything. [650]</story>
