<story>Wren, a thoughtful gravestone carver, kept her chisels in the ancient greenhouse at dusk, where amber light pooled across cracked glass. [21] She had come here every evening across the butterfly migration, when monarchs streamed south like a living, orange river. [40] Her grandmother had carved stones here too, and her grandmother's ghost had never quite left. [55] The old woman appeared among the ferns most nights, a flicker of silver guiding Wren's hand. [71] This spectral mentorship had begun the winter Wren buried her, and it had not ended since. [87] "You carve names," the ghost said, "but you do not yet know what they weigh." [102] Wren wanted, more than anything, to discover the shape of collective grief. [114] The whole village brought their dead to her, and each loss seemed separate, yet some larger pattern hummed beneath them. [134] She worked fiercely meditative, hour upon hour, listening for that shape in the strike of mallet on stone. [152] Her grandmother watched, patient as the slow turning of seasons. [162] One dusk, the ghost set a woven ribbon roll upon the workbench, its threads faded to the colors of old letters. [183] "Unroll it," she said. [187] Each ribbon, Wren found, bore a stitched line of verse. [197] She recognized them at once. [202] Her family had kept a custom of trading favorite poems at dinner, each member reciting a line until the table became a quilt of borrowed voices. [228] Her grandmother had stitched every one onto these ribbons, year after year. [240] "Read them in order," the ghost urged, "and retrace the evenings." [251] So Wren began to retrace the dinners of her childhood, ribbon by ribbon, voice by remembered voice. [268] Her father's gruff sonnet, her mother's quiet psalm, her brother's nonsense rhyme that always made the cousins laugh. [286] Each was gone now, carved into stone by her own steady hands. [298] As she read, the greenhouse filled with a thundering calm, a silence so vast it seemed to press against the glass. [319] Outside, the monarchs settled on the ferns and rafters, thousands of wings folding shut. [333] Wren understood then what her grandmother had meant. [341] Grief was not a single stone but a migration, countless small flights leaving and returning along the same ancient route. [361] Each poem traded at her table had been one wingbeat in a longer journey, and so had each name she carved. [382] The shape she had sought was not a wall of separate sorrows but a current that carried them all together. [402] "You see it now," her grandmother said, and her silver form began to thin. [416] The migration would end soon, and so, Wren realized, would the visits. [428] "Stay," Wren whispered, though she already knew the answer. [437] "You no longer need me," the ghost said gently. [446] "Carve what you have learned." [451] When the last monarch lifted from the rafters, the silver flicker was gone. [464] Wren rolled the ribbons away and took up her chisel. [474] On a wide blank stone meant for no single grave, she began to carve a river of wings. [492] It would stand at the village center, she decided, a monument to the grief they all shared and survived. [511] Beneath the carving she stitched her grandmother's first poem into the wet line of mortar, ribbon and all. [529] The greenhouse held its thundering calm around her, patient as ever. [540] Wren worked fiercely meditative into the dark, and for the first time the silence did not feel like absence. [559] It felt like a table set for many, waiting only for the next voice to begin. [575] Each spring the monarchs would return, and she would teach the village children to trade poems at her own dinner table. [596] The custom would carry on, one borrowed voice answering the next, season after migrating season. [611]</story>
