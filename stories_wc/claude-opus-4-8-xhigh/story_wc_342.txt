<story>Mara could not tell one face from another, yet portraits were the only language her hands had ever learned. [19] The scholars' guild had hollowed their library inside a single ancient tree trunk, and tonight the building around it was condemned. [40] At dawn the cranes would swing, but for now there was only the hush before the collapse, a heightened stillness in which dust hung like suspended stars. [67] Mara had bribed the foreman for one final hour inside. [77] She had come to finish the work the guild had begun three centuries before: to mirror the sky inside these wooden walls. [99] Their secret was kept in a book safe carved into the trunk's heartwood, a hollow volume that looked like any other spine on the shelves. [124] Mara pressed the brass clasp she had memorized by touch, since she could never trust her eyes, and the false book opened. [146] Inside lay a centuries-old coin, its surface worn smooth to the casual glance but alive with faint indentations only fingertips could read. [168] The guild had engraved the entire night sky into that metal, fractally precise, each star branching into smaller stars no engraver's blade should have managed. [193] Mara closed her useless eyes and let her fingers walk the coin, translating its pits and ridges into a map only she now knew how to craft. [220] Her whole life faces had dissolved into smooth, unreadable coins, but constellations had never betrayed her. [236] She climbed the spiral stair worn into the inner bark and began to paint the domed ceiling overhead. [254] Each indentation under her thumb became a pinprick of silver, placed by feel, until the wooden vault held the firmament the guild had died protecting. [279] She worked faster than she ever had, because the building's last hour was leaking away. [294] Yet near the zenith she found the limit she had always feared. [306] The coin's center was the densest region, where stars folded into stars beyond what any hand could divide. [324] Her fingers could feel the infinite branching, but her brush could not follow it; the fractal went deeper than human craft could ever reach. [348] For one trembling moment she wanted to weep at the wall between intention and limit. [363] Then she understood the guild's true lesson, hidden in that unpaintable core. [375] A sky mirrored inside walls was never meant to be complete, only honestly attempted. [389] She left the zenith bare, a small dark circle, the exact shape of everything a person cannot hold. [407] It was, she realized, the first true portrait she had ever made: not of a face she could not see, but of the limit every face had taught her. [436] Below, the foreman called up that the hour was gone. [446] Mara descended, returned the coin to its book safe, and pressed the clasp shut for the last time. [464] She could not save the tree, but she carried the coin's map now in the muscle of her hands. [483] Outside, in the heightened stillness before the strike, the demolition crew waited like mourners. [497] Mara stood among them, unable to recognize a single face, and for once she did not mind. [514] When the wall fell, the dome cracked open and its painted stars scattered into the real dawn, briefly mirroring the sky that had outlasted the walls. [540] For one breath the hollow tree became a lantern, the guild's three-hundred-year wish made visible to anyone still watching. [559] She had discovered her limits, and inside them, finally, she had found something fractally precise enough to call finished. [578] Then it was only rubble and morning, and a woman who had learned that the truest craft lay in knowing exactly where the hand must stop. [604]</story>
