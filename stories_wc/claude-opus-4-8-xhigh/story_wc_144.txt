<story>The repair shop crouched at the edge of the mist-coated rail depot, where steam and fog argued over which would own the morning. [23] Inside, Maren worked by feel, her hands moving with the magnetic calm of someone who had stopped fearing the dark. [43] She had been blind for nine years, yet she still oversaw every repair that passed through her door. [61] This morning a courier had set an artificial hummingbird drone on her workbench, its tiny wings stuttering, its body warm with a fault it could not name. [88] The drone belonged to the harbor authority, and it carried the only copy of the navigation feedback for a ship whose anchor would lift at noon for its maiden voyage. [118] If Maren could not decode that feedback into a usable direction, the ship would sail blind into the shoals beyond the bay. [140] She smiled at the irony, for she of all people understood sailing blind. [153] Maren did not read the drone's data the way the sighted engineers did. [166] Years ago she had built a row of water features along her bench, shallow glass basins fed by a slow, singing pump. [188] The hummingbird's wings emitted a thin beam when active, and by lowering the drone over the basins she could let that beam strike the moving water. [214] Examining the light refraction through the water features was her sight; the ripples bent the beam, and the bent beam warmed her cheeks and palms in patterns she had memorized like a language. [247] Where a sighted woman saw color, Maren felt heat shift across her skin, each flicker a syllable. [264] She set the wounded drone above the first basin and held her breath. [277] The beam jittered, broke, and scattered, and the warmth on her palms came in nonsense bursts. [293] There was the fault: a torn membrane in the wing was bleeding the beam into chaos. [309] She found the tear with a fingertip no wider than a wish and reached for her smallest patch. [327] The patch was a sliver of pressed resin she had made herself, warm and pliant under her thumb. [345] Working slowly, the heartfelt artisan pressed it over the wound, smoothing the edges until the membrane drew taut again. [364] She loved these tiny machines the way some loved children, and she would not send one back unhealed. [382] When she lowered the drone over the basin again, the beam held. [394] The warmth crossed her palms in clean, ordered waves, and she began to translate. [408] Each refraction told her a heading; the feedback was not numbers but a map written in bent light. [426] Northeast past the second buoy, she read, then a slow turn to clear the sandbar the charts had forgotten. [445] Her purpose had always been this: to decode feedback into direction, to turn a fault into a path forward. [464] Outside, the depot bell rang the half hour, its sound muffled by mist into something almost gentle. [481] The courier shifted on his feet near the door, anxious for noon. [493] Maren spoke the headings aloud while he wrote, her voice as even as the pump's quiet song. [510] She felt no urgency, only the magnetic calm of a craftswoman who trusts her hands. [525] When the last heading was spoken, she returned the hummingbird to his cupped palms. [539] "Run," she said, and the courier ran, the repaired drone whirring at his shoulder into the fog. [556] Maren stayed at her bench, listening to the depot breathe steam. [567] At noon she felt the distant tremor in the rails as the harbor cranes turned and the great anchor lifted, dripping, from the water. [591] Somewhere beyond the mist, a ship took her first true breath and turned northeast, exactly as the light had told her. [612] Maren turned a basin's pump higher and let the water sing, content to see the world the only way it had ever truly shown itself to her. [639]</story>
