<story>Maral tasted grief in the morning bread, a salt that no recipe explained. [13] She was a chef who could taste memories, and the fortress fed her too many. [28] Her gift was a burden as much as a craft, each meal a stranger's confession on her tongue. [46] Rain had come overnight, rare as honesty in the desert, and the dunes had shifted. [61] By dawn the sand had pulled back like a sheet from a sleeper, revealing the coral icebound pavilion beneath the dunes. [82] No one in the garrison remembered building it; the structure simply waited, frozen, in a land that forgot the sea. [102] The captain summoned her because she alone could read what the place contained. [115] Inside, the cold held rows of pale offerings, and each one hummed against her teeth with stored remorse. [133] Touch was dangerous here, for the remorse was emotionally contagious through touch, leaking from soldier to soldier like spilled ink. [153] A young guard had already pressed his palm to the coral, and now he wept without knowing the name of his sorrow. [175] Maral understood her work at once: she had come to sandpaper rough memories, to wear their splinters smooth. [193] She had done it before in kitchens, dissolving a widow's bitterness into broth until the woman could swallow her past. [213] But the pavilion was older and harder than any grief she had cooked. [226] To learn its grain, she studied the floor, analyzing tidal pool formations etched into the ancient coral. [243] The rings showed where water had once gathered and drained, where pain pooled and where it found release. [261] She traced the patterns with a spoon, mapping the channels remorse used to travel. [275] The fortress walls, sturdy and brittle at once, sweated rainwater that froze into thin needles overhead. [291] The desert fortress after rain smelled of wet stone and waking salt, a scent that always loosened the buried. [310] Maral built a fire from saxaul wood and set her great copper pot upon it. [325] Into the broth she shaved slivers of the coral, each one a frozen confession. [339] The steam carried voices: a betrayal, an abandoned child, a promise broken at a well. [354] She tasted, and the memories rushed her, sturdy enough to stand on, brittle enough to crack. [370] Her plan was to subsume the remorse, to let the broth absorb each splinter and hold it where no hand could spread it. [393] As the pot reduced, the contagion thinned; the weeping guard quieted, his palm no longer burning. [409] One by one the soldiers came and drank, and what had been jagged in them went smooth. [426] They did not forget their wrongs, but the edges no longer cut. [438] Maral worked until the last sliver dissolved, her own hands trembling with borrowed sorrow. [452] She had taken the fortress's remorse into herself, the way a riverbed takes the flood. [467] The captain asked what she felt now, and she could only say it tasted of an ending. [484] Outside, the rain stopped and the dunes began to creep back, ready to bury the pavilion again. [501] This had happened at the close of an unspoken dream, the half-thought every garrison shared of being clean. [519] They had never voiced the wish, yet here it was, finished, the bowl scraped empty. [534] Maral carried the heavy broth-memory in her chest, sturdy as the walls, brittle as the ice. [550] She would not unburden it; some grief was meant to be held, not healed. [564] As the sand reclaimed the coral, she climbed the ramparts and looked east, toward kitchens that still needed her. [583] The fortress stood lighter, its soldiers steadier, and the dream they never spoke of dissolved into morning. [600] Maral wiped her spoon, tasted nothing but salt now, and understood that the salt, at last, was only her own. [620]</story>
