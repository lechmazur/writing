<story>Mara was a cool-headed fisher who cast her lines not for fish but for sound. [15] Each dusk she waded into the sound wave garden, where ripples of music grew like reeds from the black water. [35] She had learned long ago that the garden answered only the patient, so she never hurried. [51] In her belt she carried a single rail spike, salvaged from a railway the sea had long since swallowed. [70] Struck against her boat, the spike rang a clean note that the garden recognized as her name. [87] Mara had a strange gift: she transliterates emotions, turning each feeling into letters she could hum. [103] Grief became a low rolling vowel; hope, a bright clipped consonant. [114] Tonight she came for something rarer than any harvest of song. [125] She had come to catch the echo of a goddess. [135] The old fishers spoke of Aiyala, who once sang the stars into their fixed and patient shapes. [152] But lately the heavens had grown restless. [159] It was the season when patterns break themselves, and the sleepwalking constellations had begun to drift. [175] Mara watched the Fisher's Net unstitch itself overhead, its stars wandering in a slow zigzag across the dark. [193] Each star moved as a sleeper moves, eyes shut, following some dream older than the sky. [209] The fishers feared this drift, but Mara understood it. [218] The constellations were not lost; they were searching for the voice that had first arranged them. [234] If she could summon Aiyala's echo, the stars would hear, and settle home. [247] The trouble was that a goddess could not be called by sound. [259] An echo answers only what is unspoken, so Mara would have to summon her by voicing silence. [276] She set down the rail spike and let her boat go still upon the garden. [291] Then, cool and unhurried, she began to shape the spaces between her breaths. [304] She transliterated her own longing into a silence that had a precise weight and edge. [319] Where another singer would have sung, she withheld; where a hymn would swell, she carved a hollow. [336] The sound wave garden trembled, its reeds bending toward the absence she made. [349] Slowly, from that voiced silence, a melody began to answer. [359] It was a feral lullaby, tender and toothed, the kind a mother wolf might sing to quiet a storm. [378] Mara held the rail spike loosely, feeling it warm in her palm as the echo gathered around it. [396] The spike, born of iron and old journeys, made a fine anchor for a wandering voice. [412] Aiyala's echo wound itself along the metal and poured into the night. [424] Overhead, the sleepwalking constellations slowed their zigzag. [431] Star by star, the Fisher's Net heard the lullaby and began to dream its old shape back into place. [450] Mara did not break her silence to celebrate; she knew the spell lived only as long as she withheld her voice. [471] She breathed the shapes of patience and let the goddess finish the work. [484] When the last star drifted home, the lullaby thinned to a single held note, then to nothing. [501] The echo had spent itself, and the goddess slept again inside her own creation. [515] Mara opened her eyes to a sky stitched firm and bright above the quiet garden. [530] She tapped the rail spike once against the hull, sounding her name into the calm. [545] Only then did she let herself smile, transliterating relief into one soft, rolling syllable. [559] The cool-headed fisher gathered her lines and turned for shore. [569] She had caught no fish tonight, only the echo of a goddess and the safe return of the stars. [588] It was, she decided, the finest catch a patient soul could hope to make when patterns break themselves. [606]</story>
