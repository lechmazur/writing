Vael had spent forty years as the forever war's peace negotiator, and forty years had taught her that endings were merely intermissions. [22] On the ancient terrace, where midsummer light bled gold across cracked marble, she set down her ebony clipboard and waited. [42] The two warlords arrived as the sky deepened, their retinues silent. [53] Above them, the slow dance of celestial bodies had already begun, planets sliding into the alignment that the old treaties demanded. [74] Tradition held that peace could only be sealed when the wandering stars crossed the summer meridian. [90] Vael believed in the ritual not because she was superstitious, but because ritual gave fragile promises a shape men could remember. [111] Her clipboard held the terms, written in three languages, each clause a wound she had learned to dress. [129] "You ask too much," said General Korr, the soldier whose name mothers used to frighten children. [145] "I ask exactly what you owe," Vael answered, operatively gentle, her voice soft enough to disarm and precise enough to cut. [166] She had learned long ago that softness was a tool, not a weakness. [179] Across the terrace stood Lady Esmer, who had burned three cities to avenge one. [193] Both of them had come for the same reason they always came: exhaustion dressed as honor. [209] Vael's task was never simply to stop the killing. [218] It was to anchor ideals in practice, to take the lofty words of every dead idealist and bolt them to something that could survive the morning. [244] "The price of redemption," she told them, "is that you must each give what you most wish to keep." [263] Korr's jaw tightened. [266] Esmer looked away toward the dying sun. [273] Vael knew the cost, because she had once paid it herself. [284] Decades ago she had been a soldier too, and she had ordered a bridge destroyed with a column of refugees still upon it. [307] That memory was the engine of everything she did now; she negotiated not to be praised, but to be forgiven by a world that did not know her crime. [336] The ritual required each leader to surrender a possession bound to their pride. [349] Korr unbuckled his father's sword and laid it on the marble. [360] Esmer removed the iron ring forged from her ruined city's gate. [371] Vael recorded each act on the ebony clipboard, the scratching of her pen the only sound beneath the wheeling stars. [391] "Now you must muster your soldiers," she said, "not to fight, but to witness." [405] This was the hardest part, for armies were easy to summon to war and nearly impossible to gather for peace. [425] Horns sounded across the valley below, and slowly, warily, the two exhausted hosts assembled along the terraced slopes. [443] They had expected another battle; instead they found their masters standing weaponless under the turning heavens. [459] Vael lifted her clipboard so the firelight caught its black surface. [470] "Tonight the planets cross the meridian," she announced, "and tonight you choose what the next forty years will mean." [489] The beauty of the moment was real, and so were its flaws. [501] She knew some of these men would break the treaty by winter, that some clauses were already lies. [519] But flawed beauty was the only kind she had ever been offered, and she had learned to love it without illusion. [540] Korr knelt first, pressing his palm to the cold marble where his sword lay. [554] Esmer followed, her hand beside his, two enemies anchored to the same stone beneath the same indifferent sky. [572] Vael spoke the closing words of the old treaty, ancient phrases worn smooth by generations of desperate hands. [590] When she finished, the celestial alignment held for one suspended breath, and the war was, for now, over. [608] She did not feel triumphant. [613] She felt only the familiar weight of a debt that no treaty could ever fully repay. [629] As the soldiers dispersed into the warm summer dark, Esmer approached her quietly. [642] "Does it ever buy you peace?" the warlady asked. [651] Vael looked up at the stars completing their slow procession. [661] "No," she admitted, tucking the ebony clipboard beneath her arm. [671] "But it buys them a morning, and that is enough to keep mustering for." [685] She walked off the ancient terrace into the night, carrying her ideals and her guilt in equal, balanced measure.
