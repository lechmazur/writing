<story>The letterpress operator never gave her real name, and that was the first ambiguity. [14] Customers called her Vesper, or Margit, or whatever personality she had borrowed that week. [28] She habitually borrowed personalities the way other people borrowed umbrellas, slipping into a stranger's posture, their grief, their laugh. [47] It was research, she insisted, her motivation to understand the mathematics of emotion. [60] Her topaz workshop sat under stars on a hill, its glass roof open so the constellations pressed close while her machine clacked. [82] I came to her because the stars were vanishing. [91] One by one they winked out, and astronomers had no equation for the loss. [105] She listened, then handed me a halo fragment, a curved shard of light she kept in a velvet drawer. [124] It was warm, humming, and it disoriented me instantly. [133] The floor tilted; my own sorrow drained away and someone else's flooded in. [146] This was transposed empathy, she explained, feeling exchanged between two people like sums across an equals sign. [163] The fragment, she said, was the broken edge of a dying star's halo, the residue of its final feeling. [182] Stars, she claimed, did not simply burn out; they grieved themselves dark. [194] Every vanishing happened in the timeframe between two extinguishings, a held breath in the sky. [209] If she could measure the emotion inside that pause, she could write the formula that saved them. [226] But she could not feel her own way there. [235] Decades of borrowing other personalities had hollowed her, leaving a calculator where a heart should sit. [251] So she needed mine, a fresh untransposed grief to run through the fragment. [264] She spoke through deliberate ambiguity, never quite saying whether I would survive the exchange. [278] That vagueness, I realized, was itself a tool, designed to disorient, to keep me from refusing. [294] The whole night carried a lucid turbulence, clear yet churning, like reading a truth while falling. [310] She set the fragment against the press and inked a single sheet with my grief. [325] The letters rose backward, mirror-script, as all letterpress type must be. [336] When she read the printed page, my mourning passed into her like current into copper. [351] For one moment she was wholly herself, no borrowed self, only raw feeling and the numbers behind it. [369] She wept, and as she wept she wrote, the equation pouring out in chalk across the topaz wall. [387] Sorrow equals proximity divided by silence, she murmured, multiplied by every name a star is never called. [404] Outside, a vanished star flickered, then steadied, as if hearing itself named at last. [418] The mathematics of emotion, it turned out, was simply attention given a number. [431] I stood empty now, my grief transposed away, light as borrowed air. [443] She offered to give it back, but I understood her cure required the trade. [457] One person's pain could rekindle a sun; that was the brutal arithmetic she had spent her life chasing. [475] She handed me the halo fragment as payment, now cool and inert, its feeling spent. [490] Carry it between the vanishing stars, she said, and find another griever when the sky thins again. [507] I asked whether I had saved a star or murdered my own sorrow. [520] She only smiled with a personality I could not place, refusing, as always, to resolve the question. [537] Through that deliberate ambiguity she handed me her whole vocation. [547] Now I work the press alone in the topaz workshop under stars, borrowing the personalities of those who come grieving. [567] Each night I solve a little more of the equation, and each night a faint star returns. [584] Whether I am healing the heavens or simply disorienting lonely strangers, I let the ambiguity stand.
