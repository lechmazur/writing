<story>Mira had always been a vivid mural artist, painting gardens that climbed brick walls and spilled across forgotten alleys. [19] She was quietly driven, the kind of woman who finished a wall by lantern light while the city slept. [38] When her grandmother died, she inherited little: a chipped teacup, a wool coat, and a collection of old postcards bound in red string. [61] Each card came from a town called Verlaine, and each showed the same amber pavilion in autumn, its panes glowing like honey held to a flame. [87] On the oldest card, her grandmother had inked a single question: why do flowers bloom at night? [104] Mira read it a hundred times, until the question rooted in her chest and refused to wilt. [121] It was the last thing her grandmother ever asked of the world. [133] So she set out for Verlaine, traveling across the melting of winter's last snow, when the roads ran silver with thaw. [154] The journey took three days, and she filled them studying the postcards by candlelight, memorizing the pavilion she had never seen. [175] She arrived to find the pavilion exactly as the postcards promised, though autumn had long since given way. [193] An old caretaker met her at the gate, his hands stained the same green as her paintbrushes. [210] "Your grandmother used to sit here," he said, "trying to reckon why the garden only woke after dark." [228] He led Mira into the adjoining cathedral, where a single window rose above the altar. [243] The glass was etched in countless overlapping panes, layer upon layer, like petals pressed into transparent stone. [260] "Look through the hidden layers of etched glass," he whispered, "and you will see what she saw." [277] Mira pressed her eye to the window, and the world tilted into umbral tenderness. [291] Shadows softened into something kind, and the colors bled like dusk through her grandmother's memory. [306] Through the first layer she saw the garden as it was: ordinary, sleeping, dull beneath the noon sun. [324] Through the second she saw it at dusk, the buds trembling, gathering some secret courage. [339] Through the deepest layer, the etching revealed a truth the daylight had hidden all along. [354] The flowers did not bloom for bees or for sunlight; they bloomed for the dark itself. [370] They opened in the dawning hush, that fragile silence before the first bird, when the world held its breath. [389] In that hush, Mira understood, the flowers were not hiding but listening. [401] They unfurled to hear the quiet, to drink the stillness that the loud day drowned out. [417] Her grandmother had spent her life chasing this, and now the answer pooled warm in Mira's hands. [434] She wept, not from grief, but from the gentle weight of finally knowing. [447] She stayed in Verlaine through spring, painting the pavilion's walls with night-blooming flowers. [460] By lantern light she worked, just as she always had, only now she knew why. [475] Each petal she painted opened toward an imagined darkness, reaching for the hush her grandmother had loved. [492] The caretaker brought her tea each dusk and watched the petals seem to glow against the deepening dark. [510] When the mural was finished, the caretaker wept, for the wall seemed to breathe. [524] Mira pinned the old postcards along the pavilion's beam, returning them to the place they were born. [541] On the oldest one, beneath her grandmother's question, she wrote her own answer in steady ink. [557] "Because the dark is not empty," it read, "and someone must bloom to keep it company." [573] She left Verlaine as the last snow vanished from the high peaks, carrying the hush inside her. [590] Wherever she painted afterward, her gardens glowed faintly, as if remembering a window full of light. [606]</story>
