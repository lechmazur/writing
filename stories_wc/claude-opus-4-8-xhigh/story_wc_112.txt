<story>On the xenobiology research station, the obliging sky mechanic named Vael answered every summons before the bells finished ringing. [19] She had come to this place above a frozen moon to prove that time moves sideways, a theory the senior researchers had laughed at until they needed her hands. [48] Her hands were mechanically warm, gloved in conductive mesh that pulsed faint heat as she worked the station's failing systems. [68] That paradox, the icy chill of vacuum against the steady warmth of her touch, defined everything she did. [86] Deep in the observation dome lay the magnetic nexus at twilight, a sphere of suspended fields where the station kept its most delicate specimens. [110] Inside it slept the moth-beasts, creatures the size of children, their iridescent wings folded into stillness. [126] The biologists called their slumber the great quiet, but Vael preferred to think of it as a held breath waiting to be released. [149] Tonight she had been granted permission, within the hush of beating wings at rest, to test her impossible idea. [168] The lead xenobiologist, Doctor Anselm, watched her with arms crossed and frost in his expression. [183] "You believe these animals dream the future," he said, not unkindly, only tired. [196] "I believe they dream it sideways," Vael answered, "and the nexus lets us watch." [210] She explained, as she had a dozen times, that the moth-beasts did not experience time as a line but as a wide flat plain. [234] Their subdued awakenings, those slow shivers that rippled through the colony each twilight, were not stirrings toward morning. [252] They were the creatures reaching across the plain, touching moments that had not yet arrived. [267] To prove it, she needed to render their neural fields visible through holographic principles, projecting the unseen into light. [286] Vael knelt beside the nexus and connected her warm gloves to its cold housing. [300] A single lever waited beneath the projector array, brass and worn, the only mechanical part in a room of pure energy. [321] "When I pull this," she told Anselm, "their dreaming will hang in the air, and we will see whether memory and prophecy share the same shape." [347] He said nothing, but he did not stop her. [356] She drew the lever down. [361] Light unfolded from the nexus like petals, and the dome filled with drifting holographic ghosts. [376] There hovered the moth-beasts' dreams, and Vael's breath caught in the freezing air. [389] She saw the station as it had been, corridors bright with a crew now long buried under the moon's ice. [409] She saw the station as it would be, abandoned, its dome cracked and silent. [423] And she saw both at once, layered, neither past nor future, only sideways. [436] The moth-beasts were not predicting; they were remembering forward and recalling ahead, all of it equally present to them. [455] Anselm stepped into the light, his frost melting into something like wonder. [467] "They don't wake because there is nothing to wake from," he whispered. [479] "For them, every moment already exists." [485] Vael nodded, tears stinging warm against her cold cheeks. [494] Her theory was no longer a joke; it was hanging luminous before them, proof rendered in folded wings and projected light. [515] Yet the holograms showed something she had not expected. [524] In one bright thread she saw herself, older, still aboard the station, still tending the sleeping colony. [541] The moth-beasts had dreamed her staying, and so, she understood, she would stay. [554] She had come to prove time moved sideways, and the proof was that her own future already lived inside their rest. [575] The icy warmth of the moment settled over her, the chill of certainty wrapped in the heat of purpose. [594] Gently, she eased the lever back, and the holograms folded once more into the nexus. [609] The dome dimmed to twilight blue, and the moth-beasts breathed on, undisturbed. [621] Anselm offered his hand, no longer frost, only quiet respect. [631] "Stay," he said, though the dreams had already told them both she would. [644] Vael pressed her warm palm to the cold sphere one last time, a mechanic obliging the future she had finally learned to read. [667] Above them the wings rested, holding every moment at once, sideways and complete. [680]</story>
