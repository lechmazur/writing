<story>Mara called herself an intrepid orchard pilot, though the title fit her better than her cramped skiff did. [18] She flew Pellucid, an orchard planet riddled with floating rock arches, where pear trees grew sideways from stone. [36] For three seasons a blight had hollowed the groves, yet one arch still bore fruit that refused to rot. [55] Mara's motivation was simple and stubborn: to find the source of resilience before the blight took everything. [72] The arches kept their secrets, but their hanging streams did not. [83] Pellucid's water fell upward and sideways, threading between the stones, and Mara had learned to read it. [100] Navigating by water flow, she let the silver ribbons guide her skiff toward the one surviving grove. [117] She timed her approach for the inversion hour, the strange interval when gravity flipped across the whole planet. [135] When gravity inverted, fallen fruit rose, loose soil lifted, and the streams reversed into climbing veins of light. [153] Only during that hour could the deepest arch be reached, when its waters rose to meet a pilot halfway. [172] Mara cut her engine and rode the inverted current, surrounded by a whirling silence she had come to crave. [191] Petals spun past without a sound, and even the wind seemed to hold its breath. [206] The surviving arch loomed ahead, a cathedral of stone hung with trees that glowed faintly green. [222] Mara moored her skiff to a root and climbed into the heart of the floating grove. [238] There, lodged in the central trunk, she began to discover something she did not expect. [253] A broken arrow tip, ancient and black, was buried where the bark had grown around it like a wound healing over iron. [275] Where the metal touched living wood, the tree pulsed, and new shoots unfurled in seconds. [290] The arrow tip accelerates plant growth, she realized, feeding the tree against the blight. [304] But it was not only the metal that lived inside the trunk. [316] As Mara pressed her palm to the bark, she heard a gathering of echoes rise from the wood. [334] Voices of every grower who had ever tended Pellucid, layered like rings, whispered through the whirling silence. [351] They told her the arrow had been fired by a desperate settler centuries ago, aimed at the sky in protest of a dying world. [375] It had struck this arch and never come loose, and the tree had grown its resilience around the broken point. [395] The echoes were not ghosts but memory, the orchard remembering how to survive. [408] Mara understood at last that the source of resilience was not a thing to be taken, but a story to be carried. [430] She did not pry the arrow tip free, for tearing it loose would silence the gathering forever. [447] Instead she gathered the glowing shoots that had sprouted around it, cuttings already carrying the same fierce hunger to grow. [467] The inversion hour was waning, and the streams began to settle back toward the ground. [482] Mara raced the returning gravity, her arms full of light, navigating by water flow as the veins reversed again. [501] She fell back into her skiff just as fruit dropped and soil resettled across the dying groves. [518] Over the seasons that followed, Mara planted the cuttings beneath every barren arch on Pellucid. [533] Each took root with impossible speed, and each, she swore, carried a small voice of its own. [550] The blight retreated, unable to outpace a forest that grew faster than it could rot. [565] And when the gravity inverted, the new groves sang together, a whole planet of echoes rising into the whirling silence. [585] Mara, the intrepid orchard pilot, flew among them, no longer searching, only listening to the resilience she had finally learned to grow. [607]</story>
