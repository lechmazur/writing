<story>Mira kept the beacon-tower long after the lights of the world went out. [13] After the collapse of civilization, no ships sailed and no travelers came, yet she climbed the spiral stairs each dusk out of stubborn habit. [37] The tower's heart was a broken crystal prism, fractured the night the cities fell silent. [52] She was retrocognitively blessed, able to lay her palm on any object and witness its past. [68] When she touched the prism, she saw the exact second it cracked, splintering as a strange hush rolled across the land. [89] That hush had stolen sound from a billion throats, and a single piece of it survived: a shard of hush, cold and weightless, lodged in the prism's wound. [117] Mira believed the shard was no accident. [124] She had spent years gathering evidence that coincidences carried hidden design, and the world had laughed at her for it. [144] Now, with the world gone, only she remained to prove that coincidences have meaning. [158] Her one clue lay east, in the glass humming pavilion her grandmother once described. [172] The pavilion was built of curved panes that sang whenever wind threaded through them, a structure tuned to catch and hold vibrations. [194] If sound could be trapped, Mira reasoned, then perhaps silence could be released there too. [209] She wrapped the prism in cloth and walked across thistle-choked plains, the seedheads breaking loose and drifting like pale sparks on the breeze. [232] That blown thistle felt like hope made visible, scattering forward even when nothing waited ahead. [247] For three nights she navigated by tracking star paths, following the slow wheel of constellations her grandmother had taught her to read. [269] The stars, she noticed, returned to the same positions her ancestors had charted, repeating their patterns with a faithfulness that felt deliberate. [291] Coincidence, the old world would have said; design, Mira insisted. [301] On the fourth dawn she found the pavilion glinting on a hill, its panes humming a low, lonely chord. [320] Inside, she set the broken prism on the central plinth and pressed both hands against it. [336] Her retrocognition flooded back through the glass: she watched the pavilion's makers, centuries dead, aligning each pane to the very star paths she had just followed. [362] They had built it as a vault, certain that someday a hush would need undoing. [377] They could not have known her, yet they had aimed their work straight at this moment. [393] That was the proof she had chased her whole life, the coincidence that was no coincidence at all. [411] Weeping, Mira tilted the prism so the rising sunlight struck its fractured face. [424] The light split, refracted, and poured into the humming glass, and the pavilion's chord swelled into something vast. [442] The shard of hush trembled, then dissolved, its captive silence answered at last by sound. [457] A wave of released noise rang outward across the plains: birdsong, wind, the murmur of returning life. [474] The crack in the prism sealed, and its old fire began to rekindle, a warm amber glow rising from within. [494] Mira carried the mended prism home and restored it to the beacon-tower's heart. [507] That night the light blazed seaward for the first time since the silence, sweeping the dark in steady arcs. [526] She did not know who, if anyone, still lived to see it. [538] But the stars wheeled overhead in their ancient, repeating paths, and thistledown drifted past the glass, glinting in the beam. [558] Every coincidence, she finally understood, was a message sent forward by people who trusted someone would arrive to read it. [578] And so she kept the beacon burning, a custodian of light and proof, certain now that nothing in the world had ever truly been by chance. [604]</story>
