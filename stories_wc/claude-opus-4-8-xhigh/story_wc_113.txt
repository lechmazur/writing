Mira had played the orchestra pit for thirty seasons, but she had never seen the opera house drowned like this. [20] Water filled the aisles to her knees, and flickering lanterns hung from the boxes, throwing trembling gold across the black surface. [41] She waded between the rows alone, amid the hush of unstruck chords, where every instrument waited and nothing dared to sound. [62] In her coat she carried an antique opera program, its paper soft and swollen, salvaged from the conductor's flooded desk. [82] The conductor, old Halloran, had died the week the river rose, and the company swore his ghost still lingered. [101] Mira had loved him quietly for years, never speaking it, their friendship a wordless conversation of glances over the music stand. [122] Lately inspired by grief, she had come back each night to study the program he had left for her alone. [142] He had hidden something in it, she was certain, and she meant to unravel the code left by a ghost. [162] The names of arias were printed in his old-fashioned hand, but beneath them ran faint pencil marks she could not yet read. [184] She lifted the program toward a lantern and let the warm light dry one corner. [199] As the paper warmed, the water damage began creating images across its surface, shapes blooming where the flood had bruised the ink. [221] Stains spread into the outline of a staircase, then a doorway, then a number scrawled like a watermark: seven. [240] Mira understood at once; he was pointing her toward the seventh box, high above the drowned stalls. [257] She climbed the slick marble stairs, her lantern swinging, and the flood fell away below her like a sinking orchestra. [277] The seventh box was a small velvet room, one of those in between spaces that belonged neither to audience nor stage. [298] Halloran had always lingered there during intermissions, watching her tune, saying nothing, meaning everything. [312] He had taught her that the truest things live in silences, in the breath before a downbeat. [329] Now she set the lantern on the rail and held the program against the water-streaked wall. [345] More marks surfaced as the damp paper pressed to damp plaster, the stains aligning, completing a melody in faded notation. [365] It was the unfinished aria he had been composing the winter he fell ill, the one he never let her hear. [386] Mira read the notes hungrily, and beneath them, in the same brown bloom, three words gathered: play for me. [405] The code was not treasure or accusation; it was an invitation, the last sentence of their wordless conversation. [423] She descended to the pit, where her cello waited half-submerged, its body trembling with the lapping water. [440] She lifted it, propped herself on the conductor's stool, and arranged the program where he would have read it. [459] Then she drew the bow, and the first note disrupted the long silence of the unstruck chords. [476] The aria rose, imperfect and his, filling the flooded hall until the lanterns shivered in answer. [492] Water carried the sound between the rows, into the boxes, through every in between space the music had never reached. [512] For a moment she felt him beside her, not a chill but a warmth, the old glance returned across the dark. [533] She played until the last phrase dissolved, and the hush settled again, gentler now, finished. [548] The program, fully dried, held its images forever, a score and a farewell pressed into ruined paper. [565] Mira lowered her bow, lately inspired no longer by grief but by gratitude, and whispered the only word the night required. [586] Goodbye, she said, and the water, at last, lay perfectly still. [597] Above her, one lantern guttered out, content. [604]
