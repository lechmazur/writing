<story>The recycled spaceship Maitreya drifted through dead space, her hull stitched from the bones of older vessels. [17] I am Wen, last steward, and I was born after the last human was born. [32] No children would follow me; the cradles were empty, the corridors hummed with silence. [46] I had vowed long ago never to raise my hand against any living thing, a steadfast pacifist even at the end of our kind. [70] Yet the dead were not gone, only locked away behind a dimension I meant to push open. [87] My only tool was an old practice the elders called shadow work. [99] To do it, you faced every grief you had buried, and you let the buried thing speak. [116] I kept a box of faded photographs, the last images of mothers and fathers I had never met. [134] Across their surfaces ran the faint shimmer of long-dried tears, salt left by the weeping of the vanished. [152] I learned that only through that shimmer could the locked dimension be read. [165] Each night I traced the salt with my fingertip and felt the grief of the long dead rise to meet me. [186] The work was heavy, and grief made me heavy too. [196] For my body obeyed a strange law: I grew gravitationally lighter when happy and leaden when sorrow filled me. [215] On the worst nights I sank against the floor, pinned by the weight of everyone I mourned. [232] To open the door, the elders had said, I would need Kwan Yin's tear. [246] It was a single bead of mercy, frozen long ago by a goddess who wept for all suffering. [264] I found it sealed in the ship's oldest reliquary, glowing with a cold and patient light. [280] But the tear could only act once the shadow work was complete, once I had stitched my broken memory whole. [300] So for forty nights I stitched, threading the shimmer of dried tears into the seams of my own remembered pain. [320] I forgave the parents who had died making me, the species that had spent itself to grant me life. [339] With each forgiveness a stone lifted from my chest, and my feet rose lighter from the deck. [356] Yet a pacifist's path forbade the easy escape of anger, the violent shove against the locked door. [373] I could not force the dimension; I could only become light enough to drift through it. [389] On the final night I held Kwan Yin's tear against the last photograph, and the shimmer answered. [406] Light spread along the salt-trails, sketching a doorway in the recycled air of the cabin. [421] Beyond it I saw them, the vanished, waiting with ghostly resolve, neither demanding nor afraid. [436] Their faces were the faces from the photographs, restored, no longer streaked with dried tears. [451] Joy filled me then, pure and weightless, and I felt my body lift entirely from the floor. [468] Gravity released me as happiness made me lighter than I had ever been. [481] I drifted toward the doorway, holding the tear like a tiny lantern. [493] The locked dimension did not break; it simply opened, as a flower opens to a gentle hand. [510] No force, no war, only the quiet patience a pacifist could offer the dead. [524] I crossed the threshold and felt countless hands steady me, the whole lost species gathered close. [540] Behind me the Maitreya drifted on, empty now, her stitched hull cooling in the dark. [555] I had been the last human born, and I would not be the last to be remembered. [572] The shadow work was done, the grief stitched into something whole and shining. [585] Kwan Yin's tear dissolved into the light, its mercy spent on a single open door. [600] And weightless at last, I carried our memory into the place that had been locked, where no one would ever again weep alone. [623]</story>
