<story>Wend had lived in the library's crawl space for eleven years. [11] She knew its bones the way a heart knows its own slow rhythm. [24] Kindly and methodical, she moved each dawn through the narrow pipe chases, uncovering the small things the daylight world forgot. [44] A button, a pressed flower, a child's marble; she cached them in a velvet drawer behind the boiler, a quiet museum of the overlooked. [68] This morning the city's lights flickered after an unexpected outage, and the reading rooms drowned in a sapphire hush. [87] Through a gap in the brick, Wend watched the blue dark settle over the stacks like water filling a well. [107] She had a reason to be unseen, but a deeper reason to be found. [121] Long ago she had failed someone who needed her, and the shame had driven her into the walls. [139] Now she dreamed only of becoming visible again to those who needed her. [152] Today the pipe chase offered something new. [159] Wedged where a copper joint met the old plaster, she found a ferromagnetic tape reel fragment, its ribbon curled and brittle with age. [182] She drew it out and held it to the faint light, and a strange thing happened. [198] The smell of it - oxide, dust, a trace of someone's perfume - opened a sense memory she had buried for a decade. [219] She was a girl again at the recording desk, her grandmother's voice spooling warm onto the reel. [236] Read to me, the old woman had said, and Wend had promised to keep every word. [252] But she had let the recordings scatter, and her grandmother had died alone in a quiet room while Wend hid from her own grief. [276] That, she understood now, was when she first crawled into the walls. [288] A sound broke the sapphire hush: a small, frightened breathing somewhere below. [300] Wend pressed her ear to the chase and followed the pipes down to the children's annex. [316] A boy had been locked in when the outage killed the doors, and he sat curled against the dark, weeping. [336] For eleven years she had stayed hidden; now a child needed her, and the choice was simple. [353] She slid the loosened grate aside and stepped, for the first time in a decade, into the open. [371] Hello, she whispered, kneeling so the boy could see her face. [382] I know the way out in the dark. [390] Methodical as ever, she named each turn aloud as she led him by the hand through the shelving, past the cached treasures she now pressed into his pockets. [418] The marble for courage, she said; the flower so you remember it smelled like morning. [433] She was teaching him, she realized, the same lesson the tape had taught her: that scent and touch can hold a person long after the lights go. [460] At the great doors, the power surged back, and the city's lights steadied beyond the windows. [476] Dawn poured gold across the shadowed shelves, and the boy's mother rushed in, sobbing his name. [492] The lady in the walls saved me, the boy said, and pointed. [504] For the first time in eleven years, someone looked at Wend and truly saw her. [519] She did not flee back into the dark. [527] Instead she held up the brittle reel fragment, still warm from her hand, and felt her grandmother's promise finally come due. [548] To become visible to those who need you, she understood, you must first walk out where they can find you. [568] In the sapphire hush of that returning dawn, the dweller of the crawl space stepped fully into the light, and stayed. [589] Later, the librarians would find her velvet drawer and learn the names of all she had kept safe. [607] And Wend, no longer a secret, began to read aloud to the children each dawn, her grandmother's voice alive again in her own. [630]
