<story>After tomorrow disappeared, the titan-gear librarian kept opening the atrium at dawn anyway. [13] Her body was a quiet engine, brass cogs the size of millstones turning behind a chest of frosted glass. [32] Around her shoulder hung a fisherman's bamboo creel, woven tight, smelling of salt and old rain. [48] The tidal atrium was her whole world: a glass hall where the sea climbed the shelves twice a day, then withdrew. [69] Books bobbed in the high water like patient fish, their pages safe in waxed leather. [84] She loved this routine, and she guarded the wonder inside it as carefully as any rare manuscript. [101] But after tomorrow disappeared, identities began to migrate. [109] People woke wearing the wrong names, the wrong faces, drifting from self to self like birds confused by a vanished season. [130] Her own identity fluctuated with the moon phases now. [139] At the full moon she was Mara, gentle and certain; at the new moon she forgot even her hands. [158] Each tide brought new visitors who had lost themselves, and each begged her to set them right. [175] So she fished. [178] When a stranger's true self surfaced in their eyes, she lowered the bamboo creel into the flood and caught the shimmering thing inside. [201] Names wriggled there, cold and bright, until she could return each to its rightful owner. [216] But the creel could not hold her own scattered selves. [226] An old chart, salvaged from a drowned shelf, offered one remedy. [237] She must smelt a single anchor-coin from all her shifting names, pouring them into one steady metal. [254] The furnace stood at the atrium's heart, fed by the friction of her own great gears. [270] Yet the chart warned that the metal would only set by accepting contradictions, not by resolving them. [287] So at the next low tide she opened the creel and tipped every name she had ever worn into the fire. [308] Mara who was certain, and the nameless one who was lost; the keeper of routine, and the lover of wonder. [328] She did not choose between them. [334] She said, calmly, that she was all of them at once, and the contradiction did not break her. [352] The molten names ran together, hissing, and cooled into a coin warm as a small winter sun. [369] She pressed it against the glass of her chest, where it settled among the turning cogs. [385] At once the moon lost its power over her. [394] Full or new, waxing or dark, she remained herself, a self made wide enough to contain its own opposites. [413] Now steady, she could finally help the others. [421] Each dawn she stood waist-deep in the rising atrium, the creel ready, and taught the drifting visitors her secret. [440] You are not one thing, she told them, and you need not be. [453] Hold your contradictions gently, and your name will come home. [463] One by one, the migrating identities settled, like tired birds finding the right branch at last. [479] Tomorrow never did return; the calendar simply ended at today, over and over. [492] But she had learned that a single day, repeated, could still be endlessly strange and lovely. [508] She watered the floating books, shelved the ones the tide returned, and listened to the cogs keep their slow time. [528] This was her purpose: to preserve wonder inside the smallest routine. [539] Visitors came expecting a machine and found instead a patient, contradictory heart. [551] When the water rose, the winter sun slanted through the glass and lit the whole atrium gold. [568] She would stand in that calm light, creel on her shoulder, coin warm at her core, and feel utterly at peace. [589] There was no tomorrow to wait for, only this bright, brimming now.
