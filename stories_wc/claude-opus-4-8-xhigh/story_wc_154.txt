<story>Soraya had spent years as a Pacific conflict-zone translator, turning gunfire into grammar so wounded strangers could understand one another. [20] Now she worked the mist-clad atrium of a children's hospital at twilight, where fog pooled against the glass like a tide returning. [42] In her pocket sat a brain coral fragment, the last thing she had carried from her drowned island, its ridges mapping a brain that could not stop remembering. [70] Tonight her task was a boy named Ilo, eight years old, shrewdly innocent in the way of children who have learned to bargain with fear. [95] He had broken his arm fleeing a collapse, and across his white cast he had scrawled dense, smudged notes that no nurse could read. [119] They had asked Soraya to translate, assuming the marks were her language, but they were no language she knew. [138] Ilo only smiled and said the words were a secret meant to keep the building from falling again. [156] Surgery came at dusk to reset the bone, and Soraya stayed because she could not abandon a child who guarded the world with ink. [180] After the anesthetic kicked in, Ilo's grip on his secret loosened, and he began murmuring in a soft, sliding tongue. [200] Soraya leaned close, and suddenly the smudged notes unknotted themselves: they were a private alphabet, each smear a sound he had heard the night the ceiling came down. [228] She set the brain coral fragment beside his hand, tracing its grooves against his letters, decrypting the smudged notes on the child's cast one ridge at a time. [256] The translation was a list of fears: the groan of beams, the dark, the silence where his mother's voice had been. [277] Soraya recognized every word, for they were her shared anxieties too, the same tide that had risen over her island and never fully receded. [301] She understood then why she had truly come to the atrium night after night. [315] It was not only to translate; it was to graft new meaning onto old wounds, both his and her own. [335] When Ilo surfaced from the anesthetic, blinking at the fogged glass, she did not erase his secret. [352] Instead she pressed the coral into his palm and told him the truth she had learned underwater years ago. [371] Coral, she said, is built from countless small fears stacked into something that shelters whole reefs. [387] His alphabet, she explained, could become a wall not to hold up a falling roof, but to hold the names of what he loved. [411] Together, in the lotus-hearted ache of that twilight, they rewrote his cast. [423] Beside each fear he wrote one good thing, and Soraya translated each into both their languages, until the white plaster bloomed with two scripts. [447] The groan of beams became the hum of his mother singing; the dark became the coral's slow, patient growing. [466] When the boy finally slept, the mist outside the atrium glowed amber under the dying light. [482] Soraya sat with the fragment in her open hand, feeling her own shared anxieties loosen and settle, no longer a knot but a structure. [506] She had crossed oceans of grammar to arrive here, and only now did she grasp that translation had always been her quiet method of mending. [531] Two strangers, one small and one weary, had handed their fears across a language and found them, astonishingly, the same. [551] In the morning the cast would set hard, carrying their new meaning into every clumsy, healing day ahead. [569] Soraya closed her fingers around the brain coral and, for the first time since the water took her home, she was not afraid to remember. [594] Outside, the twilight deepened, and the atrium held them both like a slow, breathing reef. [609]</story>
