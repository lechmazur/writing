<story>Beyond the murmur of lost languages, where even names had eroded into wind, the marsh-reed sculptor Ysolde climbed toward the crystalline orchard perched at the edge of a shimmering crater. [30] She had braided reeds her whole life, and yet every village she passed treated her as a stranger threading through their lives without ever knotting in. [56] Here, the orchard folk lived in glass-leafed trees that hummed, and they told her of one final rite: to belong, a newcomer must find the tag of an elusive street artist who had once marked every trunk. [93] The artist had vanished generations ago, leaving only rumors of a single signature hidden somewhere in the grove. [111] A boy named Pell volunteered to guide her, boldly sympathetic toward the weary woman whom the others eyed with suspicion. [131] No one finds the tag alone, Pell said; that was the point of the search. [146] So they began a shared search for the elusive street artist's tag, wandering between the chiming trees while the crater below pulsed with a slow, opaline light. [173] Pell knew the histories that others had forgotten, and as they walked he taught her the webs of kinship that bound the orchard: who had married whom, whose grandmother had grafted the first crystal sapling, which families shared the labor of polishing the leaves. [217] Ysolde listened with a luminescent ache, longing to be one strand in that bright weave instead of a loose thread fraying at its edge. [241] Days bled into nights of refracted starlight, and still the tag eluded them. [254] To keep her hands from trembling, Ysolde gathered the dry marsh-reeds she always carried and began to weave as they searched. [275] Pell told her the artist had favored the orchard's heart, where an old story spoke of a glimmering obsidian arbor that had been planted before the languages were lost. [304] They pushed through curtains of crystal until they reached it: a black, glassy lattice of branches that drank the crater's glow and gave nothing back. [329] On the darkest bough, almost invisible, curled the artist's tag, a looping mark that no one had deciphered in lifetimes. [349] Ysolde traced its shape and laughed through tears, for she recognized it instantly. [362] It was a reed-knot, the same binding stitch her own people used, a word in the lost language of weavers that meant, simply, together. [386] The elusive artist had been a marsh-reed sculptor too, a wanderer like her, who had grafted herself onto this place and left her name written in the only tongue she trusted. [417] Pell stared, understanding nothing of the script, yet everything of her trembling joy. [430] You can read it, he whispered; no one has ever read it. [442] That night the orchard folk gathered beneath the obsidian arbor, and Ysolde taught them the knot, letting their fingers learn the shape that meant together. [467] She wove a great lattice of marsh-reed to crown the dark tree, threading her craft into theirs until the two could not be told apart. [492] The orchard hummed louder, as if a long-silent voice had finally answered. [504] They asked her to dedicate the woven crown, to speak the old word aloud so the orchard might hold it forever. [525] Ysolde laid her palm against the obsidian and dedicated the arbor to every wanderer who had ever ached to belong. [545] In that moment she understood the rite's true purpose: the tag could never be found alone, because kinship itself was the thing being sought. [569] The lost artist had not hidden her name; she had planted an invitation, waiting beyond the murmur of dead tongues for another lonely weaver to arrive and read it. [598] Pell took her hand, knotting his fingers through hers, and at last Ysolde was no loose thread, but a living strand grafted firmly into the weave. [624]
</story>
