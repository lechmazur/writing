<story>Mira called herself a luminous skeptic, a woman who glowed faintly in the dark yet refused to believe in anything she could not measure. [24] She had come to the city of forgotten fountains because someone had to test the rumor. [40] They said the dry basins remembered every wish ever dropped into them, and that the memories had grown stuck, calcified beneath the limestone. [63] Mira intended to unstick them, to liberate trapped memories, though she expected to find only mineral deposits and disappointment. [82] Her only instrument was a stained glass box, willed to her by a grandmother she barely trusted. [99] The box hummed when held to moonlight, projecting fractured colors that, the old woman swore, were keys. [116] Mira's work began from the pause of migrating geese, that brief autumn hush when the flock settles on the rooftops and the whole sky holds its breath. [143] In that defiant hush, she lifted the box and let it scatter its colors across the cracked plaza. [161] The light fell in a deliberate trail, dot after colored dot, and she realized she was meant to proceed by following breadcrumbs of refracted glow. [186] Skeptic that she was, she still followed, because a realistically imaginative mind tests the impossible before dismissing it. [204] The trail led between toppled angels and choked aqueducts, past fountains shaped like fish that had not spat water in a century. [226] At each basin the colored dots pooled, and Mira pressed the box against the stone. [241] The first wish surfaced as a child's voice asking for snow, and the memory rose like steam, then dissolved into the cold air, freed. [265] Mira's measuring instincts screamed that this was nonsense, yet her hands trembled with something close to wonder. [282] She moved deeper, and the geese above her stayed motionless, as if the whole migration had agreed to wait. [301] Her grandmother had a phrase for this: sidereal patience, the slow constancy of stars that keep their promises across centuries. [321] Now Mira understood that the fountains had practiced exactly that, holding each wish with sidereal patience until someone arrived to release it. [343] Basin by basin, she worked through the night, and lost loves, forgotten names, and unspoken apologies lifted free into the dark. [364] The breadcrumbs of light grew sparse, narrowing toward a final fountain at the city's heart. [379] There, the last colored dot trembled over a basin sealed with the same stained glass as her box. [397] Mira realized the box was a fragment broken from this very seal, carried away long ago by her grandmother. [416] To unstick the deepest memory, she had to fit the piece back into the wound it had left. [434] She set the box into the gap, and it clicked home with a sound like a held breath finally exhaled. [454] Light flooded upward, and within it stood her grandmother, young again, the memory she had hidden here for safekeeping. [473] The skeptic in Mira wanted proof, but the luminous part of her, the part that glowed, simply wept. [491] Her grandmother's image whispered the apology she had never managed in life, and then dissolved, liberated at last. [509] The instant the memory loosened, the geese stirred, and their pause ended. [521] Wings beat overhead, lifting the whole flock back into the migrating sky. [533] Below them the fountains, emptied of their hoarded wishes, began at last to weep water again. [549] Mira stood in the rising spray, a luminous skeptic no longer certain what she disbelieved. [564] She had come to measure a rumor and instead had measured her own grief, finding it lighter once spoken aloud. [584] The stained glass box, its purpose finished, dimmed quietly in her open palms. [597] In the defiant hush before dawn, she promised the waking city she would keep its secret faithfully. [614] Stars wheeled overhead with their patient light, and for the first time, Mira believed they were listening. [631]</story>
