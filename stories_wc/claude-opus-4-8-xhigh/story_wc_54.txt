The carnival left town on a Tuesday, hauling its lights and clamor north, and silence settled over the abandoned spaceport passenger terminal like dust. [24] Mara, a curious civil engineer intern, arrived the next morning with a clipboard and a thermos. [40] Her assignment was simple: survey the ruin and recommend demolition. [50] But Mara had never learned to look at anything simply. [60] The terminal sprawled before her, a maze of intentions left half-finished when the colony funding collapsed. [76] Corridors forked toward gates that opened onto nothing; conveyor channels twisted back on themselves like questions nobody had answered. [95] She walked its arteries slowly, tracing each dead-end with a stoic wonder that surprised her. [110] In the central rotunda, where departing travelers had once watched ships rise, she found the carnival's leavings. [127] Ticket stubs, a broken ring-toss, and half-buried in confetti, a small brass instrument. [140] She lifted it: an explorer's sextant piece, its arc engraved with constellations no longer visible through the cracked dome. [159] Someone had hung it here, perhaps a carnival prize, perhaps an offering. [171] Mara turned the brass in her hands and felt the old machine of her mind begin to whir. [189] The instrument was built to measure angles between a traveler and the stars, to convert longing into position. [207] She wondered if she could do the reverse for this place. [218] Demolition would be easy; resurrection was the harder equation, and Mara had always preferred the harder equation. [235] Over three days she mapped the terminal's bones. [243] The dead gates, she realized, were not truly dead; they were merely waiting for water. [258] The colony's old hydraulic veins still threaded the walls, dry but intact. [270] If she could resurrect the terminal, it would be through redirected flows, not new construction. [285] The mountain spring that the carnival had dammed for its rides was already roaring downhill, unguided, wasted. [302] She could steer it into the terminal's veins, let pressure turn the old turbines, wake the lights. [319] It was a functionally creative idea, the kind her professors had warned would either earn promotion or ridicule. [337] Mara used the sextant piece as her guide. [345] Holding it to the broken dome, she measured the slope of every channel, treating the building as a sky to be navigated. [367] The maze of intentions, once baffling, resolved into a single elegant path for the water to follow. [384] She redirected the spring with sandbags and a salvaged valve, then waited. [396] Water entered the long-silent arteries with a groan that became a hum. [408] Pressure climbed; somewhere a turbine remembered its purpose and spun. [418] One by one, the gate signs flickered awake, casting amber light across the rotunda's dust. [433] Mara stood in the glow, breathing slowly, her wonder still stoic but no longer quiet inside her. [450] She had not rebuilt the spaceport; she had simply let it live again on the resources it already held. [469] In her notebook she wrote not a demolition order but a proposal. [481] The town could come here again, not to flee the planet, but to gather beneath working lights. [498] Her true motivation, she admitted, had never been the survey. [508] She had come to puzzle out new equations for hope, and the terminal had handed her the variables. [526] She hung the sextant piece back on its hook, where it caught the amber light like a small recovered star. [546] The carnival had left town, but it had unknowingly delivered the one instrument capable of measuring a way forward. [565] Mara gathered her thermos and clipboard, leaving the lights on behind her. [577] Tomorrow she would face skeptical engineers, but tonight the dead terminal breathed again, and that was enough. [594] She paused at the threshold and looked back one last time. [605] The water hummed through the walls, faithful and unhurried, redrawing the ruin into a promise. [620]
