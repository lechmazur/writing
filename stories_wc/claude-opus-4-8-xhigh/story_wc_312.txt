<story>Oren was a lukewarm mage, and everyone in the harbor town knew it. [13] His fire spells warmed soup but never seared, and his frost left puddles instead of ice. [29] Yet he carried an earnestly naive faith that he had been born for one true purpose. [45] That purpose arrived the morning after the last fish was caught. [56] The nets came up empty forever, the sea fell mute, and the tides forgot which way to lean. [74] An old astronomer explained it plainly: a cosmic imbalance had begun, and someone had to right it before the moon drifted loose. [96] The cure, she said, was the broken ship's bell that hung beneath the sapphire viaduct. [111] Long ago its ringing had set the rhythm that kept stars and oceans in step. [126] When it cracked, the rhythm slowly unraveled, and the fish had been the first to vanish. [142] Oren climbed to the viaduct at dawn, where blue stone arched over a valley of mist. [158] The bell hung in two halves, a jagged seam running through its bronze like a frozen lightning bolt. [176] Greater mages had tried to rebuild it and failed, because brute magic only widened the crack. [192] But Oren's weakness, it turned out, was exactly the gift the task required. [205] He could not melt the bronze, but he could warm it just enough to make it flow like slow honey. [225] The secret, he realized, lay in understanding dripping rates. [234] If the metal seeped too fast, it would pool and harden wrong; too slow, and it would cool before it bridged the seam. [257] So Oren counted drops against the dripping of dew from the viaduct's stones. [270] One bead of dew, one bead of bronze; the morning itself became his metronome. [284] For hours he held the lukewarm spell steady, sweat tracing his jaw, his devotion brittle but unbroken. [301] He knew that a single lapse would shatter the seam wider than before, ruining the bell forever. [318] His faith was the only thing that did not waver, though it felt thin enough to snap. [335] Drop by drop, the bronze crept across the wound, filling it with a thread of living gold. [352] As the sun cleared the horizon, the viaduct blazed sapphire and the last drip settled into place. [369] The bell was whole, though a faint scar still marked where it had broken. [383] Oren reached out with a trembling hand and struck it once. [394] The note that rolled out was deeper than any sound he had heard, and the mist shivered into rings. [413] Far below, the silent sea answered with a long, returning sigh. [424] The tides found their direction, and somewhere out in the dark water, a single fish leapt. [440] Oren laughed, surprised that something so impossible had obeyed something so ordinary as patience. [454] He had not needed to be powerful, only steady enough to count. [466] When he descended into the town, the harbor lamps still burned and people slept, unaware of what dawn had spared them. [487] He did not wake them to boast, for boasting had never been in his lukewarm nature. [503] Instead he sat on the pier and watched the water climb back to its proper line. [519] The old astronomer came at last, leaning on her staff, and studied the restored horizon. [534] How did you mend what stronger hands could not, she asked. [545] Oren shrugged and said he had simply listened to how slowly the world wished to be repaired. [562] The cosmic imbalance had not demanded a hero, only a witness willing to match its quiet pace. [579] She smiled, understanding that his brittle devotion had held exactly long enough to do the impossible thing. [596] Above the sapphire viaduct, the mended bell swung gently in the dawn wind, marking time for a sky that finally remembered its song. [619]</story>
