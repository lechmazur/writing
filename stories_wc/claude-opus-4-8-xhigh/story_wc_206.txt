<story>Elias kept a shop where broken songs came to heal. [10] He was the last music box repairman in Verdigris, a town the color of old copper. [26] Long ago, the people had signed a covenant of rust, promising that no machine would ever be forced to lie, and in exchange the metal would never wholly die. [55] On his bench sat a single iron tulip, a wonder of gears that wilts near dishonesty, its petals curling whenever a customer shaded the truth. [80] Verdigris had a wound it spoke of only in whispers: years before, a figure called Midnight had slipped into the great clocktower and stolen the town's light, leaving the streets dim and the people half-asleep. [115] Elias had inherited one defense: a chip from the philosopher's stone, dull as a fingernail, that hummed when held against true gold. [137] His motivation was simple and enormous: to reclaim the light stolen by midnight before the last lamp guttered out. [156] An old clockmaker's letter had told him the secret lay beneath the tower, in the resonance chamber where every bell in town had once been tuned. [182] So one grey evening Elias climbed down, the iron tulip pinned to his coat and the stone-chip warm in his palm. [203] The chamber was a vast bronze egg, its walls studded with tuning forks and silent music boxes furred in rust. [223] At its center stood Midnight, a tall shape stitched from shadow, cradling a lantern that held the town's stolen glow. [243] "You cannot break what I have made," Midnight said, and the iron tulip wilted at once, its petals folding tight. [263] A lie, then; Elias smiled, because dishonesty had a shape he knew how to work with. [279] He did not reach for the lantern; instead he knelt at the rusted music boxes and began mending what isn't torn. [300] Each box was whole, yet stilled by a single loosened pin, and as he tightened them they woke one by one into a small, bright tinkersong. [326] The covenant of rust stirred, for the metal had been promised it would never wholly die, and now it remembered how to sing. [349] Note by note the chamber filled with melody, and the resonance gathered the sound until the whole bronze egg trembled like a struck bell. [373] Midnight flinched, for shadow cannot abide an honest harmony. [382] Quickly Elias pressed the chip from the philosopher's stone against the lantern's brass throat, and it hummed, proving the stolen light was true gold after all. [408] With the song as his rope, he used the chip to tether the lantern's glow to the singing boxes, binding light to honest sound. [432] Midnight pulled, the boxes pulled back, and for one long breath everything hung in the balance. [448] Then the iron tulip on his coat slowly opened, petal after petal, sensing that no falsehood remained in the room. [468] Its bloom was the signal: the tether held, and the light poured out of the lantern into every waiting box. [488] Midnight thinned to a wisp, unmade by the very harmony it could not endure, and was gone. [505] The boxes blazed gold, and through the tower's seams the light climbed back into Verdigris. [520] Lamps that had slept for years brightened, and sleepers woke with copper morning on their faces. [536] Elias climbed up into a town that glowed once more, the iron tulip nodding at his collar. [553] He went back to his bench, for there would always be songs that needed healing. [568] And whenever someone asked how he had beaten the dark, the music box repairman only smiled and said that the cure had never been to break anything. [595] It had been to mend what the world only thought was whole. [607]</story>
