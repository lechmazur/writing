<story>Mara had always been a broken-clock tinkerer, mending hours that other people had given up on. [16] Her workshop sat at the bottom of a long stair, beside a subterranean waterfall whose roar kept her company through the night. [38] She came here to befriend uncertainty in planning, because clocks, like lives, refused to keep the schedules people demanded of them. [59] One damp morning a stranger arrived, carrying a small brass case wrapped in oilcloth. [73] Inside lay the admiral's lost compass rose, its needle bent, its enamel cracked like dried mud. [89] "They pulled it from a burned ship," the stranger said, "found beneath the ashes when nothing else survived." [107] Mara turned it over, feeling its weight, uncertainly certain that she could make it point true again. [124] The admiral, the stranger explained, had vanished years ago, and this was the only relic that remained of him. [143] For weeks Mara worked, but the needle drifted, settling on no north she could trust. [158] Each test sent her wandering the underground halls, until she noticed something strange. [171] Whenever she carried the compass past the old door at the corridor's end, the needle swung sharply, as if remembering. [191] Once was accident; twice was odd; but when coincidence becomes pattern, even a careful tinkerer must follow it. [209] She opened the door and stepped through the swirling leaves in a neglected greenhouse, where the waterfall's mist had nurtured a wild forest of vines. [234] The compass jerked toward a mound of pale ash beneath a shattered glass roof. [248] Mara knelt and brushed it aside, and beneath the ashes she uncovered a sealed iron box. [264] Inside were charts, letters, and a logbook in the admiral's own hand. [276] He had not been lost at sea at all; he had fled here, to this hidden garden, to escape the fame that had hollowed him. [301] The final entry confessed that he had burned his own ship to be declared dead and free. [318] He had spent his last years here, learning at last to live without a fixed course. [334] Mara understood then why the needle would not behave. [343] The compass had stopped serving certainty; it had learned to point toward the things that mattered instead. [360] She returned to her bench and decided not to force it straight. [372] Instead she would improvise, fitting a freer gimbal so the rose could wander as the admiral had wandered. [390] When she finished, the needle floated gently, never quite still, never quite lost. [403] The stranger came back at dusk, and Mara placed the compass in his palm. [417] "It will not point north," she warned, "but it will point somewhere honest." [430] The stranger smiled, and Mara recognized, beneath weathered features, the eyes from the logbook's sketched self-portrait. [446] The admiral had returned for the only thing he had left behind: his uncertainty, made into a gift. [464] He bowed, and the swirling leaves closed behind him as he climbed toward the surface. [479] Mara listened to the subterranean waterfall and, for the first time, did not mind that she could not predict tomorrow. [499] For years she had measured her worth by precision, by gears that obeyed and hands that struck on time. [518] The admiral's compass had taught her a different craft, one that welcomed the unknown rather than caging it. [536] She began to leave certain clocks deliberately unwound, letting their silence become a kind of music. [552] Visitors descended the long stair just to watch her work, and they left calmer than they came. [569] When they asked how to plan amid so many accidents, she told them what the admiral had finally learned. [588] A true course is not the absence of doubt but the courage to sail through it. [604] And beneath the ashes of who she had been, the broken-clock tinkerer became, at last, uncertainly certain of her way. [624]</story>
