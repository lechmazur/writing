<story>While waiting in the glass atrium beside the quiet brook, Orsolya oiled the elbow of her mechanical arm and rehearsed the day's first impossible question. [25] She was a dueling philosopher, and the academy paid her to fence ideas as readily as blades. [42] Her reputation was diplomatically fierce: she could disarm a senator's pride and his rapier in the same breath. [60] Today she waited for the envoy of the rival school, whose arrival had been promised at noon. [77] The two academies had quarreled for a decade, and the brook between their halls had become a border no one dared to wade. [100] Orsolya, though, believed her purpose was to turn questions into destinations, to make every argument a road rather than a wall. [121] On the atrium's center pedestal stood a half-painted sundial, abandoned by an artisan who had fled the feud. [139] Most visitors saw only unfinished work, but Orsolya had noticed that its hour-lines were skewed at deliberate, peculiar angles. [158] She suspected a hidden overture lay coded in those angles, a quiet first move toward peace that the late artisan had been too frightened to speak aloud. [185] To read it, she needed a precise rule, and from her coat she produced a danish tin sewing needle card. [205] Each slim needle, slotted in tidy rows, made a perfect protractor when laid across the painted lines. [222] Measuring patiently, she matched each angle to a letter, and the hidden overture spelled itself out: meet me unarmed, and let the brook be a bridge. [248] The discovery thrilled her, for it meant the feud could end through coded angles rather than crossed steel. [266] Then the doors banged open, and the envoy strode in with three guards, a drawn sword, and the swagger of a man expecting a brawl. [291] What followed was dignified chaos. [296] The envoy demanded a fencing match to settle whose academy held the truer wisdom, and his blade was already singing. [316] Orsolya answered courteously, raised her own rapier in her flesh hand, and parried with her mechanical arm whenever his cuts came too fast for manners. [341] Between clashes she asked him questions, the kind that demanded he choose a direction instead of a target. [359] "If you win," she said, catching his blade in her brass fingers, "where does your victory lead you?" [377] He faltered, having no answer beyond the duel itself, and in that pause she lowered her sword and laid the danish needle card upon the sundial. [403] "Your own master painted this," she explained, "and he hid a message in its angles before he ran." [421] She walked him through each measurement, needle by needle, until the envoy read the overture for himself. [438] His sword arm sank as the painted lines became words, and the words became a question he could finally turn into a destination. [461] The guards, unsure whom to threaten, lowered their pikes in confusion as the philosopher and the envoy bent together over a half-finished clock of shadows. [486] Outside, the brook kept its patient murmur, indifferent to borders. [496] By the time noon's true shadow fell across the painted dial, the two schools had agreed to finish the artisan's work, completing the sundial as a shared monument. [524] Orsolya sheathed her rapier and flexed her mechanical arm, satisfied that she had fenced well without spilling a drop. [543] She had spent the whole tense morning while waiting for a fight and had instead delivered a peace. [561] Tucking the danish tin sewing needle card back into her coat, she smiled at the envoy and asked him one last question. [583] "Now," she said, "where shall we go from here?" [592] And for the first time in a decade, the question had a place to lead. [607]</story>
