Mira trained courier pigeons in the attic of a vintage bookshop, where the smell of old glue bloomed and withered in equal measure. [23] She had come here a year ago with a forged name and a story she invented to outrun her past. [43] Reinventing her personal history had been simple; trusting another living thing had not. [56] The shop's owner, half-blind and incurious, never asked who she had been, which suited a woman remaking herself from nothing. [76] Every morning she sat beneath the single skylight and learned the birds by mapping the swirl of dust motes that drifted through its light. [100] The motes told her where the drafts moved, and the drafts told her how her pigeons would fly. [118] Balance was everything: too much trust and a bird vanished, too little and it never returned at all. [136] Her oldest pigeon, Ardent, wore a tiny capsule against his breast, and inside it lay a chip of Mars meteorite no larger than a tooth. [161] The chip had belonged to her brother, a musician who once said that everything worth saving was just an echo trying to outlast its source. [186] He had died before his songs were ever written down, and Mira carried only the fading memory of them. [205] Her motivation, the thing that kept her awake under the cold attic beams, was to preserve the echo of those lost songs. [227] So she had begun a strange project, sending her pigeons across the city with scraps of staff paper tied to their legs. [249] On each scrap she wrote a single remembered phrase of melody, then asked old musicians to copy it, add to it, and send it back. [274] The paper trails grew, knitting the broken songs together from a hundred fading hands. [288] Some pages returned blooming with new harmony; others came back withering, smeared by rain into silence. [304] Mira had learned not to mourn the lost ones too long, because mourning was a kind of distrust in what still flew. [326] One grey afternoon Ardent did not come home. [334] She climbed to the skylight and read the dust again, charting each curl of light as though it might spell out where he had gone. [359] The motes drifted east, toward the river, and so she went. [370] She found him on a windowsill, exhausted, the capsule open and the Mars meteorite chip gone. [386] In its place, tucked behind the staff paper, was a finished song. [398] Some stranger down the trail had traded the meteorite for the last missing measures, scrawling a note: a stone from another world for one returned from yours. [425] Mira held the page and felt her brother's voice rise in her chest, no longer an echo but a sound. [445] She had spent a year reinventing who she was to escape grief; now grief had reinvented itself into music. [464] Carrying Ardent home, she understood that learning to trust again had never been about the birds. [480] It had been about letting something fragile leave her hands and believing it might come back changed. [497] Back in the attic she pinned the finished song beneath the skylight, where the dust still bloomed and withered in the slow turning light. [521] She set the pigeons loose to carry copies outward, no longer afraid of which ones might not return. [539] Balance, she finally saw, was not holding on but knowing when to release. [552] The song would travel paper trails she would never see, blooming in rooms she would never enter, withering and renewing across years she would never count. [578] Strangers she would never meet might hum a phrase her brother once invented in the dark. [594] And somewhere out there a chip of Mars would keep its silence, paid gladly so that her brother's echo could finally sing. [616]
