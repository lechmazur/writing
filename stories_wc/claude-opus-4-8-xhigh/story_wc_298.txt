<story>The reckless idealist named Vael descended into the place where the dead grew fruit. [14] A catacomb orchard sprawled beneath the city, its tree roots snaking between bones like patient fingers seeking warmth. [32] Within the glow of half-lit corridors, he carried the only thing he still owned that mattered: a burned tarot card, edges curled black, its painted figure scorched into a riddle. [62] He had come to barter destinies. [68]

The orchard keeper waited among the pale trunks, a woman with eyes the color of dusk. [84] She was fiercely gentle, the way a surgeon is, the way grief becomes when it finally decides to heal you. [104] "You bring me a ruined thing," she said, lifting the card to the dim light. [119]

"It is all I have left," Vael answered. [127] "I burned my own fate so I could finally feel something true." [139]

She nodded, unsurprised, and walked him deeper. [146] Here, she explained, beneath roots and rib cages, the desperate traded the lives they were owed for the lives they craved. [167] People came to patch holes in the soul, swapping a destiny that ached for one that might not. [185]

The keeper inlaid meaning everywhere—she had spent decades pressing significance into bone and bark. [200] Every skull wore a story; every apple held a stranger's surrendered tomorrow. [212] "Eat one," she offered, "and you take their road instead of yours." [224]

Vael studied the fruit hanging cold above the ossuary. [233] Each one glowed faintly, a half-lit promise. [240] He had always been reckless, leaping toward any cause that promised to mend the world, and he reached for the brightest apple. [262]

"Wait," the keeper said, and her voice trammelled his hand mid-air, an invisible snare. [276] "Whoever's fate you take, you give them yours. [284] The burned card. [287] The empty years. [290] Are you so eager to hand a stranger your ruin?" [300]

Vael froze. [302] The roots seemed to tighten, ready to trammel him should he choose wrongly, binding fool to consequence. [319] He looked again at the orchard, truly looked, and saw the meaning she had inlaid: each tree was a person who had bartered too quickly and rooted here forever, half-tree, half-regret. [350] The bones between the roots were not the dead. [359] They were the traders, trammelled by their own impatience. [368]

"This is the trap," he whispered. [374] "You don't sell better lives. [379] You harvest reckless ones." [383]

The keeper's dusk-colored eyes softened, fiercely gentle still. [391] "I warn every soul. [395] Most do not listen. [399] You did." [401]

She handed back the burned card. [407] "A hole in the soul cannot be filled with someone else's destiny. [419] It can only be tended, like an orchard, until something grows across it." [432]

Vael held the scorched card to the corridor's faint glow. [442] The riddle on its face no longer seemed cruel; it seemed unfinished, waiting. [455] "Then I will keep my ruin," he said, with dusky resolve. [466] "And I will plant something in it myself." [474]

The keeper smiled, and for the first time the roots loosened, releasing the snare they had laid for the greedy. [494] He climbed back toward the living world, the burned tarot card warm in his fist. [509] He understood now that to patch holes in the soul, you did not barter them away. [525] You inlaid them with meaning, slowly, deliberately, until the wound became a window. [538]

Behind him, the catacomb orchard breathed in the dark, its bones patient, its fruit aglow, waiting for the next reckless idealist who mistook escape for healing. [564] Vael would not be among the rooted. [571] He carried his fate scorched but whole, a fiercely gentle ember in the half-lit dark, and he meant to grow. [591] Above, the city did not know what mercy hummed beneath its streets, but Vael did, and he would tend it. [611]</story>
