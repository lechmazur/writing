<story>The keeper of the nebula garden was an otherworldly sentimentalist who had outlived her own name. [16] She tended rows of luminous seedlings that drifted between cold suns, each bloom a memory pressed into vapor. [34] Long ago her people had vowed to share light without using fire, for fire devoured and forgot, while gardens remembered. [54] In her palm she carried a calculating machine gear, the last fragment of a vanished engine, its teeth worn smooth by centuries of worry. [78] The gear had belonged to her mother, and to her mother before that, passed down across generational patterns like an inherited heartbeat. [100] Each keeper had turned it once and listened. [108] Tonight, across the quiet sigh of falling petals, she turned it again. [120] The garden answered with sonar pinging, soft pulses that mapped the dark and told her where the dying stars were drifting. [141] She had learned long ago that sound could see what eyes could not. [154] The pings returned slow and stretched, describing a distance that approximates infinity, a gulf her ancestors had crossed and recrossed in patient transit. [177] That transit was the family burden: to ferry collected light from the failing edge of the galaxy toward the seedbeds at its heart. [200] Her grandmother had done it, weeping. [206] Her mother had done it, silent. [212] Now the petals fell around her in a slow snow of phosphor, and a troubling serenity settled over her shoulders like a shawl woven of someone else's calm. [240] She did not feel peace; she felt the shape of peace pressed against her, hollow and waiting. [257] The gear's teeth caught a thread of starlight and braided it into the soil. [271] A new seedling unfurled, pale as breath. [278] She recognized its color at once, the exact shade her mother had carried on her final transit, and the recognition frightened her. [300] The garden was repeating itself. [305] The same blooms, the same griefs, the same slow surrender, looping through daughters who never escaped the pattern. [323] She understood, kneeling there, that she had become her mother, and would soon become her grandmother, and that the gear would pass to hands not yet born. [350] For the first time she wondered whether remembering was a gift or a cage. [364] The sonar pinging continued, mapping a far nebula where the light was almost gone. [378] Duty pulled at her: to make the crossing, to gather the dimming embers and plant them where they might glow again. [399] But she paused, the gear warm in her fist, and listened to the petals fall. [414] Each petal was a year, she realized, and the falling never truly stopped. [427] She thought of fire, how easy it would be to simply burn a beacon and be seen, to be loud and brief and free. [451] But fire was forbidden, because fire was forgetting, and her line had chosen memory instead. [466] So she would share light the slow way, the patient way, the way that asked everything and promised nothing but continuance. [487] She rose. [489] She fitted the gear into the small mechanism at the garden's center, and the seedbeds began their gentle rotation. [508] The pings deepened, a heartbeat thrown into the void. [517] Then she stepped into the transit current, the river of dark that her foremothers had ridden, and felt the garden recede behind her. [540] The petals followed her a little way, then let go. [550] Ahead, the dying nebula waited, its faint glow approximating the last ember of a hearth no one would ever light again. [571] She would gather what remained. [576] She would carry it home. [581] And one day a daughter she had not yet imagined would hold the same worn gear, turn it once, and listen to the same soft sonar return. [608] The pattern would continue, troubling and serene, across more quiet sighs of more falling petals than any heart could count. [628] She did not know if this was tragedy or grace. [638] She only knew the light must move, hand to hand, without ever becoming flame. [652] And so, weeping the way her grandmother wept, silent the way her mother was silent, she made the crossing again. [672]</story>
