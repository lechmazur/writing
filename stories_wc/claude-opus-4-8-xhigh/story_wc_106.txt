They called her the golden-hour portrait thief, though she stole nothing but light. [13] Mira worked only under a fleeting golden sunset, when faces blurred into amber and guards squinted against the glare. [32] Quietly savvy, she had learned that people guard their valuables but never their reflections. [46] Her real prize hung in Emerald Station, a glass-domed terminus where trains no longer ran, only memories. [63] There, in the abandoned stationmaster's office, sat a jar of captured moonbeams. [75] She had glimpsed it once, weeks ago, glowing faintly behind dusty windows like a trapped tide. [91] The legend said the jar held illuminated moments - every instant a person had truly lived, distilled into cold silver light. [111] Mira did not believe in legends; she believed in leverage. [121] But she could not explain why her hands trembled at the thought of it. [135] Her informant communicated only through codes in flower arrangements left at her doorstep. [148] Yesterday's bouquet - white lilies bound by a single black ribbon - meant tonight, the back gate, the moon at its zenith. [168] So she came to Emerald Station by moonlight, when the green glass dome glowed like the inside of a wave. [188] The lock surrendered to her in seconds. [195] Inside, the jar waited on the desk, breathing soft pulses of light. [207] She lifted it, expecting weight, and found it nearly empty of any. [219] When she shook it gently, the moonbeams swirled and resolved into images. [231] A child laughing in summer rain; an old man weeping at a wedding; two strangers meeting on a platform under this very dome. [254] Mira recognized none of them, and yet her chest ached with luminous disbelief. [267] These were not her memories - so why did they feel like home? [279] A voice spoke behind her: "You came to steal moments, but you don't even know which ones are yours." [298] An old woman emerged from the shadows, the florist who sent the coded bouquets. [312] "I have watched you for years," she said, "stealing portraits because you cannot find your own face." [329] Mira's grip tightened on the jar. [335] "What is this thing?" she demanded. [341] "A way to reinterpret a life," the woman answered. [350] "Most people race past their brightest instants, never feeling them; this jar saves what they let slip." [367] She explained that the moonbeams were moments people forgot to feel - gathered, preserved, and waiting to be returned. [385] "And tonight," she whispered, "one of them belongs to you." [395] Mira looked again, and there, in a single trembling beam, she saw herself. [408] A small girl on this platform, holding her mother's hand under a fleeting golden sunset, the last evening before the trains stopped forever. [431] She had buried that memory so deep that she stole a thousand others trying to feel it again. [449] "I sent you the flowers," the woman admitted, "because you were finally ready to press your ear against destiny's door." [469] "I am your mother's sister, and she left this jar for you." [481] The thief who stole light now held the brightest thing she had ever touched, and could not speak. [499] She unscrewed the lid. [503] The moonbeam rose, wrapped around her like an answer, and the dome above filled with silver dawn. [520] For one impossible breath, the empty station was crowded again - travelers, lovers, and the ghost of a girl beside her mother. [541] Then it faded, and only the two women remained. [550] Mira set the jar down, lighter now, but no longer empty inside herself. [563] "I don't want to steal moments anymore," she said. [572] "I want to live the ones that find me." [581] The old woman smiled and pressed a final bloom into her hand - a single sunflower, the code for begin again. [601] Outside, the green dome dimmed toward morning, and the portrait thief walked into a sky preparing its next golden hour, finally part of the picture instead of its quiet shadow. [631]
