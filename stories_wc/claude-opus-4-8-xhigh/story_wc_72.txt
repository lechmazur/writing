<story>Mara threaded her line through the drowned colonnade, where coral had claimed the marble like slow green fire. [18] The forgotten city's garden lay beneath two hundred meters of cold Adriatic, its stone roses forever mid-bloom, its fountains pouring nothing. [39] Her lamp swung across paths where fish drifted like fallen petals. [50] She had come for the statue that every surface guild called a myth. [63] It stood at the garden's heart, a robed figure of grey stone, eyes half-lidded as though dreaming. [80] Time had not stopped here so much as forgotten to continue. [91] When her light touched it, a voice arrived inside her skull, unhurried and warm. [105] "You came to take what I guard," the living statue said, and she felt no accusation in the thought, only something strange and incisively kind. [130] In its open palm rested a foxglove-stained glass ampoule, the liquid within glowing faint violet. [145] Her guild had been certain of one thing: the ampoule held the poison that had drowned this city centuries ago. [165] "Take it," the statue urged, "but first let me give you the truth, so your certainty can learn nuance." [184] What followed came as cascading revelations, each one toppling the last. [195] The city had not been poisoned, the statue explained; the ampoule was a cure, brewed from foxglove to steady failing hearts. [216] The drowning had been deliberate, a choice by frightened people who feared the cure more than the sea. [234] And the statue itself had once been the physician who made it, her body turned to stone by the same draught she now offered. [258] "I drank too much, hoping to outlast the lie," she confessed, "and the stone preserved my mind but stilled my hands." [279] Mara had descended believing herself a tomb-robber; now she felt like a witness. [292] Each revelation threaded into the next, until her single hard fact unraveled into a tapestry of motives. [309] "There is one structural weakness in me," the statue said, "a hairline crack across this stone heart, where the draught never finished its work." [333] "Thread the cure into that fissure, and the foxglove may animate what is lifeless and call me back to flesh." [353] Mara weighed the ampoule, the only proof her guild's certainty had ever been wrong. [367] To use it was to lose the evidence; to keep it was to leave a kind woman in stone. [386] "You fear no one will believe you," the statue said, reading the thought. "Believe me instead: a truth that admits nuance outlasts any sealed jar." [411] Mara thought of all the years she had hauled answers to the surface, hard and whole, never once doubting them. [431] The certainty had felt like safety; now it felt like a cage she had carried down with her. [449] So she tipped the ampoule and let the violet drops thread into the crack. [463] For a long moment nothing moved in the garden of dead flowers. [475] Then grey toes curled, color bled back into the robe, and the physician drew her first breath in three hundred years. [496] Around her, the stone roses softened and opened, animate at last, lifeless no longer. [510] Bubbles rose like applause through water that had kept its silence for centuries. [523] The forgotten city seemed to lean closer, as though it, too, longed to be believed. [538] After revealing the secret, the woman who had been a statue took Mara's hand, no longer cold. [555] Together they threaded back through the colonnade toward the distant, swaying light. [567] Mara carried no poison home, only a story too tangled for any guild's tidy certainty. [582] And that, the physician told her on the long climb up, was the point: to crack a certainty open and welcome the nuance waiting inside. [607]</story>
