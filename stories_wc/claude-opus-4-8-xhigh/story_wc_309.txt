<story>In the age when creativity got automated, the spell-engines wrote every poem and painted every dawn, and Veil had nothing left to be bad at. [25] He was an underachieving mage, the kind whose grades had always glowed a polite, forgettable gray. [41] Now even mediocrity was obsolete. [46] So he climbed, because climbing was the one act no machine had bothered to optimize. [61] His destination was an icebound spire above the clouds, a tower so old that frost had grown into its bones like a second architecture. [85] Legends said it accommodates impossibilities, that within its walls a thing could be both true and untrue and never apologize for it. [107] Veil reached it the only way left to him: mist walking, the slow art of folding fog beneath your feet and trusting it to hold. [132] Each step was a small heresy against gravity. [140] He had never been good at it, which is precisely why it felt honest. [154] At the summit he found not treasure but a garden, frozen mid-bloom, every petal sheathed in clear ice. [172] At its center stood a folly, a little ornamental ruin built to look romantic, and its keystone pulsed with a faint, lingering luminance. [195] This was the thing he had come for: the garden folly keystone, said to hold the last light untouched by automation. [216] Veil knelt before it, breath fogging. [222] The stone did not blaze; it merely glowed, dim and certain, the way a memory glows. [238] A voice rose from it, dry as old paper. [247] "You came to steal me," it said, "or to worship me." [258] "Most do one, then the other." [264] "I came," Veil admitted, "because everything brilliant out there is borrowed." [275] "I wanted to find the sacred in the simple." [284] The keystone considered this. [288] "Simple," it repeated. [291] "You walked through cloud to tell me you want less." [301] "I want something a machine wouldn't bother to make," Veil said. [312] He reached toward the glow, and felt a strap tighten across his chest. [325] It was the harness of enchantments he had worn his whole life, the certified spell-rig every mage was issued, humming with borrowed genius. [348] The keystone's light flickered against it and would not pass. [358] "That cage answers for you," the stone murmured. [366] "It thinks your thoughts faster than you do." [374] "While you wear it, nothing here is yours." [382] Veil understood, then, the only spell the spire demanded. [391] He began to unstrap the harness. [397] The buckles resisted, frozen, reluctant, as if they too feared obsolescence. [408] One by one they came loose, and the borrowed brilliance drained from his shoulders into the cold. [425] For the first time in his life his mind was dim, unaugmented, ordinary. [438] And in that dimness the keystone's luminance reached him at last. [449] It was not dazzling. [453] It was small, warm, and entirely his to perceive, the way a single lit window is sacred to a traveler who has walked all night. [478] He saw the frozen garden not as a marvel but as a moment someone had once loved enough to preserve. [498] He saw the folly for what it was: a deliberately useless thing, built only because building it felt good. [517] That was the impossibility the spire accommodated, the one no engine could replicate. [530] Not perfection. [532] Purpose without profit. [535] "You feel it," the keystone said, softer now. [543] "I feel how little it is," Veil whispered, "and how that is the whole point." [558] The light did not grow. [563] It simply stayed, lingering, patient, refusing to be optimized into something grander. [575] Veil settled onto the ice beside the folly and let his automated harness lie discarded, a heap of brilliant tools gone quietly dim. [598] Below him the clouds churned with engineered sunsets, each one flawless and forgotten the instant it formed. [615] Up here a single old stone breathed its weak, undeniable glow, and he found he did not want anything more. [635] When he finally descended, he left the harness behind on the summit. [647] He mist-walked home without it, slower, clumsier, occasionally sinking ankle-deep into fog before catching himself. [662] It was the worst walking he had ever done. [671] It was the first walking that had ever been wholly his. [682] And somewhere above the clouds, the keystone kept its lingering luminance lit, dim and faithful, waiting for the next soul brave enough to want less. [707]</story>
