<story>The grave bore my name, weathered and lichen-spotted, and after seeing it I could no longer pretend the dates meant anything. [21] I am a lucid dream chronicler, which is a polite term for someone who drifts through other people's sleep and records whatever soars there. [45] For years I worked from a mountain ranger cabin where the snow forgot to melt and the woodstove hissed like an old confession. [68] My only instrument was a birch bark scroll that unrolled forever, swallowing ink the way the mountains swallow echoes. [87] My job, as I understood it, was to distill chaos into narrative, to take the formless flood of dreaming and give it a beginning, a middle, an end. [115] It paid nothing, but it kept the silence from winning. [125] But the dreams I logged were not strangers'; they belonged to my own past selves, each version of me sleeping in a different decade. [149] That was when the chronological confusion began in earnest. [158] I would write Tuesday and mean a Tuesday from forty years earlier, and the scroll never once corrected me. [177] Somewhere along the way I started getting jealous of those past selves. [189] They dreamed in colors I had since misplaced, and they could still soar over the ridgelines without wondering when they would land. [211] I envied the boy who flew simply because he believed flight was ordinary. [224] This is the feeling I could only name feral nostalgia, a longing so wild it bared its teeth. [242] It was not gentle remembering; it hunted me through the dark cabin and refused to be tamed. [259] I had felt the jaded part keenly, the wonder less so, until the night I dreamed my way down to the cemetery. [281] There I read my own headstone, the granite patient, the carved years already complete. [295] After seeing your own grave, the arithmetic of a life rearranges itself. [307] I woke clutching the birch bark scroll, and for the first time the chronology did not matter. [324] If the ending was already engraved, then the jumble of decades was simply mine to arrange however I pleased. [343] So I stopped envying the past selves and began stitching them together instead. [356] I wrote the flying boy into the same paragraph as the tired man, and let them share one sky. [375] The feral nostalgia did not vanish, but it lay down at my feet like a wolf finally fed. [393] The cabin, snowbound and humming, became less a hermitage than a workshop. [405] Every dawn I unrolled more bark and copied the night's chaos into something with shape. [420] I learned to honor the chronological confusion as a kind of freedom rather than a flaw. [436] What did it matter which decade a memory belonged to, if every one of them was already mine to keep? [456] I copied a winter when I was nine and flew over the frozen lake, and a summer when I was fifty and could barely climb the porch steps. [484] Side by side on the bark, the two ages stopped arguing about which had come first. [500] The wonder, I admit, returned slowly, dragging its jaded shadow behind it. [512] On clear nights I still dreamed myself aloft, and I let the old self and the new self soar together over the white peaks. [536] The jaded wonder of it was that I had to die, at least on paper, to learn how to live the chaos as story. [560] When the scroll is finally full, I will roll it tight and leave it on the headstone I have already seen. [581] Let whoever finds it read the distilled chaos and decide, in whatever order they like, who I was. [599] And if they get jealous of the version of me who could finally fly, well, I will understand that hunger better than anyone. [622]</story>
