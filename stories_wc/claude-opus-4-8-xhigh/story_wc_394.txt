<story>Mox called herself a pioneering cart glider, which mostly meant she crashed gorgeous arcs across the sky and lived to brag. [21] Her workshop was a raft of gears-and-cogs lashed together, drifting on an acid sea that hissed whenever it tasted her dropped tools. [43] She loved that hiss; it sounded like applause from something that wanted to eat her. [58] Bolted to her workbench sat the strangest treasure she owned: a hunter's arrow tip, black and notched, older than any language she could name. [82] She had pulled it from the gut of a dead machine-beast, and ever since she had been steadily curious about the marks scratched into its flange. [108] They were not decoration; they were instructions. [115] Mox was building a yearning algorithm, a little mind of springs and punch-cards that could want things the way she wanted them. [137] Everyone on the floating shops laughed at her, because a machine that yearns is a machine that complains. [155] But Mox had a private goal, absurd and holy: she wanted to ring a bell that no one had heard yet, a tone so new the acid sea itself would flinch. [186] To do that, she needed the arrow tip to talk. [196] The trouble was that its marks belonged to no alphabet, so she could not simply read them; she had to transliterate them into something her gears could chew. [224] Her method was ridiculous and it worked. [231] She fed light from a lantern through the images in a kaleidoscope, then let the scattered patterns fall across the arrow tip's etchings. [254] Each twist of the tube broke one ancient symbol into a constellation of familiar shapes, and those shapes she could copy onto punch-cards. [277] Throughout the evolution of the machine, from a clattering wreck to a humming organism, the kaleidoscope was her dictionary. [296] Turn, the symbol became a spiral; turn again, the spiral became a number; turn once more, the number became a note. [317] Slowly the yearning algorithm learned to want a single sound. [327] Mox watched its desire grow with the delight of a parent and the suspicion of a thief. [344] On the ninetieth morning the raft tilted, and the arrow tip slid toward the bubbling water. [360] She caught it a finger's width from the surface, laughing like a lunatic at her own luck. [377] In that instant she understood the last untranslated mark: it was not a letter at all, but a warning. [396] The bell no one had heard was the bell that summoned the beast whose belly had held the arrow. [415] The hunter had not made the tip to kill; he had made it to silence a song that called monsters. [435] Mox sat with this knowledge as the acid chewed its patient applause beneath her. [449] Her machine was nearly finished, and it wanted, with all its punch-card heart, to ring. [464] She could let it sing and meet whatever rose from the green deep, or she could gut the algorithm and keep the silence the hunter died defending. [491] Steadily curious to the end, she chose a third, more irreverent path. [503] She transliterated the warning itself into the song, folding the danger into the melody so the bell would announce both the wonder and the teeth behind it. [530] Then she fed the final card into the machine and stepped back. [542] The yearning algorithm shuddered, drew breath through a hundred brass throats, and rang. [555] The note was new; she was certain of it, because the acid sea went utterly still, as if the whole green ocean had stopped to listen. [581] Somewhere far below, something ancient turned over in its sleep, considered the call, and chose, for now, to stay down. [601] Mox stood on her trembling raft of cogs, deafened by a sound only her invention had ever truly heard, and grinned at the impossible. [625] She had rung the bell no one heard yet. [634] And the bell, gloriously, rang back. [640]</story>
