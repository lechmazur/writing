<story>Mira built hedge mazes for a living, but she had never designed one she could not leave. [17] The living bridge garden grew across a gorge where roots of ancient fig trees had been woven, generation by generation, into a span that breathed. [42] She had come seeking the halo fragment, a sliver of cold gold rumored to rest at the maze's heart. [61] Old letters claimed it could let a person discover the texture of time, the grain and weave beneath every passing hour. [82] That phrase had haunted her since childhood, since she first watched photographs of her mother fade. [98] The images had dimmed year by year until only pale ghosts remained, and Mira had vowed to understand why moments thinned and vanished. [121] So she entered the garden, where the hedges were not planted but plaited from the living bridges themselves. [139] The walls curved inward and outward at once, folding space the way no ordinary geometry allowed. [155] She realized, with a quaking reverie, that the designer here had built the maze through hyperbolic geometry, so that every step multiplied the paths around her. [181] Corridors that should have narrowed instead bloomed into more corridors, endless and impossible. [194] Mira, herself a maze-maker, recognized the genius and the cruelty of it. [206] Here, distance was not measured in footsteps but in remembrance. [216] When she rounded the first hedge, she saw her mother standing young and laughing. [230] When she rounded the second, she saw herself as a girl, clutching a fading snapshot. [245] The maze did not show illusions; it showed the past, layered and breathing, drawn close by the warped curvature of the walls. [267] She understood then that she was becoming retrocognitively trapped, snared in perceptions of bygone things she could not stop seeing. [287] Each turn pulled her deeper into memory, and the present grew faint as an old photograph left in sunlight. [306] At the maze's center, suspended above a pool, hung the halo fragment. [318] It glowed with the same pale gold as the haloes in the saints' pictures her mother once kept. [336] Mira reached for it, and the texture of time unfolded against her fingertips. [349] She felt the weave at last, threads of every instant pressing like coarse cloth against her skin. [366] She felt how the present was thick and warm, and how the past frayed thinner with each year. [384] She understood, finally, why photographs faded: time itself was disintegrating behind her, unspooling thread by thread. [400] The knowledge was beautiful and unbearable, a reverie that made the bridge tremble beneath her. [415] But the hyperbolic walls held her fast, each receding corridor a year she could not walk back through. [433] She saw her mother again, and again, and the sight was a hook in her heart. [449] If she stayed, she would dissolve into those faded images, become one more ghost upon a curling print. [467] Mira pressed the halo fragment to her chest and forced herself to feel the present's rough, living grain. [485] She fixed on the bridge's breathing roots, the warm sap, the now that had not yet thinned. [502] Using the maze's own logic, she imagined a path that curved back toward the single thread of the present. [521] Through the hyperbolic geometry that had trapped her, she traced a line of shortest return, a geodesic homeward. [539] The corridors of memory began to disintegrate around her, folding shut like closing eyes. [553] Her mother's laughter dimmed, the young face faded as gently as the photographs had once faded. [569] Mira wept, but she walked, holding the texture of the present like a rope. [583] The living bridge carried her out, root by root, breath by breath, into open air. [598] She stood at the gorge's edge, the halo fragment cooling in her palm, no longer reaching backward. [615] She had discovered the texture of time, and learned that to touch every thread was to be unwoven by them. [635] Now she designed her mazes differently, planting paths that always led forward. [647] And though her own photographs would fade in their turn, she let them, for she had felt the warm, coarse grain of being here. [671] She had felt the now, and chosen it, again and again.
