<story>Mara was a natural history illustrator, and her hands knew the exact weight of a wing. [16] Tonight, though, she traded her pens for a paddle and steered a thin boat through the rooftop canal system that wove between the city's leaning towers. [42] Around her neck hung an infinity symbol pendant, warm as a coal, given by her grandmother with a single instruction: find the place where echoes are born. [69] The pendant, her grandmother had sworn, sculpts time itself, but only by borrowing it from someone vast and sleeping. [88] That someone was the giant beneath the city, whose slow breathing tides filled the canals, and whose nap lasted exactly one night. [110] Mara had to complete her search across the giant's nap, before he woke and the borrowed hours were reclaimed. [129] She pressed the pendant, and the world stuttered like a needle on a scratched record. [144] Time bent; the canal froze mid-ripple, and she drifted forward on borrowed minutes. [157] As an illustrator, she had spent her life cataloguing things that no longer existed, and she suspected echoes were simply the last drawings sound left behind. [183] If echoes were born somewhere, she meant to sketch their first cry. [195] The canals climbed in tiers, looping over arched bridges and threading through bell towers that had long ago stopped ringing. [215] At the highest tower, she heard it: a melody rising toward resolution, then breaking off. [230] It was an interrupted cadence, a phrase that promised an ending and refused to give one. [246] The unfinished sound hung in the air, trembling, unable to die because it had never been allowed to land. [265] Mara understood then that echoes were not born from finished music, but from interrupted music, from cadences that never resolved. [285] Inside the tower she found a brass cage, and within it a small wooden bird, its beak open on a note it could not complete. [310] Every echo in the city, she realized, was this bird's single suspended song, reflected and multiplied across stone and water. [330] Her grandmother had not asked her to find the echoes, but to free their source. [345] To uncage the bird would let the note finally resolve, and the borrowed time would carry the resolution back to the waking world. [368] But Mara hesitated, sketchbook open, because to resolve the cadence was to silence every echo ever born from it. [387] The giant's breathing slowed beneath her; his nap was ending, and her borrowed hours thinned to a single trembling thread. [407] She made her choice the way an illustrator does, by drawing what must be true. [422] On the page she sketched the bird in flight, beak closed, the note completed and gentle. [438] Then she lifted the latch and let the wooden bird out into the frozen air. [453] It sang the missing note, and the cadence resolved at last, a soft chord settling like dust. [470] Across the towers the old echoes did not vanish; instead, freed from their endless waiting, they finished themselves and faded one by one into peace. [495] The giant exhaled, and time snapped back into motion, the canals surging warm around Mara's boat. [511] Her pendant cooled, its borrowed power spent, the infinity loop now an ordinary circle of silver. [527] She paddled home as dawn climbed the towers, the city strangely quiet, scrubbed clean of its restless echoes. [545] In her studio she pinned the new drawing beside her extinct birds, the first creature she had ever illustrated back into freedom. [567] Some nights she still hears one faint echo near the highest tower, the bird's completed note, looping softly, infinite and finally free. [589] It is the only echo she never tries to draw, because some songs are meant to keep singing. [607]</story>
