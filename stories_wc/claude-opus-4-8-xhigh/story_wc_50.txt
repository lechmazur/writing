<story>Maren had always been a reflective locksmith, the kind who heard more in a tumbler's click than the lock itself intended. [21] She had come to the marble cavern in the lull of a wounded season, when the orchards above lay frostbitten and the village had little work for hands like hers. [51] Word had spread of a strange door deep in the white rock, sealed by a broken timer mechanism no smith could coax open. [74] The locals called what lay behind it the harvest of echoes, though none of them could say what that meant. [94] Maren intended to find out. [99] She set her lantern on a shelf of stone and studied the door for three days before touching it. [118] The mechanism was unlike any she had seen: gears tied not to springs but to slivers of polished glass, angled to catch light that drifted down from a fissure far above. [149] To reverse-engineer it, she realized, she would have to think the way its maker thought, in terms of sun rather than steel. [171] Maren had a peripherally focused mind, attentive to the things at the edges of a problem rather than its center, and that habit served her now. [197] She did not stare at the glass; she watched what the glass threw onto the surrounding marble. [214] Her method was patient and simple: studying reflection angles at different times, dawn through dusk, marking each bright sliver with a scratch of chalk. [238] Day after day, across the lull of that wounded season, she returned, and the chalk marks crept across the pale stone like the track of a slow, deliberate animal. [267] By the second week she understood: the door had never been meant to open by force, but by recognition, when light struck every glass at once. [293] The broken timer mechanism had simply lost its memory of which moment that was. [307] So Maren rebuilt the memory herself, bending each sliver toward the angle her chalk had proven true, until the gears remembered the hour they had forgotten. [333] On the last cold afternoon of the wounded season, light fell through the fissure and met every glass together. [352] The mechanism turned, the door drew back, and the cavern beyond filled with sound. [366] This was the harvest of echoes: every voice the cavern had ever held, stored in the marble itself, released now in a single shimmering chord. [391] Maren heard children who had played here centuries ago, miners singing, a woman weeping, a man laughing at a joke long dissolved. [413] She stood very still, letting the wonder pour over her, and understood at last why she had come. [431] All her life she had locked things away to keep them safe, and now she wanted to do the same with this. [453] Her motivation, she admitted, was to store wonder in the cellar of reason, for later. [468] She wanted proof, kept somewhere cool and orderly inside her, that the world still held things she could not pick apart with a file and a tension wrench. [496] But the echoes were already fading, as echoes must, and she let them go. [510] A locked-away wonder, she saw, was only a memory of wonder, dry as a pressed flower. [526] Wonder lived only in the moment the glass aligned. [535] So she did the thing a locksmith almost never does. [545] She loosened the slivers again, scattering their angles, so the door would close and the echoes would gather once more in the dark, waiting for the next patient stranger. [574] She climbed back toward the frostbitten orchards carrying no proof at all, only the knowledge that she had heard it. [594] The wounded season would heal into spring, and the orchards would bear again, and Maren would mend locks in the village as before. [617] But she walked differently now, peripherally focused on the bright edges of ordinary things, listening for the small echoes everyone else stepped past. [640]</story>
