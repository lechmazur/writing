<story>Maren had spent forty years growing younger, and tonight her hands looked sixteen. [13] As a backwards aging researcher, she had charted every reversal in her own skin. [57] The boy's name was Eli, and he wanted to learn the oldest art: how to vanish so that he might appear. [78] "Sit," she said, and the sycamore hush of the grove folded around them like a held breath. [95] Below the platform lay the magnetic grotto, its iron walls drinking starlight until the whole cavern glowed faintly silver. [114] Eli watched the stones around them drift, pulled and steadied by the grotto's strange pull. [129] "You think vanishing is about leaving," Maren said. [137] "It is not." [140] She lifted a smooth stone and weighed it in her palm. [151] "Watch the water, and do not blink." [158] She threw the stone. [162] It struck the pond, and rings spread outward in patient circles. [173] Maren read the patterns in the rings of a pond after a stone is thrown the way other scholars read books. [194] "Each ring is a year," she murmured. [201] "The first is loud, the last is silent, yet the silent one travels farthest." [215] Eli leaned forward through the trance that the rippling light induced, his pupils wide and slow. [231] "I do not understand," he whispered. [237] "You will," she said, and she chose to withhold the rest. [248] This was the discipline she had learned across her unwinding decades: to weave truth carefully, never all at once. [293] "When I was old," she said, "I feared disappearing." [302] "Now that I grow young, I understand it differently." [311] Eli's eyes followed the last ring as it kissed the pond's far edge and dissolved. [326] "Where did the ring go?" he asked. [333] "Nowhere," she said. [336] "It became the whole pond." [631] Soon she would be too young to teach, too young to remember the patterns. [645] But the lesson was placed, like a stone, and its rings would travel long after she forgot the throw.
