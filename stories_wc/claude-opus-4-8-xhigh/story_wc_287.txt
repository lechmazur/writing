<story>The last rose on the fire escape had held on through the cold, and the fire escape gardener, Maela, waited for it the way some people wait for trains. [29] She was wistfully pragmatic, the sort who pressed dried blooms into books yet still swept the dead leaves before breakfast. [49] Tonight, after the final petal dropped from the old rosebush, she knew the season of waiting was finished. [67] In her palm she held the one thing her grandfather had left her: a lockmaster's bitting gauge tooth, a small brass sliver worn smooth by his thumb. [94] He had been a lockmaster, a man who measured keys and trusted the precise teeth that opened safe, locked doors. [114] Before he died he polished that tooth until it shone like a coin, and told her it would one day reflect light into dark corners. [139] She had never understood the riddle, only kept it close, the way one guards a small bright secret. [157] Below the building lay the luminous harbor by moonlight, its water silvered and trembling under a low and watchful moon. [177] A quivering hush lay over the docks, the kind of silence that holds its breath before it speaks. [195] Maela took the petal and the tooth, climbed down the iron stairs, and untied the little rowboat at the pier. [215] She rowed out with the rhythm of oars in water, each stroke a slow heartbeat against the hush. [233] Out past the moored ships there was a boy she had seen for weeks, sleeping in a hull no one claimed. [254] He guarded himself like a locked thing, flinching at footsteps, trusting nothing that came near. [269] Maela understood that wary, protected vulnerability, for she had grown her garden on a fire escape so that no one could trample it. [292] She rowed closer and rested the oars, letting the boat drift into the dark beneath the hull. [309] There, in that lightless corner where the boy hid, the moon could not reach. [323] Now she understood her grandfather's riddle at last. [331] She lifted the polished bitting gauge tooth and angled it against the moon. [344] A thin blade of silver light leapt from the brass and slid into the dark corner, landing on the boy's startled face. [366] He froze, then stared at the small wavering glow as if it were a hand offered without demand. [384] Maela did not speak, for words might break the quivering hush between them. [397] Instead she set the dropped petal on the water and nudged it toward him with one oar. [414] The petal floated into the beam of light, glowing pale red, a small proof that something could still bloom. [433] Night after night she returned to instill that fragile certainty in him, one careful beam at a time. [451] She brought bread, then seeds, then a clay pot, and taught him to coax green things from nothing. [469] He rowed with her, learning the rhythm of oars in water until his hands stopped trembling. [485] The harbor stayed luminous and patient, holding their small ritual in its silver hush. [499] Slowly the boy unlocked, the way a careful tooth turns a stubborn mechanism without forcing it. [515] By spring he stood on her fire escape, watering a cutting they had saved from the old rosebush. [533] Maela handed him the bitting gauge tooth and folded his fingers around it. [546] It is yours now, she said, wistfully pragmatic as ever, because every dark corner needs someone to light it. [565] The boy looked at the harbor, where the moon still waited to be borrowed and given away. [582] He understood his task, and held the small bright tooth as if it were a heart he had finally been trusted to keep. [605]</story>
