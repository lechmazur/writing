<story>The glass-orchid caretaker arrived before the clouds had finished blooming. [10] Her name was Sel, though she rarely spoke it aloud anymore. [21] She tended the opal library above clouds, where every shelf held a marvel that no one came to see. [40] These were deserted marvels: instruments that had outlived their players, maps of countries that had dissolved, jars of weather that no longer fell. [63] Sel moved among them in a way that was gently systematic, dusting each in the order it had been forgotten. [83] Her true work, though, lay deeper, across the museum of extinct feelings. [95] It was a wing of the library where emotions humans had abandoned were kept alive in fragile cases. [113] There was the ache of waiting for letters, the joy of naming stars, the particular grief one felt for a house never built. [136] Sel cataloged them with the foxglove regret of someone who knew that beauty, once unvisited, slowly forgets itself. [154] Her single purpose was simple and impossible: to preserve the sound of forgotten names. [168] Names, she believed, were the last echoes a feeling leaves behind. [179] When a name went unspoken long enough, the feeling attached to it would dim and finally go out. [197] To keep them, Sel used the only tool that still answered her: a wind chime whose bamboo tube had cracked long ago. [219] The crack changed its voice; instead of ringing, it breathed, and that breath could hold a name the way wax holds a thumbprint. [242] Each evening she walked the cloud-lit aisles and let the chime catch the names off the cases before they faded. [262] But the bamboo could only release what it caught by the echo of a long-forgotten song. [278] It was a lullaby her grandmother had hummed, a melody Sel herself was now the last to remember. [296] So each night she sang it, and the cracked tube exhaled the rescued names back into their cases, keeping the feelings warm. [318] On this evening, Sel noticed a case she did not recognize, its glass clouded, its label blank. [335] Inside lay a feeling she had no word for, trembling like a moth at the end of its season. [354] She lifted the chime, but the tube stayed silent; this feeling had no name left to catch. [371] Then she understood, with foxglove regret, that the feeling in the case was her own. [386] It was the feeling of being the last keeper, the very emotion that had no audience and would die with her. [407] If she did nothing, it would join the deserted marvels, beautiful and unwitnessed. [420] So Sel did the only thing left to a caretaker with no successor. [433] She gave the feeling a name, her grandmother's name, the one woven into the lullaby. [448] She spoke it aloud, and the blank label filled with letters of light. [461] The cracked bamboo, hearing a name at last, breathed it in and held it fast. [476] Now the feeling had a sound, and a sound, Sel knew, could outlast its keeper. [491] She hung the chime in the highest window of the opal library, where the wind off the clouds would never stop. [512] Each gust now sang her grandmother's name, and her own quiet feeling, across the museum of extinct feelings. [530] Sel sat down among the cases, gently systematic even now, folding her hands in her lap. [546] She did not need to be remembered, only heard. [555] And somewhere far below, a child looking up at the clouds paused, certain she had caught the echo of a long-forgotten song. [577] The child did not know the name she heard, but she hummed it anyway, and the marvels above were deserted no longer. [599] In that small, rising tune, every forgotten name found a new mouth to live in. [614]</story>
