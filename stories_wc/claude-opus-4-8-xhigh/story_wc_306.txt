<story>They called me a conscience for hire, and I had never once refused a paying doubt. [16] Mara found me at the breezy lighthouse at dawn, where the lantern still spun out of habit. [33] She had climbed to me after the campfire's last spark died, ashes still cooling on her sleeves. [50] In her fist she held a hollow mirror shard, its silver scraped away so it reflected nothing. [67] "I need you to decide if I should forgive my brother," she said, sincerely insincere, like someone rehearsing grief. [86] My work was simple: clients wore certainty like armor, and I sold them the cloak of doubt to soften it. [106] Most people hire a conscience to be told they are right; the rare ones come to welcome nuance into certainty. [126] Mara, I suspected, was the rare kind, though she hid it behind a flat, practiced voice. [142] I did not read palms or cards. [149] My method was deciphering wind chime selections, and the lighthouse balcony held nine of them, each tuned to a different question. [170] The dawn wind chose which chimes to wake, and from that choosing I drew my answers. [186] "Hold the shard up," I told her, "and ask the wind, not me." [199] She lifted the hollow mirror shard, and because it reflected nothing, she had to look past it to the sea. [219] The breeze quickened, and the copper chime rang first, the one I called Mercy. [233] Then the iron chime answered, the one I called Wound, and the two would not stop arguing. [250] A cleaner reader would have lied and named one winner. [260] I had been that reader for years, selling tidy verdicts to people who paid for permission. [276] "They are both ringing," I admitted, and the honesty surprised me more than her. [290] Mara's flat voice cracked, and underneath it I heard the protected vulnerability she had carried up the stairs. [308] "I wanted you to choose so I wouldn't have to," she whispered. [320] That was why she had played sincerely insincere, dressing a true wound as a transaction. [335] I realized then why she had brought the hollow shard. [345] A whole mirror gives you back a single face, certain and final. [357] A hollow one forces you to choose what to see, and to keep choosing. [371] I took the cloak of doubt from my satchel, the grey thing I usually sold and never wore. [389] Instead of draping it over her, I draped it over myself. [400] "I can't sell you certainty," I said, "because Mercy and Wound are both true." [414] "Forgive him on Tuesday, resent him on Thursday, and let both be honest." [427] She laughed, wet and startled, and the chimes settled into a chord rather than a contest. [443] Then she reached up to unhook the iron chime from the rail. [455] "Let me keep Wound," she said, "so I remember the forgiveness cost something." [468] I let her, though no client had ever unhooked a chime before. [480] She pressed the hollow mirror shard into my palm in payment. [491] "You should look through it sometime," she told me, descending into the brightening morning. [505] When she was gone, I held the shard to my eye and saw the sea moving through the empty frame. [525] For the first time my trade felt honest, because I had stopped pretending to be sure. [541] I kept the cloak of doubt on, and I have worn it ever since. [555] A conscience for hire who is never uncertain is just a flatterer with good acoustics. [570] Now, each dawn, I let the breezy lighthouse and its nine chimes argue without rushing to silence them. [588] My motivation had quietly changed: not to deliver verdicts, but to welcome nuance into certainty. [603] And whenever a new client climbs up after the campfire's last spark dies, hands clenched around something sharp, I hand them the hollow shard first. [628]</story>
