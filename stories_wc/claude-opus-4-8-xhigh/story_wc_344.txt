<story>Mira had earned the name lunar-eclipse locksmith for the way she opened things only when the light went strange. [19] She climbed to the frozen library above clouds because every other door in her life had jammed shut. [37] The shelves wore frost like old varnish, and the whole place smelled of driftwood pulled from a tide that had forgotten her. [59] She had come to learn the rhythm of repair, the patient cadence that turns broken things back toward function. [78] Under the soft hush of a distant morning, she found the librarian waiting beside a cold lantern. [95] He was old, hauntingly bright in the eyes, as if some inner candle refused to gutter. [111] "You want the workshop," he said, "but the workshop wants you to read first." [125] He led her to the library's corner, where scraps of prophecy lay hidden between two leaning atlases. [142] The fragments were torn and brittle, yet through them she read instructions no single book had dared to keep whole. [162] They spoke of a clockmaker's gear assortment sealed in a tin, and of a hall of mirrors that remembered every hand that had ever failed to fix it. [190] "To learn repair," the prophecy warned, "one must first repair the thing that breaks the reader." [206] Mira followed the corridor the scraps described until the frost gave way to silver, and she stepped into the hall of mirrors. [228] A thousand Miras turned to face her, each holding a different broken thing she had once loved. [245] One cradled her father's stopped watch, the very reason she had ever wanted to mend anything at all. [263] On a pedestal at the center sat the tin, exactly as the prophecy had promised. [278] She knelt and tried to open it, but the lid would not yield to ordinary strength. [294] The locksmith in her listened instead, waiting for the light to go strange the way it always did at an eclipse. [315] When the mirrors dimmed, swallowing their own brightness, she felt the tin loosen and uncap with a soft sigh. [334] Inside lay the clockmaker's gear assortment, dozens of tiny brass wheels, each toothed for a purpose she did not yet understand. [355] She lifted her father's watch from the mirror-hand that offered it and pried open its frozen back. [372] Gear by gear she tried each piece, learning the rhythm of repair the way a tide learns a shore. [391] Wrong sizes slipped loose; one fit, caught, and held with a click that felt like forgiveness. [407] The watch ticked, small and certain, and the thousand mirror-Miras lowered their broken things as if released. [424] She understood then that the thing the prophecy meant by the reader was her own stalled grief. [441] The watch had stopped the morning her father died, and she had stopped with it. [456] Now both moved again, gentle and inevitable, under that same soft hush of a distant morning. [472] When she returned to the corner, the librarian was gone, but a fresh scrap waited atop the leaning atlases. [491] It read, in her own newly steady hand, that every repair begins by mending the mender. [507] Mira pocketed the gear assortment, the lone fitted wheel still warm against her palm. [521] She had become the clockmaker the library had been waiting for, the keeper of its frozen, ticking heart. [539] Outside the frost-bright windows, the clouds rolled like an ocean she no longer feared to cross. [555] She wound the watch once more, listening, finally fluent in the patient music of things made whole again. [573] Somewhere below, a tide she had grieved for years turned without her, and she let it, smiling. [590] Here, above the clouds, the broken would always find her, and she would always know how to begin. [608]</story>
