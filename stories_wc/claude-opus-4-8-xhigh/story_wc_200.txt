<story>Mireille had been a lost soul collector for thirty winters, and she wore the title the way others wore old coats. [21] Her work began where rivers forgot themselves: in Echo Gorge, that long stone throat where every cry returned softened, as if the cliffs were learning to forgive. [48] She came in the hush of a hidden sunrise, the brief grey moment before light spilled over the eastern rim and the gorge began its singing. [74] In her satchel she carried a single tool: a crocodile's tooth, yellowed and curved, given to her by a dying ferryman who swore it could open the mouths of the silent dead. [106] Souls did not speak easily; they had to be coaxed, and Mireille coaxed them through the distorted notes of a misaligned harp. [128] The harp had been hers since girlhood, and she had never once tuned it true. [143] A perfect chord, she had learned, frightened the dead, but a crooked one matched the way grief actually felt, and the souls leaned toward it like cold hands toward fire. [173] This morning she had come to pursue one soul in particular: a boy named Toller, drowned three years past, whose mother still could not laugh. [198] That was the heart of Mireille's strange vocation, the reason she walked into gorges others feared: she meant to integrate laughter into healing, to gather the confessions of the dead so the living might smile without guilt. [235] She was modestly fearless, not brave in the loud way of heroes, but quietly unwilling to flinch from sorrow. [254] She knelt on the cold riverstones, set the crocodile's tooth between two strings as a plectrum, and drew the first sour, beautiful note. [277] The gorge caught the sound and bent it, and from the bend rose a shape no larger than a sleeping child. [298] "You play it wrong," the spectre of Toller said, and almost smiled. [310] "Wrong is the only language you'll trust," Mireille answered, plucking again. [321] The spectral confessions came then, as they always did, tumbling out in the crooked rhythm the harp permitted. [339] Toller confessed that he had not drowned saving anyone heroic; he had simply waded too far to catch a floating apple, laughing the whole time. [364] "Mother thinks I died sad," he whispered. [371] "That's why she buried her laughing along with me." [380] Mireille felt the luminous ache of it, the way truth could shine and wound in the same breath. [398] Here was the gift she had come to collect: not grief, but the memory of joy hidden inside it. [417] She played the boy's last laugh back to him, distorted and tender, until the spectre brightened like dawn touching water. [437] "Tell her," Toller said, dissolving into the gorge's softening echoes, "tell her the apple was sweet." [453] Mireille caught the confession in the hollow of the crocodile's tooth, where it glowed faintly, a small warm seed of mirth. [474] The hidden sunrise finally broke over the rim, and the whole gorge filled with gold, every echo turning to a chord at last in tune. [499] She climbed out as the light grew, carrying the tooth like a lantern against her chest. [515] That evening she would sit with Toller's mother and, instead of offering the cold comfort of solemnity, she would press the warm tooth into the woman's hand. [542] She would let the mother hear her son laugh once more, and watch the unbearable thing become bearable, become even sweet. [563] This was Mireille's whole creed: that healing was not the absence of pain but the return of laughter into it, the way light returns to a gorge. [590] She had collected one more lost soul, and given it back as joy. [603] That was enough; that was everything. [609]</story>
