<story>Three weeks after I stopped the medication, I came to the disused railway station, its platform swallowed by ivy. [19] I am a river chronicler, paid to follow water and write down what is true about it. [36] Truth was the only thing I trusted once the pills stopped softening the edges of my days. [53] My method had always been simple and stubborn: I am daringly methodical, charting each bend by its map references before I let myself feel anything. [78] The river I had been following dried into mud a mile back, and the map references ended at this ruin, as if the cartographer had given up here too. [107] I had come because of my brother. [114] He had drowned in a river I was supposed to be watching, and for a year my work had felt like an apology nobody would accept. [140] I had stopped the medication so I could finally meet that grief without a chemical fog, hoping the rawness might burn a path to self-forgiveness. [165] Inside, the ticket hall smelled of wet leaves and rust. [175] On a bench sat an old man holding an accordion, its bellows patched with strips of silver tape. [193] He squeezed it, and the patched bellows wheezed a single chord that seemed to hold the whole empty station together. [213] He nodded toward a door at the back. [221] Go read the wall, he said, the way men say things they cannot say themselves. [236] The old bathroom was a shrine of graffiti, decades of travelers leaving their truths on the cracked tiles. [254] I read it the only way I know how, methodically, line by line, as if each scrawl were a tributary feeding some larger current of meaning. [280] Most was noise, but one sentence, written in faded marker, stopped me cold. [293] The river does not blame the stone it cannot move. [303] It did not name my brother, but it alluded to him completely, to the morning I could not reach him through that current. [326] Beneath it, someone else had written a second line: You are a stone too, and the water still loves you. [346] I sat on the cold floor and let the rawness come, the grief I had spent a year medicating into silence. [367] It hurt, but it was honest, and honesty was the closest thing to solace I had felt since the funeral. [387] When I went back out, the old man was still playing. [398] Those tiles have outlived every train, he said, and the bellows folded and unfolded like a slow, forgiving lung. [417] I told him about my brother, about the map references that always promised I could chart my way out of any disaster. [439] Some rivers, he answered, you cannot chart; you can only forgive them for being deeper than your courage. [457] I opened my chronicle and, for the first time, wrote nothing about water. [470] I copied the graffiti instead, both lines, treating that broken bathroom as the truest map I had ever found. [489] The station, overgrown and forgotten, had given me a destination my methods never could. [503] I stayed until dusk, listening to the taped bellows breathe. [513] When I finally rose to leave, the old man pressed the accordion once more, a chord that sounded like permission. [533] I walked out through the ivy, methodical as ever, but lighter. [544] I had come seeking a river and found a wall instead, and somehow the wall had told me the truth the water never would. [568] I was a stone, the water still loved me, and that, at last, was a path I could follow home. [588] Behind me, the music thinned into the dusk, but I carried its rhythm in my chest like a second pulse. [608]</story>
