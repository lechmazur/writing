<story>Prince Aldric had not bathed in nine days, and the soot of the forge had become his second skin. [19] His palace, suspended over the abyss, hung anchored to giant stalactites by chains older than memory. [35] Below, the mechanical world ticked on, a lattice of brass valleys where iron birds no longer sang. [52] Aldric remembered when beauty lived there, before the engineers replaced every living thing with gears. [67] Now he labored alone to reawaken that lost beauty, and his only hope rested in a single object. [85] On his workbench sat the last extinct bird's egg, smuggled from a museum collapsing into rust. [101] The egg was cracked, its life long fled, yet within its shell slept the blueprint of song. [118] Aldric intended to assemble a creature from its fragments, half-flesh and half-clockwork, a flawed genesis born of desperation. [136] He worked through the night, hypothesizing about everything: how warmth might coax tissue, how brass might mimic a heart. [155] Every hypothesis trembled like the palace itself, which swayed whenever the deep winds rose. [169] The whole chamber existed in a quaking reverie, lamplight shivering across his instruments. [182] He guessed that the membrane needed copper threads, and that the yolk could feed a tiny engine. [199] Each theory branched into ten more, and he chased them all until dawn bruised the stalactites silver. [216] With trembling fingers he set the gears, wound the spring, and warmed the shell against his chest. [233] He whispered every prayer he half-remembered, hypothesizing that even faith might be a kind of ingredient. [249] For three days the egg lay silent, and the prince's hope curdled into doubt. [263] Then, on the fourth night, the shell quivered, and a sound like a struck wire escaped it. [280] Aldric leaned close, breath caught, living the entire moment as one long held heartbeat. [294] The egg split, and out crawled the creature he had dreamed: feathers stitched to gears, eyes of dim glass. [313] It was beautiful and broken at once, its left wing trembling, its right wing locked and still. [330] The flawed genesis had given him life, but life that could never truly fly. [344] Aldric wept, and his tears fell upon the small creature's broken plumage. [356] Yet then it opened its beak and sang one note, ragged, off-key, and impossibly alive. [371] The sound was bitterly sweet, a melody mourning all that the mechanical world had murdered. [386] Through the discovery of that single note, the prince understood his error and his triumph together. [402] He had not restored the old beauty, for the old beauty was extinct beyond all recall. [418] Instead he had made something new, imperfect, and his own. [428] The bird hobbled to the window's edge, where the stalactites dropped into the brass gloom. [443] It could not fly, but it could sing, and its song drifted down into the valleys of gears. [461] Far below, an engineer paused at her workbench, lifting her head toward a sound she had never heard. [479] Aldric watched the palace tremble on its ancient chains, and for once the quaking felt like applause. [496] He had set out to reawaken beauty in a mechanical world, and beauty had answered, though wearing scars. [514] The bedraggled prince knelt beside his creation, no longer ashamed of the soot or the failure. [530] Both of them were flawed, both of them were born from ruin, and both, somehow, endured. [546] He decided he would assemble more of them, a whole choir of broken birds, until the gears remembered music. [565] Outside, the wind rose again, and the suspended palace swung gently like a cradle. [579] The little bird sang its bitterly sweet song into the dark, and the dark, at last, listened. [596] Aldric closed his eyes and let the trembling reverie carry him into a fragile, hopeful sleep. [612]</story>
