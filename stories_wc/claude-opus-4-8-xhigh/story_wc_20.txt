<story>Mira called herself a balanced alchemist, which meant she weighed every wish before granting it. [15] She arrived at the rain-washed foundry the morning after the search party disbanded, when the last lanterns had drowned in the puddles. [37] They had hunted for a boy named Tomas for nine days, and on the tenth they folded their maps and went home. [59] Mira had not joined them, because she had been the one who lost him. [73] He had been her apprentice, eager and careless, and she had sent him into the smelting hall to fetch a zodiac coin she should have carried herself. [100] The coin was factually mystical: stamped with all twelve signs, it answered questions truthfully, but only about things already broken. [120] Tomas had asked it where he could prove himself, and the coin, obeying its narrow law, had shown him the foundry's deepest furnace. [143] He had gone down to explore its limits, and the floor, rusted thin, had given way. [159] Now Mira stood where the search ended, holding the recovered coin, rain threading through the broken roof in luminous silver lines. [180] She had come to understand the weight of names, for she had spoken his too lightly and too often, as if a name were a tool and not a tether. [210] An alchemist learns that every element wants its true name, and that calling something wrongly bends its fate. [228] She had called Tomas brave when she meant reckless, and the coin had heard the lie inside the praise. [247] She knelt by the cold forge and unwrapped a small kit of crucibles, fine brushes, and a vial of liquid gold. [268] The zodiac coin had cracked when it struck the furnace floor, split clean along the line between the Ram and the Fish. [290] Mira meant to mend it with kintsugi intentions, joining the halves with gold so the wound became the most honest part. [311] She would not hide the break; she would honor it, name it, and carry it forward. [327] As she worked, she balanced the gold against the crack itself, adding only as much as the loss required, no less and no more. [351] This was how a balanced alchemist grieved: by measuring sorrow so precisely that it could be set down without spilling. [371] When the seam set, the coin grew warm, and she understood she could not bring Tomas back, but she could redeem the name she had bent. [397] She held the mended coin to the rain and asked it the only question worth its truth: what was Tomas, truly? [418] The gold seam glowed, and the answer rose in her like heat: he was curious, and curiosity is not a flaw to be punished but a gift to be guided. [448] She had failed that guidance once; she would not fail the next apprentice the same way. [464] By exploring the limits of what the coin could mend, she had found the limits of her own forgiveness, and pushed past them. [487] The rain washed the soot from the foundry walls, revealing old maker's marks, the names of every smith who had ever balanced metal here. [511] Mira took out her smallest chisel and added a new line below them. [524] She carved his name slowly, feeling its full weight, each letter a tether to the boy she had loved and lost. [545] The work felt luminous and full of regret at once, the way gold looks when it fills a wound it can never erase. [568] She set the mended coin into a niche in the wall, where it would answer travelers honestly about their broken things. [589] Then she gathered her tools and walked out into the clearing storm, lighter by exactly the weight she had finally agreed to carry. [612]</story>
