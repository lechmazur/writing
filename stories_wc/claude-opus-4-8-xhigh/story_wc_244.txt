<story>Mira Tann, the elusive disaster-relief logistician, arrived at the disused railway station overgrown with ivy the way she did everything—sideways, unannounced, already half a rumor. [26] She came because of a story that was about to change. [37] In this country, everyone was bound by reciprocity, a law older than parliaments: accept a kindness and you owed one back, exactly weighed, or the debt curdled into something worse than hunger. [69] It made people lonely in a peculiar way, since affection itself became an invoice. [83] Mira had spent years moving water and blankets after floods, and she had noticed that the deepest disaster was always the same one: a man on a roof who could not bear to wave for help because waving would cost him. [124] So she had set herself an absurd logistics problem: to find the cure for loneliness, and to deliver it like sandbags. [145] The station was her depot, ivy threading through ticket windows, timetables eaten green. [158] People drifted in, drawn by the only thing that travels free of debt: a rumor. [173] Mira's method was elegant and faintly criminal—she fed improbable rumor chains into the towns and let them braid into action. [194] A whisper that the baker forgave a debt; a whisper that forgiveness was, this week, fashionable; a whisper that the lonely tailor had been seen laughing. [220] Rumors, unlike gifts, demanded nothing, so they slipped past the law and let people meet without invoices. [237] She was desperately jocular about all of it. [245] "The trick," she told the dozen strangers gathered under the ivy, "is to be so funny that nobody notices we're starting a riot of being kind." [271] They laughed, which was free, and the laughing felt like the polite anarchy she lived for: rules disobeyed gently, with apology and a grin. [295] From her coat she drew the fragment of moon rock, gray and unimportant-looking, which was the whole engine of her scheme. [316] Years ago she had bought it from a sailor with a more dangerous rumor, and she had built around it the only legend powerful enough to catalyze a country. [345] The legend said the rock had fallen from the moon, which had once owed the earth a great debt and could never repay it—proof that even the sky could carry an unbalanced ledger and keep shining. [382] If the moon could owe forever and still be loved, then perhaps people could too. [397] That was the cure she carried: not a medicine, but permission to be in debt and unashamed. [414] She passed the fragment hand to hand, and each person who held it was instructed to lie cheerfully: "Tell three towns the moon forgave its debt today." [441] The improbable chains spread that night through markets and mail routes, mutating, growing kinder with every mouth. [458] By morning, a widow had hugged a stranger without tallying it; a creditor had torn a ledger and called it a holiday. [480] The law of reciprocity did not break so much as forget its own grip, the way ivy forgets a wall it has finally swallowed. [504] This was the timeframe Mira waited for, the exact hour when the story changes and a rule becomes a habit becomes a memory. [527] But here was the joke she had not budgeted for. [537] When the fragment came back to her, returned hand to hand around the whole circuit, a child added one line of her own: "And the logistician is invited to stay." [567] Mira, who cured loneliness by remaining elusive, who delivered belonging and then fled before she had to accept any, felt the old invoice rise in her throat. [594] Someone offered her bread, and the law she had just dismantled no longer required her to repay it. [612] She could simply receive. [616] It terrified her more than any flood. [623] "All right," she said, desperately jocular to the very end, "but I warn you—I snore in three languages." [642] She set the moon rock on the ivy-choked windowsill, where it caught the light like something that had finally stopped fleeing its own debt. [666] And the disused station, for the first time in years, sounded like a place where someone meant to stay. [685]</story>
