<story>The frugal astronomer Tabni-Ishtar counted her oil by the drop, for night was expensive and stars were free. [18] She served in the reimagined palace of Babylon, a ziggurat of blue glaze whose terraces had been re-cut by exiled architects into observatories of polished bronze. [44] This was a season between regimes: the old king had died, and the new one had not yet learned to fear the heavens. [67] In that hush, the priests entrusted her with a single duty, to seal a covenant with the unknown before the next coronation. [89] She found her instrument in the treasury, a magic square tile of black diorite, its surface scored with numbers that summed alike across every row and column. [116] To anyone else it was decoration; to her it was a cipher. [128] For the tile held an encrypted devotion, a prayer that the last astronomer had hidden inside arithmetic so no usurper could burn it. [151] Tabni-Ishtar realized this only when she laid the tile beneath a lamp and saw the numbers cast shadows like constellations. [171] Each value, she understood, marked a star, and the magic constant traced the order in which they must be read. [191] Night after frugal night, she rationed her flame and copied the heavens through schematic representations, sketching the bronze tablets with charcoal she ground herself. [215] She drew the sky not as it looked but as it meant, every dot a coordinate, every line a vow. [235] The work was empirically ironic, for the more precisely she measured the cold and distant lights, the more the cold and distant lights seemed to lean toward her in warmth. [265] She mocked herself for it; an astronomer was meant to doubt, not adore. [278] Yet the tile insisted otherwise, summing always to the same total, devotion encrypted in a number that refused to change no matter how she turned it. [304] On the seventh night the palace held its breath in dusky harmony, lamps low, courtiers asleep, the river outside murmuring a single sustained note. [328] Tabni-Ishtar arranged her charcoal schematics across the bronze floor until they mirrored the tile exactly. [343] Then she began to uncloak the prayer, lifting the linen the dead astronomer had draped across the diorite to hide its meaning from the priests of the fallen king. [372] Beneath the cloth, faint silver letters glimmered between the numbers, visible only when starlight and lamplight crossed at the angle her diagrams predicted. [395] The message was simple, and it undid her frugal heart. [405] "I gave the sky my whole salary," it read, "and called the debt joy." [419] She laughed, because she had done the same, hoarding every coin save those she spent on lenses and oil. [438] The covenant, she saw now, was not a bargain struck in fear but a love letter written across centuries. [457] To seal it she needed only to answer. [465] So she pressed her thumb in lamp-soot and added a single dot to the tile, a new star in an empty cell. [487] The square still summed true; her devotion, encrypted, had joined the old. [499] At dawn the new king came to demand his omen, expecting threats of flood or famine to bind his subjects. [519] Instead Tabni-Ishtar showed him the tile and the matching sky, the schematics laid like a map between the world and the dark. [541] "The covenant is kept," she said, "not with terror but with attention." [553] The king, young and unsure, looked at the numbers that would not betray him and felt, perhaps for the first time, unafraid of what he could not see. [581] He let her keep her lamp and her thrift, and named her keeper of the unknown. [597] That night she spent freely, burning oil until morning, watching the stars she had finally learned to love return her gaze in silence. [620] The regimes would change again, and again, but the tile would sum the same, holding its quiet, encrypted devotion for whoever next dared to uncloak it. [646]</story>
