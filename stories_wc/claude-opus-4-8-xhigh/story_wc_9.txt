<story>After the birth, Mira had stopped sleeping, and so she had returned to her cards. [15] An onyx-ink cartomancer learns to read the futures of strangers, never her own, yet tonight the dark spreads of cards pointed inward. [37] They showed a midnight glade above the clouds, the place, they insisted, where lost time accumulates. [53] Mira wanted that, wanted to find where the first weeks of her daughter's life had gone, those hours that vanished before she could hold them. [78] So she climbed the stairs the cards drew for her, up through fog that tasted of milk and ash. [97] The glade waited at the top, silver grass swaying over a floor of cloud, beautiful enough to choke her. [116] Figures knelt among the grass, translucent, folding moments into the soil like penitents at prayer. [131] This was cloudbound repentance, she understood, the labor of those who had wasted their living hours and now sorted them, grain by grain, hoping to give them back. [159] Among them she saw her mother. [165] Her mother, dead three winters, was tracing a small hand onto a tablecloth spread across the clouds. [182] It was the family custom, the way they had always marked a child's growth: on every birthday someone laid out the cloth and traced each small hand in ink, so the years could be measured by widening fingers. [220] Mira knelt beside her, willingly perplexed, asking no questions, only watching the ghost's careful pen. [235] "I came up here for the time I owed you," her mother said, not looking up. [251] "All the birthdays I worked through, all the mornings I hurried past you, I am tracing them again, hoping to hand them down." [274] Beside the cloth sat a dolls' tea set, the same one Mira had owned at six. [290] She remembered the day a saucer cracked under her mother's distracted elbow, and how no one had ever spoken of it again. [312] The crack was still there, a thin black river across the porcelain, and Mira saw now that every wasted minute had drained through it. [336] "You don't have to repent for me," Mira whispered. [345] "I forgave the saucer years ago." [351] Her mother's hand trembled, and the ink blurred. [359] Mira understood then why the cards had sent her: not to recover her own lost hours, but to unfasten her mother from this endless penance. [384] She reached for the clasp at her mother's collar, the little brooch she had worn even in the coffin, and worked it loose. [407] The brooch held a folded square of tablecloth inside it, every traced hand from Mira's childhood pressed flat against her mother's heart. [429] She had carried all that lost time herself, hoarded it as guilt. [441] Mira pressed her own grown hand over the faded outlines, matching finger to finger. [455] "The time isn't lost," she said; "I'm holding it, and look how it fits." [469] The glade brightened, unbearably, and the kneeling penitents began to dissolve into the cloud like sugar into tea. [487] Her mother smiled, the way Mira had forgotten she could, and faded after them. [501] Only the dolls' tea set remained, the cracked saucer now whole, its black river healed. [516] Mira carried it down the cloud-stairs and woke in her chair as dawn touched the window. [532] Her daughter was crying in the next room, alive and small and immediate. [545] On the table sat that very tea set, real and dusty, the saucer's hairline crack still catching the dawn. [564] Mira lifted her daughter, found the old tablecloth in a drawer, and laid it across the table. [581] She dipped her pen in onyx ink and, for the first birthday, traced the impossibly small hand, four days old, onto the cloth. [604] Lost time, she finally knew, did not pool in any glade above the clouds; it gathered here, in the patient outline of a hand someone chose to hold. [632]</story>
