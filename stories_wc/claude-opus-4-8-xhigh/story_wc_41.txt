<story>They called him the junkyard prophet, though he prophesied nothing he could keep. [13] He was precognitively forgetful, blessed and cursed: he glimpsed tomorrow, then lost it before noon, the way tide forgets the shape it carved in sand. [38] His home was a fog sculpture garden at the edge of the salt flats, where the mist froze into figures each dawn and dissolved by dusk. [64] To remember anything, he wrote on the world with a piece of plain chalk, scrawling fragments on driftwood, on rusted fenders, on the white bones of the fog before it thinned. [95] The chalk was his only honest tool; ink lied, but chalk admitted it would vanish. [110] Among the wrecks he kept a wall of old videotapes left unwatched, their hush a kind of music, each cassette holding a voice he had loved and never pressed play to hear. [142] He feared that watching them would replace the echoes he clung to, and clinging to echoes was the only faith he practiced. [164] For he had set himself one task, the work of a lifetime he could barely hold from one tide to the next: to capture the sound of growing older. [193] Not a photograph, not a face, but the noise a person makes while becoming someone slower. [209] He believed the sound hid somewhere in the garden, and so each morning he tried to locate it. [227] His method was patient and strange. [233] He went down to the shore and gathered driftwood, then walked back arranging the pale branches as directional markers, arrows pointing inland toward wherever his vanished foresight had promised the sound would be. [266] He could not recall the promise, only the urgency it left, like a name on the tongue of the sea. [286] "Follow your own arrows," he told himself, chalking the words on a barrel so the noon-forgetting would not erase them. [306] One evening the fog hardened into the shape of a woman. [317] He knew her, or his hands did; they reached before his memory could catch up. [332] She was the voice on the topmost tape, the wife the salt had taken years ago, back when he still owned a future he could keep. [358] The fog-figure did not speak, but it tilted its head the way she had, listening. [373] He understood, suddenly, that the sound he hunted was not out in the wrecks at all. [389] It was the difference between her young laugh on the tape and the silence she had become. [406] Growing older was simply that gap, the long erosion between an echo and its quiet. [421] He hurried to his driftwood arrows and saw they all pointed back to the videotapes. [436] His forgotten foresight had aimed him home the whole time. [446] With the chalk he marked one cassette, the way fishermen mark a net, and at last he slid it into the machine. [468] The hush broke. [471] Her voice came through thin and bright, then aged across the tape's worn ribbon, warping lower, slower, until it folded into static. [493] There it was: the sound of growing older, captured not by him but by the simple cruelty of time on magnetic tape. [515] He wept, and the chalk dust on his fingers turned to paste. [527] By morning he would forget the discovery, as he forgot all things tomorrow had told him. [543] But the fog-woman lingered one breath longer than usual, and he arranged a final driftwood arrow pointing at the playing tape, so the next forgetting self might find the way. [573] The garden dissolved into white. [578] Somewhere in the mist a younger voice kept aging, over and over, clinging to its own echo against the pull of the patient, returning tide. [603]</story>
