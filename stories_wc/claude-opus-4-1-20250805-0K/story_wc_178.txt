[27] Mara had spent years deciphering the palace's architectural blueprints, discovering that each room's dimensions corresponded to celestial ratios—empirically ironic, given how the ancient builders had encoded their deepest beliefs in measurable stone. [60]

Between the fall of the old theocracy and the rise of the merchant princes, she alone tended the observatory, subsisting on barley bread and rainwater while others plundered the palace's golden fixtures. [91] Her frugality wasn't poverty but purpose; every saved coin bought her another month to complete her work. [108]

The chamber she entered now had waited three thousand years. [118] Its walls bore schematic representations of constellations unknown to modern astronomy, each star marked by a depression that once held a ceramic tile. [141] All were missing save one—a magic square tile embedded in the eastern wall, its surface etched with sixteen numbers whose sum equaled perfection from every angle. [169]

Mara traced the numbers with trembling fingers. [176] The tile wasn't merely mathematical; it was a key, a covenant waiting to be sealed. [191] Her calculations had revealed that the palace's entire structure formed a vast encryption system, where devotion to understanding became indistinguishable from the understanding itself. [216]

She pulled out her hand-drawn charts, overlaying transparent sheets marked with planetary orbits onto the wall's star patterns. [235] As Mars aligned with the tile's central number, the stone began to warm. [249] The empirically ironic truth was that the Babylonians had hidden their greatest discovery in plain sight—not gold or jewels, but a method for communicating across impossible distances. [277]

The tile loosened. [280] Behind it lay a shallow cavity containing a second tile, this one covered in cuneiform that seemed to shift in the filtered moonlight. [304] Mara recognized the script from her years studying erosion patterns on palace stones; time itself had become the encryption method. [326]

She pressed the second tile into her palm and felt knowledge uncloak itself in her mind—not words but pure mathematical harmony. [348] The ancient astronomers hadn't worshipped stars; they had discovered something watching from between them, something that communicated through numerical patterns scattered across centuries. [371]

The palace walls began to resonate with a frequency below hearing but felt in bone. [387] Each schematic representation on the walls lit with phosphorescent minerals embedded in the mortar, revealing the complete stellar map.
