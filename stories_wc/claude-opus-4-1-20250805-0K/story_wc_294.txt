[32] Marcus ran his fingers along the weathered bills pinned to the wall of what had once been the ringmaster's car—rupees, yuan, euros, pesos—each one a marker of territory his father had claimed along the highways before prison took him. [109] They wanted to convert the circus train into offices for their logistics company, TerraLoop, which promised to revolutionize supply chains by predicting theft patterns along trade routes. [136] The irony wasn't lost on Marcus. [142]

[184] So Marcus began his work slowly, methodically, with the perceptive calm he'd inherited from generations of men who understood that the best deterrent wasn't violence but suggestion. [212]

First, he rearranged the foreign bills into patterns that spelled warnings in a dozen languages, visible only when the morning sun hit them at precisely the right angle. [240] The TerraLoop team noticed but dismissed them as old circus decorations. [250] Marcus smiled at their blindness and moved to phase two. [260]

As autumn deepened and TerraLoop secured its first round of funding, Marcus began leaving items in strategic locations—a trucker's CB radio in the conference car, still crackling with ghost frequencies; his father's old highway maps with routes marked in red, showing how every path through these hills eventually circled back to this train. [316] He watched Chen study these artifacts during late nights, saw him beginning to understand the pattern. [332]

The winter brought investors, and with them, the kind of nervous energy that Marcus recognized from his father's descriptions of convoy guards. [383] The junior developers started leaving earlier each day. [391]
