[155] Today, however, his work was interrupted by a glint of brass caught in his net. [174] It was a tiny, intricate thing: a seafarer’s chronometer cog, perfectly preserved, its teeth still sharp and precise. [198] It was an artifact of absolute, measurable order, a stark contrast to the chaotic, decaying world he now inhabited. [222]

This cog, Elias realized, was a key, but to which lock? [237] He held it up, letting the faint green light filtering through the water above play across its machined surface. [258] It was a relic from before the city sank, from the great maritime culture that had built these stone channels. [281] He knew he had to trace its origin, to understand its narrative. [297] His method was his own: he would read the ruins through sedimentary layers, descending through time itself, stratum by stratum. [324] He secured his diving bell and began his ascent into the higher, older districts of the sunken metropolis. [347]

[378] But as he sank deeper, pushing through cold, dark waters, he found the older strata. [398] Here were the robust foundations of the seafaring empire, the mammoth stones fitted without mortar, the statues of stern navigators staring blindly into the abyss. [544] The cog in his palm was not a relic of decline, but a piece of a machine that was shattered in its prime. [571] He had to agonize over this contradiction, his mind a battlefield between the sanctioned past and the evidence he now clutched in his hand. [602]

The abrasive calm of his existence was ripped away, replaced by the furious peace of a scholar on the verge of truth. [625] He was rationally rebellious, not a mere contrarian; this proof demanded a new synthesis. [644] The plague had not ended a decadent civilization; it had finished off a wounded one. [698] The slow sinking was a consequence, not the cause. [712] The plague was merely the final infection in a corpse that was already dead. [730]
