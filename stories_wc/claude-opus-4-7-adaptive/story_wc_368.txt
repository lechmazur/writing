After receiving the donor heart, Ilsa climbed the thousand stone steps to the floating rock tea house, where the valley mist rose like slow breath from a sleeping animal. [29] She had come to find the watchful dye master, whose reputation wound through every market in the province. [47] Ilsa's question was quiet, but it had been quietly explosive inside her since the transplant: why did some people magnetize stories, drawing rumors and legends the way iron drew filings? [77] The donor, she had learned only in whispers, had been one of those people. [91] Now, something of him seemed to beat beneath her ribs like a second, luminous pulse. [106] The tea house hung on its island of stone above the valley, tethered by nothing her eyes could see. [125] Inside, copper kettles steamed and the dye master sat by a low window, his fingers stained the color of crushed plums. [146] He did not look up when she entered, though she felt him counting her steps. [161] "You want to know," he said, "why certain lives gather tales around them like moths at a lantern." [179] She nodded, unsurprised that he knew. [185] He lifted from a paper envelope a single blue morpho wing, and laid it on the dark table between them. [205] The wing shifted through sapphire, lapis, and a blade of cold silver as the mist outside thinned. [222] "Not all blue is blue," the dye master said. [231] "This is iridescent caution; the butterfly wears no pigment at all, only tiny roofs that split the light." [249] "A warning without poison, you see, and every predator believes it." [260] He gestured for her to sit. [266] "People who magnetize stories work the same trick." [274] "They do not possess more life than others; they only catch the light at sharper angles." [289] Ilsa's hand went to the long pink seam down her sternum. [300] "Then how does one learn the angles?" she asked. [309] "By tracking sun trails," he answered, as though it were obvious. [320] He led her to a courtyard where threads of dyed silk were pegged in long arcs across the stones. [339] "Each morning I follow where the sun falls and fades," he said, "and where it lingers longest, I steep my deepest colors." [361] "A story-bearer does the same with attention; they notice which moments the light chooses, and they stand there." [379] He handed her the morpho wing. [385] "Carry this until you understand." [390] For seven days Ilsa stayed in a sleeping-nook above the kettles. [401] She walked the floating courtyard at dawn, watched where gold first touched the railing, where shadow pooled last. [419] She began to see the valley as the dye master saw it: a loom of brightnesses, and certain knots where everything gathered. [441] Her new heart, she realized, had been trained in those knots. [452] Its donor had not been louder than anyone else; he had simply stood where the day chose to illuminate him, and had allowed the world to read meaning in the accident. [483] On the eighth morning she returned to the low window. [493] The dye master was waiting with two cups of pale green tea. [505] "Well?" he asked. [508] "Stories do not find people," she said. [514] "People find the hours where stories are already being told." [524] He inclined his head, plum-stained fingers wrapped around his cup. [534] "And the caution?" he prompted, nodding at the wing she still held. [546] Ilsa lifted the morpho into a bar of sun. [555] Blue burst out of it, brighter than any dye in his kettles, a warning and an invitation at once. [574] "That to be seen that brightly is to be mistaken," she said. [586] "The story is never exactly the life." [593] The dye master smiled for the first time. [601] Mist climbed the railings around them in slow, curious coils, and the tea house drifted a little in its unseen current. [622] Ilsa felt her borrowed heart settle, no longer restless, finally at the angle it had been seeking. [639] She would descend the stone steps by noon, carrying the wing wrapped in rice paper, and she would stand, from now on, wherever the sun insisted on pointing. [667] If stories came, they would come for the light, not for her. [679] That, she thought, was enough to carry down the mountain. [689]
