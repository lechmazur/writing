<story>The palace hung from the cavern's throat, anchored to giant stalactites by chains the engineers swore would never rust. [19] Prince Aurel arrived bedraggled, his coat stitched with mud from seven kingdoms and one forgotten marsh. [35] He had not slept through a full night since the Council replaced the last nightingale with a clockwork one. [54] In his satchel, wrapped in linen that had once been a christening gown, lay the egg. [70] It was pale as bone, speckled with a blue that no living bird still wore. [85] The librarians called it impossible; the alchemists called it a hoax; his mother, before the gears took her voice, had called it hope. [108] Aurel crossed the suspended bridge with his hand on the rope, feeling the whole palace sway in that slow quaking reverie the cavern granted to anything daring enough to hang above an abyss. [141] Wind moved between the stalactites like breath through a flute. [151] He had come to find the Artificer, who was said to assemble miracles from pieces other people threw away. [170] The Artificer's workshop occupied the palace's lowest chamber, where copper pipes drooped like vines and every surface was scored with equations. [191] She was small, older than rumor suggested, and her left eye had been replaced with a lens of smoked quartz. [211] "You brought it," she said, before he'd spoken. [219] "I brought what remains." [223] He set the egg onto her workbench, between a dismantled lark and a jar of clockwork hearts that still ticked faintly out of sync. [247] "It is the last," he said. [253] "The last of the last." [258] The Artificer studied it with a patience that felt geologic. [268] "You understand," she murmured, "that nothing about this world was built to hold a living song anymore." [285] "That is why I came." [290] For three days and three nights she worked, hypothesizing about everything: temperature, sympathy, the resonance of shell against steel, the moral weight of warmth. [314] Aurel fetched her tools and held her lenses and listened while she talked. [327] She spoke of flawed genesis, of how the first machines had been dreamed up to imitate birds and had ended up replacing them; how each copy had been a little colder, a little more exact, until exactness became the only music the world remembered. [371] "We didn't mean to kill beauty," she said. [379] "We only meant to keep it." [385] The egg warmed under her hands, though neither of them could say why. [398] On the fourth morning, through the discovery that the stalactite above the chamber rang at the egg's own pitch, she understood what to do. [422] She did not build a machine around the egg; she unbuilt one. [434] She took apart the clockwork nightingale Aurel had carried in his coat pocket for a year, separating its hinges from its hollow throat, and laid the pieces in a ring around the shell. [467] "A nest of surrender," she called it. [474] "Everything mechanical must agree to be less, so that something living can be more." [488] Aurel knelt beside the bench and breathed slowly, as his mother had taught him before her lungs turned to bellows. [508] The palace swayed on its chains. [514] The stalactites hummed. [517] Somewhere in the shell, something answered. [523] The crack, when it came, was small enough to miss and loud enough to remember. [538] A beak emerged, then a damp, crumpled wing, then an eye that had not existed in the waking world for two hundred years. [561] The chick was bitterly sweet to look at: beautiful beyond argument, yet shivering in a cavern that had forgotten how to feed it. [584] Aurel wept without performance. [588] "It will need a sky," the Artificer said. [596] "It will need insects, and seasons, and other birds to teach it how to be one." [612] "Then we will make those," Aurel said, and for the first time his voice did not sound bedraggled. [630] "Not from gears." [633] "From permission." [635] He carried the chick back across the bridge at dawn, cupped against his chest, while the Artificer stood in the doorway of her unbuilt workshop and watched the palace sway. [665] His motivation had always been simple, though he had lacked words for it until now: to reawaken beauty in a mechanical world, not by winding it tighter, but by letting some part of it, at last, be allowed to breathe. [705] Behind him, the clockwork nightingale lay quiet among its own components, finally outperformed by a thing that could die. [724] Ahead, the morning cavern brightened, and a single, unrepeatable note rose from his palms. [738]</story>
