<story>The cobalt terrace darkened at dusk, its tiles bruising purple where sea wind scraped them. [15] Orrin set the broken watch hand on the balustrade, a thin brass needle that had once belonged to his grandfather, and tried to remember what hour it had stopped. [44] He could not. [47] That was the trouble with being a black market genealogist: after twenty years of manufacturing false memories for clients who paid in unmarked notes, his own recollections had begun to sift out like flour through a torn sack. [85] Clients came to him wanting ancestors they did not have. [95] A duchess in exile. [99] A shipwright uncle. [102] A grandmother who had hidden partisans in her cellar. [111] Orrin gave them paperwork, photographs aged in tobacco tea, and, crucially, the small private memories that made the lineage feel lived. [132] He called his specialty the sieved hush, that careful quieting of contradiction, the way a fabricated past settles into a person only when the real one has been strained away. [162] He had been good at it. [168] He was guilt-ridden now. [172] The letter that had brought him to the terrace lay under the watch hand, held against the wind. [190] It was from a woman named Inez, whose mother he had reinvented eleven years ago. [205] The mother had died last spring believing she was the daughter of a lighthouse keeper on this very coast. [224] Inez wrote that she had found carvings in the driftwood along the shore below, carvings her mother had spoken of in her final weeks. [248] Were they real, Inez asked, or had Orrin put them there, too? [260] He did not know. [264] That was the unbearable part. [269] Somewhere in the years of invention he had lost the boundary between what he had placed and what had always been. [290] He descended the stone stairs. [295] The shore was forgotten the way such places are forgotten: not absent from maps, only absent from attention. [313] Bleached logs lay tangled like the bones of a slow animal. [324] Under the weight of unspoken words, all the apologies he had never sent to clients who had believed him, he knelt and began sifting through the driftwood carvings along the forgotten shore. [356] Birds, mostly. [358] A boat. [360] A woman's face repeated in four logs, each older than the last. [372] He turned each piece, searching for a hand that would betray itself, a mark of the carvers he sometimes hired in the port to age his fabrications. [399] None came. [401] The wood was salt-rotted beyond his craft. [408] These had been made by someone who stood here long before Orrin's ledger opened. [422] He sat back on his heels. [428] So the lighthouse keeper had been real, or someone had. [438] The mother he had rewritten had, perhaps by accident, been handed back a fragment of her own true blood. [457] His fraud had, improbably, told the truth. [464] The tempered regret of it moved through him slowly, as cold water moves through cloth. [479] He had come here to discover the weight of a withheld truth, to measure what he owed Inez, and the truth was heavier than he had budgeted for. [507] It was not that he had lied to her mother. [517] It was that he had never known he might, on any given day, be lying over the top of something real, burying a genuine ancestry under manufactured grief. [545] He climbed back to the cobalt terrace. [552] The dusk had deepened to near-ink. [558] He placed the brass watch hand into an envelope with Inez's address and a single sheet of paper on which he had written, in his neatest hand, every false detail he had given her mother, and beside each, the word unknown. [599] It was not absolution. [603] It was a map of where to search. [611] To quell the old habit of smoothing things over, he did not soften the letter. [626] He did not call it apology. [632] He sealed it, set it in the outgoing tray of the small inn behind him, and stood a long moment with his palms on the cold railing. [659] Below, the tide was returning to the carvings, lifting them, knocking one against another with a sound like a clock that had finally found its hour. [685] Orrin listened. [687] He did not manufacture anything from it. [694] He let the hush, for once, remain unsieved, and waited for whatever memory might arrive that he had not made himself. [715]</story>
