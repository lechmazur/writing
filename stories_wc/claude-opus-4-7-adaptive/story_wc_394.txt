<story>Mira Kowalski was the first person to glide a cart across the acid sea, and she had the burns on her boot soles to prove it. [26] Her workshop, a barge of gears and cogs lashed to copper pontoons, drifted in slow circles above the green murk. [46] Steam hissed from valves; a hunter's arrow tip, ancient and obsidian, dangled from a chain over her workbench like a pendulum she refused to interpret. [71] She had pried it from the ribs of an extinct thing she'd dredged up while testing her glider's first prototype. [91] Mira was steadily curious in the way some people are steadily devout, and she answered every question the world posed with three more of her own. [117] The latest question was the loudest: why did the arrow tip hum? [129] It hummed only when she ran her newest invention, a brass kaleidoscope she'd geared to the workshop's mainspring. [147] Through the images in a kaleidoscope, she'd discovered, the arrow's hum resolved into syllables, and the syllables into a yearning algorithm she could almost transliterate onto paper. [174] Almost. [175] The algorithm wanted something. [179] It looped through prime numbers, paused on a cluster of glyphs that resembled hands cupped around an ear, and started over. [200] Mira called it yearning because no other word fit; the math had a posture, leaning forward, listening for an answer that had not yet arrived. [225] She laughed at herself for anthropomorphizing a rock, then laughed harder when the rock hummed back in apparent agreement. [244] Her assistant, a pelican-shaped automaton named Bib, ratcheted over with a fresh spool of foil tape. [260] "You realize," Bib clicked, "that you are trying to ring a bell no one hears yet." [276] "That," Mira said, threading the tape, "is the only kind of bell worth ringing." [290] Throughout the evolution of her glider, from the splintering raft she'd ridden off the cliff to this brass cathedral of pulleys, that conviction had never wavered. [316] Pioneers, she believed, were just people stubborn enough to keep speaking before the language was invented. [332] She set the kaleidoscope to its slowest rotation and pressed her eye to the cup. [347] Hexagons of green light folded around the arrow tip's reflection, and the humming sharpened into a chord. [364] Bib fed the foil tape under a stylus, and Mira began to transliterate the chord into characters of her own invention: stacked spirals for the long tones, dashes for silences, and a small wide-eyed circle whenever the algorithm "leaned." [403] By the third hour, the tape was a forest of spirals and circles. [416] By the fifth, she understood. [421] The arrow tip was not a weapon; it was a transmitter, and somewhere in deep history a hunter had loosed it not at prey but at the future, hoping a stranger millennia downstream would catch it and listen. [459] The yearning algorithm was a greeting written before greetings existed, and it had been ringing for ten thousand years waiting for an ear shaped to hear it. [486] Mira sat back on her gear-strewn floor and laughed until the workshop swayed. [499] The acid sea hissed below, indifferent and beautiful. [507] Some predecessor genius, painted in ochre, eating mammoth, had built a bell out of stone and patience and aimed it at her. [529] It was the most irreverent, awestruck thing she had ever encountered: a prayer disguised as a projectile, indifferent to whether the listener would arrive in fur or in brass. [558] She would answer. [561] She began assembling a new arrow, this one tipped with a coil of her own glyphs, calibrated to vibrate at the same yearning frequency. [585] Bib watched, head cocked. [589] "Who will catch it?" the pelican asked. [596] "Someone," Mira said, lifting the cart glider's tarp, "who hasn't been invented yet." [609] She climbed aboard, aimed her arrow at the slow horizon of acid and time, and let the bell fly. [628]</story>
