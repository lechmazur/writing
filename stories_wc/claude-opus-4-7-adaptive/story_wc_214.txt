<story>Mira swept the observation deck with a broom older than the station itself. [13] She called herself a cosmic janitor, though no one had visited to confirm the title in decades. [30] The shooting star tracking station perched on a cliff of black basalt, its brass instruments still faithfully recording every streak of light. [52] Through the hush of an overlooked era, when satellites had replaced human watchers and humans had replaced wonder with schedules, she remained. [74] Her uniform was patched at the elbows; her eyes held the primal elegance of someone who had learned to move slowly on purpose. [97] Mira was spiritually curious, which the administrators had once considered a liability. [109] But after the budget cuts and the transfers, after the great forgetting that swallowed small outposts whole, curiosity became her only companion. [131] Each night she climbed the tower and lit the signal lamp behind a panel of yellow lantern glass, not because anyone would see it, but because the ritual steadied her hands. [162] The amber light turned the sky the color of old honey and cast her shadow long across the meteor maps. [182] She had come here young, hoping to find strength in vulnerability, to learn whether a woman could stand alone under an indifferent sky and not shrink. [208] Forty years had given their answer in slow syllables. [217] She studied the heavens by tracing the path of falling stars with a worn graphite pencil, plotting angles the machines already knew. [239] The redundancy did not bother her. [245] Numbers were a prayer and a pencil was a tongue. [255] One November a child wandered up the access road, wrapped in a coat too large for him, holding a crumpled page torn from a library book. [281] His mother had died in summer, he said, and someone had promised that certain stars carried the dead. [299] He wanted to know which one was hers. [307] Mira, who had spent decades avoiding the eyes of strangers, found she could empathize with his small insistence, because grief had once sent her up this same road. [335] She let him in. [339] They sat behind the amber panel while the Leonids began their annual descent. [352] Mira taught the boy to follow each streak with his finger, to honor its arc before naming it. [370] He chose a pale green one above the northern ridge. [380] She wrote the coordinates into the ledger as if filing an address for heaven. [394] When he left before dawn, he hugged her with the weightless force children use, and something long dormant inside her softened without breaking. [417] She understood then what she had always called flourishing stillness, and why she had stayed. [432] It was not absence but attendance. [438] It was the quiet cultivation that happens when a person consents to be small beneath something immense. [455] The station had not been a place of exile; it had been a garden. [469] In the morning Mira resumed her sweeping. [476] Dust lifted and settled, as dust will. [483] The brass instruments ticked their ancient counts. [490] She dusted the telescope and the shelf of unread manuals, and she polished the amber panel until it caught the early sun like a held breath. [516] She thought of the boy's mother, of her own mother, of all the unnamed women who had waited at unfashionable outposts keeping small fires alive. [541] The cosmos did not require spectators, but she suspected it welcomed them anyway. [554] That night she opened the log to a fresh page and wrote a single line before the stars arrived. [573] Observer present. [575] The sky obliged, as it always had, with its bright and patient descent. [588] She traced each one, pencil moving like a slow river. [598] Somewhere a pale green meteor burned its small mercy across the dark, and Mira, who had found at last what she came for, simply watched. [623]</story>
