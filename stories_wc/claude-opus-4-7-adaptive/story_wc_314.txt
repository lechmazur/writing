<story>Mira pressed her palm against the waterfall, and its kaleidoscopic colors parted like oil on water. [16] Behind the cascade lay the orchard she had sought for seven years. [28] The auburn-haired alchemist stepped through, her boots meeting moss that smelled of apples and old rain. [44] Every apprentice in the Collegium knew the legend, but only she had believed it enough to follow. [61] The trees here grew in slow spirals, their bark silvered, their fruit bright as molten copper. [77] An ancient freshness filled the air, as though the morning itself had been preserved under glass for a thousand years. [97] She had come for one thing: the frozen dew crystal said to form only on the southernmost bough, only on the dawn before the orchard's final transformation. [124] For the grove was dying, or rather, becoming something else; the Collegium's astronomers had measured it in the drift of stars. [145] Tonight the place would fold inward and vanish, taking its secrets with it. [158] Mira was not sentimental, or so she told her colleagues. [168] Logic was her religion; the scale and the flame were her sacraments. [180] Yet secretly she kept her mother's thimble in her coat pocket, and a pressed violet between the pages of her ledger, and she had wept once, quietly, at the sight of a heron taking off from a pond. [218] She did not admit such things aloud. [225] An alchemist who felt too much was an alchemist who measured poorly. [237] Still, she had come here partly for the crystal and partly for something she could not name. [254] She found the southernmost tree and, cradled in the fork of two branches, the dew crystal itself: a teardrop of frozen water holding a tiny, trembling ember at its heart. [284] Fire and ice, bound in a single jewel. [292] Her hands shook as she reached, which surprised her. [301] Then, before she could close her fingers, a flock of starlings burst from the canopy. [316] They wheeled above the orchard in long, breathing shapes, and Mira, trained to see patterns in everything, froze. [334] Through the patterns in the flock she read an answer she had been circling all her life. [351] The birds wrote equations no chalkboard could hold: loops that became petals, petals that became waves, waves that resolved into the exact proportion by which a seed decides to open. [381] It was mathematics, yes, but it was also music, also grief, also joy. [394] She had spent a decade trying to harmonize logic and intuition, believing they were opposed, two warring counselors inside one skull. [415] Now the starlings showed her that they were only one mind, seen from two angles. [430] The crystal in her hand grew warm. [437] She understood, then, why the orchard had to go. [446] It was not dying; it was teaching its final lesson, and a lesson once learned does not need its classroom. [466] The flame inside the dew crystal flared, softly, and she knew she had a choice. [481] She could carry the crystal back to the Collegium, present it under glass, win the Argent Chair, write the monograph that would bear her name into the next century. [510] Or she could let the lesson travel by the only road a lesson travels: through the one who learned it. [530] Mira, the secretly sentimental alchemist, pressed the crystal to her sternum. [541] It sank into her like a held breath finally released. [551] Warmth bloomed outward, a small sun under her ribs, and the starlings wheeled once more before settling into the copper-leaved branches. [572] The touch of wonder, she realized, was not an interruption of reason; it was reason remembering where it came from. [592] She stood in the orchard until the light began to change. [603] The trees lowered their silvered leaves, the moss exhaled, and the air thickened with the sweetness of something ending well. [623] Before the final transformation took the grove, Mira walked back through the waterfall, stepping from color into ordinary morning. [642] Behind her the orchard folded itself into a single point of light and was gone. [657] She did not look back, because she was carrying it now. [668] At the Collegium gates she paused and laughed, for the first time in years, at nothing at all. [686] Her ledger, when she opened it that night, held a new entry in her steady hand: the patterns of starlings, the warmth of a vanished orchard, the flame that asks no fuel. [718] Beneath it, smaller, she wrote: I was wrong to separate them. [729] Then she closed the book on the pressed violet and went out to teach her students how to see. [748]</story>
