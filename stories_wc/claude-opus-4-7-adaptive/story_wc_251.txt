Mr. Halloran coached after-school chess for eleven years, and in that time he learned to hear the unspoken words children carried like loose change in their pockets. [27] He listened past the timer's tick to the small panic of a queen left undefended, the held breath that meant "I am about to lose something I love." [55] That sensitivity made him a strange kind of coach: he taught not openings but second thoughts, the tiny pause before a hand commits. [78] His dream, whispered only to the cobalt library in mist where he read on Saturdays, was to plant forests of second thoughts in every hurrying town. [104] The town of Brinsmere did not hurry exactly, but it obeyed, which Halloran thought worse. [119] A new ordinance forbade loitering, then forbade leaflets, then forbade gatherings of more than six, and each time the shopkeepers simply nodded and unlocked their doors the next morning. [148] Halloran decided on a humble rebellion. [154] He would not shout; he would not march; he would organize synchronized window displays across town. [170] He spoke to the baker, the tailor, the locksmith, the widow who sold postcards, all of them old chess parents whose children he had coaxed out of reckless sacrifices. [199] Each window, at eight o'clock on Thursday, would show the same scene: a single chess piece, a pawn, holding its square. [220] Beneath it, a card: THINK AGAIN. [226] It was too small to be banned, too polite to be feared, and that was the point. [243] To rehearse the alignment, Halloran met his conspirators the night before in what the children called the magnetic grotto under starlight, a derelict observatory hollow in the hillside where compasses spun and the sky felt close. [279] They came with lanterns and their small fears. [287] The tailor's hands trembled; the baker kept glancing at the road. [299] Halloran heard what they did not say - that they were afraid of losing custom, of losing sons to the garrison, of losing the shape of their quiet lives. [327] He did not argue with their silence. [334] Instead he opened a cloth and laid a single wooden pawn in each palm, letting them cradle it, feel how light a beginning could be. [359] "Thursday at eight," he said. [364] "Just a pawn in the window." [370] Thursday came wrapped in the low river fog. [378] At a quarter to eight the town slipped into the hush where past meets prophecy - that amplified quiet of streets emptied for supper, when the old bells have died and the new hour has not yet rung. [415] Halloran walked the square carrying his pocket watch like a relic. [426] Eight. [427] The baker's window: a pawn. [432] The tailor's window: a pawn, stitched from black felt, standing in a ring of pins. [447] The locksmith: a pawn behind a small open padlock. [456] The postcard widow had painted one herself, a fat blue pawn on a card that said THINK AGAIN in her careful, spidery hand. [479] Window after window, the same quiet argument. [486] A constable paused at the corner, reading, puzzled. [494] He could not arrest a chess piece. [501] He moved on. [504] Children pressed their noses to the glass and asked their parents what a pawn was doing there. [521] The parents, who had signed petitions they didn't read and nodded at rules they didn't choose, found their mouths suddenly full of answers. [544] A pawn, they said, goes forward one square at a time. [555] A pawn can, if it reaches the end, become anything. [565] A pawn alone is small, but a row of them is a line that cannot be crossed without cost. [583] Halloran heard the unspoken words beneath those answers - relief, and shame, and a thin green hope. [599] He walked home through the mist toward the cobalt library, where the lamp burned for him as it always did, and thought: a forest begins like this, in the pause between moves. [631] One square. [633] Then another. [635]
