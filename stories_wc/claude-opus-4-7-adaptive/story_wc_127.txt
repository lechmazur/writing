Yune sold feelings she did not feel. [7] In the amber grotto at dusk, where the resin walls held the last orange light like honey trapping a wasp, she set out her wares on a velvet cloth. [36] Bottled grief, decanted longing, a small vial of borrowed forgiveness; each one a counterfeit she had blended herself, professionally thorough in the way only a former chemist could be. [65] Her customers wandered in through the cave mouth: merchants who needed to weep convincingly at funerals, lovers who wanted the trembling lip without the ruinous risk. [91] Yune liked them with affectionate detachment, the way a beekeeper might love bees: aware of the sting, charmed by the dance, unwilling to be stung again. [117]

She had built her trade on half truths. [125] A vial labeled "Devotion" actually carried the chemistry of fixation; "Courage" was little more than rehearsed indifference; her "Empathy" was a clever mimic that smiled at the right intervals. [154] They worked, mostly. [157] They worked because nobody really wanted the whole feeling, only its silhouette. [169]

On the dusk she remembered later as the dusk that mattered, a stranger entered carrying a thin wooden case. [188] He opened it on her cloth without asking, and inside lay a single fragrance chemist's blotter petal, a slip of white paper cut in the shape of a rose petal, faintly stained at the tip. [223] "Tell me what is on it," he said. [231]

Yune lifted the petal between two fingers. [238] Her old training returned: she rubbed the paper lightly against her sleeve and brought it close to the fine hairs of her wrist, sensing through static electricity the way the molecules tugged or refused to tug. [274] Volatile esters lifted first, then a slower base of something animal and warm. [287] Beneath these, almost shy, sat a note she could not name. [298]

"Bergamot," she began, professionally thorough out of habit, "civet, iris root, and..." [310] She faltered. [312] "And something true," the stranger finished. [318] He watched her with patient eyes. [324] "I am a perfumer," he said, "and I have spent ten years trying to reproduce the smell of my mother forgiving me." [346] "I have the molecules," he said. [352] "I do not have the feeling." [358]

He laid coins on her cloth, more than she charged for any vial. [371] "Sell me real empathy," he said. "Not the half truth. The whole one." [384]

Yune almost laughed. [387] She did not own the whole one. [394] She had survived for years on the conviction that wholeness was a marketing claim, that insight could substitute for empathy if one was clever enough about the phrasing. [422] But the petal was on her tongue now, that nameless trace, and it had begun to subsume the careful inventory of her shop. [445] Vial by vial her counterfeits dimmed, as though embarrassed in the presence of an honest molecule. [461] She thought of the merchants and lovers, of the funerals where her grief had stood in for theirs. [479] Her motivation, all along, had been to fuse insight with empathy without paying empathy's price. [494] The petal informed her, gently, that there was no such fusion at a discount. [508]

She closed the case and pushed his coins back. [517] "I cannot sell what I do not have," she said, "but I can make it with you, if you are willing to wait inside the work." [543]

They worked through the deepening dusk. [549] The grotto's amber walls cooled from honey to bronze to a slow cathedral red, and the petal lay between them like a small white compass. [574] He told her about his mother. [580] She listened, not as a chemist this time but as a woman with two free hands. [596] She let his sentences settle on her sleeve and rubbed them, and what lifted toward her wrist this time was no longer a molecule but a recognition. [623]

As the transformation completes, she would think later, you do not become someone new; you simply stop subtracting yourself from the room. [645] When dawn was still hours away and the perfumer's eyes were wet, Yune realized she had given him something genuine and had, accidentally, kept a copy. [671]

Outside the grotto she could hear crickets tuning their small, persistent instruments. [683] She pocketed the blotter petal. [688] It was the only honest thing she had ever owned, and she found, with affectionate detachment toward her old self, that she did not intend to sell it. [716]
