<story>After luck became measurable, the Crown hired auditors to weigh every fortune in bushels and thimbles. [16] They came eventually to Ottilie's garden maze, which the ledgers had flagged as suspiciously lucky for three hundred years. [35] Ottilie was a withered vine dresser by then, stooped and brown as a bean pod, her hands permanently curled around shears that were no longer there. [61] She met the auditor at the maze's mouth, where hawthorn bloomed and withered on the same branch, as it had always done under her tending. [86] "Your hedges," the auditor said, consulting his abacus, "have yielded twelve percent more windfalls than chance allows." [103] Ottilie smiled without showing teeth. [108] From her apron she produced a collection of marbles, cracked and clouded, each one wound through with a hairline seam of gold. [130] "I mend them," she said. "When a guest drops one, I glue the halves and keep them. Follow me." [149] The maze opened in patient spirals. [155] At each junction stood a small pedestal, and on each pedestal perched a single repaired marble, catching the light at angles only Ottilie's generations of pruning could have arranged. [184] "Each marble marks a visitor who turned back," she said. "A lost child found. A stubborn suitor who chose kindness. Small victories. I commemorate them elaborately, because gardens remember what gardeners celebrate." [216] The auditor frowned into his ledger. [222] "Superstition inflates your numbers," he said. "The maze does not actually bend its paths. Luck is a measurable quantity. It obeys arithmetic, not affection." [246] Ottilie, wisely pragmatic after eighty years among thorns, did not argue. [257] She bent instead to polish the nearest marble with her sleeve until its gold seam glowed like a struck match. [277] "Then walk out," she said. "Measure what you like on the way." [289] The auditor strode forward, confident, counting paces. [296] At the first pedestal his left shoe found a root that had not been there at his approach; he caught himself against the yew and felt, for the first time in a long career, foolish. [331] At the second pedestal, a bee escorted him sideways. [340] At the third, he smelled his grandmother's kitchen and wept without knowing why. [353] The maze, blooming and withering around him in the same breath, did not bend its paths; it only remembered, aloud, every small victory it had been told to keep. [382] When he emerged he had been inside four hours, though his watch insisted upon forty minutes. [398] Ottilie sat on the bench by the gate, cracking the shell of a walnut with a stone. [415] "You cheat," he said, but gently. [421] "I mend," she corrected. "There is a difference between a lie told whole and a lie told in halves and glued. The first deceives. The second asks permission from what was broken." [453] This was her mended deception: the marbles were not talismans and never had been. [467] They were reminders, propped in the maze so that she, walking her rounds at dawn, would pause at each and recall whose small kindness it represented. [493] Pausing, she had pruned; pruning, she had shaped light and scent and shelter around those memories until the maze itself had learned the shape of gratitude. [519] The windfalls were real. The arithmetic simply did not know where to put them. [533] The auditor sat beside her. [538] After a while he asked for a marble to mend. [548] Ottilie handed him one cracked clean through, and a vial of slow gold. [561] She wanted to prove, before her hands finished their curl into root, that gardens know their gardeners, and choose them back. [582] The auditor held the two halves together and, for the first time, noticed that the seam between them looked less like a repair than like a vein. [609] Somewhere deeper in the hedges, a new windfall dropped, and Ottilie, content, did not bother to count it. [627]</story>
