<story>During the fallow period, when the fields lay bare and the villagers shuttered themselves against the wind, Idris returned to the chapel that had haunted her maps for years. [29] She was a claustrophobic explorer, which sounded like a contradiction, and was. [41] Every cellar she descended cost her something, but she descended anyway, prudently adventurous, measuring each breath the way she measured each wall. [63] The chapel stood at a shadowed nexus under starlight, where three old roads forgot to meet and a hill rose only enough to keep its secret. [89] Her grandfather had drawn the plans once; he died before the builders laid the foundation, and the chapel went up wrong. [110] Wrong in ways no surveyor could catalog: corridors that lengthened at dusk, a nave that felt larger than the shell containing it, pews that seemed to shift if you counted them twice. [142] Idris carried her grandfather's blueprint drafting compass point in a velvet pouch, its needle worn to a splinter from decades of pressing paper. [165] He had hidden something inside the chapel's design, a vital key lost to time, and she meant to uncover it before the building remade itself past reading. [192] She entered with a lantern dimmed low. [199] The doors shut behind her, not cruelly, but firmly, the way a patient teacher closes a book. [216] Her chest tightened; she counted four corners, then five, then four again. [228] The walls were in spatial mutiny against their blueprint, refusing the geometry her grandfather had tried to impose. [246] She understood, then, that he had not failed; he had designed a room that would choose its own shape, and had trusted her to negotiate with it. [273] Idris knelt at what she hoped was center and pressed the compass point into the stone. [289] A thin circle scored the flagstone; dust rose in a pale ring. [301] From the loft above, the organ began to breathe. [310] No one stood at the keys. [316] The chapel was speaking through the reverberation of a lonely church organ, each chord a nudge, a correction, a confession. [336] Low notes pulled the north wall closer; high notes eased the ceiling up; dissonance widened the doorway she had forgotten existed. [357] Idris listened like a map being redrawn. [364] She followed the shifting geometry, compass point in hand, scratching small reference marks so she could find her way back to herself. [386] Her claustrophobia pressed behind her eyes, but the music gave the walls room to breathe, and in breathing they gave her room too. [409] At the far end of a corridor that had not existed an hour before, a single brass key hung from a nail. [431] It was engraved with her grandfather's initials and a date from the year she was born. [447] The sight transfixed her; she stood still long enough for the organ to finish its sentence. [463] He had left the chapel free to rearrange itself precisely so that no thief, no surveyor, no careful man could ever reach this nail. [487] Only someone willing to listen, to yield, to trust a mutinous room, could come this far. [503] She took the key, and with it a small folded note: Forgive me for the walls; I could not build you a world, only a door. [529] Idris laughed, wetly, and the organ answered in a minor chord. [540] Outside, the stars had wheeled a quarter turn. [548] She stepped into the cold with the key pressed to her sternum, a woman who had bargained with architecture and won a small wistful rebellion against the tyranny of straight lines. [579] The chapel behind her settled like a sleeping animal. [588] She did not know yet which lock the key would open. [599] She knew only that her grandfather had trusted the world to be stranger than its blueprints, and that, for once, the world had agreed. [623]</story>
