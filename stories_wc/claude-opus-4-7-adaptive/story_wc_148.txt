<story>My father was the boy who cried wolf, and I am what his last laugh left behind. [17] They call me Hensel, though the keepers here rarely call me anything at all. [31] The lighthouse prison hunches on a jagged reef, a copper-capped spindle where the tide chews itself hoarse. [48] Evenings settle in copperlight repose, the lamps warming old stone into something almost tender. [62] I was sent here because nobody believed me, the same curse my father wore like a second skin. [80] When I told the village a man with iron teeth was walking the pasture, they laughed at the cry-wolf boy's son. [101] Then four shepherds vanished, and because the truth had been spoken first by me, they decided I must have carried it there. [123] My only companion in this tower is Brother Amiel, a defrocked priest who smuggled one relic with him: the lattice from his confessional, a wooden grille worn smooth by seventy years of whispered sins. [157] He props it against the wall of his cell and speaks to it as if listening for absolution rather than giving it. [179] Amiel insists that silence has a body, that one can go chasing hush the way fishermen chase herring, and that a certain species of quiet sustains impossibilities. [206] I did not believe him until the night I saw the dead walk the gallery. [221] Four shapes, the shepherds perhaps, drifted between lamp and wall and did not cast shadows. [236] They hover at the stairwell each midnight, before they find the bodies somewhere below on the reef, as though rehearsing an ending the world has not yet reported. [264] Amiel said my father's sin was not lying but leaving a labyrinth in the village's trust with no thread to follow. [285] My task, he told me, was to thread a labyrinth with my own story, to walk back through the false cries and lay a true one in each footprint. [314] He showed me how to begin: not through ledgers or prayers, but through the scuffs on a narrow door left ajar for decades at the base of the tower. [343] Each scuff was a confession, he said, left by every prisoner who had knelt there to whisper goodbye to land they would never touch again. [368] I read them the way a blind man reads rain, my fingertips moving like a pilgrim's through the gouged wood. [388] Behind the door I found my father's name scratched small, a date older than my birth. [404] He had been here once, before the village that punished him for lying; he had learned, in this same tower, how to turn terror into a tale no one would buy twice. [436] That, Amiel murmured through the lattice, is how an unspoken thing keeps spreading its roots. [451] On my twenty-first night, the apparitions lingered longer, and I recognized one: a shepherd I had played stones with as a boy. [473] He pointed downward, as the dead sometimes must. [481] I climbed past Amiel's cell with a lantern and opened the narrow door the rest of the way, and the sea beneath the reef gave up its secret at dawn. [511] Four bodies lay tangled in kelp below, and a fifth, newer, wearing the coat of the magistrate who had sent me here. [533] I sat down at the confessional lattice and told Amiel everything, each word a thread laid behind me in the labyrinth my father had begun. [558] When the keepers came to count us, they would find the bodies themselves, and my story would be the only map back. [580] Amiel blessed me in copperlight, though he had no right left to bless. [593] The cry-wolf boy's son had finally cried true, and this time, quietly, the wolf answered back. [609]</story>
