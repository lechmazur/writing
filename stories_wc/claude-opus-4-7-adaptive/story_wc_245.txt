Iola was born without sight, but her palms read the world like other people read books. [16] She drew her maps by pressing them into wet clay, and travelers said her charts knew rivers before the rivers knew themselves. [33] What no cartographer's guild ever guessed was the truer gift: her skin absorbed languages the way linen absorbs rain, so any tongue spoken near her bare wrist became hers within an hour. [62]

This was why the courier came to her hut at the edge of the ultrasonic forest, where the trees sang at frequencies only bats and grieving women could hear. [89] He carried a platinum compass ring in a velvet pouch and a letter sealed with the crest of the Margrave of Vellin. [111] "His daughter has gone into the forest," the courier said, removing his gloves so Iola could feel the script. [129] "She left a trail. [133] He needs someone who can follow it across the pause of shifting constellations, before the stars resume their drift and erase her path." [156]

Iola slid the ring onto her index finger. [164] It was warm, strangely warm, as though the metal remembered a pulse. [176] "Why me?" she asked, though she suspected. [183]

"Because the trail is made of notes," the courier said. [193] "And because the Margrave's daughter wrote to him, last spring, that only a blind woman could navigate the uncharted emotional depths her sorrow had become." [218]

Iola thanked him and walked into the singing wood. [227]

The ultrasonic forest pressed against her ears like cold water. [237] She could not hear its true voice, but her skin caught the vibrations and translated them: a dialect of moss, a grammar of bark splitting in slow centuries. [263] Within an hour she was speaking back to the trees in their own dialect of pressure and pulse. [280] They told her the girl had passed three nights ago, weeping in F minor. [294]

The platinum ring grew warmer when Iola turned north. [303] She understood, then, what it was for: to dowse. [312] Not for water, but for the hidden melody the Margrave's letter had hinted at, the song the daughter had composed and scattered behind her like crumbs. [338] Iola raised her hand and let the ring lead, drifting like a needle toward something magnetic and grieving. [355]

The first note hung from a low branch, a hummingbird's egg that chimed when she touched it. [371] D. [372] The second was a stone that buzzed against her ankle. [382] A. [383] The third was a feather caught in spider silk, vibrating B-flat against the wind. [397] She gathered each tone into her memory the way she gathered borders for her maps, and the forest hushed around her, listening. [419]

She walked across the pause of shifting constellations, that brief astronomical breath between one season's sky and the next, when navigators traditionally refuse to travel. [445] But Iola had no stars to lose. [451] She had a ring, a melody assembling itself, and the slow patient blaze of someone who has decided to find another person no matter what shape that finding takes. [479]

On the seventh note, the trees parted. [486]

At the center of the forest lay a stillpoint, a place the wind did not enter and the trees did not sing. [508] The Margrave's daughter sat in it, knees folded, eyes open, neither alive nor dying, suspended in the silence she had composed for herself. [532] The melody was complete now in Iola's chest, all eight notes humming under her sternum. [547]

Iola knelt. [549] She did not speak; she let her skin learn the girl's grief instead, drinking it through her palms until she carried the language of that particular sorrow. [575] Then, in that borrowed tongue, she whispered the melody back. [585]

The girl blinked. [588] "You found me." [591]

"I followed what you left." [596]

"I did not think anyone could." [602]

"Your father sent a ring," Iola said, "but the ring only worked because you wanted, somewhere, to be heard." [621]

They sat together in the stillpoint a long while, until the constellations resumed their slow drift overhead and the forest remembered how to sing. [646] Then Iola took the girl's hand, placed the platinum ring upon her thumb, and led her out by the small steady warmth of it, two women walking home through a wood that now knew both their names.
