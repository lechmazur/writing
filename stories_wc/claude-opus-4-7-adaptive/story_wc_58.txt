<story>After the last ship sailed from the harbor at Yevren, Ilan carried his notebook into the shadowed garden behind the abandoned summer palace. [23] He was a traveler who wrote obituaries for the living, and the living paid him well for the privilege. [42] His commissions arrived on letterhead edged in gold: merchants, generals, dowagers, all wanting to know how their absences from the world would be explained. [66] He wrote vividly descriptive lives for them while they still lived, pressing their virtues into sentences the grave could not revise. [87] The last commission had come from the Empress herself, now dead these six months, though her obituary had never been released. [108] Ilan had come to the garden to finish it, or to fail. [120] Autumn had stripped the pear trees to bronze filigree, and the fountain held a skin of red leaves that did not stir. [142] In his pocket he carried a single weft thread pulled from the palace carpet where the Empress once received him. [162] She had given it to him at their first meeting with a small, intimate gesture, tucking it into his palm as though handing over a key. [188] "Write me honestly," she had said, "and do not make me brave." [200] Now he sat on a bench veined with moss and turned the thread between his fingers. [216] He had always worked the same way, by projecting presence onto the page until the subject rose from it whole. [236] A soldier's limp, a banker's patient laughter, a mother's way of counting stairs; these he summoned, and the living read their obituaries and wept with relief, as if his words had loaned them another year. [271] But the Empress would not rise. [277] He had spent three cities trying, the page refusing every sentence. [288] He had come here at last because she had told him, in that first meeting, that grief required a shape you could walk through. [312] He had not understood then. [317] He wanted now to understand the geography of healing: what roads it used, what bridges, what weather it preferred. [336] The garden answered in lonesome splendor. [342] Gold light slid between the colonnades; somewhere a dove shook itself out of the quince. [357] He laid the thread across the open page. [365] It was a small, crimson filament, once part of a pattern of cranes and rivers that covered the throne room floor. [386] Pull one weft, she had told him that day, and the whole picture shifts; that is what we are to each other. [408] He began to write, finally, not an obituary but a letter. [419] He wrote about the absences she had explained to him over the months of their commission: the father she had buried as a child, the brother who had sailed and never come home, the son she would not name. [458] Each absence, she had said, had its own climate, its own map. [470] She had lived among them the way a city lives among its ruins: unevenly, gratefully, sometimes with music. [488] He had not loved her; he had not been asked to. [499] But in writing her he had absorbed her weather, and now, after the last ship had gone and the court had scattered, he alone held the unfinished sentence of her life. [530] The page filled slowly. [534] He wrote her bravery, though she had forbidden it, because honesty required him to name the thing she had refused to see in herself. [558] He wrote the carpet, the cranes, the thread, the way her hand had closed over his. [574] He wrote the garden around him, the bronze pears, the doves, the last ship receding like a thought one cannot finish. [595] When he had done, he understood what she had meant about walking through. [608] Healing was not a country but a route, marked only by what one agreed to carry. [624] He folded the thread into the letter, and the letter into his coat, and walked out of the shadowed garden while the light still held. [649] There would be no more ships this season. [657] He would winter here, and in the spring he would publish her, honestly, and take up a different trade. [676] He had enough obituaries of the living. [683] It was time to write something that could be mourned. [693]</story>
