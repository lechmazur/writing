Vael had served as lore keeper of the Sundered Capital for ninety-two synthetic years, long enough to remember when the throne still mattered. [23] Now the throne sat unclaimed under a dome of cracked solar glass, and the city's children invented gods to explain it. [44] On the eve of the equinox, a courier-drone delivered a single ruby flake sealed in a cube of programmable resin. [64] Vael recognized the cut at once: a fragment from the old Ascendancy Key, lost when the last queen vanished into the orchard. [86] He had spent decades cataloging that orchard's myth, the hidden grove blooming behind a kaleidoscopic waterfall whose colors were said to refract the souls of the unborn. [113] He had never thought to visit it himself. [121] But the flake hummed against his palm with the resonance of a summons, and even an old archivist could be wisely adventurous when destiny knocked politely. [147] He packed lantern-batteries, his transcription stylus, and a thermos of bitter chicory, then sallied forth at moonset. [164] The pilgrimage carried him beyond the magnetic gates, through dust-flats where wind turbines stood like the skeletons of giant flowers. [184] By the third hour the waterfall appeared, throwing prismatic ribbons across a basalt cliff, and behind its glittering veil he found the orchard exactly as the texts described. [212] Pear trees grew in concentric rings around a single building: a crumbling kiosk no larger than a tram-car, its walls scaled with peeling paint. [236] Vael understood at once that he had to read it the way the queen had taught her scribes, through the peel of paint layers revealing hidden murals. [263] He set the ruby flake against the kiosk's eastern wall, and the resin-cube melted into a solvent that ate one century of pigment per minute. [288] Beneath the bureaucratic beige came corporate slogans, beneath those came protest graffiti, and beneath those came the original mural: a spiral staircase ascending into a sky of nested suns. [317] The Stairway of Ascension. [321] Vael's chest tightened with a feeling he could only name as futuristic nostalgia: longing for a tomorrow that had been promised before he was born and had never quite arrived. [351] The mural was not decoration; it was schematic. [359] Each painted step encoded a coordinate, and the entire spiral mapped a route into the orbital ring above the dead capital, the platform where, in optimistic decades, citizens had imagined ascending to claim a stewardship of stars. [396] He copied the figures into his stylus, his hand trembling, and the orchard around him began to chime softly as the kaleidoscopic spray caught a paler dawn. [423] He worked until sunrise, racing the solvent and the rising light, because the texts warned that the mural would oxidize and dissolve once exposed to direct sun. [450] When the first true ray cleared the cliff, the spiral flared once, gold and impossible, then crumbled into iridescent dust that settled among the pear roots. [476] Vael sat with the data in his stylus, understanding at last what the lore keepers had always been: not guardians of the past, but smugglers of unfinished futures. [504] The throne in the cracked dome would remain unclaimed, because the throne itself had been the wrong question. [522] The queen had not vanished; she had simply gone up, leaving a stairway in plain sight for anyone patient enough to peel back the layers. [547] He drank the last of the chicory, watched the waterfall multiply the morning into a thousand colors, and began the long walk home. [570] In his pack, the coordinates pulsed like a second heart. [580] He would publish them at the equinox bell, free of charge, addressed to no one and to everyone. [598] Let the children who had invented gods invent astronauts instead. [608] Above the orchard, the orbital ring caught the new sun and shone, patient as a promise that had only been waiting to be remembered. [632]
