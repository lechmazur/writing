After the aliens were discovered, the sea no longer felt like the bottom of anything. [15] Mira Kestrel taught navigation in the benthic city of Halocline, where pressure-glass domes held back two miles of indifferent ocean. [36] Before the contact, she had trained pilots to read currents and read sonar; afterward, the curriculum cracked open like a shell. [57] Her students wanted to learn to navigate by starlight. [66] It was a strange request, down here where no star had ever shone. [79] But the visitors, who had drifted into orbit eighteen months ago and politely declined to land, communicated in pulses that mimicked stellar parallax. [102] To speak with them, humans would have to think in constellations. [113] Mira accepted the assignment with what her colleagues called her balancedly curious temperament: neither breathless nor dismissive, weighing each new wonder against the work it demanded. [139] She set up her classroom in the old harbormaster's tower, a glass spire rooted in basalt where the city's first anchor still rested. [162] That anchor, abandoned by a submersible long ago, had become a relic; she kept one cold link of its chain on her desk as a paperweight. [188] The link was heavier than it looked, scaled with rust the color of dried kelp. [203] She liked that it had once held something steady against drift. [214] Her first lesson began with a confession. [221] "I have never seen a star with my own eyes," she told the seven students arrayed before her. [238] "Neither have most of you. We were born under water." [248] She lifted the chain link and set it on the table between them. [261] "Navigation used to mean this: a known weight, a known place. The aliens have offered us something else." [279] She switched on the dome's projector, and the ceiling unfolded horizons no benthic citizen had been taught to expect: not the flat rim of a sonar map, but a curved sky, simulated, populated with the real positions of stars as they would appear from the surface a hundred fathoms above. [329] Several students gasped. [332] One wept quietly, which Mira pretended not to notice. [341] "To speak with our visitors," she continued, "we have to learn their geometry. Not the geometry of currents, which curves around obstacles, but the geometry of edges, where light from a star meets the edge of a planet, where one constellation ends and another begins." [386] She called it edge geometry: the discipline of measuring the world by where things stop being themselves. [403] It was the only mathematics, she believed, that could expand a mind raised in pressure and shadow into the vacuum-thinking the aliens used. [426] For months they worked. [430] She taught them to triangulate by imaginary stars, to feel the angle between Sirius and Canopus on their fingertips, to plot vectors against a sky they would never breathe under. [461] Some students faltered. [464] The benthic city did not encourage upward thinking; its founders had built downward as a creed, a refusal of a surface they considered ruined. [488] But Mira was patient, and patient with herself, too, on the nights she sat in her tower and turned the anchor link in her hands and wondered whether the visitors had a word for nostalgia. [523] The tone of the city had shifted since contact. [532] People moved through the corridors with a somber marvel, as if they had been told a beautiful thing and a terrible thing in the same sentence and were waiting to learn which was which. [566] Mira recognized the feeling. [570] It was the same one she felt looking at the chain link: gratitude for what had held, grief for what had been let go. [593] On the last day of the term, she took her students up. [605] The ascent capsule pressed them against their seats for forty minutes before breaching at dusk over the Coral Sea. [625] The hatch opened. [628] The sky above was clean, and the stars were already pricking through the indigo. [642] Her students, those born of the deep, looked up for the first time in their lives at the actual map they had been learning by proxy. [668] Mira watched them, balancing her own awe against the work still ahead. [680] Tomorrow, the visitors would arrive in low orbit, and someone would have to greet them in the language of edges and angles. [702] She thought of the anchor link on her desk far below, holding nothing now but the weight of a place that had decided, at last, to look up. [731]
