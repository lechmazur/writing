Orel tended the hydraulic clocks at the Horizon Measurement Station, where the fjord held its breath between mountains. [18] He was an eminently peculiar shepherd, for his flock was not sheep but brass dials and copper pipes that drank from glacial meltwater. [41] Every morning he walked the catwalks, listening for the hiss of pressure, the tick of escapements, the slow sigh of water pushing time along its grooved track. [68]

The station sat on a cliff where the sea narrowed to a blade. [81] Scientists said the fjord's edges were so sharp they called them razor edges, the cliffs falling straight into dark water without apology. [103] From this vantage, measurements were taken of the sun's arrival and the moon's retreat, numbers telegraphed south to cities Orel had never visited. [126]

He kept one object that did not belong to the station: a mantle clock key, bronze, worn smooth by his grandmother's thumb. [148] The mantle clock itself had burned in a house fire forty years ago, but the key survived, and Orel carried it in his pocket like a question he could not yet phrase. [180]

Because this was his question: what did clocks actually measure? [190] The hydraulic ones around him measured pressure translated into seconds. [200] His grandmother's mantle clock had measured afternoons of bread and silence. [211] But the thing behind the measuring, the underlying substance, he could not name it. [225] He had come to the station to find out. [234]

The chief engineer, Vask, thought Orel mad but useful. [243] "A clock measures intervals," Vask said, tapping his pipe. [252] "Nothing more." [254] "Then why does my key still fit nothing, and still mean something?" Orel asked. [268] Vask shrugged and went back to his charts. [276]

The fjord quiet was the kind that made a dropped spoon sound like artillery. [290] Orel learned to move gently, to breathe in rhythm with the pistons. [302] On clear evenings he would stand on the observation deck, squint into the horizon where the sun drowned behind serrated peaks, and try to catch the precise moment when day became night. [334] The clocks registered it mechanically. [339] He wanted to feel it. [344]

One autumn afternoon, the station's master whistle sounded, calling all hands to verify the equinox reading. [360] After blowing the whistle, the senior observer retired to his logbook, and Orel was left alone on the platform with the measurement scope. [383] It was then he tried his experiment. [390] He had read in an old monastery text that certain truths could only be approached through counting backwards - that forward counting built the world, but backward counting unmade it, thinning the veil between measurement and the thing measured. [428] He took out the mantle clock key and held it against the brass rim of the scope. [445] He began to count aloud, from one hundred down. [454]

The fjord listened. [457] Water slid along pipes. [461] The hydraulic flock ticked, each tick a small shepherd's bell. [471] At fifty, his voice dropped to a whisper. [479] At twenty, the light along the horizon seemed to pause, and he squinted harder, seeing - or imagining - that the razor edge of the cliff and the razor edge of the setting sun were the same edge, that every measurement was only a cut made into something that had been whole. [529] At ten, the hydraulic clocks shivered out of sync for the first time in Orel's tenure. [545] At one, he understood. [549]

Clocks did not measure time. [554] They measured the refusal of time to stay whole. [563] Every tick was a blade separating a now from a then, a small violence necessary for living. [580] His grandmother's afternoons had not been intervals but continuous weather, and the key in his hand had never unlocked anything because the mantle clock had only ever pretended to cut her afternoons into pieces she could count. [617]

He sat down on the platform. [623] The clocks resumed. [626] The fjord held its silver weight between the cliffs, uninterrupted, and Orel breathed with it, feeling for the first time the difference between the measurement and the measured. [654]

When Vask found him, Orel was smiling, the bronze key warm in his palm. [668] "Well?" Vask said. [671] "Did you find what clocks measure?" [677] Orel closed his fingers. [681] "Their own teeth," he said. [686] Vask frowned, then laughed, not understanding. [692] Orel did not try to explain. [698] He rose, checked the pressure gauges, and went to feed his patient, peculiar flock. [712]
