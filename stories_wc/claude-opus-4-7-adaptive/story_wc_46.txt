Nerin had always been called the earthbound star watcher, the girl who charted constellations from the cliffs because she could not fly up to meet them. [26] When her younger brother Kelo fell ill with a fever that dimmed his eyes, she promised to bring him a light he could hold without burning. [52] A torch would frighten him; a lantern would smoke the small room. [64] Her task, then, was to share light without using fire. [74] The old tidewoman told her of a serpentine grotto in mist, a curling cave where the sea threaded beneath basalt and salt air hung like breath on cold glass. [103] Inside, she was told, tumbled pieces of sea glass caught the moon and returned it softer, kinder, bright enough to read a child's dreaming face by. [129] Nerin set out at dusk with a leather pouch, a whetted file, and a square of chamois for gem polishing. [149] The wind that day carried a zephyr bloom off the cliff lavenders, warm and forgiving, nudging her down the switchback path as if the island wished her well. [177] The grotto entrance coiled like a sleeping snake, and mist curled inside its throat. [191] She waded in with her lantern unlit, letting eyes adjust to the slow, patient glow of glass. [208] As memories resurfaced, the cave became less stone and more childhood. [219] She remembered Kelo at four, laughing under a coverlet, asking what stars tasted like. [233] She remembered their mother's voice, the wooden spoon, the salt-scrubbed floor. [244] Each memory arrived like a tide and went out again, leaving her hands steadier than before. [260] She gathered a dozen shards and began to weigh them in her palm, one by one, feeling for the right heft. [281] A true companion stone, the tidewoman had said, would sit in the hand like a small moon, neither eager nor shy. [302] One piece, a bottle-green oval, rested as though it had chosen her. [314] She set to gem polishing against the chamois, slow strokes, patient breath, the file coaxing rough edges into a soft lozenge. [335] With every pass, another memory surfaced and resolved: the night Kelo was born, the morning he first named a star, the evening he had whispered that he was afraid of the dark. [367] Nerin polished until the glass grew reflectively vibrant, alive with borrowed light, each facet cradling a different shade of sea. [387] When she held it up, the grotto's moonlight gathered inside the stone and steadied. [401] No flicker, no heat, no hunger for air: only a pearl of stillness, a round quiet that seemed to hush the cave itself. [424] She understood then that the grotto did not give light; it taught the finder how to carry it without consuming anything. [445] Patience was the fuel; attention the wick. [452] Climbing home, the path seemed shorter, though her ankle throbbed against every step. [465] Kelo was still awake, eyes too large in his thin face. [476] She knelt and set the polished sea glass into his open palm. [488] He closed his fingers; light leaked gently between them, cool as river-stone. [500] "Is it a star?" he asked. [506] "It's a piece of one," she said, "brought home the long way." [518] He laughed, and the laugh was small and steady, like the stone. [530] Nerin watched his chest rise, even at last, and felt her own breathing fall into the rhythm of the tide she had left behind. [554] Outside, the sky gleamed with the stars she had always watched from the ground, unreachable and beloved. [571] For the first time, one of them answered back from her brother's hand, and the room needed no fire at all. [592] She leaned her head against the bedpost and, for the first time in weeks, allowed herself to close her eyes. [612]
