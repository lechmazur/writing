The workshop drifted on the acid sea like a brass insect pinned to green glass, its gears-and-cogs floor wheezing under Iselin's boots. [22] Below, the verdant tide hissed against the hull, eating any pilgrim who slipped. [35] Iselin was an uncertain alchemist, and her hands shook over the workbench where a centuries-old statue lay half-submerged in lamplight. [55] The statue had been hauled from a drowned shrine, its surface cratered with pockmarks left by the acid's slow appetite. [75] She had been told, in a voice like rusted bells, that the statue would teach her how to repair what could not be repaired. [99] She did not believe it. [104] She did, however, believe she had nothing else. [112] Her sister Maren lay in the next room, breathing the way one breathes underwater: shallow, miserly, and counted. [130] Each inhale cost something Iselin could not name; each exhale spent it. [142] The doctors of the floating cities had refused her, citing the acid in Maren's lungs and the hopelessness of charts. [162] So Iselin had come here, to the last workshop above the sea, motivated only to conjure meaning from the edge of oblivion. [184] On her belt jingled a key ring charm, a small lacquered fish her sister had pressed into her palm at age seven. [206] It was the only object she trusted. [213] She set it beside the statue, where its red enamel caught the lamp like a heartbeat. [229] The work was called kintsugi intentions: a craft that taught the practitioner to mend a wound by speaking its history aloud, gilding each crack with words instead of gold. [258] To perform it, one had to narrate the broken thing's life as though every scar were deliberate. [275] But Iselin did not know Maren's life well enough. [284] She had been gone for years, chasing tinctures across distant archipelagoes, and her sister's recent days were a stranger's diary. [304] The statue, the master had said, would help. [312] She bent close, breath fogging the brass. [319] The method was strange: by matching the pockmarks on the centuries-old statue to the gaps in her own remembering, she could find the shape of what she had missed. [348] Each crater on the stone corresponded to an absent year. [358] She placed her thumb against the first pit, and a tone of dire enchantment rose through the gears below, a low harmonic that quilts memories together from threads neither hers nor the statue's, but borrowed from the sea itself. [397] She began to narrate. [401] "In her tenth year," Iselin said, her voice trembling, "Maren learned the names of the floating gulls." [418] The statue brightened along one fissure, gold light blooming like a vein. [430] "In her fourteenth, she stitched her first sail and named it after our mother." [444] Another crack glowed. [447] The acid sea sloshed beneath, patient as a furnace. [456] Iselin pressed on, matching pit to absence, hour to hour, until she came to the deepest pockmark, a hollow the size of her thumbprint. [480] This was the year Maren had nearly died the first time, and Iselin had not come home. [497] She faltered. [499] The lamplight guttered, and the workshop tilted as the gears recalculated her hesitation. [512] If she lied here, the kintsugi would fracture; if she said nothing, Maren's breath in the next room would simply not return. [534] The timeframe of the spell was the timeframe of a held breath underwater, and Iselin understood, suddenly, that her sister was holding hers now, somewhere between this sentence and the next. [565] She closed her eyes. [569] "In her twenty-second year," she said, "her sister chose a stranger's coastline over her bedside, and was wrong, and is sorry, and has come back." [594] The deepest pit filled with gold. [600] The statue exhaled a thread of light that wound past the lamp, past the workshop door, into the room where Maren slept. [622] Iselin felt the key ring charm warm against her hip, the lacquered fish quivering as if remembering water. [640] In the next room, a breath was taken, then released, then taken again. [653] The acid sea sighed. [657] The uncertain alchemist did not feel certain, even now. [666] But she had quilted enough to make a blanket, and a blanket was sometimes enough to keep a body warm above oblivion. [688] She set down her tools and went to sit beside her sister, listening, between breaths, for the next story she would have to tell. [712]
