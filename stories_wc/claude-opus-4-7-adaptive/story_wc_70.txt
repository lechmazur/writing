<story>After the city-wide blackout, the lightning collection tower still hummed like a patient animal, its copper ribs glowing faintly in the nectar dusk. [23] Orin, the toll keeper, sat in his booth at the base of the spiral, counting coins by the light of a single honeycomb lantern. [47] He was candid by reputation and involuntarily truthful by curse, a condition acquired years ago when he kissed a storm. [67] The tower had failed last night, and everyone in the city below had learned to walk by candle again. [115] Without the swarm, the tower could catch bolts but not tame them, and the grid had drowned itself in wild current. [136] Orin's job, strictly, was to charge the beekeepers two copper pennies each for passage to the upper combs. [154] His unspoken job, the one he kept like a secret warmth, was to preserve wonder in routine. [267] "What sings without throat, stings without malice, and dies when you stop believing in small things?" [283] Orin read it twice. [287] "A bee," he said, because he could not help saying what he knew. [300] The girl smiled, relieved, as though his truthfulness were the exact tool she had come to borrow. [445] The cell opened with a small sigh, and the spare queen emerged, gold-dusted and drowsy. [460] Around her, the dead drones stirred, caught her scent, and lifted on new wings. [474] One by one the storage combs began to glow, amber at first, then the deep orange of the nectar dusk outside, as if the sky had agreed to be archived. [504] Below them, block by careful block, the city's windows bloomed back into light. [517] The girl asked him to reveal what he had felt while climbing, because honest mouths, she said, were also the best witnesses. [539] Orin thought of his booth, his coins, his two small pennies per passage, and the tiny truths he handed out like sugar. [561] "I felt," he said, "that routine is a hive, and wonder is the queen we must keep alive inside it." [581] The girl nodded and left the lacquer box on the sill as payment. [594] Orin carried it back down to his booth, where he placed the matchbox inside it, and the riddle inside the matchbox, and closed the lid. [619] Tomorrow the beekeepers would return, and he would charge them two pennies, and tell each one a true, astonishing thing about the weather.
