Branwen was, by trade and temperament, an orderly seed sorter. [9] She kept her drawers labeled in a hand so neat the letters seemed pressed by frost. [25] Hawthorn east, hawthorn west, three grades of rowan, and a velvet pouch reserved for seeds nobody recognized anymore. [45]

She worked in the ashen skyborne arbor, that pale grove suspended above the valley on roots no botanist could explain. [65] The villagers below called it the Cinder Canopy, and they spoke of it the way they spoke of all regional folklore: half mocking, half kneeling. [89] Their grandmothers had warned that whoever climbed up returned changed, for the arbor emerges transformed always, and so does anyone who lingers under its boughs. [114]

Branwen had lingered for nine years. [120] Her hair had gone the color of birch ash, and she no longer remembered which winter she had stopped descending. [142]

Today the leaves were falling. [147] Not in a wind, not in a season; the arbor shed in slow spirals during the hush where past meets prophecy, that thin half hour between the last bell of evening and the first true dark. [186] Branwen waited for it the way other people wait for letters. [198]

Her motivation, the one she would not say aloud even to the squirrels, was to decode the message in falling leaves. [220] She believed each leaf carried one syllable of a longer sentence the arbor had been speaking for centuries, and if she sorted them as carefully as she sorted seeds, she might at last hear it whole. [256]

So she had developed a method. [262] She built tiny monuments to forgotten things. [269] A pebble cairn for the names of cattle nobody bred anymore. [280] A spiral of acorn caps for the lullaby her mother had hummed but never finished. [295] A spire of pine bark for the river that used to run east before the quarry turned it. [313] Each monument, she reasoned, gave a forgotten syllable somewhere to land. [325] The leaves would settle on whatever they recognized. [333]

Tonight a leaf the color of weak tea drifted down and rested on the cattle cairn. [349] Another, gold edged, alighted on the river spire. [358] A third hung in the air, refusing every monument she had made. [370]

Branwen leaned in. [373] She took the slim willow stick she used to prod stubborn seedpods and gave the leaf the gentlest nudge. [393] It rotated once and revealed, on its underside, a single shining mote: a moonstone cabochon dust speck, smaller than a poppy grain, catching the last light like a swallowed moon. [424]

Her breath went thin with mossy delight, that quiet green gladness she had not felt since girlhood, when wonder still tasted of riverbank. [448]

She understood, suddenly, that she had built monuments for everything she could remember forgetting, but never for the thing she had forgotten she forgot. [473] The moonstone speck was a seed of it. [481] Some old hill rite, perhaps, or a grandmother's word for the color of dawn on snow, or her own true name before the village had given her a tidy one. [510] The leaf had been waiting for a place to land, and finding none, had brought its own. [527]

Branwen pinched the speck between two fingers and set it on her bare palm. [541] Around it, with twigs no thicker than eyelashes, she built the smallest monument yet: four uprights, a lintel, a roof of one curled petal. [566] A doorway for a thing without a name. [574]

The remaining leaf settled inside it without a sound. [583]

The arbor exhaled. [586] Every other leaf still aloft drifted down at once, each finding its monument, each clicking softly into place like the tiles of a long mosaic. [613] Branwen heard, finally, the sentence the grove had been spelling, and though she could not later repeat a single word of it, she understood that it had been, all along, an invitation. [645]

She rose. [647] Her drawers of seeds stayed neat behind her, labels firm, ready for whoever climbed up next. [664] Branwen herself walked out along a branch she had never noticed, became briefly the color of weak tea and gold edges, and stepped, transformed, into the hush. [691]

Below in the valley a grandmother told a child: see, that is how it always ends. [707]
