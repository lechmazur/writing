<story>Sir Alden had not spoken a full sentence in four winters, and the lantern he carried through the greenhouse door did not mind. [23] Inside, the alchemical orchard slept beneath a dome of endless night, each pear lacquered with starlight, each apple humming a faint, factually mystical note. [47] He had come because a rumor said the shape of possibility could be captured here, if one knew which branches to press one's ear against. [72] In his gauntlet he held an abandoned marionette theater program, its paper soft as moth wings, its ink the color of dried plums. [95] His sister had written her name on the back of it, the year before she vanished into some quieter country. [115] Alden walked between rows of glass-skinned trees, and the program trembled as though remembering its cues. [131] At the orchard's heart stood a cabinet older than the greenhouse itself, its doors sealed by seven small brass clasps. [151] The rumor had insisted he must unsnap each one through forgotten patterns, gestures older than the language he no longer spoke. [172] He studied the program. [176] Act One listed a puppet king, a thrush, a bridge, and a sleeping girl, and the woodcut illustrations showed their arms posed in specific angles. [201] Alden mimicked the puppet king's bow, and the first clasp sprang open with a small, satisfied click. [218] A shiver crossed his breastplate, and somewhere behind his ribs an old gladness uncurled, a shivering bliss he had not permitted himself since childhood. [242] He turned the page. [246] The thrush's gesture was a cupped hand lifted toward the throat, and when he made it, the second clasp answered. [266] The bridge wanted his arms spread flat; the sleeping girl wanted his head inclined as if listening to a confessional. [286] One by one the clasps opened, and with each the greenhouse air thickened, as if it were being filled slowly with a held breath. [310] By the sixth clasp, reality had begun to shift around him. [321] The rows of trees rearranged themselves between blinks, and the fruit ripened backwards through colors that had no proper names. [341] Alden, who had armored himself against wonder for so long, felt that armor crack, tenderly, from the inside. [359] He paused before the final clasp. [365] The last page of the program held no illustration, only a single printed line: Here the puppeteer murmurs her allegiance, and the stage listens. [389] He had not said his sister's name since the morning she left. [401] He said it now, quietly, scarcely louder than the orchard's hum. [412] He pledged himself, in one murmured allegiance, to whatever she had become and to whatever he might yet be permitted to become beside her. [436] The seventh clasp opened without being touched. [443] Inside the cabinet there was no treasure, no relic, no map. [454] There was only a small stage, and upon the small stage a marionette carved in his own likeness, hanging from strings that rose into an invisible ceiling. [481] Its painted eyes were kind. [486] Alden understood then that the shape of possibility was not a prize to be carried home in a satchel, but a posture one had to consent to be held in. [516] He lifted his own hands, and above the stage, invisible fingers lifted with him. [530] The marionette moved when he moved. [536] The orchard shimmered, and for an instant he saw every version of himself he had ever refused: the singer, the father, the traveler, the man who laughed easily. [564] Each stood among the factually mystical trees, patient, waiting to be chosen. [576] Alden did not choose. [580] He simply bowed, as the puppet king had bowed, and the endless night above the glass roof bowed back. [599] When morning did not come, he was not afraid. [608] He folded the abandoned marionette theater program and tucked it beside his heart, where its paper went on rehearsing a play he was finally, shiveringly, willing to star in. [637]</story>
