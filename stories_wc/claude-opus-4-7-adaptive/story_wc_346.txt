<story>The hidden lighthouse stood where no map conceded it, its beam long extinguished, its door answering only to Mira's particular grief. [21] She climbed the stairs at dusk, as she had climbed them for forty years, carrying the satchel of a doomsday clock adjuster. [43] Inside the lantern room waited the Clock itself, a mechanism of brass rings nested within brass rings, each ring a century, each century a child's held breath. [70] On the topmost gear, where a figure ought to stand and mark the hour, sat a broken puppet head, jaw hinged open in permanent astonishment. [95] It had been her daughter's, once, before the fire, before Mira had been given this office by people she no longer believed existed. [118] They had told her the Clock was beautifully flawed, that its imperfection was the only reason the world persisted: a true machine would have ended everything already. [145] Her task was to listen through nested patterns, the rhythms inside rhythms, and to nudge the escapement whenever ruin drew near. [166] Tonight, the rhythms had gone thin. [172] She could feel it in her bones, in the dust motes that refused to settle, in the gulls outside that had stopped mid-cry and hung suspended like paper. [200] A pane of hush had slid between the world and itself. [211] She knelt before the Clock with bruised awe, the kind an old widow feels examining a photograph she both loves and cannot bear. [234] The puppet head looked back, painted eyes flaking, and she remembered the lullaby. [247] Her daughter had begged for it every night: a small tune about moths and moons, nothing grand, nothing worth saving. [267] Mira understood then what the Clock had always been waiting to vouchsafe to her. [281] The mechanism was not built to prevent the end; it was built to shape the ending, to choose its song. [301] An adjuster could do only one great thing in a lifetime: she could whisper the final moment into whatever shape she carried inside her. [325] Mira opened her satchel and took out the tuning fork of bone, the calipers, the little hammer wrapped in velvet. [345] She set the puppet head gently upright on the central gear, aligning its cracked mouth with the slow passage of the minute. [367] Then she began to hum. [372] Her voice, thin as December glass, entered the mechanism and the mechanism carried it inward, winding it smaller and smaller until it became part of the oscillation itself. [400] The pane of hush trembled. [405] Outside the lighthouse, the gulls resumed falling, but slowly, as if reluctant to conclude their flight. [421] Mira sang the moths-and-moons song, and the broken puppet head began to rock gently on its gear, in time, in time. [442] She was no longer adjusting the doomsday; she was tucking it in. [454] Across the hush of time's final ripple, her lullaby spread outward. [465] It reached backward as easily as forward, threading through cradles she had never known, through the death of stars she would never witness, through a thousand lifetimes of strangers who, for a single held breath, would each dream of their mother's voice. [507] This was what she had been preserved for: to whisper a lullaby across a thousand lifetimes, to give the ending a kindness it had not earned. [533] The Clock slowed. [536] The brass rings relaxed their grip on one another, and the sea beyond the lighthouse turned the color of an old bruise, purple and gold and tender. [563] Mira smiled, or something of her did; she could no longer be entirely sure where she ended and the song began. [584] The puppet head closed its jaw, finally, for the first time since the fire. [598] Dusk held, uncompleted. [601] And in that long exhale, everyone who had ever lived was, for one unmeasurable instant, safe. [617]</story>
