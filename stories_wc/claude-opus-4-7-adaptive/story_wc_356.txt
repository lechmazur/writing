Mira climbed into Aerwood at the turning of the tide's thought, the hour when the forest below exhaled and the canopy above inhaled. [23] The mysterious treetop village floated on rope bridges and patient cedar, lanterns unlit, voices hushed like wildflowers at dusk. [42] She came as an ambitious herbalist, her satchel heavy with cuttings and one rare prize wrapped in brown paper: a corpse flower seed. [65] Mira had traded a year's savings for that seed, believing it would make her reputation in the lowland markets. [84] But Aerwood kept no markets. [89] The villagers here were olfactorily sensitive, descended from perfumers who once read weather in pollen. [104] They smelled her ambition before she spoke, a sharp green note under the soft musk of travel. [121] The eldest, a woman named Ten, met her at the landing platform and offered tea without comment on the parcel. [141] Mira had heard that Aerwood's gardeners could tame anything, even a bloom whose rot smelled of carrion for three days straight. [162] She wanted that gift, that mastery. [168] "Teach me," she said, "to cultivate serenity within motion, the way you do, so my garden will not shout when it blooms." [190] Ten examined the parcel, turning the paper in her fingers, and smiled at what she found. [206] On the inside of the wrapping, Mira had been doodling for weeks. [218] It was a family custom; her mother had doodled on the interiors of wrapping papers, and her grandmother before her, pressing small thoughts onto surfaces no one would see. [247] The seed had arrived late, so Mira had filled that paper with spirals, little root systems, a recurring sketch of a girl listening to a tree. [273] "You already know," Ten said. [278] "No," Mira answered, "I want technique." [284] Ten handed back the paper and led her along a swaying walkway to a hollow where the corpse flower would be planted. [306] For a full season, Mira was given nothing but small, exact tasks: thinning shoots at dawn, listening to rain through slit bamboo, and each night she was asked to journalize not her work but her breathing. [342] She wrote, frustrated, that she had come for secrets and received chores. [354] She wrote that the village smelled of cedar and sleeping honey, and that no one hurried. [370] She wrote that the seed, buried in a shaded pot, seemed to be waiting for her rather than the other way around. [392] By the third month her sentences grew shorter; by the fifth, she stopped underlining her ambitions. [408] On the night the seed cracked, Ten came to her. [418] "The undercurrent of wisdom," the old woman said, "is not a thing we teach." [432] "It runs beneath effort, like water under moss." [440] "You feel it when you stop pushing the river." [449] Mira nodded, embarrassed that she understood. [455] The corpse flower, when it finally opened two years later, did not reek. [468] It exhaled, instead, a strange green quiet, a wildflower hush the villagers called it, a scent so subtle that only the olfactorily trained could find it, and only the patient could bear to wait for it again. [505] Mira had cultivated it not by force but by becoming still enough to match its pace. [521] She journalized that evening on the inside of her wrapping paper, because all the notebooks were full. [538] She drew a girl at the foot of a tree, listening, and beside her a flower that did not shout. [558] Below the sketch she wrote, in a hand gentler than the one she had arrived with: I came to master a bloom and learned, instead, to cultivate serenity within motion. [588] The lowland markets would never smell this. [595] She folded the paper and tucked it in her sleeve, and went to help Ten draw water from the evening cisterns. [616] Aerwood, around her, breathed. [620]
