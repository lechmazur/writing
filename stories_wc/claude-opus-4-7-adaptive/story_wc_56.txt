<story>Mira boarded the twilight barge before the canal lanterns were lit, carrying only a notebook and an antique pocket sundial that had belonged to her grandfather. [25] She called herself an errant legend seeker, though the title felt larger than her life; she had spent six years chasing the rumor of a drowned city whose bells were said to ring under certain skies. [55] Tonight, she suspected, she would find nothing, and that suspicion had begun to feel like wisdom. [70]

The barge master, a thoughtfully clear-eyed woman named Odile, accepted her coin without counting it. [85] "You came because of the broadcasts," Odile said, not asking. [95] Mira nodded. [97] For weeks the little pirate station out of Hallow Reach had spoken of a wreck still visible at low tide, of ribs of oak cradling something bright. [124] Then, yesterday, the station fell silent, and the silence of morning radio was what finally moved her: a quiet so pointed it felt addressed to her personally. [152]

The canal narrowed. [155] Reeds leaned in like listeners. [160] Mira opened the sundial on her palm, though the sun had already bled out behind the willows, and watched the gnomon's shadow fail to commit to any hour. [190] Her grandfather had taught her to read it, and more importantly had taught her to read people the same way: by training instincts to notice where shadows refused to settle. [219] She had not understood then why a cartographer needed such a skill. [232] She understood now. [235]

Her motivation had shifted slowly over the years, the way a river changes its bed. [250] She no longer wanted the drowned city for its treasure or fame. [262] She wanted to understand the architecture of lies, the load-bearing beams of every story she had been told, including the ones she had told herself. [289] Her grandfather, the kind cartographer, had drawn false maps during a war she had only learned about after his funeral. [309] Villages that did not exist. [314] Safe roads that led to ambushes. [320] The drowned city, she had come to believe, was one of his inventions, cited too often in letters to be innocent. [342]

"There," Odile said, cutting the engine. [349]

In the shallows, a ribcage of black timber broke the surface. [360] Not a city. [363] A single barge, older than memory, its hold yawning open. Odile helped her lift the ledgers into the barge. [567] They worked without speaking as the eastern willows paled. [576] Somewhere a radio, newly switched on for the dawn hour, hissed and then steadied into music, as if the broadcaster had simply been waiting for her to arrive at the correct silence first. [610]

"You found what you came for?" Odile asked. [618]

"I found what he left," Mira said, which was more accurate. [631] She closed the sundial. [635] The shadow inside would wake at its own pace, with the sun, with her, with whatever came next. [654]
