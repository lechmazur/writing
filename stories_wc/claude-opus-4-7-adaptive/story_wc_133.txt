Mira had not slept properly in nine years, not since the water wars ended and the reservoirs were carved up like bread at a wake. [25] She painted instead, which everyone called a vocation and she called a symptom. [38] Her studio stood on Corren, a tidal island that the sea reclaimed twice daily, stranding her with her canvases and her thoughts. [60] She had come there to thresh meaning from experience, though she'd have phrased it less grandly if asked. [78] The war had given her too many unsorted images; the island gave her the hours to sort them. [96] On the October morning the stranger arrived, Mira was tracking a fox's prints by following rain trails across the salt flats. [117] She had done this for years, her method for finding the shapes the tide forgot: the silver veins rainfall drew between stones always led somewhere uncanny, somewhere she could paint. [147] That morning the veins converged at a hollow under a cairn, and inside the hollow lay an amber brooch. [166] It was the size of a plum, honey-colored, and held inside it a drowned bee and a sprig of something that had been green two thousand years ago. [194] Mira stared at it with the haggard awe of someone who has been awake long enough to recognize that the world is stranger than sleep. [219] The stranger was there when she looked up, as if he had been following the same rain trails from the other direction. [241] He was thin, gray-bearded, carrying a leather satchel and the wryly earnest expression of a man who had rehearsed a difficult conversation. [263] "I've been looking for that since before you were born," he said, not reaching for it. [279] "My grandmother traded it during the worst winter of the water wars for a single jar of clean snow." [298] "She told me it always came back to the island when the tides agreed." [312] Mira held the brooch out, and he did not take it. [323] "Not for nothing," he said. [328] "An exchange, or it won't hold." [334] He opened his satchel and took out a small jar of pigment, ultramarine so saturated it seemed to be a hole in the air. [358] "Lapis from the old mines. I cannot paint, and you cannot let a thing stay found unless you give something for it." [380] She thought about this: the rain, the cairn, the bee in amber, the grandmother's jar of snow, and herself, an insomniac painter who had walked out to catalog mud. [409] It felt like an unearthed convergence, a knot of years and weather and strangers tightening into a single gift she had not asked to receive. [434] She placed the brooch in his palm; he placed the pigment in hers. [447] The tide had begun to come in, hissing along the causeway. [458] "Make something true," he said, and started walking before the sea could trap him. [472] That night, for the first time since the reservoirs burned, Mira slept. [484] She slept four hours, which felt like forty, and dreamed in ultramarine. [496] When she woke she ground the lapis with oil and began a painting she had been failing to make for a decade. [518] It was not the brooch, and not the stranger, and not the war; it was what they had resolved into when they met on her island at low tide. [547] A convergence threshed clean, the chaff of nine sleepless years blown off at last. [561] She painted two figures on a causeway, each holding what the other had lost, and the sky above them was the exact color of a thing worth waking for. [590] When neighbors from the mainland came at the next low tide, they found her asleep on the studio floor, paint on her cheek, the canvas dry and finished. [618] None of them had ever heard of an old man with a satchel of lapis. [633] Mira shrugged with an expression somewhere between exhaustion and triumph, and said the island had paid a debt she hadn't known she was owed. [657] Then she slept again, dreamlessly, while outside the rain began to draw new trails across the salt. [674]
