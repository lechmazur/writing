<story>Calla had been called a moonstruck dreamer since she was small, when she first pressed her ear to the floorboards and heard the house breathing. [25] Now, grown and still half-asleep in daylight, she climbed the spiral stair of the observatory, carrying a brass astrolabe that had belonged to her grandfather. [50] He had told her once that every person is built from remembered stardust, and that forgetting this was the slow death of imagination. [73] Calla was dying that slow death. [79] For months her paintings had refused to arrive; her sketchbooks gaped like empty mouths; her colors lay locked in tubes that felt heavier than lead. [104] She had come to the observatory to free the trapped creativity she could feel beating behind her ribs like a bird stitched inside a cloak. [129] The rooftop opened onto an aurora-lit archway of old stone, where her grandfather had once taught her the names of constellations in a voice like rained-on paper. [156] Green and violet ribbons poured through the arch now, breathing across an unwritten calendar she had carried with her: blank pages for a year she had not yet been able to live. [188] She set the astrolabe on the plinth and turned its rings until the aurora caught in the brass and made the instrument hum. [211] Calla had always heard what others did not say. [220] When her mother lied about being fine, Calla heard the truth shiver beneath the word; when her lover left, she heard the goodbye three weeks before he spoke it. [249] Tonight, the sky itself was speaking in that buried register, and she leaned in to listen. [265] The unspoken words came in a slow, shadowed rapture: you have forgotten how to fall. [280] At once, three meteors broke across the north, and her grandfather's old lesson surfaced whole: that a dreamer finds her way back by tracing the path of falling stars. [309] She lifted the astrolabe, aligned its alidade with the first streak, and drew the arc onto the blank January of her calendar. [331] The line curved like a sleeping woman's spine. [339] She traced the second meteor into February, and the mark became a small, startled mouth. [354] The third cut across March and April together, and she realized, with a quiet shock, that she was drawing again. [374] Every falling star left her a gesture she could not have invented alone: the shoulder of a running boy, a river folding over itself, a hand releasing a key into dark water. [406] The aurora beneath the archway brightened, and Calla understood that creativity had not abandoned her; it had only been waiting for her to remember she was made of the same matter as the sky. [440] Remembered stardust, her grandfather whispered, from some place that no longer needed a mouth. [454] She filled May with a comet's long hair, June with a flock of crescents, July with a map to a city she had never visited but would paint before winter. [484] By the time the shower slowed, every blank month bore a living line. [497] Her arms ached; her eyes stung; the ink on her fingers looked like spilled galaxies. [512] Calla lowered the astrolabe and allowed herself, for the first time in a long season, to rest. [529] She lay back beneath the archway, and the aurora closed over her like a coverlet stitched from everything she had not said aloud. [552] The unspoken words returned, softer now: you are not empty; you were only listening. [566] She thought of all the moonstruck children who had been told their heads were too full of sky to be useful, and she wished she could give them this rooftop, this arch, this instrument, this hour. [602] Instead, she promised to paint the gift for them, one unwritten month at a time. [617] Above her, the last meteor fell, and she did not trace it; she simply watched it arrive, a small bright word the universe had finally spoken aloud. [644]</story>
