<story>On the orchard planet of Veor, floating rock arches hung like broken halos above the pear groves, and Emil the blacksmith counted them each dawn. [25] He was meek by habit, short by birth, and quietly determined by some private argument he had never shared. [44] The orchardists called him Emil the Small, though his anvil rang louder than any bell on the stone-floating world. [63] When the Magician came, he came at the hour between understanding and confusion, when the arches cast two shadows and no one could remember which was true. [90] He wore silk that shifted color like oil, and he carried a ledger of sleeping names. [106] The orchardists of Veor had slept through three bad harvests, lulled by a sickness he had planted in the roots. [126] He offered awakenings in trade: a wife roused for a husband's voice, a child stirred for a mother's song. [145] These were bartered awakenings, and every payment only deepened the others' dreams. [157] Emil paid first to wake his apprentice, Lio, a boy with soot in his eyelashes and summer in his laugh. [177] The Magician took his voice in return, and Emil's hammer fell now without its old, keening song. [194] Silent, he watched the groves grow heavier with sleepers, and something inside him sharpened past fear. [210] He remembered the old mine shafts, abandoned when Veor's copper failed, their mouths hidden under the hanging arches. [228] The shafts wound beneath every orchard, close enough to every root that a man with a lantern could walk unseen from house to house. [252] Emil stole the Magician's silk from its peg while the man slept off a banquet of borrowed breaths. [270] The silk was the ledger made cloth; every stitched name could be untied. [283] Using the abandoned mine shafts, Emil carried it through the black veins of the planet, surfacing beside each sleeper's bed. [303] Each awakening was deliberate: a single thread pulled, a single name freed, a single pear tree's roots clearing. [321] He moved from farm to farm under stone that floated where no stone should, and he did not hurry. [340] The Magician found him on the seventh night, in a cavern where a shaft opened onto a grove of wind-bitten apples. [361] Above them an arch drifted, shedding pebbles into the dark like slow rain. [374] "You bartered your voice," the Magician said, smiling. [382] "You have nothing left to trade." [388] Emil held up the silk, already half-unravelled, blue threads fluttering from his fist like a small, stubborn sky. [406] He had no voice for defiance, so he gave it a color instead: azure, the color of Veor's noon. [425] The Magician lunged, and Emil, meek Emil, stepped aside with the patience of an anvil. [440] He let the silk loose into the shaft's updraft, where it spiraled, catching every torch Emil had lit along the passages. [461] Fire ran up the blue like a signature, and every remaining name burned free at once. [477] Across the orchard planet, sleepers opened their eyes beneath the floating arches, confused into understanding. [492] The Magician, robbed of his ledger, aged into his true years and crumbled to a small gray heap. [510] Emil knelt by the rubble, because he had always intended this: not revenge, but to coax resilience from rubble. [529] He gathered the ash, voiceless, and carried it up the mine shaft into the first noon his people had seen in years. [551] Lio met him at the mouth, eyes wet, and spoke the word Emil no longer could. [567] "Master." [568] Emil nodded, smiled his meek smile, and pressed the ashes into the cracked soil beneath a sagging pear tree. [587] By autumn the orchard fruited azure, a color no grower had planted, and the floating arches above stopped shedding their pebbles. [608] The blacksmith had never raised his voice, and now he never would, yet the world rang on. [625]</story>
