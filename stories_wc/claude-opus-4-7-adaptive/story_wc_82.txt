Oru had been a crafty lantern maker for forty years, and every lantern he built now was a reliquary. [19]
He worked in the floating island tea garden, where hibiscus steam curled around lacquered tables and the horizon tilted whenever the wind shifted. [42]
Above him rose the glass basilica, a cathedral of panes harvested from rainwater and mourning, its spire humming with the dream recordings of every soul who had ever drunk tea there. [73]
The basilica descends while ascending, an old paradox the gardeners sang about: its base kissed the clouds, yet the higher its pinnacles stretched, the closer its foundation sank toward the drowning sea. [105]
Oru had never understood the saying until his daughter Ine drowned beneath it. [118]
Now he understood it the way a candle understands wax. [128]
Her last dream had been captured by the basilica on the morning of the accident, a shimmering reel of light folded into a prism the size of a plum. [157]
The archivist had given it to him wrapped in silk, with the warning that raw dream-light could blind those who loved the dreamer. [180]
His motivation was simple and unbearable: to fold grief into origami cranes, one for every night he would outlive her. [200]
But grief, he learned, cannot be folded in its raw state; it must first be made thin enough to bend. [220]
So he turned to gem polishing, the slowest craft he knew. [231]
Each evening, after the tea garden closed and the lanterns dimmed to embers, he set the prism against a wheel of riverstone and rubbed. [255]
Flecks of Ine's dream sheared off like pollen: the taste of pear skin, the sound of her mother humming, a summer where nothing had yet gone wrong. [282]
He caught each fleck on sheets of mulberry paper, thin as breath, until the paper glowed faintly from within. [301]
The tone of his work was luminous torment. [309]
Every polish brought her closer and further at once, a face emerging from fog that dissolved the moment he reached for it. [331]
The other gardeners watched him with the careful pity reserved for men who are still useful but no longer entirely present. [352]
On the ninety-ninth night, something unexpected happened. [359]
As Oru leaned over the wheel, his own shadow, which had grown heavier each week, slid off his shoulders and pooled on the flagstones like spilled ink. [386]
It did not vanish; it merely waited, watching him with the patience of a thing no longer needed. [404]
After the shadow detached, Oru found he could breathe without effort for the first time since the funeral. [422]
He also found he could finally fold. [429]
He took the glowing mulberry sheets and began creasing them into cranes, each wing a different hour of Ine's nine years. [450]
The first crane transformed in his palm before he finished the tail fold, lifting on its own, circling his wrist three times, then settling on the rim of his teacup like a trained sparrow. [484]
The second carried the scent of her hair. [492]
The third sang a half-remembered lullaby in his mother's voice, because grief, it turns out, remembers further back than any single death. [514]
By dawn he had folded seventy-one. [520]
He carried them up the spiral stair of the glass basilica, climbing while the structure performed its old trick of sinking beneath him. [543]
At the altar he opened the highest pane and released the cranes into the thin, bright air. [560]
They did not fly away. [565]
They orbited the spire in a slow ring, each one pulsing with a fragment of dream, and the basilica's hum shifted to accommodate them, archiving what he had made from what it had given. [599]
His detached shadow climbed the stairs behind him, no longer heavy, only curious, and stood at his elbow like an old colleague. [621]
Oru understood then that he would not be finished in this life. [633]
There were still two hundred and ninety-four cranes to fold, one for every night remaining, by his best polished estimate. [653]
He would sit in the tea garden each dusk, wheel and prism and paper, shaping the unbearable into something a child might have played with. [678]
The basilica would keep descending as it ascended, the clouds would keep rearranging themselves, and somewhere in the light-filled archive above, Ine's dream would keep learning how to be a lantern instead of a wound. [713]
It was not healing. [717]
It was craft. [720]
For Oru, on the floating island, that was enough to begin again each morning. [734]
