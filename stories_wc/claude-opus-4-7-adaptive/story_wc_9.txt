After the birth of her daughter, Mira climbed the bone-pale cloud-stairs to find the onyx-ink cartomancer. [16] She carried two things in her satchel: a saucer from her grandmother's dolls' tea set, hairline-cracked across its painted rose, and a folded birthday tablecloth crowded with traced hands. [45] The cartomancer kept her parlor in a midnight glade above the clouds, where the grass glowed faintly silver and the moon hung so close you could hear it breathing. [74] Mira had been told the woman could find anything that had wandered out of a life, including the years. [93] That was what Mira wanted: to find where her lost time accumulates. [105] The cartomancer met her at the edge of the glade, sleeves stained to the elbow with ink darker than wet stones. [126] "You've come for hours," she said, not asking. [134] "I've come for years," Mira answered. [140] "Seven of them. [143] Maybe eight. [145] I can't tell anymore." [149] "After the birth?" [152] "After the birth." [155] The cartomancer nodded as though this were a common ailment. [165] She gestured to a low table where a deck of cards waited, each one black-faced, the inks shifting like oil on water. [187] "Show me what you brought." [192] Mira unfastened the satchel's stiff brass clasp and laid the saucer on the table first. [207] The crack ran from rim to center, splitting the painted rose so its petals seemed to fall in two directions at once. [229] "My grandmother's," Mira said. [233] "She used to pour me imaginary tea on my birthdays." [243] Then she unfolded the tablecloth. [248] Each year of her childhood, her mother had traced her hand in a different color of thread, ringed by the smaller hands of cousins and aunts. [274] The custom had been passed down like a recipe; every birthday tablecloth in their family was an atlas of palms. [294] "And after I had Lila, I tried to trace her hand on a new cloth," Mira said. [311] "I couldn't hold the pencil steady. [317] I couldn't remember when her birthday was. [324] I couldn't remember mine." [328] The cartomancer touched the saucer's crack with one inked fingertip. [338] "These are good anchors." [342] She turned three cards. [346] On the first, a woman climbed a ladder of milk. [356] On the second, a child slept inside a cracked cup. [366] On the third, a sky full of unspooled clocks, their hands drifting like seedheads. [380] "Your years went where most go," the cartomancer said. [389] "Up. [390] The cloudbound repentance gathers them. [395] Every mother who ever wished, even briefly, that she had her old hours back, sends a small confession skyward. [414] Those confessions thicken. [417] They form weather. [420] And inside them, the missing time pools." [427] Mira sat down in the silver grass, willingly perplexed, the way one is at a beautiful answer one cannot fully understand. [448] "Can I get them back?" [453] "Some. [454] Not all. [456] The repentance does not return what it took, only what was extra." [468] The cartomancer drew her ink across the cracked saucer until the fissure ran black, then pressed the saucer to the tablecloth so the ink bled through the traced hands. [497] A thin steam rose. [501] "Breathe it," she said. [505] Mira leaned in. [508] The steam smelled of her grandmother's parlor, of the powdered sugar on a birthday cake, of the warm crown of her newborn's head. [531] It smelled, too, of nights she did not remember, lullabies she had sung in some sleepless trance, the precise weight of Lila at three months in the crook of her arm. [562] The choking beauty of it brought tears she could not blink back; her chest tightened around years she had assumed were simply gone. [585] "Why does it hurt?" she whispered. [591] "Because you are not retrieving the time," the cartomancer said gently. [602] "You are only being shown it was not wasted." [611] The glade brightened, and the moon moved on. [619] Mira folded the tablecloth back over the saucer with great care, as if swaddling. [633] When she descended the cloud-stairs at dawn, she carried the same things she had brought. [648] But she walked as a woman who knew her vanished years were stored somewhere safe, breathing above her, willing to be visited. [670] At home, she traced Lila's tiny hand onto a new cloth, in a thread the color of ink. [688]
