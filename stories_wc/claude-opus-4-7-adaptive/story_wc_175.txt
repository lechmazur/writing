Imre called himself an esoteric realist, which meant he believed the world hummed with meaning but refused to lie about what that meaning meant. [24] He had climbed for three days to reach the grotto, a verdant pocket of moss and ferns slung impossibly above the clouds, where the air tasted of iron and old rain. [55] He arrived during the migration of the last monarch butterfly, an event the almanacs marked with small black asterisks, as if embarrassed. [77] The grotto was empty when he found it, save for a single moth wing pinned beneath a stone at its mouth. [98] Someone had been here before. [103] He knelt, lifted the stone, and held the wing between two fingers. [115] It was the color of bruised parchment, scaled with dust that came away on his skin like pollen. [133] He did not pocket it. [138] Instead, he laid it on a flat rock at the threshold, where wind could touch it but not take it. [158] He had come to honor overlooked histories. [165] The mountain below him had once supported three villages, then two, then none, and the records had been folded into the records of larger places until they vanished. [193] Imre's grandmother had been from one of those villages. [202] She had sung him lullabies whose cadence he could no longer reproduce, because halfway through any attempt his throat would close and the melody would stop, an interrupted cadence that he had come to understand as inheritance. [239] So he built. [242] That was the method he had taught himself: building temporary structures as signals, small cairns and lashed frames of birch, the way some cultures built spirit houses or way-markers. [271] They were not monuments. [275] Monuments insisted. [277] His structures merely waited. [281] Each one was transiently rooted, set in the soil only long enough to be noticed by the next traveler or the next wind, then released. [306] In the grotto he gathered fallen branches, moss, and a length of red thread he had carried from his grandmother's sewing tin. [328] He tied the branches into a tripod, knot over knot, his cold fingers slow and devout. [344] From the apex he hung the moth wing on the thread, where it turned in the small currents of the cave like a compass needle that had forgotten north. [373] He sat back. [376] The grotto held him in a blushing stillness, the kind of quiet that warms the cheeks because it seems to be looking back. [399] Outside, far below, the clouds moved in a slow herd. [409] Somewhere among them, a single orange butterfly was making its way south, its wings stuttering against the high cold air, its species ending one wingbeat at a time. [437] Imre had read the migration reports. [443] He knew the count had dropped from millions to hundreds to one. [455] He understood the math the way a doctor understands a prognosis: with precision and without consolation. [471] But the esoteric in him refused to let the realist have the last word. [485] Numbers were one history. [489] The lullaby was another. [493] The wing turning on red thread was a third, and none of them needed to agree. [509] He spoke his grandmother's name aloud. [515] The grotto did not echo; it absorbed, the way moss absorbs footfalls. [527] He spoke the names of the villages, what he could remember of them, and the names of the streets within the villages, which he had to invent because no one had written them down. [561] Inventing them, he decided, was a form of honoring too, provided he admitted he was inventing. [577] When he was finished, he stood. [583] The structure he had built would not last the season. [593] A storm would come, or a fox, or simply the patient gravity that takes everything down eventually. [610] That was the point. [614] A signal that lasted forever became a wall. [622] A signal that fell told the next person they were not the first and would not be the last. [641] He left the moth wing turning. [647] He left the red thread tied. [653] He climbed back down through the cloud, and somewhere over his shoulder, unseen, the last monarch passed, and the cadence of the world continued, interrupted, continuing, interrupted again.
