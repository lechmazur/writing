Vael had outlived seventeen empires and every friend who ever mattered. [11] Boredom, he decided, was the true immortal sickness, a hushed thunder under the ribs that never broke into rain. [30] So when the sun began to convulse and hurl its tantrum across the magnetosphere, he smiled for the first time in a century. [53] A solar storm meant the veils thinned. [60] A solar storm meant he might, at last, learn what happens after goodbye. [73] He carried only the cursed amulet fragment, a shard of black glass veined with somebody else's heartbeat. [90] It had belonged to Iren, who had whispered farewell three hundred years ago and then simply stopped existing, the way mortals do. [112] The fragment was dimensionally aware; Vael had watched it flinch from angles that geometry refused to name. [129] It knew where Iren had gone. [135] It would not say. [139] He climbed to the prismatic escondite he kept folded inside a mountain in Patagonia, a hideaway cut from a single fractured quartz, each wall refracting the others until the room contained seven of him arguing politely. [175] Here, during geomagnetic chaos, the prisms sang. [182] Tonight they did more than sing. [188] Tonight they plotted. [191] Vael felt it the moment he stepped in: a spatial mutiny, dimensions refusing their assigned stations, floors tilting against ceilings that had never asked for the argument. [218] The escondite had always been loyal, but loyalty, like empires, wore thin. [230] The cursed amulet fragment warmed in his palm, pulsing in time with the aurora outside. [245] Through the magnetic fields pouring off the storm, something spoke to him, not in words but in intrigue, the shape of a conspiracy unfolding corridor by corridor. [272] The room had been recruited. [277] Every facet of quartz now pointed toward a door that had not existed a moment ago, a seam in the air at precisely the angle the fragment kept flinching from. [307] Vael understood, with the weary delight of the truly long-lived, that the mutiny was not against him. [324] It was for him. [328] Space itself was tired of guarding the border between here and after. [340] Space itself wanted to know what lay beyond the final word, and had chosen, by whatever logic rooms use, to follow him across. [363] He pressed the fragment to the seam. [370] The storm outside doubled in fury, charged particles threading the quartz like needles through silk. [385] In the polished walls, his seven reflections turned their heads in unison and watched only him, the one about to step. [406] "Iren," he said, to test whether the name still weighed anything. [417] It did. [419] The hushed thunder in his chest cracked, finally, into something almost like weather. [432] The door opened not outward but inward, folding the escondite into a corridor of layered light, each layer a year he had spent pretending not to miss her. [460] He walked. [462] The fragment grew heavier with every step, as if collecting the weight of each goodbye it had ever chaperoned. [481] Other immortals, he knew, had tried this through ritual, through prayer, through the slow erosion of hope; none had thought to ride a magnetic tantrum into a room that had decided to defect. [514] That was the trick. [518] You could not force the crossing. [524] You had to wait until reality itself grew curious. [533] At the corridor's end stood nothing he could name, and everything he had loved. [547] Iren was not there, precisely, but the shape of her listening was, a posture the universe had preserved the way amber preserves a wing. [571] "Hello," Vael said, and felt the word travel somewhere answerable. [581] The cursed amulet fragment, its duty discharged, crumbled to salt in his hand. [594] The prismatic escondite, far behind him, collapsed into ordinary stone, its mutiny complete, its purpose spent. [610] What happens after goodbye, he learned, is not reunion and not silence, but a conversation resumed at exactly the pause where it had been left. [635] Vael, who had been bored for seventeen empires, sat down in the impossible light and, for the first time, had nothing to outlive. [658] The storm passed. [661] He did not. [664]
