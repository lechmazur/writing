<story>They called me the blessed pariah because the reactor accident that should have killed me only left me changed. [19] The technicians said I had been temporally stretched, my body smeared across three seconds while everyone else lived in one. [39] I could still walk and talk, but no one would sit beside me in the canteen, as if my unevenness might leak. [61] After the shutdown, when the great Halsey Drift Station went dark and the crews evacuated to the inner colonies, I stayed behind. [83] I had nowhere to be feared from anymore. [91] The station hung above a collapsed star, and its time dilation observation deck was the only place I had ever felt at home. [114] There, seconds outside the window stretched and curled the way I did, and for once I matched the world. [133] My grandfather had built that deck, and on the wall above its great curved port hung his old gilded compass rose applique, stitched into black canvas with thread the color of brass. [165] He used to say that true north was not a direction but a promise: wherever you were lost, something pulled. [185] I had stopped believing him on the night the reactor flared. [196] Now, after the shutdown, the corridors held only echoed silence, the kind that made footsteps sound like questions. [214] I drifted through them carrying my grandmother's leather folio, because tonight was an anniversary, and our family had a custom. [234] On every anniversary, no matter where the work had scattered us, we sketched the night sky as we found it, dating the page and signing it beneath. [261] My mother had sketched dust storms on Ceres; my father had sketched the rim of a gas giant; my grandfather had sketched the same square of stars from this very deck for forty years. [295] I opened the folio on the observation rail and uncapped the soft graphite. [308] Outside, the collapsed star bent the heavens into a lensed ring, and starlight that had left its source a thousand years ago arrived all at once, layered like pages. [337] My hand hesitated. [340] How do you sketch a sky that is also a memory of a sky? [353] I began anyway, drawing the warped constellations as my grandfather had drawn his unwarped ones, letting the curve be honest. [373] Halfway through, I lifted my eyes and caught a glimpse of something inside the lens: a faint wedge of light moving against the slow drift of the background. [401] At first I thought it was debris from the shutdown, a panel tumbling out of orbit. [417] But it held its line, and its line bent toward me. [428] I checked the deck instruments; the signal was coherent, modulated, deliberate. [439] A ship, perhaps, or a probe, threading the dilation field with a patience only an unmanned thing could afford. [458] Whoever, or whatever, had sent it had aimed for a station the rest of humanity had abandoned. [475] I looked up at the gilded compass rose, its threads catching the strange refracted light, and I understood my grandfather at last. [497] True north was not a place; it was the direction confusion pointed when you decided to follow it. [515] Below the new sketch I wrote the date, my name, and a single line: contact, bearing unknown, intent unknown, course set. [536] I would not run, and I would not call the inner colonies who had already decided I was not worth saving. [557] I would meet whatever was coming, because I was the one person left who had been stretched enough to greet it on its own time. [582] The pariah's blessing, I finally saw, was not survival; it was that strangeness no longer frightened me. [599] I closed the folio, fixed my eyes on the approaching wedge of light, and let the echoed silence of the empty station become a held breath instead of an ending. [629] Confusion, at last, had become exploration, and the dark deck around me felt less like a tomb and more like a doorway. [651]</story>
