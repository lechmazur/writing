Nadia called herself an attentive mystic, though she preferred the second word kept small. [14] For nine winters she had driven to the quartz arbor at the edge of the Haldane estate, parked her old sedan, and waited for the cold to write. [42] She came to prove what no one at the university would permit her to say aloud: that objects absorb emotions, and keep them. [65] Her evidence was a glass prism, inherited from her grandmother, who had wept into it for a decade before dying. [85] Her method was simpler and stranger. [91] She would commune through the patterns in frost on a car window, matching the feathered geometry there to the refractions the prism threw across a sheet of black paper. [120] If the two diagrams aligned, she claimed, the prism was remembering. [131] Her colleagues said this was charming nonsense dressed in the vocabulary of science. [144] Nadia answered them with truthful lies, the only kind she allowed herself: I do not believe it either, she would say, smiling, while believing every word of it. [172]

The quartz arbor in mist was the place she trusted. [182] Between its cold pillars the world went quiet in a particular way, not silence but a shifted hush, as if sound had been moved two rooms over and left humming to itself. [214] That hush was her instrument. [219] In it, small differences became audible. [225]

This winter, though, something went wrong from the first morning. [235] The frost on her windshield bloomed in spirals she had never catalogued, tight whirls that curved counter to the usual branching ferns. [257] When she set the prism in its groove and let the pale sunrise enter it, the light on her paper fell in corresponding spirals, a bright match she had not asked for. [289] It was the confirmation she had spent a decade chasing. [299] It should have made her weep. [305] Instead she sat very still, because she understood, at once, that she was about to become the kind of woman she had always pitied: one whose assumptions shatter. [333]

She had assumed she was firmly grounded, a careful reader of small signs. [346] She had assumed her grandmother's grief was the source of the prism's memory. [359] She had assumed the car window was a neutral page, waiting for cold to speak through it. [376] But the spirals in the frost were her own. [385] She recognized them now, the shape her breath made at the end of an argument, the way her fingers curled when she was lying, even to herself, about why she kept returning here. [418] The prism had not absorbed her grandmother's sorrow. [426] It had absorbed hers. [430]

Every winter she had driven out, carried the cold object close to her chest, laid her loneliness against its faces, and then gone home believing she was studying an artifact. [460] The artifact had been studying her. [466]

In the arbor the shifted hush deepened. [473] Mist moved between the quartz columns like slow breath. [482] Nadia held the prism in her gloved palm and felt, for the first time, that her theory had been a kind of confession she was too proud to make plainly. [512] Objects absorb emotions, yes. [516] Also: the person who insists on it is usually the one bleeding into them. [530]

She walked back to the car with the paper still bright in her hand. [544] The frost had begun to melt; the spirals ran, softened, became ordinary water. [557] She did not wipe the glass. [563] She let the ferns return on their own schedule, which was, she now understood, the schedule of a world that did not require her explanation. [588]

At the university the following week, a colleague asked, lightly, whether she had found what she was looking for. [607] Nadia smiled the way she always smiled. [614] No, she said, nothing came of it. [621] It was the truest lie she had ever told, and she carried it carefully, the way one carries a prism: firmly grounded, aware, at last, of what it held. [650]
