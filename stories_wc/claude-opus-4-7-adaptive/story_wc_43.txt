Mira, the wing's serene music therapist, kept the shard of vinyl in her cardigan pocket, wrapped in wax paper like a communion wafer. [23] It had belonged to Edwin once, before it belonged to grief. [34] On the fourteenth floor of St. Bartholomew's, where the hospice wing floated in a radiant haven above the clouds, she pressed the broken piece flat against her palm and listened for its old music. [68] Edwin had been a record-shop man for forty-one years. [77] He had also been a father for thirty-three, until the argument that ended with a Sinatra pressing snapped in half across a kitchen counter. [157] She was decisively kind, which was different from softly kind; she believed dying people deserved competence, not only tenderness.
