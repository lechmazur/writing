Orla called herself a myth buster, though the title had grown threadbare after twenty years of debunking ghost lights and mermaid bones. [22] She had come to the abandoned observatory on a cliff because a colleague's letter insisted the place hid a universal riddle, and Orla, widowed and bored, needed a puzzle more than she needed peace. [56] The brass dome leaked. [60] Gulls nested in the refractor's tube. [66] Rain drummed a cheerful funeral on the copper roof. [75]

She set her kit on the astronomer's old workbench: notebook, voltmeter, and the painted seashell her late husband Pieter had given her on their first anniversary, its inner spiral lacquered with tiny constellations he had invented for her. [113] She kept it as ballast, not superstition. [120]

In the corner stood a half-finished cello, its ribs glued but unvarnished, two strings short. [135] Pieter had been building it when the stroke took him. [145] She had carried it here because leaving it at home felt worse than hauling it up a cliff. [162]

The riddle, according to the letter, concerned an ephemeral compass, a direction-finder that pointed not north but toward whatever the seeker had forgotten to ask. [187] Orla snorted. [189] She had debunked thirty compasses already. [195] Still, she unpacked. [198]

On the third night, reading Pieter's marginalia in a cracked edition of Kepler, she noticed the book was nearly finished; only a few pages remained. [223] Inside the whisper of closing pages she heard a low hum, not wind, not her pulse. [239] The cello was resonating. [243]

She crossed the room. [247] When she pressed her palm to its belly, the hum shaped itself into syllables she recognized. [263] Her own voice. [266] Pieter's voice. [268] A conversation they had had in a kitchen twelve Aprils ago, about whether stars could lie. [284]

She laughed, because crying seemed redundant in a place this damp. [295] The myth buster in her catalogued the acoustics: standing waves, sympathetic vibration, the cliff's basalt acting as a sounding board. [315] The widow in her listened. [320]

Each night after, the cello hummed a different remembered talk. [330] But they were not quite as they had happened. [339] The cello reimagined past conversations, softening Pieter's stubborn edges, sharpening her own regrets, offering alternate replies she wished she had given. [360] It was not haunting. [364] It was rehearsal. [367]

She began to retell the conversations aloud, matching the cello's pitch, correcting herself toward kinder versions. [383] The painted seashell, set on the workbench, rattled faintly each time she landed on a truer phrase, as if Pieter's invented constellations approved. [406]

On the seventh night she understood the riddle. [414] The ephemeral compass was not an object but a method: a brief orientation given by any honest remembering, pointing toward the question one had failed to ask the living. [443] Hers had been simple; she had never asked Pieter what he wanted the cello to sound like. [460]

She asked it now, into the unfinished ribs. [468] The hum answered with a tone she would not have chosen, warmer and more uncertain, and she wrote the tuning down. [489]

By dawn the compass was gone, the way dew is gone. [500] That was the nature of ephemeral things; they did their work and left. [513] The seashell sat quiet. [517] The cello sat quiet. [521] The Kepler book lay closed, the whisper of its final page already part of the observatory's dust. [538]

Orla packed slowly, with the gloomy cheerfulness of someone who has lost an argument to the universe and found the loss clarifying. [560] She would go home. [564] She would finish the cello to the tuning Pieter had wanted, not the one she had assumed. [581] She would write the report for her colleague honestly: no fraud, no ghost, only a room shaped like a question and a widow shaped like an answer. [608]

At the door she looked back at the brass dome, the leaking ceiling, the gulls. [623] A myth buster's job, she decided, was not always to dismantle. [634] Sometimes it was to stand inside the mechanism and admit, without embarrassment, that meaning had visited. [650]

She tucked the painted seashell into her coat. [658] The constellations on its inner spiral glinted, small and invented and entirely sufficient. [671] Then she carried the half-finished cello down the cliff path, humming the new tuning, letting the wind retell it to the sea. [693]
