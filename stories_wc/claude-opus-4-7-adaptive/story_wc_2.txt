Mira repaired broken clocks for a living, though none of hers ever kept correct time. [15] That was the joke locals told, and she laughed along, because a broken-clock tinkerer who trusted her own schedules would have been a liar. [39] Still, she planned everything. [43] Her mornings began with lists, her nights ended with revisions, and each revision birthed a smaller list beneath it, like roots under roots. [66] She wanted, quietly, to befriend uncertainty in planning, but did not yet know how to start a conversation with it. [86] The coincidences began in April. [91] A sparrow tapped her window at 7:14, then at 7:14 the next day, then again. [106] A customer brought in a brass pocket watch stopped at 7:14. [117] Her grandfather's logbook, which she had not opened in years, fell from a shelf to the page dated April 14, entry 7. [139] Mira was uncertainly certain that when coincidence becomes pattern, a person is being asked to pay attention. [156] She followed the pattern the way she followed blueprints, but the pattern refused blueprints. [170] It pulled her, on the fourth sparrow-morning, through the swirling leaves in a neglected greenhouse behind her grandfather's old workshop. [190] The panes were green with moss; ferns had climbed the rafters and dropped their brown curls across the tiles. [209] A draft she could not account for lifted the leaves into a slow gyre, and the gyre leaned, unmistakably, toward a trapdoor she had never noticed. [235] She descended. [237] The stair was older than the house, older than the town, wet with a breath that smelled of iron. [256] At its bottom lay a chamber lit by no lamp she could see, where a subterranean waterfall fell from a crack in the stone and vanished into another crack without sound. [287] The silence of that water was the loudest thing Mira had ever heard. [300] On a flat stone between the falls sat a heap of fine gray dust, and beneath the ashes something glinted. [320] She brushed them aside and lifted out the admiral's lost compass rose. [332] She knew it at once from her grandfather's stories: the brass disc engraved with thirty-two points, gifted to Admiral Hale before his last voyage, assumed drowned with him. [360] The needle was missing. [364] In its place a tiny socket waited, exactly the size of the smallest screwdriver she carried. [380] Her impulse was to plan: catalog the artifact, research the socket, schedule a restoration. [394] But the water made its unsound sound, and the ashes stirred as if breathing, and she understood that the room did not want a plan. [419] It wanted her to improvise. [424] Mira unhooked a hairpin she had bent into a lockpick months ago for a client's music box. [441] She slid it into the socket and turned, feeling for the tension a spring makes when it wishes to be released. [462] The compass rose hummed. [466] The thirty-two points brightened one by one in no order she could predict, and on each pulse she felt the shape of a different possible afternoon: one where she stayed, one where she fled, one where she dropped the disc into the falls and never spoke of it. [514] None of the futures argued. [519] They simply offered themselves, like cards fanned on a table, and she saw that the admiral had not been lost; he had been choosing. [543] Choosing, and choosing, and learning to let the choice remain open. [554] Mira laughed, a small laugh, the first unscheduled sound of her week. [566] She pocketed the compass rose. [571] Climbing back through the trapdoor, through the greenhouse, through the swirling leaves that now settled as if their errand was complete, she let her morning list stay unwritten. [599] She would open the shop late. [605] She would repair whichever clock arrived first, and if none arrived, she would sit in the doorway and watch the sparrow. [626] The pattern had said its piece. [632] Now the uncertainty was hers to keep, a small brass friend, humming thirty-two quiet points beneath her coat, telling her nothing and everything about where to go next. [660]
