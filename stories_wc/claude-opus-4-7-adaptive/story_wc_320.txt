Mira climbed the ladder with a wrench between her teeth and a kettle clipped to her belt. [17] She was a celestial signal interpreter, which sounded grander than it paid. [29] Her job, in plain terms, was to listen to whatever the sky bothered to say and write it down legibly for the council clerks below. [54] Tonight she worked in the abandoned radio tower city suspended over the Vell Gorge, where rust-iron spires swayed on cables thick as a man's thigh. [79] The towers had been stitched together a century ago by engineers who believed altitude improved reception; the engineers were gone, the belief remained, and Mira inherited their drafts and their drafts of wind. [112] Lunar perigee was due before midnight. [118] The moon, fat and close, would press the tides of static the way a thumb presses dough, and any signal worth catching would surface for an hour, no longer. [147] She had one hour, then, to tune the lattice of antennae to a frequency no living interpreter had cracked. [166] In her satchel sat the reason: a hollywood star fragment, a chip of pink terrazzo someone had pried from the Walk of Fame before the western cities drowned. [194] It had washed up at the gorge market, and an old broker swore it sang at perigee. [211] Mira didn't believe in singing rocks. [217] She believed in measurable resonance, and she believed her sister's last transmission, six months silent now, had ended with a tone she could not place. [242] To find the beginning in every ending: that was the interpreter's first axiom, and the only sentiment she still trusted. [262] If the fragment hummed, perhaps the hum would name the silence that swallowed Ana. [276] She slotted the chip into the dish's brass cradle. [285] The cables overhead twanged in the wind like loose harp strings, serenely electric, lit blue along their lengths by St. Elmo's gentle approval. [308] She liked that color; it meant the air was honest tonight. [319] Below, the gorge exhaled mist that smelled faintly of iron and old broadcasts. [332] Mira spun the dial. [336] Static, static, the chatter of weather satellites long abandoned, the polite muttering of a dead opera relay. [353] Nothing matched her notes. [357] She checked the perigee clock: forty-one minutes left. [365] Her assumptions were elegant and useless: that Ana's final signal was modulated, that frequency mattered more than silence, that the fragment was a transmitter. [389] Her teacher had warned her about elegance. [396] The way through, the old woman had said, is by discarding assumptions, one by one, until what remains is light enough to lift. [419] Mira lowered the kettle from her belt, poured tea into a tin cup, and considered the fragment. [436] What if it was not a transmitter but a receiver? [446] What if the signal she sought was not a sound but a gap, a held breath in the noise? [465] She reversed the dish's polarity, an inelegant thing, and rerouted the lattice to hunt for absences instead of pulses. [484] The towers groaned. [487] The moon climbed its closest rung. [493] And then, between two unrelated stations, a small clean nothing opened up, no wider than a fingernail on the spectrum: a shard of hush. [517] Mira held very still. [521] The hush had texture, the way a cellar has temperature. [531] Inside it, faint as a pulse felt through a wrist: her sister's breathing, paced exactly as she had paced it as a child counting fireflies. [556] Not a recording. [559] A presence, somewhere on the other side of the silence, waiting to be located. [573] Mira logged the coordinates with a steady hand, because grief without grammar is just weather. [588] She drank her tea. [592] The hush held. [595] It would close at perigee's end, but she had measurements now, and a route, and a fragment that knew which silence was the right one. [620] Endings, she wrote in the margin of her ledger, are doorways with the hinges on the other side. [638] She would need rope, a second kettle, and someone willing to mind the dish while she walked into the quiet to fetch her sister back. [663] The gorge wind nudged the towers. [669] The blue light along the cables dimmed, satisfied. [677] Mira packed her tools with a practical reverence, the way a midwife packs hers, and began the long climb down to hire a partner before dawn. [703]
