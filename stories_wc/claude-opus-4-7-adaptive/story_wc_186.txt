Ilse kept the beacon-tower alone, which was the only job left worth keeping after the collapse of civilization. [20] The cities below had gone to moss and rumor, and the coast roads were now walked mostly by wind. [37] Still, the lamp at the tower's crown burned every night, because someone long ago had promised it would, and Ilse inherited promises the way other people inherited debts. [66]

She was retrocognitively blessed, which sounded grander than it felt. [77] When she touched an old thing, she saw what it had already witnessed: a spoon remembering soup, a hinge remembering a quarrel, a stair remembering the last foot that climbed it. [107] The gift made her excellent at custodianship and poor at sleep. [118]

On the ninety-second night of her solitude, a bird struck the lamp-glass and dropped a bundle at her feet. [137] Inside was a broken crystal prism, split clean through its heart, still faintly warm. [151] She lifted it, and memory poured into her palm. [160]

She saw the prism in a glass humming pavilion she had never visited, a dome of blown panes set on a mountain where wind passed through grooves in the walls and made the whole structure sing. [194] Astronomers had worked there once, tracking star paths across the inner glass. [207] The prism had been their instrument, splitting starlight into readable ladders. [218] She saw the last astronomer, a thin woman with ink on her cuffs, cradle the prism as the sky thickened with the smoke of the collapse, and whisper: "If you ever find someone to finish this, tell them coincidences are not coincidences." [257]

Then the vision broke with the crystal. [264]

Ilse sat on the tower steps for a long while, turning the halves over. [278] She had always wanted a reason to believe that the small accidents of her life—the bird, the bundle, the promise, the lamp—were more than stray weather. [304] She wanted, quietly and stubbornly, to prove that coincidences have meaning. [316] The astronomer's whisper felt like a door cracked open. [325]

So she left the beacon, for the first time in a decade, lamp stoked and shuttered wide. [342] She walked north with the prism in a linen pouch against her ribs. [355]

The pavilion was where memory said it would be: a blown-glass ribcage humming faintly even in the stillest hour. [375] Inside, the floor was tiled with old star-charts, and the inner dome was etched with faint silver tracks, the star paths the astronomers had recorded. [400] Dust moved in them like slow fish. [407]

At the pavilion's center stood an empty brass cradle, prism-shaped. [418] Ilse understood. [420] The instrument had to be whole to rekindle the work. [429] But the crystal was broken, and she had no forge, no glassworker, no hope of true repair. [445]

She pressed the two halves together anyway and held them to the cradle's mouth. [459]

Something inside the pavilion shifted. [464] A shard of hush slipped from between the halves—not silence, but the small specific quiet that lives at the center of meaningful things, a pocket of stillness older than sound. [495] It settled into the seam of the broken prism like solder. [506] The crystal did not mend; it simply agreed to act as though it had. [519]

Light from the rising stars entered the dome, struck the mended prism, and laddered out across the etched tracks. [538] Ilse watched the old star paths brighten one by one, and saw, in their pattern, the exact route the bird had flown to reach her tower. [564] The bird had not been lost. [570] The astronomer had not been lost. [576] Every accident, traced back, was a line drawn on purpose. [586]

She stood a long time under that quiet proof, thistle-blown hope drifting through her chest like seed-down on a warm slope. [608] The collapse had taken the cities, but not the geometry beneath them. [620] Someone, perhaps, still needed the ladders read. [628]

Ilse left the prism in its cradle and the shard of hush to hold it. [643] She walked back to her beacon with her coat full of wind, already composing the first message she would flash from the lamp that night—a pattern borrowed from a star path, aimed at whoever, in the ruined dark, might be retrocognitively blessed enough to read it. [693]

The light went up at dusk. [699] Somewhere, she trusted, another promise was beginning to answer. [709]
