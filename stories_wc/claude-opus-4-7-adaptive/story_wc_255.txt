In the glassblower's courtyard, where slag glittered like spilled stars, Mira set up her easel after the phoenix rises—the hour each dawn when the kiln's first breath turned the sky to copper. [33] She was a colorblind painter who had stopped pretending otherwise. [43] Her motivation was simple and stubborn: to find beauty in imperfection, the only beauty she could be sure she saw. [63]

The courtyard belonged to old Pieter, who blew bottles for apothecaries and sometimes for sorrows. [78] He had taken Mira in after her teachers turned her away, and he asked only that she tend the herb beds that grew between the cooling racks. [105] She did this every morning, arranging rosemary, sage, and bitter rue in patterns that he read as easily as letters. [125]

Communicating through plant arrangements was their long habit. [133] A sprig of yarrow crossed with mint meant a buyer was coming. [145] A circle of basil meant rest the furnace. [153] Mira could not read his colored signal flags, so the herbs had become their shared tongue, fragrant and exact. [172]

That morning she arranged thyme into a question mark around a single dried iris. [186] Pieter, passing with tongs, paused and frowned. [193]

"You want to know about the trader," he said. [202]

She nodded. [204]

The trader was the reason for the courtyard's recent unease. [214] He arrived weekly with a leather case and a smile that seemed innocently cunning—a child's grin worn by a man who had learned exactly which adults it disarmed. [243] He bought broken things: cracked decanters, warped lenses, and once a concertina with a split bellows. [259] From his case he drew small vials, each labeled with a sleeper's name. [272]

"Bartered awakenings," Pieter had explained the first time. [280] "He trades a little of someone's waking life for our flawed glass. [292] He sells the wakefulness to insomniacs in the city." [301]

Mira had thought it nonsense until she watched a bargain made. [312] The trader pressed a vial to a buyer's lips; the buyer drank and laughed like someone meeting morning for the first time. [334] Somewhere, she knew, a stranger lay deeper in dream, traded down for coin. [347]

Today the trader came at noon, his case heavier than usual. [358] He wanted Pieter's failed pieces, the ones with bubbles and sag. [369] Pieter refused. [371] "Those are hers now," he said, jerking his chin at Mira's canvases, where each crooked vessel had been painted larger than life, their flaws luminous. [396]

The trader turned his grin on Mira. [403] "Painter. [404] I have something you'd want." [409] From his case he drew a slim brass tine wrapped in felt: a concertina reed tongue, polished and singing-true. [428] "Pluck it and hear the note. [434] It vibrates at the pitch your eyes cannot tell you. [444] Match your pigments to its hum, and you will paint as the sighted do." [458]

Mira held the reed. [462] It was warm. [465] It hummed when she breathed across it, and within the hum she felt, dizzily, the temptation of a corrected world. [485]

"What do you want for it?" she asked. [493]

"Only one awakening. [496] A small one. [499] Yours, on a morning of my choosing." [506]

She looked at Pieter. [510] He would not meet her eyes; he would not bargain her sleep for her. [524] The choice was hers. [528]

She thought of her motivation, the vow she had made when the academy had laughed at her muddied skies: to find beauty in imperfection. [552] A reed that promised correction was a reed that promised her work would no longer be hers. [569]

Mira knelt by the herb bed. [575] She arranged hyssop and broken lavender into a clear refusal—a closed spiral, the sign Pieter used to mean no buyer today. [597] Then she handed the reed back. [603]

"Keep your tongue," she said. [608] "I have one already." [612]

The trader's grin slipped, just a flicker, the cunning showing through the innocence. [625] He bowed and left. [629]

After the phoenix rises tomorrow, Mira will paint again, mismatched and certain. [641] Pieter will blow a bottle with a deliberate flaw at its lip, and she will set it on her sill where the copper light can awaken its small, lopsided glory. [671] Between them, in the herb bed, a sprig of rosemary will lean against a stem of marjoram: the arrangement that means, in their wordless grammar, we are still ourselves. [700]
