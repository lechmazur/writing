<story>Mira called herself a secretive conjurer because the word "mathematician" frightened the villagers more than magic did. [17] She lived beneath the hill, in a cavern orchard nourished by dripping stalactites, where pale apples grew on trees that had forgotten the sun. [41] Every night, after the orchard grew silent beneath the moon that pressed its face to the sinkhole above, she began her real work. [64] Her real work was to understand the mathematics of emotion. [74] Grief, she suspected, had a shape, and if she could graph it, she could return something to the woman upstairs whose husband had simply... not come home, not died, not left, just ceased in a way that left no edges. [114] This was ambiguous loss, the widow Halbren called it, using the term a travelling doctor had once offered her like a wrapped sweet. [137] Mira kept the sweet and meant to unwrap it. [145] Her tools were gently inventive: a slate the size of a door, a protractor made from a deer's rib, and, balanced on the altar of her desk, a taxidermist's glass eye pair she had bought from an estate sale of a man who had stuffed foxes for kings. [193] The eyes were hazel and too knowing, and she used them to check her work: if a proof was wrong, the eyes would not hold her gaze. [220] Tonight she faced the hardest equation: how to dovetail two absences, the widow's and her own. [236] Her brother had walked into the cavern orchard a decade ago and never walked out, though no body was ever found among the stalactite roots. [261] She had learned, eventually, to read him via the chipped patterns on a mosaic stepping stone he had laid himself at the orchard's heart. [285] The chips were not random. [290] Where his boot had struck it that last evening, before the hush, the pattern had cracked into something like a Fibonacci spiral broken mid-turn, and Mira had spent ten years convinced the break was a sentence. [326] Tonight, with the widow's grief laid on her slate as coordinates, she noticed the two shapes rhymed. [343] Apples plinked into pools. [347] Water beaded from the ceiling and struck the mosaic in measured intervals, as if a slow metronome had been left running for her. [370] She set the glass eye pair on either side of the broken spiral, so that whatever watched would watch from both directions. [392] Then she wrote the equation she had been avoiding: let x be what is not here; let y be what might return; let the distance between them be called hope, and solve. [424] The slate stayed blank. [428] But the chipped mosaic, under the drip, began to shed mineral crust, and beneath the crust the pattern was not broken after all; it was folded, like a love letter slipped under a door. [462] Her brother had not left a sentence. [469] He had left a question, and the question was the same one the widow carried: can a person be measured by the room they still take up? [496] Mira laughed, a small laugh that the stalactites caught and multiplied. [507] The glass eyes held her gaze. [513] In the morning she would climb the ladder to the widow Halbren and say, I cannot give you an answer, but I can give you a shape, and it is not a hole; it is a pocket. [550] Pockets, she would explain gently, are made to hold things that are coming back, or to hold the memory of them as if the two were, mathematically, equivalent. [578] It was not proof, but it was optimism, buried as seed under ten winters of stone. [594] She gathered the glass eye pair, pressed them into her pocket, and, for the first time since her brother had vanished, she took an apple from the silent orchard and ate it, counting seeds, feeling the dovetail of his absence and her own going quietly, privately, beautifully closed. [642]</story>
