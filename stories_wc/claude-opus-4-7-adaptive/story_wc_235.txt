<story>The ranger cabin smelled of cedar smoke and wet wool, a scent I recognized before I remembered my own name. [20] On the table lay a birch bark scroll, curled at the edges like a sleeping animal, and I understood, with the jaded wonder that had become my only weather, that I had left it there for myself. [57] I am a lucid dream chronicler. [63] That is not a profession one advertises. [70] It means I walk through sleep the way mountain rangers walk through snow: slowly, marking trees, noting where the avalanches have been. [193] My handwriting is chronologically confused, which is the occupational hazard of my trade; the ink from next winter bleeds into last summer, and paragraphs argue with their own beginnings. [222] I read about a boy who could soar in his dreams, lifting off pine canopies with ribs made of laughter. [242] I read about a man at thirty-two who traded that flight for a mortgage he does not remember signing. [261] I read about tonight, the cabin, the scroll, the reader, all written in a hand I have not yet learned to forge. [283] Here is the trouble with chronicling your own sleep: you begin to envy the dreamer. [298] The boy soaring above the pines does not know what the scroll costs. [495] Outside, an owl declared itself and was answered by nothing, which is how most declarations end up here. [513] I wrote the boy down first, because he was loudest. [572] That is what chronicling does, when it works: the thing that pressed against you becomes a thing you can walk through.
