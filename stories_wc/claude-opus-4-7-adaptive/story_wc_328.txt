<story>I took the job at Miriam's apothecary because cryptid researchers need cover, and a woman who sells valerian and yarrow in a frost-bitten mountain town asks fewer questions than a landlord. [31] My real assignment was the creature locals called the Return, a thing that vanished children and gave them back wrong. [51] Then, after the missing return changed -- after the last boy came home humming a song that made gravity misbehave in the kitchen -- my supervisors stopped answering their radios. [79] Miriam found me that night in her cellar, where the real shop lives: a herbalist's underground garden lit by honey-colored bulbs, rows of mugwort and moonwort climbing trellises rooted in black soil. [111] She pressed a flint arrowhead into my palm, pale and cold as a tooth. [125] "The boy spit it out," she said. "Older than any of us. Older than the Return." [141] I should have reported it up the chain, but the chain had fallen apart, and I had been bitter for months about being sent to die at the end of a dirt road. [174] Miriam watched me, patient as root rot. [181] "You want to know what it is," she said, and handed me a cordless phone so old its antenna bent like a broken finger. [205] The number was already dialing. [210] What followed came via the static-laden confessions on an old cordless phone -- voices I recognized and voices I didn't, all overlapping, all admitting things. [234] My supervisor confessed he'd known the creature was not hostile; it was lonely. [247] A mother confessed her son had not been stolen; he had chosen. [259] A voice I eventually recognized as my own confessed that I had joined this work to outrun a grief I would not name. [282] Between the confessions ran music -- not a melody exactly, but a harmony untamed, voices of stone and child and wind braided together without a conductor. [307] The arrowhead in my hand began to hum in reply. [317] Miriam smiled like someone who had waited a long, cold winter for this exact sound. [332] "The Return doesn't take children," she said. "It teaches them the old song. They come back changed because the song changes them." [354] "Your agency was hunting a teacher." [360] I thought of the boy in the kitchen, his gravitationally playful steps lifting him half an inch off the linoleum whenever he laughed. [383] He had not come back wrong. [389] He had come back lighter. [394] I asked Miriam what I was supposed to do now, with my dead agency and my living informant and a flint older than the glaciers. [419] She told me the choice was small and enormous: stay bitter, or metamorphose. [432] She said it the way a gardener says prune. [441] So I sat among the moonwort and listened to the phone until dawn, letting each stranger's confession fold into mine. [461] Somewhere between the third and fourth hour, my resentment unhooked itself from my ribs. [475] I began, very quietly, to laugh. [481] The agency had sent me to kill a teacher of joy; the universe had sent me to become her student. [501] If that wasn't the universe's way of turning bitterness into a cosmic joke, nothing was. [516] At sunrise Miriam brought tea brewed from a root I could not identify. [529] She said the Return would come for me soon, not to take me, but to see whether I was ready to carry a verse. [553] I held the flint arrowhead to my chest and felt it warm like an ember that had been waiting centuries for a willing lung. [577] Outside, winter was still winter -- trees glassed in ice, sky the color of a held breath -- but something inside me had opened its small, improbable window. [603] Call it frostbitten hope. [607] Call it the first note of a song I finally wanted to learn. [620]</story>
