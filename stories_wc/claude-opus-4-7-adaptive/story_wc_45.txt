<story>After the substitute body was found in the orchard — straw-stuffed, wax-faced, still warm in a way wax shouldn't be — Mireille stopped pretending she knew what the world was made of. [30] She had been a forgiving skeptic all her life: the sort of woman who doubted miracles but never punished them for disappointing her. [53] When her son Orin disappeared at seven and returned at seven and a half wrong, she had forgiven the wrongness, believing he was only growing into a stranger the way all children do. [86] Twelve years of that forgiving. [91] Twelve years of loving a boy whose laugh never quite landed where his mouth was. [106] Then the gardeners cut back the hawthorn and found the other Orin, the wax one, curled under roots like a discarded draft. [128] Mireille did not scream. [132] She walked, instead, through the hedge maze her grandmother had planted, the one everyone in the village had long forgotten how to solve. [155] Mireille remembered. [157] At the center waited the secret garden: a round green room walled in yew, roofed in sky, furnished with a single stone bench and a sundial whose gnomon had been broken and mended so many times it looked like a healed femur. [199] The changeling was already there. [204] He sat cross-legged, shrewdly innocent, the way only not-quite-children can be — eyes too clear, hands too still, a smile calibrated to whatever it thought you wanted. [230] "You knew I'd come," she said. [236] "I yearn for you to come," he answered, and the phrase was too old for him and too honest. [255] "Every evening." [257] "I have practiced the feeling until it fits." [265] She sat. [267] Between them on the bench lay a baby tooth, small as a rice grain and pearled with something that wasn't quite enamel — the changeling's own, fallen out that morning. [296] It was the first human thing he had ever produced. [306] "They found him," she said. [311] "I know." [313] "Was he alive in there?" [318] "All this time?" [321] The changeling's face did the thing faces did when they were made, not grown — a flicker, a seam. [339] "He was sleeping." [342] "I was keeping him." [346] "I didn't know how to put him back without breaking the place he came from." [361] Mireille discovered she had been holding a cleft in her certainty for twelve years like a stone in her shoe, and now, at last, she could set it down. [390] She had loved something that wasn't her son. [398] She had also, somehow, loved her son through it — the way light loves a window without caring about the glass. [418] "What do we do," she said, "now that the truth has a body?" [431] The changeling picked up the tooth. [437] "Bone setting," he said. [441] "That's what my people call it." [447] "When a thing heals crooked, you break it again, gently, and guide it straight." [461] "The orchard boy can be woken." [467] "I can be unmade." [471] "Or —" and here his shrewd innocence cracked into something almost hopeful "— we can try a third bone." [488] "A new shape neither of us was born into." [497] "You'd stop being my son." [502] "I was never your son." [507] "I was your mistake, kept warm." [513] She thought about this. [517] About her grandmother planting the maze to hide a place where impossible conversations could happen. [532] About how every generation of her family had inherited a different grief and tried, poorly, to turn mistakes into constellations — to arrange the wrongs into patterns that could at least guide the next traveler home. [567] "Teach me the bone setting," she said. [574] He placed the tooth in her palm. [581] It was warm. [584] It smelled faintly of milk and iron and the inside of a fox's den. [598] "Plant it," he said. [602] "Here, at the sundial's foot." [607] "What grows will tell us which Orin to keep, or whether to weave them." [621] She dug with her fingers. [626] The soil accepted the tooth like a tongue accepting a word. [637] Above them the sky turned the particular violet of old photographs, and Mireille felt the nostalgia of an innovation she was only now inventing: a way of being a mother to something the world had no name for. [675] "I forgive you," she said, meaning the changeling, meaning her grandmother, meaning herself. [688] The sundial's mended gnomon cast a shadow that pointed, for the first time in a century, at something other than the hour. [710]</story>
