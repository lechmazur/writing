In the forgotten orchard deep in a bamboo forest, Mira worked her loom beneath pear trees nobody had tended since her mother's mother. [23] She was a wistful loom spinner, and the canes around her clacked like patient teeth. [38] Her husband, Ren, had walked out of this orchard six winters ago to fight a war in the lowlands, and he had not come back. [63] No letters reached here anymore; the roads had forgotten the way through the bamboo. [77] Only the hush of time's unraveling tapestry touched these rows now, a raveling silence that grew thicker with every bloom and every fallen pear. [101] Mira wove anyway, because weaving was the only way she knew to speak. [114] She needed to carry a message across impossible distance, across trench and rumor and the gray mouth of whatever war still chewed on her husband. [139] For months she had tried threads dyed in persimmon, in ash, in bamboo lye, and each bolt unraveled itself overnight, as if the orchard itself refused her grief. [167] Then, in early spring, she walked the old footpath to the village rummage sale her niece had set up beside the temple wall. [190] There, inside a cardboard box marked FIFTY YEN, she found a jigsaw puzzle, edges soft from damp, pieces missing, the picture long forgotten. [213] Mira did not want the picture. [219] She wanted the edges. [223] All night, under a single lamp, she trimmed the straight cardboard borders and soaked them in rice paste until they softened into ribbon. [246] Through jigsaw puzzle edges found in a rummage sale box, she would build a frame that could not unravel, a border stubborn enough to hold her weaving still. [274] At dawn she returned to the orchard, strung the new warp, and let the shuttle find its old, honest song. [294] This time the threads held. [299] Into the weft she spoke everything she could not mail: the sound of the bamboo when wind ran through it, the taste of green plums, the name he had given their unborn child. [332] At the center of the cloth she knotted a single tree sap amber bead, a bead she had carried in her sleeve since girlhood, the only thing she owned that had once been alive and was now forever. [370] Inside its honey-colored pane, an ant stood stoically sparkling, caught mid-stride a hundred thousand mornings ago. [386] She believed the ant understood impossible distance better than any courier. [397] When the cloth was finished, Mira did not send it. [407] She could not; no road went where Ren had gone. [417] Instead she climbed the tallest bamboo at the orchard's eastern edge and tied the weaving to its crown, where the wind had a thousand mouths. [442] The orchard held its breath. [447] She felt something inside her chest loosen, a thread she had spun her whole life suddenly given freely, and she understood that this was the cost: to send a message across such a distance, one must put oneself into the sending. [488] Latent sacrifice, her grandmother had called it, the kind paid quietly, long before anyone noticed the loom was empty. [507] Mira walked home lighter, and smaller, and more translucent. [516] Weeks passed. [518] The bamboo groaned; the pears ripened and fell; the raveling silence began, at last, to rewind itself, thread by thread, into something that almost resembled listening. [544] One evening, as dusk combed the canes, a traveler limped into the orchard with a bandaged hand and a face she had memorized decades before. [569] Ren carried nothing but a scrap of cloth he had found snagged on a mountain pass a thousand miles north, and in its center, still warm, a tree sap amber bead. [600] He did not speak. [604] He did not need to. [609] Through the hush of time's unraveling tapestry, the loom in the corner began, on its own, to recommence. [627]
