<story>After the oasis dried, Veyen moved her ink pots into the glass ward of the greenhouse city and told everyone she was fine. [23] She was not fine. [27] A gilded-leaf calligrapher is a peculiar tenant of the domes, for her paints are alive; she wilts near dishonesty the way orchids wilt near salt. [52] Her own lie therefore yellowed the edge of her left hand before the sentence had finished leaving her mouth. [71] Under the toxic sky, the greenhouse city sprawled in panes and pulleys, every street a nave of green light, every citizen a gardener of something. [96] Veyen kept to the postal quarter, where old letters went to be read one last time before the damp dissolved them. [117] She had come for the letters, yes, but more honestly, she had come to find where lost conversations go. [136] Somewhere, she believed, there had to be a reservoir of all the words people meant to say and didn't. [155] Her brother's voice was in that reservoir; the oasis had taken him before either of them apologized. [172] In the third week she found a zinc-etched postal plate fragment lodged in the drain of a fern trough, green-furred, the engraved address half-eaten by rust. [198] It bore the seal of the Quiet Office, a postal bureau no one had acknowledged in decades. [215] She cleaned the plate with a sable brush and set it against the light. [229] The numerals pointed to a warehouse on the city's eastern rim, a greenhouse long since given over to tomatoes and rumor. [250] When she went, the caretaker there - a boy with lungs bandaged against the sky - lied about having a key. [269] Veyen felt her wrist blanch; the leaf of her sleeve curled. [280] "Try again," she said softly, and because she had stopped pretending, the boy did too. [295] He fetched the key from inside his shoe. [303] That was the first small candor of the search, and it fed her like water. [318] Inside the warehouse, under loose floorboards webbed with moss, she found a shredded treasure map - not of gold, but of routes the Quiet Office had once walked to collect unsent letters from the city's ventilation shafts. [354] She took the pieces home and spent a week piecing together the shredded map found under the floorboards, using tweezers and breath and patience. [378] Each fragment she rejoined against the zinc plate's broken edge, which fit one corner like a signature. [395] When the map was whole, it showed a dry well at the city's heart, directly beneath the atrium where calligraphers once lettered marriage vows. [419] She went at dusk, when the toxic sky glowed the color of steeped tea. [433] The well was indeed dry, the oasis gone from it years before the surface one had quit. [450] But at the bottom, the air was thick with whispering - a low, layered murmur like pages turning in another room. [470] She climbed down with a lantern and her plate and her brush. [482] The walls were papered with unsent letters, some folded, some only half-written, all of them pinned by the weight of their own reluctance. [505] Here, she understood, was where lost conversations went: not destroyed, only postponed. [517] She found her brother's handwriting on a sheet no bigger than a leaf. [530] It said only, "I was wrong about the garden, and I missed you that summer." [545] She would reread that line for years. [552] She took down nothing; instead she wrote back, in gilded leaf, on the plaster beside it: "I missed you too, and I am not fine, but I am here." [581] Her sleeve did not curl. [586] When she climbed out, the boy caretaker was waiting with a thermos. [598] "You look greener," he said, which was the smallest true thing anyone had told her since the oasis dried. [617] She thanked him, meaning it. [622] After that, Veyen opened a narrow shop in the postal quarter where citizens could come and say the one thing they had meant to say, and she would letter it in gold onto zinc, and file it in the well below. [662] She charged very little. [666] Sometimes, when a visitor tried to rehearse a kinder version of the truth, her cuff would begin to yellow, and she would look up and wait. [692] They always tried again. [696] The sky stayed toxic, the oasis stayed gone, but inside the glass, small candors accumulated like dew. [713] That, she decided, was enough of a reservoir to live beside. [724]</story>
