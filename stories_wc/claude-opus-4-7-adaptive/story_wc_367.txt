The last print newspaper had gone to pulp three winters ago, and still Bex climbed the emerald lighthouse every dusk to polish a lamp no ship required. [27] The routine was otherworldly in its stubbornness, a liturgy kept for no congregation but the stars. [43] She trimmed the glass lantern wick with tweezers her grandmother had used, and she spoke aloud the headlines she invented to fill the silence. [67] Tonight's edition: Storm expected from the northeast; mariners warned. [76] She laughed at her own headline, because there were no mariners anymore, only drones and the occasional fool in a kayak. [97] Then the door at the base of the lighthouse rattled open, though she had bolted it. [113] A man climbed the spiral stairs, drenched, shaking seawater from a coat the color of thunderclouds. [129] He was laughing before he reached the top, a delighted, percussive laugh that rolled like distant weather. [146] "Your forecast," he said, bowing, "was correct." [153] Bex stared. [155] Rain had not yet begun. [160] "I am the herald," he explained, as if that clarified anything, "and you are, apparently, accidentally prophetic." [177] He plucked the wick from her tweezers and held it up to the emerald pane. [192] "Do you know what this is?" [198] "A wick," she said. [202] "A conduit," he corrected, and his grin widened until she could see the weather behind his teeth. [219] He explained, in a voice full of shadowed elation, that the old world had not died with its newspapers; it had simply gone inward, folded into rooms most people no longer knew how to enter. [254] The emerald lighthouse was one such room. [261] Its green glass, he said, worked through total internal reflection, bouncing any light struck within it back upon itself until nothing escaped - not photons, not omens, not the old ancestral dark. [292] "Light the wick," he urged. [297] "Steer the beam with intent, and you can shape a new dawn from the shadows the ancients left behind." [316] She should have laughed him off the catwalk. [324] Instead, she thought of the three winters of talking to no one but gulls, and the headlines she'd thrown at the dark as if daring it to correct her. [353] She struck a match. [357] The wick caught, and the emerald chamber flooded with green fire that refused to leave. [372] The light bounced, folded, compounded; shadows she had never cast stood suddenly around her - her grandmother, a typesetter at a press long shuttered, a monk bent over vellum, a woman in a tunic pointing at stars. [408] Ancient shadows, borrowed back. [412] "Steer," the herald whispered, still laughing, and she understood. [421] Her routine had never been grief; it had been rehearsal. [431] Each invented headline, each polished pane, had been a small act of stewardship, keeping a door from rusting shut. [450] She set her hands on the lamp's brass ring and turned. [461] The green beam swept across the starlit sea, but it carried more than light. [475] It carried the patience of the monk, the precision of the typesetter, the steady gaze of the star-pointer, and Bex's own stubborn nightly prayer. [499] Where the beam passed, something began to grow again - not crops, not signal, but attention. [514] People miles inland looked up from their screens without knowing why. [525] A child asked her father what the stars were called. [535] A woman who had not written a letter in a decade opened a drawer for paper. [551] The herald leaned on the railing, watching with the delight of a man whose forecast had finally found its weather. [571] "Will you come again?" Bex asked. [577] "I am the storm," he said, already dissolving into mist, "and storms return." [590] "Keep the wick trimmed." [594] She did. [596] Night after night under the stars, she steered green fire across a forgetful world, trimming the wick, inventing her headlines, laughing sometimes in the same rolling key she had learned from him. [628] The old dawn was gone with the paper and the ink. [639] A new one, shadowed and elated, rose each morning out of the ancient reflecting glass. [654]
