Marrow climbed service ladders for a living, but the dead climbed with him. [13] As an elevator repair technician who saw ghosts, he had learned to read a shaft the way other men read newspapers: grease smears, cable fray, the tremor of a child who had fallen in 1962 and never stopped falling. [48] The dispatcher called it a gift. [54] Marrow called it a weightless burden, the kind that bruises from the inside. [67]

His new work order sent him to the old Kettridge fairgrounds, where an abandoned circus ticket office leaned under sixty years of weather. [90] There was no elevator there. [95] There was, however, a client: a horologist named Pell, thin as a minute hand, who said the building hummed beneath its ribs. [117] "You hunt vertical ghosts," Pell said, pressing keys into Marrow's palm. [129] "This one hides upward." [133]

Inside, the ticket booth smelled of sugar turned to varnish. [144] The painted clown on the window had weathered into something apologetic. [154] Marrow was skillfully observant by trade, and his eyes went first to the cracks in the facade, where plaster scabs had fallen away to reveal older plaster, and older still, layers of lies the building had told itself. [190] A single overhead fluorescent buzzed the familiar industrial drone he knew from a thousand lobbies. [205]

He waited. [207] He always waited. [210] When the overhead fluorescent buzz dies off, the other frequencies rise, and that was when the dead spoke most clearly. [233] The tube stuttered, flickered, and then went silent as a held breath. [245]

The room filled. [248]

Not with bodies exactly, but with impressions: a ticket seller counting the same dime forever, a boy clutching a paper crown, a woman whose face would not resolve because she did not want it to. [282] They all looked up. [286] Every ghost in the office was staring at the ceiling. [296]

Marrow's motivation sharpened to a point: he needed to discover what ceilings hide. [310] He dragged a stool beneath the water-bruised tiles and began the slow craft of analyzing dust accumulation patterns, sweeping a flashlight at a shallow angle so the motes cast long Martian shadows. [341] Dust does not lie. [346] Dust records every breath, every opened hatch, every secret weight placed above a panel. [361] One tile, third from the east wall, had a clean oval where the dust thinned, as if something were periodically lifted and replaced. [382]

He pushed the tile aside. [388]

Nestled in the joist cavity sat a horologist's mainspring barrel, brass-dark, heavier than it looked, its coil wound to a tension that made his teeth ache. [416] Beside it lay a folded note in Pell's handwriting. [426] If you find this, engage the escapement. [433] Do not carry it out. [438] Wind, do not take. [443]

Marrow understood then, with the slow sinking clarity of a man realizing the floor is the ceiling of somewhere worse. [464] The ghosts had not been trapped by the building. [473] The building had been trapped by them, and someone, long ago, had wound a spring to hold the cage taut. [494] The tension was failing. [499] The cracks in the facade were not weather; they were the first yawn of release. [514]

He braced the barrel against the joist and, with the small key Pell had slipped between his house keys, he began to engage the escapement, tooth by patient tooth. [544] Each click lifted something off his shoulders. [552] The ticket seller stopped counting. [557] The boy set down his paper crown. [564] The woman turned, and her face was only tired, only ordinary, only done. [577]

One by one they drifted upward through the tile he had opened, not trapped, not escaping, simply going where the ceiling had always meant to take them. [604]

The fluorescent tube hiccupped back to life. [611] The buzz returned, smaller now, almost polite. [619]

Marrow reseated the tile, leaving the barrel wound and humming beneath it, a small mechanical heart keeping a door that had finally learned to open the right way. [650] He locked the booth behind him. [656]

Outside, dawn was bruising the fairgrounds pink. [663] He felt the absence of passengers he had carried without knowing, and walked to his truck lighter by the exact weight of other people's unfinished climbing. [690]

Tomorrow there would be other shafts. [696] There were always other shafts. [701]

But tonight, for the first time in years, Marrow looked up and saw only sky. [716]
