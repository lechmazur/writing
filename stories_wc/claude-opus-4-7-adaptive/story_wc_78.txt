At the blue hour, when the sea forgot whether it was sky, Oris the leeward tide prophet climbed down from the crow's nest of her floating orchard. [27] The orchard itself was a circle of lashed driftwood, eighty paces across, bearing twelve stubborn pear trees that had learned to drink mist. [50] She carried only a xylophone mallet, its felt head worn to the shape of her thumb. [65]

She was a prophet because she interpreted cloud movements, and the clouds that morning were braided wrong. [82] Cumulus stacked like stairs climbing west, while a thin cirrus spiraled against them, a countercurrent drawn in chalk. [101] Oris knew this shape. [105] She had sketched it three nights ago in her logbook, a fractal curl repeating inward, and written beneath it the word wall. [128]

A wall meant an obstacle, and obstacles were what she lived to dub. [141] To dub, in her vocabulary, was to knight a thing with a new name so thoroughly it forgot its former use. [161] A reef became a bell. [166] A squall became a choir. [171] This was the whole of her practice: sketched resonance, the belief that any shape, once drawn accurately, could be tapped into song. [193]

She tapped the driftwood rim with her mallet. [201] A low, mossy note rolled through the pear trunks. [210] The orchard answered, and answered again, each concentric ring of wood producing a pitch slightly higher, so the single strike bloomed outward in a fractal generating chord that fed itself from the inside. [243] The pears shivered. [246] This was her instrument; she had spent nine years tuning it by weighting certain logs with barnacles and shaving others thin. [267]

By noon the wall appeared. [272] It was not cloud but water: a standing wave, green and patient, a mile long, where two currents argued. [293] Most sailors died against such things. [299] Oris steered her orchard toward it with a long oar and a grin she could not quite govern. [316]

"I dub thee tambour," she said aloud, pointing the mallet at the wave. [331] The restless euphony of her little world, the creaking ropes, the humming fruit, the mallet clicking her teeth, rose to meet the coming impact. [354] She struck the largest trunk. [359]

The orchard sang a fundamental so deep the wave felt it first in its belly. [374] Water is a medium, Oris knew; whatever shape you sketch into it with sound, it will try to become. [394] She sketched the fractal she had drawn in the logbook, each strike a smaller echo of the last: whole trunk, half branch, quarter twig, leaf. [419] The wave, meeting this insistence, began to organize. [428] Its crest split into twelve smaller crests, then each of those into twelve again, a comb of harmonics rising where a wall had been. [452]

The orchard passed through the tambour as through a curtain of glass beads. [465] Each bead was a chord. [470] Each chord was the wave forgetting what it had intended. [480] Behind her, the water resumed its argument, but gentler now, as though embarrassed. [494]

At the second blue hour, the one at dusk that mirrors the dawn, Oris sat with her logbook and sketched what she had heard. [517] The page filled with nested spirals. [523] She dubbed the new shape reef-choir and noted its pitch. [533] The cirrus above had unbraided; the cumulus stairs had collapsed into ordinary pillows. [547] Her reading had been correct. [552]

A gull, lost or curious, landed on a pear bough and regarded her. [565] "You too," she told it, raising the mallet. [574] "I dub thee metronome." [578] The gull croaked once, obligingly, on the beat. [587]

She laughed, and the orchard laughed with her in twelve pitches. [598] Somewhere beyond the horizon, another wall was gathering; she could feel it in the pull of the leeward tide against her ankles. [621] Good, she thought. [624] Another instrument waiting to be named. [630] She lifted the mallet, studied the darkening clouds for the shape of tomorrow's song, and began, softly, to tap the rim of her small, stubborn, singing world. [658]
