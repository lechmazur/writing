<story>The courtyard lay silver under snow, and Vesna crossed it barefoot, counting nothing but her breath. [16] She was called a courtesan in the ledgers of the Marble House, though she had never lain with a patron. [36] Her art was listening; she sold attention, not skin, and the lords who paid her came for the bodily poetic way she leaned toward their sorrows, as though each confession were a line of verse her spine was learning. [75] Tonight she was walking out of all of that. [84] At the courtyard's far edge stood the prism sanctuary, a glass pavilion built by an astronomer-saint three centuries dead, and under starlight its facets hung like frozen tears. [112] Inside, the old records said, one could perform the harvest of echoes: gather every spoken word a place had kept, every promise dissolved into its stones. [138] Vesna wanted only one echo, and she wanted it unspoiled. [148] The city around her slept the dreamless sleep of obedient people, rocked by the lullaby of conformity that the Council broadcast each dusk from the bell towers, a slow five-note phrase calibrated to still rebellion in the blood. [186] She had heard it for twenty years and hummed along. [196] Then last week a woman named Irin had died in her arms between sigh and smile, and in that slender pause had whispered a single sentence Vesna could not remember, though her body still ached with the shape of it. [236] The sanctuary, legend held, would return such lost words to anyone who could unlock it. [251] Snow squeaked under her heels as she approached the door. [261] There was no keyhole, only a lattice of crystal filaments woven across the lintel, glittering faintly in the cold starlight. [281] She understood then what the old books had meant: one did not open the sanctuary with a key but by untangling threads. [303] Her fingers, trained by years of loosening knotted confessions from reluctant throats, began their patient work. [319] Each filament sang a different pitch when it parted, a note that drifted upward and vanished into the constellations. [338] The alarming serenity of the task unnerved her. [346] There was no guard, no ward, no curse; the city simply trusted that no one would want this badly enough to stand in the cold long enough to finish. [375] Conformity, she realized, was defended not by walls but by comfort. [386] An hour passed, perhaps two; the stars wheeled a careful arc. [397] When the last thread slipped free, the pavilion drew a long breath of air through its open facets, and every surface lit from within. [421] Vesna stepped inside. [424] The sanctuary's interior was a single round room whose walls were tiers of prisms, and each prism held a captured voice, a pressed flower of sound. [450] She did not know how she knew which one was Irin's, only that her years of listening had taught her to read rooms the way others read faces, and one small prism in the northern tier pulsed at the rhythm of the ache beneath her ribs. [494] She lifted it down. [498] The voice inside waited, patient as seed. [505] To hear it, Vesna understood, she would have to break the prism, and in breaking it she would release every other voice too, the entire harvest loosed over the sleeping city like a contrary weather. [542] The lullaby would not survive such a chorus. [550] Neither, perhaps, would she; the Council punished disturbers. [558] But Irin had spent her dying breath on a sentence, and Vesna had spent her life on silences that paid well. [579] Between sigh and smile, she told herself, a person decides what her listening was for. [594] She carried the prism out into the silver courtyard under snow, held it up to the patient starlight, and with a small firm motion pressed it against the stone. [623] It broke like ice breaking on a river in spring. [633] A sound rose, many sounds, Irin's voice among them saying at last, clear as a struck bell: Do not sing along. [654] Across the city, bells meant for the dusk lullaby began, one by one, to forget their tune. [671] Vesna stood in the snow and smiled, at last defying, with her whole body, the lullaby that had rocked her to sleep. [693]</story>
