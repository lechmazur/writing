<story>Mira Calvet climbed the ridge in her patched pressure suit, boots whispering through regolith older than any prayer. [18] As the Lunar Survey's last crater inspector, she had outlived the bureau that once issued her badge. [35] The space age had aged too, its bright promises corroded by budget cuts and quieter ambitions. [51] Now only she remained on Selene-7, scheduled for decommission at sunrise. [62] She had come to photograph the last of something precious: the shadow-garden inside Hallow Crater, a lattice of ice crystals that bloomed only in permanent dark. [88] Once the station's reflectors toppled, sunlight would spill across the basin and evaporate the garden in minutes. [105] Mira intended to outlast the schedule long enough to capture it. [116] She reached the moonlit summit, where Earth hung low and blue like a lantern held by a tired friend. [135] In her glove she cradled the nightwatch's hourglass sand grain, a single dark speck she had pried from the observatory's broken timepiece decades ago. [159] Her mentor, old Nightwatch Orrin, had pressed it into her palm the night he retired. [174] "One grain," he'd said, "for when you need to remember that time is made of small, countable things." [192] She tucked the grain into the shutter-cap of her camera, a small ritual for a small ceremony. [209] Purposefully curious even at sixty-four, Mira still believed a photograph could argue with erasure. [223] She set the tripod, angled the lens down the crater wall, and began her preparations. [238] Because radio relays had been silenced for the decommission, she used chemical reactions for timed messages, a trick from her field days. [260] Three vials of silver nitrate, copper sulfate, and a slow iron catalyst, taped along a strip of survey paper, would change color in sequence: first amber, then green, then black. [290] When the final band darkened, her automatic transmitter, crude, analog, legal, would squeeze one last packet toward Earth: the photograph, and her goodbye. [313] She started the reaction and descended the rim. [321] The shadow-garden was more delicate than memory allowed. [329] Ice blossoms climbed the crater wall in pale filigree, fed by cold exhalations from fissures no survey had properly mapped. [349] She photographed slowly, bracketing exposures, speaking softly to the garden as if it could hear. [364] "You were here," she whispered, "I saw you." [372] Above her, the amber vial had already turned; green crept into the second like moss through a crack. [390] She worked faster, feeling the dawning hush settle across the summit, that peculiar silence the Moon keeps just before terminator light. [411] Everything waited; even her breath seemed borrowed. [418] She thought of Orrin, who had loved that hush more than any sunrise. [431] When she climbed back to the summit, the sky along the eastern limb was already bleeding a pale, honest gold. [451] Dawnlit melancholy filled her chest, heavy and clean. [459] She set the camera into the transmitter cradle and watched the last vial darken. [473] The packet clicked away into the vacuum, carrying the garden's final portrait toward a planet that might or might not remember to look. [496] She removed the hourglass grain from the shutter-cap and held it up against the rising Earthlight. [512] One grain, one garden, one inspector; small countable things. [521] Reflector motors began their scheduled tilt, humming through her boots. [531] Light poured into Hallow Crater like water into a cupped hand, and the shadow-garden turned to vapor without complaint. [550] Mira did not look away. [555] She had promised to witness, and witnessing was a kind of keeping. [567] When the crater was bare, she set the sand grain on the summit stone, where the next terminator would find it. [588] Somewhere, she imagined, Orrin's old clock had one fewer grain and one more story. [602] She unlatched her helmet visor's sun-filter and began the long walk down, humming a tune older than the space age, purposefully curious about whatever came next, already outlasting the ending she had been assigned.
