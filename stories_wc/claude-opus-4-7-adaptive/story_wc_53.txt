The carousel halted at dawn, and the lagoon above the clouds forgot which century it belonged to. [17] Mira had been waiting for this stillness her whole life, or perhaps only since last Tuesday; she could no longer tell. [38] Time here moved like oil on water, slick and unwilling to commit. [50] She was a scavenger by trade and by temperament, resolute in the way driftwood is resolute: shaped by what refuses to release it. [73] The kinetic lagoon churned beneath her boots, its glass-bright currents driven by some forgotten engine the old builders had buried in the reef. [96] Gondolas drifted sideways through the air, half-sunk in mist, and from them leaked conversations. [110] Mira collected fragments of overheard stories the way other people collected coins. [122] A woman in a green coat had once said, "He promised me a cartographer's life," and Mira kept that sentence in her pocket beside a rusted key. [149] A boy had whispered, "I never told her I saw the fire start," and she kept that one too. [168] Each fragment was a coordinate; together they pointed toward the reason she had come. [182] In the shallows, she found what the tide had promised: a disposable camera wheel, its plastic spine warped, twenty-seven exposures still sealed inside. [205] She recognized it immediately, though she had not seen it in eleven years, or perhaps eleven minutes. [222] The label, in her own younger handwriting, read: Ascent, summer, do not lose. [235] Mira sat on the bleached bench where the carousel horses had frozen mid-gallop, their painted eyes fixed on a horizon that no longer turned. [259] Fractured stillness settled over everything; even her breath seemed to arrive in pieces. [272] She had come here to negotiate with her own ghost. [282] The ghost was not dead, only left behind, a version of Mira who had believed the lagoon was a beginning rather than a pawnshop for the things people ascended to forget. [313] "You took too long," the ghost said, stepping out of a gondola's shadow with the camera wheel's twin in her hand. [334] She looked sixteen and sunburned and furious. [341] "I know," Mira answered, because regret was the only currency the ghost accepted. [354] "You were going to photograph every cloud city, build an atlas, teach children the names of the winds." [372] Mira turned the warped wheel in her fingers and felt the eroded ambitions inside it rattle like seeds in a dead gourd. [394] "I traded the atlas for rent, the winds for a quiet apartment, and you for someone who could sleep through the night," she said. [418] The ghost did not soften. [423] "Then why come back now, after the carousel halts, after the lagoon stops pretending?" [437] Mira held up the fragments she had gathered: the cartographer's promise, the boy's confession, a dozen other borrowed griefs. [456] "Because I finally understood that everyone's story gets eroded, that I am not uniquely ruined, only late," she said. [475] She offered the camera wheel to the ghost, and the ghost offered hers in return; the exchange was not forgiveness but acknowledgment, which was the best trade available in this market. [506] When Mira cranked the old wheel, it clicked once, and the lagoon above the clouds shivered as if remembering its own pulse. [528] The carousel, halted for so long, released a single note from its calliope. [541] Not music, exactly, but permission. [546] She photographed the ghost before she vanished, and the ghost photographed her; somewhere in the mechanics of cheap plastic lenses they agreed to share the blame and the remainder. [575] Mira stood, chronologically confused but no longer afraid of it. [585] The lagoon began its kinetic murmur again, gondolas drifting, stories leaking; she would gather what she could, because that was still her trade. [608] She stepped onto the nearest gondola as the carousel resumed, slower now, and carried her toward a horizon that had, at last, agreed to turn. [633]
