Della kept her father's antique watch fob tight in her fist, a second heartbeat against her palm. [17] She was the moonshine runner's daughter, and tonight she had descended beneath the rotting pier to trade with thieves until sunrise. [38] The pirate market lived there, between the barnacled pilings, where lanterns swung like hanged stars and every whisper was a price. [59] A truce of hush governed the place: no shouting, no names, no songs loud enough to wake the harbor patrol. [79] Breaking the silence broke the market, and breaking the market meant a knife between the ribs before dawn. [97] Della had come to buy a cylinder recorder, one of the old wax kind, because she needed to capture the sound of trust breaking. [121] Not metaphor, not memory: the literal sound. [128] Her father had made a deal with a revenue man named Corbin, and Corbin had turned, and her father hung now in a county cell awaiting a rope he did not deserve. [160] If Della could record Corbin confessing, recant in his own voice, the circuit judge had promised to listen. [178] The judge was an old friend of her grandfather, curiously steadfast for a man who drank the family's liquor. [197] He would thwart the hanging if, and only if, she brought proof before morning bell. [212] She moved through false starts. [217] The first stall sold only brass hinges and rumor; the second, a man with a gullspine beard, offered her a recorder that turned out to be a child's music box with the teeth filed down. [252] The third vendor tried to sell her back her own watch fob, claiming he had found it, though she had never let it leave her hand. [278] Each false start cost her minutes she did not have, and each refusal had to be made in the pirate market's soft, respectful murmur, the truce of hush demanding she smile while being robbed. [312] At last a woman with ink-stained cuffs tapped a tarpaulin and showed her a real cylinder phonograph, brass horn folded like a sleeping swan. [336] The price was the watch fob. [342] Della held it up into the lantern light; the gold caught and scattered, and she remembered her grandfather winding it before a run, saying that time belonged to whoever kept moving. [373] She gave it over. [377] She had no other currency, and her father's neck was worth more than brass. [391] With the phonograph strapped to her back, she climbed the ladder up into the town, where Corbin drank in a back room she had scouted for a week. [419] She set the horn beneath a loose floorboard and waited, breathing the comforting unease of a plan too thin to fail twice. [441] Corbin came in with another revenue man and, warmed by rye, began to laugh about the runner's daughter who would never know what he had done. [467] He spoke plainly of the false ledger, the forged letter, the coin he had taken. [482] The wax drum turned, and Della heard it, the exact sound of trust breaking, not a crack, not a snap, but the patient hiss of a needle cutting a groove into soft wax, recording one man's voice ruining another's life. [522] It was softer than she had imagined. [529] It sounded like rain on a tin roof. [537] When Corbin left, she slid the cylinder into a velvet sleeve and ran back down to the pier, because the judge kept chambers above the customs house, and sunrise was pressing its pale thumb against the water. [574] At the mouth of the pirate market, the ink-cuffed woman waited, holding out the watch fob. [590] "A runner's daughter keeps her time," she said, and pressed the fob back into Della's hand. [606] Della did not ask why; the market had its truces, and some of them were kind. [622] She climbed toward the judge's window with the wax in one pocket and the fob in the other, and the morning, curiously steadfast itself, held its breath until she arrived. [652]
