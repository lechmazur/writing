The last human was born yesterday, and Wren named him Tomas. [11] She held him in the observation nacelle of the Gleaner, a recycled spaceship welded from the corpses of seven older vessels. [32] Somewhere beyond the glass, a dying sun shed copper light across her sewing kit. [46]

Wren was a steadfast pacifist; she had refused every weapon the colony offered her, even the polite ones. [64] Her only tool was a needle. [70] With it, she meant to push open a locked dimension and ferry the last child somewhere kinder than this hollow universe. [91]

The key lay folded in a silk pouch at her throat: Kwan Yin's tear, a gem of frozen mercy smuggled out of old Earth. [115] It could cut a seam in reality, but only if the seamstress knew her own darkness. [131] Shadow work, her grandmother had called it. [138] You cannot thread the needle with a trembling hand. [147]

So Wren had spread her inheritance across the floor: faded photographs of everyone she had loved, hated, failed, or buried, each one salted with the long-dried tears of its keepers. [177] In the cabin's low light she could see them: faint shimmers where old grief had dried into silver constellations. [196] Each shimmer was a door; each door opened onto a room inside herself she had never agreed to enter. [215]

She began to stitch. [219] Needle through the photograph of her mother, who had died choking on apology; needle through the cousin whose letters she had burned; needle through her own younger face, eight years old, laughing by a lake that no longer existed. [258] Each stitch pulled two images into one, and each stitch pulled a buried feeling up into her chest, heavy as wet wool. [280]

She worked without speaking, every stitch a small funeral. [289] The ship around her creaked; being recycled, the Gleaner held the memory of other crews in its welds, and she felt their attention gather over her shoulder like cold breath. [319]

The shimmer on the photographs began to rise, a faint aurora lifting from the dried tears, tracing a map only grief could read. [342] She followed it with her needle, stitching the paths together. [352] When the final stitch closed, she lifted Kwan Yin's tear from her throat and set it upon the quilt, and the gem began to glow at the places where tears met thread. [384]

Then Wren did the hardest work: she looked at each photograph and admitted what she had done, what had been done to her, what she had pretended not to see. [414] She named her jealousy, the day she had let a friend drown in an argument, the small cruelties of her pacifism, how refusing to fight had sometimes meant refusing to help. [445]

With each admission something broke loose inside her, and she grew, literally, lighter. [458] Wren had always been gravitationally lighter when happy, a childhood anomaly doctors had dismissed as harmless. [474] Now, as forgiveness settled into her bones, she drifted off the floor. [486] Tomas floated up beside her, his small breath steady against the quiet. [498]

The quilt glowed; Kwan Yin's tear split the air like a seam loosened by a patient hand. [515] Beyond the opening, a dimension she had locked against herself fifty years ago unfolded: green, breathable, waiting. [532]

She gathered the child. [536] She did not look back at the Gleaner, though its ghosts murmured a blessing through the rivets. [553] A steadfast pacifist carries her war inside her, and Wren had finally laid hers down. [568] She stepped through the stitched seam, lighter than she had ever been, the last human of the old world breathing softly against her shoulder. [592] Somewhere behind her, the Gleaner drifted on, empty at last, a ship of ghosts sailing a species into memory. [611]
