<story>Halder kept a small house of driftwood where the cobalt lagoon lay quiet after rain, and there he wrote poems no one read. [23] He was the shoreline's quiet poet, warmly attentive to every small thing the tide surrendered. [38] His notebooks filled with half-lines, scraps, unfinished stanzas he refused to complete, because he believed beauty lived in the incomplete - in what gestured beyond itself. [63] The villagers called this eccentric; he called it honesty. [72] On the morning the lagoon turned to hammered cobalt beneath a clearing sky, Halder walked the tideline and found a feather laid across a flat stone. [98] It was long and iridescent, banded with storm-green and copper - a drake's tail feather, though no drake had been seen in these waters for a generation. [124] He lifted it, and the air seemed to hum in a burnished hush, as if the lagoon itself were holding its breath. [146] That night he dreamed of woven dawns - a tapestry of mornings stitched together, some bright, some bruised, some missing their last thread. [168] In the dream a voice said: tend them. [176] He woke uncertain what to tend, only that the feather lay beside his pillow, still warm. [192] In the following weeks, the village began to quarrel. [201] The fishermen accused the kite-makers of scaring the shoals; the kite-makers accused the temple singers of bad omens; the singers accused the fishermen of vulgar noise. [227] Halder, who had never taken sides, found himself drawn from his driftwood door by the feather's faint thrum against his palm. [248] He went first to the fishermen and listened warmly, writing down fragments of their grievances. [263] He went to the kite-makers, to the singers, to the children who sided with none of them, and filled pages with contradictory lines. [286] Then he did what he had always done, only louder: he refused to finish any of them. [303] He nailed the fragments together on a long board along the quay - a fisherman's complaint next to a singer's lament next to a child's joke - and let the mismatched voices stand side by side. [337] People came to read, expecting mockery, and found instead their own small truths laid among others. [353] The arguments did not vanish, but softened - the method was not agreement but harmonizing discord, letting each unfinished voice lean against another until the chord held. [379] Halder understood then what he had been asked to tend: not one answer, but the fragile gaps between many. [398] On the first clear dawn after the quarrels calmed, he walked to the cobalt lagoon once more, feather in hand. [418] A child was flying a paper kite above the shallows, laughing. [429] Halder lifted the feather and felt in it a last, lingering warmth - the attentive warmth of a thing loaned, not given. [450] As a kite string snaps somewhere behind him, he opened his palm and let the feather rise. [467] It climbed the wind that had stolen the child's kite, and the two - paper diamond and storm-banded plume - drifted seaward together, neither whole, both beautiful. [492] The child cried, briefly, then laughed again. [499] Halder opened his notebook to the oldest unfinished poem and did not finish it. [513] Instead he wrote beneath: woven dawns are sewn with missing thread; that is how the light gets through. [531] He closed the book and watched the lagoon hold its burnished hush, cobalt beneath a newly rinsed sky. [549] Later, villagers would say the poet had learned to mend their quarrels, but Halder knew he had mended nothing. [568] He had only stood among their unfinished voices, warmly attentive, and let their discord harmonize of its own accord. [587] That, he thought, was the whole of his craft: to tend the incomplete until it sang. [603] And above the lagoon, morning after morning, the dawns kept weaving themselves, bright thread beside broken one, indistinguishable in their small, imperfect light. [626]</story>
