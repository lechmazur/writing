Iris Bellmare had written six novels and believed in none of them. [12] The seventh manuscript lay open on the driftwood desk, its pages breathing in the damp air like something still deciding whether to live. [35] She was a conflicted novelist by every measure that mattered: she wanted to tell the truth and she wanted to be loved, and she had learned, slowly, that these ambitions rowed against the current of one another. [72] Tonight she had come to the place the locals refused to name, a feral nexus in mist where three tidal rivers braided together beneath low pines. [98] Here, rumor said, a writer could bless her unfinished book, vow it into being the way lovers vowed at altars. [118] Iris carried only two things: the manuscript, and a spiral seashell fragment her mother had pressed into her palm on the last coherent afternoon of her life. [145] The fragment was broken at its widest turn, revealing the chambered staircase inside, each room smaller, each more private. [164] Her mother had said: people are like this. [172] People open only so far. [177] Mist moved across the water in a swirling hush, neither wind nor weather, something older that the place exhaled to keep itself secret. [200] Iris sat on a flat stone and waited, lucidly watchful, the way she had trained herself to be at readings and funerals and the bedsides of the dying. [228] She had come to collect the shapes of different hopes. [238] That was the honest way to say it. [246] Her novel was about a town that drowned and refused to admit it, and she had written five drafts without understanding what any of its people actually wanted. [274] So she had come to the nexus to listen. [283] They arrived through ephemeral cameo appearances, figures who stepped from the mist and lingered only as long as a held breath. [304] A fisherman with salt scabbed into the creases of his neck told her he hoped only to outlive his debts. [324] He was gone before she could ask his name. [333] A girl in a yellow slicker said she hoped her brother would come home from wherever brothers went. [351] An old woman, translucent at the edges, said she hoped to be forgotten gently, which Iris understood was the most sophisticated hope of all. [375] Each visitor pressed a word or two into the air and dissolved. [387] Iris wrote them down. [391] The seashell fragment warmed in her palm, as if its chambers were filling, one by one, with the voices she gathered. [412] She noticed that the hopes did not line up. [421] They argued with each other. [426] The fisherman's solvency pressed against the girl's faith; the old woman's gentle erasure contradicted the novelist's own quiet wish to be remembered by strangers on trains. [452] This, she realized, was what her drowned town had been missing. [463] Not consensus, but friction. [467] Hope was not one river but several, each cutting against the current of the others, and a town was only real when its hopes collided. [492] Near dawn the mist thickened and a final visitor came: a man who looked the way she imagined her father might have looked if he had lived past forty. [521] He did not speak. [525] He only set his hand beside hers on the stone, and she understood he hoped she would finish the book, which was a hope she had not allowed herself to carry alone. [557] Then he, too, was gone. [562] Iris opened the manuscript. [566] Inside the whisper of closing pages she could hear the nexus already beginning to forget her, as the nexus forgot everyone who left it. [590] She wrote until the sun slid its pale blade under the pines. [602] The novel was not finished, but it was now populated, peopled by voices that did not agree and therefore could not be dismissed. [625] She stood, stiff in the knees, and walked back along the braided water. [638] The spiral seashell fragment she set at the meeting of the three currents, a small white vow, her way to solemnize what the mist had given. [664] Walking home, she did not feel certain, which was the correct feeling. [676] A conflicted novelist only wrote well when conflict was her compass rather than her apology. [691] Somewhere behind her, three rivers kept arguing toward the sea, and her seventh book, she suspected, would finally be worth the reading. [713]
