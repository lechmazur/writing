<story>On the island of Meru-Varin, where the sea remembered more than the people did, Ingo served as the reality checker. [20] His office was empirically whimsical by charter: verify which village legends had truly happened, which had been embroidered, and which had drifted in from some neighbor's dream. [47] He carried a ledger, a lantern, and a cup of salt, and he walked the ancient ridge in bloom, where jacaranda and bone-orchid shared one crown. [73] The monsoon was three days out. [79] Before it arrived, the elders needed his verdict on the strangest claim of the season: that the ridge itself was about to waken. [102] Children across seven villages had been dreaming the same dream. [112] In it, a tree they had never seen spoke their grandparents' names, offered each child a single knowledge fruit seed, and asked them to plant it before the rains. [141] Ingo did not believe in shared dreams. [148] He believed in coincidence, in gossip, in the way stories borrow feet from one another and walk across thresholds in the night. [170] Still, he had been trained to test belief through interpreted dreams, because his island's logic ran that way: a thing was real if it recurred with precision, and untrue if its details drifted. [203] So he went from hut to hut, ledger open, asking the children to describe the tree. [219] They spoke of gray bark scored like old script, of leaves that rustled even in stillness, of a seed warm as a held hand. [243] Their words matched, down to the warmth. [250] Ingo climbed the ridge that evening with luminous doubt blooming in him like a second lantern. [266] He had been a reality checker for twenty years, and his motive had always been plain: to protect cultural memory from the softening of nostalgia and the hardening of rumor. [296] If legends went unchecked, they calcified into law; if laws went unchecked, they forgot where they came from. [314] But the children's dream had the same warmth in every telling, and that was the signature of truth. [332] At the crown of the ridge, among petals that would not survive the first storm, he found a sapling he had never catalogued. [355] Its bark was scored like old script. [362] At his feet lay a single seed, warm as a held hand, though no fruit hung anywhere above. [380] He understood then what the elders had suspected and what his ledger could not easily hold. [396] The island's collective memory, pressed thin by departing youth and imported songs, had found a way to plant itself into sleep. [417] The dream was not a contagion; it was an inheritance, distributed in small, portable pieces. [432] The seed in his palm was a grandmother's recipe, a fisherman's route, a lullaby about the shape of the harbor before the new pier. [456] If he filed it as fiction, the children would stop dreaming, and what the dream carried would dissolve with the next generation. [478] If he filed it as true without evidence, he would break the charter that kept the island honest. [496] He sat with the seed while the wind shifted and the pressure dropped. [509] The monsoon was closer now, perhaps a day. [517] At last he wrote, in a new column he invented on the spot: Verified by recurrence, method: through interpreted dreams, status: to waken. [540] He carried the seed down to the village square and asked each dreaming child to meet him at dawn with a trowel. [562] They planted their seeds along the ridge as the first fat drops began. [575] The rain came kindly, then hard, washing the ledger's ink into faint blue veins. [589] Ingo stood soaked, laughing quietly at himself, empirically unsure and certain all at once. [603] He had not proven the dream true; he had built a place where its truth could be tested by growing. [623] Next season, if green pushed up from all seven plots in the same week, he would know the island had remembered itself awake. [646] If nothing rose, he would accept the charter's older verdict, and grieve. [658] Either way, the checking would continue, which was, he decided, the only faithful form of love a small island could offer its own ghosts. [682]</story>
