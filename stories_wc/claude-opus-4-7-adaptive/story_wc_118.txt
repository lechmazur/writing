<story>The silo had been a granary once, then a missile, then a grave. [13] Now it was a library, and Odo Vance climbed its spiral in the hour they called bronze quiet, when the sun struck the iron ribs and every shelf hummed like a tuning fork. [46] He was the odd historian, the one the others did not invite to suppers, because he listened to walls. [65]

This was after the last human dream had been dreamt. [75] No one knew exactly when it happened, only that one morning the whole species woke blank, as if a lantern had been snuffed inside each skull. [101] Stories, the council said, would have to be found now, not invented. [113] Odo had volunteered for the silo because he was sensitively perceptive in a way that embarrassed polite company; he heard the grain's memory in the concrete, the missile's regret in the rebar. [145] His motive was simple and, to his mind, sacred: to collect the stories walls had heard. [161]

He had brought with him a fragment of a medieval tapestry showing dragons. [174] It was the last relic from his mother's museum, smoke-stained, the red dye still furious. [189] He hung it on the stone near the reading lectern because he believed the dragons would help him listen; their woven eyes had, after all, watched seven centuries of human breath. [220]

The council had tasked him with a peculiar commission. [229] Somewhere in the silo's lower archive there was rumored to be a bundle of half-burned diaries, pulled from a fireplace during the evacuation of the northern towns. [256] Through these diaries, he was to probe the shape of what they were calling the displaced axis - the theory that the world's spiritual pole had slipped, the way a wheel slips its hub, and that the slippage, not any war or plague, was why dreaming had stopped. [303]

He found the diaries in a brass crate. [311] Their edges were black lace, their middles legible. [319] Seven hands, seven women, seven winters. [325] He read them by the bronze light, one page at a time, and after each he pressed his palm to the silo wall to let the concrete confirm. [353]

The first diarist, Mira, had written of waking to find her compass needle pointing at her own chest. [371] The second, Annelise, reported that a river near her house had begun to flow uphill for nine minutes each dawn. [391] The third simply drew spirals. [396] The fourth described her daughter walking in the garden and casting two shadows, one following the sun, one ahead of it. [417] The fifth was the one that made Odo stop breathing: she wrote that the dragons in an old tapestry she owned had turned, slightly, in the night, so that they no longer faced east but faced inward, toward the viewer. [457]

He looked up at his own fragment. [464] The dragons there faced each other still, but the angle of one tail, he was almost sure, had shifted since morning. [485]

The walls, when he pressed them, began to speak without speaking. [496] They had heard the evacuation. [501] They had heard the fire in which these diaries had almost died. [513] They had heard a woman, the sixth diarist, whisper that the axis had not been lost but relocated, on purpose, by something that needed the dreams for its own winter. [543] The seventh diary was only a single sentence: We kept writing so that someone would press an ear to us later. [564]

Odo wrote his report by lamplight. [570] The displaced axis, he argued, was not a catastrophe but a theft, and a theft implied a thief, and a thief implied a return journey. [595] If the walls remembered, the dragons remembered, and seven burned women remembered, then the species had not gone blank; it had been quietly copied and stored elsewhere, awaiting retrieval. [624]

He closed the brass crate. [629] He touched the tapestry once more, gently, the way one touches a sleeping animal that might, if coaxed, wake up and lead the way home. [654] Outside, the silo rang softly with the bronze hour, and somewhere, perhaps, a first new dream was beginning to gather itself, slow as rust reversing, patient as a historian who had finally been heard. [688]</story>
