<story>Yelen arrived at the crystalline orchard perched at the edge of the shimmering crater with nothing but her bundle of marsh-reeds and a hunger she could not name. [28] She was a marsh-reed sculptor by trade, her fingers stained green from years of braiding stems into herons, lovers, and grieving mothers. [50] The orchard's trees were fruiting quartz, and the villagers who tended them spoke in half-remembered dialects beyond the murmur of lost languages. [72] Yelen had come to graft herself onto community, because her home village had drowned and her kin were scattered like seed. [93] On her first evening, she saw the glimmering obsidian arbor at the orchard's center, a trellis of volcanic glass twisted like black lace. [116] Upon one of its polished faces, someone had sprayed a small silver glyph, a looping rune no one could read. [136] The villagers called the unseen artist Oru, and they spoke of the tag the way people speak of weather: inevitable, mysterious, almost kind. [159] A boy named Tev, boldly sympathetic to the newcomer's loneliness, asked her to help him search. [175] Every child in the orchard dreamed of catching Oru mid-spray, and a shared search for the elusive street artist's tag had become the village's quiet ritual. [201] Yelen agreed, because loneliness is a kind of hunger that answers to any errand. [215] Through autumn, she and Tev walked the crater's lip at dusk, when the quartz fruit cast pink constellations across the basin. [236] They found tags on cistern walls, under the aqueduct, braided into the bark of the oldest glass-pear tree. [254] Each rune resembled a knot, and Tev insisted they were webs of kinship, lines tying every neighbor to every neighbor in ways only Oru could see. [280] Yelen began to sculpt as she searched, leaving small marsh-reed figures at every tag she catalogued: a grandmother, a lame dog, a girl with a broken flute. [307] The villagers noticed; they began to linger by the tags, recognizing themselves in the reeds, and slowly they began to nod at Yelen in the lane. [333] It was a luminescent ache, this belonging, to be counted among strangers who had their own dead to mourn. [352] One night, Tev fell ill with the crater-cough, an old sickness the orchard thought it had forgotten. [369] Yelen sat with him for nine days while his mother worked the quartz harvest, and in the nights she whispered the names of her own drowned siblings, hoping the boy would hear them and live. [404] He lived. [406] On the morning he stood again, Yelen went alone to the obsidian arbor and saw a new tag had appeared overnight, fresh and silver-wet. [430] The knot bore her own likeness, a woman of reeds with her hands open. [444] She understood then that Oru was no single person but the village itself, speaking in the only dialect that had outlasted its languages: the dialect of marking and being marked. [474] Every tag had been made by someone different, each adding a knot for the person they loved, and together they wove a map that no outsider could finish but anyone could join. [506] That evening Yelen climbed the arbor with a pot of silver paint Tev pressed into her palm, and she added her own knot beside the reed-woman: a small curl for her mother, for her village, for the boy whose breath had returned. [548] She chose to dedicate the rest of her seasons to this orchard, to the crater's rim, to the slow grafting of self onto soil. [572] Somewhere past the crater's shimmer, she thought, her drowned siblings were listening, and they would know she had at last been found by strangers willing to hold her name. [601] The arbor glimmered; the knots held. [607]</story>
