Milo had worked the 5:42 commuter ferry for eleven seasons, and he greeted every passenger like a returning cousin. [21] His coat was salt-stiff, his grin resiliently positive, and his pockets always rattled with peppermints for the nervous. [40] The regulars called him the ardent deckhand, though he preferred simply Milo. [52]

On the night the clocks began running sideways, he was the first to notice. [66] The wheelhouse chronometer ticked left, then up, then into a direction no compass could name. [81] Captain Ibarra thumped it twice, shrugged, and told Milo to keep the lines coiled. [95] Passengers boarded as usual, tapping phones that now displayed three times at once. [108]

Halfway across the harbor, the fog arrived without wind. [117] It folded around the ferry like a second hull. [126] When it lifted, the skyline was gone. [133]

In its place rose a spectral grotto under starlight, an enormous cavern whose ceiling was open to a sky that did not belong to their city. [159] Constellations wheeled there that Milo had only seen in his grandmother's embroidery. [173] The water beneath the ferry glowed the soft green of memory. [184]

"Everyone stay calm," Milo called, because somebody had to. [194] "We're just early. [197] Or late. [199] Or both." [201]

Captain Ibarra reached for the radio, but the handset crackled with voices speaking over themselves, each one saying the same sentence a half-second apart. [227] Milo recognized his own voice among them. [234]

On the bow stood a woman none of them had seen board. [246] She wore a conductor's uniform two fashions out of date, and she carried an empty hourglass, its bulbs clear of any sand. [270]

"The ferry vanishes here every crossing," she said, though her mouth moved oddly, as if dubbed. [286] "You simply haven't noticed before. [291] Tonight, the noticing is the point." [297]

Milo stepped forward, because he always stepped forward. [305] "Ma'am, we have commuters. [309] They need to be home." [313]

"They are home," she answered. [317] "In one version. [320] In another, they are still on the dock. [327] In a third, they never boarded. [332] My task is to merge those versions before the grotto closes them off forever." [345]

She lifted the hourglass. [349] "The sand is kept in all timelines at once. [357] To gather it, we must find the interconnected patterns, the places where each version of the voyage touches the others." [375]

Milo didn't understand, but he understood work. [382] "Tell me what to do." [386]

She pointed to the passengers. [391] "Ask them what they remember. [395] Not tonight. [397] Every night. [399] The same ferry. [402] The small differences." [404]

So Milo moved down the aisles, notebook in hand, the way he once checked tickets. [419] A banker recalled a gull that never landed. [427] A student recalled a gull that landed twice. [435] A child recalled feeding the gull a peppermint Milo had no memory of giving. [449] Each memory shimmered as he wrote it, and in the hourglass, a single grain of sand appeared, then another, then a slow amber stream. [473]

The grotto trembled. [476] The stars above shifted into the pattern of his harbor's skyline, then back, then into something that was both. [497] Milo realized the ferry had always been here, between crossings, and the harbor had only ever been one of its stops. [520] The tone of his whole life adjusted inside him: permanent transient, a home that was always leaving and never gone. [540]

"Almost," the woman whispered. [544] "One more pattern." [547]

Milo raised the radio himself. [552] He keyed it and spoke slowly, so that every version of him on every overlapping frequency could say it with him. [574] "This is the 5:42, all runs, all nights. [583] We are accounted for. [587] We are coming in." [591]

The hourglass filled. [594] The grotto exhaled. [597] Fog returned, softer now, and when it parted the dock lights of the harbor were waiting, patient as parents. [620]

Passengers disembarked chatting about a strange dream they had shared. [631] The captain logged nothing unusual, because the log would not hold the words. [645] The woman in the old uniform was gone, but the empty hourglass sat on Milo's bunk, clear again, ready for the next crossing. [670]

Milo coiled the lines and whistled. [677] He would be aboard tomorrow, and every tomorrow, ferrying the living between versions of themselves, resiliently, ardently, home. [699]
