<story>The grotto city of Vadranesc lay behind the monumental waterfall, its lamplit streets suspended in permanent, rosewater dusk. [18] Ilka, the museum guide, unlocked the archive each dawn and inhaled the chilled air of a culture waiting to be rescued. [39] She was cultured in the old way, historically oriented, able to date a brushstroke by the copper in its shadow. [59] Above her head, the waterfall roared; the grotto trembled like a tuning fork nobody dared silence. [75] The refugees had been arriving for months, displaced persons from the burnt provinces above, carrying satchels of cracked icons and children asleep on their shoulders. [100] Ilka's task, self-assigned, was to rescue the dying arts they brought with them: the weavers' knots, the bone-flute tuning, the shaped-bread alphabets of forgotten valleys. [125] She had begun it herself, each Sunday, by baking bread shaped like the initials of her mother and grandmother, a family custom she had once found embarrassing and now believed to be holy. [158] The new arrivals learned the recipe in her kitchen, pressing their own letters into the dough until an entire alphabet of exile lay rising on the boards. [185] Bread, she said, was the only archive that fed you back. [196] That winter, after the beekeeper's last visit, everything changed. [205] Old Marek had come down through the sluice-tunnels every March, bearing wax for the manuscripts and honey for the choir's throats, and had warned her, quietly, that the upper hives were empty. [237] He did not come again. [242] Without wax, the candles guttered; without candles, the illuminations lost their colors to the damp. [257] Ilka worked by a single tool now, an heirloom: one emerald-tinted opera glass lens her great-aunt had pocketed fleeing a burning theatre. [279] Through the green circle, faded scripts resurrected themselves; red inks bloomed back into legibility like thawed berries. [296] She pressed the lens to her eye at every lectern, a one-eyed oracle translating the city's memory into the notebooks of refugees who had no alphabet of their own left. [326] But the air that spring carried a rosewater dread: too sweet, too warm, too early. [341] The thaw had come to the mountains above schedule. [350] Meltwater was swelling the river that fed the waterfall, and every night the grotto's chandeliers trembled a little more violently, the ancient stalactites ticking like nervous teeth. [377] If the river crested, the curtain would collapse, and Vadranesc would drown behind its own front door. [394] Ilka made her decision on a Friday, kneading seven loaves while the refugees watched. [408] She shaped their initials first, then hers, then a single great V for the city. [423] They ate standing, the crumbs speckling their coats like snowfall in reverse. [435] Then she led them, by lantern and emerald lens, into the deepest archive, where the oldest manuscripts lay in cedar drawers. [456] One by one she taught them to read through the green glass, to copy, to carry. [472] A weaver memorized psalms; a stonecutter's widow learned the bone-flute scale; a child, laughing, traced his own initial in flour. [492] If the waterfall took the grotto, at least the arts would walk out on a hundred tongues. [509] They climbed the sluice-tunnels on the eighth night, the same ones Marek had used, each refugee carrying a loaf and a page. [531] Ilka went last, the opera glass lens pressed to her palm, warm as a living coal. [547] Behind them the water sang its swelling aria; ahead, the thaw had opened the mountain passes, and pale daylight fell on faces that had forgotten it. [573] They emerged onto a hillside wet with crocuses and ash. [583] Ilka turned, lifted the lens, and watched the waterfall through its green heart one last time, as though bidding farewell to an illumination that was already bleeding. [610] Behind her, a girl pulled a bread letter from her coat and bit into it, soft, yeasted, still faintly warm. [630] The museum guide smiled, because the archive was walking. [639] And every loaf was a letter, and every letter was a door back home. [653]</story>
