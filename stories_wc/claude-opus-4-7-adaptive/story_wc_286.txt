Ot found the oasis by following sun patterns, the way a potter reads heat off a kiln. [17] The scorched wasteland around him had forgotten color, but inside this ring of fig trees twelve white beekeeping boxes still stood, abandoned yet intact. [41] He had come to understand why some places resist change. [51] That was the only question he still carried; everything else he had set down years ago, on the long walk east. [72] In the center of the oasis sat a small clay hearth, and from its chimney a thin line of smoke rose and flattened against the sky, though no fire should have been lit. [105] As the chimney smoke flattened, Ot felt the air thrum beneath his sandals, a vibration older than bees, older than the wasteland itself. [128] He knelt by the hearth and found what he had been half-expecting: a piece of faded lantern glass, its amber rim worn translucent by decades of hands. [155] When he lifted it, the oasis moved. [162] Not in space, but in time. [168] Through the faded glass he saw the beekeeper, a woman in a straw hat, tending her hives. [185] He lowered it and she was gone, only dust. [194] He raised it again and saw her daughter, older, grayer, pouring honey into jars while soldiers passed on the ridge. [214] Fractured timelines, the potter understood, layered here like slip on a wheel. [226] The oasis had not resisted change by refusing it; it had resisted by keeping every version at once. [244] Ot sat cross-legged in the warm ash and watched. [253] Through the glass he saw a boy feeding a lame dog; in the next moment the same courtyard emptied by fire. [274] Yet the fig trees still stood, and the bees, where they had been, hummed in some other layer he could not see but could feel. [299] He began to notice a pattern: the oasis was phototropic to kindness. [311] Where a small mercy had been done - a beekeeper leaving water for a stranger, a child lifting a scarab from a dry pan - that moment leaned toward the sun and stayed. [342] Cruelty withered; kindness rooted. [346] Over generations the oasis had become a museum of small goodnesses, each one refusing to be erased. [363] That was its resistance. [367] Ot laughed once, softly, a sound with the sharp gentleness of a blade trimming clay. [382] He had walked the wasteland for years asking his question and here it was, answered by a piece of lantern glass he could hold in his palm. [409] He took from his satchel a small bowl he had thrown that morning, unfired, the clay still damp. [427] He filled it with water from his skin and set it by the hearth for whoever, or whenever, came next. [447] Through the faded lantern glass he watched the bowl multiply across timelines: here a thirsty fox drank, there a soldier knelt and wept into it, elsewhere a child floated a fig leaf on its surface. [482] Each version was real. [486] Each version would remain. [490] He understood, then, that he was not a witness to the oasis but a contributor; the glass in his hand had once been someone's lantern, lit to guide a neighbor home. [521] Its kindness had survived the wick, the oil, even the fire that ate the rest of the village. [539] Ot placed the glass gently back into the ash, aligned it so the afternoon sun caught its amber edge, and rose. [560] He would walk on, following sun patterns as he always had, but lighter now. [574] Some places resist change by holding, very tightly, onto the moments worth keeping. [587] A potter, he thought, could learn from that. [595] Behind him the chimney smoke kept flattening, patient as a wheel, and somewhere in its layers the bees thrummed on. [615]
