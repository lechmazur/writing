Odwin Reach had wound the town's clock tower for forty-one years. [11] He was the precise local clock tower winder, ethically diligent, counting gear-teeth the way monks count beads. [28] One winter he had failed a man. [35] He had sworn to ring the warning bell before the tide rose past the obsidian ridge, and he had not, because his hands were full of oil and apology. [64] A gardener named Sefen was swept from the tidal obsidian ridge that night, his broken rake left wedged between two black stones. [86] Odwin kept the rake. [90] He kept it to remember, and because he had begun, in secret, to carve. [104] Each dusk, when the tide pulled back and the ridge emerged like a wet shoulder of glass, Odwin climbed down with a needle and a grain of guilt. [132] He believed in incremental absolution, that a soul restored itself the way a gear did, one tooth at a time. [152] So he worked through tiny inscriptions carved along the broken rake handle, each a confession no longer than a fingernail. [172] I heard the bell rope and ignored it. [180] I chose oil over men. [185] I let the sea take what I could have turned back. [195] His plan was simple and strange. [201] Each inscription had to be finished during the pause in the pendulum's swing, that held breath between tick and tick when the clock tower pendulum bob hung motionless above the stairwell like a drop of iron about to fall. [240] Only in that pause, he believed, did time grant him a loophole wide enough to mend himself without stealing from the town's count. [263] The work was a searing reverie. [269] His breath went shallow, his fingertips burned against the cold handle, and the salt wind off the ridge tasted like Sefen's name. [291] He carved one letter per pause, no more, because to take two would be theft. [306] A winder who cheated time could not be trusted to keep it. [317] Months passed; the rake grew a silver script along its splintered length. [329] The townsfolk noticed Odwin was gentler, slower to judge, quicker to warn when the fog came low. [346] He motivated himself with one thought: to restore shared balance, not merely his own. [360] The tower kept time for the harbor, and the harbor kept time for the boats; if he was crooked at the center, the whole town leaned. [386] On the evening he carved the final letter, a storm stacked itself above the tidal obsidian ridge. [403] Odwin climbed to the belfry and set the rake against the pendulum bob's housing, letter by letter facing inward. [422] He waited for the pause. [427] When the bob hung still at the apex, he whispered the confession whole, in one breath, and the tower seemed to exhale with him. [451] Below, the warning bell rang before he touched it. [460] Fishermen looked up, saw the signal they had almost missed, and turned their keels away from the ridge. [478] No one drowned that night. [483] Odwin did not call it forgiveness. [489] He called it balance, returned in pieces no larger than a carved letter, a steadied gear, a bell rung at the right second. [512] When the storm passed, he carried the rake down to the ridge one last time. [527] He watched it emerge black and slick between receding waves, and laid the handle among the obsidian as if returning a borrowed tool. [550] The tide would read it now, letter by letter, with a patience longer than any winder's. [566] Odwin climbed back to his tower and resumed the only work he trusted himself to do. [582] He wound the clock. [586] He listened to the pendulum hold its breath, and in each pause he promised, quietly, to be worthy of the next swing. [608]
