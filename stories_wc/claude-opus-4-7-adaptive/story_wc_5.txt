Ilsa crossed the rim of the crater at the hour when the world held its breath. [16] Below her, the withered orchard slept under permafrost, its apple trees curled like the knuckles of old hands. [34] She was a flash-paper alchemist, and she had come here to finish a slow thing. [49] In her satchel rode a storybook illustration plate, copper with a tin-bright face, pressed once for a children's tale about a girl who carried stars in her apron. [77] The plate was the reason for everything that followed. [86] Her teacher, before dying, had whispered that every heartbeat was a small map, and that if one could find the right stillness, one could draw the country of oneself. [115] Ilsa meant to translate her heartbeats into maps, and she believed the orchard, being silent, could help her listen. [134] She knelt between two trees and began. [141] First she emptied her pockets: apple pits, burnt fuse ends, a torn page, two pewter buttons, the foil from a lozenge, the cracked spine of a pencil. [168] She arranged these discarded items in careful rings, creating patterns that matched the rhythm thudding under her ribs. [186] Long pause, short pause, long; the buttons marked the heavy beats, the pits the light ones, the foil the catch in her throat that appeared only when she thought of her teacher. [218] Snow drifted down in the shape of patience. [226] It is difficult to describe how a person demonstrates patience with her body, but Ilsa did it: she let her shoulders fall, unclenched her fingers one knuckle at a time, and breathed so slowly that frost flowered on her lashes without melting. [268] The orchard watched. [271] When the pattern was complete, she set the storybook illustration plate at its center, face up, the little painted girl still gathering her starry apron forever. [297] From a flat envelope, Ilsa drew her flash-paper. [305] This was her craft: sheets treated to vanish in a breath of light, leaving only what they touched behind. [324] She had folded tonight's sheet into the silhouette of a heart, because she was tired of metaphor and wanted the symbol plain. [346] She laid the paper over the plate. [353] Then she waited. [356] Above the crater, the sky pulled back its curtain, and a single thread of old light, older than the orchard, older than the teacher, leaned down through the bowl of the world. [388] People called such light many names; Ilsa called it safekept starlight, because it had been held for her by distance, for centuries, until she was ready. [414] She struck her flint. [418] The flash-paper went in one white exhalation, and in that instant the starlight passed through the vanishing sheet, through her heart-shape, and onto the copper plate. [444] The engraved girl drank it. [449] Ilsa pressed her palm to the plate to seal the impression, counting her pulse against the metal as it cooled. [469] One beat, two, three. [473] Each throb settled into the copper like a pin into a map, and where her blood had argued loudest, tiny bright points bloomed, constellations of her own making. [501] When she lifted her hand, the plate bore a chart: not of streets, not of stars, but of the private country she had never before been able to show anyone. [531] She recognized the shape of it, and wept once, silently, the way a bell finishes ringing. [547] The orchard did not stir. [552] The crater did not stir. [557] Only the tone of the air shifted, as though stillness itself had stepped forward a pace. [573] Ilsa gathered the discarded items back into her pockets, leaving the pattern only in memory. [588] She wrapped the plate in felt, slid it beneath her coat, and climbed out of the crater while the world was still holding its breath. [613] Behind her, the orchard kept its silence, which had never been empty, only patient, waiting for someone small enough to be honest and brave enough to listen. [640] Somewhere in her satchel, a map now existed that no cartographer could have drawn. [654] She walked home carrying safekept starlight against her ribs, and her heartbeat, for the first time in years, matched the country it had made. [678]
