Ilyen was older than the sky she lived in. [9] She remembered mammoths the way other people remembered childhood pets, fondly and without apology. [23] That was the trouble with the reincarnation memory returning in late middle age: every small grief turned out to be ancient. [44] The tidal library stood on a reef of cirrus, a cathedral of reading rooms whose shelves rose and fell twice a day as the cloud-tides breathed. [70] At high tide, books floated to the upper galleries; at low tide, whole centuries surfaced on the lower deck, inviting whoever was brave enough to read them. [97] Ilyen was the only librarian who had been a contemporary of extinct species, and the administration believed this qualified her to catalogue what the world had lost. [124] It was lonely work. [128]

On her desk sat a brass sundial she had carried through seven lives. [141] It was atomically reflective, polished so deeply that the shadow it cast seemed to belong to a different hour than the one she was living. [166] When she tilted it, she saw not her face but the faces of every woman she had been. [184] They all looked tired. [188]

After the reincarnation memory arrived, Ilyen had stopped sleeping properly. [198] Her nights were brittle awakenings, each one a sharp crack between dreams of tundras and the quiet humming of the library's lamps. [220] She would sit up gasping, certain she had heard hooves on frozen ground, only to find the cloud-tide rolling in beneath the windows. [243] Something in her wanted to dissolve back into the long past, where she had not been alone. [260] She fought it. [263] She had not survived extinction after extinction to become a ghost in her own life. [278]

Her plan was simple, though it took months to admit: she wanted to translate isolation into independence, to make solitude a tool rather than a wound. [304] To do that she needed counsel older than grief. [313] So, one windless morning, she descended from the tidal library by the cloud-stair and walked inland by the hum of an ancient stone circle that only she could still hear. [343] The stones stood on a hill where aurochs had once grazed, their low frequency thrumming up through her soles. [362] She set the brass sundial at the center. [370] Sunlight struck it and split, throwing a ring of small suns across the grass. [384] The stones answered. [387]

Their hum thickened, and within it she heard the voices of her previous selves, not as chorus but as conversation. [407] The girl who had mourned mammoths spoke to the woman who had mourned the great auk, who in turn spoke to the one who had watched thylacines fade. [435] Ilyen listened. [437] She had expected wisdom; what she received was company. [446] They told her that to coexist with absence was not to carry it, but to let it stand beside her like another stone in the circle. [472] Extinction, they said, was not silence; it was a different kind of presence, and she had always known how to hold it. [494]

By dusk the hum had settled into her bones. [503] Ilyen climbed back to the tidal library as the lower shelves surfaced, the day's lost centuries glistening. [520] She returned to her desk, tilted the sundial, and for the first time saw only one face in it, her own. [541] Her previous lives were still there, she understood, but they had stepped back to make room. [557] That night she slept through without the brittle awakenings. [566] In the morning she began a new catalogue, not of what the world had lost, but of what had learned to coexist with loss: the lichen on the stones, the salt on the cloud-tide, the librarian at her desk. [605] She wrote steadily, independent at last, a contemporary of extinct species who had finally agreed to be a contemporary of herself. [626]
