Piran had been aboard the antique airship Cartwheel three months before she noticed the hopscotch board chalked on the observation deck. [21] It was faint, half-scuffed, eight squares curling between the brass telescope and the rail. [35] The crew walked over it daily without looking down. [44] Piran, a map apprentice by trade and diligently curious by temperament, always looked down. [58] She was the youngest aboard, and though the navigators invited her to supper and the stokers taught her card games, her work kept her largely alone in the chart-room, surrounded by parchments she could read and companions she could not quite. [99] It was a sociable sort of isolation, the kind that taught a person to speak with ink. [116] Her master had sent her up here with a single charge: to chart the next great discovery, whatever it proved to be, and bring home coordinates worth a ledger's weight in silver. [148] So when the hopscotch squares caught the low autumn light one evening, she crouched and looked properly. [165] The numbers were wrong. [169] Instead of one through eight, the chalk showed a scatter of glyphs - some Antic, some Mareen, some she did not recognize. [190] She fetched her polyglot's phrasebook from her bunk, thumbed to the dog-eared page that marked her last unfinished translation, and began to work. [213] The dog-ear was her grandfather's habit; he had folded pages like prayers, and she had inherited the book and the folding both. [235] Square by square, she began to note the symbols in her field journal. [248] Mareen glyphs meant "light," Antic meant "stranded," and a third tongue, Old Felish perhaps, read "above the wheeling birds." [267] She sat back on her heels. [273] The Cartwheel was, that very week, crossing the Vellimark flyway amid starling murmurations so thick the portholes went dark whenever a flock rolled past. [297] Above the wheeling birds. [301] Piran traced each square's position and compared them to the airship's deck plan. [314] The hopscotch was a map. [319] By noting the secret patterns in that dusty hopscotch board, measuring the distances between glyphs as if they were bearings, she began to reconstruct a route. [345] The squares plotted a course not outward, but downward - through the flyway and into the Hollow Fells, where sailors' tales spoke of a stranded luminance: a lantern-glow trapped in a valley, not a star nor a fire, that refused to travel with the turning of the earth. [392] Her master had called such tales nonsense. [399] Her grandfather had called them unfinished. [405] That night she brought her lantern to the captain's cabin. [415] Captain Oray listened while the birds boiled past the windows, a living weather. [428] "Whose chalk?" he asked, and Piran admitted she did not know. [439] "A previous apprentice," he guessed, stirring his tea. [447] "They come and go - some leave songs, this one left a puzzle." [459] He tapped the journal. [463] "You've done more with it in an evening than three have done in a year." [478] He agreed to deviate - two days south, no more - because a diligent apprentice who translated a hopscotch was the sort of apprentice whose hunches were worth indulging. [505] They descended at dawn through the last of the murmurations, the starlings parting around the envelope like water around a prow. [526] In the Hollow Fells, precisely where the chalk had foretold, a cold glow lay pooled between two ridges. [544] It did not flicker. [548] It did not move with the sun. [555] It simply remained, patient as a dog-ear, waiting for someone diligently curious enough to notice. [570] Piran stood at the rail with her phrasebook open and her pencil poised. [583] She would not name it yet; naming was the last step, after measurement, after sketching, after the long labor of understanding. [604] But she took out her journal, turned to a fresh page, and wrote the first note of the next great discovery: coordinates, a date, and - in her grandfather's hand - a careful, deliberate fold.
