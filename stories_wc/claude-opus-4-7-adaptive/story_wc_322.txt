Mara called herself a fever dream architect because the title fit better than "designer." [14] She built rooms inside other people's delirium—spaces that held fast while the rest of the mind liquefied. [32] Her latest commission was the Hollerith Optical Illusion Gallery, a long hall of mirrored alcoves where paintings shifted their geometry depending on where a visitor stood. [58]

The curator, an old friend named Esdras, had hired her for a reason he refused to name until she arrived. [78] He led her past the Penrose stair, past the anamorphic skull, past the tunnel whose vanishing point seemed to breathe. [98] At the last alcove, he stopped. [104]

"It is losing people," he said, with the unsettling calm of someone who had already grieved. [120]

"Losing them how?" [123]

"They step inside. [126] They come out changed. [130] Their memories alter as they look. [136] Small things. [138] A brother's name. [141] The color of a childhood door." [147]

Mara examined the alcove. [151] It held nothing but white walls and a single spool of sinew thread resting on a pedestal—tendon-pale, knotted in three places, older than the gallery. [177]

"The previous architect left this," Esdras said. [184] "He called it the seed. [189] He said the room's enchantment was rational, not mystical. [198] A system. [200] But he could not tell me the system before he forgot his own hands." [214]

Rational enchantment. [216] Mara had trained in the discipline herself. [223] It meant magic by weaving patterns—lattices of intention, repetitions that compelled the world to agree. [239] Nothing arcane; only a logic so precise it bent perception the way a lens bends light. [255]

She understood, then, why Esdras had called her. [263] The previous architect had begun something and lost himself finishing it. [274] The gallery was trying to complete its own pattern by borrowing from whoever entered. [288]

"Why did he build it?" she asked. [295]

"To discover the texture of connection," Esdras said. [303] "He thought if he could weave a room where strangers briefly shared one remembered thing, he would know at last what it felt like—to be attached to another mind." [333]

Mara laid her palm against the wall. [340] Realistically imaginary: that was the phrase her teacher used for structures that were not real but behaved with consistent physics. [360] This alcove was one of them. [366] A room that existed only when observed, and that observed back. [377]

She sat cross-legged on the floor and took up the sinew thread. [389] The work was slow. [393] She did not chant or invoke. [399] She knotted. [401] Each knot was a node in a diagram, each span between knots a proposition: here, perception folds; here, memory resolves; here, the observer and the room exchange nothing. [429] She was rewriting the alcove's argument so that it no longer asked a tithe. [443]

Hours passed—or what felt like hours. [450] The gallery outside fell quiet. [455] The optical illusions along the corridor held their breath, their impossible staircases not climbing, their vanishing points not receding. [474] Within Mara, a feeling began to erupt: grief, recognition, the strange tenderness of understanding a dead stranger. [491]

The previous architect had not been cruel. [498] He had only been lonely, and he had tried to solve loneliness like a problem in geometry. [515] He had woven a pattern that took pieces of people because pieces were what he could comprehend. [532]

Mara finished the knot she was working on and tied it off. [544] She did not undo his pattern. [550] She completed it—rerouted the final thread so that the texture of connection he had reached for was carried outward, diffused into the gallery itself, rather than drawn inward from each visitor. [582]

When she stepped out of the alcove, Esdras was waiting with two cups of cold tea. [598]

"Well?" he said. [601]

"It will not take from them anymore," she said. [610] "It will give a little instead. [616] A small, shared impression. [620] Warm wood. [622] A window. [624] Nothing anyone will miss gaining." [629]

Esdras drank his tea. [633] He was, she noticed, almost smiling. [639]

"And its architect?" [642]

"He got what he wanted," Mara said. [649] "Too late for him. [653] Just in time for the room." [659]

They walked back down the corridor. [665] The impossible staircase behaved, for once, like a staircase. [674] The skull looked only like a skull. [681] Mara kept the sinew thread, the last loose end, coiled in her palm—a receipt, or a promise, or a map to the next delirium someone would need built. [710]
