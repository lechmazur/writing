Decades later, Imra returned to the coast where her grandmother had vanished. [12] She came as what she had become in the intervening years: a hands-on folklorist, chronically curious, with dirt perpetually under her nails. [34] The village called the place the Drowned Orchard. [42] A sunken orchard greenhouse ringed by shipwrecks lay just past the tideline, its iron ribs still holding panes of green glass against the pull of the Atlantic. [69] At low tide you could wade inside, where drowned apple trees leaned like mourners. [83] Her grandmother had tended those trees, and according to the villagers, the sea had simply taken her one autumn evening, leaving behind a basket, a shawl, and the stubborn insistence among locals that memory itself had drained from the family like water through sand. [127] Imra's mother had forgotten her own wedding song; Imra had forgotten her grandmother's face. [141] She had come to recover stolen memories. [148]

The old fisherman Ahren met her at the breakwater with a lantern and a warning. [163] "The tide-thieves work when habits break," he said. [171] "Between the old season and the new, when the fishermen change nets and the orchard stops bearing, they reach through and take what's unanchored." [195] He pressed something small and pale into her palm: a dried fairy ring mushroom, brittle as paper. [212] "Your grandmother gave me that the night she left," he said. [223] "She said it would help whoever came looking." [231]

Imra waded into the greenhouse at midnight, the mushroom dissolving on her tongue like a wafer of chalk. [249] Above her, through the salt-mottled panes, the sky was visible in fractured pieces, constellations sliced by iron mullions into unfamiliar shapes. [270] She understood, then, what her grandmother had meant in old field notes about reading the heavens in panes. [288] One could detect the tide-thieves by studying broken constellations; where a star sat behind a cracked pane, it fractured into a direction, a hint, a thread. [314] Imra traced them patiently, the folklorist's habit of taking the world at its word. [328] Orion's belt split across three panes and pointed down, through silt, to the base of the oldest apple tree. [347]

She dug with bare hands until she found a tin box, and inside, wrapped in oilcloth, a bundle of small things: a photograph, a pressed violet, a lock of child's hair, a recipe in her grandmother's cursive. [384] Each object landed in her chest like a bell. [393] She remembered: the smell of yeasted bread on Sunday mornings, the cadence of a lullaby in a language no one now spoke, her grandmother's hands slipping a ribbon into her hair. [424] The thieves had not taken memory so much as buried it, the way squirrels bury acorns and forget them, hoping rot and time will do the rest. [451] Her grandmother, hands-on as any folklorist, had done the only thing she could before the tide took her: she had hidden everything important beneath a star that would someday point the way home. [484]

Imra carried the tin up through the kelp and the slanted trees, past the shipwrecks whose ribs had cradled the greenhouse for generations. [507] Ahren waited with his lantern lifted. [513] He wept, quiet and embarrassed, when she showed him the photograph of a laughing boy he'd also forgotten. [531] "My brother," he whispered, astonished to have him back. [540]

The air tasted of cold apples and iron and autumn bonfires elsewhere. [552] It was the crisp nostalgia of an October that keeps its promises. [564] Imra stayed three weeks, returning each dusk to the greenhouse with notebook and lantern, cataloguing what the village had lost and mapping the star-shards pane by pane. [591] Neighbors brought her tea and old grievances, and left carrying names, faces, songs. [604] She did not catch the thieves; folklorists rarely do. [613] She only made the stolen things louder than the silence, until the sea grew embarrassed and let them go. [632] On her last night she left a fresh fairy ring mushroom in the tin, reburied beneath the pointing star, for whoever would come looking next. [657]
