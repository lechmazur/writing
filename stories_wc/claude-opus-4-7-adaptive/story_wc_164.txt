<story>Mira worked as a sea-foam recollectionist, walking the shore at low tide to skim memories from the froth. [18] She transliterates emotions into soft glyphs on wet paper, so grief becomes a spiral, wonder a comma, regret a long unbroken line. [40] Her trade paid poorly, but it had purpose: she chased missing pieces, the small truths people lost before memory faded. [60] One gray morning she found crater glass half-buried in the sand, black and pocked like a fallen star. [78] Inside it she glimpsed a reflection that was not her own - a child walking a path that ended mid-step, then vanished. [99] The image shook her. [103] For years she had wondered why some paths disappear, why certain trails in her own life dead-ended without reason. [122] The glass was a clue. [127] She carried it home, where a migrating bird tapped at her window. [139] A stormfinch, off course, its beak full of seeds it kept dropping absentmindedly on her sill. [155] Each seed, when it struck the wood, gave off a faint chime. [167] She realized the bird was scattering directions. [174] Following the seeds, bending to collect each one, she traced a winding route past the harbor, through a pine draw, up a hill she had never climbed though it stood in plain view. [207] The final seed rolled into a crack in the hillside. [217] Mira pried the stone aside and found an iridescent cave inside a giant crystal geode, its walls shimmering violet and rose. [238] The air hummed. [241] Light broke and remade itself on every facet. [249] She laughed, then felt her chest tighten - the cave had a tone she could only call cheerful gloom, like a lullaby sung at a funeral. [274] At the center sat a low pool, and in it floated fragments of paths: cobble sections, a garden gate, a staircase ending in mist. [298] Missing pieces. [300] Each fragment pulsed faintly. [304] She understood, by conjecture more than proof, that this was where disappeared paths came to rest - the roads people had started down and then surrendered, the choices unchosen. [332] She lifted the crater glass and held it over the pool. [343] Through it she saw the child again, and this time recognized herself at seven, walking toward a lighthouse. [361] She had turned back that day because her mother had called. [372] The path closed behind her. [377] It had not been erased; it had been set adrift, here, waiting. [389] Mira pressed her palm to the water and transliterated what she felt: longing, relief, a thin cheerful note underneath. [408] The glyphs bled into the pool. [414] The floating fragments began to knit, not into her old life, but into something new - a bridge made from all the turnings she had not taken, offered back. [442] She did not cross it. [447] She only looked. [450] The bridge was proof that paths disappear not because they are lost, but because they are stored, held for anyone who remembers to come looking before memory fades entirely. [479] That was answer enough. [483] She thanked the stormfinch, which had followed her inside and now preened on a quartz spur. [499] She pocketed the crater glass. [504] On the walk home, the gloom in her chest turned strangely bright, and she whistled a tune she had not known she remembered. [527] That night she wrote her first honest ledger. [535] For every client who came to her asking where a sibling had gone, why a love had cooled, what had happened to the child they once were, she copied a glyph from her palm. [569] She told them the truth: the paths were not gone, only elsewhere, and the crater glass could show them if they were willing to look. [594] Some wept. [596] Some laughed. [598] Most did both, which was, she thought, the proper response to being alive. [611] Cheerful gloom suited the work. [616] The stormfinch stayed through winter, dropping seeds across her floor like punctuation. [628] Mira followed each one, mapping more caves, more pools, more bridges made of abandoned roads. [643] She never ran out of missing pieces. [650] She never stopped transliterating. [654] And sometimes, when the froth rolled in just right, she swore she could hear the lighthouse calling, patient as a pulse, waiting for whoever remembered to walk back. [682]</story>
