<story>Horan had buried two hundred strangers before the package arrived. [10] It sat on his porch wrapped in oilcloth, smelling of kelp and cold iron. [24] Inside lay a lighthouse lens, cracked down its spine, each ring of glass shivering with a light that had no source. [45] The note beneath it held one line in his sister's handwriting: "They are still calling." [60] Mera had been dead nine years. [66] He set the lens on his kitchen table and listened. [76] Through the fracture came a faint Morse-patter, thin as a moth at a window, the kind of distress signals only the drowned could send. [100] Horan was a weary undertaker. [105] He had spent three decades closing eyelids, folding hands, whispering apologies to people who could no longer accept them. [124] He had promised himself, each winter, that he would retire. [134] He never did. [137] His neighbors called it devotion; his wife, before she left, had called it something else, inadvertently stubborn, a man who mistook exhaustion for purpose. [161] The lens pulsed again. [165] At dawn he walked down to the harbor, the lens wrapped against his chest, and hired a diving bell from a man who asked no questions. [191] The coordinates from the note led past the shelf, past the trench, to a place the charts marked only as silt. [212] It was not silt. [216] It was a city. [220] A forgotten underwater city library spread below him in drowned colonnades, its roof of barnacled domes split open like a cracked egg. [242] Books floated in the currents, their pages swollen into strange fruit, their inks long since read by fish. [260] Horan descended through a broken skylight and felt the weight of the sea stop pressing him. [276] The water here behaved like air; lamps burned without fuel; corridors bent into rooms that should not have fit inside the building that held them. [301] He was occupying impossible spaces, and the spaces seemed to know him. [313] At the center of the atrium waited a great bronze frame where a lens had once turned, the twin of the one he carried. [337] He understood, then, what Mera had sent. [344] The library was a lighthouse turned inward. [351] It had once gathered the lost and given them shelves to rest on: a name, a page, an address. [370] When its lens shattered, the signals had scattered, and the dead had wandered the tides ever since, tapping at lighthouse glass the world over, searching for a door. [398] Horan's whole trade had been a small version of this work. [409] He had always wanted, without knowing he wanted it, to give rest a permanent address. [424] He fitted the broken lens into the frame. [432] The cracks refused to close, but the light caught anyway, leaning through the fractures in angles that felt like listening. [452] Names began to arrive. [456] They came as cold breaths along the shelves, as inked letters reassembling themselves on swollen pages, as small pressures at his sleeve. [478] He worked. [480] He wrote each name into the library's ledger with a pencil that never dulled, and each name, once written, went quiet. [501] He had spent his life chasing stillness for other people; now the stillness chased him, gathered around him, thickened into something like gratitude. [524] Mera's name came last, small and apologetic. [531] He wrote it twice, to be sure. [538] Above, in the world of bells and burials, the lighthouse on his own coast blinked back to life after decades dark. [559] Sailors would later say the beam seemed to revitalize the whole shoreline, gulls returning, nets full, fog lifting at strange hours. [580] They would not know that its light now came from below. [591] Horan did not return. [595] The diving bell surfaced empty, its rope neatly coiled, as if someone had made a point of tidying after himself. [615] In the underwater library he keeps the ledger still, turning pages that never wet, answering the distress signals as they come. [636] He is no longer weary. [641] He has, at last, an address.
