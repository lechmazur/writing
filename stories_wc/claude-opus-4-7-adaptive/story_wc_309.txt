Wel was an underachieving mage in an age when creativity gets automated. [12] The conjuring guilds had outsourced wonder to humming engines that spat out spells faster than any apprentice could memorize them. [32] Wel had failed his last three exams. [38] His sigils smudged, his incantations hesitated, and his familiar, a moth, kept drifting toward the lampposts of more competent tutors. [58] So when the College assigned him to inventory an icebound spire above the clouds, he understood it as exile dressed up as errand. [81] He climbed by the only practicable route: mist walking, a slow craft that traded muscle for patience, letting fog harden under each careful boot. [105] The automated mages flew on schedule; Wel walked, and arrived breathless. [116] The spire was beautiful in a way that seemed lit from inside, even though the windows had been frozen blind for centuries. [138] Inside, he found dust and clarity. [144] He set his pack down to unstrap the inventory glass, the brass buckles complaining in the cold. [161] The catalog was short: a kettle, a chair, three uncatalogued books, and, on a velvet pedestal at the top of the stair, a single weathered stone. [187] It was labeled, in a hand older than the College, garden folly keystone. [200] Wel laughed, because keystones held arches, and there was no arch here, only ice and wind and an absurdly modest rock. [221] He picked it up to log its dimensions, and the room answered. [233] A faint glow rose from the floor, not bright, not theatrical: a brilliant dimness, as though the air remembered being a candle. [255] Wel knew the term from a dull lecture he had nearly slept through: lingering luminance, the residue of a place that had once been loved. [280] He turned the keystone in his hands and felt the spire shift around him, accommodating impossibilities the way a kind host accommodates a late guest. [305] Walls thinned. [307] Where the icebound stair had ended, a small grove unfolded, sun-warm, smelling of mint and wet stone. [324] At its center stood a folly: an arch of weathered brick missing only its keystone. [339] He understood, slowly, what the spire had been built to preserve. [351] Long ago, a mage had loved a garden so much she had bound its memory into a stone, then hidden the stone where wonder could not be cheaply replicated. [380] The College had labeled it inventory because the College only respected what it could quantify. [395] Wel set the keystone gently into the arch. [403] Nothing thundered. [405] The grove only breathed a little easier, and the dim light grew steady, the way a held hand grows warm. [425] He sat on the threshold of the impossible garden and ate the apple from his pack. [441] It tasted like an apple. [446] That, he realized, was the point: to find the sacred in the simple, the way the old mage must have, before the engines and the schedules and the perfect spells generated by faceless looms. [480] He stayed three days. [484] He cataloged nothing. [487] When he descended, mist walking again because flying would have been a kind of forgetting, his report consisted of a single sentence: The folly is in good repair. [515] The College fined him for brevity and reassigned him to a remote weather post he had not requested but secretly wanted. [536] His moth followed him there, and stayed. [543] On clear nights he could see the spire, faintly glowing above the clouds, a small lantern in a sky full of automated stars. [566] He thought, often, of the keystone, holding nothing that needed holding except memory. [579] That, he decided, was magic enough for one underachieving life. [589] He began keeping a journal of small things: the steam off tea, the way frost edged a windowpane, the precise hush before snow. [612] None of it could be automated, and that, at last, was the lesson. [625]
