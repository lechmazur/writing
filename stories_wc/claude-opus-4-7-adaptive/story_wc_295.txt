<story>The observatory sat on the ridge like a half-closed eye, its copper dome tarnished green by a century of salt wind. [21] Iona climbed the spiral stair at the hour she preferred best, that hushed trough between midnight and the first gray, when the telescope drums were cool and the charts smelled faintly of cedar. [54] She was a lucid dream chronicler by trade, paid in small coin to record the visions of sailors and widows, and she had long believed she healed them with words, because the right sentence, written down, could lift a sleeper's weight like a tide lifts a stone. [101] Tonight she carried a daylily-pressed telegram, its paper gone the color of old tea, the orange petals still faintly ribbed across the message like veins across a palm. [129] It had come from her brother Emeric three weeks before his fever took him, pressed flat by his own hand so it might survive the journey. [155] The telegram said only: The temple reads itself in the sky. Help me finish. [169] Emeric had spent his last year trying to decode temple inscriptions at Keral Vash, a ruin where every lintel bore a chain of glyphs no scholar could reconcile with any known tongue. [201] His final letter, fuller than the telegram, had insisted the glyphs were not words but cues: each mark corresponded to a variable star, and the rhythm of brightenings and dimmings carried the meaning. [234] The priests, he wrote, had not carved sentences; they had carved schedules, and a reader had to wait centuries for the sky to finish speaking. [259] Iona had laughed when she first read it, gently, the way one laughs at a feverish child; now, in the celestial mapping observatory that had been their father's, she bent over his notes as if over a cradle. [297] She aligned the great refractor to Mira, then to R Coronae Borealis, then to a dim flicker in Lyra whose period Emeric had matched to the third glyph of the western door. [329] Night by night, through variable stars, she worked his table of correspondences, and night by night the glyphs began to yield. [350] They did not spell commandments or histories. [357] They spelled, Iona realized, a kind of lullaby. [365] The temple at Keral Vash had been a place where mourners came to lay their grief on the priests, and the priests, unable to carry it alone, had asked the sky to hold a portion forever. [401] Shared burdens, the inscriptions repeated in their patient, pulsing cipher: no one bears alone what the stars agree to witness. [421] Iona set down her pen and felt Emeric near her shoulder, not as a ghost but as a weight finally shared. [442] He had been carrying the temple for a year, and the temple, she understood now, had been carrying him in return; it had only needed a second reader to complete the circuit. [474] She copied the lullaby into her chronicle in her slow, careful hand, phrase by phrase, because this was how she had always worked: she healed with words the sleepers who could not heal themselves, and now she healed her brother, posthumously, by finishing his sentence. [519] Outside the slit of the dome, the variable stars kept their long appointments, dimming and brightening with the patience of old mourners. [541] Iona pressed the daylily telegram flat between the pages of the new chronicle, so the orange veins could lie against the lullaby like a hand on a hand. [569] When understanding dawns in a celestial mapping observatory, it does not arrive as thunder; it arrives as a slow bluing of the east, as a single unhurried breath. [597] She let the refractor drift closed and sat in the midnight solace of the hushed room, no longer its only witness. [618] Somewhere over Keral Vash, she knew, the first ray was finding the lintels; somewhere in her chronicle, Emeric's unfinished sentence lay at last completed, not by her alone, but by all the slow lamps overhead that had agreed, long before either of them was born, to help carry what the living could not.
