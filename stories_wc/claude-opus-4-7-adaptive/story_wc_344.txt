Vesna climbed the spiral of frost-glass stairs under the soft hush of a distant morning. [15] She was a lunar-eclipse locksmith, and her trade only opened when the moon swallowed itself. [30] Tonight there was no eclipse, only the long pale lull before sunrise, but she had come anyway, because the frozen library above the clouds did not wait for her schedule. [60] Inside, the air smelled of cedar and old snow. [69] Books slept in their alcoves with their spines turned inward, shy as wintering animals. [83] Vesna carried a satchel containing her grandfather's clockmaker's gear assortment, brass teeth in graduated rows, each tooth sized for a different broken silence. [106] He had been a horologist; she had inherited only his patience and the lonely afterimage of his hands. [124] She had come to learn the rhythm of repair. [133] At the library's heart waited the thing that had drawn her: a hall of mirrors, frozen mid-shimmer, each pane fractured in a different direction. [157] The hall had once shown travelers their possible mornings; now it showed only static, hauntingly bright, a noise of light that never settled. [180] Travelers had stopped coming, of course. [186] Only Vesna still climbed, drawn by the quiet driftwood ache of a craft that had outlived its world. [204] She knelt at the threshold and unclasped the satchel. [213] The repair was beyond any apprenticeship she had ever finished. [223] Her instructions, if she could call them that, came through scraps of prophecy hidden in the library's corner: torn vellum slipped between volumes nobody read, written in a hand that resembled her grandfather's but was older, much older. [261] She had spent three winters collecting them, smoothing them flat, arranging the fragments into something almost like a manual. [280] She drew out a tiny copper vial and worked to uncap it with cold-stiff fingers. [295] The prophecy said one drop on the central pivot would let the gears find their seats. [311] She tipped the vial; a single bead of oil, thick as honey, fell into the cracked silver. [328] Then she chose her gears. [333] One by one, she fitted the brass teeth from her grandfather's assortment into the empty hollows along the mirror frames, listening for the small click that meant a tooth had agreed to its place. [367] Some refused; she set those aside without bitterness. [375] Repair, she was beginning to understand, was less a craft of forcing and more a craft of asking. [393] Hours passed. [395] The hush of distant morning thickened, and the clouds outside the great windows began to pinken at their undersides. [414] When the last gear settled, the hall of mirrors began, very softly, to turn. [428] Each pane caught a different sliver of the rising light and threw it onto the next, until the whole corridor brimmed with the current the prophecy had promised. [456] In the centermost mirror, Vesna saw not herself but her grandfather as a young man, bent over a workbench, mouthing the same patient counts she had taught herself in his absence. [487] He looked up, as if he had been waiting only for the sound of her gears. [503] She did not cry; the cold would not allow it. [513] She only nodded, the way one nods to a colleague across a quiet workshop. [527] The mirrors continued their slow rotation, casting morning into morning, every reflection a possible day for someone, somewhere, still willing to climb. [549] Vesna gathered her remaining gears, recapped the vial, and folded the prophecy scraps into her coat. [565] There would be other halls, other broken mornings, other locks that opened only beneath shadowed moons. [581] She had learned, finally, the rhythm of repair: it was not a hammering but a listening, a long patient agreement between yesterday's hands and today's. [606] Outside, the clouds opened like the pages of a book she had finally been allowed to read. [623]
