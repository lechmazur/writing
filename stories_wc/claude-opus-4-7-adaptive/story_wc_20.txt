<story>The search party disbanded at dusk, leaving Ister alone with the rain and the cold iron of the foundry. [19] He was a balanced alchemist by training, which meant he weighed everything twice and believed nothing once. [36] Tonight he weighed a name. [41] Mira. [42] She had worked the bellows beside him for six years before vanishing into the flood channels three nights ago. [61] The constables had called the search off an hour before sunset, citing the rising water and the thinness of hope. [81] Ister did not call it off. [87] He walked the rain-washed foundry as though it were a cathedral, past cooling crucibles that hissed when drops found them, past molds heaped like patient skulls. [113] In his palm he turned a zodiac coin, its twelve faces still warm from his pocket. [129] Mira had pressed it into his hand on her last shift, laughing, telling him it was factually mystical: a trinket stamped in a real mint, yet blessed by an aunt who claimed the stars listened. [164] He had pocketed it without argument, because that was his way, balanced, even when balance looked like indifference. [182] Now the coin felt impossibly heavy. [188] He set it on the anvil. [194] Rain drummed the tin roof in uneven meter, and the forge coals still glowed where he had banked them that afternoon, certain he would return before the search turned grim. [224] He wanted to redeem the hours he had spent not listening. [235] Not the Mira who joked about star charts, but the Mira who had, two weeks back, asked him quietly whether names had weight, whether being called apprentice forever might slowly crush a person. [268] He had answered with a proverb, balanced and useless. [277] She had smiled and stoked the fire. [284] Now he understood his motivation had been cowardice dressed as equilibrium: he had refused to understand the weight of names because weighing them meant picking one up. [311] He lifted the hammer. [315] Alchemy, real alchemy, was not transmutation but attention; he had been taught this and forgotten it. [331] Tonight he would relearn it by exploring limits, the method every apprentice feared and every master eventually required. [349] He struck the zodiac coin once, cleanly, and it split along the ram and the scales. [365] The sound traveled through the wet foundry like a bell remembering itself. [377] From a clay jar on the shelf he drew gold dust, the kind he kept for inlays and favors, and he thought of kintsugi, the craft of mending breakage with precious seams so the scar becomes the story. [415] Kintsugi intentions, Mira had once called his habit of fixing guilt with gestures; she had meant it kindly, but the phrase had stung because it was true. [442] He would honor the sting now. [448] He melted the dust in a thimble crucible, then laid the coin's halves together on the anvil, pressing gold into the fracture until the seam glowed like a vein of dawn. [479] As he worked, he spoke her name aloud. [487] Mira. [488] The syllables dropped into the rain and did not float; they sank, exactly as heavy as a life. [506] He spoke his own name afterward and felt it weigh the same. [518] That was the lesson, factually mystical: every name, said with full attention, pulled the speaker toward the named. [536] He wrapped the mended coin in oilcloth and pocketed it. [546] The search party had disbanded; that did not mean the searching was over. [559] He would walk the flood channels himself, lantern lit, calling her not as apprentice but as Mira, the whole of her, until the water gave her back or the night refused him. [591] Either outcome, he understood at last, would be his to carry, seamed in gold, glinting under every future rain. [610] He stepped out of the foundry into the downpour, the coin warm again against his ribs, luminous with regret and purpose. [631]</story>
