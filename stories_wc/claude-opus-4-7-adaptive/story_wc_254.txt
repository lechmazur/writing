<story>Nine winters had passed since the Council forbade the cloud-shadow poets from speaking in the open air. [17] After the ban lifts, Veda returns to the cobalt atrium by the frozen lake, where the humming quartz colonnade still waits. [38] She had been trained, aesthetically driven, to compose only from what she could see cast upon stone: the slow drift of vapor, the purple bruise of storm-light, the hush a cumulus throws across a valley. [73] The atrium's roof was open to the sky, its floor a pane of dark blue glass laid over the lake ice, and every column of the colonnade sang at a pitch only shadow could translate. [108] Veda stepped inside with a lantern-moth hush in her chest, breath pale, certain she had forgotten how to listen. [127] The ban had been enacted because a previous poet, Oren, tried to paraphrase the quartz itself, to translate its tremor into human syllables, and the columns had cracked at the presumption. [158] For nine years the sanctuary had been sealed, its music left to drift unheard over the ice. [175] Now she was here because the Council, weary of its own prohibitions, had finally conceded that beauty might justify its own risks. [197] Veda's motivation was older than the ban; she had come to stride through boundaries once thought sacred, not to desecrate them, but to learn whether they had ever been walls or only long, careful shadows. [232] She set her lantern on the blue glass and waited for a cloud. [245] One came, slow as a thought surfacing, and threw a gray dialect across the atrium floor. [261] The colonnade hummed in response, each quartz pillar lighting from within as if recognizing an old companion. [278] Veda began to write the shadow, line by line, as she had been taught. [292] But the old method would not hold tonight. [300] She felt, rising in her, a tremor of certainty that the shadow and the quartz were not two things to be described separately but one phenomenon moving through dissolving boundaries: cloud into stone, stone into light, light into the ache behind her sternum. [343] Oren had failed, she suddenly understood, not because he tried to paraphrase the quartz, but because he had paraphrased it as if it were separate from him. [370] He had stood outside the music and reached for it. [380] Veda, aesthetically driven past safety, did the opposite. [388] She let the hum enter her ribs and spoke the cloud's gray dialect back to the columns in a voice that was no longer only hers. [414] The colonnade answered; the ice beneath the atrium floor answered; the lake answered in slow, blue syllables. [431] Nothing cracked. [433] The lantern on the glass dimmed, and in that lantern-moth hush Veda finished the poem, though finished was the wrong word. [454] She released it, and it kept going, moving outward into the lake country as weather, as rumor, as the first line of something the Council would not be able to ban again because it would not be able to locate it. [495] When she stepped back out onto the frozen lake, the cloud had moved on, leaving the quartz bright and patient. [515] She understood the Council would come tomorrow to ask what she had done. [528] She rehearsed an answer and found it would not hold: any phrasing smaller than the thing itself would be a lie, and any phrasing equal to it would be the thing again, and thus ban-worthy. [563] So she would paraphrase, badly and on purpose, and let them believe the poem had been smaller than it was. [583] The cloud-shadow poet walked home across the lake, leaving one set of footprints that, from any distance, looked exactly like weather. [604]</story>
