Mira Solberg had edited atlases for nineteen years, and she still mistrusted any map that claimed certainty about its own edges. [25] That was why she had come north, alone, to the rusted outpost by the frozen lake, carrying a satchel of corrected proofs and a single private question: where did the rivers begin? [56] Every cartographer she respected had a private question. [64] Hers was older than her marriage and had outlasted it. [74] The outpost was a tin-roofed shack the color of dried blood, propped on stilts that the lake had been trying to swallow for forty winters. [101] Inside, a stove ticked, a lamp guttered, and the falconer Aslak waited with the patience of a man who measured time in feathers. [125] "He hasn't returned," Aslak said, before she could ask. [134] "Then I'll wait," Mira answered, and shrugged off her coat with the subtly confident ease of someone who had outwaited harder things than weather. [159] She did not say that the falcon was the last instrument she trusted. [171] Satellite imagery lied politely; surveyor's notes lied with conviction. [180] But a bird sent up to ride the thermals above the watershed would, on its return, drop a thread tied to its leg, and the angle of that thread, plus the hour, plus the wind, would herald the location of the unmapped springs better than any committee in Oslo. [228] Aslak had learned the trick from his grandmother. [236] Mira had learned to believe him from a single corrected coastline that had saved her career. [251] While they waited, she unpacked. [256] Among her papers was a polyglot's phrasebook she had carried since girlhood, its Sami section dog-eared so many times the corner had worn translucent, like a fingernail held to lamplight. [285] She used the dog-ear now to mark a sentence she meant to ask Aslak: the one about whether a river that begins underground may still be said to begin. [311] He smiled when he read it and did not answer. [321] The afternoon thinned. [324] A worrying tranquility settled over the outpost, the kind of quiet that arrives before something large makes up its mind. [344] The lake did not creak. [349] The wind, which had harassed them all morning, lay down. [359] Mira watched the single window and noticed that the condensation on the glass was forming an unusual pattern: not the random ferns of cold air meeting warm, but parallel rivulets that bent, all of them, toward the lake's northwest shore. [400] She studied it the way she studied a contour line that did not close. [414] The pattern of condensation on glass was, she realized, the room's own breath being pulled, very gently, toward an opening she could not see. [440] "Aslak," she said, "is there a draft?" [448] "There is always a draft," he said. [455] "But not that one." [459] He came to the window, looked, and went still. [468] In nineteen years of editing, Mira had developed a feel for the moment when several uncertain truths collapse into one. [488] Geographers called it a singular convergence: when independent errors, instead of canceling, point at the same hidden fact. [507] The springs were northwest. [511] The bird, when it came, would confirm it. [518] The draft already had. [522] They heard the falcon before they saw him, a dry rasp of wings against the flat air. [538] After the falcon returned and settled on Aslak's glove, Mira did not look first at the bird. [555] She looked at the thread. [560] It hung at the angle she had hoped for, the angle the window had already promised. [575] "Northwest," Aslak said, unnecessarily. [580] "Three valleys. [582] Under the scree." [585] Mira opened her notebook and, with the dry wit she reserved for small victories, wrote: Source located by consensus of bird, breath, and stubborn woman; recommend revising plate 14. [615] She underlined stubborn twice, then crossed it out, then wrote it back in. [628] Outside, the lake held its silence like a held note. [638] She thought of all the maps she had corrected, and of the one map, her own, that had finally corrected her. [659] Rivers, it turned out, did not begin where the eye expected. [670] They began where the room leaned, where the bird circled, where the phrasebook fell open of its own accord to the question one had been afraid to ask. [698] She closed the book on its worn dog-ear and went to put on her boots. [712]
