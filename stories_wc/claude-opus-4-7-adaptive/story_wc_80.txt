Every evening after the drawbridge rises, Imra wades into the drowned quarter with her satchel. [15] As a firm shore surveyor, she is paid to measure erosion, log tide heights, and certify which pilings still hold the city together. [38] That is the ledger she hands in on Friday. [47] The other ledger she keeps for herself. [54] It lives inside an antique traveling inkwell, a brass-hinged thing her grandfather carried across three colonies, its stopper warm as a held breath. [77] She unscrews it inside the submerged pavilion at the end of Sill Street, where once a summer orchestra had played and lovers had leaned over a railing now green with river. [108] Water reaches her ribs. [112] The pavilion's dome, half caved, filters the dusk into a hushed blaze, copper light trembling on copper columns, silent as a cathedral after Mass. [136] Imra is stoically sensitive, which is how the town describes her when they mean she will not weep in public but will not forget a single name. [163] She had surveyed this pavilion before it sank. [171] She had measured it, in fact, the week the council voted to abandon the lower ward rather than wrest the river back from its new course. [197] Wresting costs money. [200] Abandonment costs only memory, and memory, the mayor said, is free. [211] Imra disagreed then and disagrees now, in her private and unofficial capacity. [223] She has made herself the archeologist of abandoned dreams. [232] The pavilion is her first dig. [238] From her satchel she draws a slip of oilcloth paper and uncaps the inkwell. [252] She writes with the brass pen tucked in its lid: the name of the bandleader who drowned here, the promise a girl once shouted from the upper tier, the vow of a merchant to rebuild and the month he left town instead. [294] She writes in small, neat script, the hand of a woman used to surveys. [308] Then she folds the paper into a tight strip. [317] The city has budgeted her a modest sum each quarter for foundation repairs, iron clamps and cement, the cosmetic shoring of a ward nobody will return to. [344] It is here, placing notes in foundation repairs, that she smuggles her private archive into the official bones of the town. [365] She tucks the oilcloth between clamp and stone, troweling mortar over it like a secret into a mouth. [383] If anyone ever dredges the pavilion, they will find not only iron but testimony. [397] If no one ever does, the absences she records will at least be held, like pressed flowers, interlaced with the city's own forgetting. [420] That is her theory of what has happened here: not simple loss, but interlaced absence, where every missing thing is woven through what remains, impossible to extract without unmaking the whole. [451] The orchestra is gone, but the bandstand's curve still hums when wind crosses it. [465] The lovers are gone, but the railing holds the polish of their hands. [478] The money that might have saved the ward is gone, but the debt endures in the tilt of every upriver wall. [499] One cannot wrest a single thread without pulling the tapestry. [509] So she writes, and mortars, and wades home. [517] Tonight she finishes three notes. [522] The last concerns her sister, who left for the capital the year the flood came and never answered any of the letters Imra sent through the post. [549] She hesitates only a moment before pressing the folded slip into the north pier. [563] The inkwell goes back in the satchel, warm against her hip. [574] Above, the drawbridge will not drop until dawn. [582] The quarter belongs to her and to the water and to whatever saints watch over undone things. [599] She climbs the old stair to street level, pauses at the seawall, and looks back at the copper dome beneath its skin of river. [623] Somewhere under it her handwriting is setting into the stone, sentence by sentence. [636] Someday, she believes, a diver will come with a chisel and a curious heart and crack open a clamp and find her folded words, and the abandoned dream will have, at last, its archeologist. [670] Until then, Imra surveys. [674] The river rises. [677] The brass inkwell, patient as a relic, waits to be opened again. [689]
