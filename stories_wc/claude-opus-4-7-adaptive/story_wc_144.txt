<story>The repair shop sat at the far end of the mist-coated rail depot, wedged between a coal bunker and a fountain no one remembered commissioning. [25] Inside, Orrin worked with a magnetic calm that travelers mistook for indifference. [37] He was a heartfelt artisan who oversaw the bench while blind, having lost his sight to a boiler burst twenty years earlier. [59] Clients came to him anyway, because he repaired what no sighted technician could. [72] His method was strange but famous: he read machines by examining light refraction through water features he kept near every station of his shop. [96] Small basins, dripping siphons, a shallow glass pool fed by a thin copper pipe -- each one translated vibration into ripple, and ripple into a language he could feel with his fingertips. [127] On the morning the courier arrived, the depot bells were ringing in sympathy with the harbor two miles south. [146] A ship called the Verity was preparing for her maiden voyage, and her anchor was being raised within the hour. [166] The courier set a velvet case on Orrin's counter and opened it with trembling fingers. [181] "An artificial hummingbird drone," she said. [187] "The captain's navigation aide." [191] "It stopped singing last night." [196] Orrin lifted the drone with both hands, feeling the tiny rotors, the filigree of its throat, the almost-warm hum still trapped in its core. [220] The Verity relied on the bird to decode wind feedback into direction, he knew; without its voice, the ship would sail blind. [242] He carried it to the largest of his basins and set it gently on a brass cradle above the water. [262] Steam drifted in through the depot windows, mingling with the mist already pooling along the floorboards. [278] The courier watched, uncertain, as Orrin tapped the drone's wing and listened to the ripples break across the pool. [297] "Light," he murmured, "tell me where the wound is." [306] The morning sun, slanting through the depot's high glass, struck the ripples and scattered refractions across his workbench in quivering stripes. [327] Orrin could not see the stripes, but he felt their warmth shift across the skin of his forearm, and that was map enough. [350] A coolness at his wrist meant a shadow; a shadow meant a break in the bird's inner lattice near its second gear. [372] He reached for a strip of brass foil and began to patch the fracture, his fingers moving with the trained tenderness of a surgeon who trusted the world to stay still for him. [405] The courier glanced at the station clock. [412] "They're lifting the anchor in twelve minutes," she said. [421] Orrin nodded once, unhurried, and the calm of him seemed to steady her shoulders. [435] He soldered a hair-thin seam, realigned the throat coil, and breathed a test pulse into the chamber at the bird's belly. [456] The drone shivered, hummed, and lifted an inch above the cradle, wings a blur. [470] Ripples spread in a perfect, symmetrical star, and Orrin smiled. [480] "Balanced," he said. [483] "Tell the captain to trust the song, not the compass, for the first three leagues." [498] The courier tucked the drone back into its velvet case and ran for the platform. [513] Through the depot's open arches, Orrin heard the distant clank of a great chain catching, link by link, as the Verity's anchor climbed toward her hull. [539] The fountains in his shop trembled faintly in answer, and the stripes of refracted morning light slid once across his hands like a farewell. [563] He turned back to the bench, where another machine waited, ticking softly, hoping to be heard.
