"Behold," Iren announced to no one, "the glassblower ascends the tower stairs, bearing kettle and conviction." [16] Her voice rang through the abandoned control room, bouncing off defunct monitors and a console furred in dust. [34] Outside, the ice was melting. [39] For the first time in twenty winters, water ran openly down the observatory walls, ticking against the copper gutters like a patient clock. [62] Iren set the kettle on the old induction plate she had wired back to life and narrated, with theatrical gravity, the placing of the kettle, the flipping of the switch, the dutiful glow of the filament. [98] She liked to describe her chores aloud; it was how a thoughtfully spirited woman kept herself company after the institute's staff had fled south. [122] They had not exactly been forced to abandon the station, merely advised, then re-advised, until advice acquired the shape of eviction. [143] Iren had stayed. [146] Someone, she reasoned, ought to keep a lamp lit in a place that had once been dedicated to careful thinking. [166] Her own work waited in the hangar below: a kiln she had rebuilt from the reactor's shielding, and rows of molten-glass domes cooling on racks. [191] She called them inquiry-shells. [195] Each dome was a small, transparent room, warm enough to stand inside while the world outside dripped and shifted, quiet enough that a person could hear themselves ask a question without flinching. [227] That was her motivation, stubborn and unfashionable: to craft safe zones for inquiry in a century that preferred answers shouted fast. [248] While the kettle climbed toward boiling, she opened the station's last logbook. [260] Tucked between two pages lay a cracked leather bookmark, its edge worn pale where a thumb had worried it for years. [281] It had belonged to Doctor Veern, the director, who had pressed it into her palm on the day of the evacuation and said, "Hold my place." [307] He had not returned. [311] Iren ran her finger along the crack, which had deepened since last spring. [324] "The glassblower," she murmured, her narration softening, "considers a small brown wound in leather and wonders whether every object eventually learns to split." [347] The kettle whistled. [350] She poured, steam rising in an unhurried column that reminded her of her own breath inside the kiln's mouth. [369] Between sips, she studied the control panel, its dead dials, its labels in three languages, and permitted herself a thought she usually postponed: the ice was really going. [397] Not metaphor, not forecast. [401] Going. [402] She had lived so long inside the logic of permanence that the thaw felt personal, as though some interior glacier, calved and carried along with the outer one, had begun to let go. [435] The thawing heart, she thought, half-smiling at the phrase, is a nuisance because it drips on the floorboards. [453] She wiped at her cheek with the back of her wrist and declared, for the benefit of the empty chairs, "The glassblower weeps efficiently, using only one eye and finishing under schedule." [485] The joke steadied her; that was the trick of weighty lightness, to carry the load by laughing at the strap. [505] She returned to the logbook and set the cracked bookmark carefully forward by one page, marking a future she intended to reach. [527] Then she described her descent: "The glassblower takes the stairs two at a time, having decided that grief is a poor reason to refuse tea to strangers." [554] For strangers were coming. [558] A radio burst last Tuesday had carried three voices through the static, researchers walking north along the exposed shoreline, looking for shelter and something like a library. [585] Iren meant to have an inquiry-shell ready for each of them, warmed, lamplit, stocked with paper that would not rot. [605] In the hangar she unbanked the kiln and began, once more, to narrate. [618] "The glassblower gathers the gather," she said, "lifts the pipe, turns the molten heart until it learns a shape that can hold a question without breaking." [644] Outside, water found new routes over old stone. [652] Inside, in the hush between the kiln's roar and her own small voice, Iren worked on, contemplative, unhurried, refusing to abandon the one post still worth keeping: a warm, transparent room where anyone arriving cold could sit down and begin to wonder aloud.
