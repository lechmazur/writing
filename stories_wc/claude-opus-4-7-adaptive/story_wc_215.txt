<story>Hale had kept the hives for nine silent years before the ghosts arrived. [13] The beekeeping oasis sat like a bruise in the scorched wasteland, a dozen crumbling apiaries ringed by date palms whose fronds had turned to brittle copper. [39] No bees remained. [42] Hale remained. [44] He was a recluse by habit and by vow, tranquilly perceptive in the way of men who have learned that listening outlasts speaking. [67] The morning the dead became visible, he was sweeping ash from the threshold of the honey-house. [83] He straightened, astonished, broom forgotten. [88] A woman stood among the hives, translucent as rinsed linen, her hands folded over something that caught the pale sun. [108] Behind her, others gathered: a boy with cracked sandals, an old apiarist in a veil, a soldier whose uniform Hale could not place. [131] They did not speak. [135] None of them spoke. [139] Hale, whose own tongue had grown rusty from disuse, understood at once that speech was not the currency here. [158] He set the broom against the wall and walked out to meet them. [171] The woman held a stained glass box. [178] Its panes were the colors of things Hale had not seen in decades — marsh green, iris blue, the violet of a storm about to break — and inside it, suspended as if in honey, lay a folded parchment. [215] She offered it without gesture; her gaze did the offering. [225] Hale accepted. [227] The lid opened to a hinge so fine it might have been woven from eyelashes. [242] The parchment inside was blank. [247] He understood this, too, through contextual clues: the way the old apiarist touched his own throat, the way the boy's mouth moved without sound, the way the soldier's hand hovered over a pocket that no longer existed. [284] These were the words they had not spoken in life. [294] The covenant — he felt it settle over his shoulders like a veil — was tangled, older than the oasis, older perhaps than the wasteland itself. [318] Somewhere, long ago, the living had promised the dead that nothing said in love or rage or apology would be lost, that if the mouth failed, the world would keep the weight of it anyway. [353] The world had broken its promise. [359] The fires that blackened the horizon had seen to that. [369] Now the dead had come to the last man who still listened, and they had brought him their unsaid things in a box of colored glass, and they wanted the covenant mended. [401] Hale understood his task was to preserve the weight of unspoken words. [413] He did not know how. [418] He carried the box into the honey-house and set it on the scarred oak table where once he had strained wax. [439] The ghosts followed him, not crowding, simply present, a quiet congregation. [450] He sat. [452] He took up the blank parchment and a stub of pencil. [463] He did not write words; words would betray them. [472] Instead, as each ghost stepped forward in turn, he gazed at them — truly gazed, the way his grandmother had taught him to gaze at a hive before opening it — and he drew a single mark for each. [509] A curve for the woman's withheld forgiveness. [516] A short line, broken, for the boy's unsaid goodbye. [525] A closed circle for the soldier's confession. [532] A tiny spiral for the old apiarist, who had died with a recipe on his tongue. [547] The marks accumulated into something resembling a score for music no instrument could play. [561] Mute eloquence, he thought, and the phrase steadied him. [570] When the last ghost had passed, Hale folded the parchment and returned it to the stained glass box. [588] The lid whispered shut. [592] The ghosts remained a moment longer, and then, one by one, they inclined their heads and were gone, not vanished so much as finished. [616] The hives stood empty in the red noon. [624] Hale carried the box to the deepest cellar of the honey-house, where the heat of the wasteland could not reach, and set it upon a shelf beside the last dark jar of honey. [657] He spoke aloud, for the first time in a long while, though only to himself. [672] 'It is kept,' he said. [677] The oasis did not answer. [682] It did not need to. [687]</story>
