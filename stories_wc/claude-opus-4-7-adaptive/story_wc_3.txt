<story>Ishbi-Erra had spent forty years translating cuneiform before the arbor called him upward. [13] It was a magnetic arbor suspended above the clouds, its iron branches humming with a quiet polarity that held him upright when his old knees wished otherwise. [40] He had come here for one reason: to understand the physics of transition, the moment a soul crossed from one state into another. [63] His wife, Mira, had died in spring, and the tablets he translated now seemed full of thresholds he had failed to notice when she was alive. [89] At the center of the arbor grew a single black pod, and inside it rested what the old texts called the knowledge fruit seed. [113] Ishbi-Erra approached it the way he approached a damaged tablet: kindly, methodically, as if gentleness might coax meaning from the fractures. [134] He did not pluck it. [139] Instead, he sat beneath the branches and waited, because Mira's spirit had followed him up here, and he could feel her pacing the magnetic lines like a moth along a wire. [170] They spoke, though not in words. [176] He showed her his work by thinking through analogical thinking: a soul leaving a body, he suggested, was like a cuneiform stroke leaving the stylus, the intention outlasting the pressure. [206] She offered her own comparison back: a transition was like a seed, full of a tree it had not yet become. [227] That was when he understood the arbor's logic. [235] The seed was not a gift but a test of ambition, and ambition, he knew, was a haunted thing. [254] Every scholar who had climbed these branches before him had carried the same weight: a dead wife, a lost son, a vanished teacher, some beloved ghost they hoped to reestablish contact with through forbidden botany. [289] Their bones now powdered the roots. [295] Mira drifted closer, close enough that he felt the temperature of the air change, that peculiar touching distance where the living and the dead can almost exchange breath. [323] He did not reach. [327] He had translated enough elegies to know that reaching was how the others had fallen. [342] Instead he asked her, in their wordless exchange, what the physics felt like from her side. [358] She answered by pressing one cold, bright thought against his mind: it felt like reading a tablet in a language he had not yet learned, but whose grammar he already trusted. [389] It was enough. [392] He took out his stylus and his last wet tablet and began to record her words the way he always had, stroke by patient stroke. [417] He did not eat the seed. [423] He did not pocket it. [428] He placed his hand, palm-up, beneath the pod and waited until Mira's presence, satisfied, thinned and lifted away into the clouds below the arbor. [452] After the spirit leaves, the true work begins; this was something his oldest teacher had written, and only now did he understand it. [475] The seed dropped of its own accord into his palm, small and warm. [488] Not because he had earned it by taking, but because he had refused to. [502] He descended the arbor slowly, the magnetic currents humming approval through his bones, and when he reached his workroom he planted the seed in a clay cup of river silt. [532] It would not grow for him. [538] He understood that now. [542] It was a tablet meant for a student he had not yet met, a cuneiform for an era that had not yet begun. [565] He had come for physics and received grammar instead, which, he suspected, was the same thing described by a kinder god. [586] Outside his window the clouds held the arbor's shape for a long moment, then released it, and Ishbi-Erra bent again to his patient, methodical work, translating the living toward the dead and the dead toward whoever came next. [624]</story>
