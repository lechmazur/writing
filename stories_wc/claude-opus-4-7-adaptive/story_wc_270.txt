During the reconstruction, after the capital fell and the councils began stitching the provinces back together with hasty laws, Grandmother Irsa returned to the volcanic hot springs of Halvek to finish what her comrades had started. [36] She was eighty-one, an insurgent still, though the new regime called her a "founding elder" and printed her face on ration stamps. [58] She hated the stamps. [62] They softened her, made her seem settled, when in truth she had come back to the steaming basalt pools for a weapon she had buried there forty years before. [91] Not a rifle. [94] A seed, of sorts: an embryo in amber, honey-colored and no larger than a plum, sealed by the old mothers before the war and hidden beneath the hottest spring. [123] Inside it slept the last heir of a lineage whose destiny had been implanted, generation by generation, through ritual and grafting and whispered oath. [147] The mothers had called it duty. [153] Irsa, who had burned three garrisons and lost two sons, called it a leash. [167] She had come to unseal it, but not to fulfill the old prophecy. [180] She meant to rewrite it. [185] Her motivation, she told the junior cadre who ferried her up the slope, was to evolve tradition with intent, not to abolish it and not to obey it. [213] The girl nodded as if she understood and went back down the path. [226] Irsa preferred the company of the ferns anyway. [234] Since a fever in her sixtieth year she had been accidentally telepathic with plants, a condition the doctors dismissed and the ferns confirmed. [257] They spoke in pressures rather than words, in the slow green insistence of growing things, and they had been the ones to tell her where the amber truly lay. [286] The maps were wrong. [290] The old mothers had moved it before the final raid, and only the lichen remembered. [305] On the third morning Irsa climbed to the highest pool and found, snagged in the sulfur-yellow reeds, a child's kite, abandoned years ago, its paper bleached but its long cloth tail intact. [337] The ferns hummed with recognition. [342] Her hands trembled as she drew the tail free of the mud. [354] Down its length ran a cryptic script, inked in a hand she knew: her own sister's, dead these thirty years. [374] The mothers had always said the seal would yield only to one who could read what the wind had carried, and Irsa had dismissed that as riddle for riddle's sake. [404] Now she sat cross-legged on the hot stone, steam curling around her, and began to decode. [420] The script used the cipher the insurgents had invented in their first winter, the one Irsa herself had helped design: verbs hidden in color-names, nouns folded into the count of stitches. [451] Her sister had written it knowing Irsa, and only Irsa, would come. [463] "Unseal it, but choose," the tail read. [470] The child inside is neither weapon nor saint unless you make it so. [483] We bred destiny in; you may breed intent. [491] Irsa wept, quietly, for the sister who had left her this permission. [503] Then she walked into the scalding water, knelt until the heat bit her bones, and reached beneath the ledge where the lichen had directed her. [528] The amber came up warm, alive with trapped light, the small curled shape within it no bigger than her thumb. [548] She held it to the steam. [554] The ferns on the bank leaned toward her, attentive. [563] "You will not inherit a war," she told the embryo, and she spoke aloud because the plants were listening and would remember for her. [587] "You will inherit a question. [592] The old mothers implanted a path. [598] I am unsealing it, and I am widening it, so that when you wake you may walk sideways off it if you choose." [621] She cracked the amber against the basalt. [628] It split cleanly, the way ripe fruit splits, and the reconstruction around her, with its committees and its ration stamps, felt suddenly like the shallowest surface of a much older water. [659] The embryo breathed. [662] Irsa, insurgent grandmother, cupped the new life in palms that had held rifles and grandchildren and now held a tradition she had finally agreed to carry, because at last she had been allowed to change it. [698] Above her, the empty kite lifted in a gust and drifted out over the steaming pools, its decoded tail streaming, its message spent. [721] She watched it go. [725] The ferns, pleased, went quiet.
