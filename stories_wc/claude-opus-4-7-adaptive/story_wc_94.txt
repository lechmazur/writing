Ilke climbed to the observatory the night the censors finally came for her last notebook. [15] She was a poet whose verses had been blacked out so often that her own pages looked like maps of some redacted country. [38] The old astronomical tower stood above the clouds, and the storm below unfurled in silver terraces, lit from within by lightning. [59] She had come to keep a vigil in pause, a waiting that refused to end or begin. [76] In her satchel she carried an old bus timetable, its pages yellowed, and wedged between two forgotten routes was a sealed envelope addressed to a name she had not spoken in years. [108] The envelope held a poem that no censor had ever seen, the only uncensored thing she had left. [126] On the instrument bench sat a darkroom timer, a relic from the astronomer who had once photographed comets here. [145] Ilke wound it with fingers that shook, not from cold but from the strange calm she wore tonight, a mood she could only call furiously tranquil. [171] She had promised herself one hour, no more, to decide whether to burn the envelope or send it. [189] The timer began its slow grind, counting down the only honest minutes she had known in a decade. [207] Storm-kissed wonder filled the dome as lightning branched across the glass and the wind whistled through the slit where the old telescope pointed at nothing. [232] Ilke sat with the envelope on her lap and thought about why she had come all this way, up past the tree line, up past the masks. [259] High places were where masks slipped; she had learned that from her mother, and later from interrogators who tired of their own faces. [282] She needed the mask of the state to slip now, and her own with it, because she had climbed here to understand the cartography of scars: hers, the country's, her lost friend's. [314] If she could map them, she could rarefy the pain, thin it into something breathable, something that might keep a reader alive. [336] The poem in the envelope was addressed to Andrei, who had been taken in the winter raids and whose letters had stopped the following spring. [361] She did not know if he was alive, but the timetable in her bag had belonged to him, a joke between them about places they would visit when the borders opened. [392] The timer ticked; the storm pressed itself against the dome like a listener. [405] She opened the envelope, not to reread the poem, but to add a line. [419] In the margin she wrote: I have finally learned the shape of what they took from us. [436] She resealed it with candle wax and returned it to its hiding place between the pages of routes that no longer ran. [458] Below, the clouds rolled like a second sea; above, the stars began to bleed through the thinning storm. [476] Ilke thought of every poem the censors had drowned in black ink, and how each black bar was itself a kind of map, a coordinate of what the state feared. [506] If fear had geography, then she was its cartographer, and scars were her legend. [520] The darkroom timer stopped with a small, definite click, loud as a verdict in the rarefied air. [537] She did not move for another long minute, keeping her vigil in pause, honoring the silence the way one honors a grave whose stone has been removed. [564] Then she stood, tucked the timetable into her coat, and began the long descent. [578] She would carry the envelope until the borders opened, or until her own did, whichever came first. [595] Behind her, the observatory kept watch over a storm that was almost a sky, and she descended, furiously tranquil, into a world where she would write again. [622]
