<story>Vey stood barely as tall as the reading lectern, yet the archivists still called her titan. [16] She was diminutive by any mortal measure, the last of a bloodline that had once shaken mountains, and she bore the old authority with a regally unpretentious quiet, wearing dust on her sleeves where others would have worn crowns. [55] The temple library had been lost in time for three centuries, folded into a seam the maps refused to keep, and she had found it only because the books themselves had called her. [88] Above the shattered dome, the constellations had paused. [96] That was what the oracles named the phenomenon: across the pause of shifting constellations, a held breath between one cosmic turning and the next, a silence into which quiet work could be done. [129] Vey meant to do quiet work. [135] In the capital, a stranger sat on her grandfather's throne, wearing his face with the smugness of a borrowed coat, and the court had bowed without blinking. [162] She had come here to unmask the pretender, and the proof, she suspected, was buried in the library's oldest shelves. [182] She moved between the stacks with a single iron gall ink pellet pressed between thumb and forefinger, as small as a blackberry seed and just as potent. [209] Crushed and wetted, it would bleed onto parchment the deep, stubborn black that outlived empires. [224] But she used it sparingly, and only on the margins, because the library's custodian, whoever or whatever still lingered here, communicated through cryptic messages left in library books, and she had learned to answer in kind. [260] The first note had been tucked into a treatise on succession: a pressed violet and the line, He wears a rune of drift. [283] She had known the rune by reputation, a sigil of softening, of things sliding gently out of place, of heirs who were not heirs and oaths that could not hold. [313] The second note, slipped into a bestiary, told her the rune was sewn into the pretender's collar; the third, in a book of hymns, told her the rune's hidden anchor lay in the library itself. [348] Sunder the anchor, the last message read, and the illusion will sunder with it. [362] Vey found the anchor on the eighth night, carved into the underside of a reading desk, a small, elegant spiral that seemed almost to hum with apology. [389] She crouched on the cold tiles, crushed her ink pellet onto her fingertip, and drew the countering mark from memory, the one her grandfather had taught her when she was too young to understand inheritance. [424] The ink took. [427] The rune of drift shivered, and the library around her began its graceful collapse. [441] It did not fall in the way of stone and violence; it fell in the way of a held note finally released, shelves settling into air, columns softening into their own dust, centuries of patience at last permitted to exhale. [481] She stood amid the slow descent, unhurried, because the collapse was not destruction but homecoming, the library returning itself to the world it had been hidden from. [508] The last book to drift down to her was blank except for a final message, written in a hand she had not seen since childhood. [533] Go home, it read, and be seen. [540] She pressed her palm to the page in thanks. [549] Above her, the constellations resumed their wheeling, the pause ending with the small, courteous click of a door closing behind a guest. [571] When Vey walked back into the capital at dawn, she did not raise her voice; she simply entered the throne room, and the rune of drift on the pretender's collar unraveled like wet thread, and the face borrowed from her grandfather slid off the usurper's bones, and the court saw at last what had been sitting among them. [629] She did not ascend the dais at once. [637] She stood instead at its foot, small and unadorned, and waited for the kingdom to remember her name, which, after a long and patient breath, it did.
