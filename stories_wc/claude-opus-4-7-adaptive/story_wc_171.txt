On the ancient terrace at midsummer, Vaela set down her ebony clipboard and listened to the war breathe. [18] Below the marble railing, the plains of Orren still smoldered where yesterday's skirmish had ended in the usual nothing. [37] She had been a peace negotiator for the Forever War through seven regents, three schisms, and one false armistice. [56] The clipboard held her only remaining ideal: a single page of clauses she refused to cross out. [73] Tonight the envoys from both houses would arrive, and she had to muster whatever was left of her conviction to meet them. [95] The terrace had stood since the first treaty, older than the grievance itself, its flagstones worn smooth by the feet of negotiators who had failed before her. [122] Custom demanded the parley begin only during the slow dance of celestial bodies, when the summer moon pressed against Vehrin, the red wanderer, in what the priests named the Reconciling Hour. [153] She had always believed the ritual was theater, but theater was how humans consented to be moved. [170] Through ritual significance, otherwise unbendable men bent. [177] Lord Caen arrived first, trailed by his cousin who bore the Silvered Grievance of the southern clans. [194] General Iseret came last, as protocol required of the offended house, her armor lacquered the color of dried blood. [213] Vaela greeted each with the operatively gentle voice she had cultivated over decades, a tone soft enough to disarm and firm enough to redirect. [237] She poured the ceremonial water, recited the old apology neither side believed, and opened the clipboard. [253] On its first page, in her own hand: "No settlement that does not return the children." [269] The hostages had been exchanged as collateral in the fourth year, and had been born, raised, and conscripted since. [288] Every prior negotiator had traded the clause for easier concessions. [298] Vaela had refused for eleven years, and for eleven years the fighting had continued. [312] That was the price of her ideal, and she had paid it in other people's blood. [328] Tonight she intended to anchor that ideal in practice or abandon it. [340] Above, the moon crept its measured inch toward Vehrin. [349] "I propose," Vaela said, "a mutual return of all hostages, with full citizenship restored, enforced by joint witness." [367] Iseret laughed without humor. [371] "My son is twenty-three and does not know my face." [381] Caen looked at his own hands. [387] "The clans took daughters who are mothers now," he said. [397] "There is no return that does not wound further." [406] Vaela had expected this. [410] "Then redemption has a price," she said, "and tonight each of you will name what you can bear to pay." [430] She set the clipboard between them like an altar stone. [440] The ritual, she knew, would carry what reason could not: the old words, the slow sky, the terrace that had outlived every argument staged upon it. [466] For hours they spoke, and the moon inched, and Vehrin reddened, and the clauses on the ebony page thinned to what could survive daylight. [490] Iseret agreed to receive a stranger son and to call him hers; Caen agreed to send daughters home to mothers they had forgotten. [513] Each concession was a small ruin. [519] Vaela wrote nothing grand, only what was true: imperfect returns, imperfect amnesties, a peace made of flawed and undeniable beauty. [539] When the moon passed Vehrin and the Reconciling Hour closed, both envoys pressed their seals to the page. [557] No one wept; grief that old does not weep. [566] Vaela closed the ebony clipboard and felt the decades settle into her knees. [579] She had not won; winning was not the shape of this work. [591] She had made the ideal small enough to live inside a human life, and large enough that two houses would step into it tomorrow. [615] The terrace, ancient and patient, held them all a moment longer beneath the parting stars. [630]
