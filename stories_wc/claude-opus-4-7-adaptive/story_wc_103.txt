Mira Halverston designed hedge mazes for clients who wanted to lose themselves on purpose. [14] Her finest commission stood on the Veiled Estate: a living bridge garden suspended over a slow green river, where boxwood and yew had been coaxed into arches that breathed. [43] She had built it through hyperbolic geometry, folding more leaf and corridor into the span than its footprint should allow. [63] Visitors who entered at dawn often staggered out at dusk, swearing they had crossed countries. [78] Mira had not crossed it herself in seven years. [87] That morning she returned, carrying a tin of fading photographs and a sliver of bone-pale metal her grandmother had called a halo fragment. [110] The photographs were of her sister Edda, who had wandered into the maze at twelve and never come out. [129] Each year the images grew paler, as though Edda were forgetting how to be remembered. [144] Mira intended to find her, even if it cost her the orderly life she had built around the loss. [163] More truthfully, she wanted to discover the texture of time itself, the grain her sister had slipped through. [181] The halo fragment hummed against her palm as she stepped onto the first bridge. [195] She had set this metal into the maze's center long ago, salvaged from a chapel ruined by lightning, and the hedges had grown crooked toward it ever since. [223] Now its twin sang in her hand, eager to rejoin. [233] The garden received her in a quaking reverie. [241] Light fell sideways through unseen prisms; sparrows hung mid-stoop, painted on the air. [254] Each step folded her deeper than the last, the hyperbolic corridors blossoming wider the further she walked. [271] She passed a bench where she had once sat eating plums with Edda; the plum stones still lay there, lacquered with seven summers of rain. [296] At the second bridge she realized she had become retrocognitively trapped. [307] The maze was no longer showing her its present; it was unspooling her past around her, scene after scene, as though she walked a corridor of yesterdays. [334] She saw her father planting the first hedge; she saw herself, age nine, drawing spirals in a notebook. [352] She saw Edda, twelve forever, standing where the bridges braided together, holding the original halo fragment with both hands. [371] Edda looked up, startled, as if Mira were the ghost. [381] "I told you not to follow," the girl whispered, and Mira finally understood. [394] Edda had not been lost. [399] Edda had stepped sideways into the geometry on purpose, learning the same texture Mira now ached to taste, and she had been guarding the seam ever since. [426] The fading photographs in Mira's tin were not Edda forgetting them; they were Edda holding the past closed, gently, with her own slow hands. [450] If Mira pressed forward, both halo fragments would meet, and the seam would disintegrate. [464] Every yesterday her sister had patiently stitched would unravel into a single screaming present. [478] Mira knelt on the trembling bridge. [484] She had wanted the texture of time, and here it was, rough as bark beneath her knees, soft as Edda's twelve-year-old breath. [506] It was not a place to enter; it was a duty to keep. [519] She set her halo fragment down on the planks and slid it back the way she had come, a small bright stone retreating along the grain. [545] Edda smiled, the smile of a designer recognizing another designer's restraint. [556] "Go back and tend the hedges," she said. [564] "Keep them crooked toward the center." [570] Mira walked out at dusk, having entered at dawn, swearing she had crossed a country. [585] In her tin the photographs were no paler than yesterday. [595] They would never brighten, but they would not vanish either, so long as Mira kept designing mazes that bent the world enough to hide a sister inside it. [623]
