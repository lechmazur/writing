<story>Halvard Nyborg had skippered the supply boat to Kolm Light for nineteen winters, and in all that time he had spoken perhaps four unnecessary words. [25] Reticence suited him the way salt suited rope. [33] But he was warmly observant, noting which keeper needed extra kerosene, which one hoarded letters unopened, which one had begun stacking stones along the cliff path in small, deliberate cairns. [63] The cairns were a language. [67] Three flat stones meant all is well; a round stone capping a tall one meant send word to the mainland; a single black pebble balanced on quartz meant I am afraid tonight. [99] Halvard had learned the grammar from the old keeper Mies, who had taught it, he said, to keep sane during the long polar blacknesses. [123] This autumn, the cairns at Kolm had changed. [131] The new keeper, a young woman named Ines, left only one shape: a pebble on quartz, night after night. [150] Halvard, who generally let fear belong to its owner, began leaving things. [163] A tin of cocoa on the landing. [170] A folded paper crane. [174] A piece of sea glass the color of moonlit milk, worn soft by forty years of tide. [191] He said nothing when he handed them over, because saying nothing was his gift. [205] On the seventh visit, Ines pressed a letter into his palm. [216] It was unsigned. [219] "To whoever finds this," it began, "the lantern room is not empty after the lamp flickers out." [236] She asked him to carry it to the next light down the coast, and to ask that keeper to add a line and send it on. [262] So began the chain. [266] Anonymous letters, passed from keeper to keeper along the spine of the archipelago, each adding a confession, a warning, a small act of tenderness for a stranger they would never meet. [297] Halvard was merely the ferryman of the words. [305] But words, he found, were heavier than kerosene. [313] By midwinter the letter had grown to eleven pages, and every page circled the same question: what was Ines hearing in the haunted lantern room after the lamp flickers out? [343] Footsteps, she wrote. [346] A humming that resembled her drowned brother's humming. [354] A weight, she said, that watched her kindly, as if it wanted something she could give. [370] Halvard read it on the wheelhouse bench while the diesel shuddered beneath him, and felt a luminescent ache rise in his chest, the kind of grief that glows instead of burns. [401] He had lost people, too. [406] He had never told anyone. [411] At the next run to Kolm, he climbed the tower with her after the lamp flickers out and sat in the cold dark, listening. [436] The humming came, low and patient. [442] It was not menacing; it was lonely. [449] Ines wept quietly. [452] Halvard, who had not wept since his wife, wept too. [463] When the dawn crept in, he placed the piece of sea glass on the lantern sill. [479] "This," he said - the first unnecessary sentence of his year - "was hers." [491] "She used to say the sea made jewelry out of broken things." [503] "Keep it near the lamp." [508] He wanted, he realized, to bottle the feeling of safety he had once known beside her - to fit it inside a pane of green glass and leave it burning for someone else. [540] To protect, which was, he finally understood, the only real reason he had stayed on the water so long. [559] The anonymous letter travelled onward that spring, through eight lights and fourteen hands, and returned at last to Ines in a different envelope, thicker, rimmed with salt. [586] Every keeper had written the same last line, though none of them had conferred: you are not alone in the dark. [607] Halvard, delivering it, said only, "Read it by the glass." [617] Outside, on the cliff path, he built a cairn: three flat stones, steady, level, unmistakable. [632] All is well. [635] And for the first time in nineteen winters, it was. [645]</story>
