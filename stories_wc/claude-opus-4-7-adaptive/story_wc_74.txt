Pell had not slept in nine nights, but the rooftop greenhouse forgave her. [13] Above the smog-choked city, where neon bled into haze like watercolor on wet rice paper, she kept a glass room full of mosses, ferns, and one stubborn lemon tree that refused to fruit. [44] She painted there because the air was cleaner by a few honest degrees, and because the moonlight, when it found her, arrived filtered through condensation into something close to mercy. [73]

Tonight the moon was waxing, a pale comma over the chimneys, and its glow settled on her worktable in a hush she had taught herself to call saffron quiet. [102] That was her word for the hour when insomnia stopped feeling like punishment and began to feel like a long, patient room. [124]

On the table lay a calligrapher's moon-tipped brush, its ferrule carved from bone, its tuft pale as new frost. [144] An old woman had pressed it into Pell's palm a year ago at a night market, refusing payment, saying only, "You have the face of someone who forgets she is owed gentleness." [174] Pell had never seen her again. [180] She had been painting with the brush ever since, methodically hopeful, the way one waters a seed without knowing its name. [201]

Her project was strange and probably foolish. [208] She was copying, from a brittle book she had rescued from a flooded library, the alphabets of lost languages: Tangut, Rongorongo, Linear A, Aquitanian, the dotless script of a coastal people whose name no scholar had agreed upon. [247] She did not understand the words. [253] No one did. [256] That was the point. [260]

Each glyph she rendered with the moon-tipped brush, and each she dedicated, silently, to a stranger who had once been kind to her: the bus driver who waited; the boy who returned her dropped glove; the clerk who slipped an extra peach into her bag. [305] Her motivation was small and enormous. [311] She wanted to prove that strangers remember kindness, that the gestures travel further than the giver imagines, the way a dead language still shapes the mouths of its descendants. [340]

Tonight she meant to paint the glyph for the old woman with the brush. [354] But the lemon tree's leaves cast lacework on her page, and her exhausted mind began to drift, as it always did at this hour, into the soft method she had come to trust: daydreams sparked by moonlight. [389] She let her eyes unfocus. [394] She saw the night market again, the lanterns trembling, the old woman's hands. [407] She saw, behind the woman, a stall she had not noticed at the time: scrolls hung like laundry, each printed with a script Pell now recognized as the dotless coastal one. [437]

The daydream sharpened. [440] The old woman had not been a stranger. [447] She had been a keeper, perhaps the last, of a language Pell was now blindly tracing every night. [464] The brush had not been a gift. [471] It had been a charge. [476]

Pell sat up. [479] The greenhouse seemed to rouse around her, leaves stirring in a draft she could not feel, the lemon tree releasing one bright breath of citrus into the saffron air. [508] She lifted the brush. [512] Without thinking, she drew the glyph she had intended for the old woman, then beneath it the dotless script for "received." [533] She did not know how she knew the word. [542] She knew it the way one knows a face in a crowd is turning toward you. [557]

By dawn she had filled three pages. [564] The sky bruised pink behind the smokestacks, and the waxing moon dissolved like sugar in tea. [582] Pell set the brush down with the care owed to a borrowed thing. [595]

She would find the night market again. [602] She would carry the pages. [607] If the old woman was there, Pell would show her that the script had not died, that one stranger's gentleness had survived inside another stranger's sleeplessness, branching outward in glyphs no dictionary held. [641] If the old woman was not there, Pell would leave the pages at the empty stall, weighted with the moon-tipped brush, a quiet evidence for whoever came next. [669]

Either way, the proof would stand. [675] Kindness, like a lost language, waits only for a patient hand to speak it again. [690]
