<story>After the energy beings arrived, the highways emptied, and Odile walked them alone. [13] She was a drifting roadside poet, collecting abandoned billboards like pressed flowers. [25] Her knapsack held three notebooks, a tin cup, and a frayed coil of rope she had carried since her brother's prison escape. [47] He had tossed it from the wall, climbed down, kissed her once, and vanished into a country that no longer existed. [68] The rope was all the history she owned. [76]

The energy beings called themselves nothing. [82] They hovered over cities in shifting lattices, neither hostile nor kind, and they asked only one question of any human who approached them: what pattern would you like to see? [112] Most people answered wealth, or safety, or the faces of the dead. [124] Odile had answered, truthfully, that she wanted to discover the texture of time. [137] They had smiled, if smiling was what they did, and pointed south. [149] So she walked. [152]

South led her to a terra-cotta grotto under starlight, its clay walls cupped like a patient hand around a pool of still black water. [176] An old man sat there, barefoot, ethically playful in the way of people who had survived too much to pretend at seriousness. [198] He introduced himself as the grotto's keeper, though he admitted the grotto kept him. [212] "You've come for the texture," he said, not asking. [221] "I've come because they sent me," she answered. [229] He laughed softly, poured her tea from a kettle that had no flame beneath it, and gestured at the pool. [249] "Time lives in there, in layers," he said. [257] "You find its texture by uncovering layers, slowly, the way you'd peel an onion if the onion were also peeling you." [278] She asked what that cost. [283] "Impossible choices," he said, and his cheer did not waver. [293] "Each layer asks one." [297]

Odile knelt by the water. [302] The first layer showed her the rope, uncoiled, stretched between two possible lives: one in which she had climbed the wall with her brother, and one in which she had stayed. [333] The grotto asked her to choose which had been real. [343] She chose neither, and the water accepted it, because refusing was also a choice, and the keeper had not said the choices had right answers. [368] The second layer showed her a village she had passed through last spring, where a child had offered her bread. [388] The grotto asked: save the child, or save the poem you wrote about the child, for only one would outlast the century. [410] She chose the child, though the poem was, she suspected, better. [421] The third layer was her brother, grown old, in a kitchen she did not recognize. [436] The grotto asked her to speak to him, or to let him go on believing she had died the night he climbed down. [459] She let him believe it. [464] Love, she understood now, was often a quiet arithmetic. [473]

Layer after layer, she peeled; layer after layer, she was peeled. [484] The rope in her pack grew lighter, not because it was leaving her but because she was learning what it had been for: not escape, but pattern, the knot-and-loop rhythm by which a life holds itself together. [521]

When she surfaced, the keeper had refilled her cup. [530] The starlight through the grotto's mouth had shifted by only a finger's width. [543] "Well?" he asked, ethically playful still, though his eyes were wet. [554] "Time," she said, "feels like rope." [560] "Frayed where you've gripped it, smooth where you haven't dared." [570] He nodded, as though she had passed a test he had not set. [583]

Outside, the energy beings drifted in their lattices, indifferent and luminous. [594] She walked back into their century carrying the rope, the notebooks, and one new poem she would never write down, because some patterns are kept by being lived. [622] The road resumed beneath her feet with a texture she finally recognized. [634] It was the texture of having chosen. [641] It was, she thought, enough. [646]</story>
