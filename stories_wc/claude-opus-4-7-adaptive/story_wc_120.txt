Mira wrote fortunes for a living. [6] Her slips, folded into cookies for a bakery in the old harbor district, had always read a little sideways, a little cryptic, as though the paper itself were trying to remember someone else's life. [40] She was chronologically homesick, she had decided, pining for afternoons that had never happened and winters that belonged to no calendar she could name. [64] When the longing grew unbearable, she drove north to the glass spire by the frozen lake, a boutique observatory her grandfather had built before the silence took him. [92] Inside, she kept only three things: a kettle, a notebook, and a single segment of a nautilus shell that her mother had pressed into her palm the summer before everything had turned quiet. [125] That chambered fragment was her tuning fork. [132] She believed, with the secretive conviction of someone who had tried nothing else, that every person is born carrying a symphony no one else can hear, and that she had simply been elected to conduct hers. [168] The fortunes were practice; the spire was the rehearsal hall. [178]

She arrived on the last Friday of January, when the lake had set itself smooth as an unused mirror. [197] Through the spire's curved panes she could see the thing she had come for: a mirrored horizon, ice below and milk-gray sky above, joined so precisely that the world appeared doubled, or perhaps halved, depending on where one placed one's hope. [238] The method, taught to her by no one but discovered by repetition, was this: she would sit with the nautilus segment warming in her hand and compose by mapping childhood memories onto the seam of sky and ice. [276] Each memory was a note: the creak of her grandfather's stairs became a low D, the smell of bread in her mother's kitchen a clean F, the one afternoon she had caught a minnow and returned it trembled somewhere higher, somewhere unsayable. [318]

She worked through the night in the upturned hush of the spire, where even her breathing seemed to lift rather than fall. [340] The trouble was always the same trouble: she had to finish before exhaustion hit, because once her body surrendered, the symphony slipped sideways into dream and came back garbled, another unheard piece she could not catch. [376] So she raced, pen trembling, each phrase she wrote sounding like one of her fortunes: do not look for the door; you are already inside it. [402] By three a.m. her hand cramped; by four she was mistaking her own handwriting for someone else's. [419] Still she kept mapping, pulling memory after memory up from the nautilus's spiraled chambers, each older than the last. [438] She remembered, or thought she remembered, a winter she had never lived: a lake like this one, a spire like this one, a small girl who was not quite her, waving. [469] That was the homesickness: not for a place, but for a time that had been promised and misplaced. [487] She wrote faster. [490]

Dawn came in pale and exact, a thin pink line laid along the mirrored horizon so that for one instant the sky and the ice agreed on a single color. [520] Mira lifted the nautilus segment to her ear and, at last, heard it: the symphony her childhood had been quietly composing on her behalf, note by note, all along. [549] She raised her other hand as a conductor does, palm flat, elbow soft, and the horizon seemed to lift with it. [570] The lake stayed silent to any ordinary listener, but Mira was no longer ordinary; she was, for the span of one held breath, the conductor of unheard symphonies. [598]

When exhaustion finally arrived, it came gently, like a curtain lowered by a friend. [612] She folded one last fortune before sleep: You will not remember this, but it will remember you. [629] It was, she thought, the truest thing she had ever written. [640]
