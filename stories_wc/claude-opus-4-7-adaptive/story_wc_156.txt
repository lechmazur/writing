<story>Ines restored murals for a living, which meant she spent her days arguing politely with ghosts. [16] Her hands, stained in vivid cobalt and ochre, had coaxed saints back from plaster rot and dragons out of soot. [36] She worked methodically but playfully, humming while she mixed pigment, sometimes leaving small winks inside the restorations only she would ever notice. [58] The commission that changed her came in a padded envelope containing a hobo nickel and a map. [75] The coin was old, its buffalo re-carved into a sleeping woman whose eyelid bore a tiny spiral, and on the back someone had scratched a single word: LISTEN. [103] The map led her three hours north to a shuttered estate whose owner had died mid-sentence, leaving an unfinished fresco that clients said had begun to hum. [130] What she found behind the garden gate was not a garden of plants. [143] Brass dishes rose from iron stems, each angled to catch and throw sound; little copper bowls cupped pebbles that trembled when the wind passed; a curved wall of tile threw her footsteps back to her in neat, delayed syllables. [182] It was a sound wave garden, built, the notes said, by the owner to bewitch visitors through reflected sound. [201] Ines laughed at the word, then stopped, because her laugh came back arranged into a question she had not asked. [221] The fresco waited at the garden's center beneath a dome of mirrored bronze. [234] It showed the same sleeping woman from the nickel, her eyelid spiraling inward, and around her a dozen smaller sleepers, each dreaming the one above. [259] Nested awakenings, the commission letter called the motif; the previous restorer had written those two words and nothing else before quitting. [280] Ines set up her scaffolding, laid out brushes like surgical tools, and began. [293] On the first day she cleaned centuries of candle smoke from the outer ring and heard, faintly, someone breathing in time with her. [316] On the second she retouched a sleeper's mouth and felt her own lips move in answer. [332] On the third she asked, aloud, Who are you painting for? and the garden held the words the way a bowl holds water. [355] In the pause between question and reply, something opened. [364] She saw herself, scaffolding and all, as the painted sleeper at the center, and around that self a ring of further Ineses, each waking into the next, each certain she had finally surfaced, each wrong. [399] The hobo nickel in her apron warmed like a held breath. [410] The garden answered, not in words but through reflected sound, her own voice returning braided with the dead owner's, with the previous restorer's, with voices she did not know and somehow recognized. [442] We are painting so you will stop being sure, they said together. [454] Ines understood then why each restorer had been sent a hobo nickel: a coin once fixed and federal, re-carved by a stranger's knife into something private and strange, a tiny parable of conversion. [487] She had come to this estate believing she knew what restoration was: returning a thing to what it had been. [507] The garden proposed, instead, that she exchange certainty for curiosity, and finish the fresco not by completing it but by leaving its spiral open. [531] For weeks she worked in that methodically playful way of hers, adding a sleeper whose eyelid held her own small wink, a brushstroke that doubled as a question mark, a color no pigment book listed. [566] She tuned her brush to the garden's echoes, let her humming be bent by the brass dishes, painted what the return suggested rather than what she had planned. [594] When she finally climbed down, the fresco did not look finished; it looked alert. [608] She buried the hobo nickel beneath the central tile, so the next restorer would find it, and the garden would bewitch another listener into the same slow, reflected undoing. [637] Driving home, Ines caught herself asking aloud what she would paint next, and laughed when, in the pause between question and reply, no answer came, only space enough to wonder. [667]</story>
