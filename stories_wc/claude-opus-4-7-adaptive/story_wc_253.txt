After the happiness mandate, silence became contraband. [7] The state issued laughter quotas, smile audits, and a cheerful anthem piped through every kitchen radio. [23] Out beyond the grid, where the giant wind turbines leaned over our farmhouse like disappointed parents, my sister Mira stopped speaking. [44] She had been a performer once, in the old municipal theater, before the troupe was dissolved for insufficient brightness. [63] Now her face was blank, scrubbed of expression, a mask so perfect it unsettled even me. [79] She moved through our rooms like weather. [86] I was the one who adapted. [92] The inspectors came monthly, clipboards in hand, and I learned to greet them with our grandmother's dried flower bouquet pressed to my chest, brittle petals whispering compliance. [119] The bouquet had been a wedding gift, then a funeral keepsake, then a prop. [133] It had outlived three of us. [139] Mira refused to perform for them. [145] So I performed for her. [150] Each morning I would step into the kitchen and announce, in the grand theatrical baritone of a ringmaster, that the oats steeped in the pot of destiny. [177] I narrated the scrubbing of the floor as if it were a naval battle, the folding of laundry as if each sheet held a forgotten kingdom. [203] "And now," I boomed, whisking the kettle from the stove, "the water, having conquered its own boiling, surrenders to the leaves!" [224] Her face did not change. [229] But her shoulders, once locked, began to fall. [237] This was my motivation, narrow and stubborn: to cultivate lightness in her grief, without violating her silence. [254] The mandate demanded joy; Mira required hush. [261] I wanted to invent a third thing, a remade hush, a quiet that had a pulse inside it, a quiet that could hold a laugh the way a cup holds warm milk without spilling. [295] I began to whittle. [299] In the evenings, I sat on the porch beneath the hum of the turbines and carved small figures from fencepost scraps: a baker pulling bread from an oven, a horse refusing a bridle, a woman with no face. [337] I gave the woman no face on purpose. [345] Mira picked her up, turned her over once, and set her back down, which I counted as ovation. [363] The inspectors noticed nothing at first. [369] Then one of them, a man named Pell with a son of his own, lingered by the mantel and asked why my sister never spoke. [394] I told him she had taken a vow of gratitude so profound her mouth could no longer contain it. [413] He wrote something down. [417] He came back a week later and asked if I knew the penalty for shielding a non-compliant citizen. [435] I did know. [438] I also knew Pell drank the cider I left out, and accepted the carved horse I pressed into his hand, and told me which neighbor had reported the unlit windows. [468] I became, by small degrees, morally ambiguous. [475] I traded gossip for grace. [480] I informed on the Carlisles, who were already suspected, to buy Mira another quiet month. [495] I do not defend this. [500] I only say that the turbines kept turning, indifferent, and my sister kept breathing, and some evenings she almost smiled at the faceless woman on the mantel. [527] One night I came in to find her holding the dried bouquet. [539] She had pulled a single stem free and was turning it in the candlelight. [553] I opened my mouth to narrate, to declare the petal's valiant journey, but something stopped me. [569] The kitchen filled with an emotional void so absolute it felt like weather. [582] No grief. [584] No joy. [586] No mandate. [588] Only the two of us, the brittle stem, and the low throb of the blades overhead. [604] I understood, then, that I had been performing at her, not with her, and that my voice had been one more instrument of the state, even in resistance. [632] I closed my mouth. [636] We sat. [646] In that long, shared, unscripted quiet, Mira exhaled, and I heard for the first time the remade hush we had been building together without knowing, a silence that did not deny the world but survived it. [682] In the morning I carved her a face. [690] She took it into her hands, pressed it once to her cheek, and set it down beside the bouquet. [709] She did not smile. [713] She did not need to. [718]
