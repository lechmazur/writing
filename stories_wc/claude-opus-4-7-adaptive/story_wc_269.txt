Naima had watched the reef go white in 2031, each polyp surrendering color like a candle pinched out. [18] Twelve years later, after the fossil fuel ban had cleared the smog from the equatorial sky, she worked the night shift at the Tuvalu Sea Level Monitoring Station, alone with the hum of instruments and the slow breath of the lagoon. [59] The job suited her; she felt drifting moored, a buoy tethered to a memory that no longer matched the sea. [79] On the desk lay her only souvenir from the old world: an atlas page torn out of her grandfather's school atlas, showing the Line Islands in 1998 blue. [107] Half the atolls charted there were gone, or rather submerged; the ink remembered coastlines the satellites no longer confirmed. [126] She had come here to discover why some paths disappear. [136] Not scientifically, she knew the hydrology, but morally, structurally, in the grammar of the world. [151] Why one reef bleached while its twin, fifty kilometers away, held its pigment like a stubborn prayer. [168] At 0200 she began her ritual, a practice of tapping rhythms on surfaces, the one her grandmother had used to calm storms out of children. [193] Three short on the steel desk, two long on the window, one on her own sternum. [209] The tide gauge kept its own percussion below, a metronome of millimeters. [221] She tapped to assemble herself, every shift, bone by bone, from the scattered parts the day had left. [239] Tonight, though, the rhythm answered back. [245] A knock, faint, arriving from the glass floor panel that looked down into the splash well. [261] She froze, then tapped again: three short, two long. [270] Three short, two long came back, through saltwater. [278] She pressed the atlas page against the glass as if to show whatever was listening what she had lost. [297] Below, something pale moved: not fish, not plastic, but a geometry that resembled brain coral, pulsing. [313] She had read the quiet papers, the ones published only after the ban made hope respectable again. [330] Certain reefs, genetically prepared, returned when the water cooled in a decade-long dip. [343] Periodically eternal, the authors called them, alive in cycles humans rarely outlived. [355] She had not believed it; belief was expensive for witnesses. [365] Naima descended the ladder into the splash well, boots ringing, and knelt at the waterline. [380] She tapped the concrete: three short, two long, one on sternum, translated now into stone. [395] The answer came clearer, many-fingered, a chorus. [402] Not one organism: a colony reassembling itself from larvae that had hidden in the cold mesophotic dark for twelve years, waiting for humans to stop burning the sky. [430] The revelation, delayed by more than a decade, landed in her like a key finally finding its door. [448] Paths had not disappeared; they had gone underground, waiting for a world willing to tap back. [464] She thought of the torn atlas page, how she had always mourned its coastlines as extinct. [480] Maybe they were only absent, the way a song is absent between verses. [493] She climbed back up and opened the logbook. [501] Officially she was paid to note the sea's rising; tonight she wrote something else. [515] 0217 hours: contact established with returning colony, southeast pylon. [524] Rhythmic response confirmed. [527] Recommend bio-acoustic survey at next slack tide. [534] She paused, pen hovering, and added a line her supervisors would certainly query. [547] The reef is periodically eternal; we are the interruption, not the ending. [559] Outside, the lagoon exhaled under a clean sky, and the tide gauge ticked upward, then, impossibly, paused. [576] Naima set the atlas page back on her desk, no longer a relic but a score, waiting for its next measure. [597] She tapped once more, gently, and the station tapped with her, and somewhere beneath, the bleached generation's children answered in a language older than maps. [622]
