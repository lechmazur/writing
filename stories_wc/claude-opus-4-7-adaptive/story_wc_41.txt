They called him the junkyard prophet because he read futures out of rust, and because nothing he predicted ever stayed in his head long enough to warn anyone. [28] He was precognitively forgetful. [32] The visions came clear as tidewater, then slipped out before he could name them, leaving only a salt taste and the shape of something he had meant to say. [61] His name, when he remembered it, was Ansel. [69] Ansel lived at the edge of the fog sculpture garden, where the old coast artists had left their wire armatures to collect mist and lean slowly into new, unchosen shapes. [99] The garden breathed in gray. [104] Every morning it looked different, and every evening it looked like grief. [116] Behind his shack, the junkyard sprawled: refrigerators full of gull feathers, televisions that played weather from other decades, and a shelf of VHS tapes he had carried from his mother's house. [147] He never watched them. [151] He could not bear to. [156] He lived amid the hush of old videotapes left unwatched, and that hush was the loudest sound he knew. [175] What Ansel wanted, what he had always wanted, was to capture the sound of growing older. [191] Not the clock, not the complaint of bones, but the soft internal tide that pulled a person further from their own beginning. [213] He believed it was audible, if you stood in the right place with the right ear. [229] He believed the fog garden was that place. [237] So every day he went looking, and every day he forgot why before he found it. [253] To fight the forgetting, he used a piece of plain chalk. [264] On any hard surface, a fender, a rusted hood, the flat side of a washing machine, he wrote whatever the vision had been, the moment before it went. [292] LISTEN AT THE THIRD WIRE WOMAN. [298] THE TIDE RETURNS AT FOUR. [303] YOUR MOTHER'S VOICE IS ON TAPE SEVEN. [310] The chalk wore down to a stub that fit inside his palm like a tooth. [325] The rain kept erasing his notes, so he began arranging driftwood as directional markers instead, long pale bones of the sea laid end to end through the sculpture garden. [354] Each piece pointed toward the next clue he had chalked somewhere else. [366] In this way he built a map outside his skull, a mind that did not lose itself. [383] The map told him to locate a particular sculpture, a tall woman of braided copper wire, and to stand beneath her on an incoming tide. [408] It took him nine attempts to get there in the right order. [420] When he finally did, fog thickened inside her hollow chest like a slow inhalation. [434] He pressed his ear against the copper. [441] He heard it then, or thought he did: the long, continuous hush of his own life moving, the small current under every year, the sound he had chased since boyhood. [471] It sounded exactly like a videotape running in an empty room. [482] Ansel laughed, because of course it did. [489] All those unwatched tapes in the shed, all that clinging to echoes he had been too afraid to play, had been the very thing he was trying to record. [518] He ran home through the gray garden, knocking driftwood askew. [528] He threaded the first tape into the player. [536] His mother at thirty, teaching him to swim; his own child-voice, high and unafraid; a birthday; a hospital; a kitchen. [556] He watched for hours, and the tidal longing in him rose and did not break, because it had nowhere to break, because it was the shape of him now. [585] In the morning he went to chalk down what he had learned, but the chalk was gone, washed to powder in last night's rain. [609] He could not remember the sentence anyway. [616] Only the feeling stayed, which was perhaps the point. [625] He walked back into the fog sculpture garden, rearranged the driftwood into a fresh path, and began again. [643] The junkyard prophet, who forgot forward and remembered backward, listening for the sound of himself getting older in the hush between one tide and the next. [669]
