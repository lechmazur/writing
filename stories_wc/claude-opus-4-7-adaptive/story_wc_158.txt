<story>Mira had not slept in eighty-three years. [7] She was an insomniac poet, and the gargantuan shell she called home kept her awake with its hum, a low coral drone that braided itself through her ribs like a second spine. [39] The shell had washed ashore on a dead tide centuries ago, and Mira, who had been small then and frightened, crawled inside to ensconce herself among its pearled chambers. [68] She never left. [71] Time moved strangely inside the shell; she aged slowly, like a candle burning underwater, and wrote her poems on the walls with the inside of a broken oyster. [99] Every verse was an attempt to photosynthesize pain into beauty, to take the old human ache and turn its wet weight toward something like light. [124] Above her, in a crack of nacre, she had pinned a galaxy postcard. [137] It had been slipped under the shell's lip by some traveling tide, addressed to no one, depicting a blue-green spiral no eye had truly seen. [162] She told the postcard everything. [167] She told it about the two great fish-kings whose feud once turned the bay black, and about the mothers who drowned waiting for sons who would not return. [195] Across centuries of longing, her poems piled into drifts, and still she did not sleep. [210] Then one spring, the reef outside the shell bloomed. [219] It bloomed as reefs sometimes do when pressure and grief align: anemones opening in synchronized pink, brain-corals flushing violet, small translucent fish arriving in choirs. [244] The fish-kings, old and scarred, rose from their trenches to witness it, and for the first time in memory they did not fight. [267] The reef had declared a blossom armistice, and every creature nearby obeyed its flowering law. [282] Mira felt the truce pressing against her shell like a warm palm. [294] She wanted to join it, but she was shy; she had not spoken to anything with a pulse in longer than she could count. [318] Still, something in her was shyly unstoppable, a small green verb inside her chest that pushed her toward the opening of the shell. [341] She carried the galaxy postcard with her like a passport. [351] Outside, the water tasted of sugar and salt, and a reef-shadow joy moved through her, the kind of happiness that only grows under something larger than itself. [378] She approached the elder fish-king and offered him her poems. [388] He could not read, of course, but he could listen. [398] So she sang them to him, and to the anemones, and to the other king, and to the small bright minnows with their nervous heartbeats. [423] She sang via half-sung lullabies carried on an off-key wind, the only music insomnia had ever taught her. [441] The wind blew wrong, her voice cracked, and yet the reef leaned closer. [454] Her songs told of the mothers and the sons and the black bay and the galaxy postcard and the centuries of longing. [476] They told of a shell too heavy to leave and a girl who stayed inside anyway. [492] The fish-kings wept old mineral tears. [498] The anemones clapped in their slow, ruffling way. [506] Somewhere in the middle of her sixth lullaby, Mira realized her eyelids were heavy. [520] She had not felt that weight in decades, in a century, perhaps ever. [533] The armistice was not only between kings; it was between her body and the war inside it. [550] She returned to her gargantuan shell, tucked the galaxy postcard above her pillow of moss, and lay down for the first time as herself. [574] Sleep came like a tide that had been waiting politely for its turn. [587] In her dreams she photosynthesized everything she had lost into pale blue light, and the light curled through the shell and out into the sea, feeding the bloom that had freed her. [619] When she woke, she was still shy, and still unstoppable, and the reef was still in bloom. [636] She picked up her broken oyster and began to write. [646]</story>
