In the year after the last dictionary was printed, Ilma entered the shadowed library at dawn. [16] She was a reticent scholar, soft-footed, wary of her own voice. [27] The lamps had gone cold long ago, yet the high windows admitted a gray, careful light, and the air held a suspended hush, as if the books themselves were waiting. [57] Ilma had come for the chandelier. [63] It hung unlit above the reading floor, a ruined thing, half its teardrops shaken loose during the storm that had torn the north roof two winters ago. [90] One crystal lay on the oak table, exactly where she had left it the previous morning. [106] She cupped it in her palm, and it caught the dawn like a listening ear. [121] The crystal was sonically reflective; she had discovered this by accident, humming near it and hearing her own breath returned clarified, slowed, made strange. [145] In a world where no new dictionaries would be compiled, where language itself had been declared finished, she needed an instrument that could hold what voices had left behind. [174] Her purpose was small and stubborn: to decode the pattern of growth. [186] She believed, with the weathered innocence of someone who had outlived her teachers, that words still grew, quietly, the way lichen grows on stone. [210] The official committees disagreed. [214] They had sealed the canon, retired the lexicographers, and sent the presses to the foundries. [229] Language, they announced, was complete. [234] Ilma set the crystal upright on a brass ring and drew closer the ledger she had been keeping in secret. [254] Inside it, she had written fragments overheard in markets, on stairwells, from children pretending to read signs: phrases that had not existed a year before. [279] She had no proof these were new, only a trembling intuition, and intuition would not testify in any court the ministries still convened. [302] The crystal, however, might. [306] She began to read aloud, softly, one entry at a time. [317] The crystal answered through fractured sequences, refracting each syllable into shards that scattered along the shelved spines. [334] A word spoken once returned in three pieces, each piece lodging in a different corner of the room, and Ilma, pencil in hand, mapped where they landed. [361] Over the weeks this had become her method: not to preserve meaning but to watch how meaning split, where it chose to rest, which shelves it haunted. [388] This morning, at last, a pattern surfaced. [395] The fragments were clustering, not randomly, but along a curve that traced from the oldest folios to the newest, and then beyond the newest, onto the empty shelf reserved for acquisitions that would never arrive. [430] The curve did not end there. [436] It bent upward into the gallery, where dust had settled into perfect gray, and touched, faintly, the ceiling where the chandelier still hung. [459] Ilma understood then, or thought she understood, which in a weathered innocence is nearly the same. [475] Language had not stopped. [479] It had merely moved upward, out of reach of ministries and presses, into the architecture itself, growing the way ivy grows, by increments invisible to any single gaze. [507] The crystal in her hand was not only listening; it was a small piece of a larger instrument, the chandelier, which the library had hung as an accident of vanity and which the language, uninvited, had adopted. [544] She wrote this down. [548] Her pencil, usually steady, trembled on the page. [556] To testify was to stand alone, and she knew that when she brought her ledger to the remaining scholars, they would hear what the ministries had trained them to hear: a woman murmuring into a broken lamp. [593] Still, she closed the book and tied its cord. [602] Dawn was fuller now. [606] The hush in the library had not broken; it had only deepened, as if the room were holding its breath for her. [628] She returned the crystal to its ring, where it would continue, without her, to catch whatever new words drifted between the shelves. [650] Then Ilma walked out through the tall doors, carrying her fractured evidence into a morning that had already, in ways no dictionary would ever record, begun to grow. [678]
