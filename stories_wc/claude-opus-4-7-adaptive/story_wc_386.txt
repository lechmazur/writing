<story>Mira surfaced with the melted snow globe cupped against her chest, its glass fogged, its little drowned village slumped like a secret. [22] She was a wreck-diver by trade and a ghost whisperer by inheritance, and the two vocations had finally braided into one job she could not quit. [48] The ship she had just left, the Halvard, lay forty fathoms down with a girl named Ines still asleep in her bunk. [70] Mira had come to salvage something pure from the wreckage, and nothing aboard that rotted hull was purer than what Ines had been carrying: a child's globe, now a puddle of wax-soft glass, the souvenir of a honeymoon her mother never got to finish. [114] Ines could not tell Mira who had scuttled the Halvard; the dead, she had learned, remember feelings more than faces. [134] So Mira took the globe north, to the only witness who still owed her answers. [149] Brother Ansel kept a candle-lit shrine carved into glacier ice, a blue cathedral no wider than a rowboat, where the wicks never guttered and the walls breathed cold like a sleeping animal. [181] He trafficked in what he called windows of connection: the small frames through which the dead peer into the living and wave. [203] His method was patient and strange: he tracked the dead by analyzing cameo appearances in strangers' dreams. [220] A drowned girl, he explained, could not stage herself; she could only flicker at the edges of sleepers who had brushed her life without knowing it. [246] Find enough of those flickers, and a face would assemble around her killer. [259] For nine nights Mira lay beside the melted globe on a pallet of furs while Ansel read the dreams of dockworkers, insurers, a lighthouse keeper's wife. [285] Ines appeared as a cameo in each one: a braid passing a window, a laugh heard through a wall, a pair of boots left outside a door that should have been locked. [317] Ansel weaves truth carefully, Mira noticed; he never named a suspect, only let the geometry of coincidence narrow. [335] By the ninth dream the narrowing had a name: Captain Roe, who had insured the Halvard twice and loved his brandy more than his crew. [360] In every sleeper's mind he stood at the same rail, holding the same match, while Ines laughed somewhere below deck about snow that would not melt. [386] Mira wanted to expose him in the harbor square, to haul his name through the papers like a net full of teeth. [408] Ansel lifted the melted globe and held it toward the altar candles, where the ruined glass caught the flames and answered them with something gentler, a tone Mira could only describe as borrowed starlight. [442] Expose him, yes, he said, but not with rage; rage burns the window you were given. [458] So Mira wrote the testimony slowly, stitching dream to dream, cameo to cameo, until the court could not look away. [478] She did not shout; she arranged. [484] Roe confessed in a voice like a lamp going out. [494] Afterward Mira returned to the glacier shrine, because she had salvaged many things but not yet the one pure thing she had come for. [518] She set the melted snow globe on the ice altar and asked Ansel to find Ines once more, not as evidence, but as a girl. [543] He read new dreams: Ines on a pier eating an apple, Ines braiding a horse's mane, Ines laughing about snow that would not melt because she had held it too long in her warm small hands. [579] These cameos were not clues now; they were small bright windows where a child met the strangers whose lives had briefly touched hers. [602] Mira copied each one into a book for Ines's mother, who had lost every photograph to the same sea. [621] Outside, the glacier held its long blue breath, and the candles inside the shrine burned on with that same soft, secondhand light, as if the dead had lent the living just enough to see by. [656] Mira closed the book, and for the first time since surfacing, she felt she had brought something home. [674]</story>
