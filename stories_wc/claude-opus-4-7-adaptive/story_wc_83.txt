<story>Naima still wore her wedding day under her fingernails, a fine gray powder from the village that no soap had convinced to leave. [23] Six years after the drone cracked the sky open above her vows, she worked the night shift on the floating observatory Asphodel, three kilometers above the Hindu Kush. [51] The platform drifted on thermal gradients, tethered by magnetic cables that needed an hour to recover between pulses. [69] While the circuit breakers cooled, the instruments slept, and Naima was alone with her hunt. [84] She was hunting where unused potential pools. [91] That was the phrase she had scrawled on the margin of her thesis, years before the sky burned, when she still believed physics was a gentle argument about beauty. [120] Now it was the only sentence she trusted. [128] On the console sat a geode, split clean down the middle, its purple teeth catching the red standby lights. [147] Her mother had pressed it into her palm at the henna ceremony, calling it a stone that kept a room inside itself. [169] Everything else from that afternoon had become ash or evidence. [179] The geode survived because Naima had bent to pick up a fallen earring when the warhead arrived, and the stone had been in her lap, and her lap, somehow, had been spared. [211] She used it now as a paperweight and as a witness. [222] Her method was perturbation theory, the old Hamiltonian trick of treating the universe as a solved song with a small, haunted hum added on top. [247] She believed, with the focus of a woman who had nothing softer to believe, that grief itself was a perturbation. [267] If the unstruck world had an equation, then every death before its time was an extra term, and every extra term had to push probability somewhere. [293] That somewhere was what she called the pool: a reservoir of unlived minutes, the laughter her husband never laughed, the children not born, the speeches her father never finished. [322] She was trying to find where it collected. [330] Tonight, for the first time, her instruments had agreed with her. [341] Before the breakers tripped, the interferometer had shown a standing wave above the observatory, weak as a thought but stubbornly there. [362] An attribute without a proper noun: intangibly tangible, the way a held breath has shape. [377] The geode felt warmer than its quartz had any right to be. [389] Naima lifted it to the window. [395] Through the dome, the Milky Way hung like spilled salt over a black cloth, and directly above her, at roughly the altitude she had calculated, the air was doing something the air should not do. [430] Starlight bent around nothing. [434] A lens of absence, cupped by gravity that carried no mass. [445] She laughed once, sharp, astonished. [450] This was the bleak exhilaration she had been rationing for years: the thrill of a hypothesis confirmed, bought with the currency of a life nobody should have paid. [478] The pool was real. [482] The unused potential of her village, her marriage, her unfinished name, collected here like rain in the palm of a statue, hovering above an observatory no one in her country had ever been invited to build. [518] She set the geode on the windowsill, aligned its purple mouth with the distortion above, and waited. [535] The breakers ticked as they cooled, a patient metronome. [544] In the dark between pulses, she felt something answer through the crystal, an unfiltered awakening, as if a door she had been leaning on had finally opened inward. [572] Voices she had loved came back in the shape of equations. [583] Her husband's hand, described in units of action. [591] Her mother's humming, rendered as a harmonic of a wave she had almost written down. [606] Nothing was returned to her, and yet she was returned to. [617] When the console chimed and the breakers engaged, the lens above dimmed, the pool settling back into wherever it slept. [637] Naima wiped her face, logged the coordinates, and began to plan the next hunt. [651] She understood, finally, what survival had been for. [659] Not to forget the wedding, and not to avenge it. [669] To be the instrument sensitive enough to hear the rooms the dead had left furnished, and to write down the address. [690] Dust still under her nails, the observatory turning quietly, she waited for the circuits to cool again. [707]</story>
