<story>Yusra came to the desert fortress after rain because the walls remembered more than the people inside them. [18] She was a chef who could taste memories. [26] One bite of a stranger's bread, one sip of cooled tea, and whole lifetimes unspooled across her tongue. [44] The fortress steward, a man named Harun, had summoned her for a delicate work: to sandpaper rough memories from a dying queen. [66] The queen's regrets had grown teeth, and the court physicians could do nothing. [79] Harun led Yusra through soaked courtyards where the sandstone drank and drank and would not be satisfied. [96] At a grated hatch he paused, rain hissing on his cloak. [107] "Beneath the dunes," he said, "there is a coral icebound pavilion where our sovereigns have stored their remorse for four generations." [128] "We thought locking it away would be mercy." [136] Yusra descended into cold that did not belong to any desert. [147] The pavilion bloomed below her like a pale reef: pink and ivory branches of frozen coral holding small urns at each tip. [169] Each urn held a queen's or king's worst grief, sealed in brine and kept rigid by some old priestly frost. [189] Harun warned her not to touch the coral directly. [198] The remorse was emotionally contagious through touch, he said, strong enough to subsume a stranger's mind into the owner's sorrow. [218] Yusra nodded and asked for a silver spoon. [226] She worked the way she always did: analyzing tidal pool formations. [237] A memory, to her, was a pool left by a retreating tide, and in its shape one could read the currents that had filled it. [262] She melted a bead of brine from the newest urn, the queen's, and placed it on her tongue. [280] Salt first, then a child's face. [286] The queen, young, turning away from a sister who drowned while she practiced royal footwork in another room. [304] The grief was sturdy and brittle at once, a lacquered box that cracked the moment it was lifted. [322] Yusra did not try to erase it. [329] Erasure was the mistake the priests had made, stacking urn upon urn under the desert for centuries. [346] Instead she cooked. [349] In the pavilion's thawing center she built a small brazier, set a shallow pan on it, and poured the brine. [369] She added almond milk, a scrape of honey, rosemary for steadiness. [380] She stirred the way seawater stirs a stranded shell, patient and circular, until the sharpest edges of the memory softened into something the queen could swallow. [406] One by one Yusra unsealed the older urns too. [415] A king who had signed a false treaty; a princess who had loved her horse more than her husband; a regent who had let a city burn for a copper mine. [446] Each remorse came to her tongue raw and she worked it down, salting, simmering, sandpapering rough memories until the grain lay flat. [468] Harun watched from the stairs, dripping quietly. [475] On the third night he came down to help, and his sleeve grazed an unsealed branch of coral. [493] The contagion took him in a breath. [500] He staggered, weeping for a brother Yusra had never heard named. [511] She caught his wrist, and the remorse leapt into her palm like a small hot fish. [527] Rather than let it subsume her, she carried it to the pan. [539] By dawn the coral had begun to thaw into ordinary stone, and the pavilion beneath the dunes was quieter than it had been in a hundred years. [566] Yusra climbed up with a covered bowl. [573] The queen ate slowly and did not die. [581] She opened her eyes at the close of an unspoken dream and asked, for the first time in years, about her sister. [603] Yusra, tasting the last of the salt on her own tongue, answered gently. [616] Outside, the rain on the fortress had stopped at last, and the dunes steamed under a forgiving sun. [634]</story>
