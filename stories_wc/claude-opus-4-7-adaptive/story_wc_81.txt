<story>Mira had always been called a logical page indexer, a title she wore the way other archivists wore spectacles. [19] Her job, when libraries still floated above the waterline, was to give every text an honest place in the order of things. [41] Now the libraries were drowned, and Mira swam. [49] The forgotten library inside the sunken city of Velmar had been her destination for three months. [65] She arrived during the blackout, when the bioluminal lamps that fed the lower districts had failed and the corridors held only the green hush of moonlit kelp. [92] It was, oddly, the perfect time. [98] Without the city's machinery, the water lay still, a reclaimed stillness that no diver of her generation had ever known. [118] Mira drifted between the shelves, tranquilly perceptive, noting how the silt had settled in even layers, how each tome rested where it had been left a century ago. [146] She had come to trace honest origins. [153] The surface guilds had begun reprinting Velmar's pre-flood charters, claiming continuity with the drowned scholars, but the documents bore inconsistencies that gnawed at her. [177] Dates that contradicted the lunar tables. [183] Signatures that drifted across decades. [188] If the originals existed anywhere, they would be here, in the master archive that had sunk with the rest. [207] Her lantern caught a glint near the registrar's desk: an antique ink stamp, brass and barnacled, still fixed in its cradle as though waiting for the next petitioner. [235] Mira lifted it, turned it gently, and pressed its face against a slate of waxed paper she carried for testing. [255] The impression that emerged, faint but unmistakable, was the seal of the Old Registry, a six-pointed coil with a numbered cartouche. [276] It did not match the seal printed on the surface guilds' new charters. [289] Mira held still, tasting the implication. [295] She had assumed, like every other indexer, that the surface scribes had simply lost details in transcription. [312] Now an alternative hypothesis bloomed, sharp and quiet: the guilds had not lost the originals. [327] They had replaced them. [331] She moved deeper, past collapsed mezzanines where slow fish nosed at gilded spines. [344] The blackout meant no patrol skiffs would pass overhead, no sonar pulses would scatter her thoughts. [360] She had perhaps six hours before the grids returned. [369] In the registrar's vault she found the ledger she had hoped for, sealed in a wax-coated chest that the pressure had not yet defeated. [393] She had to readjust her grip three times before the lid yielded, silt billowing in a soft brown cloud that the still water held in suspension like dappled light. [422] Inside lay the founding registry, every page stamped with the same six-pointed coil now resting in her satchel. [440] Mira began to index. [444] Page by page she compared the original entries with the surface reprints she had memorized. [459] The discrepancies formed a pattern. [464] Names of founding families had been quietly elevated; debts of old houses had been silently erased; lineages of the current council had been threaded backward into people who had never existed. [495] It was a forgery as wide as the city above. [505] Mira felt no anger, only a dappled resolve, light and shadow alternating across the same calm intent. [522] She would copy the originals onto her waxed slates, stamp each with the antique ink stamp so that authenticity could be tested against any future challenge, and bring them up before the grids returned. [556] She worked through the long quiet hour, the kelp swaying like slow curtains, the only sound her own measured breath. [576] When the last page was indexed and stamped, she paused above the empty registrar's chair. [591] Somewhere far above, the surface would soon wake to its small lies, unaware that a stranger had returned with the truthful weight of its founding. [616] Mira tucked the stamp into her satchel beside the slates, not as a trophy but as a witness. [634] Then she turned upward, kicked once, and rose through the dark library toward a city that would, by sunrise, have to reckon with its memory. [659]</story>
