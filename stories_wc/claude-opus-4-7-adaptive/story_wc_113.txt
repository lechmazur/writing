The Teatro Belluvia had been drowning for eleven years. [9] I am Ilario, second violist, orchestra pit musician by trade and by stubbornness. [22] When the canal levees broke in '14, the city abandoned the opera house, but I kept my key. [40] Lately inspired by a dream I could not name, I returned with a waxed coat and a lantern that flickered like a nervous tenor. [64] The pit was half-submerged, my old chair floating among reeds of sheet music. [77] Upon my music stand, wedged against the brass rail, lay an antique opera program for a performance that had never finished. [98] It was Maestro Voss's last night; he collapsed at the podium before the overture resolved, leaving the orchestra poised amid the hush of unstruck chords. [123] The audience had filed out in silence, and within a week the waters rose. [137] Voss had been my teacher, my quiet friend, and, some said, my ghost. [150] I opened the program, and the paper curled in my hands like a living thing. [165] Water damage had bled the ink into shapes the printer never intended. [177] On page two, a watermark shaped like a treble clef had become a map of the orchestra pit. [195] On page five, the cast list dissolved into a constellation of numbers I recognized: measures, beats, fingerings. [212] This was Voss's handwriting, slow-leached through decades of flood. [221] He had left me a code, and the house itself had been developing it, like a photograph in a slow, patient bath. [243] My motivation sharpened to a single thin blade: to unravel the code left by a ghost, before the building collapsed and took him with it. [268] I waded between the rows, the water thigh-deep, lanterns on the balcony rail throwing trembling gold across the velvet. [287] The opera house existed now in between spaces, neither ruin nor stage, neither drowned nor alive. [303] Every arch held a pocket of echo; every doorway opened onto a hallway half-swallowed by reflection. [319] I climbed to the conductor's podium, which the water had not yet claimed, and set the program on the stand. [339] The first clue pointed to measure thirty-two of the unfinished overture. [350] I drew my bow across the viola, one careful phrase, and the sound disrupted the stillness so completely that the lanterns guttered. [372] Something answered. [374] Not a voice, for Voss had never trusted words, but a soft clack from the wings, a stagehand's signal we used to share. [396] I played measure thirty-three, and a gilded door upstage swung an inch open, water sighing against its base. [414] This, I understood, was our wordless conversation: he posed a question in geometry and stain, I answered in phrases, and the house marked the rhythm by moving. [441] The program's page seven showed a stain like a hand pointing toward the prompter's box. [456] I waded there and found, tucked behind the rotten wood, a tin cylinder sealed with wax. [472] Inside lay a sheaf of manuscript, the overture's true ending, the one Voss had died before conducting. [489] His pencil notes filled the margins: For Ilario, when the flood lets you see it. [504] He had always meant for the water to reveal the gift. [515] The code was never hostile; it was simply patient, waiting for me to grow into it. [531] I returned to the podium and set the manuscript beside the program. [543] The lantern steadied. [546] The hush of unstruck chords, kept eleven years in this building's ribs, gathered behind me like an audience holding its breath. [567] I lifted my bow. [571] Measure by measure, I played Voss's ending into the flooded nave, and the vibrations stirred the water into small bright rings that traveled outward and met the walls and came back changed. [603] Where the ripples crossed, images formed: a conductor's shadow, a hand raised for the downbeat, a smile half-turned toward the violas. [624] He was not haunting the opera house; he was simply still working, and I had finally arrived for rehearsal. [643] When the last note dissolved, the water was calm, the lanterns burned clean, and the in-between quiet felt less like absence and more like company. [668] I left the program on the stand for the next musician who might wander in, lately inspired, needing a friend. [688]
