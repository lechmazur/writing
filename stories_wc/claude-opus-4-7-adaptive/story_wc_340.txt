The brass elevator of the icebound spire shuddered awake at dawn, and Ilsa, still an ascending servant in title only, stepped inside with a tray of cooling tea and her late mistress's locked diary pressed beneath her arm. [38] Above the clouds, the spire hummed with wind that had forgotten any warmer country. [52] She had served Captain Vrey for nine winters, and loved her the way one loves weather: gratefully, without expecting reply. [72] When the captain died last week, frost on her lips, Ilsa had felt the strange affectionate detachment of a candle watching its own wick. [96] Today she would climb to the observatory at the spire's crown, as she did each morning, but with one difference she could not quite name. [121] As the elevator climbs ten floors, Ilsa had a habit of inventorying her life, and today the inventory trembled. [140] Floor one: the airship captain's goggles, left to Ilsa in a note no lawyer had witnessed, now riding her forehead like a second pair of eyes. [166] Floor two: the diary itself, iron-bound, its pages crammed with ciphered loops she had never been meant to read. [185] Floor three: the memory of Vrey saying, in the last fever, "Look through scattered light, girl; the truth prefers prisms." [205] Floor four: Ilsa lowering the goggles over her eyes just to see what the dead woman had seen each morning. [225] Floor five: through the faceted glass, the cabin split into seven soft colors, and the diary's ciphered loops resolved, impossibly, into letters she knew. [249] The goggles were the key; the captain had ground her own lenses for an encrypted diary discovery only a faithful servant would ever make. [273] Floor six: Ilsa opened the book with trembling, warmly inquisitive fingers, as if greeting an old acquaintance met at last without formalities. [295] The first entries, forty years old, were love letters to a boy lost in the southern crossings; the ink smelled of rosewater and iron. [319] Floor seven: later pages, the captain recording every small brightness she collected to survive him, a cook's laugh, a gull's shadow, Ilsa humming in a corridor. [345] The whole project, Ilsa understood, had been to seek out the joy beneath anguish, one noted pearl at a time. [365] Floor eight: her own name appeared, not once, but on nearly every page, always phrased as a quiet wonder, the girl is still kind, despite me. [391] Ilsa did not cry, exactly; she felt, instead, the clean distance of someone recognizing a debt already forgiven. [409] Floor nine: she reached the final entry, dated the morning of Vrey's death, written shakily but without cipher. [427] If you are reading this, Ilsa, then you found the trick of the light, which means you are ready to unfold the rest of my life without me. [455] The airship is yours; the spire is yours; the cold is yours, and so, belatedly, is any laughter I hoarded against it. [477] Floor ten: the elevator sighed open onto the observatory, and the world unfurled in every direction, cloud upon cloud gone gold. [498] Ilsa set down the tea no one would drink, closed the diary, and lifted the goggles onto her brow. [517] She saw a mooring post, an airship still tethered, frost like sugar on its rigging, waiting for a captain who had not arrived in a week. [543] It occurred to her, warmly, curiously, that the airship was also waiting for her. [557] She would inherit Vrey by habit, not grief, and her first order as captain would be simple: find the next small brightness and log it. [582] The cold wind tasted faintly of rosewater. [589] Ilsa smiled the small, unhurried smile of a woman who had just become, in the thinnest possible sense, ascendant. [608]
