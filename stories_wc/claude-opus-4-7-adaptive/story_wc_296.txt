<story>Bramble was a guarded druid who finds lost things, though she rarely returned them. [14] She kept a satchel of recovered trinkets buckled tight against her ribs: a wedding ring scoured by river silt, a child's tooth, a folded letter whose ink had run into a single blue tear. [48] She had come to the cursed lake of Ondren because the only thing she had ever truly lost was her brother, and the lake remembered everyone. [74] At its heart spun a swirling orchard vortex, apple trees turning slow as a millstone above black water, their fruit bobbing in a circle that had not broken in a hundred years. [106] Bramble walked the shore through expanding circles, the old druid's search-pattern, each loop wider than the last, listening for the small wrongness that meant a thing wanted to be found. [136] On the seventh circle she met the conjurer. [144] He sat on a stump with a deck of painted cards, his velvet coat soaked black at the hem, smiling the bright, brittle smile of a man whose only trick has just failed him. [178] "I was meant to pull my brother from a hat," he said, before she had asked anything. [195] "The hat is empty." [199] Bramble did not flinch, did not soften, though something in her chest tilted like a compass needle finding north. [218] "What did you offer the lake?" [224] He opened his palm: a single black seed, veined with red, the kind every druid was taught to bury under salt and forget. [247] A flesh-eating plant seed. [251] "I traded it for a glimpse," he said. [259] "The orchard shows what mirrors don't." [265] That was her own old motive, spoken in a stranger's mouth, and Bramble felt the warlike serenity descend on her at last, the cold quiet she wore the way other people wore armor. [298] She had come to discover what mirrors don't show, and now a stranger had spent her coin first. [316] "Then walk with me," she said. [322] They stepped onto the lake. [327] It held them, as it would hold anyone who carried something the orchard wanted. [341] The vortex slowed as they approached, the apples halting one by one, each suspended like a struck bell. [359] At the center, the water opened on a still face: her brother, twelve years old, mouth caught mid-word. [377] A frozen confession, held in the lake's throat for thirty winters. [388] She read his lips: I took the boat out alone. [398] I am not coming back. [403] I am sorry. [406] Bramble made herself unbend, knee by knee, until she knelt on the water's skin, her satchel of found things spilling open against her thigh. [430] She had carried the lost things of others for thirty years to keep from looking at her own. [448] The conjurer stood beside her, soaked and shivering, his own confession nowhere in the water. [463] "It only shows the dead," he whispered. [470] "My brother is alive somewhere; the lake had nothing to give me." [482] Bramble looked at him then, truly, and saw the seed still curled in his fist, hungry, waiting to be planted in the silt of his disappointment. [508] She took it from him gently, the way she had once taken a knife from her brother's small hand. [527] "Some lost things," she said, "are kinder when they stay lost." [538] She crushed the seed against the water. [545] It hissed, dissolved, and the orchard above them began to turn again, the apples taking up their slow round. [564] The face of her brother sank back into the dark, neither forgiven nor accused, simply known. [580] Bramble closed her satchel. [584] She walked the conjurer back to shore through the same widening rings she had come by, and at the treeline she pressed the wedding ring into his palm in place of the seed. [617] "Find whoever lost it," she said. [623] "Begin there." [625] Then the guarded druid turned away, lighter by one secret, heavier by none, and walked into the trees with the warlike calm of a soldier who has at last laid down a war no one else knew she had been fighting. [666]</story>
