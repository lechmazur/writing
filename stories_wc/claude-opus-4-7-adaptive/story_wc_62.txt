Odel carved gravestones for a living, and he carved them slowly. [11] Each letter was a small argument with stone, and the thoughtful carver preferred arguments that ended in agreement. [29] His workshop stood beside an ancient greenhouse whose glass had gone milky with a century of pollen, and at dusk he walked the rows between the orchids and the dying lemon trees, thinking. [62] He called this walking fiercely meditative, because the thinking refused to be gentle. [75] Grief, he believed, had a shape. [81] Not an individual shape, but a collective one, the way a flock has a shape though no bird among it knows the whole. [104] He wanted to find it. [109] That was his work's true commission, the one nobody had paid for. [121] The butterflies arrived in September. [126] Monarchs first, then painted ladies, pooling in the greenhouse because its broken panes had become their inn. [143] Across the length of their migration, from the first orange flake to the last, Odel intended to carve a single stone: a memorial without a name, for everyone the town had lost that year. [177] He had never attempted anything so ambitious, and he did not know how to begin. [192] On the first evening, as the butterflies settled into the jasmine, the spectre of his grandmother arrived. [209] She sat on an overturned pot with the patience of someone who had always been patient and now had eternity to practice. [231] "You are stuck," she said. [236] "I am listening," he said. [241] "Same thing, at your age." [246] She had been the family poet, and she had invented their dinner custom: each person, before the soup, would read a favorite poem aloud, no commentary allowed. [273] The custom had ended when she died, because nobody could bear to start. [286] Now she produced, from nowhere that made sense, a woven ribbon roll, the kind she had always kept her poems rolled inside, tied with threads of every color. [314] "Pick one," she said. [318] He untied the ribbon and chose a strip. [326] The poem was about a river that mistook itself for a road. [338] He read it aloud to the butterflies, and the butterflies did not care, but he felt something shift in the pale light. [360] "Tomorrow," she said, "pick another." [365] So it went, across the butterfly migration. [372] Each dusk he returned to the greenhouse. [379] Each dusk his grandmother was already there, sometimes humming, sometimes simply watching a moth negotiate with a pane. [397] He would retrace his steps through the rows, reading a new poem from her ribbon roll, and afterward she would ask him what the stone wanted to say today. [426] At first he answered in abstractions: sorrow, memory, endurance. [435] She shook her head each time. [441] "Those are nouns." [444] "Grief is a verb." [448] On the twelfth evening he noticed the butterflies clustered not at random but along the rafters in slow rivers of themselves, and he understood that a migration was the shape of many small griefs agreeing to move in one direction. [488] That was a thing he could carve. [495] On the twentieth evening he read a poem she had written herself, about a woman who stitched her husband's shirts after his death until the thread outlasted the cloth. [524] He wept, quietly, and the ghost did not comfort him, which was its own kind of comfort. [541] The tone of the greenhouse that night was a thundering calm, like the inside of a bell just after it has been struck. [564] "You have it now," she said. [570] He nodded. [572] On the last evening of the migration, when only a few stragglers remained clinging to the lemon leaves, he carried the finished stone into the greenhouse to show her. [601] It bore no name, only a shallow relief of wings overlapping wings, and beneath them a single line from her ribbon: we go together or we do not go. [630] She read it, and touched the letters, and did not cry, because she had done her crying long ago. [649] "Who taught you to carve like that?" she asked, smiling. [659] "A thoughtful woman," he said. [664] "Thoughtful, and fiercely patient, and dead." [670] She laughed, which sounded like ribbon unspooling. [677] "Keep the roll," she said. [682] "Read one at dinner." [686] "Start the custom again." [690] Then she was gone, and the last monarch lifted from the jasmine and followed her, and Odel stood alone in the milky glass light, holding a ribbon roll that was somehow still full. [723] He walked home through the cooling fields. [730] At the table, before the soup, he untied a strip and read. [742] His daughter, who had not heard a poem at dinner in seven years, set down her spoon and listened. [761] The shape of grief, he understood, was the shape of what kept going. [774] That was the stone he would carve next. [782]
