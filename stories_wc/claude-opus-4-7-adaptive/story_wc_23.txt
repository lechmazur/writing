<story>The signal station had been dark for three winters when Ines returned to it, after the hydraulics failed on the last ferry south. [21] She climbed the headland path alone, coat stitched from six other coats, boots mended with tar and prayer. [39] The villagers called her the patchwork navigator, and she had not corrected them in years. [54] She carried nothing but a canvas bag and the patina bronze censer foot her brother had pried from a shipwreck the summer before he drowned. [79] The starlit signal station sat at the cliff's edge, a rusting lantern house with a broken Fresnel lens and one working telephone, and Ines meant to make it speak again. [109] Inside, she set the censer foot upright on the desk, a small verdigrised paw meant to hold incense, now holding memory. [130] Her task was simple and impossible: to build a living map from the voices of everyone she had lost. [149] She would do it through cryptic phone calls at midnight, the only hour the old copper lines still carried anything resembling the dead. [172] The station's signal strength, her grandfather had written in the logbook, fluctuates with moon phases. [187] On new moons the wires hissed and gave nothing; on full moons they opened like a throat. [204] She had come to navigate the geography of loss, and loss, she had learned, obeyed tides. [220] On the first night the moon was gibbous, and at midnight the phone rang with a voice that might have been her brother asking whether the tide still turned at the Selvig rocks. [253] She said yes, though the lighthouse there was gone. [262] On the second night her mother's voice arrived, asking if the plum tree had survived the storm. [279] Ines lied gently, as navigators must. [285] Each call gave her a coordinate: a cove, a kitchen, a field she had forgotten she knew. [302] She plotted them by lamplight on the back of an old nautical chart, and the pattern that rose from the pencil was not an outward journey but a reverse migration, the dead drifting home against every current, pulling her inland with them. [344] Her brother had gone to sea because everyone left; now his voice returned because no one did. [361] She understood, finally, that the island was not emptying but refilling with ghosts who had found their way back through the copper wire. [384] On the seventh night, under a full moon so bright the cliff paths threw shadows, the phone rang before she touched it. [406] It was her own voice on the other end, younger, asking where she had gone. [421] She answered honestly: away, and now back. [428] She lit the censer foot with a sliver of driftwood and let the small flame burn in the bronze paw, not as offering but as beacon. [454] The station filled with a pale, green-tinted light, the color of patina under fire. [468] Outside, she imagined the dead returning along the ridgelines, pacing homeward the way swallows once did. [484] It was not comfort, exactly; it was raw solace, the kind that leaves the skin stinging but the lungs full. [504] By dawn she had filled the logbook with names and bearings, and the chart on the desk had become a country she could walk. [528] She would stay through the next dark moon, and the next, keeping the line open, because someone had to answer when the drowned called home. [553] The patchwork navigator leaned her forehead against the cold glass and listened to the sea, which sounded, finally, like a language she had already learned. [578] Somewhere inland, a plum tree she had never planted came into bloom. [590] The phone rang once more, gently, and she lifted it like a hand. [603]</story>
