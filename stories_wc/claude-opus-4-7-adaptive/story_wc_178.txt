Ishtaru counted her lamp oil by the drop. [8] As the palace's last frugal astronomer, she had outlived three kings and one prophecy, and intended to outlive the hush that fell between regimes. [32] The old dynasty had collapsed at dusk; the new one had not yet arrived by dawn. [48] In that interval the corridors of the reimagined Babylonian palace took on a dusky harmony, glazed bricks humming faint blue, ziggurat shadows folding into one another like patient hands. [77] She climbed to the observatory tower carrying her instruments and a single object wrapped in linen: a magic square tile of baked clay, its grid of cuneiform numerals adding to the same total along every row, column, and diagonal. [116] Her late teacher had pressed it into her palm and called it a key. [130] A key to what, he would not say. [139] Ishtaru, empirically ironic by training, had laughed; one does not unlock the heavens with arithmetic. [154] Yet here she was, between regimes, climbing. [161]

The rooftop opened to a sky still bruised with sunset. [171] Venus hung where the tablets predicted, obedient as a clerk. [181] Below her, the courtyard torches were dim; the new governor, whoever he would be, had not yet ordered them refilled. [201] Frugal as ever, she approved. [206] Darkness was cheaper than ceremony, and tonight she needed darkness. [216]

She placed the magic square tile on the slate where she usually sketched her star charts. [232] For years she had studied the heavens through schematic representations, reducing gods to geometry and omens to ratios. [250] It was the only devotion she trusted: encrypted devotion, prayer disguised as proof. [263] Tonight she would test whether the disguise had been hiding something true. [275]

Her teacher's notes claimed the tile's grid mirrored a constellation that did not appear in any catalogue, a pattern visible only when one star, a wandering nameless ember, crossed the meridian during the dark hour between dynasties. [312] The chance came perhaps once a century. [319] She had spent her thrifty decades waiting. [326]

Ishtaru aligned the tile with her astrolabe, copying its numerals onto wax: rows became right ascensions, columns declinations, diagonals the strange arc of an orbit she had never been bold enough to draw. [359] The schematic took shape under her stylus, sober and unornamented, and yet, as the lines met, it began to resemble a face turned upward in listening. [385] She set the wax beside the tile. [392] She breathed out the long breath of a woman who has bargained with her last coin. [408]

"Uncloak," she whispered, the old priestly word for revealing what custom had veiled. [421] She did not say it to a god. [429] She said it to whatever the numbers had been hiding inside her teacher's gift, and inside herself. [446]

The wandering ember crossed the meridian. [452] Light from it struck the glazed tile and split, the way a prism splits a candle, casting a pale grid across the courtyard wall. [476] Where each numeral fell, a faint Akkadian glyph flickered into existence, then another beneath it, until a sentence assembled itself in a hand she recognized: her teacher's. [503]

I asked the sky to remember you, the message read, when no king would. [517]

Ishtaru sat down on the cold stones. [524] All those careful nights of measurement, the discounted lamp oil, the ironies she wielded against believers, they had been someone's love letter, copied star by star into a code she alone could solve. [557] Encrypted devotion: he had not trusted the priests, nor the throne, nor even her doubt, but he had trusted the math. [578]

She understood, then, what the tile required of her in return. [589] Not worship, her teacher had never asked for that, but a covenant: to keep watching, frugally and faithfully, even when no regime paid her to look. [615] She pressed her palm to the warm clay and, in the dusky harmony of a palace that belonged to no one, sealed her covenant with the unknown. [642]

Above her, the nameless star resumed its quiet wandering. [651] Below her, the next dynasty was already saddling its horses. [661] Between them, an old astronomer measured oil into her lamp, opened a fresh tablet, and began the night's work. [680]
