The cobalt dock glistened after rain, and Mira arrived carrying a dental mirror wrapped in oilcloth. [16] She had borrowed it from her father's drawer, the only tool small enough to read what the canal guardian wrote. [36] He never spoke; the city had forgotten if he ever could. [47] They called him the silent canal guardian, and for nine winters he had stood at the lockhouse, opening and closing shutters in patterns no engineer had taught him. [75] Mira had spent the summer learning to read those patterns, the way fishermen learn weather by the slant of gulls. [95] Tonight the air felt spiralingly calm, the kind of calm that loops inward until it touches a held breath. [114] She knelt at the lockhouse window and angled the dental mirror up beneath the eaves. [129] In its tiny silver disc the shutters appeared upside down, their slats opening and closing like a slow alphabet. [148] Three short, one long, two short: the rhythm she had been waiting for. [161] It meant the guardian had something to say within the edge of a half-spoken secret, the narrow hour when admission was still possible and denial still polite. [188] She climbed the wooden stair. [193] He was at the lever, gray as wet rope, eyes the color of canal stone. [208] He did not turn, but his shoulders softened, which was his greeting. [220] "I read your shutters," she whispered. [226] "You said the barge will come tonight." [233] He nodded once. [236] The barge had been the rumor of the district for a month: a low black vessel that traveled without lanterns, its cargo unsaid, its captain owed favors by men who governed the cobalt dock. [270] Children had begun disappearing from the alleys behind the fish market. [281] No one named the cause aloud; to name it would invite refusal, and refusal would invite the barge to choose a different street. [304] The guardian's purpose, Mira had come to believe, was to teach darkness the value of stars. [320] He could not shout, could not testify, could not write a petition. [332] He could only weave light through the shutters, slat by slat, until the patterns reached someone with a small mirror and the patience to look. [357] "What do I do?" she asked. [363] He pressed her palm to the lever, then to her own chest, then upward, toward the rafters where a lantern hung unlit. [385] Stillwater tension settled between them, the kind that holds a coin upright on its edge. [400] She understood without translation; this was the silent empathy he had been cultivating in her all summer, the trust that did not need consonants. [424] She lit the lantern. [428] He began to weave the shutters in a new pattern, faster, a pulse like a heart that has decided. [447] From the windows of the lockhouse the light spilled across the wet stones in stripes that traveled the length of the canal. [469] House by house, the bargees' watchers would see, and they would know they had been seen. [485] Below, the water held the reflected stripes the way a child holds a borrowed coat: carefully, briefly. [502] A figure on the far quay turned, raised a hand, and disappeared into a doorway. [517] Then another. [519] The black barge, when it slid into the basin, found no slack ropes waiting, no bribed lockmen, no shadows to drift through. [541] Lanterns bloomed along the dock as fishermen, alerted by the pattern, brought their own lights to the rails. [559] The barge hesitated like an animal that has seen the trap before stepping in. [573] Then it reversed, oars dipping in apologetic rhythm, and slipped back the way it had come. [589] Mira lowered the dental mirror. [593] The guardian closed the shutters at last, leaving only the lantern's steady eye. [606] He looked at her, and the look itself was a sentence: she had inherited something. [621] Next time the rumor moved, she would be the one at the lever, weaving stars into the planks of the dark. [642] They stood together as the cobalt dock dried beneath them, neither speaking, neither needing to. [657] The half-spoken secret had completed itself, not by being said, but by being answered.
