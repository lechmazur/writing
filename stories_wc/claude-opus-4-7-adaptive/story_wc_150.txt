Mira came home to a country that no longer recognized her name. [12] Forty years abroad had thinned her accent into something her grandmother would have mistrusted. [26] Still, she returned, drawn by letters from the parish historian, who wrote of a tomb beneath the old catacomb orchard where tree roots snake between bones. [52] He believed her family's sundial base lay buried there, the last fragment of a shrine her ancestors had tended before exile scattered them across three continents. [78] Mira, a diaspora returnee with soil still strange under her boots, came to find it. [93]

The orchard slept on a hillside of knotted almond trees whose roots had, over centuries, threaded the catacomb niches below. [113] To excavate was to part living wood from the dead who held it steady. [127] The historian, Brother Anselm, was warmly curious about her, asking after cousins whose graves he kept and listening when she admitted she had stopped believing in anything at all. [156] She had come, she said, to restore a lost faith she was not sure she remembered. [172] He did not press her; he handed her a trowel. [182]

Work began on a Tuesday and would last eleven days. [192] Across excavating the tomb, Mira learned to read the orchard's odd grammar: an ankle bone wedged in a root's elbow, a femur cradled like a grafted branch. [219] She unearthed potsherds painted with pomegranates, a child's clay whistle, a coin she did not know how to name. [238] Each object felt like a letter opened late, the sender long quiet. [250] Anselm worked beside her and told stories of interrupted journeys: pilgrims turned back by plague, merchants who never made the next town, her own great-grandfather setting out for the coast and vanishing into a century's wars. [286] Every family in the valley, he said, carried a route that did not finish. [300] Mira understood then that she had inherited one such route, and that returning was only its next weather. [318]

On the seventh evening, she stopped digging and simply walked. [328] Between the trees, over quiet stones, down into the cool breath of the lower niches, she wandered with a purpose she could not yet frame. [353] It was through this purposeful wandering that she found the stone. [364] A root had cupped it like a palm offering something back: the sundial base, its gnomon long gone, its incised hours still legible beneath the moss. [390] She knelt in cautious enchantment, afraid to believe in coincidence and more afraid to believe in its opposite. [408] The stone was heavier than she expected, warm from the sun that had slipped between branches all afternoon, as if still keeping time for a house that no longer stood. [438]

Anselm came when she called. [443] Together they lifted the base free, apologized to the root, and carried it up through the orchard into last light. [463]

In the days that followed, she did not pray, exactly. [473] She set the stone on a low wall near the chapel and watched shadows cross its numerals. [490] She began to rebuild the shrine from rubble the villagers had never thought to clear: a courtyard wall, a step, a niche for water. [514] Neighbors drifted over, offered hands, offered almonds, offered their own interrupted names. [526] Faith, she decided, was not a thing one found intact; it was a base you set level, a gnomon you fitted later, a shadow you agreed to read honestly when it fell. [558]

On the last morning of the dig, she stood among the trees where roots held bones as tenderly as any reliquary. [579] Her grandmother's accent returned to her, briefly, like a bird landing on a familiar branch. [594] She spoke a name she had not used in forty years, and the orchard, which had been listening for longer than that, kept it. [618]
