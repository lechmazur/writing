After the resuscitation, Mireille woke clutching her mother's opera cloak clasp—a silver moth whose wings opened and closed whenever she breathed too sharply. [24] The paramedics had mistaken it for a locket and pressed it back into her palm like a relic. [42] She remembered nothing of the drowning, only a greenish lingering luminance behind her eyelids, the color of gaslight refracted through wet brass. [64]

Her whispering advisor, Dr. Velleau, arrived before dawn. [72] He never raised his voice; he murmured, as if every syllable were a secret he resented being asked to share. [92] "You are photographically cursed now, child," he breathed, circling her cot. [103] "Every fear that meets your eye will imprint itself upon you, and each imprint will ripen into the thing itself." [123]

He set a book at her bedside—a dream dictionary bound in cracked vellum, margins crowded with his own spidery annotations. [144] "To undo the curse, you must trick fear into revealing its weakness." [156] "Fear, like any vain creature, loves to unbosom itself before a mirror it believes sympathetic." [171] He tapped the dictionary. [175] "These are paradoxical notes; follow them precisely, and they will guide you to a place where fear has forgotten to hide." [196]

She read them by candlelight: "To find safety, enter the unsafe"; "To see clearly, close both eyes"; "Laughter here is the lowest form of grief." [221] Each entry contradicted the last, yet together they formed a map. [232] At the crossroads, turn toward what repels you most. [241]

The notes led her, by dusk the next day, to the abandoned carnival funhouse at the edge of the salt flats. [262] It had closed twenty winters ago after a child vanished inside, and the townspeople had let the pier rot rather than dismantle it. [285] Moonlight gilded the peeling clown mouth that served as its entrance, and Mireille felt the tug she had been warned of—a repulsive allure, the kind that quickens the pulse precisely because one knows one should run. [322] She fastened the opera cloak clasp at her throat and stepped inside. [334]

The mirrors had not died with the rest of it. [344] They glowed faintly, each holding the same green lingering luminance she had seen while dead. [359] Her own reflections multiplied—dozens of Mireilles, each slightly wrong. [369] One had her mother's eyes; another had no eyes at all. [380]

"Speak," she said, as Velleau had instructed. [387] "Unbosom yourself, and I will not look." [394] She pressed her palm over the clasp, turning the silver moth until its wings shut. [409]

The corridor answered. [412] Not in words, but in flickers: a drowning child, a mother's funeral, a burning house, a stranger's knife. [430] Each image tried to imprint upon her, and because she was photographically cursed, each would have. [446] But the clasp, worn closed, acted as a shutter. [455] The moth refused the exposure. [460]

Fear, accustomed to developing instantly in the mind, began to panic. [471] It unspooled faster, louder, more grotesque, flinging its whole catalogue at the girl who would not flinch. [488] And in its frenzy, it began, at last, to speak. [498]

"I cannot exist," the mirrors whispered, "where I am expected." [508] "Anticipation is my grave." [512]

Mireille smiled. [514] That was the weakness—fear lived only in the unexpected, and she had come expecting it, invited it, greeted it like an aunt. [537] She opened the clasp. [541] The moth's wings spread; the lingering luminance drained from the mirrors into its silver body and was gone. [559] The funhouse, robbed of its engine, sagged into ordinary wood. [569]

Outside, dawn smelled of salt and iodine. [576] Dr. Velleau waited by the boardwalk, his coat buttoned to the chin, his whisper already composing her next lesson. [595] "You did well," he said, and she understood, finally, that his quiet voice was no affectation. [611] He, too, had once been photographed by something hungry, and his whisper was all that remained of a larger voice. [631]

She slipped the dream dictionary into her pocket. [639] There were other entries still unread, other paradoxes still to walk. [650] But for now, for the first morning after her resuscitation, she was only a girl on a pier, and the moth at her throat slept with wings folded, dreaming in silver. [681]
