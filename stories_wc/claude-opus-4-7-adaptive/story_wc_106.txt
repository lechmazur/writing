Ivo Renne was known in three cities as the golden-hour portrait thief, a quietly savvy forger who stole only the light from a painting's face, leaving the canvas otherwise intact. [30] He worked under a fleeting golden sunset, when guards squinted and brushwork looked like honey. [45] Tonight he had come to the emerald station by moonlight to meet the woman who had hired him: a florist named Mira, who left instructions in bouquets no one else could read. [77] She believed codes in flower arrangements were safer than letters, because letters could be burned but petals only spoke to the initiated. [99] Yellow tulips meant wait. [103] White asters meant the train. [108] A single sprig of rue meant bring the jar of captured moonbeams. [120] Ivo had the jar in his coat. [127] It had come from an old astronomer who claimed to have bottled three evenings of moonlight before his eyes failed. [147] Ivo did not believe in such things, not really, but the jar glowed faintly under his lapel, and that was enough to keep his disbelief luminous rather than cold. [176] He sat on the green-tiled bench. [182] The station at this hour was a cathedral of mint and silver, its columns blooming shadows, its clocks slow with dew. [203] Mira arrived carrying a bouquet of lavender, pale iris, and one stem of hawthorn, a message he had to reinterpret twice before the meaning settled: the door is ready, but the price is memory. [237] "You brought it," she said, sitting beside him without looking. [247] "Good." [248] "I wasn't sure you believed." [253] "I don't," Ivo said. [257] "I brought it anyway." [261] She smiled the way people smile at weather they cannot argue with. [273] Then she told him why she had summoned him, why she paid a thief who stole light rather than gold. [293] Her brother had vanished between two photographs taken four minutes apart on a summer afternoon. [308] In the first, he laughed. [313] In the second, the bench was empty. [320] She had spent six years learning to read what flowers said because petals, she claimed, remembered what people forgot. [339] "There's a door," she said, "built of illuminated moments." [348] "Every time a photograph is taken, it borrows a sliver from that door." [361] "A painter's golden hour is borrowed too." [368] "You have stolen enough of those slivers to build a key." [379] Ivo laughed, because the alternative was to weep. [387] "And the moonbeams?" [390] "The hinge," she said. [394] He set the jar on the bench between them. [403] The glow spread, and the emerald tiles answered in soft reflections, and for a moment the station looked less like a building than like a lantern someone had left burning for a traveler still a long way off. [441] Mira unwrapped her bouquet and arranged the flowers along the bench: lavender, iris, hawthorn, and, from a hidden pocket, a single white rose. [464] The arrangement was a sentence; the sentence, when Ivo made himself read it properly, said: to press your ear against destiny's door, you must give up the face you wore when you began. [497] He understood, then, what she was asking. [504] Every portrait he had stolen light from had taken a year from his own reflection; he had seen it in mirrors, the slow blurring of his own golden hours. [533] One more theft, the largest, and he would hear whatever waited beyond. [545] He would lose his face entirely, but he would know. [555] "Why me?" he asked. [559] "Because you already started," she said, "and because you are the kind of man who does not run from an answer." [580] He lifted the jar. [584] The moonbeams inside stirred, restless, already reaching for the seam in the world only she could see. [601] He thought of Mira's brother, and of his own face, and of the strange arithmetic she described, how a life was just a sequence of bright instants, and how giving some up might be less a loss than a translation. [641] He poured the jar over the flowers. [648] Light rose in a clean, soundless column. [655] A door appeared, thin as a painted line, set into nothing. [666] Mira pressed her ear against it. [672] Ivo pressed his. [675] And under the last of that fleeting golden sunset, something answered from the other side, in a voice that sounded exactly like their own. [699]
