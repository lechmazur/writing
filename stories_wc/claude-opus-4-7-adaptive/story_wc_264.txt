<story>They called me the dreamless freight guard because sleep had quit me the night the last forest burned. [18] After the embers, the world kept its hours by small, broken devices. [30] Mine was a chess clock frozen at midnight, a gift from a stationmaster who had loved the game and lost it. [51] He told me it wasn't broken; it was waiting for the correct move. [64] The freight I guarded was the divergent archive, every crate a bough of the library that had once grown in soil and now grew in probability. [90] Each book inside was occasionally everywhere, its pages written through wave functions that the old archivists had coaxed from the grain of the wood itself. [115] A reader might open a volume in one car and finish it a thousand miles away, or in a dream, or never. [137] My orders were simple: keep the archive safe until we reached the glowing dune in bloom. [153] I had never seen it, but the old crews sang about it like a fractured lullaby: sand lit from within, poppies opening in slow radioactive shifts, a place where the burnt world pretended to be gentle again. [190] They sang it wrong every time, and that was how I learned the tune. [204] On the fourteenth night, bandits came aboard somewhere past the salt flats. [216] They wanted the archive, of course; men always want what they cannot read. [229] I did not fight. [233] A dreamless man has nothing to wake for, and therefore nothing to lose, and therefore nothing to prove. [251] Instead I chose to charm them. [257] I set the chess clock on a crate and told them the rules of a game that did not exist. [277] Because the clock was frozen at midnight, every move they made took no time at all, which is to say, all time. [299] I opened one book from the divergent archive and read aloud, and the wave functions braided through the bandits' attention until each of them was occasionally everywhere: halfway home, halfway younger, halfway a child listening to someone who loved them. [339] They put down their guns the way tired dogs put down bones. [351] By dawn they had chosen names I did not catch and climbed off the freight at a siding with no name. [372] The train kept going. [376] We reached the glowing dune in bloom on a morning that did not count as one. [392] The sand was warm and luminous, seeded with small blue flowers that hummed when you held your breath. [410] Here, the archivists had told me, the books would take root. [421] This was the work after the last forest burned: to cultivate the sparks in the unlit corners, to plant the impossible where the possible had failed. [447] I unloaded the divergent archive crate by crate. [455] Each time I opened a lid, a small light unfurled and drifted into the dune like a firefly deciding where to be born. [478] The dune accepted them, humming its fractured lullaby, the same one the old crews had butchered, now finally sung right. [498] When the last crate was empty I set the chess clock among the flowers. [512] Midnight, I thought, was as good an hour as any to begin. [524] The stationmaster's waiting move, it turned out, was not to press the button but to let the game remain unplayable, so that somewhere, in every unlit corner, a spark could still decide the board. [558] I lay down in the glowing dune, and for the first time since the forests had gone, I almost slept. [578] I did not dream. [582] But I listened, and the archive read itself back to me in voices I had not lived, charming me into a kind of rest. [606] Somewhere a reader was finishing a book I had just opened. [617] Somewhere the forests were beginning again, in the unlit corners, exactly where no one had thought to look. [635]</story>
