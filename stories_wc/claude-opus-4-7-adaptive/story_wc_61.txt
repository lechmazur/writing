<story>Vael came to the nebula garden because someone had to remember. [11] He was an otherworldly sentimentalist, a collector of small griefs gathered from worlds that no longer existed. [28] The garden was where light learned to bloom without burning, and that was why he had come. [45] His ship made transit through the slow currents between dust and dust, sails reefed against the indigo wind. [63] He navigated by sonar pinging, releasing soft chimes into the nebula and listening for the petals to answer. [81] Each ping returned a hush, a velvet echo that approximates infinity without ever quite touching it. [97] In his pocket he carried a single calculating machine gear, brass-toothed, older than three of his ancestors combined. [115] His grandmother had pressed it into his palm the night she died, whispering that the family had always built engines that burned, and somebody, eventually, would have to stop. [144] Generational patterns, she had called them, looping like gear teeth, each tooth driving the next without choosing. [161] Her father had built furnaces, his father had built foundries, and Vael had been groomed to inherit a forge that consumed whole moons for fuel. [186] He had refused, and refused, and refused, until refusal became its own kind of inheritance. [201] Now he drifted across the quiet sigh of falling petals, watching the garden bloom in colors his ancestors had never named. [222] The petals were not flowers but slow-collapsed stars, each one shedding photons soft as breath. [237] Here, light did not require fire. [243] Here, things glowed because they remembered glowing. [250] His motivation was simple, and very old: to share light without using fire, to carry home a lantern that did not consume what it illuminated. [275] He anchored the ship beside a low arbor of cooled hydrogen, where blossoms hung like quiet bells. [292] The brass gear in his pocket grew warm, as if it recognized this place. [306] He took it out and set it on the railing, and the gear began to turn by itself, slowly, slowly, in time with the petals' fall. [332] He sent another sonar pinging into the dark, and this time the answer was a chord. [348] Voices, he realized, or something like voices: the garden contained those who had come before him, sentimentalists and engineers alike, all of them paused mid-grief, mid-bloom. [374] His grandmother was among them, of course. [381] She was a faint amber thing now, a kind of warmth without heat. [394] She had carried this gear from the foundry on the day she chose not to light the furnace, and the gear had been turning, quietly, ever since. [421] Vael felt the troubling serenity of it all settle into his chest, a peace too vast to be entirely kind. [441] Generations of his blood had built machines that ate the sky to make a single bright moment; here, the sky made itself bright by simply being. [467] He gathered a petal in a glass jar and watched it continue to shine, undiminished. [482] It would not run out. [487] It would outlast every furnace his line had ever stoked. [497] On the ride home, the gear kept turning in his palm, and he did not try to stop it. [516] Some patterns, he thought, could be redirected rather than broken; the teeth still engaged, but the torque drove something new. [536] He would give the jar to his youngest niece, who feared the dark. [549] He would teach her the sonar pinging, the listening, the way to ask a nebula garden for a loan of its patient glow. [572] She would grow up knowing light as a gift exchanged, not a fire set. [586] Behind him, the garden kept its slow work, petals falling and falling, a quiet inheritance he was at last willing to carry. [608]</story>
