<story>No one remembered hiring Ilen the mirage painter, but every spring he returned to the convent's echoing terrace, brushes dry, pockets full of chalk. [24] He worked only over the hush before bells ring, that narrow country of minutes when the nuns paused between matins and the market below began its clatter. [51] The prism parapet, still wet after rain, threw his silhouette in seven colors along the stones. [67] Watchers said he was elusive; step too close and he seemed to fold into his own shadow, leaving only a smell of wet pigment and cedar. [93] I came to learn from him, though I was only a foundling with a borrowed heartbeat, a weak drum the physicians had patched with another boy's rhythm when I was four. [124] Ilen did not teach with words. [130] He taught through coded lullabies in children's songs, old verses the village girls skipped rope to, whose nonsense syllables, when stretched, named the pigments of possibility. [156] "Lavender-lo, lavender-lie," meant to layer a dusk across stone; "honey-come-home" meant a hinge of gold beneath any true window. [175] He was perceptively calm, the sort of calm that isn't emptiness but a listening so fine it chose what to hear. [196] On my first morning he set me before the convent's tall mirror and said his true trade was not painting at all. [218] It was to persuade mirrors to show possibilities, to coax from their flat honesty some softer, stranger yes. [236] Mirrors, he explained, only repeated what stood before them; a mirage painter asked them to repeat what might. [254] We practiced over three springs. [259] Each dawn I hummed his lullabies while he painted the terrace air in chalk that dissolved by noon, and the tone he summoned was always the same: a dusky harmony, neither mournful nor bright, like a room remembering itself at evening. [300] The prism parapet held those colors like a ledger. [309] I began to see, faintly, what I had never owned: a mother's face turning, a ship I might take, a man I might become who walked with grace instead of apology. [340] That word, grace, Ilen used only once, and carefully. [349] He said grace was not a gift but a gait; it was how you carried a borrowed heartbeat so the lender felt honored, not robbed. [374] I think that was when I understood he too had borrowed something, though from whom I never learned. [392] On the last morning of my third spring, the rain broke early, and the parapet lit up like spilled glass. [412] Ilen stood at the mirror and sang, very softly, a lullaby I had not yet learned. [428] It was shaped like the counting rhyme children used for jacks, but its numbers were directions: forward, inward, elsewhere. [447] In the glass I saw him walk into a garden I could almost name. [461] When I turned, the terrace was empty; his brushes lay aligned along the parapet, each a different color, each still faintly wet. [483] The bells, which had been gathering breath, finally rang. [492] I have taught the children of three towns since then. [502] I give them rope and melody and the older nonsense verses that carry the pigments inside them like seeds. [521] I do not claim to paint mirages; I only ask mirrors the small, insisting question Ilen taught me to ask, and sometimes, before the bells, the glass softens and answers. [551] My borrowed heartbeat keeps its steady, donated time. [559] Each spring I climb to an echoing terrace of my own, one I built in his honor, and I wait over the hush before bells ring. [585] The prism parapet catches the rain-light, the dusky harmony gathers in the air like a held chord, and in the mirror I keep there, sometimes, very briefly, a possibility walks with grace into a garden I could almost name. [624]</story>
