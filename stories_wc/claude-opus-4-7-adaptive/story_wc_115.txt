Perry had crawled through mills most men wouldn't enter sober. [10] He was reckless about collapsing floors, reckless about asbestos, reckless about the law posted in faded red on every splintered door. [31] What he was not reckless about was belief. [39] He considered himself religiously skeptical, which meant he gave equal mistrust to priests, ghosts, and the voice in his own head. [60] Tonight he sat inside the abandoned train tunnel east of the Carver mill, his back against damp brick, his radio tuned low. [82] The tunnel's mouth breathed cold; its throat swallowed sound. [91] A battery lantern stained the walls the color of weak tea. [102] In his lap rested a beaded valve wheel he had pried from the mill's boiler room three hours earlier. [121] Brass, hand-turned, with a ring of glass beads threaded through its rim like a rosary someone had tried to civilize. [141] He had come to appraise it. [147] Not its price - any scrapper could guess that - but its weight. [158] Perry needed the weight of a swallowed truth, and the wheel, he suspected, had been present when the truth was swallowed. [179] Two nights ago, in the hush that follows a sudden confession, his brother Dale had told him what happened to their father on the night shift in 1994. [207] Dale had said it in one breath and then refused to repeat it. [220] Perry had not slept since. [225] He felt the sentence lodged under his sternum like a coin he couldn't cough up. [240] So he had gone back to the mill, and then retreated here, to the tunnel, because the tunnel was the only place in the county where his phone had no signal but his transistor radio did. [276] That was his method. [280] Perry did not trust mediums, but he trusted patterns, and the patterns he trusted most were the frantic calls to talk-radio psychics broadcast out of Pittsburgh every weeknight between midnight and three. [312] Widows, gamblers, insomniac mothers - they phoned in asking the same crooked questions he was asking now, and the host's answers, stripped of their velvet, sometimes rang true by accident. [341] He listened the way a prospector pans: not for the host, but for the callers, for the tremble in a voice that meant a swallowed truth had begun to rise. [371] Static bloomed. [373] A woman from Aliquippa was weeping about her son. [382] A man wanted to know if his mother forgave him. [392] Perry closed his eyes in that drowsy alertness the tunnel encouraged, half dreaming, half hunting, the valve wheel cold against his thighs. [414] Then, at 1:47, a caller said the word Carver. [423] Perry sat up. [426] "There's a shift that isn't a shift," the man told the host, voice cracking. [440] "My uncle worked it. [444] He called it the ghost shift. [450] Nobody clocked in and nobody clocked out, but the machines ran anyway, and men came home lighter than they'd left." [470] The host laughed kindly, the way hosts do, and cut to commercial. [482] Perry turned the beaded wheel in his hands. [490] A factory ghost shift - he had heard the phrase from older men at the VFW, always half-joking, always after the third beer. [512] A run the books never recorded, where foremen squared accounts that couldn't be squared in daylight. [528] If such a shift had existed in 1994, his father might not have died by accident. [544] He might have been the account. [550] Perry did not believe in ghosts. [556] He believed in men who used the idea of ghosts to hide what they had done. [572] The wheel in his hands had turned off a steam line the night his father drowned in scalding water, and someone had kept the bead-rosary on it for luck, or for penance, or for forgetting. [607] He weighed it. [610] It weighed exactly as much as Dale's sentence, which was to say, enough. [623] Perry switched off the radio. [628] The tunnel returned its long, attentive quiet. [635] He stood, pocketed the beads, and started walking toward the mouth, toward morning, toward the one brother left who still needed to hear the question asked aloud. [662]
