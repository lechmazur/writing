<story>Mira had been the city's only leaf blower symphony conductor, a title she'd invented the autumn her daughter stopped laughing. [20] In the beginning it was a joke. [27] She gathered groundskeepers on the mall green, tuned their two-stroke engines to approximate pitches, and waved her baton until the crowd smiled. [49] Sophie, six years old and hollow-eyed since her father's funeral, had watched from a folding chair and offered nothing. [68] Mira kept conducting anyway. [72] She told herself that a mother's work was tending to the unspoken, to the griefs that settled under floorboards and in the corners of her daughter's mouth. [99] If noise could drown the silence, perhaps the silence would forget its own shape. [113] She was wrong, and she knew she was wrong, and still she rehearsed. [126]

The tunnel found her in October. [132] An old service conduit under the reservoir, brick-lined, long abandoned, a perfect echo chamber tunnel where a single cough lived nine seconds before dying. [156] A groundskeeper had shown it to her as a curiosity. [166] She returned alone. [169]

She brought one blower: an orange Homelite older than her marriage. [180] Its pull cord handle was cracked and yellowed, wrapped in electrical tape the color of bruises. [196] She had inherited it from her husband, who had used it every Saturday of his last year, patient as a metronome, raking the yard while Sophie laughed from the porch swing. [227] Sophie had not laughed since. [232]

Mira set the machine in the tunnel's throat and knelt beside it. [244] She was mournfully eager, that strange posture grief teaches, where the body leans forward to meet the next blow. [263] She wanted to hurry. [267] She wanted never to begin. [272]

The plan was simple and unreasonable. [278] She would run the blower in the echo chamber until the tunnel itself learned to sing, and she would steer its voice with hymns her husband had hummed to the cradle. [309] Half-sung lullabies on an off-key wind, that was her method, her prayer, her only superstition. [324] If the tunnel could be taught, she could bring its breath home in a jar and unseal it under Sophie's window, and laughter, so long misplaced, might find the door. [354]

She turned the hourglass she'd set on the brick ledge. [364] Ten minutes of sand. [368] After the final grain slipped through, she would be done, one way or another; she had promised herself not to ruin her hands on hope. [393]

She yanked the pull cord handle. [399] The engine caught on the third pull, screaming, and the tunnel screamed back, a second voice layered a half-beat behind, and then a third, a fourth, until the air itself seemed to macerate, pulp the sound, break it down into something soft and strange, juice pressed from dry noise. [448] She conducted with her left hand, throttled with her right. [458] She began to sing. [462]

"Hush now, the garden's asleep, hush now, the kettle is warm." [473] Her voice broke; the tunnel did not mind. [481] It took the broken edges and doubled them and fed them back, off-key, patient, the wind that had lived underground for forty years learning how to cradle a child. [510] She sang her husband's song, the one he had only ever sung when he thought no one was listening. [529] The unspoken thing. [532] The tending. [534]

The sand fell. [537] Somewhere above, commuters crossed the bridge and never heard her. [547] The machine shook. [550] Her knees went numb. [554] Still she sang, eager to finish, mourning each measure as it passed. [566] She could feel the haunting now, not the pretend kind, not the shiver of a campfire story, but the true one: the sense that something listens that should not listen, and agrees. [598] The tunnel was agreeing. [602] It had decided to help. [607]

The final grain slipped through the hourglass. [614] Mira cut the throttle. [618] Silence rushed in, and then the tunnel exhaled, one long, warm, off-key note, lullaby-shaped, that she caught in the wide mouth of her husband's thermos and screwed shut. [646]

She walked home slow. [650] She did not check the jar. [656] At Sophie's bedroom she opened the thermos, and the wind went out of it crookedly, searching, and settled on the pillow like a palm. [680] Sophie, asleep, laughed once. [684] It was small. [687] It was her father's laugh, inherited and returned. [695] Mira stood in the doorway and let the sound ruin her. [706] The house, at last, was being tended. [713]</story>
