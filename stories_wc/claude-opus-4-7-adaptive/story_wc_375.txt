Mira's fingers were always black with charcoal, even after she scrubbed them raw in the river. [16] She had been, since childhood, mnemonically gifted: one glance at a stranger, and their face lodged in her mind like a pressed flower in a book. [42] In the market she drew portraits for coins, but lately every face she rendered came out wrong, flat, lifeless. [61] The carnival had passed through Vellery a week earlier, and after the last echo of laughter faded from the emptied square, she noticed the old well behind the bakery standing uncovered. [92] A painter she had loved as a girl, a woman named Osa, had vanished near that well decades ago. [111] Her only legacy was a single landscape Mira kept above her cot, a painting with something behind it, Osa had once whispered, something that would wait. [137] Mira had never dared to look. [143] That night, under a moon the color of bone, she finally pried the canvas from its frame. [160] Beneath it, taped to the stretcher, hung a small brass combination lock, broken in half, its dial frozen on the number three. [182] Also: a folded map, its ink bleeding, and one sentence, "Rectify what I left undone." [197] Mira recognized the map instantly, having seen it once as a child when Osa spread it across the kitchen table and laughed. [219] The route led beneath the well. [225] She lowered herself down at dawn, charcoal stubs in her satchel, sketchbook against her ribs, the broken lock heavy in her pocket like a debt. [250] The shaft opened into a subterranean crystal cave that glimmered with veins of rose and aquamarine, as though some long-sleeping dawn had been buried alive. [275] Light moved along the walls the way an aurora might move, slowly, sorrowfully, the color of a grief too old to still be sharp. [299] Mira understood then what auroral sorrow meant: beauty that does not console, only remembers. [313] At the cavern's center stood an easel, its wood blackened by moisture, and on it a blank canvas Osa must have stretched half a century ago. [339] Beside the easel lay a small iron chest, its hasp waiting for a lock. [353] Mira pressed the broken combination lock into place; it fit the gouge exactly, halves aligning like a healed bone. [372] Inside the chest were brushes, pigments still faintly wet, and a letter in Osa's hand. [387] "Some callings are inescapable," the letter read. [394] "I hid mine, buried my work where no husband could burn it and no child could scorn it." [412] "I meant to return; I did not." [419] "If you are reading this, the gift has found you instead, because gifts always find someone." [435] Mira sat on the damp floor and wept, because she had spent twenty years drawing other people's faces rather than admit she had her own visions to uncork. [463] The market portraits were her hiding place, her broken lock, her refusal. [475] She had come down here, she realized, not to finish Osa's work but to uncork her own suppressed creativity, the paintings she had feared would cost her the steady coins of strangers. [507] She lifted a brush. [511] For hours she painted Osa from memory: the crooked smile, the hands stained with cadmium, the laugh that had once filled a kitchen. [534] She painted her own mother next, and the baker's daughter, and a stranger from the carnival whose face she had kept, unused, for a decade. [559] Each portrait came alive in a way her charcoal sketches never had, the crystals behind them throwing back small auroras of rose and blue. [583] When her pigments ran dry, Mira climbed back up the shaft with nothing in her satchel but the empty chest and a sketchbook full of the living. [610] She sealed the well again, not to hide the cave but to keep it sacred, a place she could return to when the world grew too loud. [637] In the market the next morning she refused her first commission. [648] A calling, she had learned, could be postponed but never outrun; it waited in crystal and in canvas, patient as grief, and it would rectify itself through you, eventually, whether you consented or not. [682] Her fingers were still black with charcoal. [689] But now, beneath the charcoal, there was color. [697]
