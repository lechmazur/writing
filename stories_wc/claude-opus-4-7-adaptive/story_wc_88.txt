<story>They scrubbed my memories on a Tuesday, which felt fitting, because Tuesdays were the days I used to forge ledgers for the Ortega family. [24] After the procedure, I was supposed to walk out a clean slate, blank as a hospital wall. [41] Instead I walked out carrying a baseball. [48] The technician pressed it into my palm like it was a passport, said a man named Hollis had left it for whichever version of me emerged. [74] Hollis, she explained, claimed to be a time traveler; he had signed the ball with a date thirty years from now. [95] I didn't remember Hollis. [99] I didn't remember anything, which was the point. [107] But the new me, the unsoiled me, felt a tug toward the signature, the way a compass needle resents being still. [128] Hollis had left instructions, too: read between the lines of the city paper every morning until you find me. [147] So I did. [150] Each dawn I bought the Tribune from the kiosk on Bell Street and decoded what wasn't printed: a misplaced comma here, a strange byline there, the obituary of a man who couldn't possibly have died. [185] The pattern, when it surfaced, pointed downward, beneath the city, to a service hatch behind the old streetcar barn. [204] I went down the iron rungs with the baseball in my coat pocket, feeling delicately robust, like a thawed bone that has decided, against precedent, to hold. [231] What waited beneath was a lake. [237] Not a sewer, not a cistern: a true underground lake, its water kindled by bioluminescent algae that pulsed soft blue with each ripple. [260] I stood at the shore and laughed, the way a ruined man laughs at a sunrise: with jaded wonder, half daring it to be cheap. [285] Hollis was sitting on a flat rock, throwing pebbles at his own reflection. [298] He looked older than the date on the ball would allow, and younger than the eyes in his face. [317] "You came," he said, as if I had options. [326] I told him I had no idea who he was. [336] He nodded, unsurprised. [339] "You wanted the wipe so you could begin again. I am here so you don't begin the same." [357] He explained the cavern was a labyrinth of choices, each glowing tunnel a different life I might still walk into. [377] He had walked them all, in his time, and come back to warn the version of me that could still be warned. [399] "Why bother?" I asked. [403] "You hurt people," he said. "The wipe took the guilt, but guilt was the only door left in you. I came to kindle empathy where none remains." [430] He handed me a lantern and pointed at the nearest tunnel mouth, where the algae thinned into something like real darkness. [451] "Each passage shows you a person you wronged. Walk one. Then choose." [463] I went. [465] The first tunnel held a woman counting empty drawers in a kitchen I had emptied; the second held a boy reading a letter from a father I had arranged to disappear. [496] The third was a courtroom, and the fourth a grave. [506] I walked seven tunnels, and on the seventh I sat down in the dark and cried for strangers whose names I could not summon. [530] When I returned to the lake, Hollis was gone. [539] Only the baseball remained on the rock, his future signature catching the blue light. [553] I picked it up and understood it had always been a map and a verdict at once: proof that some traveler, somewhere, believed I could become the kind of man worth visiting. [585] I climbed back into the city with the lantern still burning and a journey ahead of me, longer than any tunnel: the journey of paying every name I could find back, in coin or kindness or quiet. [622] The clean slate, it turned out, was not the end of the work. [635] It was permission to begin it. [641]</story>
