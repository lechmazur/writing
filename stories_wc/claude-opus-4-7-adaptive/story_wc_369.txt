Orla had kept the labyrinth for eleven years, trimming its yew walls, raking its gravel, and oiling the brass gate before dawn. [22] It was unglamorous work, and she loved it. [30] Visitors entered her hedges to get lost on purpose and came out lighter around the eyes. [46] Orla had learned to weigh that lightness. [53] In her ledger she recorded what people left behind: a sob by the fountain, three sighs at Corner Seven, laughter near the exit that sounded suspiciously like crying. [81] Quantified sorrow, she wrote, underlining it twice, because naming a thing made it manageable. [95] Among her catalogued losses, one object refused to leave her desk: an astronomer's broken sextant, its mirror cracked, its brass scratched, abandoned two winters ago by a man who had wept for an hour at the maze's heart. [133] He had told her, before leaving, that the instrument healed in moonlight. [145] She had laughed politely. [149] She had also, that night, set it on her windowsill. [159] By morning the hairline crack in the mirror had narrowed by the width of a hair. [175] Mundane miracles, she decided, were the only kind worth trusting. [185] Eleven winters of windowsills later, the sextant was nearly whole, and Orla had developed a theory. [201] The astronomer had not been measuring stars. [208] He had been measuring what the stars measured back: the slow grief of standing still and watching everything move. [227] It was the same grief, she suspected, that trees carried. [237] She had begun to want, unreasonably, to know what trees witness, what the yews of her labyrinth had seen in eleven years of weeping strangers, what the old oak on the hill knew about the valley before the valley had a name. [279] This was why, on the night of the storm, she climbed the observatory tower the astronomer had left behind. [298] The tower stood at the labyrinth's north edge, stormlit and empty, its dome open to a sky that could not decide between lightning and moon. [323] Rain washed the copper roof. [328] Moonlight cut through the clouds in white seams. [336] Orla carried the sextant, her ledger, and a jar of pins. [347] She intended to deconstruct the instrument, not to break it but to understand it, and she meant to do so by mapping the scattered dreams the visitors had left in her ledger onto the sky the sextant once measured. [386] Each pin in her jar represented one dream, a recurring mother, a drowned dog, a house with no doors. [405] She pushed the first pin into a chart she had drawn across the tower floor. [420] The sextant's mirror caught the moonlight and threw a thread of silver onto the chart. [435] She pushed another pin, then another, until the floor looked like a small constellation. [449] Lightning broke the dome open. [454] Thunder rolled the stones. [458] She worked anyway, because storms were only weather and weather was only arithmetic she had not yet finished. [476] Then the storm stopped. [480] The wind quit mid-sentence. [484] The rain hung. [487] In the corridor below, in a silence so absolute it felt engineered, Orla heard a pin drop, one of her own pins, fallen from her cuff, striking the flagstones six stories down. [518] At that exact sound, the sextant's mirror healed the last of its crack, and the chart on the floor resolved itself, and Orla saw. [542] Not stars. [544] Trees. [545] Every pin was a leaf; every leaf a year; every year a person standing still inside her hedges, feeling small. [565] The trees had been keeping a ledger too. [573] They had been counting her, counting her visitors, counting the astronomer who had come to measure grief and left his instrument because grief could not, after all, be measured with brass. [604] Only witnessed. [606] Orla gathered her pins, closed her ledger, and went back down to open the labyrinth for morning. [623] She left the sextant on the sill, whole, for whoever needed it next. [636] The yews, when she passed them, seemed to nod, though it was probably only the wind remembering how to move again. [657]
