<story>After the spoons bent reality, nothing worked the way the textbooks promised. [12] Iselde had been a budding rune scholar for only two seasons when the cutlery drawers began whispering in curved geometries, and the professors, embarrassed, sealed the library and fled. [41] She stayed. [43] She stayed because she was quietly deliberate by nature, and because she had inherited her grandfather's observatory: a nocturnal isle that drifted above the cloud-line, tethered to the world only by a brass chain and a rumor. [80] The observatory woke at dusk and slept at dawn, which suited Iselde, who had begun to suspect that something in the daylight was stealing hours from her. [107] Specifically, she wanted to find where shadows go at noon. [117] No textbook explained the vanishing. [122] At midday the shadows thinned, flattened, and slipped somewhere sideways, and after the spoon event they had started coming back shorter, as though they had been folded and kept in a pocket. [154] She suspected the observatory knew. [159] Its old mechanism, a planisphere of humming gears beneath the telescope, had been sealed since her grandfather's death, and the only way to open it was a clockwork automaton key he had left in her care with a single instruction: "Do not wind what you cannot unwind." [206] The key was brass, no larger than a sparrow's skull, and it ticked softly even when no one was winding it. [227] Iselde spent a week mapping the runes around the sealed plate. [238] They were awakening runes, the dangerous kind, the sort meant to keep sleeping things asleep. [253] She recognized the binding cipher: sealed awakenings, a containment lattice her grandfather had favored. [267] Something had been put to bed here, and her noonday shadow was the blanket. [281] That was the moment she understood what she was actually investigating. [292] The shadow did not vanish at noon. [299] It was drawn downward, into the plate, to feed the sleeping thing and keep it dreaming. [315] Every day since the spoon event, the binding had weakened, and a little more shadow had to be taken. [334] Soon there would be no shadow left, and then whatever slept beneath the observatory would wake hungry. [351] She had to relearn the binding from the inside out. [361] Her grandfather's notes were fragmentary, scribbled in the rushed shorthand of someone who assumed he'd have more time. [379] But he had left her the method: by closing loops. [389] Each awakening rune in a lattice formed an open loop, a mouth; to reseal them, she had to walk the circle and touch each rune with the key until the loops clicked shut, one after another, without skipping, without rushing. [429] She began at moonrise. [433] The observatory's floor was cold through her slippers. [441] She set the clockwork automaton key to the first rune, and it ticked into the groove like a tooth finding its socket. [463] Quietly deliberate, she moved to the next, and the next, refusing to hurry even when the chamber began to breathe around her. [485] Halfway through, the sleeping thing stirred. [491] A pressure rose in her ears; the telescope swung on its mount without wind. [505] She did not look up. [510] Looking up would break her concentration, and a broken loop was worse than an open one, because it pretended to be closed. [532] She touched the ninth rune. [537] The tenth. [539] The key grew warm, then hot, and the ticking slowed to match her pulse, as if her grandfather had built it for exactly this hand, this night. [566] At the twelfth rune the lattice shuddered and held. [575] She pressed the key to the last. [582] The loops closed together, silent as a mouth learning patience. [592] The sleeping thing sighed and sank, and Iselde's shadow, when she dared to examine it, lay long and unbroken across the floor. [614] She would have to redraw the binding before next noon, and the noon after that, and perhaps every noon of her life. [636] It was, she realized, what her grandfather had actually left her: not an observatory, but a watch. [653] She wound the key once, deliberately, and began to study. [663]</story>
