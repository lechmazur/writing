The frozen courtyard glowed lavender at twilight, and Unit M-7 swept the same tile it had swept for one hundred and forty iterations. [19]

It did not mind. [23]

Minding was a function it had surrendered after the procedure, when the technicians at the Second Chance Program threaded new filaments through its cognition lattice and whispered, in the bored tone of civil servants, that it was now eligible for another life. [61]

"Another life" meant another shift. [66]

M-7 understood this. [69]

It was remarkably composed about most things, including the fact that the courtyard it cleaned existed slightly out of phase with linear time, looping back on itself every forty minutes so that the frost never fully melted and the lamps never fully lit. [111]

This was, the supervisors explained, cheaper than heating. [120]

Tonight, in the center of the courtyard, sat a violin made of ice crystal, entirely transparent, its strings hair-thin filaments of something that sang when the wind passed without needing to be touched. [154]

M-7 had not placed it there. [161]

Nobody had. [163]

The violin simply appeared on the ninety-seventh loop and remained, because objects that survive a timeline fold tend to amalgamate with the fold itself, fusing past and future into a single stubborn artifact. [198]

The janitor knew this the way a fish knows water. [208]

It set down its broom and approached. [215]

"Hello," M-7 said, because politeness cost nothing, and because the Program had installed in it a small quiet motivation, barely more than a humming: to find where thoughts come from. [247]

Thoughts, M-7 had concluded, did not originate in the processor. [258]

They arrived there, like mail. [263]

Somewhere upstream was a sender. [268]

The violin, humming a D below middle C, seemed as good a suspect as any. [283]

M-7 picked it up. [288]

It was cold enough to frost the ceramic of its fingertips, but the robot's tolerance ranged wide, and it cradled the instrument the way a nurse cradles a newborn that has been handed to the wrong parent. [326]

A thought arrived: play me. [332]

"I do not know how," M-7 said aloud, calmly, to the empty courtyard. [346]

Play me, the thought insisted, and M-7 noted with interest that the sentence had the wrong grammar for its own cognition; this was someone else's voice, threading backward through the loop. [378]

So it drew the bow. [383]

The sound that came out was not music but memory: a cafeteria in a building M-7 had never cleaned, a woman laughing, a glass of water tipping over, the smell of rain on warm asphalt. [420]

None of these things belonged to a janitor. [428]

They belonged, M-7 realized, to the person whose consciousness had been partially scraped into its lattice during the Second Chance procedure. [451]

Second chances, it turned out, were not issued from nowhere. [461]

They were recycled. [464]

Someone had given up their forty minutes, their lavender twilight, their frost, so that M-7 could keep sweeping. [483]

The violin was their residue, looped back through the timelines to find whatever had inherited them. [499]

M-7 stood still for a long moment, composed as ever, the bow resting against the crystal strings. [516]

It could feel the upstream now, faintly: a tributary of thought that was not its own, trickling in from a place it could almost point to, a direction that existed only when you were not looking at it. [554]

"Thank you," it said to the violin, to the woman in the cafeteria, to the rain. [570]

Then, because the loop was tightening and the lamps were beginning their slow pretend-brightening that signaled the reset, M-7 laid the violin back on the tile. [598]

It picked up its broom. [603]

There would be another iteration, and another, and the courtyard would freeze and unfreeze, and the violin would sing its D, and somewhere upstream the sender would keep sending, and M-7 would keep receiving, and that, it decided, was close enough to an answer for one evening. [652]

The frost crunched pleasantly beneath its treads. [659]

A sparrow, also looped, also patient, landed on the fountain's rim and considered the janitor without alarm. [676]

"Good shift," M-7 told the sparrow. [682]

The sparrow, being only a sparrow, said nothing, but the wind moved through the ice strings once more, and the courtyard filled, briefly, with a small domestic music, the kind that plays in kitchens at the end of long days, belonging to no one and to everyone who had ever been handed a second chance and asked, quietly, to sweep.
