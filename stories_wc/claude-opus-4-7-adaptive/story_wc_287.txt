Mira tended her garden six stories above the alley, where rusted iron bars cradled pots of mint, thyme, and one stubborn rosebush her mother had planted before the war. [29] Neighbors called her the fire escape gardener, half teasing, half grateful for the basil she lowered in baskets to the second floor. [51] She was wistfully pragmatic, the kind of woman who pressed seeds into coffee cans and mourned each frost without ever letting mourning stop her hands. [76] Tonight, after the final petal dropped from the old rosebush, she stood still, listening. [90] The city held a quivering hush, as if the whole harbor had inhaled and forgotten to breathe out. [108] Below her, the luminous harbor by moonlight glimmered with reflected lamplight and the slow wakes of fishing boats returning late. [128] From her apron pocket she drew a small, strange keepsake: a lockmaster's bitting gauge tooth, brass-bright, notched, no longer than her thumbnail. [150] Her father had been a lockmaster, and before he died he had pressed the tooth into her palm like a promise. [171] Use it, he had said, to reflect light into dark corners. [182] She had never quite understood, but she had kept it polished, and tonight she meant to try. [199] The rosebush was the last living thing her mother had touched, and its bareness felt like a door closing softly. [219] So Mira began her small ritual with the rhythm of oars in water, dipping the brass tooth up, then down, up, then down, catching moonlight on its polished face. [248] A thin coin of silver light bounced off and slid along the alley wall, exploring the crevices where pigeons nested and where the boy from 3B sometimes hid to cry. [278] He looked up, startled, face bright with sudden discovery. [287] Mira waved once, the brass tooth held against her chest like a shield around something tender. [303] This was how she lived: protected vulnerability, a grief kept under glass but not hidden, a window you could knock on. [324] She flicked the tooth again, and the beam drifted to a doorway where an old fisherman mended his nets. [343] He lifted his chin, squinted, then laughed, and the laughter carried up six floors. [357] Mira understood then what her father meant: the tooth wasn't a key to any lock, but a key to attention, a way to instill in strangers the small belief that someone was watching kindly from above. [393] She moved along the fire escape, stepping between terracotta pots, and let the sliver of moon skip from wall to wall. [414] A cat's eyes flashed green in a gutter. [422] A drunk leaning against a lamppost turned his palm up as though testing for rain. [437] Two lovers on a balcony paused mid-argument to look for the source. [449] She kept her rhythm steady, oars in water, in and out, the city's stillness easing into something more like listening. [469] When the brass grew warm against her fingers, she let her hand rest. [482] The rosebush was still bare, but she had already decided to take cuttings tomorrow from the neighbor's climber and coax new roots in a jar. [507] Her mother's rose would not bloom again, but something would. [517] That was the shape of her pragmatism, wistful at the edges, stubborn at the root. [532] She tucked the bitting gauge tooth back into her apron, felt its weight settle like a small anchor. [550] Below, the harbor kept breathing in silver pulses, boats drifting home, lanterns nodding. [563] The boy from 3B climbed onto his own landing and waved up at her, no longer hiding. [580] Mira understood that this was the whole of the practice: to stand in your grief like a gardener on a narrow iron perch, and still find a way to send light into the dark corners of other lives. [618] She watered the thyme. [622] She watered the mint. [626] She watered the bare rosebush anyway, just in case. [635]
