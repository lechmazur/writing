Maren had walked the rim of Echo Gorge for seven years before she learned the trick of listening sideways. [19]

As a lost soul collector, she was supposed to gather the stranded dead and usher them onward, but her ledger was unusual: she refused any soul that had not yet laughed again. [49]

Her grandmother, a village healer, had taught her that grief without laughter was a wound that kept weeping inward, and Maren had made it her quiet mission to integrate laughter into healing, one spectral confession at a time. [86]

She was modestly fearless about this work, which is to say she trembled often and continued anyway. [103]

The gorge itself was a strange instrument. [110]

Wind slid along its throat like a bow, and somewhere in its deeper folds hung the Misaligned Harp, an old shepherd's relic whose strings had warped generations ago into a scale no living ear recognized. [144]

Souls gathered near it because distortion, Maren believed, was the only music the dead could still understand. [162]

On the morning her story turned, she descended into the gorge in the hush of a hidden sunrise, when light had not yet crested the eastern ridge but already silvered the mist below. [194]

She carried three things: a lantern, her ledger, and a crocodile's tooth strung on a leather cord, given to her by a river-witch who swore it could pull truth from any creature that had swallowed its own voice. [232]

She had never needed it until today. [239]

The harp began before she reached it, plucked by nothing, its notes bending as though the air itself were ashamed. [259]

Through those distorted notes she could hear the soul she had come to pursue: a boy named Teo, dead four winters, whose mother still left a bowl of broth out every night for a son who could not eat. [298]

Maren had tried to reach him before. [305]

He would not speak. [309]

She settled on a flat stone beside the harp and laid the crocodile's tooth in her palm, where it gleamed like a small, sharpened moon. [334]

"Teo," she said, "I brought something that listens." [342]

A shape gathered out of the frost, no taller than her shoulder, edges uncertain as breath on glass. [360]

The harp's lowest string bent a half-tone flat, and in that bending she heard him: "I was ashamed." [379]

That was the shape of every spectral confession she had ever gathered. [391]

Shame first, then the story beneath it. [398]

"I laughed," Teo said, "the day I drowned. [407]

I laughed at my brother's joke and swallowed the river, and my mother thinks I was frightened, but I wasn't. [428]

I was happy." [431]

Maren felt the luminous ache of it rise through her ribs, the grief and the gladness inseparable, like two notes played on the same warped string. [457]

"That isn't shame," she said gently. [463]

"That's a gift she needs." [468]

"She'll hate me for being happy when I died." [477]

"She'll weep," Maren said, "and then, someday, she'll laugh too, because laughter is how the body agrees to keep living." [498]

The tooth warmed in her hand. [504]

She had learned this was how it worked: it drew the hidden word out, and when the word was spoken aloud in the waking world, the soul could go. [532]

"Will you let me carry your laugh to her?" she asked. [543]

Teo hesitated, then leaned forward and breathed once against the tooth, a sound like a child stifling giggles in church. [563]

The harp straightened. [566]

For one impossible measure, it played in tune. [574]

Then the boy was gone, and the gorge exhaled, and the hidden sunrise finally crested the ridge and poured gold into the mist like something forgiven. [600]

Maren climbed out slowly. [604]

She walked three villages over, to a small house with a cold bowl of broth on the stoop, and she knocked. [625]

When Teo's mother opened the door, Maren pressed the crocodile's tooth into her hand and said, "He was laughing. [645]

He wants you to know he was laughing." [653]

The woman's face broke, as faces do, and then, after a long time, she laughed, a single startled sound, wet and astonished and alive. [678]

Maren wrote it in her ledger and walked back toward the gorge, modestly fearless, ready to listen sideways again for whoever the warped harp would sing her next. [706]
