Mira climbed the storm-worn, windblown parapet at moonrise, her cloak heavy with mist. [13] The wind here never rested. [18] For nine years she had served as prisoner exchange negotiator between the Thorne and Valun crowns, and for nine years she had climbed this same tower. [44] Neither kingdom owned it; that was the point. [52] The parapet belonged to no one, a spit of rock jutting from the mountain like a shard of broken promise - neutral territory, sanctified by old treaties nobody quite remembered writing. [82] She set her lantern on the stones and waited. [91] In her pocket, her fingers closed around an antique drawer pull. [102] Brass, shaped like a sparrow mid-flight. [108] She had taken it from the wreckage of a house in Edren years ago, the morning after she had authorized a raid that should never have happened. [135] The family inside had been civilians. [141] The pull was all that remained of the dresser, the dresser all that remained of the bedroom, the bedroom all that remained of a life she had ended with a signature. [172] She carried it everywhere now. [177] Not as penance - penance was cheap - but as a ledger. [187] Every decision since had been weighed against the bird's small, cold wings. [199] Her work, she had decided, was to recycle mistakes into wisdom, since nothing else could be done with them. [218] Footsteps scraped the far stair. [223] Captain Alven of Valun emerged from the mist, three prisoners roped behind him. [236] Mira inclined her head. [240] He inclined his. [243] Neither spoke; the hush between them was furrowed with old griefs, the kind of silence that sits between apology and forgiveness without belonging to either. [268] They had been on opposite sides of Edren. [276] He had lost a brother there. [282] She had lost something harder to name. [289] "The moon," Alven said, at last, nodding upward. [297] It hung above the mist, waxing gibbous, the agreed-upon phase. [307] Every exchange happened by moon phases: new for first contact, crescent for terms, gibbous for the handover, full for release. [327] The calendar had been devised a century ago by negotiators who mistrusted clocks because clocks could be falsified and the moon could not. [350] It synchronized two kingdoms that agreed on nothing else. [358] Mira drew the ledger from her satchel. [365] "Four for four," she said. [370] "Four for four," Alven answered. [375] She produced her prisoners from the stair behind her - a mapmaker, two soldiers, a boy who had stolen a horse. [395] Alven's list mirrored hers, carefully balanced, as the treaties demanded. [405] Then, as always, came the part that was not written. [415] Mira knelt and set the drawer pull on the stones between them. [426] Alven's face tightened. [429] He knew the object. [433] She had shown it to him the first year, when she was still learning what an uncommonly thoughtful negotiator was supposed to be: someone who could untether old angers without letting them blow away. [467] "Is this the year?" she asked. [473] He had told her once that when he could look at the sparrow and feel nothing, he would say so, and she could put it down. [499] Alven studied it under the gibbous moonlight. [506] The brass caught and held the pale glow like a held breath. [518] "Not yet," he said. [522] "But closer." [524] She nodded. [526] It was what she expected. [531] Forgiveness was not a gate that opened; it was a tide, and tides kept the moon's own schedule. [549] She tucked the pull back into her pocket. [557] They turned the prisoners loose. [562] Ropes fell away; men staggered forward into the arms of whichever side had waited longest for them. [579] The mapmaker wept. [582] The boy who had stolen the horse looked only confused, as if freedom were a language he had not yet been taught. [604] Mira watched them go, her hand still on the sparrow. [614] Wind worked the parapet, unravelling the mist in slow ribbons. [624] Somewhere below, a kingdom she had wronged continued its slow, suspicious healing. [636] Somewhere else, a kingdom she had served continued its own. [646] She would climb down now, write her report, and wait for the next moon. [660] The mistakes would keep. [664] The wisdom, she hoped, would keep growing. [671] And on some future night, under a moon neither of them had yet seen, the captain would look at a small brass bird and feel only the weight of brass. [701] Then she would set it down on the parapet and leave it to the wind, and walk home lighter by exactly the weight of one sparrow. [727]
