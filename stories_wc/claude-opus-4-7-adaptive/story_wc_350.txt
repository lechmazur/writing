Mira climbed the astronomical clock tower, the brass cuckoo clock weight swinging heavy in her satchel. [16] Below her, the township of Halversham slept between two rival grain elevators whose silos had not spoken civilly in twelve years. [37] She was the choral librarian of the tower archive, famous for her harmonious indexing of hymns in nine tongues, and tonight she had been asked to mediate the grain elevator talks. [68] The Eastern Cooperative spoke only Kestran; the Western Guild refused every word that was not Old Holm. [85] Mira intended to rehabilitate the conversation itself by interspersing languages, weaving Kestran verbs against Holmic nouns until each side heard its own heart in the other's mouth. [112] Her motivation was simple and stubborn: to construct bridges from silos. [123] At the top, under the hush of borrowed starlight leaking through the tower's glass zodiac, twelve elders waited around a table of maple and frost. [148] They would not look at each other. [154] Mira set the cuckoo clock weight on the table between them with the soft finality of a gavel. [172] "This fell from my grandfather's kitchen clock the night the border closed," she said. [186] "He kept it because gravity does not care which language it obeys." [198] Councilman Bren of the Eastern Cooperative snorted in Kestran; Elder Harr of the Western Guild answered in Holm. [216] Mira translated neither. [219] Instead, she began to sing. [224] Her voice braided the two tongues into a single stubborn rope: a Kestran lullaby for lost harvests, a Holmic work-song for rebuilt barns, and then a phrase neither language owned but both understood. [257] The elders listened, not because she was gentle, but because she was vigorous and tender at once, empathetically decisive in the way a surgeon is decisive when the patient is a village. [289] When she finished, she lifted the cuckoo clock weight and let it swing on its chain like a pendulum. [308] "Every silo in Halversham is a clock stopped at grief," she said, in Kestran, then in Holm, then in the seam between. [330] "A weight like this will not wind the mechanism alone, but without it the hands never move." [347] She set the weight down exactly between Bren's fist and Harr's folded arms. [360] "Lend me a grievance," she said to the Eastern side. [370] "Lend me a memory," she said to the Western. [379] Bren offered the burned granary of '09; Harr offered the wedding where Eastern fiddlers had once played. [396] Mira wrote them both, interspersing languages sentence by sentence, and passed the page across the table. [412] Each elder had to read the other's grief in his own tongue before continuing. [426] The astronomical clock above them turned its slow brass planets while the negotiation found an unfamiliar rhythm. [443] By the third hour, the silos had names again, not just numbers. [455] By the fifth, they had shared walls. [462] Mira, choral librarian and unlikely diplomat, did not smile; she nodded, the way a stonemason nods at a keystone finally set. [483] Before dawn, the elders signed a covenant to rehabilitate the old connecting chute between Elevator East and Elevator West, the one sealed since the border year. [509] A bridge, at last, from silos. [515] Mira pocketed the cuckoo clock weight and climbed down the spiral stair. [527] Outside, under the hush of borrowed starlight thinning toward pearl, the township exhaled like an instrument finally tuned. [545] She thought of her grandfather's clock, and of every stopped mechanism waiting for someone patient enough, and fierce enough, to lend it weight. [568] Tomorrow the chute would be cleared; next month, the wheat would flow both ways. [582] For now, Mira walked home humming in two tongues at once, empathetically decisive about the only question that had ever mattered: what song would Halversham sing when it remembered how. [612]
