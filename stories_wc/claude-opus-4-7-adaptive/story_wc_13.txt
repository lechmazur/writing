Vesna Orloff kept her sketchbook strapped to her ribs like a second heart. [13] As a natural history illustrator, she was paid to render creatures nobody else would sit still long enough to see. [33] The curators wanted the marginalia beasts: shelf-dwellers, chimney moths, the small gods that nested between architectures. [49] But Vesna had a private commission, unpaid and unspoken, which was to find the place where echoes are born. [68]

She believed the answer lay above the city, where the rooftop canal system wove between towers like silver thread through embroidery. [89] Boats the size of bathtubs drifted those high waters, poled by pilgrims and smugglers alike. [104] Vesna preferred to walk the towpaths, boots quiet on the copper sluices. [116]

The catch was timing. [120] The canals only ran when the giant slept. [128] Beneath the city, chained in a chalk cavern the architects politely called a foundation, a giant breathed in slow tidal surges. [149] His inhalations pulled the upper reservoirs dry; his exhalations filled them. [160] For six hours across a giant's nap, the aqueducts brimmed, and a determined illustrator could cross the whole skyline without ever touching street stone. [184]

Vesna had one more trick, and it hung at her throat: an infinity symbol pendant of tarnished brass, heavier than it looked. [206] Her grandmother, a cartographer of inaudible things, had left it to her with a single instruction. [222] "It sculpts time itself," she had said, "but only by borrowing time." [234] Every minute it bought had to be repaid, with interest, from the end of one's life. [250] Vesna had used it twice before: once to finish a plate of a dying firebird, once to say goodbye to her brother. [272] Today she would spend again. [277]

She climbed to the aqueduct at Starling Tower just as the giant's first snore trembled the weathervanes. [294] The water brightened. [297] She stepped onto the towpath and began to walk east, toward the Hollow Spire, where, she was certain, echoes were gestated before being loosed into the world. [324]

Halfway there, she heard it. [329] A melody played somewhere inside the masonry, a slow phrase climbing hopefully toward resolution, then stopping short. [346] An interrupted cadence: the chord that promises home and then refuses it. [358] The phrase repeated, each time breaking at the same unbearable step. [369] Vesna understood, suddenly, what a newborn echo sounded like, not a reply but a question the world could not finish answering. [390]

She followed the sound to a grate where the canal poured into the Hollow Spire. [405] Below, in a round room lined with bronze, hundreds of small birds sat motionless in wire cages, each with its beak open, each holding one note of the broken phrase. [435] They were not birds exactly; they were the first drafts of every sound the city had ever made, kept mute so that only their copies escaped into the air. [464] The cadence was interrupted because the final note was caged tightest of all, a thrush the color of candlelight, refusing to sing. [486]

Vesna knelt. [488] She understood the commission now, the real one, the one her grandmother had been building toward through three generations of borrowed minutes. [510] She gripped the infinity pendant. [515] Time thickened around her like honey around a spoon; the giant's breath slowed to stone. [530] She had perhaps four minutes of stolen life to work with, and a lifetime of drawing hands. [547]

One by one, she opened the cages. [554] To uncage an echo, she learned, was simply to draw it honestly, to sketch each bird's likeness onto the page so that the original could, at last, be released without the world losing its reflection. [589] The thrush was last. [593] When Vesna finished its portrait, it sang the withheld note, and the cadence completed itself across the rooftops in a long, astonished chord. [616]

Below, the giant stirred. [620] The canals began to drain. [625] Vesna walked home on emptying water, lighter by years she would never live, her sketchbook heavier than before, humming a resolution the city had been waiting centuries to hear. [654]
