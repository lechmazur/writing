Dris had been a stray philosopher for so long that his name felt borrowed from another man. [15] He wandered the mechanical rainforest because nobody else would, and because something there hummed at a frequency his thoughts had begun to echo. [38] The trees were not trees exactly. [44] They were brass-ribbed columns sheathed in copper leaves, each one fluttering on a hinge so fine that the wind passing through them produced not rustle but melody. [73] Pines clicked like typewriters; oaks groaned like cellos; the willows, when touched, sang in minor thirds. [89]

He had come here after the sixth sense awakened. [98] That was how the almanacs phrased it, with their uneasy capitalization, as though naming it politely might keep it tame. [118] Across the continent, people had begun to perceive a low, persistent hum, a probable miracle that refused to announce itself. [137] Most called it noise and installed filters. [144] Dris called it invitation. [148]

In his pocket, always, he carried a small gear. [158] It was the first piece he had ever pulled from a fallen mechanical sapling, years before any of this began, and its teeth were worn smooth from his thumbing. [188] He used it as a tuning fork for the mind. [198] When his thinking grew stale, he pressed the gear to a trunk and listened for how the hum answered. [217]

His attribute, the inconvenient one, was that he translocated during sleep. [229] Each night closed its eyes in one grove and opened them in another, sometimes a mile off, sometimes farther. [249] He had stopped resisting. [253] He now considered it a form of tutelage, the rainforest choosing his classrooms. [266]

On the ninety-third morning, he woke beside a grove of clockwork birches whose leaves spun like flywheels. [284] The hum here was louder, almost a chord, and it braided itself with a question he had been unable to ask in words. [307] He took out the small gear and set it on the moss of oiled felt. [322]

His motivation, if asked, would have sounded naive: to rediscover the wild frontier of his mind. [339] The cities had paved his thinking over with conveniences. [348] He had once been able to wonder about a stone for an entire afternoon. [361] Now he remembered wondering, which was not the same. [370] The rainforest, with its probable miracles, was the only place left where his attention ran unfenced. [386]

He closed his eyes and began to unravel. [394] That was the verb he used for the practice: he unravelled the tight braid of himself, strand by strand, until what remained was porous enough for the hum to move through. [426] Birch-pitch, oak-moan, willow-thirds, cedar-whisper: he listened to the sound of wind through different trees and let each tone pull a different thread loose. [451] A memory of his mother's kitchen slackened. [458] A grievance untied itself and drifted off like thistledown. [467] Certainty about what he was doing here unspooled last, and most willingly. [479]

When the final thread gave, the hum of insight arrived. [489] It was not a voice. [494] It was the shape a voice would take if a voice were weather. [506] The gear in his palm began, faintly, to turn by itself, matching a rhythm somewhere beneath the forest floor. [527] He understood, without translation, that the mechanical rainforest was not a ruin nor an invention but a tuning instrument, grown over centuries to prepare the human ear for exactly this awakening. [557] The sixth sense had not arrived from elsewhere. [565] It had been waiting, patient as rust, in everyone. [574]

He also understood that he would translocate again tonight, and that this, too, was part of the lesson: the wild frontier of the mind could not be mapped by a stationary man. [605]

Dris laughed, because laughter was the only sensible response to being correct about a miracle. [621] He pocketed the small gear, which was warm now, and walked deeper between the singing trunks. [637] A cedar near his shoulder released a note like a held breath, and a distant pine answered in a higher key, and between them his thinking stretched out long and strange and his own again. [670] He did not know where he would wake. [679] He knew only that, wherever it was, the hum would be there first, patient as rust, waiting to be heard by whoever had learned how to unravel enough to listen. [710]
