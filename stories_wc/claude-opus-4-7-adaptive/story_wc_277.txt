<story>After the journalist called, Iris Vance climbed the one hundred and twelve steps to the observatory her grandfather had built above the clouds. [23] She was, as her late father had written in every editorial about her, devotedly observant, a newsprint heiress who preferred margins to headlines. [46] The journalist, a young man from a rival paper, had asked a single question before hanging up. [63] Why, he wondered, did your father keep a rosewood pen nib locked in the observatory safe? [79] Iris had not known about the nib, nor the safe, nor, she suspected, many other small gravities her father had left spinning after his death. [104] The observatory stood on a shoulder of limestone, and the clouds tonight lay below its windows like a second, slower sea. [125] Her footsteps echoed against the brass meridian ring and the cold dome. [137] She set down the lamp and addressed the empty telescope with the solemn playfulness she reserved for places that refused to be solemn back. [161] Well, old friend, she said, let us find out what Father hid from his own daughter. [177] The safe was behind the star catalog, keyed to a date she remembered because it was the date she stopped speaking to him. [200] Inside lay the rosewood pen nib, a ledger, and a letter addressed to whoever had the courage to intervene. [219] She read the letter first, because she had always been obedient to paper. [232] Her motivation, she told herself, was simple: to discover hidden truths her father had kept because he thought her too soft to carry them. [256] The letter began with an apology and ended with a problem. [267] Assume, he had written, that the paper is clean. [276] Then why, daughter, do three councilmen pay us quarterly, and why does the mill manager's signature appear on invoices for ink we never bought? [300] He had solved it, he said, through proof by contradiction: if the paper were clean, these numbers could not exist; the numbers existed; therefore the paper was not. [328] The rosewood nib was the instrument with which he had signed the hush money receipts, kept as evidence against himself in case he lost his nerve. [354] He had lost his nerve, and then his life, within the same winter. [367] Iris lifted the nib to the lamp. [374] It was so small a thing to have orbited so many lives. [386] She thought of the concept her father used to sketch on napkins when she was a girl: orbits of regret, he called them, the loops we trace around the moments we might have corrected and did not. [423] Each year pulled you tighter toward the wound until the wound swallowed you. [436] He had drawn himself at the center of one, smiling with rueful eyes. [449] Now, above the clouds, Iris understood he had wanted her to finish the drawing. [463] The journalist would call again in the morning. [471] She could stall him, buy the rival paper outright, or, as she had been taught, bury the nib in a plot no one visited. [495] Yet the observatory, with its echoing ribs of iron and its habit of showing the world from too far away to lie about, refused any option but one. [523] She dipped the rosewood nib in fresh ink and began to write. [535] If the paper is clean, she wrote, it must be kept clean by someone willing to say so in it. [555] She signed her name with the same instrument that had signed the lies, which struck her as the only just use left for it. [579] Outside, the clouds shifted, revealing a town of small warm lights she was now bound to disappoint. [596] Iris laughed once, quietly, because solemnity alone would have broken her. [607] Then she folded the confession, addressed it to the young man at the rival paper, and stepped, for the first time in her careful life, out of her own orbit. [637]</story>
