<story>Miren had been root-bound for seven seasons by the time the future arrived early. [14] She could not walk, could not leave the cobalt glade where her ankles had fused with the silver roots of a listening tree, but she could see. [41] At dusk, when the blue shadow thickened into the hue people called her prison and her altar, the dreams of every sleeper within a day's ride flowed up through the taproot and into her chest. [76] Miren was purposefully curious about all of them. [84] Long ago, she had signed what the villagers called the Eclipsed Covenant: a pact with the tree that traded her mobility for a gift, and which promised a greater gift still, if she could find the intersection of dreams. [123] Seven sleepers, the tree had whispered, must dream the same shape on the same night, and then the covenant would open. [144] Tonight, that shape had come: a hedge maze, bone-white against black sky. [156] Miren felt it bloom seven times in seven skulls, identical down to the last crooked turn. [172] Anxious elation tightened her ribs; the intersection she had waited seven seasons to find was here, dreamed in unison, and she was terrified she would fumble it. [199] A boy named Teo crept into the glade then, carrying a small green object cupped in his palms: a maze topiary clipping he had stolen from the baron's garden that afternoon, when the sun had failed at noon and the future arrived early for everyone. [244] He had felt summoned without understanding why, and now he knelt at her roots. [258] "Heed what I tell you," Miren said, voice low. [267] "Six others are walking here in their sleep, each carrying a clipping like yours, each dreaming the same maze." [286] Teo looked at his scarred knuckles; he had many scars, earned trying and failing to be braver than he was. [306] "Why me?" he asked. [310] "Because the covenant opens only by linking through scars," she said. [321] "Every sleeper coming tonight has a wound that matches another's, stitched to each in a pattern the tree alone can read." [342] One by one, the others arrived: a miller's widow, a deserter, twin girls who had lost their mother to the same fever, a blind fiddler, a baron's son. [370] Each held a clipping, trimmed from a different hedge in a different garden, yet each twig fit the shape of the dream. [392] Miren, the root-bound visionary, guided them to sit in a ring around her, and she began to weave. [410] She asked the widow to touch the deserter's burn, the twins to touch the fiddler's blind eyes, the baron's son to press his palm to Teo's split knuckles. [438] Linking through scars, the seven formed a circuit that traced the exact maze their sleeping minds had drawn. [456] The clippings, laid at the center, stirred and knit into a single living hedge, no taller than a child, yet somehow vast. [478] Miren felt her taproot loosen. [483] The cobalt glade brightened, the eclipse behind the eclipse peeled back, and what the covenant had hidden revealed itself: a door inside the hedge, small as a fox, simple as a promise. [515] Through it, Miren saw every dream she had ever gathered, braided into a single river, and she understood: the intersection of dreams was not a place but a permission. [544] Permission to step off her root, if she chose, and to leave the future she had glimpsed to whoever was brave enough to walk into it. [570] She looked at Teo. [574] "You first," she said, and pressed her clipping into his hand. [585] The boy stepped through the fox-sized door and did not shrink; the door widened to heed him. [602] One by one the others followed, their scars glowing faintly where they had touched. [616] Miren stayed, the hum in her chest steady now, and felt the taproot tighten once more, content. [633] She had found the crossing; she did not need to cross it. [645] Somewhere beyond the hedge, the future was waiting, already arrived, patient for the seven scarred travelers who would make it make sense. [667] </story>
