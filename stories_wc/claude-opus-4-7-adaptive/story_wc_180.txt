<story>After the breach of contract, Odile climbed into the rafters of the abandoned cathedral with her tool-bag slung across her back. [21] She was the quartz-eye mender, named for the pale stones set into her sockets after a childhood fever — stones that, locals swore, let her see what was breaking before it broke. [52] Above her, the orchard thrived: apple and quince and pear trees rooted in the vaulted beams, their canopies lush where stained glass had fallen away. [77] The trees had been planted generations ago by an order who believed that vows, like leaves, must fall before new ones could grow. [100] They called them deciduous vows — promises meant to be shed, composted, and renewed each season, never clutched past their usefulness. [120] Odile's granddaughter, Wren, ten years old and endearingly feisty, scrambled up behind her, kicking loose a wooden shingle in protest. [140] "You said I could help fix the contract tree," Wren huffed. [151] "I said you could watch me try," Odile corrected, settling on a broad oak rafter where a single pear tree stood blackened at the trunk. [176] This was the covenant pear — the tree into which the town's mutual promises had been grafted each autumn, a living ledger tended by menders like her mother and grandmother before. [206] Last week it had split down the middle, the precise hour the miller publicly denied the debt he owed the weaver. [227] The breach had traveled up through the roots. [235] "How do we fix it?" Wren demanded, crouching beside a bruised quince. [247] "With whatever we remember to bring, and whatever we forgot we had," Odile said, tipping the tool-bag out onto the rafter. [268] Out fell the expected things — twine, grafting wax, a pruning blade — and then the forgotten things: a pouch of dried comfrey her mother had tucked in years ago, a wooden spindle, a stub of red chalk, and, fluttering loose, Wren's hair ribbon, lost the previous summer and mourned for weeks. [318] Wren gasped and snatched it up. [324] "I knew you had it!" [329] "I didn't," Odile said honestly, and that was the first accidental wisdom of the afternoon: the things we carry are rarely the things we think we carry. [356] She studied the wound in the pear tree. [364] A mender's task was not to restore what was, but to persist at the seam where old and new had to be coaxed together. [388] "The comfrey for the inside hurt," she murmured, crushing leaves into the split. [401] "The wax to hold it while it decides." [409] "The spindle —" she turned the little thing in her palm, "— to remind the grain which direction it grew." [427] "And the ribbon?" Wren asked, already guarding it. [435] Odile held out her hand. [440] "Ribbons bind two things and let them breathe." [448] Wren bristled. [450] "It's mine." [452] "I know," Odile said. [456] "That's why it'll work." [460] She waited, the way she had learned to wait on her own mother thirty years before on this same rafter. [480] Wren's face worked through its weather — indignation, pride, then the quieter thing beneath, which was the wish to be the kind of person who could give. [506] She surrendered the ribbon. [510] Odile tied it around the healing split, knotted loosely, the way her mother had taught her: a vow that could be undone when its season ended. [536] "That's all?" Wren asked. [540] "That's the start," Odile said. [545] "The tree will decide the rest." [551] "So will the miller, and the weaver, and the town." [561] "We only persist at the seam." [567] Wren looked out across the rafter-orchard, its impossible trees bright against the ruined stone. [581] "Why did you bring me up here?" she asked, suspicious. [591] Odile thought about saying something grand — about bridging what her mother knew to what Wren would one day know, about how a mender without an apprentice was only a mourner. [621] Instead she said, "Because I forgot half of what's in my bag, and you notice things." [637] Wren considered this, then nodded, accepting the compliment as her due. [648] "Next time I'm packing it." [653] "Next time," Odile agreed, "you are." [659] Below them, in the split trunk, sap began to move again — reluctant, feisty, persistent — carrying the new vow outward through the grafted branches of the town. [685] The ribbon fluttered. [688] A single pear leaf, yellowing early, let go of its stem and drifted down through the ribs of the nave, keeping its old promise by falling. [714]</story>
