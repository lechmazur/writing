<story>The pressure suit hissed as Ines sank past the second terrace of the drowned city. [15] Its gardens had been forgotten for two centuries, but lanternfish still tended the rows, and anemones bloomed where roses had once been pruned. [38] At the center stood the gardener. [44] She was marble, or had been; coral now threaded her robes, and a small octopus lived in the crook of her elbow. [66] Ines had come to destroy her. [72] In her glove she carried a foxglove-stained glass ampoule, dark as a bruise, prepared by her grandmother decades ago. [91] The family story was simple and certain: the statue had poisoned the reigning duchess, sunk the city by treachery, and had to be ended. [115] Ines had rehearsed the certainty until it gleamed. [123] Then the statue looked at her. [129] *You carry foxglove,* a voice said, not in her ear but behind her eyes. [143] Ines staggered. [145] The statue had not moved, but her thoughts arrived polished, incisively kind, as though someone were slicing away her defenses with the careful edge of affection. [171] *You have come to kill me.* [177] *I am glad you came.* [182] "You're the one," Ines said aloud, stupidly, the speaker fogging. [192] "You drowned them all." [196] *That is the certainty you were given.* [203] *May I welcome a little nuance into it?* [211] Ines's hand tightened on the ampoule. [217] She had prepared for defiance, not invitation. [224] *I will not stop you,* the statue said. [232] *But listen first.* [235] And because it cost nothing to listen, Ines listened. [244] The first revelation came gently: the duchess had not been poisoned but had poisoned herself, and the statue, then still the court's sculpted herbalist, enchanted to stand in for a living one, had been commanded to carry the cup. [283] The second came harder: the grandmother who had pressed the ampoule into Ines's small hand had been the duchess's own apothecary, and had fled the city carrying guilt the size of an ocean. [316] The third revelation was that the city had not been sunk by treachery at all. [331] *The sea wall had a flaw,* the statue said, *a seam the masons hid under ivy.* [347] *When the duchess died, there was no one left who knew to reinforce it.* [361] *I tried.* [363] *I was stone, and slow.* [368] Ines felt her certainty crack along a seam of its own. [379] "Then why," she whispered, "do you want me to end you?" [390] *Because I have been standing here two hundred years with the knowledge, and no living mind to pass it to.* [410] *Until now.* [412] The statue's marble lips did not move, but Ines felt something like a smile. [426] *The ampoule is not poison, child.* [432] *It never was.* [435] *Your grandmother dyed it with foxglove so that no one would open it.* [448] *Inside is a key, a resin her guild used to seal the sea wall.* [462] *If you thread it through the structural weakness behind me, the garden will be preserved another century, and the truth with it.* [484] Ines turned. [486] Behind the statue, half-swallowed by sponges, ran a hairline fissure in the old stone. [500] She stared at the ampoule in her glove, at the dark foxglove tint she had trusted like scripture. [518] After revealing the secret, the statue seemed lighter, as though telepathy were a weight she had been permitted at last to set down. [541] *I cannot move,* the statue said. [547] *You can.* [549] Ines knelt. [551] She uncapped the ampoule carefully and threaded the resin through the fissure, watching it bloom into the stone like ink into paper, sealing what two centuries of neglect had worried open. [582] The garden pulsed once, softly, as though a held breath had been released. [595] *Thank you,* the statue said, and Ines understood then what it meant to animate the lifeless: not marble into flesh, but certainty into question, inheritance into choice. [622] She rose. [624] Above, her boat waited; below, the gardener would stand another century, patient and smiling and stone. [640] Ines kicked upward through the anemones, carrying nothing now but the truth, which was heavier than any poison, and lighter. [660]</story>
