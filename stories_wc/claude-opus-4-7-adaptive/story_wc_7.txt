The rusted greenhouse leaned against the hillside like a tired old man, its iron ribs flaking orange onto the glass. [20] Mira arrived just after dusk, carrying her grandmother's knife and a paper envelope that rattled softly. [36] Inside the envelope sat a single knowledge fruit seed, black as a pupil, warm from her pocket. [53]

She was a chef who could taste memories. [61] Butter held the kitchens it had churned in; apples remembered the hands that plucked them. [76] The Ministry had outlawed her kind after the war, claiming their tongues made citizens nostalgic and therefore disobedient. [94] Mira had not argued. [98] She had simply retreated, little by little, into this ruined glasshouse where her grandmother had once grown impossible things. [117]

Tonight the planets were lining up. [123] A once-in-a-lifetime celestial alignment, the almanac said, six worlds stitched into a single bright seam above the broken panes. [142] Grandmother had written in her margins that the knowledge fruit would only take root under such a sky, and only if planted by someone who had already tasted sorrow. [171]

Mira knelt in the cracked soil. [177] She pruned the dead fig beside the bench first, snapping off blackened stems with careful thumbs, because her grandmother had said you cannot plant new things in a crowded grave. [207] The pruned branches smelled of smoke and childhood summers. [216] She ate a sliver of one and tasted her grandmother humming. [227] Then she pressed the seed into the dirt. [235]

Nothing happened, at first. [239] The planets kept sliding toward each other, patient as clockwork. [249] Mira sat back on her heels and waited, and the greenhouse creaked around her like a ship. [266]

"Only if someone tells," Mira said. [322] "I won't." [324] Oren settled on an overturned pot. [330] "But you know what that seed does, yes? [338] Not magic. [340] Not really. [342] The fruit rearranges you. [346] Through synaptic plasticity, the old books say, it teaches your brain new pathways, new tolerances. [361] People used to eat it when their grief had nowhere else to go." [374]

[492] They ate together in the rusting greenhouse, under a sky that would not repeat for lifetimes, and Mira tasted everything at once: her grandmother's hands flouring a counter, her father's laugh before the war, the first soldier who had come to seize their spice garden, the neighbor who had hidden them in a cellar, the bread she had baked last Tuesday, the bread she had not yet baked. [643] Mira gathered the seeds from the core of the fruit, seven of them, small black pupils, and tucked them into the envelope for whoever came next. [669] The rusted greenhouse held its breath around her, and for the first time in years it felt less like a ruin and more like a room waiting to be set for supper. [701]
