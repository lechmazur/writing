<story>Within the fold of a daydream, Ilse wandered into the ruined orchard district lost in centuries of thick fog. [19] She was a vivid mural artist, though her hands had forgotten color the way some mouths forget songs. [37] Her satchel held one clue: a fresco fragment pried from a collapsed chapel, its pigment still somehow warm. [55] She had come to rediscover lost joy, believing, against all reason, that the orchard remembered how to give it back. [75] The trees stood as gray suggestions, limbs braided with mist, apples long fossilized into pale knots of wood. [93] Ilse walked with the pensive mirth of someone who has laughed at her own grief often enough to trust it. [113] She hummed; the fog hummed back, a little off-key. [122] In the old days, before the blight and the silence, muralists here had painted with light. [138] They did not mix pigment so much as coax luminescence from resin, phosphor, and patience. [153] Their works glowed by dusk and faded by dawn, each wall a breath the city took before sleeping. [171] Ilse's grandmother had been one of them, and the fresco fragment in her satchel was the last surviving scale of that dragon. [193] To restore it, she would need the knowledge network that had once bound the painters together, master to apprentice, letter to letter, recipe to recipe. [218] Most of those threads had rotted with their keepers. [227] But a few, she suspected, still hung in the fog like spider silk, waiting for a careful finger. [245] She found the first at the base of a split pear tree: a tin box, rusted, half-buried. [262] Inside lay exposed film, curled and bruised brown from decades of uncovered light. [275] Any image it had held was presumably ruined, cooked into gray static by the accidental sun of some forgotten afternoon. [295] Ilse laughed softly, the kind of laugh that admits defeat and keeps walking anyway. [309] Then she noticed that the film was not uniformly spoiled. [319] Patterns, faint as frost, scaled across it: letters, diagrams, a hand-drawn map with tiny numbered dots. [335] Her grandmother's circle had used exposed film as a cipher, trusting that anyone who destroyed the picture would assume the message gone too. [358] The dots were addresses; the letters, names; the map, a route through the orchard to whatever remained of the guild. [378] She followed it tree by tree, dot by dot, gathering tin boxes like bruised fruit. [393] Each held a fragment of the old recipes: how to bind phosphor to lime, how to time a mural to moonrise, how to coax a wall to smile. [421] By the time the mist thinned into something like evening, Ilse had assembled a knowledge network of strangers long dead, their instructions passing through her hands as if through a translator. [452] She returned to the chapel at the district's center, where her fresco fragment belonged. [466] The wall was blank plaster, wet with fog. [474] Working by lantern, she mixed what the film had taught her: crushed shell, resin, the blue dust of a certain lichen that only grew on grief. [500] She painted an orchard in bloom, a grandmother under a ladder, a child with an apple too large to bite. [520] She pressed the fragment into its matching gap, and the seam vanished. [532] Then she stepped back, waited, and let the wall display itself. [543] Dusk arrived like a held breath releasing. [550] The mural woke, pale green at first, then warm gold, until the chapel glowed as though someone inside had remembered a joke. [572] Fog recoiled from the light; the dead orchard, briefly, looked merely asleep. [584] Ilse sat on the cold step and felt lost joy returning, not in a rush but in increments, the way color returns to a numb hand. [610] It was enough. [613] Within the fold of that daydream, which may have lasted a minute or a century, she laughed again, pensive and mirthful, and the orchard, listening, tried its best to laugh too. [644]</story>
