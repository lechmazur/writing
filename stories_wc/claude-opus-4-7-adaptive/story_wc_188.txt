Yusra arrived at the blooming fortress at dusk, where jasmine had colonized the cannon slots and bougainvillea spilled through arrow loops like slow, pink lava. [25] She was a wandering fireworks designer, pack rattling with paper tubes, cedar bark, and three stubborn colors of strontium. [44] She chanted while walking, an old habit traded from a grandmother who believed syllables could lift feet that no longer wanted lifting. [66] The fortress had been invited by the townspeople to bloom instead of crumble, a mercy granted after the last siege ended in nothing but tired hands. [92] Yusra had been summoned, she thought, though the letter from the town council had arrived wordless: a tile, a small mosaic posted inside a flat wooden box. [119] The mosaic was a note: indigo chips for music staves, amber for a single firework bursting above a child, and a chip of mirrored glass where her face ought to be. [150] She read such things the way other people read weather, learning to filter meaning through ambient awareness instead of grammar. [170] The courtyard smelled of wet stone and something sharper. [179] On a low bench lay a fire spinner's kerosene rag knot, the cotton wound tight in a monk's fist, damp and patient. [201] Beside it sat a boy of maybe nine, knees scraped, eyes enormous, practicing a face of studied indifference that fooled nobody. [222] "My sister left this," he said, lifting the knot as if it weighed more than kerosene should. [239] "She used to spin fire here every festival, and then last winter she stopped doing anything at all." [257] Yusra understood the rest without him saying it. [265] The mosaic had been the boy's work, and the council had merely carried it. [279] He wanted fireworks the way drowning people want rope: not for spectacle but to defy the gravity of hopelessness, to prove the sky could still hold something upward. [307] "Tonight is a hush night," she said, crouching to his level. "No stars. A very honest audience." [324] He almost smiled, which she counted as an invoice paid. [334] They worked as dusk thickened into the promised starless quiet, the fortress breathing jasmine around them. [350] Yusra assembled three small shells on the ramparts, tamping strontium for the color of stubborn things. [366] She let the boy dip wicks, coached him in the serious playfulness her craft demanded: hands steady, grin optional but recommended. [387] She chanted under her breath between instructions, and he began, uncertainly, to chant along. [401] When the first shell left the mortar, it climbed without apology. [412] The second bloomed into a slow chrysanthemum of amber, mirroring the mosaic tile the boy had pressed. [429] The third Yusra handed to him, because a hush night deserves a witness who did more than watch. [447] He touched match to fuse, and the kerosene rag knot on the bench below seemed to lean forward in its sleep. [468] Indigo pierced the black, a staff of light long enough to hang a song on. [483] Somewhere below, windows opened; somewhere further, a figure in a long coat stepped from a doorway and stopped walking. [502] Yusra sensed her before seeing her, and nudged the boy gently. [513] His sister did not run toward them, nor away; she simply watched the slow chrysanthemum die and the ember-threads drift like a paragraph she had forgotten she knew. [541] Later, she would pick up the kerosene rag knot, untie nothing, and carry it home to soak again. [559] Later still, Yusra would leave at dawn, pack lighter by three shells and one small weight she had not been carrying in her bag. [583] For now, the three of them stood under a sky with no stars, which felt, against all evidence, like permission. [603]
