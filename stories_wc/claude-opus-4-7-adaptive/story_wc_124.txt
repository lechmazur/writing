Ilse Varn sold lanterns in the lower arcades of Nyx, and she sold them wistfully, like someone handing away pieces of a life she could not quite finish. [28] Her customers called her the wistful lantern broker, though most did not know why the name fit so perfectly. [47] Every evening, when her shop closed, she walked uphill to the Conservatory of Absences, a greenhouse for extinct plants tended by the old botanist Harun. [72] Inside, behind fogged glass, the last ghost-orchids, salt-laurels, and ember-figs grew under lamps he could not afford to replace, which was why he bartered with her. [98] Ilse provided lanterns; Harun let her sit among the greenery and breathe. [110] She had been aromatically cautious since childhood, avoiding bakeries, cedar chests, and the perfume counters of department stores. [128] Certain smells made her weep without reason, and she had come to the greenhouse to understand why some smells trigger tears. [149] Harun believed every scent was a folded letter from the past. [160] One rainy evening, he placed a crystal perfume vial in her palm and told her it had belonged to her mother. [181] Ilse had not seen her mother since the famine year when the salt-laurels vanished from the coast. [198] The vial was stoppered, warm, and full of something pale that did not slosh like liquid. [214] Harun dimmed the greenhouse lamps and lit an ultraviolet tube he used for authenticating old botanical labels. [231] The crystal caught the purple beam and began its ultraviolet revealing. [242] Ghost-letters swam across its facets: her mother's handwriting, the date of a coastal wedding, a recipe for a soup made from a flower that no longer existed. [269] Harun explained that certain glassmakers once engraved scent-memories directly into crystal, preserving not the perfume itself but its meaning. [288] The tone of the room shifted into crystal shadows, violet and trembling, the plants around them looking like drowned constellations. [308] Ilse opened the stopper because she was finally ready. [317] The scent that rose was salt-laurel and bread-yeast and rain on warm stone, the exact weather of a morning she had forgotten she owned. [341] Tears came at once, but this time she did not resist them; she let herself grieve openly, for the first time, for the mother she had outlived without knowing. [370] She understood now that smells were not triggers but doors, and she had been standing outside them all her life, refusing entry to protect a self she did not need anymore. [401] Harun watched quietly, hands folded, respectful of the impact of memories long sealed. [414] He had waited years to give her the vial; he had only been sure when he saw her begin to trust scent again. [437] As the transformation completes, something in Ilse settled, like sediment in still water. [450] She was no longer the wistful broker who flinched from bakeries and department stores. [464] She was a woman who had inherited a whole coastline in a single breath. [478] She asked Harun if there were other vials, other voices trapped in glass, waiting to be decoded by ultraviolet light. [498] He nodded toward a cabinet behind the ember-figs, where dozens of stoppered crystals waited in careful rows, each one a small unread library of someone's vanished afternoon. [525] Ilse realized her lanterns had been preparing her for this work all along; light and memory were the same craft, practiced in different mediums. [549] She would learn to read these bottled pasts, to return them to the descendants who wandered the arcades, hungry for a voice they could not name. [575] The greenhouse exhaled its green, impossible breath around her, and for a long moment she stood inside the crystal shadows without fear. [597] Then she pocketed the vial, the first of many, and began her new apprenticeship in the aromatic past. [615]
