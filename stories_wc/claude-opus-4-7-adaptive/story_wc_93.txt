Nen was an elusive eel herder, known only to the handful of villages that still needed eels guided through the drowned canyons off the coast. [25] He kept to the tidal bluff under aurora, a long spit of basalt where the northern lights folded themselves into the sea like paper into water. [51] People said he was precognitively cursed, that he knew each visitor's question before it was spoken and answered the wrong one on purpose. [74] The truth was quieter. [78] He could see what would happen, but never why. [87]

That was the ache of it. [93]

For years he had kept a lingering promise made to his sister before she walked into the surf with a pocketful of what she called collapsed star dust, a pinch of gray powder she swore had fallen from a dying constellation. [131] She had made him swear to finish her work, though she had never told him what the work was. [149] Only that the answer lived in the patterns, and the patterns lived in the stones. [164]

So Nen spent his evenings placing painted rocks in mathematical sequences across the bluff. [178] Fibonacci spirals near the tidepools. [183] Prime-numbered cairns where the wind cut hardest. [190] Triangular progressions along the cliff edge, each stone blushed with ochre and indigo he ground himself from crushed shell. [209] His motivation was to understand sacred patterns, the grammar behind the shape of things, the reason a wave curled at exactly the angle it did and not a degree otherwise. [237] He believed his sister had glimpsed that grammar. [245] He believed the collapsed star dust, still wrapped in oilcloth in his coat pocket, was a key he had not yet learned to turn. [269]

The eels helped, in their way. [275] They schooled in threads of silver he could almost read, and he had learned to harness their motion, steering them with low hums along the kelp corridors where the farmers' traps waited. [308] Each herd paid him in salt and bread and news. [318] News was how he knew the world was ending on a Tuesday. [330]

Not ending, exactly. [333] Changing. [334]

His curse had shown it to him in fragments for weeks: a morning when the sea would stop resisting itself, when the oars of passing boats would slip without drag, when his own footsteps would glide strange across the rock. [370] The physicists, if he had known any, would have called it the timeframe after friction disappears. [385] Nen called it the hour his sister had warned him about without naming. [398]

He woke before light on the promised day and climbed the bluff with his pouch of dust. [415] The aurora was down low, combing the horizon in a borrowed dawn, green and rose where sunrise should have been. [435] Everything felt glassy. [438] A pebble he nudged with his boot slid for half a minute before stopping against a tuft of lichen. [457]

He walked the stones. [461] The sequences were finished now, every rock in place, a great spiraling diagram stretching from cliff edge to tidepool. [482] From above it would look like a single enormous glyph. [492] From inside it, he saw at last what his sister had been building toward. [506] The patterns were not a map. [512] They were a net. [516]

He understood then why she had walked into the water. [526] Someone had to be on the other side to catch what fell through. [539] The collapsed star dust in his palm was not a key. [551] It was a seed, a speck of something that remembered being whole, waiting for a morning smooth enough that it could roll all the way home. [578]

He scattered it along the outermost spiral. [585] The grains moved without resistance, sliding across the painted rocks, threading the sequences in the exact order he had never quite dared to calculate. [613] Above him the aurora tightened like a drawn breath. [622]

Nen felt his foresight, the old curse, finally go quiet. [633] He would not see what came next. [640] That, he realized, was the gift his sister had been saving for him, the single unknowable hour at the end of a lingering promise. [665]

He sat on the cold rock and watched the borrowed dawn finish becoming a real one. [681]

The eels, below, were waiting for their herder. [690]

He went down to meet them. [696]
