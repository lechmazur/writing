<story>Meira had chronicled the tidal pools of Orrin's Shelf for thirty seasons. [12] She was known in the villages as the warmly observant woman who listened to water more than to people. [31] Her notebooks were full of transition periods: the exact moment a barnacle first spread its feathered hand, the slow minute when a limpet loosed its grip and slid a quarter-inch toward the sea. [64] She wanted, she said when pressed, to discover the texture of time. [76] Her brother Teo had come up from the capital wearing new shoes and an old grievance. [92] He wanted her to sign papers selling their mother's house. [102] Meira had not refused; she had only suggested they talk in the ancient grotto under stars, where the tide would be low by midnight. [126] Teo had laughed at first, then shrugged, then followed her down the cliff path. [140] The grotto was older than any village chronicle. [148] Its mouth opened on the sea; its roof opened on the sky; and at the back, in a niche worn smooth by worship long forgotten, Meira kept a small jade cicada that their mother had left her. [185] She lifted it now and set it in Teo's palm. [195] "Mother told me cicadas were buried with the dead because they teach the body how to shed what it no longer needs," she said. [219] "I don't want a lecture," Teo answered. [226] "I want a signature." [230] The conversation grew sharper, the way conversations do between people who love each other and disagree on what love should pay for. [252] Then Teo fell silent, staring at the green stone in his hand. [264] Across the pause in that difficult conversation, Meira did what she always did: she moved her attention sideways. [282] It was the only method she trusted, this tangential approach, learned from years of watching creatures that flinch under a direct gaze. [304] To know a pool, she had written once, never ask it directly; study the shadow it throws. [321] So instead of answering Teo she watched the small pool at her feet. [334] A hermit crab had outgrown its shell and was testing a new one, half in, half out, the most exposed minute of its life. [358] A plume of sand drifted; an anemone pulled in its fingers; above the grotto, a star she knew well moved one slow handbreadth westward. [382] Meira could foresee, from these small signs, that the tide would turn in seven minutes. [397] She could foresee, too, what Teo was thinking, though she waited to be sure. [411] He was turning the jade cicada over and over, and his face had gone soft at the edges, the way a cliff goes soft under a long rain. [439] "I haven't held this since I was nine," he said at last. [451] "She used to put it on my pillow when I had fevers." [463] "I remember," Meira said. [467] She did not push; she only kept her quiet around him, the same attentive quiet she gave the pools. [486] After a while he said, "The house is falling apart, Mei. I can't keep up the taxes. That's all I came to say." [509] "Then we'll sell it," she answered, "but we'll do it together, and slowly." [522] "The in-between," she added, half smiling, "is the only texture I believe in." [535] The tide turned, as she had known it would. [544] Water climbed one inch up the barnacled wall, then two. [554] The hermit crab committed to its new shell and scuttled off into the dark. [568] Teo set the jade cicada back in its niche. [577] He did not say thank you; he said, "I'll come back in spring." [590] Meira opened a fresh page in her chronicle and wrote the date, the star, the crab, the brother. [608] She had wanted her whole life to know the texture of time, and it was this: grit and salt, an old stone warm from a palm, a long pause, and the slow return of water. [643]</story>
