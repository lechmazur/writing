Nellwyn had not left her cottage in seven winters, but the crumpled invitation on her kitchen table behaved as if it wanted her to. [24] She had smoothed it, uncrumpled it, smoothed it again, each pass deepening her suspicion that whoever sent it already knew she would come. [47] A compassionate recluse, they had called her in the village, which was a gentler name than hermit and a truer one than saint. [70] She lived alone because she was keenly empathetic, and crowds pressed on her like hands on a bruise. [88] The invitation asked her to attend, within arm's reach of midnight, the opening of a door she had spent years pretending did not exist. [112] Long ago, when she was still a girl who trusted her own cleverness, she had betrayed a friend by repeating a secret that was not hers to carry. [140] The friend vanished; the guilt did not. [147] Her retreat into the woods had been an attempt to hide from the memory, though memories, she learned, were patient houseguests. [168] Now, on this frost-silvered night, she walked down the lane to the town bakery, unlocked its rear door with a key she had never been given, and descended. [196] Beneath the ovens lay a secret society library hidden in plain sight: every villager had smelled its bread, none had guessed the heat rose from lamps reading books. [224] The shelves curved like the inside of a nautilus, and upon them rested volumes of nestled paradoxes, a book that grew longer when read quickly, a catalogue that listed only the titles it did not contain, a map whose legend advised you to get lost. [269] An old woman waited at a reading table, and Nellwyn knew her at once, though thirty years had rewritten every line of the face. [293] "Meret," Nellwyn whispered. [296] "I did not vanish," Meret said gently. [303] "I joined. The library keeps those the world misplaces." [312] Meret slid forward a slim volume bound in pale leather, its title pressed invisibly into the cover: On Staying While Leaving. [333] It was, Meret explained, the method by which the society's members lived, present in the world, absent from its demands, a body in the village, a mind beyond it. [362] Nellwyn's years alone had been a rough sketch of this same craft, drawn without a teacher. [378] "Why tell me now?" Nellwyn asked. [384] "Because your punishment has outlived its lesson," Meret said. [393] "You came here to unearth wisdom in a mistake. You have mistaken the wisdom for the mistake." [410] The clock above the kneading bench crept toward the hour, its hands folding into an almost-prayer. [426] Nellwyn opened the book. [430] The pages were mostly blank, waiting, she realized, for the reader's own remembered wrongs. [444] She wrote, with a slow hand, the name of her lost friend and beneath it the single word: Found. [463] A warmth opened in her chest that she recognized only by its absence of noise, an enlarged tranquility, as though a window long painted shut had been eased upward by a patient hand. [491] She understood, then, the compassion the villagers had detected in her; it had been grief all along, politely rerouted. [512] Meret watched in silence, leaving space for the confession to finish itself. [524] They spoke until the bread-smells above thinned and the hour slipped past its apex. [538] When Nellwyn climbed the stair again, she carried the book under her arm and the society in her chest. [557] She would return to her cottage, and she would also not return; she had learned the trick of staying while leaving without noticing she had. [582] The invitation, uncrumpled one last time, she pinned above her door as a small, private banner. [598] Behind her, the library remained where it had always been, in plain sight and nowhere anyone would think to look, keeping its quiet for the next person who needed to hide inside a truth rather than away from one.
