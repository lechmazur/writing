<story>Father Emory had not set foot in Halberd's Antique Music Box Repair Shop in eleven years. [16] The bell above the door rang once, then refused to ring again, as though embarrassed by its own enthusiasm. [35] Inside, the air held a vibrant hush, as if every cylinder and comb on the shelves were mid-breath, waiting for someone to deserve a song. [60] Halberd himself stood behind the counter, hunched over a tray of gears, aptly deliberate in every movement, the way a surgeon is deliberate, or a man defusing something private. [89] "Chaplain," he said without looking up. [95] Emory placed a small velvet bag on the counter and unwrapped a clockwork heart, its escapement bent, its mainspring snapped clean through. [117] It had belonged to a boy named Thomas Reeve, who built it at fifteen and who, at twenty-two, had been shot in a grocery store holdup by a man Emory had since spent four thousand evenings counseling in the penitentiary. [157] The heart was Thomas's mother's only keepsake, and she had given it to Emory last Christmas with a look that was not quite forgiveness. [181] "I've had two watchmakers tell me it's beyond repair," Emory said. [198] "I came for a third opinion." [204] Halberd finally looked up. [208] "Second opinions are for the living, Father; third opinions are for men who can't accept an answer." [225] "Then call it the first honest one." [232] Halberd lifted the heart under his loupe. [239] Outside, a storm had been building all afternoon, and now the first fat drops struck the shop's transom windows. [258] "The mainspring I can replace," Halberd murmured. [265] "The escapement I can straighten. But the balance wheel has a hairline I'd have to weld, and welds always remember." [285] "So it will work, but not cleanly." [292] "It will work like something that was broken," Halberd said. [302] "Which is, I suppose, what you wanted." [309] Emory sat on the stool by the counter and listened to the rain. [322] He had been telling Thomas's mother for three years that he was sorry, and sorry had become a word she flinched at the way one flinches at a bell that keeps ringing. [354] He had come here to turn an apology into action, to put back into her hands something that ticked. [373] Halberd worked for over an hour, his magnifier a small lit moon. [385] The shop smelled of oil, brass, and cedar shelving. [394] Emory thought about the man in the penitentiary, who had asked last week whether God kept spare parts. [412] He had not known how to answer; now, in this calm before realization, the answer was assembling itself in another man's hands. [434] The storm slackened. [437] A slow column of light broke through the clouds and slanted through the transom, and by the way light breaks through storm clouds, precisely so, angled and temporary and undeniable, Emory understood what he had been unable to say from the pulpit or the prison cell. [483] Forgiveness was not a verdict. [488] It was a repair, imperfect, audible, welded in places that would always remember. [501] Halberd closed the case and wound the key three turns. [511] The clockwork heart began to beat, uneven at first, then finding its cadence, ticking like something that had chosen to continue. [532] "What do I owe you?" Emory asked. [539] Halberd shook his head. [543] "Give it to whoever deserves it. Make sure they understand it isn't new." [556] Emory wrapped the heart and stepped outside. [563] The wet street mirrored a cleaner sky. [570] He would drive to Mrs. Reeve's house tonight, not with words this time, but with a small brass thing that ticked against his palm, proof that sorry could be machined into a sound she might finally let herself hear.
