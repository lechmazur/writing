<story>I rowed into the mangrove labyrinth at dusk, a satchel of unsent letters pressed to my chest like a second, smaller heart. [22] The spirits were already singing. [27] Their voices moved through the roots in low, braided chords, as if the water itself remembered every name it had ever swallowed. [49] I had come to find Odeya, the cryptic medium, whose hut balanced on stilts above the tide. [66] She did not greet me. [71] She only tilted her head, as though listening for the weight of what I carried. [86] "Seven letters," she said. [90] "Six to my sister." [94] "One to myself." [97] She nodded, unsurprised, and stepped aside. [103] Inside, the hut smelled of brine and green smoke. [112] On a low table lay a gargoyle's rainspout shard, chipped from some cathedral a continent away, pale as bone. [131] "It heals in moonlight," Odeya said, noticing my stare. [140] "Tonight it will be whole enough to listen." [148] I sat. [150] I did not ask how she knew my sister, Ilse, had drowned in the shallows last spring, or that I had not spoken to her in two years before that, or that every letter in my satchel began with the same word: Wait. [193] "You want to send them," Odeya said. [200] "No." [201] My throat closed. [204] "I want to stand in the space where an apology waits unspoken." [216] "I want to find the moment before deciding - before I chose pride, before she chose the boat, before any of it became a thing that could not be taken back." [246] Odeya watched me with something like fierce tenderness, the way a blacksmith watches iron. [260] "Then we do not send them," she said. [268] "We evoke them." [271] She carried the shard outside and laid it on a flat stone. [283] Moonlight pooled into its cracks and the shard knit slowly, seam by seam, until it gleamed as if newly broken from the rain-dark mouth of its gargoyle. [310] The spirits in the mangroves sang louder, curious. [318] Odeya placed the shard against the cold glass of her single window. [330] "Read them," she said. [334] "To whom?" [336] "To the air between then and now." [343] I read. [345] My voice shook on the first letter, steadied on the second, broke entirely on the fifth, when I reached the sentence I had crossed out three times: I was afraid you were becoming our mother. [380] As I spoke, condensation bloomed across the window where the shard touched it. [393] Letters formed in the fog - not mine, but answers, written in a hand I had not seen since childhood. [412] I am not her. [416] I was never her. [420] Wait with me. [423] I pressed my palm flat against the glass. [431] The fog took the shape of Ilse's fingers beneath mine. [441] "She is not here," Odeya warned, gentle. [448] "Only the moment is here." [453] "Only the before." [456] I understood. [458] This was not resurrection. [462] It was the hinge on which the door had not yet shut, preserved in mangrove song and moonlit stone, so that I could stand in it once, fully, and know what I had failed to do. [498] I told her everything I would have said. [507] I told her the pride, the fear, the small mean thing I had said about her husband, the way I had rehearsed forgiveness and then refused to offer it. [536] The condensation kept answering, tender and ferocious by turns, the way Ilse had always been with me. [553] Finish your letter to yourself, she wrote. [561] I opened the seventh envelope. [566] It said only: You are allowed to have loved her badly and still be her sister. [582] I read it aloud. [586] The spirits in the roots went quiet, as though making room. [597] When I looked up, the shard had cracked again along its old seam; the moonlight had moved on. [615] Odeya gathered the pieces without ceremony. [621] "It will heal next month," she said, "for someone else." [631] I rowed home before dawn, the letters still unsent, still mine, but lighter now, as if each one had been read by the only reader who mattered: the version of me who had not yet decided.
