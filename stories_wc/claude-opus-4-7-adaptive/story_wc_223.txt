Mara had played a grieving widow on television three weeks before the real bomb took her sister. [17] After that, she could not tell which tears were hers. [27] She had been hired as a crisis actor for drills, and then she had become a real crisis survivor, and the difference, people insisted, was supposed to matter. [55] It did not, or it did, but only in the way a shadow matters to the thing casting it. [74] She left the studio town the day the old prime minister died and the new one had not yet been sworn, a brief interregnum when the country could not agree on whose flag to raise. [109] Mara found that absence suited her. [115] She walked. [117] She walked in patterns, and the patterns spelled words. [126] On a map of any city, her route on Monday might read SORRY, on Tuesday UNBIND, on Wednesday the long and patient letters of her sister's name. [153] No one saw the words except the satellites, and perhaps a god with a taste for footnotes. [170] This was how she intended to become the historian of small gestures: by making them herself, one footstep at a time, until the grammar of her grief was written across the paving stones of strangers. [205] She called herself a nomadic penumbra, half in jest. [214] A travelling half-shadow, attached to no body, thrown by the vague light of public mourning. [229] In the capital she slept in a hostel near the old cathedral, whose dome was famous for its whispering gallery. [249] A guide had told her, years ago, that a secret murmured into the curve of the wall could travel a hundred feet and arrive intact at another ear. [277] She climbed the narrow stairs the morning after the prime minister's death, expecting to be alone. [293] Instead she found a man kneeling with a chessboard open on the stone floor. [307] All the pawns were missing. [312] Rooks, knights, bishops, king, queen: present and polished. [320] The front row, where the small and expendable should have stood, was bare. [333] He looked up at her as if she had been expected for some time. [347] "I design drills," he said, before she had spoken. [356] "Earthquakes, shootings, collapses." [359] "I hire the actors, I choose who dies on paper, I coach the ones who lie bleeding in the hallway for training videos." [382] Mara recognized him, in the way one recognizes a signature on a bad cheque. [396] He had been her employer. [401] He had also written the scenario that most resembled the bomb that took her sister, two years before any such bomb existed. [423] "I took the pawns out," he said, "because I cannot keep playing with them." [437] His voice, tuned to the gallery, slid along the curve of the dome and arrived at her ear sounding almost tender. [458] A benevolently ruthless man, she thought; the kind who plans your extinction with kindness in his notes. [475] He wanted absolution, or a witness, or possibly both. [484] Mara sat on the cold stone and considered the board. [494] The pieces without pawns were a government without citizens, an army without soldiers, a country in interregnum. [511] She thought of her sister, who had been a school librarian, nobody's pawn except on the day she had been counted as one. [534] "I have been walking your name across cities," she said into the wall. [547] "Today I was going to spell FORGIVE." [554] "I will spell UNBIND instead." [559] The word travelled the curve of the dome and reached him cleanly. [571] He closed his eyes. [575] She would not forgive him, because forgiveness was a larger thing than she had; but she would unbind him from the private hell where she had kept him these two years, because carrying him there had become a kind of second job. [617] That was a small gesture, and she meant to record it. [628] Outside, under the luminous absence of a flagless sky, she began to walk. [641] Her footprints traced letters no one would see from the ground. [652] Behind her, in the gallery, the designer gathered his rooks and bishops into a velvet bag and left the chessboard on the stone, missing all the pawns, as a confession no one could overturn. [686] Mara, nomadic and half-lit, crossed the square writing her sister's full name in steps, a historian of the smallest possible gestures, beginning her archive. [710]
