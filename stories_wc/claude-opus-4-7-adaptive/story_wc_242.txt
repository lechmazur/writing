Mira kept the only working phone booth left in the city, and it lived twenty feet beneath Canal Street. [19] The booth had been forgotten by the transit authority, marooned in a subway utility tunnel where rats negotiated with steam pipes. [40] She called herself an underground phone booth operator, though no payroll listed her, and no one had assigned the post. [60] Mira had simply descended one winter, found the receiver warm, and stayed. [72] The job, as she understood it, was to answer. [81] Calls arrived through a fault in the city's switching grid, misrouted dials and confused operators dumping their orphans down to her. [102] Wrong numbers, all of them. [107] This suited her purpose, which was to teach endings how to begin. [119] She had practiced cultivated estrangement most of her adult life, drifting away from family the way a kite slips its string, telling herself the loneliness was clean. [146] But clean things gather dust, and dust gathers names, and one night a man dialed his dead sister and got Mira instead. [168] He spoke for forty minutes; she spoke for three. [177] When the call ended, she realized she had been listening for the click the way the faithful listen for bells. [197] That was how it began, her ministry through wrong number friendships. [208] She kept a logbook: the trucker who needed someone to say goodnight to a wife he had outlived; the teenager who pretended Mira was a college admissions hotline; the widow who simply wanted directions to a bakery that had closed in 1987. [250] Mira gave each of them a small new beginning before letting the line go dark. [265] The booth itself helped her work. [271] Bolted under the receiver's cradle was a glass harmonica disc, salvaged from a Franklin instrument lost in some forgotten warehouse fire. [292] When a caller's voice trembled, Mira would draw a wet thumb across the rim, and the tunnel would fill with a single sustained note, melodically haunted, a tone that seemed to remember more than it played. [328] Strangers wept against the tone and, weeping, found doorways inside their grief. [340] She worked only between the hum of streetlamps, those small minutes when the sodium bulbs above the grates clicked their pitch from waking to dreaming. [365] During those intervals the city was honest, and dishonest people called more freely. [378] She would loop the same closing phrase to each, never the same shape twice: I am hanging up so you can pick something up. [402] It was a stitch she pulled across every farewell, a loop binding their ending to a beginning they had not yet named. [424] Months braided into years. [428] Then, in the wettest April of her tenure, the phone rang for her. [441] The voice belonged to her brother Daniel, who had been trying every disconnected number from a list their mother had left behind. [463] He had reached the bottom of it and dialed wrong, gloriously wrong, into Mira's tunnel. [478] He did not recognize her voice, only the silence behind it. [489] Mira understood, then, what her years of distance had been protecting: not herself, but a door she could open only by accident. [511] She drew her thumb across the disc. [518] The note rose, traveled the copper to a kitchen four boroughs away, and arrived where her name still meant something tender. [539] Daniel began to cry the way people cry when they realize a stranger is family. [554] Mira spoke her closing line, and for once it cost her something. [566] I am hanging up so you can pick something up. [576] She gave him the cross street above her, the manhole near the bakery their mother used to love, and asked him to come find the booth himself, in twenty minutes. [606] She hung up. [609] Then she sat down beneath her single bulb, in the tunnel that had taught her so much about other people's beginnings, and waited to learn one of her own. [638]
