<story>Mira arrived at the velvet observatory the night of midsummer, when the dome's plum-colored drapes hung heavy with dew and the stars seemed close enough to bruise. [27] She was a provenance researcher by trade, paid to trace objects back through hands and centuries until their stories matched their price tags. [50] Tonight her client, the Halberd Trust, wanted certainty about a transparent violin made of ice crystal, an instrument that should not exist and yet rested on the lectern beneath the telescope, perfectly unmelting. [83] If she authenticated it, the Trust would buy it and lock it in a vault, and Mira would be paid enough to retire before forty. [108] If she did not, the violin would pass to a wandering luthier named Ilse, whose dream was to propagate its strange music among children's orchestras across the cold provinces. [137] Mira had been hired to weigh a dream against an empire, and she had three hours before the promise lost its glow. [159] She unpacked her tools: loupe, ledger, and the small copper pot she used for divination, because her gift, embarrassing and unverifiable, was reading the future in coffee stains. [187] She had another gift too, less useful: she was accidentally telepathic with plants. [200] The observatory's potted mulberry tree, half-asleep in its brass urn, was already murmuring something about ache, about the slow grief of fruit no one would taste. [226] Mira shushed it gently and lit the burner. [234] She brewed coffee thick as river silt, poured a careful spoon of it onto her saucer, and tilted. [252] The stain crept outward in a shape she had seen once before in a Vienna kitchen, the night her mother told her every life is a perforated destiny: holes punched through a long ribbon, and fate only the music that played when the ribbon passed through the machine. [300] Tonight the holes spelled the violin's history. [307] She saw a glacier carved by a grieving father in 1788, a daughter who had loved music and frozen waiting for spring. [329] She saw the instrument passed from hand to hand, never sold, always given, until Ilse found it in a barn. [349] She saw the Halberd vault: climate control, silence, the violin mute forever behind tempered glass. [364] She saw, too, an alternate ribbon where Ilse carried it north, and small hands drew small songs from it, and the violin did not melt because grief, properly played, keeps its shape. [396] The mulberry tree, eavesdropping, exhaled a thought that tasted purple and bruised. [408] Give it away, it said, in the shy way plants always speak. [420] A thing that sings is not a thing to be owned. [431] Mira pressed her palms against the lectern and considered the empire she could buy with her signature: a quiet house, a garden, a guaranteed old age. [457] She considered the dream she could midwife instead: a luthier's van, a winter of cold concerts, children learning that sorrow has a sound and it is beautiful. [484] The saucer's stain darkened as the coffee cooled, as the ribbon kept turning. [497] Somewhere below, Ilse would be waiting by her battered van, warming her hands on a thermos, believing in Mira without evidence. [518] The promise was still glowing, faintly, the way midsummer twilight refuses to die. [531] Mira wrote the report. [535] She catalogued the glacier, the grieving father, the unbroken chain of gifts. [547] She concluded, in her careful archival hand, that the violin's provenance was impeccable but inalienable: a gifted instrument could not legally be sold, and the Trust's claim dissolved in that technicality like sugar in hot water. [583] It was a small lie, and a large truth. [592] She carried the violin downstairs, cradling it against her coat so it would not crack, and the mulberry tree sighed behind her with something that in a kinder language might have been thanks. [625] Ilse was waiting, as foreseen, the thermos steaming. [633] Mira handed her the violin and did not accept payment. [643] The ache she felt afterward was mulberry-colored, specific, survivable. [652] She had chosen a hole in her ribbon, and through that hole the long night began, at last, to sing. [672]</story>
