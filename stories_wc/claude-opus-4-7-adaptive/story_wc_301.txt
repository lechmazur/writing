Three weeks after stopping the medication, I returned to the Halverson rail station with a notebook, a flashlight, and the accordion bellows my father had left me, patched with silver tape along every seam. [34] Ivy had swallowed the platform signs, roping the iron benches and the waiting-room door in green. [50] For twelve years I had worked as a river chronicler, paid by the county to log floods, source-springs, and the small drownings nobody else wrote down. [76] My job was truth: the exact place a body surfaced, the exact hour a culvert gave way. [93] One of those drownings had been my brother Eli, whom I had told, that August, to take the shortcut across the trestle. [115] The pills had kept the guilt at a polite distance for a decade. [128] Without them, grief rose like groundwater, and I decided to meet it somewhere honest. [142] Halverson station was where Eli and I had once hidden from a thunderstorm, laughing, teaching ourselves to read the railway's old map references scratched into the ticket-office wall. [170] I had come back, daringly methodical, to trace those references against my own survey of the river, to see if any line connected the boy I was to the man I had become. [203] The bellows were for Eli. [208] He had played the accordion badly and with great joy; I kept the bellows because the keyboard had rotted, and breath without keys felt like the shape of what I owed him. [240] Inside the ticket office, I found the coordinates still faintly legible: MP 47.2, siding 3, the spur where the river bent. [261] I transcribed them, cross-checked my county charts, and marked the exact bend where Eli had slipped. [277] It was three hundred yards from where I had told him to cross. [290] Three hundred yards: a small, exonerating distance, and also no distance at all. [303] I wandered toward the station's restroom, needing a wall to lean on. [315] The door hung by one hinge. [321] Inside, decades of travelers had layered their confessions onto the tiles in marker and knife-scratch. [336] I read them the way I read riverbanks: slowly, with respect for sediment. [349] Most were names and curses, but one line, low on the wall in pencil, made me sit down on the cracked floor: "You were not the current, only the leaf that saw him fall." [383] It did not name me, could not have; yet it alluded to the exact architecture of my guilt, as if the station itself had been waiting with an answer. [412] I had always mistrusted such coincidences; a chronicler wants provenance. [422] But through bathroom graffiti wisdom, scrawled by a stranger who had perhaps survived their own bend in the river, I received what no prescription had managed to deliver: not absolution, exactly, but permission to grieve without authoring the death. [461] I took out the bellows. [466] I pulled them open, slow, and the tape along the seams creaked like a throat clearing. [482] I pushed them closed; air sighed out, tuneless, enormous. [491] It was not music. [495] It was the sound of a brother not playing, and for once I did not try to translate it into fault. [516] Outside, evening light fell through the ivy in green stripes like staves of empty music. [531] I opened my notebook and began a new chronicle, one that was not about the river at all. [549] I wrote: Eli, at MP 47.2, the current took you three hundred yards past my voice. [565] I wrote: I have been a bad mapmaker of my own life. [577] I wrote: here is the siding where I will stop blaming the boy who ran back for his brother. [596] The bellows rested against my knee, patched and patient. [605] The station held its breath with me, ivy tightening, rails pointing toward some town I no longer needed to reach. [625] For the first time since the medication, since August, since the trestle, I felt the raw solace of being merely human near a place where something terrible had, simply, happened. [655] I closed the notebook. [659] I walked home along the riverbank I had mapped a thousand times, and for the first stretch, I did not take notes. [681]
