After the factory shut down, Mira began collecting petrichor in earnest. [11] The whistle that had once split the valley fell silent on a Tuesday, and by Wednesday she was kneeling in the gravel lot with corked vials, waiting for rain. [40] Townsfolk called her sensibly eccentric, which she preferred to either word alone. [52] She owned ledgers, she paid taxes, and she also believed certain smells were memories the world had not yet finished thinking. [73] Her shop, three blocks from the shuttered turbine hall, sold tinctures of first-rain, summer-storm, and the green hush after lightning. [93] Customers came less for the perfumes than for what Mira did with them: a quiet psychometry reading, palm to glass, eyes closed, listening to the petrichor speak of the stones it had wet. [126] On the third Friday after closure, an old man brought her a brass compass with a cracked bezel. [144] It belonged, he said, to the Halverson expedition, which had vanished in 1911 beneath the limestone cliffs east of town. [164] His grandfather had been the only survivor and had refused, until his death, to explain what he'd seen. [182] Mira poured a vial of last week's storm onto the lost expedition compass and pressed her thumb against the glass. [202] What rose was not a place but a hinge. [211] She felt a door swing open inside her chest, then another behind it, then another, each one waking a room she had not known was sleeping. [237] Hinged awakenings, her grandmother had called the sensation, and warned her never to follow it past the third door. [256] Mira followed it past the seventh. [262] She told her apprentice to mind the shop, packed the compass, two lanterns, and a notebook, and drove east at dawn. [283] The cliffs leaned over the road like patient witnesses. [292] She had spent her life cataloguing the world's small honest mysteries, and she had grown tired of them; she wanted, just once, to delve toward the place where explanations failed. [322] The compass led her past a collapsed sluice, through a fissure barely wider than her shoulders, and down a switchback of wet stone. [345] After an hour the passage opened, and she stepped into a cathedral of dripping limestone above a subterranean lagoon. [364] The water was glass-flat and luminous, a color she had no word for, lit from beneath by something she chose not to name. [387] A resonant hush filled the chamber, the sound a bell makes the moment before it is struck. [404] She smelled petrichor, though no rain could fall here. [413] She knelt at the lagoon's edge and uncorked an empty vial out of habit, then set it aside; some things refused to be bottled. [437] The compass needle spun once, slowly, then settled pointing straight down into the water. [451] She placed her palm on its glass and gave it the proper reading, the kind she charged strangers nothing for. [471] The compass remembered Halverson's last hour: the men kneeling here as she knelt now, the same hush, the same impossible scent. [492] It remembered them looking into the water and finding their own faces looking back from a great depth, smiling, already at peace, as if they had arrived somewhere long expected. [522] Only the grandfather had turned away. [528] Mira looked into the lagoon. [533] Her own face waited there, calm as still water, and behind it more faces, hinged and opening, each one a room she had not yet learned to enter. [561] She understood, with the clarity granted only at the threshold, why the grandfather had refused to explain. [578] Explanation was the wrong instrument; one might as well measure rainfall with a compass. [592] She did not step into the water. [599] She had come to find the place where explanations failed, and finding it, she discovered she still wanted to come home. [620] She left the compass on the limestone shelf, a small offering to the next pilgrim who needed a needle to follow. [641] Above ground the rain had begun, and Mira knelt in the mud with a fresh vial, collecting what she could still name. [663]
