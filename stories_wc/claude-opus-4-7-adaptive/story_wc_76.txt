Ilse built clocks that ran backward. [6] She called herself a counter-clockwise clockmaker, and the village called her worse. [18] Her shop on the mainland had shuttered the winter the river went bone-dry and the hunger stone rose from the mud, its old Gothic warning biting the air: "If you see me, weep." [51] She had read it, and she had not wept. [60] Instead she had packed her tools, her small neatly inventive hands trembling not with fear but appetite, and she had rowed out to the lighthouse on the rocky island her grandfather had tended before fate, in its tidy bookkeeping, decided he would drown. [103]

She meant to step beyond fate's design. [110]

The lighthouse had been dark for forty years. [118] Its lantern room smelled of kelp and cold brass, and at its center, set into the floor like a dropped coin, lay an object her grandfather's letters had only ever called the Spindle: an ancient yoyo of pale horn, grooved with symbols that wound both inward and out. [166] He had written that it remembered. [172] He had written that it refused. [178]

Ilse worked under the slow exhale of low tide, when the sea drew back from the reef and exposed the black teeth of the island, because that was the only hour the Spindle would turn. [213] She laid out her loupe, her mainsprings, her tweezers in their neat rows, and she began reverse-engineering the puzzle of the ancient yoyo the way one reverse-engineers a heart that has stopped. [245]

It was, she discovered, a clock. [251] Not a clock that counted hours, but a clock that counted choices. [263] Each descent of the horn wheel unspooled a moment already lived; each ascent rewound one not yet spent. [281] She watched her grandfather die in the bob of it, then watched him not die, then watched him die again. [301] She watched the hunger stone sink. [307] She watched it rise. [311] She watched herself refuse to weep. [317]

The pattern, when she mapped it on graph paper in careful counter-clockwise spirals, resembled nothing so much as a lathe cutting the same groove over and over: fate, her grandfather had written, is only a habit the universe has acquired. [357]

Habits could be broken. [361] Clocks could be re-geared. [365]

She worked for eleven tides. [370] She ate the dried fish she had brought, and when the fish ran out she ate nothing, and the hunger stone she had pocketed on the mainland grew warm against her ribs, as if approving. [405] On the twelfth low tide she understood the final trick: the yoyo had to be thrown, not wound. [423] One had to initiate the motion from inside the loop, with one's own thumb looped into one's own thread. [442]

She tied the string around her wrist. [449]

Above her, the unlit lantern began to glow. [457] Not with flame: with latent radiance, the stored light of every keeper who had climbed those stairs, every vigil, every beam cast across black water to ships that had foundered anyway. [488] The glass bloomed pearl-white. [492] The lighthouse, after forty dark years, had remembered it was a lighthouse. [504]

Ilse stood at the gallery rail and laughed, and the laughter was bleak and exhilarated at once, the laugh of someone who has calculated exactly what it costs to stop being a cog. [537] The sea below was flat and exhausted. [544] The hunger stone in her pocket pulsed in time with the unspinning Spindle. [557]

She threw the yoyo outward, over the reef, and let it fall. [569]

It did not reach the end of its string. [578] Somewhere above the rocks it caught, snagged on a moment that had not yet occurred, and the tide, which had been drawing out, hesitated. [602] For a breath the whole Atlantic held still. [610] Ilse felt her grandfather's hand on her shoulder, warm, unwet. [620] She felt the village children she would have taught, the clocks she would have built, the groove of her life cut suddenly across its grain. [645]

Then the yoyo climbed back up the thread, neat as a returning thought, and settled into her palm. [663]

The lantern kept burning. [667] The hunger stone cooled. [671] The tide, after its long pause, began to come in differently. [682]

On the mainland, when the river rose, it rose around a stone no one could read anymore. [699] The words had been polished smooth, as if by a thumb, working patiently counter-clockwise. [713]
