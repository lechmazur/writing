<story>Mira had been called a landscape astrologer only twice in her life, both times by strangers who meant it as a joke. [22] She preferred the term herself, since her work involved reading hills and rivers the way others read birth charts. [41] Tonight, though, there were no hills. [47] The descent module settled onto a cracked dock of the forgotten underwater city research facility, and the sea pressed in on every porthole like a patient animal. [74] Her commission was odd: map the facility's drowned courtyards the way she would map a valley, and find whatever constellation the architects had hidden in its floor plan. [102] They gave her until sunrise. [107] After that, the tides would shift and the airlocks would seal for another forty years. [122] She was not alone. [126] A caretaker waited at the inner hatch, an old man named Osri whose lantern threw shadowseam wonder across the tiled corridors, the kind of wavering light that stitched dark to dark and made each seam feel like a secret. [165] He handed Mira a burnt feather wrapped in oilcloth. [174] "The last astrologer left this," he said. [181] "She claimed it was the key." [187] Mira turned the feather in her palm. [194] Its quill was intact but the vanes were charred, curled like a question. [207] She had come here hoping to learn the rhythm of belonging, though she had not told anyone that, not even herself in words. [230] Landscape astrologers drifted; clients hired them for a week and forgot them for a decade. [245] She wanted a place that would remember her back. [254] Osri led her through flooded atria where fish hung in suspension above mosaic floors. [268] Each tile was a star; each corridor a line between them. [279] She began to see what her predecessor had seen: the facility was not a city but a sky, laid flat and sunk to preserve it from weather. [306] A softly ingenious design, she thought, the kind of idea that does not shout. [320] By the third hour she had walked most of the map. [331] The constellation emerged beneath her boots: a figure bending to cup something tiny in its hands. [347] At the figure's center was an empty socket of stone. [357] Osri watched her kneel. [361] "The feather," he murmured. [365] She set the burnt feather into the socket, quill down. [375] Something beneath the tiles sighed, a hydraulic exhale forty years old. [386] Then the chamber bloomed. [390] Bioluminescent plankton, stirred from dormancy, rose in slow spirals through the water above the glass ceiling, and the whole vaulted room filled with a luminous respite that seemed to pause the ocean's weight. [423] Mira had been hired to captivate a council with her findings, to produce a report dense with figures. [441] But standing here she understood that the facility itself did the captivating; she was only its newest witness. [459] Osri came to stand beside her. [465] He had lived down here longer than he had lived above. [476] "My wife and I watched the first fireflies of summer every year," he said. [490] "We'd hold one breath together, and when we let it go the meadow answered." [504] He smiled at the plankton. [509] "This is the same trick, only slower." [516] They stood together and, without planning it, matched their breathing. [526] A shared exhale spotting the first fireflies of summer, transposed to the deep: the plankton pulsed in answer, brighter where they looked. [548] Mira felt something settle in her ribs, a quiet arithmetic finally solving itself. [561] Belonging, she realized, was not a place that claimed you. [571] It was a rhythm you agreed to share. [579] She stayed until sunrise, charting the sky beneath her feet while the sea above turned from ink to iron to pale gold. [601] When the airlocks groaned their forty-year farewell, she left the burnt feather in its socket. [616] Some maps, she had learned, were meant to be kept by the ground that held them. [632]</story>
