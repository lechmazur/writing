The prep room smelled of formalin and old roses, a deathly vitality that Mira had learned to breathe like mountain air. [21] Her father had been a con artist of the finest grade, and she, in stubborn rebellion, had become the most honest mortician in the city. [46] Tonight the body on her table was Anton Orlov, a journalist who had been chasing political asylum the way other men chase women, and who had died in customs limbo with the paperwork three signatures short. [82] His widow had arrived an hour earlier carrying a vintage ink pot sealed with wax and a daughter's trembling hands. [102] "Inside is his own blood mixed with gall ink," the widow had whispered. [115] "He meant to sign the asylum affidavit himself, so that our children still across the border could be granted passage in his name." [138] But Anton had not lived long enough to dip the pen. [149] Mira stood beside the stainless table, waiting actively, which was the only kind of waiting she trusted. [166] She turned the ink pot in her gloved fingers and let the wax warm toward softness. [182] To breach the seal would be a small crime; to forge a dead man's signature, a larger one; to leave two children stateless, the largest of all. [209] Her father used to say that every con was simply an arithmetic problem: what you owed the world, what the world owed you, and how to collect without being seen. [239] She had hated him for the cleanness of his logic, and had spent her adult life refusing to subtract. [258] Now she was searching for the mathematics of a fair trade, and her honesty was the first variable that would not hold. [280] The prep room did something to her when she lingered too long in its hum. [295] The chill, the slow drip, the bodies patient under sheets -- all of it liquefied rigid thinking, softened the edges of law and loyalty until even she could pour them into a new shape. [328] Mira opened the ink pot. [333] Inside, the fluid was nearly black, with a faint rust sheen where Anton's blood had settled. [349] She thought of the two children across a border they had never chosen, waiting actively as she was, for a name. [370] A fair trade, she reasoned, was not equality of method but equality of consequence. [384] If she signed, she spent one lie; if she refused, two children spent a lifetime. [399] The arithmetic her father had taught her, which she had refused for decades, was finally asking an honest question. [418] She fetched the affidavit from the widow's folder and set it beside Anton's cold right hand. [434] Using a fine sable brush, she inked his fingertip and pressed it where the signature should have been, then guided the pen herself with the patience of a mortician dressing a wound that would never heal. [470] It was not a forgery, exactly; it was a translation. [480] Anton had intended the stroke; she only lent him a posthumous hand. [492] When she had finished, the ink pot was almost empty, and the affidavit bore a signature that would, in a week, cross a checkpoint inside a diplomatic pouch. [520] Mira washed her gloves, then washed them again. [528] Somewhere in the building a clock ticked past a number she did not read. [542] She laid a fresh sheet over Anton and spoke to him the way her father had once spoken to marks: softly, truthfully, for the first time in his life. [571] "You owe me nothing," she said. [577] "I owe your children a border." [583] The prep room exhaled its cold breath, and Mira, waiting actively for dawn, felt the deathly vitality of the place settle in her bones like the answer to a long equation. [614] She was her father's daughter at last, and honest still -- the two no longer a contradiction, only the two clean sides of a fair trade. [639]
