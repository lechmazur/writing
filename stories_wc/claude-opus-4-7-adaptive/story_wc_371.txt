After the birds flew south, Mira returned to the overgrown courtyard. [11] The starlings had wheeled above the chimneys for a week, then thinned, then vanished, leaving the city quieter and somehow lighter. [32] She carried a paper sack with a heel of dark rye inside, the last loaf from Halbrun's, the bakery on Aster Street that had closed without warning two winters ago. [62] She had hoarded it in her freezer, refusing to eat the final slice, because the bread was proof that she had not imagined Halbrun's existence. [87]

She was a noticer of creeping normality. [94] That was how she described herself to no one but the kettle. [106] Other people forgot the bakery, forgot the trolley line that once ran to the river, forgot that the palace had ever been guarded. [129] Mira remembered, and the remembering had started to feel like an illness. [141] She kept a notebook of small disappearances. [148] Each entry was an accusation she could not deliver to any court. [160]

The palace had been empty for thirty years. [168] Ivy had eaten the south wing, and a juniper grew through the throne room floor. [183] In the third courtyard, where the fountain had cracked, Wen had built a tea house out of doors no one had noticed were missing. [207]

Wen was heuristically gentle. [211] He did not pretend to be wise. [218] He simply tested kindnesses the way a cook tastes broth, adjusting until the room softened. [233] He had asked Mira, on her first visit, what she carried in the paper sack, and when she told him, he had not laughed. [257] He had only nodded and made her tea from chamomile he had planted, illegally, in the cracks of the old parade ground. [279]

That was his work, and the work of the others who came to the tea house: guerrilla gardening. [297] By night they pried up cobbles and tucked in seed. [307] By autumn the boulevards bristled with sorrel, mint, and small tomatoes that no city map acknowledged. [323] They were rebuilding what had vanished, not the buildings but the act of tending. [337] Mira had not joined them yet. [343] She only watched and wrote. [348]

Tonight Wen poured her a cup and asked, "Have you decided?" [359] She set the paper sack on the low table between them. [370] The bread inside had gone hard as a relic. [379] "If I eat it," she said, "Halbrun's is gone." [388] "If I don't, I am the only one keeping it alive, and I am tired." [403]

Wen considered the sack. [407] "You think eating it is forgetting." [413] "Isn't it?" [415] "It might be the opposite." [420] He turned his cup so the steam drifted toward her. [430] "You came here because you wanted to find the sacred in the ordinary." [443] "The bread is not sacred because it is preserved." [452] "It is sacred because someone made it for someone to eat." [463]

Mira looked at the courtyard, at the seedlings pushing through the flagstones, at the paper lanterns Wen had hung from a vine. [485] She thought of her notebook, full of small accusations, and how it had begun to feel like a coffin she carried. [506] "I don't know how to forgive the city for forgetting," she said. [518]

"You won't, fully," Wen said. [523] "What you can have is tenuous absolution." [530] "Not the kind that closes the wound." [537] "The kind that lets you put the knife down." [546]

She broke the heel of bread. [552] It cracked drily, and a smell rose, faint but unmistakable, of caraway and the long-ago oven on Aster Street. [571] She offered half to Wen. [576] They ate slowly, and the bread was terrible, and it was also the bakery, briefly, sitting between them in the dark. [597]

When she had swallowed, Mira reached into her coat and uncloaked the small trowel she had been hiding for weeks, the one she had bought meaning to use, never daring. [627] She set it beside her cup. [633] "Where do you need me?" she asked. [640]

Wen smiled. [642] "The east wall." [645] "We're putting in garlic before the first frost." [653] "It will come up green in March, when no one expects it." [665]

Mira nodded. [667] She left the empty paper sack on the table. [676] The starlings would return in spring, and by then there would be something growing for them to puzzle over, something the city had not yet learned to forget. [704]
