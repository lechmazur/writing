<story>Ansel Orvo tended the lamp at the end of the opal dock, where the planks glowed milky through the perpetual mist. [21] Most keepers inherited routines; he had inherited a question. [30] Twelve winters back, at nautical twilight, a stranger had pressed a dried Edelweiss boutonniere into his palm and boarded the last ferry without speaking. [54] Ansel had been trying to decode that single gesture ever since. [65] Visitors called him eccentric because he kept the flower pinned above the logbook, because he muttered to the fog, and because he bought borrowed memories from sailors who passed through Kessel Harbor. [97] The practice was old and half-illegal: for a coin, a sailor would exhale a recollection into a glass vial, and Ansel would inhale it later by the lamp. [125] He did not buy memories of the stranger directly — no one had any — but memories of places the stranger might have been. [147] A climb in the Dolomites. [152] A chapel in Bruges. [156] A hospice kitchen whose windows smelled of lemon balm. [165] He arranged them like cards and read the silences between. [175] His method, he insisted, was to investigate by observing gaps. [185] Real memory, lived from the inside, had texture; borrowed memory was flat, a painted backdrop. [200] But the borrower noticed what the original rememberer never saw — the small refusals, the turn of a head before a name, the blank where a wife should have been standing. [230] In the gaps, the stranger lived. [236] Tonight the mist came in softly persistent, the way grief does when it has decided to stay. [253] Ansel trimmed the wick, set out three vials bought that afternoon from a Norwegian quartermaster, and unpinned the boutonniere from the logbook. [275] The Edelweiss had thinned to near translucency, like something preserved against a promise. [288] He inhaled the first vial. [293] An alpine path, August, a boy running ahead, a woman's laugh cut off by the cough of a faulty engine below the pass. [316] In the gap where the laugh stopped, Ansel felt the Edelweiss pressed into a lapel, not a palm — a boutonniere on a groom. [339] He inhaled the second. [343] A chapel where a wedding had waited, candles burning down, the priest apologizing, rain, rain, the long wrong silence. [362] In the gap between two apologies, Ansel smelled lemon balm. [372] He inhaled the third. [376] A hospice kitchen: a woman near ninety, lucid only about one thing — that she had worn Edelweiss for a man who never came, and kept it for him anyway. [405] The gap was the life between the wedding that failed and the dying that remembered. [420] Ansel set the vials down. [425] The stranger on the dock, twelve winters gone, had not given him a flower. [439] The stranger had been returning one. [445] The boutonniere had belonged to a groom who had turned back on an alpine road, heard an engine fail, climbed down to help, and never arrived at his own wedding. [475] He must have kept the flower, pinned and re-pinned, through years of unbearable arithmetic, until he could no longer carry it, and had chosen the farthest dock he knew — this one — to leave it with a keeper who looked like a man who could hold other people's weather. [523] The final gesture had not been for Ansel. [531] It had been for the woman in the hospice, passed along a chain of strangers the way lamps pass light. [551] Ansel stepped to the rail. [556] The opal planks held his lantern's reflection like a second, gentler lamp. [568] Nautical twilight deepened — that brief interval when both horizon and first star agree to be visible. [584] He understood, now, why the stranger had chosen this hour: it was the hour when a thing could be both carried and released. [607] Ansel unpinned the Edelweiss and laid it on the water. [617] It floated, softly persistent, refusing to sink, the way certain promises do. [629] Somewhere up the coast a hospice window would be opening at dawn; somewhere a woman might wake unsurprised. [647] He watched until the mist took the flower, and then he watched the place where the flower had been. [666] Witnessed isolation, he had learned, was not loneliness. [674] It was the lamp's work: to stand apart, and to see, and to let the gap do the rest of the speaking. [696]</story>
