<story>After first contact, the world did not end; it merely grew quieter, like bronze cooling after the casting. [18] Irem Valade had been a luminous skeptic long before the sky answered, and she remained one afterward, out of habit and principle. [40] The visitors had not arrived in ships but in equations, folding mathematics into the evening news and then withdrawing, leaving humanity with the peculiar chore of replying. [67] Irem's reply, scribbled into a grant proposal, was a question: what is the chemistry of homecoming? [83] Her committee called the project academically rebellious, which meant they funded it reluctantly and hoped it would fail in interesting ways. [104] She designed what she named an artificial pilgrimage: a route engineered to reproduce, step by step, the neurochemistry of returning to a place one loves. [129] If aliens could write us a theorem, she reasoned, we could write them a walk. [144] The path she chose wound up to a rainwashed tower in spring, the old observatory at Mont Cerise, abandoned since the observatories of the sky had become redundant. [172] Pilgrims would converge on the tower from three directions, guided not by signage but via dusty footprints left on an overgrown sculpture path. [195] Irem had seeded those footprints herself, each one a calibrated stride, each sculpture a chemical cue: rosemary for memory, wet stone for childhood, iron oxide for the color of a mother's kitchen at dusk. [229] At the summit, pilgrims would be handed a single object: a droplet of fly amber, forty million years old, holding a gnat inside its honey-colored silence. [255] The amber was the thesis: home is a place one cannot return to without becoming fossilized by the attempt. [274] She expected, as skeptics do, a neat null result. [283] The first pilgrim was a retired cartographer who wept at the second sculpture and turned back, saying the footprints matched her dead husband's gait. [307] Irem logged it: coincidence, average stride length, selection bias. [316] The second was a linguist who climbed without stopping and said the tower smelled exactly like the apartment in Beirut he had fled at nine. [341] Irem logged it: suggestion, priming, the well-known treachery of olfactory memory. [352] But she began, between entries, to climb the path herself, first at dawn, then at dusk, then in the rain that spring hung over the tower like a loose sleeve. [382] The bronze quiet of the sculpture garden pressed against her like a patient hand. [396] She had designed each cue, yet the cues worked on her anyway, as if the mechanism cared nothing for its engineer's credentials. [418] On the ninetieth day she reached the summit with the amber in her palm and understood the experiment had inverted itself. [439] The visitors had sent equations; she had answered with a trail. [450] The trail, however, was answering her. [456] Homecoming, she wrote in the tower's damp logbook, is not a chemistry but a convergence. [471] The body returns; the mind returns; the place returns; and where they meet, briefly, something becomes preserved. [488] She held the fly amber to the wet light and saw, for the first time, that the gnat inside was not trapped but arrived. [512] It had walked into resin the way she had walked into her own artificial pilgrimage: willingly, curiously, and only in retrospect irreversibly. [534] When she descended, the grant committee asked for deliverables, and she gave them a map and a single instruction: walk it. [555] They did, because academically rebellious ideas, once funded, must be walked to disprove. [568] They came back quieter, as she had, carrying droplets of amber they could not quite set down. [585] Somewhere beyond the atmosphere, Irem suspected, the visitors were watching the slow converging of human feet toward a rainwashed tower in spring. [607] She hoped, against her own training, that they understood the reply. [618] A luminous skeptic, after all, is only a believer who has learned to walk the long way home. [636]</story>
