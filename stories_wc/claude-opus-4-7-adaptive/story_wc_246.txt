<story>The observatory hung in a gravitational anomaly three lightyears past the Kuiper fringe, where clocks argued with themselves and starlight arrived pre-bruised. [22] Mira was its only celestial signal interpreter, a post she had accepted after planting the wrong seeds in a garden that was never hers to tend. [48] She did not like to talk about the garden. [57] She preferred the long, static hum of the array, the patient work of threshing meaning from experience, sifting noise until something like a sentence emerged. [82] On her desk sat a dried-up fountain pen with an exotic nib, forked like a heron's tongue, nickel-bright in the lamp. [103] It had belonged to her predecessor, a man who had reportedly walked out an airlock whispering corrections to no one. [123] Mira kept the pen because the weight of it steadied her hand when the signals grew strange, and because she had begun to suspect, in the dim way one suspects weather, that it still wrote. [158] The anomaly did odd things to causality. [165] Letters drafted on Tuesday arrived on Monday; memories unspooled backward across breakfast. [177] When Mira tried to straighten a thought, she could feel it unbend again, like a fern loosening in warm water. [197] Most interpreters were driven mad by this. [204] Mira had learned, instead, to be confidently uncertain, to trust each guess while expecting it to be wrong by morning. [224] Three months into her posting, the array caught a signal she could not classify. [238] It arrived in pulses that overlapped themselves, a chord of tenses. [249] Decoded, it resolved into a directive: Edit the record. [258] Adjust. [259] Her console blinked: AUTHOR UNKNOWN. [264] She thought it was a prank until she tried the pen. [275] The nib, dry for decades, took ink from nowhere and wrote a clean, wet line across her log. [293] The line read: You planted rye where wheat was wanted. [303] Mira felt her stomach drop through several floors. [311] She had never told anyone about the rye. [319] She had not even told herself in plain language. [328] The pen, she understood then, was an editing tool. [337] Not for paper, but for history. [343] Whoever had trained it, or grown it, had calibrated it through contingent relationships: a word changed here reshaped an obligation there, a deleted promise unraveled a marriage two decades downstream. [373] The anomaly made such edits possible; the signal merely requested them. [384] Whoever was on the other end wanted the past pruned. [394] Mira did not write back immediately. [400] She went to the viewing port and watched the anomaly breathe: galaxies bent like reeds, their light pooling and pulling. [420] She thought about the garden, the rows she had sown in grief, the wrong crop rising green and indifferent while her mother thinned. [443] She could edit it. [447] The pen was in her hand. [453] One correction and the rye would become wheat, her mother would eat bread instead of resentment, the garden would feed someone. [474] The thought carried a strange comfort, not because it promised absolution but because it admitted the possibility of a self who had chosen better. [498] She did not make the edit. [504] Instead she wrote, in her steady, forked script: Acknowledged. Declining. [514] She felt the words settle into the log and, she suspected, into other ledgers she would never see. [532] The signal returned the next cycle, softer. [539] It asked why. [542] Mira considered. [544] Her job was to interpret, to thresh, to separate the grain of meaning from the chaff of event. [562] If she edited the chaff away, there would be no threshing left to do; there would be no her, only a smoother woman in a smoother garden, ignorant of what rye cost. [594] She wrote: Because I learned from the wrong seeds. Remove them and you remove the harvest. [610] The pen dried again as she finished, the ink retreating like a tide that had decided, after all, not to come in. [632] The anomaly rippled once and stilled. [638] The signal did not return. [643] Mira set the pen down. [648] She was still confidently uncertain; she might be wrong tomorrow, next year, at her death. [663] But the observatory hummed its patient hum, and the bruised starlight kept arriving, and somewhere downstream a version of her mother was, perhaps, still hungry, and she found she could live with that, as one lives with weather, with gravity, with the unbent shape of a life actually led.
