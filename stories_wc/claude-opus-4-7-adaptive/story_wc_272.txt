Mara climbed the lighthouse because the paintings had stopped speaking back. [10] She was a crestfallen portrait artist whose last commission had refused to resemble its sitter, whose brushes had started weeping pigment onto the wrong faces. [30] The keeper, long dead, had left behind a forgotten library inside a lighthouse, and the rumor in the harbor town was that the books there remembered things people had misplaced. [59]

She brought only one possession: a broken tarot card box, its hinges snapped, its lacquered lid held shut by a length of fisherman's twine. [84] It had belonged to her mother, who used to ask, "Why do we keep asking questions we already answered?" [102] Mara had inherited that habit too; she questioned rhetorical questions as if each one were a latch she could pry. [122]

The library spiraled upward around the lantern shaft, shelves warped by salt, books bloated with weather. [138] Someone had catalogued the volumes by color rather than content, and the effect was a bruised rainbow climbing into shadow. [157] At the top, a single reading desk waited under the extinguished beacon. [169] Mara set the broken box there and began to unpack. [179]

The cards inside were not the ones her mother had owned. [190] Or rather, they were, but each face showed a different version of Mara, painted in her own recognizable strokes. [210] Mara at eleven, holding a dead sparrow. [217] Mara at thirty, alone in a studio that smelled of turpentine and regret. [230] Mara in a dress she had never worn, smiling at someone whose face the pigment refused to fix. [248]

"Is this really what I came here for?" she asked aloud, then corrected herself. [262] Yes. [263] She had come here to descend into necessary darkness, because her work had grown brittle from avoiding it, because every portrait she attempted these months emerged polite and hollow. [293]

That was when the walls closed in. [300]

Not metaphorically: the curved stone of the lighthouse contracted by a visible inch, then another, shelves groaning closer, the spiraled staircase tightening like a throat swallowing. [328] The windows iced over with something that was neither frost nor salt but the suggestion of both, a fragmented reality where the sea outside appeared in three time signatures at once. [358] Mara saw the harbor at dawn, at storm, and at some future dusk where the town was only a field of reeds. [379]

She did not scream. [383] She had, after all, come for exactly this. [391]

The twine around the broken box was the thing she attended to. [403] Her mother's voice, or a voice her mother had loaned her, suggested she work by untangling threads. [422] So she did. [425] She loosened the fisherman's knot with stiff fingers while the walls pressed in, while the cards on the desk began to rearrange themselves into sequences she almost recognized. [454]

Each thread she freed corresponded to something she had refused to look at. [467] The sparrow. [469] The studio. [471] The dress, and the face she could not paint because she had already painted it once and then destroyed the canvas in a fit she still would not name. [501]

As the threads came loose, the cards settled. [510] The Mara on each face grew stiller, less fractured, and Mara understood that her task was not to assemble a single self but to redesign the arrangement entirely. [541] A portrait was not a resemblance; it was a conversation between what the sitter permitted and what the painter could bear to see. [563]

She had been painting only what she could bear. [572]

The walls held their encroachment now, breathing. [579] Mara lifted the loosened twine, wound it around her wrist like a bracelet, and opened the broken box fully. [601] Beneath the cards lay a small mirror, fogged. [609]

She looked, and did not flinch. [615] The woman there was not whole, but she was honest, and her ache glowed faintly, a luminescent ache, the kind a beacon might throw before it chooses to burn again. [645]

Mara began, at the cramped desk, in the tightening tower, with salt on her lips and twine at her pulse, to sketch herself for the first time. [672] She understood she would descend the lighthouse stairs a different painter. [684]

Outside, slowly, the three seas agreed to become one. [694]
