Ansel Voss kept two lives balanced on the nib of a crow-quill pen. [13]

By day he was an urbane calligrapher, lettering wedding vows and museum placards in a copperplate so precise it almost apologized for its own beauty. [38]

By night he carried a battered campfire story stick into the dim bars of the harbor district and performed subversive puppet shows for longshoremen, runaway clerks, and the occasional priest. [68]

The stick had belonged to his grandfather, notched along its length for every tale told around wood smoke. [86]

Ansel had added his own notches, each one a vow to explore ethical storytelling in places that official theaters would never touch. [108]

He believed that a story spoken under a low lamp carried weight a printed one never could, because the listener could still argue with the teller. [134]

Tonight's argument, however, had been louder than most. [142]

His partner Rhea, a documentary sound recordist, had stood in the door of their apartment and accused him of romanticizing the very inequities his puppets pretended to mock. [170]

"You reverse the meaning of every cruelty by making it clever," she said. [183]

"Cleverness is not repair." [187]

She left before he could answer, and in the hush left behind by their heated argument, Ansel felt something crack quietly inside his practiced composure. [212]

He was known, even by enemies, as uncommonly serene. [221]

It was an attribute he had cultivated the way he cultivated his letters: stroke by stroke, refusing any tremor. [240]

But Rhea's accusation had slipped past the guard of style and lodged somewhere truer. [254]

He put the story stick in his satchel and walked, without deciding to, north along the breakwater road. [272]

At the end of that road stood the decommissioned radar dome, a white geodesic hemisphere left over from a war no one wanted to name. [297]

Locals called it the Egg. [302]

Ansel had performed there once, years ago, for a birthday that had ended badly. [316]

Tonight he let himself in through the rusted service hatch and climbed to the platform where the antenna had once turned. [337]

The dome's interior was half-lit by a single work lamp someone had forgotten. [350]

Through triangular panels the sky showed in pieces, and a meteor passed, soundless, drawing its thin chalk across the dark. [370]

He waited for the crack of its passage and heard only his own breathing; the building itself seemed to hold its breath with him, a meteor hush that pressed gently on the ears. [403]

He took out the story stick and laid it across his knees. [415]

In this odd, half-lit geometry his usual routines felt obscene, and he understood why. [429]

His puppet shows did reverse cruelties into punchlines; that was the craft. [441]

But reversal was not the same as understanding, and a joke aimed downward only looked like satire from above. [460]

Rhea had been right, and it was possible to admit this without shame, the way one admits that a letter has been badly kerned and must be inked again. [489]

One by one, he let small half-lit epiphanies arrive. [498]

That the people in his audiences were not material. [507]

That ethical storytelling meant asking permission from the lives he borrowed, not merely lettering them prettily. [523]

That his serenity had been, in part, a way of not feeling the cost of what he made. [541]

He took a pocketknife and cut a fresh notch into the stick, but this time across the grain, a mark of refusal rather than record. [566]

He would retire the old repertoire. [572]

He would build new pieces with the dockworkers and clerks themselves, letting them hold the puppets, letting him only letter the programs. [594]

When he climbed back down, dawn was a pale ink wash at the horizon. [608]

Rhea was awake in the kitchen, coffee cooling between her hands, and she looked up without anger. [625]

"I'm listening," she said. [629]
