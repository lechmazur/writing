Merel had painted a thousand faces and recognized none. [9] She was a portrait artist cursed with face blindness, and she built her likenesses from measurements rather than memory. [28] Her patrons called her fractally precise: every eyelash accounted for, every pore nested inside a grid nested inside a grid. [48]

Tonight she stood inside the tree trunk library, hollowed centuries ago by a scholar's guild whose names she would not have remembered even if she had met them. [76] The guild had packed the oak with spiraling shelves, and the building had finally been declared unsafe. [93] The wrecking crane waited outside, its iron ball dark against the dawn. [105]

There was a hush just before the demolition, the kind of stillness that made breathing feel like trespass. [123] She had one hour. [127] She had come for a rumor: that somewhere in the oak was a secret book safe, shaped like a ledger but hollowed to hold a single coin. [154] The coin, guild legend said, carried faint indentations that, when rubbed to paper, revealed the sky-map the guild had painted on their inner walls before plague closed them. [182]

Her motivation was simple: to mirror the sky inside walls. [192] She had been commissioned by the town to paint the new civic hall, and she wanted the old guild's constellations, not a stranger's invention. [216] She needed the coin. [220]

She found the safe inside a hollow knot at waist height, carved to look like burl. [236] The lock gave under her pick. [242] Inside rested a ledger-shaped box, and inside that, a copper coin older than the library's bark. [258]

She laid rice paper over it and rubbed charcoal until the indentations surfaced. [271] Stars bloomed. [273] Not just stars: entire constellations of fractally precise nested dots, each star a small cluster, each cluster a constellation in itself. [294] She realized, with the calm of a woman who had long since learned not to trust her first impressions, that she could not craft what she was seeing. [322] The map was beyond her hands. [328] She had spent her life trusting grids, and this pattern refused to flatten. [341]

Outside, a whistle blew, five minutes. [347] She breathed. [349] The hush tightened. [352] She thought about the faces she had painted, all those strangers whose features she had assembled like locksmiths assemble wards. [372] She had always believed the limit was her eye, not her hand. [384] Tonight she was discovering the opposite. [390]

She could not copy the coin. [396] But she could carry it. [401]

She folded the rubbing, pocketed the coin, and climbed down the spiral of shelves. [415] On the lowest ring she paused beside a self-portrait one of the dead scholars had left tacked to the wood. [435] She had no idea whose face it was. [443] She never would. [446] But the man had signed it with the same fractal dots that crowned the coin, and for the first time in years she felt she recognized someone, not by his face, but by his pattern. [481]

Outside, the foreman counted down. [486] She stepped into the pale morning and held up the coin. [497] The foreman, who had hired her for the civic hall, lowered his hand. [510] "Ten more minutes?" she asked. [515] He nodded, because he trusted her, because the town trusted her grids. [527]

She went back inside and did what she should have done first: she stopped trying to copy and began to listen. [548] She pressed her palm to the oak and let the hush settle against her ribs. [563] She traced the indentations with a fingernail, feeling rather than seeing, and she understood that the sky on the civic wall would not be the sky on the coin. [592] It would be the sky she had learned to feel inside the hollow trunk during the ten borrowed minutes before the iron ball swung. [616]

She emerged. [618] The crane pivoted. [621] The oak opened like a struck bell, and the hush broke into dust. [634]

Merel walked home through pale streets with a coin warm in her pocket and a plan in her hands. [653] She would craft walls that mirrored a sky she had never quite seen, not the scholars' sky, not hers, but the one that lived between them. [679] She had discovered her limit, and then she had walked past it. [691]
