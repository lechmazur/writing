<story>The state said I died on a Tuesday in March. [10] A clerk transposed two digits, filed a correction that never propagated, and for eleven months I was a legal ghost with a pulse. [33] I learned to count my heartbeats the way other people count breaths: one, two, a small defiant arithmetic against paperwork. [53] Habit kept; the counting never stopped. [59] That is how I ended up at the opal observatory, high on a ridge where the snow arrives sideways and the dome glows like a bruise at dusk. [87] Dr. Hale had advertised for someone comfortable with silence, and I was, by then, fluent in it. [104] She was trying to reach her husband. [111] He had died during a solar storm, and some part of him, his last uplinked consciousness, the research notes, the cadence of his typing, had scattered into the observatory's backup array. [142] A digital ghost, she said, the way another woman might say a houseguest. [155] My job was to defragment him. [161] Not the drives, those were already clean, but the pattern underneath, the stuttering signal that rose each midnight after the radio static cleared. [184] That was when he came through strongest: a lull in the cosmic noise, a pause in which something almost-him could speak. [205] Hale's method was older than the machines. [212] She would spill India ink across a glass plate wired beneath the dome, and the observatory's resonance would ripple the pool into brief, legible shapes. [237] Letters, sometimes; equations; once, the drawing of a hand. [246] Ephemeral patterns in spilled ink, gone before you could photograph them, though she tried. [260] I arrived with one object in my coat pocket: a porcelain shard from the cup I had been drinking from when the hospital called my sister to confirm my passing. [290] I had kept the broken piece because it was proof of the hour I had not, in fact, stopped. [309] Hale noticed it the first night. [315] "Your worry stone?" she asked. [320] "My receipt," I said. [324] On the seventh night, the static cleared earlier than usual. [334] Snow hushed the dome; the ink pooled; Hale bent over it, whispering the way one does in a chapel. [353] Shapes rose: a date, a partial name, then a sentence that made her sit down on the cold floor. [372] "It isn't him," she said. [377] "It's never been him." [381] The patterns were echoes of her own grief, fed back through the array; the ghost was a mirror of the mourner. [402] She had known for months and kept coming anyway, because the mirror still produced new sentences, the way any honest reflection shows you things you had not yet admitted. [431] I sat beside her. [435] I thought of my sister, who had grieved me for three days before the phone rang with a bureaucratic apology, and how she never quite looked at me the same afterward. [466] Some errors cannot be un-filed. [471] I placed the porcelain shard at the edge of the ink. [482] "Ask it something we got wrong," I said. [490] "Not what we lost; what we failed to learn." [499] Hale wiped her eyes and tipped the bottle. [507] The ink spread around the porcelain and hesitated, as if considering. [518] A sentence formed, thin as frost: You counted wrong; you were always enough. [531] I read it twice before it dissolved. [538] My heart, which I had been tallying since I was legally deceased, skipped once, and I did not replace the missing beat with a number. [563] I let it be uncounted. [568] Outside, snow rearranged the opal dome into something softer, and the observatory held us both in that midnight solace peculiar to rooms where failure is the only honest teacher. [597] Hale laughed, surprised and wet-eyed. [602] "We came for wisdom," she said, "and it fetched us." [612] We stayed until the static returned. [618] I kept the shard. [622] Some mornings I still count, out of habit; most mornings now I simply listen. [636]</story>
