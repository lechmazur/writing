<story>After the liquidation, Mara walked the wasteland orchard alone. [9] The Trust had condemned this county years ago, declared its soil unfit, and bulldozed the homes of everyone who refused to leave. [31] Yet the apple trees thrived, fat and red, even as radioactive dust storms scoured the horizon copper. [48] That contradiction was the reason she had come. [56] Mara was a conspiracy debunker by trade, a columnist who made her living puncturing the bright balloons of paranoid theories. [76] She believed, sincerely, in boring explanations. [82] She believed even more sincerely that her job was to excavate truth from comfortable lies, whichever side had poured the concrete. [103] The comfortable lie here was simple: the land was poisoned, the displaced had been paid fairly, and the Trust's orchards elsewhere supplied the market. [127] The uncomfortable observation was that the fruit in the grocery bins matched the fruit at her feet. [144] Sharply observant by habit, she noted the drip lines, the lead-lined nets catching the worst of the fallout, the tidy rows that suggested decades of care, not abandonment. [172] Somebody had been farming here the whole time. [180] In the farmhouse at the orchard's center she found what the evacuation crews had missed: a study, its wastebasket overflowing with the ribbon-cut residue of a shredder. [207] She carried the basket out to the porch and began reassembling shredded letters on the warped pine boards, weighting each strip with pebbles against the wind. [233] It took three days. [237] The dust storms came and went in copper curtains; she wore a filter mask and kept working. [254] Slowly, paragraphs surfaced. [257] The letters were between a Trust accountant and a geologist, and they described a stillpoint at the orchard's heart, a pocket of land where, for reasons no one fully understood, radiation readings dropped to background levels and stayed there, a hidden stillpoint inside the larger ruin. [303] The geologist had wanted to publish. [309] The accountant had wanted to liquidate. [315] Liquidation had been cheaper: evict the residents with a radiation scare, buy the deeds at salvage prices, then keep the quiet little Eden producing for a buyer list of the very rich. [347] Mara read the pages twice, waiting for the loophole that would let her dismiss them. [362] The loophole did not come. [367] Among the reassembled fragments she also found an inventory, and halfway down it, a line that did not match the others: one uncut stone, flawed, held in office safe, provenance disputed. [398] In the safe, behind ledgers, she found the diamond. [407] It was the size of a walnut, cloudy with an internal fracture, and the accountant's notes called it cursed, pried from a mine collapse that had killed nine men, kept by the Trust as a private talisman, never sold because selling it felt like tempting something. [453] Mara, who did not believe in curses, turned the flawed diamond in the dim light and felt a cold clarity she chose to call intuition. [478] She could intuit the whole shape of it now. [487] The curse was not in the stone; it was the convenient story the Trust told itself to justify hoarding. [506] The poisoning was the same kind of story, told outward. [516] Every paranoid theory she had ever debunked had been a cheap substitute for this: the real conspiracies were dull, bookkept, and profitable. [538] She photographed the letters, the ledgers, and the diamond under the kitchen window's brittle light. [553] Then she walked back through the orchard to the stillpoint the geologist had mapped, a circle of grass where the Geiger counter went quiet as a held breath. [581] She sat in it until the sun dropped. [589] There was no magic in that patch of ground, only geology and luck, but she understood why the accountant had kept the diamond and never sold it. [616] People wanted somewhere still to stand, even if they had to build fences of lies around it. [633] Her column, when she filed it, began with the orchard and ended with the safe. [648] She did not mention curses. [653] She named the buyers, the accountant, the displaced families by name. [664] The diamond she left in its velvet, flawed and unsold, a small dull weight at the hidden center of a county that had been sold for parts. [691]</story>
