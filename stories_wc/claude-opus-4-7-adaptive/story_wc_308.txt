Vey was a harmonic stone skimmer, which meant she listened to what the canyon remembered. [15] The towers of bone rose around her in the canyon of endless echoes, femurs and ribs of creatures whose names had been eaten by weather. [40] She came here historically reverent, bowing once at the mouth of the gorge, because her grandmother had done so, and her grandmother's grandmother before her. [65] In her palm lay a stone arrowhead, black glass chipped to a narrow leaf. [79] It was not hers. [83] It had been found, centuries ago, lodged in the sternum of the tallest tower, and passed down through her line as both inheritance and instrument. [108] Through centuries of layered dust, the arrowhead had lost nothing of its edge; it had only gained a hum. [127] A pulse, her mother had called it, left wandering after the hunter who shot it had died on the ridge above. [148] The pulse wanted to go home, but home was gone, so it looked instead for any hand steady enough to carry it. [170] Vey's hand had been steady since she was nine. [179] Today she meant to appraise it properly for the first time. [190] She stepped onto the flat shale at the canyon's center, where skimmers had stood for a thousand generations, and waited. [210] Above, a flock of pale birds rose from one tower and wheeled between two others, and Vey watched. [228] Her craft was not the throwing of stones, though that was its oldest name. [242] Her craft was reading: the shapes a flock made when a pulse passed through the air, the way a wingbeat lagged or led, the pockets that opened in the pattern like letters in a sentence. [277] Through the patterns in the birds in flight, a skimmer could weigh a thing's history as surely as a jeweler weighed gold. [299] She held the arrowhead up. [304] The flock folded. [307] Where they should have banked right around the fourth tower, they hesitated, split, and rejoined beneath the overhang, drawing a long trembling arc that doubled back on itself. [335] Vey read it twice to be sure. [343] The arrowhead's pulse was not merely wandering; it was circling. [353] It had been circling for so long that it had worn a groove in the air itself, and the birds simply fell into that groove when she lifted the stone. [383] This was the weight of it: a grief that had outlived grief, kept moving because nothing had yet taught it to stop. [405] She sat. [407] Her knees found the shale with the old weathered resolve of women who had learned that some questions cannot be hurried. [428] Her grandmother had tried to break the pulse with motion, carrying the arrowhead across three deserts, and failed. [446] Her mother had tried to bury it, and the dust simply sang louder. [459] Vey's task, she had decided on the long walk down, was to learn to navigate without moving. [476] If the pulse had worn a path, she would sit inside the path. [489] She would become the still point the hunter never reached. [499] She watched the flock until her shadow shortened and lengthened again. [510] She did not skim. [514] She did not throw. [518] She let the arrowhead rest in her open hand and matched her breathing to the birds, letting their loop become her loop, until the canyon's echoes, which had been a hundred overlapping voices, thinned to one. [554] It was a man's voice, wordless, apologetic. [561] She answered without speaking, only by staying. [568] The flock straightened. [571] The groove in the air, having at last been met by something that refused to flee it, closed. [589] The arrowhead in her palm went cold, the way embers go cold: quiet, complete, not absent but finished. [607] Vey exhaled. [609] She understood now why the towers of bone stood upright after so long; they had each been met, once, by someone willing to stop for them. [635] She tucked the arrowhead into the pouch at her throat, where it would now only be stone. [652] When she rose, the birds had settled on the high ridges and were preening as if nothing had happened, which, to them, was true. [676] She walked out of the canyon at the same pace she had walked in, carrying a silence she had earned. [696] Behind her, the dust kept layering, patient as ever, and the echoes, at last, had one fewer name to repeat. [716]
