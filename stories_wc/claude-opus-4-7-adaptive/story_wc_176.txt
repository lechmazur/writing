Vesna came north only after forgiving herself, and even then the walk took seven days. [15] The icebound sanctum waited at the end of it, hunched beneath an aurora that breathed green and rose across the roof. [36] She was a lost wax caster by trade, and her hands still remembered the weight of molten bronze even in this cold. [58] In her satchel lay the only thing she had carried with her from the burned quarter: a street organ punched card scrap, brittle, soot-fringed, pocked with holes whose pattern she had learned by heart. [92] Each hole was a note; each run of notes, a name. [103] The organ had belonged to her mother, who had played the names of the dead on market mornings so no one would be wholly forgotten. [128] When the quarter burned, Vesna had been across the river casting a bell she never finished, and her mother and the organ and most of the cards had gone to ash. [159] For years she blamed herself for not being there, for the bell, for surviving. [173] Only when the blame at last loosened did she remember what the keeper of the sanctum had told her long ago. [194] There was a rope inside, centuries old, ceremonial, said to hold transcribed memory in the tension of its braids. [213] Pilgrims brought their small griefs and retied a strand; the rope remembered on their behalf. [228] She meant to use it for something larger. [236] Inside, the sanctum smelled of candle tallow and old stone. [246] The rope hung from a beam in the vaulted ceiling, as thick as a man's wrist and glowing faintly where the aurora found it through the skylight. [273] Vesna laid the punched card scrap on the altar stone and read it one last time with her fingertips. [292] Aino, Dravko, little Mila who had been three. [300] She began the work she had come for: to preserve the sound of forgotten names by reconfiguring the braids of the centuries-old ceremonial rope. [324] Each hole in the card became a pinch, a cross, a turn of fiber. [338] A caster's hands are technically gentle; they must coax without crushing, guide molten weight into mold without bruising the wax pattern beneath. [360] Those same hands now tugged history into new shape, threading the punched cadence of her mother's music into hemp that had been braided before her country had a name. [389] She worked for hours, maybe days; the aurora did not keep time. [401] Sweat ran, froze, ran again, snowmelt ardor, the keeper would have called it, the thaw of grief into purpose. [420] When the last braid settled, something shifted in the air. [430] The rope began to illuminate from within, strand by strand, as if a slow tide of light were rising through its core. [452] And then it sang. [456] Not with notes exactly, but with a thick, warm hum in which she could hear the organ's old timbre, and beneath it, names, Aino, Dravko, Mila, and all the others the scrap had held. [490] The rope had become the instrument now, a transcribed memory that any pilgrim could wake by pressing a palm against its fibers. [512] Vesna pressed her cheek against it and wept, and the rope hummed her mother's voice back into the sanctum. [531] Outside, the aurora shifted color, as if listening. [539] She stayed through the winter, teaching the keeper how to read the braids. [552] Travelers began to arrive, some carrying punched cards of their own, some only a name whispered between their teeth. [571] Vesna showed them where to thread a new strand, how to tie it so the rope would remember without forgetting what had come before. [595] She did not go back to casting bells. [603] The bronze had been her grief; the rope was her gentler craft now, the shape her life took after she had finally laid the burned quarter down. [630] On clear nights, under aurora, the sanctum sang quietly to itself, and no one within a hundred miles went fully nameless into the dark. [654]
