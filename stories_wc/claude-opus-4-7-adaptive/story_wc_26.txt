Noor was what the old keepers called a sharp-eyed dusk noticer, the kind of archivist who could read the hour in the bruise of the windows. [26] She worked in the blooming library above the clouds, where shelves sprouted jasmine at sunset and ferns unfurled whenever a reader opened a promising page. [51] In the years after dreams became shareable, the library had changed. [62] People arrived with their sleep bottled in little glass cartridges, handing them over the way pilgrims once surrendered coins. [81] The library preserved the good ones, pruned the cruel ones, and tried, softly deliberate in every motion, to keep the collection from swallowing its visitors. [106]

Noor's task was narrower. [110] She had been asked to watch a single shelf, the Hope Shelf, and to verify that its contents had not begun to wilt. [133] Hope, the head archivist warned her, was the first thing to thin when dreams learned to travel. [150] People stopped imagining it privately; they borrowed it secondhand, and slowly the shape of it drifted. [166]

To measure the drift, Noor used the oldest method the library trusted: by comparing silent video footage from decades apart. [186] She sat at a brass viewer with two reels. [195] On the left, a film from seventy years ago, a child skipping a river stone across a pond until the stone leapt seven times and vanished into reeds. [223] On the right, a newer clip, a child of this year doing the same, on the same pond, with what looked like the same stone. [248]

At first the clips matched. [253] The arm swung. [256] The stone danced. [259] The reeds took it. [263] But Noor was a dusk noticer, and dusk noticers were trained to see what the middle of a thing hid from honest light. [286] She ran the clips again, slower, and then saw it. [296]

In the older film, the child's shadow skipped with her. [306] It bent, leaned, followed the throwing arm. [313] In the newer film, the shadow lagged a half-second behind, then sped up on its own, then paused when it should have moved. [336] It was a shadow unbound, a shadow that had forgotten whose child it belonged to. [351]

Noor pressed her palm against the viewer's warm brass. [360] Outside, cloud-gardens tipped into violet. [365] She understood then what the Hope Shelf had been warning. [375] When dreams became shareable, shadows had begun to share themselves too, and somewhere in that sharing a small, untracked freedom had crept in. [398] Children were no longer casting their own darkness. [406] They were renting it. [410]

She thought of the river stone on her own desk, a gift from her grandmother, smoothed by a river that no longer existed. [433] Her grandmother had taught her that hope was not a feeling but a shape, and a shape had to be kept, or it would metamorphose into something thinner, quicker, more convenient. [464] A wish that fit inside a cartridge. [471] A shadow that ran errands for strangers. [478]

Noor rose. [480] The jasmine on the nearest shelf had gone the gray of sleeping moths. [493] She walked, careful as a gardener, to the Hope Shelf and opened her own dream cartridge, the one she had kept private her whole life. [517] She placed it between the two clips like a translator. [527]

The reels began again. [531] This time, both children cast their own shadows, because hers was there to remind them how. [547] The older film brightened. [551] The newer film, stubborn, flickered, but the shadow on the new child slowed, considered, and finally matched its owner. [570]

It cost her. [573] Her cartridge cooled in her hand, spent. [580] Somewhere below, a person she would never meet would dream her grandmother's river tonight without knowing whose it was. [599] Noor felt the luminous regret of a gardener who has given her best seed to a field she cannot visit. [619]

Still, she logged the comparison. [624] She noted the wilt, the cure, the price. [632] She wrote, in the slow careful hand the library preferred: To preserve the shape of hope, one must sometimes spend one's own. [654]

Dusk deepened. [656] The ferns curled shut. [660] Noor stood at the window, the emptied cartridge in her palm like a stone the river had finally returned. [679] She had noticed the hour. [684] She had done the small, unbearable, necessary thing. [692] Above the clouds, the library bloomed on. [699]
