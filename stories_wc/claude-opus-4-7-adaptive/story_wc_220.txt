After the last resource war, the scrapyard settlement of Hollow Hum sprawled across the cracked wasteland like a rusted constellation fallen flat. [22] Old Bram, the bloodhound retired from finding bodies, slept beneath a tarp stitched from billboard scraps. [38] His handler had died years back, but the dog still twitched in dreams, nose chasing absences that no longer needed naming. [59] Children called him Grandfather; adults called him Lucky; the wind, which here moved with a wild hush, called him nothing at all. [81] I came to Hollow Hum carrying one thing my mother had pressed into my palm: an autumn leaf preserved in wax, amber-bright as a held breath. [107] She had told me, before her lungs gave out, that distance could be translated into closeness if a person knew the proper grammar. [130] I did not, then, know the grammar. [137] What I had instead was a riddle she'd whispered, four lines I could not parse: "Where the needle hums in iron furrows, where the wax remembers what the air forgot, look for the houndless hound; he will read what cannot be spoken." [179] In the settlement's central pit, where salvagers traded copper and prayers, an old archivist named Yune kept a phonograph and a crate of cylinders. [203] He was intuitively descriptive, the kind of man who could glance at a stranger and tell you the weather of their childhood. [225] When I showed him the waxen leaf and recited my mother's riddle, he closed his eyes a long time. [244] "Your mother," he said, "encoded letters by reading secret texts in the grooves of a phonograph cylinder. [261] Not the music, child; the silences between the music." [270] He explained that during the war, lovers and dissidents had hidden messages in the microscopic shudders between recorded notes; only certain hands, and certain hounds, could feel them. [298] That was when I understood why the riddle pointed to Bram. [309] Yune led me to the dog's tarp at dusk. [318] Bram lifted his graying muzzle, and for a moment his nostrils worked with the old precision, sorting me from the wind. [339] "He used to find the dead," Yune murmured. [347] "Now he finds what the dead left behind." [355] I pressed the wax leaf beneath his nose. [363] He inhaled, shuddered, and walked, slow as memory, toward a shelf of cylinders stacked between two gutted refrigerators. [381] With one paw he nudged a single black tube from its sleeve. [393] Yune lifted it reverently, set it on the phonograph, and dropped the needle. [406] What came out was a waltz, tinny and half-eaten by time. [417] But Yune was not listening to the waltz. [425] He was watching the grooves, running a magnifier across their rings, and translating. [438] "Tinted revelations," he whispered, because that was what her letters had always been: truths colored by the tremor of her voice, by the season she'd spoken them in, by how far away she was when she said them. [476] He read aloud, and my mother's voice came back to me through his mouth, patient as a lamp. [494] She described the kitchen of our old house before the bombs. [505] She described the maple whose last red leaf she had dipped in candle-wax on the morning the sirens first rose. [525] She described how she had carried that leaf through three refugee camps, afraid it would shatter, because she needed something small enough to hand to me when she could no longer hold my hand. [559] "To translate distance into closeness," Yune read, "press the leaf to a nose that remembers grief, and play the song we danced to." [582] Bram lay down against my boot. [588] The waltz cycled again, and the wasteland outside held its wild hush, and I felt my mother's hand in mine, warm, continuous, arriving at last across the cracked miles. [617] I stayed in Hollow Hum. [622] Bram chose me, or the leaf did, and each evening we played another cylinder, and each evening the dog found another buried sentence, and each evening a little more of the world came home. [656]
