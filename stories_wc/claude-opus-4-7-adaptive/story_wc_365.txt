<story>Ves worked as a sensory map maker, charting the textures of places most people never noticed. [16] Her latest commission had brought her to the abandoned spaceport repair bay on Heloa-IV, where rust and silence braided themselves into something almost holy. [40] She came to record impressions, but she had also come to discover the weight of a withheld truth. [58] Her brother Kell had died in this bay twelve years ago, and nobody had ever told her why. [76] The bay smelled of ozone and old coolant. [84] Ves drew her sensory stylus across the air, and the glyphs it left behind trembled like wet ink. [102] That was when she saw the green glass shard lodged between two deck plates, catching a light that had no local source. [124] She pried it loose, and the bay blinked. [132] In the murmur of half-remembered dreams, the walls softened and showed her Kell, eighteen and alive, arguing with a foreman about a cargo manifest. [156] The shard, she realized, was a key to weaving between worlds. [167] Cartographers whispered about such relics, cyclically spontaneous objects that opened and closed access through parallel universes with no regard for permission or clock. [190] She had minutes, perhaps, before it sealed again. [198] Ves stepped into the shimmering seam and found herself inside a Kell-world where the accident had not yet happened. [217] Her brother looked up, confused by her older face, her stylus glowing with maps of places he had never visited. [237] "You were never supposed to be here," he said, and she understood at once that he knew what was coming. [257] The withheld truth clicked into place like a bolt. [266] Kell had chosen his death. [271] He had known about the failing coolant lines, had known that if he did not redirect the pressure surge into himself, the entire labor shift of forty-three workers would vanish in a plasma bloom. [305] Their parents had known, and they had hidden it from her, believing a martyr would weigh more on a child than an accident would. [329] She wanted to warn him, to pull him out of the bay, to rewrite every deck plate in this universe. [349] "Map me instead," Kell said. [354] "Not the ending. [357] Map the part that was mine to give." [365] Her stylus shook. [368] She drew him: his steady hands, the smell of machine oil on his sleeve, the argument he loved about which sun was warmer. [391] The shard pulsed, cyclically spontaneous as rumored, and began to close its seam. [404] Ves stepped back into the repair bay just as the green glass shard dimmed to ordinary slag. [421] She sat down on the cold floor and let the new weight settle across her ribs. [437] A withheld truth, she understood now, weighed exactly as much as one chose to carry of it. [454] Her parents had carried its full mass; she would carry something lighter and truer. [468] She finished the sensory map of the bay before dawn. [478] She marked the corrosion, the acoustic hush, the faint oil-ghost where Kell had knelt. [492] She marked, too, an annotation only another cartographer would read: here, the world once allowed itself to be rewoven. [511] Walking to her ship, Ves thought about how grief had always arrived in her like weather, a storm she could predict only after it had already wet her through. [540] Now she had a new map to guide her between one grief and the next. [555] She tucked the dead shard into her pocket, a souvenir she would never again need. [570] Above the bay's broken roof, two moons were rising, and she wondered, not for the first time, which of them her brother had meant as warmer. [596] She mapped the question, smiled, and did not answer it. [606]</story>
