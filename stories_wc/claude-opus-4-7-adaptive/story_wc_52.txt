Mira set the kettle on the burner and climbed the ladder to her loft. [14] Rain beaded on the skylight overhead, each drop a small lens holding the gray afternoon. [29] She had eight minutes, give or take, while the kettle boiled. [40] Eight minutes was enough to begin a face. [48] On the workbench waited an ash wood carving blank, pale and rectangular as a slab of butter. [65] Mira chose the gouge her husband Tomas had used for his cabinet faces, back when his hands still belonged to wood instead of whips. [89] He had quit carpentry the year the circus came through Linz and the lions had whispered louder than the maple. [109] Twenty-two years he had stood inside their breath; on the twenty-third, a tired lioness named Ester forgot the rules. [128] The lion tamer's widow was what the neighbors called Mira now, as if grief had married her instead of Tomas. [148] She did not mind the title; she only minded that nobody asked what she did with it. [165] What she did was carve. [170] Not flowers, not saints, not the whittled ducks her father had sold at fairs. [184] Mira carved moments. [187] Each blank became a vessel meant to contain one specific instant from her marriage, a way to harvest lessons from loss before loss harvested everything else. [213] The shelf above her cot already held nineteen finished pieces, each smaller than a pear, each holding a Tomas she could no longer trust her memory to keep. [241] Tomas tying his bootlaces. [245] Tomas burning toast. [248] Tomas asleep mid-sentence. [251] Each was hauntingly practical: not the grand circus moments she might have romanticized, but the small ones she could still verify against her own hands. [276] Today she wanted to catch the way he had once stopped on the stairs to listen to a sparrow, here and gone in three seconds, twenty years ago. [304] She had not thought of it since. [311] It surfaced this morning while she was rinsing a cup, the way old reels rise through crystallized time when the surface above finally stills. [335] Mira pressed the gouge into the ash. [342] The wood gave like patient skin. [348] She did not need the whole man, only the angle of his shoulder, the cock of his ear, the lifted heel of someone deciding to remain still. [375] Above her, rain ticked against the skylight in a rhythm that matched the kettle's rising hush. [391] Outside, the city was unraveling its evening; inside, a rust-hued peace settled over the loft like a blanket pulled by someone who knew her. [415] She thought, not for the first time, that grief was a strange apprenticeship. [428] It taught you that nothing could be re-entered, only described. [438] And description, if done with enough attention, became a kind of keeping. [450] The lions had taught Tomas the same lesson, in their way; you could not own the moment, you could only stand inside it without flinching. [475] He had flinched once, and that had been enough. [484] Mira did not flinch now. [489] The shoulder emerged. [492] The ear. [494] The suspended heel. [497] By the time the kettle began its first thin whistle, the figure was rough but recognizable, a husband caught listening, a sparrow implied by the tilt of attention. [525] She set the gouge down and climbed back to the kitchen. [536] Tea steeped. [538] Rain continued. [540] Twenty pieces now, she thought, and then she said it aloud to the empty room, because saying things aloud had become a small ceremony of staying alive. [567] The lion tamer's widow drank her tea beneath the rain-beaded skylight of the loft she had made her own. [586] Tomorrow there would be another blank, another minute pulled from the long river he had left her standing beside. [605] For now, she had what she had: a shelf of small witnesses, and the strange, useful knowledge that love, properly carved, could outlast the hand that shaped it. [633]
