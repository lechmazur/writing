Mira climbed the stargazer's open tower on the night the whales began to pass. [14] She had never believed in the old woman's claims, but she had loved her, and that was a different kind of faith. [36] Her grandmother, Lio, had called herself a keeper of entangled memories — recollections shared across minds, she said, like threads knotted into one cloth. [59] Mira, a sympathetic skeptic, had always nodded politely and marked the claims as poetry, not physics. [75] Still, here she was, because Lio's last request had been specific. [86] Climb during whale migration, she had whispered, when the songs below tune the stone above. [101] Bring the tile. [104] The tile lay heavy in Mira's satchel: a magic square tile Lio had pressed into her palm at the hospice, its nine engraved numbers summing identically down every row, column, and diagonal. [136] Lio had called it a doorway, though Mira had called it a souvenir. [149] The tower, roofless and circular, gathered starlight like a bowl. [159] Below the cliff, the sea exhaled long, low notes; somewhere under the swell, humpbacks traced their ancestral route, trumpeting a music Mira could feel in her sternum but not quite hear. [190] An unheard symphony, Lio used to call it — the vibrations beneath knowing. [202] Mira set the tile on the tower's central plinth, where a spiral of older tiles waited, their patterns pitted and half-erased by weather. [225] She had been asked to do one thing: transcribe what she heard. [237] A blank vellum lay rolled beside her, pen ready, though she doubted she would fill a single line. [255] Still, she uncapped the ink. [260] When the first whale cry reached the tower, the magic square began to warm. [274] At first she assumed body heat, or the flush of foolishness. [285] Then the numbers on the tile dimmed and rekindled in sequence, and Mira understood she was looking at a rhythm — a key being offered through forgotten patterns her grandmother had studied for sixty years. [319] Mira pressed her palm against the central square and felt the tower open inside her. [334] Memories that were not hers arrived as if they had always been: Lio at seven, learning the sums; Lio at twenty, transcribing songs nobody else admitted hearing; Lio at sixty, losing a daughter — Mira's mother — and folding that grief into arithmetic so it would keep a shape. [381] Mira gasped, because she recognized the grief; it had been in her too, unlabeled, a pressure she had mistaken for weather. [402] Their memories were entangled, and always had been, whether or not she had chosen to believe it. [419] The whales sang on. [423] Mira began to write. [427] She transcribed the tile's flickering order first — nine numbers, then their sums, then the intervals between them that matched the whale pulses note for note. [452] She wrote as fast as the light permitted, no longer worrying whether the page made sense; it was enough that her hand kept up, and that Lio, somehow, was present in the motion. [485] She understood, finally, what the tower was for. [493] It was not a telescope for stars, nor a shrine for relics. [505] It had been built, stone by stone, to sculpt space for reflection — a quiet geometry where a mind could listen without interruption to everything that had ever cared for it. [535] Lio had been courageously kind: she had held her losses in ordered squares so that, one night during a whale migration, her granddaughter might inherit them gently, rather than find them by accident. [568] Mira sat back as the last song faded. [576] Her cheeks were wet; her vellum was full. [584] She rolled it carefully and slipped the tile back into her satchel, knowing she would return next migration, and the one after that. [607] Belief had not arrived like a conversion — it had arrived like a tide, slowly, and without argument. [624] She descended the tower as the dawn unfolded, still skeptical of many things, but no longer of love's arithmetic, or of the songs only some of us are fortunate enough to almost hear. [657]
