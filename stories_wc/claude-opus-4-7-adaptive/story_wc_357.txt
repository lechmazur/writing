Wren Calloway flew the last working skiff on Pomara, an orchard planet riddled with floating rock arches that drifted like sleeping whales above the cider seas. [26] She was the colony's intrepid orchard pilot, the one who threaded harvest barges through stone needles when no one else dared. [47] For three seasons the trees had been dying, blight crawling root to crown, and the council had finally agreed to send her after the rumor. [72] Somewhere beyond the Hollow Arches grew an orchard that refused to die, and her mission was to find the source of resilience before the last cultivar collapsed. [99] The catch was timing. [103] Pomara's twin moons conspired once each cycle to invert gravity for an hour, and only during that strange suspension could a pilot ride the upward rivers that braided through the arches. [134] Wren launched at first chime, navigating by water flow as the seas peeled themselves off the planet and climbed. [153] Currents that had pooled all month in hidden cups now poured skyward, threading the arches like silver yarn through stone needles. [174] She read each ribbon, throttle low, letting the rising water decide her course. [187] Above her, apple boughs unfastened from their hills and floated upward in slow bouquets, fruit and pilot drifting in the same suspended hush. [210] It was a whirling silence, the kind that pressed against her ears even as the world spun gently around her. [230] Forty minutes in, the water led her under an arch she had never charted, where pale roots dangled like the hair of drowned giants. [254] Beyond it lay a hidden grove cupped inside a hollow stone, and at its center stood a single black tree heavy with green fruit. [278] Wren cut the engine and let the skiff drift to its trunk. [290] There she was meant to discover what kept this one orchard alive while the rest of Pomara wilted. [308] What she found instead was a broken arrow tip lodged in the bark, its iron blackened by sap that glowed faintly in the upside-down light. [333] The wood around the wound bristled with new shoots, and she realized the metal itself accelerates plant growth, drawing minerals from the rock and weaving them into living tissue. [362] The tree had been wounded, and the wounding had taught it to thrive. [376] She pried the fragment loose with her glove and felt the air shift. [389] From the surrounding bark rose voices, layered and overlapping - a gathering of echoes left by every hand that had ever touched the tree. [412] She heard the first colonists, the hunter who had loosed the arrow centuries ago, and the slow chorus of pollinators that had passed through since. [437] Each echo carried a memory of injury survived, and the tree had remembered all of them as a single instruction: bend, knit, continue. [460] Resilience was not a chemical here; it was a conversation. [470] Wren's hour was thinning. [474] She wrapped the arrow tip in linen, took three cuttings from the lowest bough, and slid them into her sap-jar before the gravity could right itself and crush her against the cavern roof. [507] She fired the skiff back along the same silver braid, water now hesitating in its bed, ready to fall. [526] She rode the last drop down with the seas, the arches sighing back into place above her like closing hands. [546] At the colony she planted the cuttings beside the dying orchards and pressed the broken arrow tip into the soil between them. [568] By the next inversion, new shoots were rising, and the wind through the boughs carried a low chorus that the elders said sounded almost like memory teaching itself how to begin again. [600] Wren listened, hands still smelling of iron and sap, and smiled. [611]
