The orchard planet Oren was riddled with floating rock arches, and beneath each one grew a different kind of fruit. [21] Settlers called the arches "the hanging stones," and pretended not to wonder why they never fell. [36] We, the newest pickers, had arrived from seven scattered colonies, brought here to harvest whatever the blight had left. [53]

The blight was winning. [57] Bark split; leaves curled; cider moths laid their ashen eggs in the hearts of the last good pears. [75] We worked in separate rows and slept in separate tents, each of us nursing private griefs we did not yet trust strangers to hold. [97]

Then the chalk began appearing. [102]

At dawn, someone had written across the deserted stone sidewalks that wound between the orchards: REEXAMINE THE NORTHERN ARCH. [122] THE ROOTS THERE ARE STILL CLEAN. [128] The handwriting was careful, lettered by a patient hand, signed only with a small drawing of a bell. [146]

Marsa, our foreman, scowled, but she walked north anyway. [155] She came back with three baskets of unblemished quince. [163]

The next morning, new messages: BOIL THE MOTH EGGS IN SALT WATER. [175] SING WHILE YOU SHAKE THE PLUM TREES; THEY LIKE IT. [185] USE THE BUTTERFLY GARDEN WATERING BELL ON THE APRICOTS, NOT A HOSE. [198]

None of us had mentioned owning such a bell. [207] Yet, digging through the dead cottage at the orchard's heart, Ilo found it: a copper watering bell shaped like a swallowtail, its handle green with age, meant for misting delicate wings and thirsty seedlings alike. [241] When he tipped it over the apricots, a fine rain fell that smelled faintly of lavender, and by evening the blight on those trees had receded a finger's width. [267]

We started to gather at dawn to read the chalk together. [277] The messages were strangely trustworthy; every instruction, no matter how odd, worked. [289] Braid the cider moth wings into the netting. [296] Leave cups of cream at the threshold of each arch. [305] Plant the bruised fruit, not the sound. [311]

"Someone is helping us," said Quill, the youngest of us, a boy who had not spoken in the three weeks since his ship had dropped him here. [337] "Someone who wants us to live." [343]

Marsa insisted on watching the sidewalks through the night. [352] She posted herself with a lantern and waited. [359] In the morning she returned, wordless, and only said, "I saw a woman made of faint light. [376] She wrote, and smiled at me, and walked through the northern arch. [388] She was not afraid of me, and I think I was not afraid of her." [402]

The old cottage had a name plate: MIRA HOLLOWFAST, ORCHARDIST. [412] Her diary, tucked behind the stove, described a woman who had spent her life trying to orchestrate collective effort among the quarrelsome settlers of Oren, and who had died of the first wave of blight with her work unfinished. [450] The final entry read: If I can linger, I will. [460] There is still so much to teach. [467]

So: an altruistic ghost, tending us through chalk. [475]

We changed, after that. [479] We ate at one long table under the plum arch. [488] We shared the cream we had been hoarding. [496] Quill began to speak, first to the bell, then to the rest of us. [510] Ilo taught Marsa the lullaby his mother had used on fruit trees back home, and Marsa, tough as pit-stone, wept without hiding it. [533]

After strangers become family, the work gets lighter even when the work gets harder. [547] The blight kept coming; we kept learning; and some nights, exhausted, we lay in the grass beneath the hanging stones and watched the sky. [571] Above Oren hung what the old charts called a nebula of respite, a slow violet cloud that the settlers believed soothed the dying. [592] We thought, perhaps, it had soothed Mira enough to keep her here, bramble-patient, brambled with hope, waiting for hands she could guide. [614]

On the last dawn of harvest, the chalk said only: THANK YOU. [626] LEAVE THE BELL FOR WHOEVER COMES NEXT. [633]

We did. [635] We hung the butterfly garden watering bell from the northern arch, where the wind could sound it, and we boarded our ships carrying baskets of fruit and a grief that had learned, at last, how to become a family. [673] Behind us, faint in the rising light, a woman waved, then turned to welcome the next lost strangers walking up the lane. [694]
