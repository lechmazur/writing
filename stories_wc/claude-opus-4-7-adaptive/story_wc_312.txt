Ollen was a lukewarm mage. [5] His fire spells warmed soup but not stew; his wards kept out drizzle but not rain. [21] Still, he had taken the oath, and he meant to keep it, even if his devotion was the brittle kind that chipped when examined too closely. [47]

The oath said: when the last fish is caught, a mage shall rebuild the balance. [62]

For centuries that line had seemed ornamental. [69] Then the trawlers came, and the reefs went gray, and one gull-colored morning a fisherman named Merra hauled up a single silver anchovy and wept, because the nets behind hers came up empty, and the nets behind those, and the ones after, forever. [112]

The sea, having given its last, began to tilt. [121]

Ollen felt it in his tea first: the cup leaning a hair, water pooling on one side. [138] Then the stars slid. [142] A cosmic imbalance, the archmages agreed from their towers, but none of them stirred. [156] Correcting the sky was apprentice work, they said, beneath a ranked mage. [168] So it fell to Ollen, who was earnestly naive enough to believe a lukewarm spell could hold up a world. [188]

He went to the sapphire viaduct at dawn because the old songs placed the fulcrum there -- the arched aqueduct of blue stone that carried meltwater over the gulf, built by engineers who had known, as Ollen did not yet, that everything important in the world was a question of dripping rates. [239]

He carried the broken ship's bell in both arms. [248] It was his grandfather's, cracked down the lip where a gale had thrown it against a mast. [265] It no longer rang; it only thudded. [272] But the songs said: the bell that cannot ring is the bell that can be tuned. [288] He did not quite understand this. [294] He trusted it anyway. [298]

Dawn came pink, then blue, matching the stones. [306] The viaduct dripped. [309] Water slipped from a dozen hairline cracks in steady percussion, some faster, some slower -- a kind of music, if one listened with the ears of a mathematician. [336] Ollen sat cross-legged and listened. [341]

The secret, he realized after an hour, was that the viaduct had been designed to leak. [357] Every drip was a counterweight against some other drip. [366] The engineers had hung the sky on water. [374] And now, with the ocean emptied, the rates were wrong. [384] Too much water rushed down one channel; too little climbed another. [395] The world tilted because the plumbing had failed. [403]

He placed the broken bell beneath the fastest drip. [412] Water struck the cracked bronze and did not ring -- only thudded, softly, at a pace Ollen began to count. [431] Thud. [432] Thud. [433] Thud. [434] He compared it to the drip from the next crack, and the next. [447] He began, with a trembling finger, to plug the faster ones with wax from his satchel, and to widen the slower ones with a whisper of lukewarm heat. [475]

Tune the drips. [478] Tune the world. [481]

It took until the sun cleared the mountains. [489] His devotion wobbled several times -- what if the songs were wrong, what if he was wrong, what if the archmages laughed -- but brittle devotion, he learned, could be repaired in place, the way a cracked bell could be retuned rather than recast. [531] You did not need to believe perfectly. [538] You only needed to keep counting drips. [545]

At the last adjustment, the broken bell caught a drop at exactly the right interval, and the thud it made was, impossibly, a note. [569] A low, true note. [573] The viaduct hummed along. [577] The stars, somewhere above the blue, slid back into their grooves. [588] Ollen's teacup, far away in his cottage, sat level again. [598]

Merra, out on her dock, did not catch a fish -- there were none left to catch, and would not be for a generation -- but she felt the horizon settle, and she understood that someone, somewhere, had remembered the old line of the oath. [641]

Ollen walked home along the viaduct with the broken bell under his arm, lukewarm and tired and absurdly pleased. [660] He had not become a great mage. [667] He had only rebuilt a small, necessary thing by learning, at last, that the world was held together by attention to the rate at which water falls. [694]
