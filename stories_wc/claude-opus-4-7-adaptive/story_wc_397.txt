<story>Mira came to the drift-ice marina as a euthanasia tourist. [10] She had booked the clinic, paid the deposit, and written the letters. [22] Then she woke up changed. [27] Not cured, not saved, only altered, as if someone had slipped a fold in her patience and smoothed it shut. [47] The appointment was in three weeks. [53] She decided to spend those weeks learning the sound of approaching endings. [65] The marina sat at the edge of a town the glaciers had begun to reclaim. [80] Every morning the drift-ice ground against the pilings with a noise like a slow, polite cough. [96] Mira, who had always gripped reality loosely, found the sound kinder than any hospital had been. [112] She signed on with a disaster relief crew that had overwintered there after the coastal flood. [128] It was not penance; she had nothing to atone for but her own leaving. [142] She told the coordinator only that she wanted steady work and a warm room. [156] They gave her both, and a pair of gloves too large for her hands. [170] Her assignment was to trawl the marina flats each dawn for debris. [182] She dragged a weighted net through the shallow lanes between floes and pulled up whatever the storms had scattered. [201] Photographs dissolved to pulp, a child's boot, a wedding ring wrapped around a stick. [215] Each object was someone's ending, small and particular. [223] She listened for them the way her grandmother had listened for weather. [235] In her pocket, wrapped in tissue, she kept the lace parasol tip. [247] It had capped an heirloom parasol her grandmother carried through two wars and one long hospice summer. [264] The parasol itself had burned in a house fire; only the brass tip survived, scorched to honey. [281] Mira rubbed it between her fingers when the cold climbed her wrists. [293] On the eleventh morning she hauled in something that sang. [303] A cracked buoy bell, bronze, its clapper half gone. [312] She set it on the dock and struck it with the parasol tip. [325] The note it gave was not loud. [332] It was the sound she had come north to find: a tone that did not argue with its own ending, only leaned into it like a sail into wind. [361] She sat down on the frozen planks and wept without sorrow. [372] The fold in her patience opened a little, enough to let something breathe. [385] The relief crew came to know her strangeness and protected it. [396] The foreman, a widower from the flooded lowlands, brought her coffee and asked nothing. [410] A boy who had lost his mother to the tide began walking the flats beside her. [426] He did not speak, but he pointed when the net caught on something gentle. [440] She found that she was teaching him, without words, to trawl for endings the way she did. [457] Not to fear them, but to hear them coming like bells in fog. [470] The week before her appointment, she wrote to the clinic. [480] She did not cancel; she postponed. [486] The difference mattered. [489] She was still a euthanasia tourist, still carrying her exit in her coat lining, still someone who gripped reality loosely enough that the world sometimes slipped sideways on her. [518] But she had learned, on the drift-ice marina, that endings had a register she could tune to. [535] Bells, hulls easing into slack water, a boy's breath steadying after crying. [547] She wanted more of them, and then more, until she had gathered enough to understand what hers would sound like when it came. [570] On her last dawn of that season she walked to the end of the pier with the parasol tip in her fist. [592] The wind carried a thin, patient music off the floes. [602] It sounded like solace, windborne, moving toward her without hurry. [612] She lifted the brass to her ear and listened, and for the first time since waking changed, she was not early for her own life. [637]</story>
