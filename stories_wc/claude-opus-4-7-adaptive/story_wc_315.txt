<story>The arbor hung above the clouds like a lantern someone forgot to snuff. [13] Iron vines laced between cedar boughs, and moonlight pooled in the hollows where the platform met the sky. [31] Wren crouched at its edge, her coat flared by updraft, her gloved fingers working a parachute buckle she had opened and closed a thousand times. [56] She was the midnight pattern seeker the Observatory trusted to watch the sky for signals no one else could parse. [76] Tonight, the radio in her ear had hissed for three hours with nothing but white noise. [92] After the radio static cleared, a voice would come through, and she would either confirm the pattern or deny it. [112] Two pilots below her, folded into glider harnesses beneath the arbor, were waiting for that confirmation to decide whether to jump. [133] If she was wrong, they would fall into weather they could not outfly. [146] If she was right, they would reach the coast before dawn. [157] The buckle in her hand was her old instructor's, a talisman that steadied her breath whenever the stars felt too many. [178] Watchers carry a burden the flyers never see, he had told her once: you keep your mouth shut, you keep your eyes moving, and you never let them know how often you are guessing. [212] She had never let them know. [218] She had, however, guessed. [222] The stars over the arbor arranged themselves in a lattice she had memorized not by sight but by hand. [241] She traced them now, fingertip over charcoal chart, remembering through muscle memory the position of each waypoint as it had drifted across seven seasons of her watch. [268] Something was off. [271] A star she expected at the third knuckle sat instead at the second. [284] Either her chart was old or the weather front had bent the visible arc by a degree. [301] Both were possible. [304] Neither would forgive her if she guessed wrong. [312] The radio cleared. [315] "Seeker, pattern check," said the voice, thin as a wire. [325] She drew a breath and held the buckle tighter. [334] To preserve the pilots' trust under pressure, she knew, she had to sound certain whether or not she was. [353] But certainty bought with a lie would kill them later if the lattice really had shifted. [369] She was attentively inventive by nature, a watcher who had always found a third door when told there were only two. [390] "Stand by," she answered. [394] She closed her eyes and ran her thumb along the buckle's catch, feeling the tiny burr her instructor had filed into it as a private signature. [420] Remember the shape before you name it, he used to say. [431] Her hand did the work while her mind let go. [441] The lattice settled. [444] The displaced star was not displaced; her chart was. [453] She had drawn it in spring and never updated it for the season's drift. [467] She would revise it at dawn, but the pattern itself held. [478] "Corridor open," she said. [482] "Wind southeast, ceiling six thousand, launch on my mark." [491] Below her, two harnesses clicked. [496] Two gliders slid from the arbor into a dark so quiet it felt like a soundless echo of every jump that had come before. [520] She did not watch them fall. [526] Watchers never do; watching the fall is not what the watcher is for. [539] She kept her eyes moving across the stars instead, counting, checking, re-counting. [551] When the first horizon paled, the radio crackled once more. [561] "Coast reached, Seeker. Clean landing." [566] She let out a breath that had waited seven hours to leave her. [579] Then, because the burden did not lift so much as shift shoulders, she uncapped her pen, pulled her chart into her lap, and began to revise it against the slow pink rising. [611] The buckle lay beside her, catching the earliest light, waiting to be closed again tomorrow night, and the night after that. [632]</story>
