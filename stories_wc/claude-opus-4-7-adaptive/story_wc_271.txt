Noor arrived at the arboretum three winters after the last lighthouse keeper was buried in the chapel garden below. [19] The cliffs still hummed with his absence, as if the sea had not yet learned to signal itself. [37] She carried a canvas bag, a thermos, and a crumpled photograph folded so many times its creases had grown soft as moth wing. [60] In the photograph, seventeen people stood under a lemon tree, laughing. [71] Nine were dead by sunset that day, killed by a drone whose pilot, she later read, had mistaken a wedding drum for a weapons cache. [96] Noor was the bride. [100] She had survived because she had bent to fix her sister's sandal. [112] For years afterward she could not bend, could not kneel, could not look at lemons. [127] The arboretum had hired her as a groundskeeper because she was quietly talented with grafts, with soil pH, with the stubborn rhododendrons that refused the salt wind. [154] Nobody knew about the photograph. [159] Nobody asked why she sometimes stood among the Monterey cypress with her hand on her sternum, breathing slowly, as if counting pearls on an invisible string. [185] Her therapist, back in the city, had taught her to work through somatic markers: the tight throat meaning grief, the cold palms meaning fear, the unexpected warmth behind the eyes meaning something closer to hope, which she distrusted most of all. [226] When a marker arrived she would name it, locate it, and freeze in place until her body agreed to move again. [247] She called these pauses her pearls of stillness. [255] One string of them could carry her through an entire afternoon. [266] The arboretum suited this practice. [271] Perched on the cliff with only the abandoned lighthouse for a neighbor, it offered long corridors of hedge where she could stand motionless without alarming visitors. [297] In April the director, Mr. Olave, asked her to lead a public tour. [310] "The trustees want someone grounded," he said. [317] "Someone who won't oversell the place." [323] She understood. [325] The arboretum was dying in patches; funding was thin; a storm had taken three of the oldest oaks. [343] Optimism alone would not save it, but pessimism would empty the ticket booth by July. [358] What the trustees needed, she realized, was someone who could anchor optimism in realism, the way a fig tree anchors itself in a cliff by admitting the cliff's refusal to be soil. [390] She agreed. [392] On the morning of the tour she walked the paths alone first, the photograph in her pocket. [409] She stopped at the lemon grove the last keeper had planted against all horticultural advice. [424] The trees had not died. [429] They had not thrived either. [434] They stood in the salt light, yellow-fruited and stubborn, exactly what they were. [447] Noor felt her throat tighten, named it, and froze. [456] A gull cried. [459] The sea exhaled. [462] When her body released her, she took out the crumpled photograph and looked at it without flinching for the first time in eleven years. [486] Nine faces, still laughing. [490] Eight survivors, including her. [494] She did not fold the photograph back. [501] She slipped it into a knot of the oldest lemon tree, where the bark had split to make a small hollow. [522] Then she went to meet the visitors at the gate. [532] Fourteen of them waited: a retired teacher, a boy with a sketchbook, a couple holding a baby. [549] Noor led them first to the cliff edge, where the lighthouse stood dark. [562] "The last keeper died two years ago," she said. [571] "No one replaced him, because the ships have new instruments now." [582] "But the arboretum remains, and it is not thriving, and it is not dying." [596] She gestured toward the lemon grove, the cypress, the patched lawns. [607] "What grows here grows because someone admits the wind." [615] The boy with the sketchbook wrote that down. [623] The baby slept. [626] Noor felt the warmth behind her eyes arrive and this time did not distrust it. [641] She named it, located it, and let it move. [650] Somewhere behind her, in the hollow of a stubborn tree, the photograph softened into the weather, joining everything else that had learned to stand on a cliff without pretending the cliff was kind.
