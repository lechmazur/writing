The Archive of Extinct Tongues slept beneath the city like a held breath, its shelves crowded with scripts no mouth had shaped in centuries. [24] Inez, arcane linguist and stubborn optimist, knelt among them with a glass ink well cradled in both palms. [42] The ink inside was not ink but memory, distilled from promises spoken aloud and then broken. [58] Seven years of silence stretched between her and her brother Calo, and she had come here to end it before their paths diverged for good. [83] Calo would leave at dawn on a vessel bound for the outer colonies, and dawn was three lamp-turns away. [102] Their quarrel had begun over a small lie and grown into a knot she now meant to unsnarl, one strand at a time. [125] The archive's texts taught her that the oldest cultures restored trust via interconnected promises, a lattice where each vow supported the next like ribs in a vaulted roof. [153] Break one, and the ceiling held; break many, and the sky fell in. [166] Inez had broken too many. [171] Tonight she would rebuild them in a language old enough that no living tongue could betray it. [188] She set the ink well on a brass tray beside a sheet of vellum and unrolled the concordance of Vhesh, an extinct liturgical tongue whose speakers had believed that honest words produced symphonies of truth audible only to the attentive heart. [229] It was a beautiful idea, and a dangerous one, because Vhesh refused ornament and would scald any writer who lied while its ink dried. [253] Inez had always been carefully reckless, the sort of scholar who read warning labels and then proceeded anyway with gloves on. [274] She dipped a reed pen into the ink well and began. [285] The first promise: I will not shape your life to spare my own discomfort. [299] The vellum warmed but did not burn. [306] The second promise: I will tell you when I am afraid, instead of calling it advice. [322] A tone hummed from the shelves, low and clean, and she felt the old grammar take hold like a tuning fork struck inside her ribs. [347] The third promise was harder. [352] I was wrong about the loan, and I let you carry the blame because pride was cheaper than apology. [371] The ink flared briefly, testing her, and then settled into a deep, unembarrassed black. [385] She wrote each vow so that it leaned on the last, weaving a structure that could bear weight. [403] Around her, the archive seemed to lean in too, the dead languages attending with the patience of witnesses. [421] When the seventh promise was dry, the glass ink well chimed once, softly, and emptied itself. [437] Inez rolled the vellum, tied it with a ribbon the color of river silt, and climbed back into the waking city with a cautious exuberance she had not felt in years. [468] The harbor smelled of tar and tangerines. [475] Calo stood on the gangway in his new coat, already half a stranger, already half gone. [491] She held out the scroll without speaking, because Vhesh required silence from the giver. [505] He unrolled it, and though he could not read the script, she watched him hear it. [521] His shoulders dropped; his jaw softened; a smile moved across his face the way weather moves across water. [539] He did not need translation; the symphonies of truth carry their own meaning, and a brother knows his sister's honest voice even when the notation is older than their country. [569] Calo tucked the vellum inside his coat, against the ribs she had written toward. [583] He said, I will write back in a language we can both learn. [596] The ship's bell rang, the gangway lifted, and the harbor filled with a small, ordinary music. [612] Inez stood waving until he became a figure, and then a suggestion, and then only the memory of a promise still holding up the sky. [637]
