The Odeon had been dark for twelve years when Mira climbed the iron stairs to the projection booth. [18] She was a crystal gazer by trade, though she preferred the older term: psychometrist. [32] Objects remembered what people forgot, and Mira remembered for them. [42] The owner's granddaughter had hired her to settle an estate of silences. [54] Somewhere in this rotting palace, the old man had hidden forty years of goodbye letters he never sent. [72] They were addressed to one woman, a ticket-taker named Alma, who had loved him back and left anyway. [90] Mira's task, phrased delicately in the contract, was to find where unused love is stored. [105]

The booth smelled of hot celluloid and cold rain. [114] Two projectors squatted like patient horses, and reels lay stacked against the walls in towers she read the way other people read rooms. [137] Mira was spatially imaginative, a gift more useful than any crystal; she could feel the geometry of a secret before she touched it. [160] The secret here hummed in one corner, low and insistent, like hushed thunder behind a closed door. [177] She followed it to a battered tin canister labeled MARQUEE TEST, 1961. [189] Inside coiled a film strip, its emulsion warped and breathing. [199]

Mira pressed her palm to the reel and began a psychometry reading, letting her pulse slow to match the object's. [219] Visions never arrived like films; they arrived like weather. [228] A young man, Henry, threading a reel with trembling hands. [238] Alma below, singing to herself in the lobby, a melody she had half-written and never finished. [254] Henry had promised to set her words to music; he never did, and she married a man from Cleveland. [273] Every night afterward, Henry wrote her a letter, sealed it, and did something strange. [287] He tucked it between frames of test footage, winding each page through the gate, splicing love into nothing. [305] He meant to incubate the letters inside the film, believing that light would eventually hatch them into a picture Alma could watch from wherever she'd gone. [331] The booth was his nesting box; the projector, his patient sun. [342]

Mira opened her eyes. [346] The canisters were stacked not by date but by shape, each tower describing a letter of Alma's name in negative space. [367] Only someone who thought in rooms could see it. [376] Mira mapped the aisles of reels, the way an astronomer maps a sky, until she found the center: one canister, unlabeled, placed precisely where A and M kissed. [404]

She opened it. [407] Forty years of envelopes, each sleeved in a curl of film like a mother around her egg. [424] This was where the unused love had been stored: not in a heart, not in a drawer, but in the small warm dark between exposures. [449]

Mira threaded the first reel, because the dead deserve audiences. [459] The projector coughed awake, and against the scarred wall of the booth a face flickered: Alma, younger than forgetting, mouth open around a song she'd lost. [485] Mira felt it then, the particular ache of that hour, while forgotten lyrics search for tune and find only air. [505] Henry's voice rose from the speakers, reading his first letter. [515] Dear Alma, I have been writing you the melody. [524] I keep it under the projector so it will stay warm. [535] When you are ready to remember the words, it will be here. [547]

Mira stayed until dawn, reading each letter aloud with Henry, so the picture would be complete. [563] Outside, in a nursing home in Cleveland, an old woman woke humming a tune she had not known she remembered. [583] She found a pencil, and the lyrics came back one by one, as if they had only been waiting in a warm dark place. [607] Somewhere above the marquee, a projector kept its slow quiet rumble, finally understood. [620]
