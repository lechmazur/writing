<story>Isen was a broken mask restorer, and the masks he loved best were those nobody else would touch. [18] He worked inside the prism sanctuary, a chapel of angled glass built above the sea cliffs, where starlight fractured into ribbons across every wall. [42] The sanctuary had a peculiar quality the villagers only whispered about: it accumulates silence, drinking sound the way dry cloth drinks wine. [64] Fishermen's widows brought him cracked porcelain faces and split lacquer shells, begging him to mend them so their drowned husbands might at last lie still. [89] That was Isen's vocation: to ground restless spirits by making whole what grief had shattered. [104] On the longest night of autumn, a woman named Yera came carrying a bundle wrapped in sailcloth. [121] She set it on his workbench and watched the prism light crawl across her knuckles. [136] Inside lay the fragments of a pale driftwood mask and, strangely, a centuries-old fisherman's net dense with salt-stiffened knots. [155] I need you to exhume something, she said, and her voice was so soft the sanctuary nearly swallowed it. [174] Not a body, she clarified, but a secret my grandfather knotted into this net before he sank with his boat. [194] Isen understood at once. [198] Old sailors used to bind confessions into cordage, trusting the sea to either keep them or release them as it saw fit. [220] The mask, she explained, was my grandmother's; it broke the hour he drowned, and she has not slept since, though she has been dead forty years. [246] Yera asked if he would listen as he worked, for she could only tell the story once. [263] So began their strange timeframe: across sharing a secret, they would each do their part. [278] Isen took up his tapestry needle, the only tool delicate enough to stitch driftwood grain back to driftwood grain without splintering the fragile weave of memory inside. [305] Yera sat opposite him and began, by unraveling knots in the centuries-old fisherman's net, to exhume what her grandfather had hidden. [326] Each knot she loosened coughed up a word, and each word settled into the air between them like a moth. [346] The sanctuary drank each word as readily as it drank their breathing, layering everything into what Isen had long called the interlaced hush. [369] It was not true silence but silence woven of many silences, each thread a confession that the glass had kept. [389] Yera spoke of a brother lost overboard on a calm night, of a lantern left unlit on purpose, of a promise her grandfather had made and then betrayed to save his own skin. [422] As she spoke, Isen's needle moved in answering rhythm, binding driftwood seams with thread thin as spider-silk. [439] He felt the tone of the work shift into lucid unrest: clear, wakeful, yet troubled, like a tide that knows exactly where it intends to rise. [465] The starlight tilted through the prisms and painted colored maps onto the workbench, and in those maps Isen saw the grandmother's sleepless face. [488] He saw also the grandfather, waterlogged and apologetic, waiting for someone to speak his shame aloud so he might finally put it down. [511] When Yera pulled the final knot free, the net lay smooth across her lap like a cleaned bone. [529] Isen slid the last stitch home, and the mask was whole again, cool and pale beneath his palms. [547] For a long moment neither of them moved. [555] The interlaced hush held them like water holds a swimmer who has stopped struggling. [569] Then Yera exhaled, and Isen understood that the secret had left her and entered the sanctuary's deep reservoir of kept things, where it would trouble no one further. [597] She wrapped the mask gently and thanked him with only a nod, because words now felt like too much furniture for the room. [620] Isen cleaned his tapestry needle and set it back in its velvet groove. [633] Outside, the sea smoothed itself, and somewhere beneath it two restless spirits sank at last into the floor of sleep. [653] He had not asked what the secret was. [661] He never did. [664] That was the covenant of the prism sanctuary under starlight: to restore, to listen, and to let the glass remember so the living no longer had to. [691]</story>
