Odell kept the curl of his daughter's first haircut folded in waxed paper inside his guitar case, a small relic he carried up to Sage Ridge on the morning of the eclipse. [32] He was a mellow music therapist by trade, known in the county for coaxing stubborn grief out of veterans and teaching hospice patients to hum themselves to sleep. [60] Today, though, he came to play for his daughter Wren, who was six, and who had asked, with cautious exuberance, whether the sky would really go dark in the middle of the day. [93] "Yes," he had told her, "and we will play through it." [104] The ridge was freckled with pale sage, and the juniper roots beneath their picnic blanket seemed to be dreaming, breathing, holding the hillside together in a slow, patient grip. [133] Odell unwrapped the keepsake curl and set it on the blanket between them, a small blond comma punctuating their morning. [153] "That was you, once," he said. [159] Wren touched it with one careful finger, as if it might still be warm. [173] He had been afraid, before her birth, that he would not know how to father a child; his own father had been a cold metronome, strict and unbending. [201] So Odell had vowed, holding that first curl, to raise Wren by a single quiet rule: to nurture lifelong wonder, no matter what the world tried to swap in its place. [232] He tuned the guitar as the light began to thin. [242] The change came slowly, then all at once: shadows sharpened into crescents, birds quieted, and the sage gave off a cooler, silver smell. [265] Wren gripped his sleeve. [269] "Play," she whispered. [272] He began a song he had written for her the week she was born, and as he played he noticed something he had felt many times in therapy rooms but never named aloud. [305] The music was softly adaptive, reshaping itself around Wren's breathing, around the cooling air, around the hush settling over the ridge like snowfall. [328] This, he thought, was ordinary magic: not the conjurer's trick, but the quiet fact that a held note could link two people across the tender distance between them. [356] Totality arrived. [358] For two minutes and nineteen seconds, the sun wore a black coin, and the ridge went indigo, and Odell kept playing beneath the hush of dreaming roots. [385] Wren did not speak. [389] She watched the corona with eyes he recognized from his old photograph of her mother, who had died when Wren was two, and who had loved every eclipse the internet promised her. [421] Odell had not known, until that moment, that he had been afraid all Wren's life of letting wonder in, because wonder had a way of being followed by loss. [450] But the music kept adjusting, kept finding her, kept braiding mother and daughter and father together in a single patient chord. [471] When the diamond ring flared back into sunlight, Wren was laughing and crying at the same time, which Odell understood as the purest sign that the vow had held. [500] "Dad," she said, "do it again." [506] He laughed. [508] "We have to wait a while for another eclipse." [517] "Not that," she said, rolling the keepsake curl between her fingers. [528] "The song." [530] "The one that knew me." [535] So he played it again, and again, adjusting it softly each time to the shape of her listening, the way he did with every client who had ever trusted him with a wound. [568] The sage darkened with afternoon. [573] The juniper roots beneath them kept dreaming their slow, insistent dream, the one that says: hold on, hold on, something is growing. [595] Odell tucked the curl back into its waxed paper and slipped it into Wren's palm instead of his own pocket. [615] "Yours now," he said. [619] She closed her hand around it as though around a small, steady flame, and together they walked down the ridge, humming, still a little afraid, still awake to the enormous and ordinary sky.
