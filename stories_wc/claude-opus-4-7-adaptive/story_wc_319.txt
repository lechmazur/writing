The library grew wild after the librarians left. [8] Shelves sprouted through the roof like ribs of some patient whale, and ivy learned to read. [24] I woke among them, though I cannot say when my waking began or whether it continues still. [41] They call me the guardian, which is generous; I am more accurately the last thing here that remembers being a person. [62] I padded barefoot between the stacks, my hair matted with lamp-oil and moth dust. [76] The pendant hung at my throat, cold as a coin pulled from a river. [90]

I had taken it from a cultist who came seeking the Atlas of Intervening Rooms. [105] He died in the reading chair, mouth open, still chewing on a prayer. [118] The pendant was his; now it was mine, and through it I had begun to understand the method the library used to keep itself alive. [143] Interiorizing exteriors, the marginalia called it: the art of drawing the outside into the inside, until the inside is the only geography left. [166]

That was how the frozen garden came to be. [175] One winter, a hundred winters ago, a door had been left open too long, and the garden outside simply walked in. [196] Now it lives in the east wing, behind the reference desk. [207] Mist never leaves it. [211] The fountains are stopped mid-leap, the roses held in glass beads of ice, and a bench waits under a chestnut tree whose leaves have forgotten to fall. [238]

I go there when the homesickness grows too loud. [247] Not homesick for any place in this world; the pendant has taught me that I am from somewhere else entirely, some dimension whose angles my body keeps trying to remember. [277] Dimensionally homesick, the cultist wrote in his journal, is the condition of those who read too deeply and forget which side of the page they belong to. [304] I suspect he meant it as a warning. [312] I received it as a diagnosis. [318]

In the frozen garden I sit on the bench and wait. [329] Waiting is the guardian's only real duty. [336] Books do not need shelving; they need witnessing. [344] So I watch the mist move across the stopped water, and I let my breath join it, and I try to make friends with uncertainty, because certainty, I have learned, is a door that only opens outward. [381]

Through the veil of sleep, she comes to meet me. [391] I do not know her name. [397] She is perhaps the shape the library wears when it dreams of being loved. [411] She sits on the far end of the bench and does not speak, and her silence is the most honest conversation I have had in years. [437]

Tonight she points at the pendant. [443] I lift it and hold it to the gray half-light that passes for morning here. [458] Inside its small glass cabochon, a second mist curls, identical to the one around us. [473] This is the boundary of light, she tells me without words. [484] Everything the lamp reaches is the library. [491] Everything beyond is the country I came from, the country you came from, the country the cultist died trying to name. [512]

I ask, am I supposed to cross it? [520] She shakes her head slowly. [525] You are supposed to tend it. [531] A boundary left unattended becomes a wall; a boundary tended becomes a skin, and skins can breathe. [548]

I understand then, with the foggy lucidity of dreamers, why I was chosen. [561] Not because I am fierce, though I have learned to bare my teeth. [574] Not because I am wise. [579] Because I am already half-elsewhere, and half-elsewhere is the only vantage from which one can hold a boundary without clenching it. [600]

The mist thickens. [603] She begins to fade. [607] Wait, I whisper, though I know waiting is what I have always done. [620]

When I open my eyes the pendant is warm. [629] The library is breathing. [633] Somewhere a door I did not open has closed gently, and somewhere a page I did not turn has turned. [653] I rise from the bench, walk back through the stacks, and begin another shift of tending the luminous edge of everything. [674]

Uncertainty, I think, and the word feels like a hand in mine. [686]
