<story>Idris played the piano at Cloudspur Station for forty years, and every evening at six he wound the anniversary clock his wife had left him. [25] On the morning the clock broke, its hands stuck at 6:14, he felt the first tug of something pulling him upward. [46] Above the concourse hung a glass case of station compasses, and today their needles were flickering like moths against lantern glass. [67] The stationmaster, seeing Idris's face, only shrugged: "When the compasses flicker, somebody has an appointment they didn't schedule." [85] Idris had been strategically creative with his grief for a decade now, hiding small hopes in strange places so that despair could never find them all at once. [113] His favorite refuge was the station's old projection booth, where a custodian named Mira ran black-and-white film reels for insomniac travelers. [134] Mira had told him, years ago, that certain reels contained hidden sections, frames spliced in by a forgotten projectionist who believed cinema was a door. [159] Idris had always laughed at that. [165] He did not laugh when the broken anniversary clock began ticking backward in his pocket. [180] He climbed to the projection booth, fed a reel through the projector, and watched the tidy gray world unspool. [199] Between two frames he glimpsed what Mira had promised: a flicker of arbor trellis, pale ropes of wisteria, and beyond them sky. [221] He pressed his hand against the screen, and it let him through. [233] He stepped onto a tidal arbor suspended above the clouds, where pale vines rose and fell as if breathing with an unseen sea. [256] At its center stood a piano. [262] At its bench sat a woman who had been absent from his life for ten years. [278] Elena did not turn; she only said, "You are late for our anniversary." [291] A steely longing rose in him, sharpened by every unplayed note of the years between. [306] He understood then what the flickering compasses had meant: a seam in fate, a brief opening, and only a pianist would know to fill it with music. [333] He sat beside her. [337] "The clock broke," he said. [342] "Good," she answered. "We were never going to finish on time." [353] They played together then, a duet they had abandoned the winter she fell ill, his right hand remembering her left. [373] Clouds pulled against the arbor like surf; petals drifted upward into nothing. [385] Idris had come to surprise fate with an unplanned encore, and fate, for once, seemed willing to listen. [403] They played until the horizon invited night, a long blue slope tipping slowly toward dark. [418] When the final chord settled, Elena lifted her hand from the keys and pressed the anniversary clock back into his palm. [439] Its hands, he saw, were turning again, though now they pointed inward, toward the center of the dial like two small compasses agreeing at last. [464] "Go back," she told him. "Your station will miss its song." [475] He wanted to argue, to stay, to pretend the arbor could hold him. [488] But he had lived long enough to know that a refuge is not a home, only a room where grief is given permission to rest. [513] He stepped back through the frame. [519] The booth was dim; Mira was asleep; the reel spun on its last empty loop. [534] Downstairs the 6:14 was boarding, late by a single merciful minute. [545] Idris sat at the piano and began to play the duet alone, his right hand steady, his left hand hearing what was no longer there. [570] Travelers paused in the concourse. [575] The glass case of compasses steadied one by one, each needle settling north as if reassured. [591] Outside, the horizon closed its blue eye. [598] In Idris's pocket, the anniversary clock kept a slower time now, measured not by minutes but by the distance between an unplanned encore and the next chance to play one. [628]</story>
