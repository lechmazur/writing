The alchemist's market swayed on braided cables above the city, a bazaar strung between chimneys like laundry forgotten by giants. [20] Mira climbed the rope-ladder she had climbed in dreams since she was seven. [33] She was thirty now, and the rungs felt the same. [43] That sameness was her trade. [48] Mira called herself a between-worlds translator, though no guild issued the title. [60] She read the living language of dead painters, tracing meaning through hidden details in paintings: a cracked tile pointing north, a dog whose collar spelled a banned surname, a window where the sky was the wrong blue for the stated hour. [101] Collectors paid her to authenticate; historians paid her to remember; she herself worked to honor overlooked histories, the ones that had survived only by being folded into brushstrokes. [129] Today a vendor had summoned her. [135] His stall hung from a copper hook above the clockmakers' quarter, its floor a plank that bowed under her boots. [155] He offered her tea the color of rust and placed a small wrapped bundle on the counter. [172] Inside lay a curved shard of glass, thick as a thumb, its edges ground smooth by sea and time. [191] "A lighthouse lens fragment," he said, "from the Verel light, before the fire." [204] Mira lifted it. [207] The shard was elusively tangible, heavy in a way that exceeded its weight, as if it carried the reflection of every ship it had ever guided. [233] She had seen this shape before. [239] Between childhood and now she had catalogued seventeen paintings of the Verel coast, and in each of them the lighthouse bore a distinctive flaw, a prism hairline near the upper lens, which the official record denied. [275] The paintings insisted otherwise. [278] The paintings, she had come to understand, were the archive the city refused to keep. [293] "I need to know who owned this," the vendor said, "and whether it remembers." [307] Mira carried the fragment to the painters' platform, three gangways over, where the Guild of Restorers kept their reference canvases pinned to suspended frames. [331] She held the shard to a dim oil of the Verel storm, aligning its curve with the painted lens. [350] Light threaded through, striking the canvas, and a mark she had never noticed unfolded in the varnish: a small ribbon tied to the lighthouse rail, painted the color of mourning. [380] A name was stitched into the ribbon, minuscule, almost a tremor of pigment. [393] Idra Solm. [395] The keeper's daughter, erased from the records after the fire because she had refused to leave her post. [413] Mira's hands steadied the way they did when intuition locked into place. [425] She thought of intuition as a harbor, not a feeling but anchors of intuition dropped years ago by a younger self who had trusted the first strange shimmer in a gallery corner. [457] Those anchors held her now. [462] She had read Idra's presence in six other paintings without naming it, believing the mourning ribbons to be a motif. [482] They were a signature. [486] Idra had painted herself back into the coast, standing guard in pigment because the city would not permit her in ink. [507] Mira returned to the vendor's stall as the market lanterns lit. [518] The wind rocked the platforms, and the rooftops below drifted like a slow river of slate. [534] She set the fragment on the counter and asked him to unfasten the silver clasp that bound his ledger. [553] He did. [555] She wrote Idra Solm, keeper, lost 1871, painter of her own remaining, and pressed the entry with her thumb. [574] The vendor read it twice. [579] Then he nodded, the small nod of a man who had waited to be told what he already suspected. [598] Outside the stall, the city held its breath in the way it always held at dusk, when the hour refuses to progress. [620] Mira felt the moment widen into an eternal instant: the shard warm in her palm, seventeen ribbons aligning across seventeen canvases, a girl painting herself free of erasure one hidden stroke at a time. [654] She would write the catalogue tonight. [660] Tomorrow she would descend into the archives and make them flinch. [671] For now she stood on a swaying plank above the chimneys, translator between a drowned keeper and a forgetful century, and let the lighthouse fragment catch the last red thread of the sun. [704]
