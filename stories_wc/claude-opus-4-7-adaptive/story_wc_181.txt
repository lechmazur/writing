<story>The parcel appeared on Mireille's desk without postage, wrapped in brown paper that smelled faintly of almonds. [17] Inside lay a slim catalogue and a pistachio shell cameo blank, its inner curve polished to receive a face that had not yet been carved. [42] As curator of the Museum of Unlisted Things, Mireille was accustomed to odd donations, but this one bewildered her. [61] The catalogue's frontispiece named her as the heir. [69] Heir to whom, it did not say; the will, it claimed, would be written between the first and last page, in sequence she chose herself. [94] Her grandmother had called such bequests nonlinear inheritance: gifts that rearranged themselves depending on the reader's questions. [111] Grandmother had also warned her never to inquire too earnestly of dead books. [124] Mireille inquired anyway. [127] On page one she asked where the catalogue came from, and the ink bloomed an answer: an astral orchard blossoming on a lonely asteroid, harvested once per century. [155] The orchard grew pistachio trees whose shells held portraits of everyone their eaters would become. [170] Her blank, then, was unfinished; her face not yet decided. [180] The disquiet this produced was, strangely, bright. [187] For the first time in a decade of dim cataloguing, Mireille felt chosen by something that still believed in her capacity to change. [210] She took the catalogue home, locked the door, and read it in the only room where she trusted her mind to split open cleanly: the bathroom. [236] All her philosophical breakthroughs had happened there, between faucet and mirror, where a person could be simply complicated without apology. [256] Page two asked what she wanted most. [263] She wrote: forbidden knowledge, of the kind my grandmother hid. [273] The page warmed in her palm. [279] Page three showed her grandmother as a young woman kneeling beneath a silver tree on the asteroid, carving the first cameo blank. [301] Page four showed Mireille herself, aged ninety, teaching a child to read the pages backward. [316] Page five was blank. [320] Page six held her death. [325] The order, the catalogue reminded her, was hers to arrange. [335] Inheritance here did not flow from ancestor to descendant along a tidy river; it pooled, looped, traded tenses. [353] A grandmother could inherit courage from the granddaughter she had not yet taught. [366] A curator could inherit a face from a shell no one had yet eaten. [380] Mireille understood, tile-cold under her feet, that to read further was to commit to being carved. [396] She turned the page. [400] The forbidden knowledge her grandmother had hidden was not a secret but a technique: how to choose which inheritances to accept, and in what order, so that the self arriving at the last page was recognizable to the self who had opened the first. [444] It was simply complicated work, the way love is. [453] She took the pistachio shell cameo blank from her pocket and held it to her cheek. [469] Warm already, it began to render her profile, though she had chosen nothing yet. [483] Reading, she realized, was choosing. [488] She skipped her death and went back to the orchard. [498] On that asteroid, alone under a blossoming canopy that drank starlight instead of sun, her grandmother looked up. [516] She was old there, not young; the pages had shuffled again. [527] Good, the old woman said, you inquired. [534] Most heirs never ask; they only accept. [541] Mireille asked the last question she had in her: what happens if I close the book? [557] Her grandmother smiled and handed her a pistachio. [565] Then you live between the first and last page forever, she said, which is what most of us were going to do anyway. [588] Mireille closed the book. [592] In her palm lay a finished cameo: a woman bewildered, brightened, mid-inquiry, carved from a shell that had grown on a lonely asteroid between the first breath she took and the last she would ever need to. [629] She set it in the museum's smallest vitrine and labelled it, simply: Inheritance, nonlinear; open to inquiry. [646]</story>
