<story>Hessel climbed because stopping was worse. [6] The staircase had no landing, no summit, no cellar; it threaded itself through a grey air that neither brightened nor dimmed. [27] He had learned to name his condition honestly: grim optimist, a man who believed the next step might matter while admitting the last ten thousand had not. [54] In his coat pocket rolled a pair of dice, one chipped at the corner, the chip an old fact he refused to sand smooth. [78] The dice were the reason he was here, and the reason he kept moving. [92] Years ago, though years meant little in a place without sun, he had been offered a wager by a traveling geometer who claimed to have solved perpetual motion through mathematical proofs. [123] Roll, the geometer said, and if both show six, I will show you the engine that never tires. [141] Hessel had hesitated. [144] He had been balancedly daring all his life, which is to say he weighed risk like a grocer weighs flour, never spilling, never shorting, never quite filling the bag. [173] He set the dice down unthrown. [179] The geometer smiled with the sorrow of someone who had met this answer before, pressed the chipped die into Hessel's palm as a souvenir, and walked out into weather Hessel could no longer remember. [213] Shortly after, the staircase began. [218] At first he thought it a dream, then a punishment; now he understood it as something subtler. [235] A man who refuses a throw receives, in lieu of luck, a corridor, boundless and confining at once, every tread identical, every banister the same cool iron under the same unwilling hand. [267] He climbed to capture the texture of that missed opportunity, to reconstruct it by feel, to know exactly what he had declined so that the declining might mean something. [296] On the two thousandth landing that wasn't a landing, he began writing proofs on the risers in chalk. [314] He was not a mathematician, but grief had taught him rigor. [325] He wrote equations for a wheel that would turn forever, borrowing the geometer's claim and wrestling it down to letters. [345] He filled step after step with lemmas, corollaries, the small truths that hold larger truths upright. [361] Each proof faltered in the same place: energy, like courage, leaks. [372] No closed system stays wound. [377] No refusal stays painless. [381] He understood then that the geometer had lied, or rather had been generous with a lie; there is no perpetual motion except the kind a coward invents to keep himself climbing. [412] Hessel stopped. [414] He took out the dice and considered them in the flat light. [426] The chipped one sat a fraction off true, its faces no longer equally likely; the geometer's souvenir was itself a proof, a little argument in ivory that fairness is an assumption, not a law. [459] Good, Hessel thought. [462] Then nothing is owed. [466] He rolled them on the step where his last equation had broken. [478] They clattered, an impossibly loud sound in the grey hush, tumbled, tumbled, and came to rest. [494] A five. [496] A three. [498] Not sixes. [500] Not the winning throw. [504] But he laughed, the grim half first, then the optimistic half catching up, because a result, any result, was a rupture in the smooth wall of his hesitation. [532] The staircase shuddered. [535] Not dramatically; it was not that kind of place. [544] A hairline crack opened along one riser, admitting a slant of ordinary daylight, the dusty yellow kind that falls through kitchen windows onto tables where decisions are made. [572] Hessel knelt. [574] He pressed his eye to the crack and saw a road, a bright morning, a younger version of himself standing across from the geometer with the dice uncast. [602] He could not go back. [607] He had never been able to go back. [615] But he could, at last, feel the exact shape of what he had turned away from, its warmth, its hazard, its breathable weight, and holding that shape was not the same as holding the thing, yet it was not nothing. [655] He gathered the dice, chipped and whole, and pocketed them. [665] Then he descended. [668] The stairs, which had refused to end upward, consented to end downward, because a man who has thrown at least once is no longer the man they were built for. [698] At the bottom, which existed now, he opened a door he did not remember, into weather he had forgotten, and did not stop to count his steps. [725]</story>
