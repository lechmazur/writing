Mira painted the amber pavilion every autumn, layering ochre and rust across its slatted walls until the structure seemed to glow from within. [23] She was a vivid mural artist, quietly driven, the kind of woman who finished a wall and immediately began sketching the next one in the margins of her notebook. [52] That year, she carried her grandmother's collection of old postcards in a tin box, each one stamped from a city she had never seen. [76] The postcards were her inheritance, and on their backs her grandmother had written a single repeated question: why do flowers bloom at night? [99] Mira had taken the question as her own, half a riddle, half a prayer, and it became the motivation behind every commission she accepted. [123] The pavilion stood in the courtyard of an old cathedral, and the abbot had hired her to refresh its weathered panels before the first snow. [148] She worked slowly, watching the cathedral's tall window catch the late sun, its etched glass scattering pale shapes across the pavilion floor. [170] One evening, kneeling to wash her brushes, she noticed something strange through the hidden layers of etched glass in a cathedral window: a faint garden, pressed between the panes, drawn so finely it could only be seen at dusk. [209] The garden glimmered with night-blooming flowers, and Mira understood that someone, long ago, had asked the same question her grandmother had. [230] She returned each night, ignoring the cold, ignoring the abbot's polite confusion, sketching the etched garden across her own pavilion panels. [251] Winter came hard, then stalled; the work stretched across the melting of winter's last snow, and Mira's hands cracked from the cold even as the eaves began to drip. [280] She painted moonflowers, evening primrose, datura with their pale faces turned upward, and a single moth balanced on a stem of jasmine. [302] The abbot, an older man with patient eyes, began to bring her tea in the evenings, asking gentle questions about her grandmother's postcards. [325] He recognized one of the postmarks: a village near the cathedral, where a glassmaker had once etched a garden into a window for his dying wife, who loved only flowers that opened after sundown. [359] Mira sat with this for a long time, the tin box open in her lap, and felt a dawning hush settle over her, the kind that arrives when a question finally meets its answer. [393] Her grandmother had grown up in that village; she had passed beneath that very window as a girl, and the question had traveled with her across decades like a seed in a coat pocket. [427] Mira began to reckon with what the answer might be. [437] Not science, though she knew the moths and the cool air; not symbolism, though she felt the tug of it. [457] Some flowers bloomed at night because they had been made for the people who could not bear the daylight of their grief, who needed beauty to come quietly, late, when no one was watching. [491] She finished the pavilion on the last day of thaw, when the cathedral courtyard ran with bright water and the air smelled of stone waking up. [517] The walls held an umbral tenderness, dusk-colored gardens that looked, at certain hours, as if they were breathing. [535] The abbot stood beside her at sunset and said nothing, which was the right thing to say. [552] Mira tucked a single postcard into a crevice between two pavilion boards, where the wood would slowly accept it, the way the cathedral window had accepted its hidden garden. [581] She did not need to answer her grandmother's question aloud; the murals had answered for her, and the answer was tender, and shadowed, and enough. [606] She packed her brushes, lifted the tin box, and walked out into a courtyard where the last snow was vanishing into earth. [628]
