[16] Outside, the chiseled spires of Asteroid Adagio pierced the blackness, a city carved from a dream of baroque excess. [56] Now his battles were fought in hushed archives and dusty debating halls. [68]

[95] This was the core of their archaeological dispute, a chasm between Valerius's sterile data and Kaelen's own, more fragile theory. [115] Kaelen believed they were poets. [120] His proof, or what passed for it, rested on the desk beside him: a single crystal vase shard, salvaged from the Oblivion Pits. [142] It was a sliver of frozen light, its edges sharp enough to draw blood or a memory. [158]

[174] This was a phenomenon unique to the system since the Great Unraveling, the moment when time became visible, its currents and eddies lapping at the shores of the present. [204] Kaelen sighed, a sound of burnished sorrow, and began his ritual. [216] He had learned the hard way the local method of covering mirrors during storms. [229] The baroque sensibilities of the First Ones had left reflective surfaces everywhere, and during a squall, they did not just reflect your image, but echoes of every you that could have been, a chorus of ghosts that could shatter a mind. [270] He draped velvet cloths over the gilded looking-glass in the hall, then the polished silver samovar, and finally the obsidian tabletop of his desk, leaving only the shard exposed. [303]

[329] His motivation was not merely to win an argument but to reimagine an entire civilization, to grant them the grace in death that Valerius's narrative denied them. [355] He sought to restore their soul. [361]

[479] He saw an old man, not issuing edicts, but weeping at the beauty of a sunset over a world long since atomized. [501] These were not the actions of despots. [652] He held in his hand not just a piece of a vase, but the cornerstone of a lost people's dignity. [672] His first revolution had failed to build a new world, but this one, this quiet war of interpretation, might just resurrect an old one from its undeserved grave.
