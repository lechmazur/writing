Elias, son of a man who’d once held up armored cars with a sawed-off shotgun, lived in the belly of an abandoned circus train. [22] The air inside the lion tamer’s carriage tasted of rust and dusky harmony, a quiet he had cultivated over years of stillness. [45] His only inheritance was a worn leather satchel containing a collection of foreign paper money, currencies from countries his father had never visited but whose citizens he had certainly met on lonely highways. [82] Each evening, Elias would lay the notes out on a faded velvet seat, the faces of forgotten leaders and exotic animals staring up into the gloom. [110] He wasn't counting wealth; he was tracing the arteries of a life he was desperate not to re-live, driven by a need to discover why some paths circle back to their violent beginnings. [146]

[175] Their surveyors came first, their fluorescent vests an insult to the muted greens and browns of the woods. [195] Elias watched them from the grimy window of the caboose, his posture a study in a polished calm he’d learned was a better shield than any weapon. [226] His father had met trespassers with rage and noise; Elias met them with a perceptive calm, noting their jittery conversations about stock options and their fear of ticks. [257]

The startup’s rise was swift, marked by the clearing of brush and the roar of engines that threatened to erase his sanctuary.
