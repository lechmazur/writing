Elara was quietly driven by a question that had haunted her family for generations: why do certain flowers bloom only in the darkness, revealing their beauty when most of the world sleeps? [38]
Across the melting of winter's last snow and into the golden embrace of autumn, she had devoted herself to this pursuit with singular focus. [30]

Nothing changed until she discovered a particular postcard at the bottom of her collection, one showing not flowers, but the intricate etched glass of a cathedral window bathed in moonlight. [40]

Each panel told a story of transformation, of light becoming something else in darkness, of patterns emerging that remained invisible during the day. [32]
She traced the etchings with her fingertips, feeling the temperature change as the glass cooled in the evening air, noticing how the moonlight caught different angles than sunlight ever could. [38]
