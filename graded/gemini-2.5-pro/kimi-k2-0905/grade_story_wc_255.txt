The narrative logic is internally consistent and elegant.</comment>

The glassblower's courtyard, with its soot, discarded plants, and furnace, provides every key object and constrains every action. <question>4</question><grade>9.0</grade><comment>The central stake—Milo's quest for beauty—is established immediately and undergoes a sophisticated deepening. The conflict escalates from a search for perfection to a confrontation with the "violence" of imperfect color. The resolution, where the characters choose to smash their own creations, is a decisive act driven by agency, paying a clear cost by foreclosing the path of creating permanent, "perfect" objects in favor of a transient, broken beauty.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>7.2</grade><comment>While the theme of finding beauty in imperfection is potent and well-developed through powerful imagery, the final third of the story undermines this by lapsing into overt thematic statements. Lines like "beauty wasn't found in perfection but in the courageous act of remaining broken" and the entire final sentence state the moral directly. This "Thematic Echo" anti-pattern in the closing moments significantly harms the story's literary quality by refusing to trust the reader.</comment>
<question>6</question><grade>8.8</grade><comment>The voice is confident, lyrical, and consistently maintained, establishing a modern fable tone that perfectly suits the material. The third-person limited POV remains tightly focused on Milo, and his perceptual signature—both in his colorblind state and his fractured-color vision—is distinct and compelling. The sparse dialogue is impactful, and the overall narrative stance is controlled and effective.</comment>
<question>7</question><grade>7.5</grade><comment>The prose is ambitious, with many striking and original images. However, it is weakened by a shift from concrete scene-work to abstract summary in the final paragraphs, which stalls momentum at a crucial point. Phrases like "a living gallery that proved beauty wasn't found in perfection" are prose artifacts that tell rather than show, and this proximity to the closure dulls its impact. The piece sacrifices immediacy for explanation.</comment>
