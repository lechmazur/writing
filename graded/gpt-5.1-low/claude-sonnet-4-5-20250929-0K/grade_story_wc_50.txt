<question>2</question><grade>7.8</grade><comment>The causal chain is clean: discovery of the device → observation of light/reflections → reconstruction → activation → communal witnessing, with no major logic gaps. Orientation is solid from early on (who/where/why in the cave), and the structure holds as a single through-line of investigation. The main limitation is that the broader survival stakes stay mostly off-stage, so the climax feels thematically, not situationally, decisive.</comment>

The environment charges the final image—“The marble remembers”—with both physical and thematic weight. This is one of the piece’s strongest dimensions.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>7.2</grade><comment>The dominant stake is existential/psychic—whether the group has access to stored wonder and historical continuity versus mere subsistence—and this is clearly signaled. The wounded season and resource scarcity function as backdrop rather than live antagonistic forces that test his commitment. As a result, the final act is moving but only gently contested.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>8.0</grade><comment>Theme coheres around memory, resilience, and the idea of wonder as a survival resource, expressed through the concrete mechanism of the timer and the marble’s echoes. However, a few lines tilt toward stated thesis (“wonder itself was a resource that needed storing”) rather than wholly trusting image and action. The resonance is strong but could gain subtlety by shifting more thematic work into subtext.</comment>

There are no POV slips or register jolts, and the sparse dialogue aligns with the piece’s introspective mode. The voice is distinct enough to be memorable, though not so idiosyncratic that it fully anchors the piece at a stylistic level.</comment>
