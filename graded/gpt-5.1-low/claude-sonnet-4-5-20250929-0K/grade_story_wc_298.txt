<question>2</question><grade>8.6</grade><comment>Structure is clean: one continuous scene anchored by a brief, relevant backstory block, all under a clear causal lens (past trade → present misfit joy → failed reversal → new path). Cost is legible: he chooses real pain over magically altered emotion. <question>3</question><grade>8.7</grade><comment>The catacomb orchard is vivid and particular—apple trees in ribcages, roots coiling around skulls—and the setting’s bone-threaded growth tightly mirrors the theme that life and rot are inseparable. Details are consistently filtered through the Oracle’s perspective, and the final image of the burned card on the martyr’s palm is charged by everything established about this place. While the environment doesn’t heavily constrain physical options, it profoundly charges the emotional and thematic frame of every choice.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>7.8</grade><comment>The dominant stake—whether Marcus will continue to try to outsource his suffering or accept an integrated self—is clear early and tightened by the revelation that Rebecca has been harmed by the trade. Escalation is primarily internal and thematic rather than situational, but it does deepen: what began as a bad fit of happiness becomes an ethical and ontological problem once we know destinies deform in the wearing. The final choice to embrace pain over borrowed joy is coherent and costs him his fantasy of an easy fix. The piece stops short of dramatizing a sharper on-page struggle before he accepts her offer, which keeps pressure strong but not searing.</comment>

The line “real, whole pain is better than borrowed joy any day” edges toward explicit thesis, slightly reducing the subtextual power the images already carry. Overall, the story invites reflection on mental health shortcuts without sermonizing.</comment>

Rhythm and diction are controlled, with varied sentence structures that support the solemn, uncanny tone. A very slight omniscient drift in a few declarative lines keeps this shy of the top band.</comment>

The burned Lovers card with merged faces is a particularly deft, specific image that reconfigures the idea of relational exchange and selfhood. While the basic arc (accept your whole self) is recognizable, the vehicle and texture are distinct and memorable.</comment>
