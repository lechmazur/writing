Kale’s agency at the end (choosing to touch the box, accept teaching) resolves the conflict, but the emotional and ethical cost of that surrender feels somewhat smoothed over. <question>5</question><grade>7.8</grade><comment>Themes of inherited resentment, the cost of hatred, and nonviolent transformation are deeply embedded in the premise (weapons fueled by rage, a brother who collapses them by becoming its absence). However, the piece edges toward didacticism in places (“Resentment was their fuel,” “He chose this”), spelling out ethical takeaways rather than fully trusting implication. The ending offers a resonant answer—release as difficult freedom—but in a slightly too-tidy, less morally tangled way for top-tier literary work.</comment>
