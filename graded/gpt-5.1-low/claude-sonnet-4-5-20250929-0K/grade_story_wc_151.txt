<question>3</question><grade>8.5</grade><comment>The golden arcade, smog-thick New Manila sky, and chalk-covered marble floor are vivid and specific, with details filtered through Iris’s sensibility and profession. World rules (near-obsolete surface mail maintained by romantics) are integrated smoothly into the scene. The environment subtly constrains the action—the choice must be enacted physically here, now.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>7.8</grade><comment>The dominant stake—whether Iris risks revealing her true identity to her daughter—is clear early and remains coherent. The cost is largely internal and relational rather than externally enforced, which keeps escalation on the quiet side but still meaningful. A slightly sharper sense of potential negative consequence (beyond emotional vulnerability) could have deepened the stakes further.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>8.4</grade><comment>Themes of identity, authenticity, and “meaning in the margins” emerge organically through Iris’s trade, the chalk codes, and the archaic mail system, without overt moralizing. The mother–daughter thread quietly complicates the romanticism of analog with the real pain of abandonment. Ambiguity about the eventual reply leaves a resonant, non-tidy aftereffect.</comment>
<question>6</question><grade>8.2</grade><comment>The close third-person voice is consistent, slightly elevated but believable for a reflective, detail-oriented professional like Iris. Interior access is clean and focused on the present decision, with backstory woven rather than dumped. There’s a distinct perceptual signature in how she reads codes and infrastructure that helps the voice feel particular rather than generic.</comment>
<question>7</question><grade>8.3</grade><comment>The prose is controlled and economical, with most sentences doing double duty for atmosphere and character. A few phrases brush the edge of ornamentation, but they don’t obscure clarity or dull the closure beat.</comment>
<question>8</question><grade>8.0</grade><comment>The synthesis of a pseudonym creator, a declining analog mail system, and children’s chalk codes in a future megacity feels fresh and cohesive.
