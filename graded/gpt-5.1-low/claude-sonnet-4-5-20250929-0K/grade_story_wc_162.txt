<question>3</question><grade>8.4</grade><comment>The drifting prism terrace and gray void are vivid and specific, and the wooden rail as a micro-setting is excellent: tactile, history-bearing, and crucial to Mara’s method of reading. While the environment doesn’t strongly constrain choices, the terrace as liminal edge and the rail as recurring object charge the final image. World rules (probability wake, refugees intuiting direction) are sketched efficiently and stay consistent.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>7.5</grade><comment>The dominant stake is Mara’s need to decide how to relate to her father’s disappearance and to thresholds generally; this is clearly signaled and stays in focus. However, conflict remains low-temperature—Thomas offers only gentle questioning, and Mara rarely seems in danger of choosing a different, more damaging stance—so the sense of cost is muted. The emotional price (accepting his distributed absence rather than reclaiming him) is there but could be sharpened.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>8.0</grade><comment>Themes of distance, thresholds, and love as the “necessary distance” emerge organically from mazes, weather codes, and the terrace as liminal space. Some of the thematic articulation is a bit over-explicit (“The necessary distance, she understood now, was love”), tipping toward statement rather than leaving more work to image and implication. Even so, the story invites reflection on how we honor or cross emotional thresholds without collapsing into sermonizing.</comment>
