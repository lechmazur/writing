<question>2</question><grade>8.4</grade><comment>Structure is clean: we move from the empty hives to discovery, to confrontation at the tree, to release and a new role for Mara, with no orientation issues. Causality is coherent under an accumulative lens—her long observation of ghosts informs the decision to open the box, the letter’s content reweights the covenant, and reading it aloud allows the ghosts to dissipate. Closure is strong: Mara’s function in the oasis decisively reconfigures from beekeeper-surrogate to keeper of unspoken truths, with a visible new pattern of action implied. The cost remains mostly conceptual (breaking a sacred covenant and accepting ongoing solitude), which fits the piece but could be shaded more concretely.</comment>
<question>3</question><grade>8.9</grade><comment>The beehives, scorched oasis, and eucalyptus above a hidden spring are vivid and unified, with precise images like “wooden frames bleached white” and the gathered ghosts at dusk grounding the mood. Setting isn’t just backdrop: the dead hives, drought, and ghost-haunted oasis all embody the theme of silenced, withered communication, and the eucalyptus roots become the stage for the final ritual. The stained-glass box as an object-in-setting is effectively charged in the last image, where it catalyzes Mara’s new vocation. A touch more tactile interaction with the harsh environment (heat, thirst, physical discomfort) could heighten stakes, but the atmospheric and thematic integration is already strong.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>7.6</grade><comment>The central conflict—whether to uphold a covenant of silence or break it to release trapped truths—is clear and resolved by Mara’s choice to read the letter. The pressure escalates modestly from discovery to the silent circle of ghosts to the act of opening and reading, and the conceptual stakes (perpetuating vs. releasing hauntings) are nicely aligned. However, tension is largely intellectual; Mara faces little real resistance or risk beyond abstract taboo, and her inner conflict at the decision points is underplayed. As a result, the climax is resonant but feels relatively low-friction for the gravity of the thematic claim.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>7.0</grade><comment>The thematic throughline—unspoken words becoming ghosts that haunt individuals and communities—is coherent and well-mirrored by bees, drought, covenant, and ghosts. The notion of becoming a “keeper not of bees, but of what people had been too afraid to say” is a strong, image-based articulation of the story’s idea. However, the theme is often stated outright (“this silence is poison”; “what we don’t say becomes the ghost that haunts us”), and the final paragraph explicitly explains the moral rather than letting images and implication fully carry it. That didactic tilt blunts some potential subtlety and complexity.</comment>
<question>6</question><grade>8.0</grade><comment>The third-person voice is controlled, lyrical, and consistent with a quietly observant focalizer; diction like “tranquilly perceptive” and “mute eloquence” matches the contemplative, almost sacred tone. The narrative maintains a stable psychic distance, blending external detail and interior inference without head-hopping. Rhythms vary enough to avoid monotony, and the prose is confident without over-explaining mechanics. It does read more like an elevated literary narrator than something deeply idiosyncratic to Mara herself, but within this mode it's effective and polished.</comment>
<question>7</question><grade>8.2</grade><comment>Line-level craft is generally tight and image-rich, with many sentences doing double duty for setting and theme (e.g., “their mouths moving in silent testimony to words they'd never spoken in life”). A few lines veer toward abstract summary or slogan (“The weight of unspoken words had followed them past death”), but they don’t seriously muddy clarity or pacing. The overall rhythm and syntax are smooth enough to meet a high-bar literary standard, even if they occasionally lean into familiar lyrical phrasings.</comment>
<question>8</question><grade>8.3</grade><comment>The fusion of beekeeping, drought, and ghosts as embodiments of repressed speech feels fresh, and the stained-glass reliquary of forbidden words is a strong, memorable device. The conceptual move that ghosts are made of unsaid words and can be released by witnessing rather than exorcism is an elegant twist on haunting. At the same time, the core thesis about the harm of silence is familiar and expressed somewhat bluntly, which keeps the piece from feeling fully surprising. Still, the specific imagery and the reimagined role of “keeper” elevate it above standard speculative allegory.</comment>
