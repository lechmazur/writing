However, the ticking clock “before they found the bodies” never meaningfully constrains or redirects on-page choices, so the external stakes feel underused. The cost of his decision remains mostly conceptual rather than concretely pressed in the final beat.</comment>
<question>3</question><grade>7.5</grade><comment>The lighthouse prison, jagged reef, and copper-lit chamber are vivid and coherently imagined, giving the piece a strong, moody atmosphere. However, the setting rarely constrains tactics beyond providing a staircase and a pedestal; the reef and storm-battered walls don’t materially affect choice. The final outdoor moment gestures toward setting-charged freedom, but the environment’s pressure could be sharper.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>6.3</grade><comment>The declared stakes—Elias’s name, the vanished guards, the danger of becoming an “offering”—are strong on paper and interwoven with his inheritance. <question>5</question><grade>6.5</grade><comment>Themes of truth versus silence and inherited guilt are coherent and organically tied to the boy-who-cried-wolf lineage and the hush. The piece gestures toward ambiguity about the cost of truth but doesn’t quite let that unease linger.</comment>

<question>7</question><grade>6.8</grade><comment>The prose is grammatical and generally fluid, with some striking images and no major syntactic or clarity problems. Line-level, it’s solid and readable but not yet honed to elite compression and precision.</comment>
<question>8</question><grade>7.4</grade><comment>Reframing the boy-who-cried-wolf myth through a lighthouse prison guarding a literal hush is a fresh, engaging conceptual move. However, the resolution—discover the cost, reject the dangerous power, claim truth—is familiar in shape and somewhat tidily expressed. The piece shows clear imaginative ambition but doesn’t fully exploit the darker or stranger implications of its own premise.</comment>
