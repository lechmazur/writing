<question>2</question><grade>7.2</grade><comment>Structure is clean: single scene, clear orientation, a coherent accumulative drive from entering the booth to leaving with the strip. Closure lands as a quiet Resonant Reconfiguration: she exits with a new sense of purpose and responsibility. <question>3</question><grade>7.6</grade><comment>The vintage movie palace and projection booth are vividly rendered—dusty reels, cracked window, “time capsule” feel—and consistently filtered through Elara’s sensibility. The setting charges the final image by making the theater feel like a living repository of emotion. However, the space doesn’t significantly constrain her choices; it’s more evocative backdrop than active pressure.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>6.4</grade><comment>The dominant stake—discovering where unused love is stored—is signaled early and followed through to a conceptual answer. Conflict is largely internal and contemplative; the pressure point is her decision whether to leave the film or take on its burden, which does create a modest tightening. Still, stakes remain abstract and are never tested against a concrete opposing force (time, danger, other people, self-sabotage), so tension plateaus rather than escalating. The absence of a visible price for taking the strip keeps the final choice from feeling fully costly.</comment>

The final decision to carry the strip and maybe “release the love it held” produces a quiet, earnest aftereffect. Some thematic statements are a bit on-the-nose (“She had found what she was looking for”), and the moral complexity around using or altering others’ emotions is underdeveloped.</comment>

<question>8</question><grade>7.3</grade><comment>The blend of occult psychometry, a vintage projection booth, and a film of unlabeled goodbye letters as a repository of unused love is an appealing and relatively fresh synthesis.
