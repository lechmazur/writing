The culminating choice to walk away rather than re-engage the house functions as a quiet renunciation but the emotional cost of that refusal remains relatively understated. <question>2</question><grade>7.8</grade><comment>Orientation is clean and early: Elias, bay, breathing houses, then a clear present-time mission with the shard and the specific target house. Closure lands as a conceptual repricing: houses listening to themselves and the shard as “permission to stop,” with Elias’ departure fixing a before/after. Stakes and cost are relatively quiet and mostly conceptual, which works, but a slightly sharper sense of what his new knowledge forecloses would deepen the structural punch.</comment>
<question>3</question><grade>8.4</grade><comment>The quiet bay in spring, the streetlights that “daydream,” and the leaning clapboard house all build a cohesive, tactile atmosphere that directly supports the story’s eerie restraint. The orris shard as a small, charged object is integrated into setting as well as plot, especially once it becomes a “shard of hush.” The setting rarely constrains tactics, but it strongly charges tone and the final image of the bay waiting.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>7.2</grade><comment>The dominant stake—Elias’s attempt to neutralize or comprehend a living house—is legible, and the insertion of the shard under the door is a clear tightening move that leads to the house’s alarming “pause.” Internal and external tensions intersect in the moment he recognizes he has granted the house a different kind of agency than intended. However, the pressure remains more contemplative than fraught, and the cost of his choice to leave the paused-then-resuming house behind is implied rather than viscerally felt. A touch more sense of risk (ethical, existential, or physical) in that central experiment would heighten stakes.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>8.5</grade><comment>Themes of creeping normality, sentient spaces, and the ethics of imposing stillness on living systems emerge organically from images and events rather than overt statement. The final refusal to seek further answers—“some questions weren’t meant to be answered”—lands as earned unease rather than a pat moral. The piece could press the personal cost of that stance slightly harder, but the thematic architecture is already robust and resonant.</comment>

Psychic distance is well managed, comfortably close to Elias without omniscient leakage.
