Closure is a resonant reconfiguration: she converts private insomnia into a purposeful, ongoing act of generosity, with the cost (sleepless effort in a poisoned city) implied. The main limitation is that no turn sharply tightens options or stakes; escalation is more of a gentle deepening than a clear hinge.</comment>

The micro-setting meaningfully charges the final image—the moonlight through glass, the warm greenhouse against a rotting city, the bone-handled brush as relic. It stops just short of 9-band only because setting doesn’t significantly constrain specific tactical choices beyond shaping her isolation and ritual.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>6.8</grade><comment>The dominant stake is internal: whether she can transform sleeplessness and a poisoned city into proof (for herself and others) that kindness persists. There is a soft but real tightening when she shifts from private ritual to the decision to leave paintings for strangers, turning memory into risked contact. Still, conflict remains low-grade and largely unopposed—no concrete counter-force tests her commitment, so costs and trade-offs stay mostly conceptual.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>7.8</grade><comment>Theme centers on kindness as a “lost language” and the possibility of re-teaching it through art and anonymous generosity. The line “She had read once that kindness was a language” edges toward explicit statement but is grounded enough in her worldview to avoid feeling like a tacked-on moral. The piece could push complexity further by staging a sharper doubt or failure; as is, the hopefulness, while moving, borders on tidy.</comment>
