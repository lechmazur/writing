The environment meaningfully participates in the climax when trees rearrange into a geometric grid and equations hang in the air, turning landscape into the visualization of his sought-after “math.” The forest is more a charged stage than an active constraint on choice, which keeps this just shy of the top band.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>6.6</grade><comment>The dominant stake—can Elias find an ethically legible limit to cruelty—is clear, but conflict is mostly internal and relatively under-contested on the page. There is one major deepening—the drink that shifts perception and grants knowledge—but no strong reversal or narrowing of options beyond that conceptual turn. The cost of what he learns (or of accepting it) is more implied than felt as an immediate sacrifice.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>7.2</grade><comment>The thematic core—whether mercy can be quantified, and how trauma distorts one’s calculus of cruelty and absolution—is integrated through recurring math imagery, the “Calculator” title, and the engraved numbers on the strap. However, some lines verge on thesis-statement territory (“His motivation was simple, if impossible: to discover the mathematics of mercy”; “The mathematics of mercy wasn’t about absolution”) which flattens subtext into declared theme. More dramatized ambiguity around how he might apply this knowledge would give the ending a sharper, less didactic resonance.</comment>
