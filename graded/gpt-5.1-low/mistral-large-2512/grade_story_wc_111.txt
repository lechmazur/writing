<question>4</question><grade>7.6</grade><comment>The dominant stake—whether the garden sees and “knows” him, and whether he can mend his self-deception about that—remains coherent and gently tightens as the garden begins to respond to his marble placements. However, pressure never feels truly acute; the garden’s “judgment” and any risk or consequence of failure stay relatively abstract. The ending’s renunciation has cost, but the conflict could bear a slightly sharper test of his need for control to reach a higher band.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>7.8</grade><comment>Themes of control vs tending, mutual recognition between creator and creation, and “mended deception” about one’s own desires are woven effectively through marbles, roses, and the final choice to leave breadcrumbs. The last two sentences (“The garden had never been his to control, only to tend. / And in the end, that was the only victory that mattered.”) tilt toward explicit moralizing, slightly flattening the otherwise subtle resonance. Earlier particulars already articulate this insight; trusting them more would elevate the thematic finish.</comment>
