Closure is solidly a Resonant Reconfiguration: they agree to “start over,” with the projector relighting as a clear before/after image. The main constraint is that the cost of this shift is more implied than dramatically tested on the page, keeping the overall drive on the quieter situational side.</comment>
<question>3</question><grade>7.6</grade><comment>The cinema and projector are vividly and economically evoked, with textures of rain, celluloid, and empty lobby contributing to a hushed, liminal atmosphere. The lens and ticket stubs work as micro-setting objects that recur and are folded into action and meaning at the end. This is strong atmosphere, though the world around the cinema feels more decorative than functional in terms of altering tactics.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>7.2</grade><comment>The central conflict—whether these two can trust each other enough to drop their defensive secrecy—is clear and sustained. Pressure escalates modestly from a procedural breach (missing lens) to a moral/emotional breach (systematic eavesdropping) to a mutual admission of fear and weariness. The narrative earns one meaningful tightening when Elias spreads the tickets and admits “I’ve been eavesdropping,” forcing Mira to reinterpret him and herself. Still, the stakes remain small-scale and largely interior; there’s little sense of a real alternative path being chosen against with visible cost.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>7.5</grade><comment>Trust, secrecy, and the idea of being “freed by honesty” emerge organically from their occupations and habits of control. Subtext in lines like “Would you have told me the truth?” and “Now I think we’re both tired of pretending” works, though it borders on stating the theme. The piece opts for a relatively neat moral resolution, with limited lingering ambiguity or bite.</comment>

Psychic distance shifts gently closer at emotional beats without head-hopping or register slips.</comment>
<question>7</question><grade>8.0</grade><comment>Line-level craft is polished: sentences are clear, varied in rhythm, and usually do more than one job in terms of mood, character, and motion. <question>8</question><grade>7.4</grade><comment>The combination of an old cinema, a registrar, and a projectionist who literally maps overheard confessions on ticket stubs offers a reasonably fresh container for a trust story. However, the underlying emotional arc—two guarded coworkers admit mutual mistrust and decide to be honest—is familiar and resolved in a conventional way. The piece feels thoughtfully crafted rather than truly risk-taking or formally surprising.</comment>
