<question>4</question><grade>6.8</grade><comment>The dominant stake—whether Liora will keep trying to control magic or accept a more vulnerable, intuitive relationship to it—is clear but only mildly pressured. The one major tightening is her admission that “some things could not be measured,” which leads to burying the crystal, but this costs her mostly pride and worldview, not something palpably at risk in the moment. The conflict is thoughtful and coherent, but escalation is internal and gentle, never truly cornering her.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>7.0</grade><comment>The story’s thematic argument—about the limits of control, and wonder as something to be felt rather than solved—is coherent and carried through the fire backstory, the starlings’ shifting patterns, and the crystal’s fate. The final image of the shattering crystal and departing starlings does offer some resonant subtext about release and reconfiguration. Overall the piece is thematically clear and sincere but a bit over-articulated for top-tier literary subtlety.</comment>

A few thematic lines slip into a more generic, essayistic register, but they don’t fundamentally break the voice.</comment>

Information flows smoothly, and both opening and closing lines land with a satisfying sonic and imagistic cadence.</comment>
