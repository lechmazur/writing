The stained glass is not just backdrop; it is the mechanism of discovery and a key visual at the story’s center. The final return to the pavilion with the new flower painted under twilight shows the environment charging the closing image effectively.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>7.2</grade><comment>The dominant stake—Liora’s need to understand why flowers bloom at night and what that means for her art and self—is clear from early on. There is no real external opposition or strongly felt alternative path, so the “reckoning” is more a deepening than a contested choice. That restraint fits the mode but keeps the stakes from feeling truly sharp or costly.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>7.8</grade><comment>Themes of darkness as its own illumination and thaw/endings as beginnings are woven coherently through postcards, snowmelt, and night-blooming flowers. However, the theme is stated somewhat directly rather than left entirely to image and implication, edging toward aphorism. The emotional aftereffect is gentle and lingering rather than piercingly complex, but it’s honest and well integrated.</comment>

Structural choices are conservative rather than formally daring, yet the accumulative pattern of motifs is handled with care. The piece reads as a well-executed refinement of familiar concerns rather than a formally or conceptually groundbreaking work.</comment>
