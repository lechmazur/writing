<question>3</question><grade>8.8</grade><comment>The grotto above the clouds, with its volcanic stone, condensation, and wind-swept ferns, is richly specific and integrated with action. Details like “ghost-impressions of green against the gray pumice” carry thematic as well as sensory weight without feeling like a mere postcard. The setting also charges the final image of rebuilding in a place that may vanish.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>7.6</grade><comment>The central stake—whether the last monarch can find shelter and continue its journey—tightens well when the wind disrupts both Elara’s work and the butterfly’s path. However, her extreme competence and lack of serious doubt keep the pressure from feeling truly risky or costly; success feels almost inevitable once she acts. The cost is primarily the loss of three days’ work, which she accepts with little on-page struggle.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>8.0</grade><comment>Themes of overlooked histories, transient monuments, and the ethics of subtle, temporary care are woven through objects (moth wing, vines, monarch) and situation rather than bolted on. That said, some lines toward the end explicitly articulate the thesis (“the act of building was itself the honor,” “refused to let silence have the final word”), nudging into mild moralizing instead of trusting the images. The resonance is still strong, but the piece would be sharper if it leaned a bit more on implication.</comment>

The emotional arc—rescuing a single fragile creature as an act of honoring vast overlooked histories—is non-obvious yet feels inevitable in hindsight.
