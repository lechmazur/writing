The environment operates more as a resonant stage than as a hard constraint on choices, but it consistently mirrors and deepens the theme of transformation. World rules around the quasi-mystical elements feel internally coherent for this lyrical, slightly fabulist frame.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>6.8</grade><comment>The dominant stake—Mara’s need to understand transformation—remains largely internal and contemplative, with minimal on-page opposition. However, she never faces a concrete, pressured trade-off or a hard renunciation; the “price” of not keeping beauty is stated rather than fought over. Escalation is therefore more about increasing symbolic density than about narrowing her options under visible strain.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>7.9</grade><comment>Thematic material around transformation, non-possession of beauty, and unintended prophecy is rich and well-sutured to images of jars, fire, and celestial motion. However, the explicit thesis spelled out by lightning bugs—“transformation is not a destination but a continuous becoming…”—reads like direct moralizing and undercuts some subtextual power. The piece would feel more elite-tier if it trusted its images to carry that insight instead of articulating it so literally.</comment>

The language leans elevated but feels intentional rather than accidentally academic or mismatched. It’s not quite singular enough to be unforgettable, but it’s well-crafted and highly competent.</comment>
