<question>4</question><grade>6.9</grade><comment>The dominant stake—recovering her mother’s deleted memory and locating where forgotten things go—is clearly stated, and the move into the densest gravity pocket is a meaningful tightening. Her agency is mostly in deciding to descend and then to touch the crystal, both of which feel foregone rather than contested. <question>5</question><grade>8.2</grade><comment>Thematic threads of grief, memory, erasure, and custodianship are woven through particulars—the whale’s death, exhumed grave flowers, salt as crystallized tears, and the question of where deleted memories go. Subtext about communal shame and buried truths (“shameful moments… preserved in crystalline form”) enriches the piece beyond her individual arc. It flirts with explicitness in lines that near-thesis territory, but mostly lets image and situation carry meaning.</comment>

<question>8</question><grade>8.7</grade><comment>The synthesis of concepts—beached whale as catalyst for human memories, salt crystals as repositories, pockets of gravity mapping forgetting, coffee-ground divination—is striking and feels idiosyncratic rather than templated. The story commits to its weird ecology of memory and follows through to a thematically charged role revelation. Its main limitation, originality-wise, is that the central emotional arc (child seeking mother’s hidden truth) is familiar, even if the worldbuilding around it is inventive.</comment>
