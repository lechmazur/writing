<question>2</question><grade>7.8</grade><comment>The accumulative structure is coherent: arrival at the railing → approach to the door → entry and kneeling → charged final stance before the village’s future return. The temporal and spatial anchors (lighthouse prison, impending discovery of the bodies) are clear and stable, with no orientation debt.</comment>
<question>3</question><grade>8.5</grade><comment>The lighthouse prison, jagged reef, and copperlight are vividly rendered and consistently filtered through Marcus’s sensibility, giving the piece a strong, haunted atmosphere. The setting is not postcard backdrop: the lighthouse’s isolation as a repository of “unwanted truths” and the reef as grave actively frame the stakes and the final image. The confessional cell within the tower and the narrow door’s scuffs are concrete, recurring anchors that charge the ending.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>6.8</grade><comment>The dominant stake—whether Marcus will enact his father’s truth rather than collude with silence—is legible but plays out with limited on-page escalation. Pressure feels more atmospheric than actively constraining, so the cost of his stance is more implied than viscerally felt.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>8.2</grade><comment>Themes of inherited myth, the social cost of truth-telling, and story as a form of justice are strong and woven through concrete motifs (the lattice, the lighthouse as quarantine for truths, the village’s disbelief). At times the text edges toward explicit statement (“He was here to write the ending that had been stolen from them both”), which slightly softens the subtext’s potency.</comment>

There’s no head-hopping, and the brief mention of the keeper at the end doesn’t disturb the tight focalization through Marcus.</comment>

The emotional logic—choosing to inherit “story instead” of silence—feels specific rather than generic, even within familiar myth-retelling territory.</comment>
