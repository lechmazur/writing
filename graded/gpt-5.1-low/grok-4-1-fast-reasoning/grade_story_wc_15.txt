<question>2</question><grade>7.5</grade><comment>The structure is clean: storm → ruined village → decision to enter the labyrinth → sleepwalking sewing sequence → dawn reconfiguration of the world. However, the risk of becoming one of “the lost” remains mostly theoretical; the narrative never tightens options or makes the danger bite in real time. The result is a satisfying conceptual arc that lacks some lived tension and concrete cost.</comment>

Sensory detail (crimson walls, sea brine, glowing glyphs) is selective and purposeful, supporting the mythic, elegiac tone. While the setting does not heavily constrain tactical choices, it charges the final image and is integral to the story’s logic.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>6.8</grade><comment>The dominant stake—whether Elara can transform a tabooed labyrinth of loss into a communal path of renewal—is present and conceptually strong. There is one main deepening when she enters despite the broken precedents and succumbs to sleepwalking, but after that the conflict flattens into an unopposed, almost effortless sewing sequence. The threat of becoming lost never escalates into a specific on-page dilemma or sacrifice, which keeps the price of her defiance abstract. The resolution feels thematically earned but not hard-won.</comment>

Overall, though, the piece earns its hopeful, complex repricing of the old order.</comment>
