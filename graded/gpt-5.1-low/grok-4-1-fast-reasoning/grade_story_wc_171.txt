Orientation (who/where/when) is anchored early via terrace/midsummer/Elara’s role, and the ritual mechanics remain consistent. The final image—stars resuming their waltz over “the flawed beauty of mended fractures”—clearly depends on this environment and its cosmology. Occasional lushness borders on decorative, but overall the specificity is purposeful and thematically tuned.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>7.0</grade><comment>The dominant stake—ending the Forever War/redemption—is announced early and paid off in her blood sealing the accord. Internal conflict exists but is concentrated late; we don’t see her nearly falter or consider refusal in a way that would heighten tension.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>7.8</grade><comment>Themes of the price of redemption, flawed beauty, and turning ideals into practice emerge naturally from the ritual, her past mistake, and the cracked terrace imagery. Some lines verge on thesis-statement (“to anchor ideals in practice,” “To muster peace, we must first confront the wars within”), edging toward overt commentary rather than pure scene-driven resonance. Still, the ending image—peace as a fragile flower amid thorns—lands with honest, non-tidy complexity.</comment>

While the basic trope of “the negotiator paying in blood to end a great war” isn’t wholly new, the story’s particular constellation of objects, cosmology, and guilt-driven redemption reads as its own.
