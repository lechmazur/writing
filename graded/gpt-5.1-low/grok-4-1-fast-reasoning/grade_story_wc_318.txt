<question>4</question><grade>7.4</grade><comment>The dominant stake—can Elias transmute the household’s fossilized grief into laughter before the hourglass runs out—is clear and tightened by the temporal limit and by gradually intensifying winds and lullabies. The primary cost is temporal and existential: he succeeds but is snapped back into solitude and silence, which is felt but not agonized over. The conflict leans meditative rather than sharply oppositional, so tension lives more in cadence than in genuine risk of failure.</comment>

The final hush conceptually reprices silence as earned peace rather than erasure, giving the ending emotional afterglow. The treatment is more elegiac than interrogative—less moral complexity than beautifully executed consolation.</comment>
