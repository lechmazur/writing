The cost is mostly internal and conveyed in conceptual terms, with little sense that the unmaking imperils him or others beyond abstract destiny. This is more than postcard description; the space feels like a chosen crucible for absolution. At times the density of description skirts excess, but it remains legible and integrated with the scene.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>6.8</grade><comment>The core stake—whether Elias will successfully sever his bond to the treason and truly forgive the unfinished—is clear, and the unlinking of each specific edict provides a nice structural beat. The stalactites dripping faster and the aurora’s “approval or warning” hint at rising pressure but never solidify into a felt dilemma or risk. As a result, the ending satisfies quietly but without much teeth.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>7.0</grade><comment>The thematic core—revenge vs. forgiveness, unfinished mentorship, turning deception into deliverance—is strong and coherent, with the rose thorn and “calibrated treason” as recurring vehicles. The closing lines neatly articulate the lesson instead of trusting the snapped chain and changed use of the thorn to imply it. Resonance is present but could be more subtextual and ambivalent.</comment>
