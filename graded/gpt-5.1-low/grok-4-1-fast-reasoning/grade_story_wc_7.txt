<question>2</question><grade>7.2</grade><comment>The causal spine is clean: alignment → pruning → planting → first sprout → envisioned legacy; the piece builds to a clear closure image with conceptual cost (defying the Regime, risking erasure). That said, much of the Regime conflict and the danger around the act is told rather than dramatized, so the cost remains mostly conceptual, not felt. The environment meaningfully constrains choice—this place is both illegal and uniquely fertile under the alignment—and charges the final image of the sprouting seed. A bit more interplay between physical danger of the location and his actions could push this into truly exceptional territory.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>6.8</grade><comment>The dominant stake—whether Elias will successfully seed a quiet, mind-altering rebellion—is clear and his actions steadily move toward that. The main tightening is the need to prune before planting as the alignment peaks, which interweaves external and internal stakes decently. However, there is minimal resistance: no concrete threat of discovery or failure, and no visible sacrifice beyond labor and risk implied in exposition. Because nothing on the page seriously threatens his plan or forces a trade-off, tension feels muted.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>7.5</grade><comment>Themes of “beauty from ruin,” memory, and gentle rebellion via neuroplastic change are woven thoughtfully through pruning, cooking, and outlawed growth. Still, the closing legacy-focused vision reweights earlier details in a satisfying, non-trivial way.</comment>

Despite that, POV control is stable and rhythmically varied enough to feel polished.</comment>
