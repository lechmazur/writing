<question>3</question><grade>8.5</grade><comment>The treetop village, tidal marshes, and perpetual sway are richly realized and tightly tied to Elara’s goal of “serenity within motion.” Environmental details—pots lashed to platforms, gales rocking walkways, hammock-homes like pods—are filtered through her sensory and emotional lens, not presented as neutral catalog. The setting constrains her tactics (the flower must survive constant motion) and charges the final image where she achieves poise within this environment rather than escaping it. A slight tendency toward decorative neologisms (“tide’s thought”) keeps it just shy of top-tier precision.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>7.4</grade><comment>The dominant stake—can Elara endure the corpse flower’s stench and extract a deeper serenity from it—is clearly signaled and escalates from faint rot-scent to overwhelming bloom. The turning point at the full eruption, where she collapses yet keeps sketching and finds meaning inside the assault, credibly tests her initial ambition. Internal and external pressures (her nose, the village’s motion, social doubt) interweave. However, the danger feels largely experiential rather than consequential—there’s little sense that failure would carry lasting personal or communal loss beyond a missed insight—so the cost reads more thematic than sharply dramatized.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>7.2</grade><comment>Themes of decay-as-nourishment and serenity as poise within change emerge organically from the corpse flower’s lifecycle and the swaying village. Yet the piece lapses into explicit moral statement at key beats (“the stench was life's truth, decay fueling rebirth,” “impermanence births peace”), dulling subtext and making the epiphany feel slightly didactic. Allowing more of that wisdom to remain implied in image and action would deepen resonance.</comment>
