Closure lands as a conceptual repricing and charged poise: the city shifts in her mind, and she chooses to hide the stamp, holding a new, heavier relationship to the archive. <question>4</question><grade>7.6</grade><comment>The dominant stake—whether the official narrative of the flood is true—is clearly signaled and deepens as documents contradict one another. However, pressure remains largely intellectual; no external counterforce or immediate risk sharpens her choices beyond the abstract danger of knowledge. The final act of hiding the stamp is a form of earned stasis, but the price of that stance could be more concretely dramatized.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>8.7</grade><comment>Themes of authorship, power, and the politics of record-keeping emerge organically from Ossa’s method and discoveries, not from authorial lecturing. Recurring motifs—the stamp, hum vs stillness, ink and ledgers—shift in meaning by the end, supporting a strong conceptual repricing. If anything, the piece edges toward thematic saturation; a single beat of resistance to Ossa’s emerging thesis might add even more complexity.</comment>
<question>6</question><grade>8.7</grade><comment>The first-person voice is assured, lyrically precise, and consistent with a hyper-attentive archivist in a ruined city; diction like “logical page indexer” and “alternative hypothesis” feels character-true.
