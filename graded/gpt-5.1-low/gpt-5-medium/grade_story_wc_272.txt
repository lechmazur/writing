<question>2</question><grade>9.0</grade><comment>The structure is coherent and accumulative: discovery of the threaded tarot → reconstruction of the keeper’s intent → descent to the failed mechanism → repurposing the threads into a functional wick → reillumination and a new ledger entry. Closure is a resonant reconfiguration: the before/after is legible in the relit beam, her new identity as keeper, and the tower itself seeming to widen rather than close in. The cost—taking responsibility in solitude, facing the dark instead of fleeing it—is implicit but palpable in those final acts.</comment>
<question>3</question><grade>9.2</grade><comment>The lighthouse library and gearhouse are rendered with vivid, selective detail (“stones sweating brine,” “rust ticked like a clock that no longer knew time”), and setting is always filtered through Mara’s sensibility. Environment meaningfully constrains and shapes choices: the forgotten ledger, the layout of stairs, the perished wick, and the threaded cards all emerge from and depend on this place. Weather and architecture apply pressure (“the weather thickened the windows… the hour when the walls close in”) and then are reconfigured by the relit beam and the sea’s “breath unclench[ing].” The final image—“column of brightness that stitched her to the weather”—is inseparable from this specific micro-world.</comment>

Her agency drives the turn: choosing to descend, to interpret the keeper’s redesign as a message, and to repurpose the threaded system into a new wick. The conflict remains primarily internal and atmospheric, so the danger never feels life-threatening, but the emotional stakes land.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>9.0</grade><comment>Theme emerges organically through action and imagery rather than statement: masks vs faces, necessary darkness vs guiding light, fragmentation vs threading/keeping. The tarot threads, ledger line (“Only by untangling threads can the light know where to go”), and her portraiture all converge in the act of remaking the wick without burning the cards, a precise embodiment of how to handle broken fates and identities. The final inscription—“I will keep the light tonight, and I will keep the faces that come to it”—reweights everything that came before without lapsing into moralizing; it’s a pledge, not a thesis. Ambiguity remains (how long she stays, what it costs her life in the city), but the emotional and conceptual stance is satisfyingly crystallized.</comment>

A slight reduction in metaphor-per-sentence density in one or two mid-story paragraphs could sharpen momentum further.</comment>

While lighthouses and tarot are familiar motifs, their integration here is distinctive and memorable.</comment>
