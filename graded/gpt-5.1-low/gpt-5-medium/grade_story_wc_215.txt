Causality is subtle but coherent (his past clauses → wasteland → covenant → queen’s demand), and the cost is legible in his acceptance of pain and responsibility. The stained glass box and comb function as micro-setting tools that shape his rituals and choices. World rules around ghosts, wind, and light remain consistent and legible without exposition.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>8.4</grade><comment>The dominant stake—whether he will accept an ethically costly role as custodian of unspeakable loss—is clear and escalates when the queen-ghost arrives as the “other party” to the covenant. The price (accepting his complicity and ongoing duty, plus immediate physical pain) is felt rather than merely declared. Because the conflict is primarily interior and situational, the sense of narrowing options is strong but not quite brutal, keeping it just below the highest band.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>8.7</grade><comment>The piece powerfully explores witness, culpability, and the preservation of the unsayable through concrete motifs (contracts, pesticides, bees, the archive, “mute eloquence”). Lines like “The box did not cure the world, but it taught the world to listen” flirt with aphorism but feel earned by prior scene work. Thematic complexity is high, though a touch more resistance or failure in his role would deepen the moral texture further.</comment>

The lushness may be marginally more uniform than ideal for peak-pressure beats, but clarity and closure remain fully intact.</comment>
