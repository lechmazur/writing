<question>3</question><grade>9.0</grade><comment>The lighthouse, wind, chimes, and height above the waking town are all vividly rendered and tightly integrated with the choice being made. The sense of dawn, breeze, and weather as moral climate is both concrete and thematically functional.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>8.5</grade><comment>The dominant stake—whether to confess morally tainted but life-saving bribes before an election—is sharply drawn and present early. The ultimate stance (confess, return money, name names, change tax policy) clearly forecloses easier paths of silence or self-exonerating martyrdom. The only thing keeping this just below the very top is that Lark’s resistance to the chosen path is implied more than dramatized, slightly softening moment-to-moment tension.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>8.9</grade><comment>Theme emerges organically from events and images: doubt as a service, politics as performance, rescue entangled with rot, and the refusal of “flawless fiction.” The wind-chime method, cloak of doubt, and hollow mirror shard all develop a coherent argument about embracing nuance and visible cost rather than purity or denial. The ending’s plan to “accept both the rescue and the rot, and to demand a better bargain” offers a resonant, non-literal answer to the central question without slipping into didacticism. A few near-thesis lines (“welcome nuance into certainty”) slightly foreground theme-labels but do not undercut the earned complexity.</comment>
