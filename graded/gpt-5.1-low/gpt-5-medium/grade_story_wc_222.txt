<question>3</question><grade>8.8</grade><comment>The icebound spire, chessboard of seasons, and wrong sky are richly imagined and consistently leveraged to shape action—the protagonist must engage this specific environment and its rules to fix the world. A hair more tactile interaction with the spire’s physical risk could deepen the embodied stakes, but as is, the atmosphere is outstanding.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>8.3</grade><comment>The dominant stake—resetting weather/sky without repeating the wardens’ harms—is clear and coherently tied to each move on the board. Pressure escalates as the protagonist uncovers ethically fraught secrets (feast-protecting drought, hoarded warmth) and must respond with counter-promises, culminating in the queen’s letter and the unpaid commitment to future mending. The conflict is primarily moral/philosophical rather than bodily or social, which suits the piece but leaves the sense of “what could go terribly wrong” a bit abstract. Still, the final acceptance of an open-ended obligation functions as a genuine narrowing of options.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>8.8</grade><comment>Themes of reparative theft, wonder as a guiding ethic, and refusing wages for necessary mending emerge organically from the board’s revelations and the protagonist’s responses. The wind’s letter and the closing image of “carrying play like prayer” offer a resonant, non-didactic response to those questions. A touch more acknowledgment of the lingering moral gray around rewriting others’ vows would push this even higher.</comment>

At times the lyricism risks uniformity of tone, but it never breaks POV plausibility or clarity.</comment>
