The locust shell and bridge work as micro-settings/tools that charge key beats and the ending image. World rules feel consistent within the fable-like frame.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>8.4</grade><comment>The dominant stake—risking punishment to awaken the orchard and city—is signaled early and tightened when the statutes approach and the crowd gathers, turning a private act into a public confrontation. The protagonist’s agency drives the escalation: adjusting the softness of playing, adding the locust shell, persisting when a statue reaches for her wrist. The tension between being silenced vs catalyzing a larger awakening is clear, though the story stops at the first opening rather than dramatizing a concrete repercussion or specific loss. That keeps the pressure strong but just shy of brutal.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>8.3</grade><comment>Thematic throughline—beauty versus mechanical control, breaking free as deeper attention rather than flight—is rich and well-integrated with images (statues, orchard, locust shell, heart-signal). The main ding is explicitness in lines like “The message was simple: breaking free is not an escape but a fuller attention,” which pushes toward thesis rather than pure scene-driven implication. Even so, the final image of beauty “teaching stone to dream in daylight” offers a resonant, non-literal answer to the story’s question.</comment>

POV distance is stable, with no head-hopping or implausible knowledge, and the lyric compression is taught early and sustained. Occasional aphoristic lines brush the edge of authorial commentary but remain believable as this narrator’s idiom.</comment>

A few formulations flirt with abstraction (“map of wounds that become windows”) and could be pruned or grounded, but they do not obscure causality or the closure image. Overall, line-level craft is very strong and publication-ready with only light tightening.</comment>

Recurring objects (locust shell, halos, bridge) and the mechanical city’s reaction prevent this from being mere mood-piece; it follows its own implications.
