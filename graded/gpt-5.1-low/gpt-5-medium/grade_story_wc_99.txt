Weather and water mirror interior states without feeling like blunt allegory. The final bow to the tide and diffusion of sea-blue into the garden miniatures shows setting as an active thematic engine, not backdrop.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>8.4</grade><comment>The central stake—whether the narrator will continue to live inside unspoken regret or risk changing by voicing apology—is clear and sharpened by the compass/lace instructions. Pressure escalates as they obey the ritual, arrive at the cove where they failed before, and must decide whether to finally speak. The decision to speak “not to summon forgiveness, but to find the color” and to alter their teaching approach is a credible, costly shift. The conflict remains primarily internal and quiet, so the sense of jeopardy never becomes intense, but for this mode it’s sufficiently tight.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>9.1</grade><comment>Themes of control vs surrender, the ethics of “pruning” others’ storms, and how regret can be acknowledged without being erased emerge organically from specific images and choices. The final image reweights everything prior without any explicit thesis-stating.</comment>

The ghostly-maritime and horticultural registers intertwine in a way that would be hard to transplant to another premise without losing essence. The risk of dense lyricism pays off in a memorable, specific vision rather than generalized “poetic” mood.</comment>
