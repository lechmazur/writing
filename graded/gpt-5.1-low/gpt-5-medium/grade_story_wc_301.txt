<question>4</question><grade>8.8</grade><comment>The dominant stake—whether she can renegotiate her guilt and relationship to the river—is clearly signaled and escalates as she moves from symbolic spaces (station) to literal disaster sites (sluice, shallow). The only slight limit is that opposition remains purely internal and environmental, though it’s handled with enough tension not to feel static.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>8.9</grade><comment>Themes of responsibility, the limits of modeling/control, and the danger of turning the natural world into a flattering mirror emerge organically from concrete acts (“River does what it was asked to do” shifting from verdict to instruction is especially strong). The final act of writing that the river “was itself” offers a resonant, non-didactic answer to the central question. A few lines verge on explicit thesis but stay anchored enough in image and context to avoid sermonizing.</comment>
