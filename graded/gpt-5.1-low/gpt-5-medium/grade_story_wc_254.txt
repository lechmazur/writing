<question>3</question><grade>8.9</grade><comment>The setting is extraordinary and integral: the cobalt atrium, humming quartz colonnade, and frozen lake are not just pretty backdrops but embodiments of the boundary at stake. Sensory specificity (mineral air, ink, “snow pressed into pages”) is high and always filtered through the communal consciousness. The final image of moving from floor to lake with the colonnade’s hum on their backs shows the setting actively charging closure.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>8.6</grade><comment>The central stake—whether this formerly banned collective expression can be embodied without collapsing back into fear—is clear and steadily tightened. Escalation is subtle but definite: from listening for permission, to shared breathing, to crossing the inlaid line, to the guard’s renunciation, to stepping onto the lake’s ice. Agency is centered in the poet’s invitations and the group’s incremental risks, with visible emotional cost in leaving the safety of the old law and roles. While the conflict remains primarily internal/ritualistic, it never feels static.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>8.7</grade><comment>Themes of fear vs. meaning, boundaries vs. porousness, and an ethics of presence emerge organically from concrete acts (paraphrasing the decree, touching thresholds, crossing ice). A few lines border on explicit thesis (“but it meant, Keep afraid, lest meaning live”), but they’re embedded in image-rich language rather than bald moralizing. The ending offers a non-literal answer—that change should be humming, porous, and responsible—without closing interpretive space.</comment>

The narrative distance is well modulated, close enough to feel bodily (“tiny trustworthy earthquakes under each name”) while still capable of mythic phrasing.
