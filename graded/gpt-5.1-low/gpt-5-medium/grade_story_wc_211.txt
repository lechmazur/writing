<question>3</question><grade>8.8</grade><comment>The orchard-on-a-skyscraper setting is richly specific and thematically aligned, and its rules remain consistent. Environmental details—foghorn, rainwater “remembering roofs,” fruit that might drop, roots that could crack the rooftop—directly shape stakes and options. The final image of the orchard relaxing and the fruit “keeping their grip” clearly charges the ending. A hair more tactile interaction with the city below could deepen the physical sense of risk.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>8.7</grade><comment>The central conflict—whether to record a dangerous prophecy—is sharply framed and tightened by the specific consequences for both orchard and child. Escalation is credible: what begins as routine archival work becomes a personal warning from the narrator’s own future voice. The final choice to “trust unrecording” is a genuine trade-off between vocation and compassion, with visible cost to the narrator’s identity as a scribe. Because the pressure is almost entirely internal, a touch more external resistance or friction could amplify tension even further.</comment>

The pinned-breath metaphor returns in the imagined child, tying vocation, trauma, and responsibility together. The complexity around duty and omission lingers, with no tidy answer, which suits the piece’s ambition.</comment>

Psychic distance is well-managed, allowing both ritual description and felt hesitation at the moment of refusal. There are no POV slips or explanatory intrusions that break the spell.</comment>
