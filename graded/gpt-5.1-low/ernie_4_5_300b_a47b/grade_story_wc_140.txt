The drive lens is primarily causal with some accumulative texturing, and it holds. The recurring physical world (containers, harbor, tide) also mirrors his interior erasure/fragmentation without becoming heavy-handed allegory. At this length, the setting feels lived-in and integral rather than ornamental.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>7.2</grade><comment>The dominant stake—recovering his erased identity—runs cleanly through the piece, with escalation from passive collecting to targeted pursuit to a personal, high-stakes revelation. The storm and rising tide provide an external tightening that forces movement and creates a sense of danger. However, the cost at the end is primarily conceptual/emotional; there’s little concrete loss or foreclosed option dramatized beyond the shock of knowledge and implied future risk. The conspiracy hook gestures toward deeper stakes but isn’t yet pressure-tested on the page.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>7.4</grade><comment>Themes of erased histories, memory as bricolage, and the ethics of “remembering” versus “poisoning rats” recur through specific images (label, letters, locket, tide). The final configuration—that his own life was deliberately buried—reweights earlier fragments effectively. The late move toward a named “conspiracy” and revenge tilts slightly toward genre setup over a fully explored moral or existential reckoning, which blunts some potential complexity.</comment>

There are a few moments of slightly elevated diction, but they fit the chosen lyrical-noir register and don’t break POV plausibility.</comment>

The skeleton-with-locket reveal is a classic trope and plays a bit expectedly, but the cumulative imagery of tides, containers, and labels lifts the piece above template noir.
