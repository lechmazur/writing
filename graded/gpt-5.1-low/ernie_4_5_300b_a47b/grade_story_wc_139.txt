<question>2</question><grade>6.2</grade><comment>Causality is straightforward: eavesdropping → reveal → confrontation → negotiation → exit, and the drive lens is clearly causal. Closure exists (she leaves with asylum papers and a new mandate), but the cost and resistance are barely dramatized. This weakens the felt before/after despite a clean structural arc.</comment>
<question>3</question><grade>6.5</grade><comment>The funeral home prep room is efficiently evoked—“smelled of lilies and ironed linen,” dummy casket, mahogany, fluorescent lights—and the sensory palette is brisk and apt. The ink pot functions as a micro-setting/object anchor that recurs and colors the final image. However, the room itself rarely constrains tactics; aside from the hiding place and the fake-body reveal, the environment doesn’t evolve or apply much pressure. The setting feels vivid but somewhat interchangeable for the core negotiation.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>5.8</grade><comment>Stakes are high on paper (asylum, a system-threatening formula, lethal government interest) and signaled early. The initial beat with guns drawn is strong, but pressure dissipates almost immediately once Elara speaks; the agents lower their weapons and agree to “Talk” with very little pushback. The central conflict—will she secure safety and protect the math?—resolves via a compressed, low-friction trade with no visible sacrifice beyond risk that never truly bites on the page. This leaves escalation feeling underdeveloped and the resolution under-tested.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>6.0</grade><comment>Themes of fairness, systems vs. individuals, and “trust the math” versus belief are intriguing and conceptually coherent. However, the story leans into explicit thesis statements (“My father’s math proves your system’s broken”; “we find the mathematics of a fair trade”) that read more like taglines than emergent insight. The ending moralizes instead of allowing the particulars of the scene to carry the thematic weight.</comment>

Dialogue generally advances plot cleanly, though at times it drifts into expositional or slogan-like territory, especially in the bargaining about systems being broken. Overall, the voice is competent and coherent but not yet distinctive enough for elite publication.</comment>
<question>7</question><grade>7.2</grade><comment>Sentences are clean, grammatical, and mostly free of redundancy, with some efficient dual-purpose lines that carry both mood and information. The main weakness is a certain plainness and occasional slogan-level phrasing that blunts the impact of big thematic beats. <question>8</question><grade>7.4</grade><comment>The fusion of a funeral-home caper, fake death, a fairness-focused economic formula, and a mind-altering “not magic, just chemistry” ink pot is fresh and appealingly odd. Yet the resolution—child negotiates asylum and systemic reform with agents in an hour—slides toward wish-fulfillment and YA-genre convenience, feeling less inventive than the setup promises. The piece shows distinctive imaginative premises but doesn’t fully follow through with equally rigorous or surprising consequences.</comment>
