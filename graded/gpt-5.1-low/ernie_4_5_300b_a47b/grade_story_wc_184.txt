<question>2</question><grade>7.6</grade><comment>The causal chain is clean: summoned → investigates door → links seal/rope/symbols → reconfigures braids → enters chamber → reads scroll → returns altered in understanding. The corridor and circular chamber are more archetypal but still serviceable, with the pedestal and single scroll focusing attention. The environment doesn’t heavily constrain her choices, but it does charge the images and supports the story’s temporal and mechanical themes.</comment>

<question>4</question><grade>6.5</grade><comment>The dominant stake—recovering/preserving lost knowledge and understanding the door—is clear, and pressure does rise as the machinery protests, shadows lengthen, and the air chills. The main tightening is the jammed gear and heightened glow as she finishes reweaving, which she moves through without serious obstruction. As a result, the resolution feels satisfying but low-risk, more discovery than hard-won survival or sacrifice.</comment>

<question>5</question><grade>7.2</grade><comment>Themes of absence, preservation, and the transformation of chaos into ordered knowledge are well-integrated through the fables, the seal, the rope, and Elara’s profession. Some thematic articulation is a bit on-the-nose (“She wasn’t just an archivist; she was a weaver of timelines, a translator of chaos”), leaning toward thesis-statement rather than letting the images fully carry it. Still, the final redefinition of her role offers a resonant, if not deeply unsettling, afterimage.</comment>

<question>6</question><grade>7.5</grade><comment>The close third-person voice is stable, mildly elevated, and suitable for an academic-fantastical tale; diction like “fables of absence” and “threads of history, myth, and memory” fits Elara’s sensibility. There’s occasional explanatory phrasing at thematic beats, but not enough to break immersion. Overall the POV control is confident, with no head-hopping or stance confusion, though the voice could lean even more into her idiosyncratic way of thinking to become truly distinctive.</comment>

Information density is good: each paragraph generally carries multiple functions (setting, interiority, plot).
