The final return to the greenhouse with seeds uses the space as a reconfigured, hopeful version of earlier decay. A slight excess of evocative detail in places risks decorative feel, but it rarely stalls momentum.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>7.1</grade><comment>The dominant stake is Elias’s need to find and understand the compass as a way to reconcile his compulsion for order with the lure of uncertainty. We do get a solid escalation—from intellectual obsession to literal physical danger (the fall, near-drowning) and then to an internal decision about what the compass means. However, much of the pressure is gentle; the danger of the fall resolves quickly and the internal conflict doesn’t bite as hard as it could, since he adapts almost immediately to the compass’s ambiguity. The ending choice to plant clockwork flowers is thematically apt but doesn’t feel like it forecloses a costly alternative on the page.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>6.9</grade><comment>The theme—befriending uncertainty versus controlling time—is coherent and nicely embodied in recurring motifs (broken clocks, ash, swirling leaves, the spinning needle). Yet the story undercuts itself with explicit thesis statements like “Uncertainly certain, he realized, wasn’t a contradiction—it was a compass of its own” and “To befriend uncertainty in planning… is to let the leaves swirl,” which edge into moralizing. The emotional aftereffect is pleasant and thoughtful but a bit pre-chewed. A more image-driven, less declarative ending would let the thematic resonance breathe.</comment>

Some sentences verge on essayistic abstraction in the final third, where the narrator starts summarizing insight rather than dramatizing perception. Overall, the perceptual signature is clear but could lean more into idiosyncratic noticing and less into generalized aphorism.</comment>
