<question>4</question><grade>7.0</grade><comment>The dominant stake—capturing the sky inside before demolition—exists and is time-bounded, but pressure remains mostly conceptual rather than viscerally escalating. The demolition itself happens almost offhandedly at the end; Lira doesn’t have to adapt tactics in response, she simply finishes and then the building falls. As a result, the conflict never quite sharpens into a difficult choice with clear cost, keeping escalation mid-range.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>7.6</grade><comment>Themes of limits as thresholds, art as defiance of erasure, and interior versus exterior sky emerge coherently through recurring motifs (coin, constellations, secret safe, demolition). However, the text leans on explicit thematic phrasing (“She felt that discovering limits was a gift…,” “the map to new horizons”), edging toward telling rather than letting images fully carry the insight. This slight didactic gloss flattens what could be a more haunting, ambiguous aftereffect.</comment>
<question>6</question><grade>8.0</grade><comment>The close third-person voice is consistent, with a distinct, slightly mystical texture that suits Lira’s perceptual peculiarities and the fantastical setting. There is some risk of tonal sameness—almost every line hums at the same lyrical register, even during moments that should feel more urgent—but it doesn’t break clarity. Controlled modulation (slightly starker, plainer diction during peak pressure) could heighten contrast and make the climax land harder.</comment>

This uniform lushness slightly dulls momentum and can blur rather than sharpen certain beats, especially under the ticking-clock premise. A bit more ruthless pruning and variation in texture would improve propulsion without sacrificing lyricism.</comment>
