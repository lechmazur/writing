The cost is implied (he must accept loss as transformation and leave, not restore fully), but it’s quiet and largely conceptual. Still, the before/after in understanding is legible and the scene resolves under its chosen logic.</comment>

The environment doesn’t quite constrain choices as hard as it could, but it decisively shapes tone and the closure image. Overall, atmosphere and specificity are publication-level.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>5.0</grade><comment>Conflict and stakes remain highly abstract: the goal is understanding ambiguous loss, but there is no meaningful opposition, deadline, or risk of failure on the page. The insight that grief plus hope leads to new growth is not bought with a visible sacrifice or dilemma. As a result, tension stays low and the piece functions more as a contemplative vignette than a story with live stakes.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>7.0</grade><comment>Themes of ambiguous loss, transformation, and “buried optimism” are coherent and threaded through objects (glass eyes, cracks, petals) in a pleasing way. The ending’s garden-of-possibilities image does carry real resonance, but also feels slightly moralizing and tidy. More restraint in overtly naming the thesis would strengthen subtext.</comment>

Occasionally the explanatory lines flatten the lyricism, but overall the voice feels polished and intentional.</comment>

Still, the prose is strong enough for serious venues, with only minor overwriting as a drag.</comment>
<question>8</question><grade>8.3</grade><comment>The synthesis of a cavern orchard, taxidermist’s glass eyes, and the “mathematics of emotion” is striking and feels genuinely non-generic. Structurally, though, it remains a fairly straightforward ritual-epiphany piece, and the explicit spelling-out of the insight softens the originality of the thematic turn. With a riskier, less didactic ending or sharper stakes, this could push into truly exceptional territory.</comment>
