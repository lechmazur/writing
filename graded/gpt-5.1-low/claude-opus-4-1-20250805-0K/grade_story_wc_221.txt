<question>3</question><grade>8.6</grade><comment>The observation deck, void of stars yet ringed by crystalline walls, is vivid and tonally apt, and the gilded compass rose functions as a charged micro-setting object that literally and thematically shapes the final act. The environment constrains choice—she is isolated on this deck, and the only working navigation system becomes the locus of her solution—so the final image of the pulsing compass and reappearing crewmates is strongly grounded. A slightly wider sensory range beyond visual/thermal (e.g., sound or bodily disorientation in the dilation fields) could deepen atmosphere further.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>7.4</grade><comment>The dominant stake—whether Kira can transform her isolating, harmful temporal gift into a means of guiding the lost crew—is clear, and there is a meaningful tightening when she reframes stretching from curse to tool and commits to sketching the new “constellation” of the station. Her agency does drive the resolution, and the crew beginning to appear gives concrete payoff, but we do not witness a significant trade-off or foreclosed alternative in the moment. The conflict is effective for quiet SF, but for a “prestige” tier it could use one sharper on-page price or risk.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>7.9</grade><comment>Themes of navigation, home, and transforming isolation into a guiding presence are strongly integrated through the compass, “true north” line, and the family sky-sketching ritual reimagined as temporal cartography. However, this line and the subsequent sentence about “confusion into exploration” verge on direct moralizing, stating the thesis instead of letting it rest fully in image and action. Subtext around generational inheritance and the burden of giftedness is present but could be allowed to resonate more implicitly.</comment>

There’s no head-hopping or stance confusion; the narrative’s knowledge stays plausibly within Kira’s experience.
