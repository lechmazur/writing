The ridgeway and lake don’t tightly constrain tactical choices, but they strongly charge the liminal nature of the crossing and the final threshold image. World rules (twilight-only crossings, vigil) feel internally consistent without over-explanation.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>7.6</grade><comment>The dominant stake—Marina’s ability to relinquish control and access unused courage—is present early and coheres through the ferry journey. The price (relinquishing the leash and her old pattern of fear) is conceptually clear but not viscerally tested against a hard alternative; there’s little sense she might truly refuse the crossing. It’s a strong, quietly resolved conflict, but the tension never tightens to a knife’s edge.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>8.0</grade><comment>Themes of holding on vs. letting go, unused courage, and the cost of control are well-integrated into objects (leash, ticket) and images (star-shaped holes, indirect routes home). Some lines verge on explicit thesis (“Unused courage waits in the spaces we create when we stop gripping so hard”; “ridgeway between holding on and letting go”), slightly flattening subtext into statement. Even so, the final image of star-punched light and an unleashed walk offers a resonant, non-literal thematic answer.</comment>

Syntax and rhythm vary in a way that matches the scene’s calm, dreamlike pressure and give the piece a polished literary feel. There’s no head-hopping or register clash that disrupts the spell.</comment>

<question>8</question><grade>8.2</grade><comment>The psychopomp crossing is a familiar frame, but the specific inflection—a leash-snapped dog leading to a twilight ferry where unused courage is the fare—is fresh and emotionally grounded. The piece doesn’t radically reinvent its underlying trope, but it synthesizes elements (vigil, ridgeway, shoreline lullaby tone) into a distinctive, coherent vision.
