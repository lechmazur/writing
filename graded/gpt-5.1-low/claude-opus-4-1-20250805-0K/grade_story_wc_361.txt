<question>4</question><grade>6.8</grade><comment>The dominant stake is experiential and vocational—whether Elara remains a routine courier or becomes something more, and whether Corvin’s theories (and disappearance) gain meaning—which is clear but relatively low in immediate risk. Elara’s agency is primarily in showing up with the relic and allowing resonance; after that, events largely happen to her, with little real possibility of refusal dramatized. The result is thematically satisfying but dramatically gentle.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>7.4</grade><comment>The story’s thematic core—that artifacts are seeds of connection that can collapse centuries into “brief encounters,” and that preservation should resurrect wonder rather than merely conserve—is coherently woven through Corvin’s philosophy and the Convergence. However, the theme is sometimes over-articulated in essayistic lines (“Our job isn’t just to deliver these objects—it's to coax wonder…”, “to ensure that humanity's treasures didn't just survive in museums but lived again”), which edges toward moralizing rather than fully trusting subtext. The final insight is earned but somewhat tidy and thesis-like.</comment>
