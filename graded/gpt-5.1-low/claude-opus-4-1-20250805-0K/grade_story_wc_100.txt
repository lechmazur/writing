The recurring physical environment and the lens object directly charge the final image of “rest” as shadow rather than light.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>7.6</grade><comment>The dominant stake—whether and how exhausted beings (Silas, librarians, dead civilizations) can finally rest—is clear and escalates from personal insomnia to cosmic burden. However, external resistance and on-page struggle are minimal; the work of transformation occurs in a largely frictionless montage, which keeps this from reaching a higher tension band.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>8.7</grade><comment>Themes of rest vs vigilance, the ethics of endings, and knowledge as a kind of corpse are woven through concrete images (the mortuary, the grief-bubble, the library as morgue) rather than abstract statement. Lines like “some spaces become possible only when we stop trying to escape them” flirt with explicitness but remain rooted enough in the particulars to avoid feeling like pasted-on moralizing.</comment>

The few lines of collective dialogue from the librarians fit the established tone and deepen the uncanny exhaustion rather than breaking it.</comment>
<question>7</question><grade>8.8</grade><comment>The prose is dense but controlled: “bubble of breathable grief,” “bodies of knowledge,” “mausoleums from coral and memory” each do multiple jobs without stacking into muddle.
