<question>4</question><grade>7.4</grade><comment>The central conflict—whether Lucien’s art must serve revolution or retain its own ethical ambiguity—is well signaled via the gallery quarrel and sharpened in the ritual. However, the stakes stay largely ethical-philosophical; there’s little sense that he could choose an easier path and pay a painful relational or material price, so the cost feels mostly conceptual. <question>5</question><grade>7.0</grade><comment>The thematic field—ethical storytelling, ambiguity, reversal as revelation—is integrated across images (smoke, ash, reversal, half‑lit epiphanies) and character history (grandmother’s “every character contains both truth and lie”). Risk‑taking in imagery and concept pays off without losing structural legibility. Slightly heavy explicit moralizing keeps it from feeling fully inevitable and effortless, but the originality is well above average.</comment>
