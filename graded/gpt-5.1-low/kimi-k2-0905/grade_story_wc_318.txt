<question>1</question><grade>8.7</grade><comment>Ellery’s grief, hope, and self-awareness are rendered with concrete, particular detail (leaf-blower “symphony conductor,” marble-laughter), and his core action—taking the blower to the tunnel and modulating it—is clearly motivated by the need to recontact his daughter. His internal contradiction (knowing the sound is impossible yet chasing it anyway) plays out credibly in scene. The final endurance—accepting that what returns is shape, not the dead—feels earned and costly. <question>2</question><grade>9.0</grade><comment>The structure is tight and clean: hourglass empties → silence and long-term grief → deliberate experiment with sound in the tunnel → unexpected “return” of laughter → reconfigured home at the end. Causality is clear and accumulative under a textural lens: each sound experiment reshapes the emotional field rather than producing a literal ghost. Closure lands as a strong Resonant Reconfiguration—the house, marriage, and Ellery’s grief-state all feel different by the final paragraph, with cost intact. No orientation or lens ambiguities undercut the ending.</comment>
<question>3</question><grade>9.0</grade><comment>The setting is exceptionally integrated: the tunnel as “concrete throat” that echoes and reshapes sound is both atmospheric and functionally indispensable to how Quinn’s laughter is “restored.” The hourglass and blower function as micro-settings/objects that constrain behavior and charge the final image (blower set beside the empty hourglass, cord like a pendulum). Environmental details consistently mirror and pressure the internal state without feeling like rote symbolism. Spatial and sensory choreography (echoes, porch lights, dog bark, concrete vibration) is clear and purposeful.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>8.5</grade><comment>The central stake—whether Ellery can transform suffocating silence into something that admits Quinn’s laughter—is established early and escalates as he moves from raw noise to careful modulation. The key tightening comes with the impossible sniffle and his decision to persist, risking emotional re-injury for the chance of connection. The price is legible: he doesn’t get Quinn back, only a fragile, mediated echo, and must live with that limit. The conflict is primarily internal/textural rather than confrontational, which fits the piece but keeps the pressure in a more subdued register.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>8.2</grade><comment>Themes of grief, memory, and “tending to the unspoken” emerge organically from the repeated objects and actions (blower concerts, hourglass, echoing tunnel) and culminate in the idea of giving absence a shape. The final images—the house “exhaling something like a laugh,” the tunnel as organ pipe—offer resonant, non-literal answers about what can and cannot be restored. However, the explicit line “He understood then that tending to the unspoken does not mean bringing the dead back; it means…” tips into direct statement and slightly blunts the subtextive power. Even so, the emotional aftereffect is complex and avoids sentimentality.</comment>

There’s no head-hopping or stance confusion; lyricism remains plausibly within the narrative register. The diction occasionally edges toward the elevated, but it matches the story’s chosen lyrical mode.</comment>
<question>7</question><grade>8.7</grade><comment>Line by line, the prose is dense but controlled; most metaphors are fresh and coherent, often doing double duty for setting and emotion. The final paragraphs land with clear, charged images and no figurative muddle.</comment>
<question>8</question><grade>8.8</grade><comment>Using a leaf blower as an instrument of grief and haunting, and an echo tunnel as a vessel for “restored” laughter, is a genuinely fresh angle on familiar bereavement material. Recurring objects (hourglass, pull-cord, blower) are reweighted in a way that reads inevitable in hindsight. The piece takes a stylistic risk with high lyricism and sustained metaphor, and largely pulls it off without sacrificing clarity.</comment>
