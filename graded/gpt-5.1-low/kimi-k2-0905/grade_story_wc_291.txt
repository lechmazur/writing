A slight limitation is that institutional consequences are gestured at rather than dramatized, though for this length it still lands.</comment>
<question>3</question><grade>8.9</grade><comment>The quarry and mist are vivid, textured, and filtered through Juniper’s guilt and method: the “quarry’s jagged throat,” the mist soaking the page, the cliffs that “lean closer to watch.” Setting is not postcard; it shapes mood and meaning, especially in the final image of the quarry “exhal[ing]” and its shadows rearranging like shoulders relaxing, which charges the sense of long-held tension released. Objects-as-setting (lamp, ribbon, easel, shale) are tactically used, not just described. The only limit is that the physical danger of the quarry doesn’t materially constrain her tactics; it’s more atmospheric than logistical.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>8.5</grade><comment>The dominant stake—whether Juniper can pierce her own protective erasure to recover the lost babysitter and thus rehumanize the cold case—is signaled early and escalates coherently. Internal and external conflicts interweave: her method and face-blindness collide with institutional erasure (burned evidence, last detective) in a way that forces a stance. The conflict is largely internal/lyric rather than confrontational, but the cost and narrowing of options still feel legible.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>8.6</grade><comment>Theme—about empathy, memory, erasure, and the moral work of “unifying opposites”—emerges organically from events and images: blood and silk, predator and prey, evidence and art, cold case and warm empathy. The ribbon’s reweighting from mere clue to personal relic, then to treaty-seal, deepens the thematic question of what kind of evidence truly matters. The ending’s notion that the portrait will subtly make officers “solve smaller injustices with gentler hands” offers a resonant, non-literal answer without becoming didactic. The closing sentence does edge close to thesis-statement explicitness, but it’s grounded enough in prior particulars to avoid feeling like a sermon.</comment>
