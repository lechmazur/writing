Density skirts excess at times, but the world remains coherent and tactically relevant throughout.</comment>

<question>4</question><grade>7.8</grade><comment>The dominant stake—whether she can transmute “unused love” before it calcifies—is clear, and it tightens when the man appears and mirrors her fracture, turning a private quest into a shared risk. However, the process from encounter to healing stays relatively frictionless; there’s little on-page resistance or possibility of failure once the ritual starts, which softens the sense of price paid. The cost is more conceptual than agonized, which fits the mode but limits escalatory bite.</comment>

<question>5</question><grade>8.3</grade><comment>The piece develops a coherent thematic argument about breath, posture, and the storage of love, with recurring objects (the tear, the camera, the viaduct) shifting meaning by the end. At times the story states its thesis a bit directly, especially in the final paragraph where forgiveness and “unfinished hearts” are named, edging toward overt moralizing. Still, the closing movement—from purple-tasting breath to transparent medium—is a strong non-literal answer to the opening question.</comment>

Nonetheless, clarity is maintained through the climax and closing images; cause and effect are never obscured by the lyric surface. There’s little scaffolding or redundancy, and the final images (red silk as flag, transparent breath) land cleanly.</comment>
