<question>2</question><grade>8.5</grade><comment>The accumulative structure is clean: nightly ritual → maze → orchard → Lila → weaving → transformed self, with each step causally and texturally building on the last. Orientation (who/where/when) is clear very early, and the magic rules stay internally consistent. The only small limit is that the “price” of his new stasis remains somewhat gentle and could be shaded with sharper consequence.</comment>
<question>3</question><grade>8.8</grade><comment>The setting is richly particular—the hedge maze, mystic orchard, cracked marble stage, and sensory-laden theater details form a coherent, lived-in world. Sensory detail is varied (sound, scent, texture, even synesthetic taste) and always filtered through the ghost’s theatrical, time-haunted perception. A hair more interaction with physical constraint (e.g., spatial difficulty or risk in the maze) could deepen the sense of external pressure.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>7.8</grade><comment>The dominant stake—reclaiming possibility/continuity of self through performance—remains coherent and escalates as the ritual turns from private yearning into a full temporal reweaving. Pressure deepens when Lila appears and forces him to reframe applause as “luring possibility,” then peaks as they bind echoes to the quince tree and condense everything into one heartbeat. The decisive change (his outline sharpening, carrying an “endless encore”) is clearly tied to his actions rather than coincidence. Still, the conflict stays contemplative; there’s little sense of risk that the attempt could fail or harm him, which softens the perceived stakes.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>8.6</grade><comment>Theme emerges organically around memory, performance, and the idea that nostalgia can be both a trap and a generator of new “possibility.” The repeated theatrical images (encore, curtain, ghost-light) and the orchard’s backward-blooming quince reframe time as something art can fold rather than merely lose. Lila’s line about making audiences “feel time is a song they almost remember” elegantly encodes the thematic question without tipping into manifesto. The ending’s image of an “endless encore” beneath translucent ribs offers a resonant but admirably non-didactic answer, though the despair/possibility dichotomy could be pressured slightly more on the page.</comment>

Dialogue between Casper and Lila is brief but tonally apt and carries subtext about art, longing, and complicity. A few lines brush against over-rich description, but the rhythm and lexicon remain controlled and consistent with the piece’s ambitions.</comment>
<question>7</question><grade>8.4</grade><comment>Line-level craft is strong: sentences are varied, information-dense, and often do double duty for mood and world-building, with few redundancies or scaffolding phrases. Momentum is well-managed for a lyric piece: each sentence tends to add a new facet rather than rephrase the last. The core emotional arc (dying for an encore, then finding a different kind of endless performance) dovetails tightly with the metaphysics. It’s ambitious, distinctive work that takes stylistic and conceptual risks without losing coherence.</comment>
