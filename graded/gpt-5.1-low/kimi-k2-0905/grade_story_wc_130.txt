<question>3</question><grade>9.1</grade><comment>The cavern orchard is vivid, strange, and consistently leveraged: the stalactites’ dripping (and prior silence) define the stakes, the mosaic stone and calcite tree provide the physical grammar of the ritual, and moonlight is literally refracted into meaning. The environment doesn’t sit as backdrop; it actively constrains tactics and powers the solution, then charges the final image when the dripping resumes. Details like “fruit of crystal and calcite,” “turquoise chips,” and “liquid starlight” are specific yet never feel like a catalog. Spatial relations (stone, tree, stalactite) remain coherent throughout the transformations.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>8.5</grade><comment>The dominant stake—finding a way to move within ambiguous loss, embodied in reviving the orchard—is established early and tightens as each ritual step either fails or partially unlocks something. The key deepening turn is the drop hitting the right eye, triggering the mosaic’s opening and escalating the narrator’s commitment. The conflict is primarily internal but externalized through concrete magical tasks, and the final act of sealing the astrolabe rather than clinging to it reads as an earned stance. Stakes do stay somewhat conceptual, which keeps this just below the very top band.</comment>

There is a touch of explicit statement (“emotion is not a number but a locus of possible intersections”) that edges toward telling, but it’s still grounded in the story’s conceit. Overall, the piece invites reflection without collapsing into moralizing.</comment>
