<question>3</question><grade>9.1</grade><comment>The arboretum on the cliff, abandoned lighthouse, and violent sea are rendered with precise, sensorial detail that is always filtered through grief (“sound like tearing paper,” waves that “detonate against basalt”). The environment directly charges the final image—the narrator’s new “lighthouse” is literally the mapped body and the resin-sealed tree facing the sea. Weather and terrain acoustics (wind, waves) modulate pressure and tone through the piece.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>8.7</grade><comment>The dominant stake—how to live with catastrophic grief without erasing it—is clear from early on and remains coherent. Escalation occurs through deepening, not external reversal: the invented “freeze” exercise, then the resin-transfer, then the harder choice of letting the photo go. Agency is quiet but real: they refuse the option of self-erasure at the cliff and instead choose a costlier endurance (living “as the trees live”). The only slight limitation is that conflict remains purely internal, so pressure is intense but not varied across different external fronts.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>8.3</grade><comment>Themes of memory vs. optimism, grief as landscape, and the ethics of surviving violence emerge powerfully from concrete particulars (trees outliving lighthouse, somatic map, resin-sealed laughter). However, the explicit aphoristic lines—“grief is not a landmine to avoid; it is a coastline to learn by heart” and “optimism without memory is just noise, but memory without optimism is a tomb”—edge toward thesis statement, slightly over-articulating what the images already carry. Subtext elsewhere is strong enough that those sentences feel a shade didactic for a top-tier venue.</comment>
