World rules (phoenix ash, glass rain) feel internally consistent in a mythic register and are learned through action, not exposition. This setting is inseparable from the story’s logic and theme; only the absence of any hard physical constraint on immediate tactics keeps it from the absolute top band.</comment>

<question>4</question><grade>7.8</grade><comment>The primary stake—Milo’s need to transform his relationship to beauty and perception—intensifies from curiosity to profound, costly reorientation. However, tension remains largely internal and aesthetic; there is little sense of risk beyond artistic/spiritual stakes, and no substantial reversal tests their bond or commitment. The pressure is coherent and deepening but not sharply contested.</comment>

<question>5</question><grade>7.2</grade><comment>The thematic core—beauty in brokenness, transformation through trading wounds—is powerfully integrated into images (cracked marble, fractured reed, smashed glass spiral) and Milo’s arc. Yet the story repeatedly states its thesis in near-aphoristic form (“we are most alive where we are broken,” “we trade our wounds not to heal them but to teach them new languages”), sliding toward explicit moralizing. These lines partially duplicate what the images and actions already convey, dulling the subtext. The resonance is strong but lessened by the didactic surface.</comment>

<question>7</question><grade>8.0</grade><comment>Line-level craft is generally excellent: sentences carry multiple jobs (image, emotion, worldbuilding) and the rhythm supports the accumulative, incantatory feel. The mythic conceit is transformed by concrete, idiosyncratic details (concertina reed tongue, tobacco-stained fingers, glass-blown leaves of permanent autumn).
