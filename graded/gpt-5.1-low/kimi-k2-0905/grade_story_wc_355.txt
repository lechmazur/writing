Closure lands as a resonant reconfiguration: the clasp’s function redefined and Mirelle’s relationship to memory and silence altered, with the cost of relinquishing control over what’s “locked.” All beats link logically with no orientation or causality breaks.</comment>
<question>3</question><grade>8.8</grade><comment>The workshop under stars, the gunpowder-for-fireworks, and the skylight-pressed constellations are vivid and tightly integrated with the theme of weaponized vs. celebratory noise. The environment directly charges the last image—silent fireworks through the workshop and the open doors as a “lighthouse.” A slight risk of sensory overload in places is the only limiter.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>8.4</grade><comment>The dominant stake—whether Mirelle can transmute inherited war trauma into a non-lethal, empathetic form of silence before the fireworks erupt—is clear and escalates via each tuned frequency. Pressure tightens as she nears the dial’s end and the fireworks begin, forcing her to accelerate the search for forgiveness. The cost is largely internal (tears, reinterpreting the clasp, surrendering it to the world), but it is legible and thematically scaled. Conflict remains mostly interior and lyric rather than sharply dramatized, which keeps it just shy of the very top band.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>8.6</grade><comment>Themes of empathy, the ethics of silence, and transforming instruments of containment into instruments of listening are deeply woven into objects and actions rather than simple exposition. The ending image of a child momentarily choosing not to grow up angry grounds the abstract “path to empathy” in a specific, affecting consequence. Occasional near-thesis statements (“Silence That Lets the World Speak Without Killing”) flirt with over-clarity but remain within lyrical framing.</comment>

Minor familiarity in some war/fireworks imagery is outweighed by the fresh central conceit and execution.</comment>
