<question>3</question><grade>8.8</grade><comment>The underwater city garden is vividly realized and always filtered through the narrator’s sensibility (“grief of barnacles,” “heartbeat of silt”), avoiding postcard description. Environment directly constrains choice: the structural weakness of the keystone arch, thirty fathoms down, is the necessary vessel for releasing the memory. The setting continuously mirrors and pressures the thematic stakes—submerged, preserved certainties vs. living ambiguity. This integration is excellent and charges the final image of the “gate” being open beneath oblivious ships.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>8.0</grade><comment>The dominant conflict—whether to preserve a dead, certain order or risk dissolving it in nuance—is clear and tracks through the story. Escalation is mostly internal and conceptual but does tighten as the statue approaches the arch and contemplates institutional hypocrisy and self-implication. The choice to break the ampoule forecloses a path (remaining a passive sentinel of certainty) with real cost, and the reverberation through the city makes that cost concrete. The pressure is strong but relatively quiet; more palpable resistance or risk in the environment could push it higher.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>8.6</grade><comment>Theme emerges organically from image and action: poison vs. remedy, memory as nuance rather than verdict, revelation as dialogue not hammer. The ending’s invitation to divers reweights the whole piece as an argument for living with ambiguity, not as a tidy moral. A few lines (“poison and remedy share roots,” “revelation is not a hammer”) come close to explicit thesis but are supported enough by scene work to still feel earned.</comment>

Psychic distance is managed well; reflection is anchored in present stimuli (ampoule, arch, other statues waking). No head-hopping or stance drift appears, and the modulation from wonder to grave resolve at the arch is smooth and effective.</comment>
<question>7</question><grade>8.5</grade><comment>Line-by-line craft is strong: sentences carry multiple functions (worldbuilding, emotion, theme) without obvious redundancy, and metaphor chains stay within an aquatic/stone/memory logic. The final invitation to divers to “welcome nuance” extends the originality beyond image into an ethical/aesthetic proposition. It doesn’t quite reach the shock of never-seen-before, but it’s well above most in invention and follow-through.</comment>
