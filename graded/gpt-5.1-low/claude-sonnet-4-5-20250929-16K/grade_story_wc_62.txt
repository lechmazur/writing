Her shift from isolating individual losses to perceiving a network of grief is well motivated by the lessons and the ribbon. However, her inner conflict is relatively smooth—there’s little resistance, self-deception, or messy contradiction during the transformation—which keeps the arc from feeling fully lived-in. The emotional cost of realizing thirty years of work was too narrow is more implied than intensely embodied.</comment>

Each visit layers a new insight, moving from confusion to apprenticeship to transformed practice, and the final unrolling of the ribbon functions as an on-page conceptual repricing of her craft. Causality is clean (questions → lessons → re-seeing old work → new carving approach), and the ending decisively reconfigures meaning with a felt price. Stakes are modest but appropriate to the mode and land coherently.</comment>
<question>3</question><grade>8.8</grade><comment>The cemetery and ancient greenhouse are vividly evoked and tightly integrated with Elena’s vocation and the story’s mood. The butterflies, glass, vines, tools, and ribbon all recur and evolve, charging the space as a liminal classroom between life and death. The greenhouse meaningfully shapes what can happen—Thomas’s appearances, the monarchs’ passage, the final empty stillness. The final image of her leaving the greenhouse with tools and ribbon, as the place falls back into silence, is strongly environment-charged.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>7.2</grade><comment>The dominant stake—whether Elena will remain a carver of isolated monuments or evolve into a maker of connective markers—is clear and thematically coherent. Pressure escalates in a quiet, internal way: her dissatisfaction, Thomas’s critique, and the mental retracing of past work gradually force her to adopt a new practice. However, the conflict remains largely contemplative, with little on-page friction or hard trade-offs beyond the conceptual cost of admitting past inadequacy, so the sense of risk and sacrifice stays relatively gentle. The final commitment to “carve differently now” is earned but not heavily contested.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>7.8</grade><comment>The thematic throughline—grief as communal, patterned, and migratory rather than purely individual—is well realized through concrete motifs (ribbon, monarch migration, echoing gravestone designs). The idea of individual utterances becoming a chorus is nicely dramatized via poems and grave patterns speaking across distance. That said, several lines state the theme quite directly (“Grief isn’t uniform,” “That’s collective grief—individual utterance becoming chorus”), nudging into mild didacticism instead of letting subtext fully carry the weight. The ending reweights earlier images effectively, but with a slightly essayistic explicitness.</comment>
<question>6</question><grade>8.2</grade><comment>The close third-person voice is consistent, measured, and well suited to the contemplative subject matter. Diction and syntax are controlled and lyrical without tipping into obscurity, and Thomas’s sparse dialogue feels appropriately gentle and instructive. The narrative maintains a stable focalization through Elena, with no head-hopping or stance confusion. Rhythmic modulation between description, reflection, and brief spoken lines is handled with quiet confidence.</comment>
<question>7</question><grade>8.0</grade><comment>The prose is clean, vivid, and mostly economical, with many sentences doing double duty for atmosphere and theme. A few phrases lean toward abstraction or repetition of stated ideas about grief, but they don’t significantly drag momentum or clarity. Overall line-level craft is strong enough for a competitive venue, if not yet at a truly razor-edged, cut-every-extra-word standard.</comment>
<question>8</question><grade>8.0</grade><comment>The combination of a gravestone carver, spectral mentor, abandoned greenhouse, butterfly migration, and collective grief as an interlinked pattern feels fresh and thoughtfully synthesized. Ghost-mentor and artisan-learning-new-meaning tropes are familiar, but here they’re enriched by specific, idiosyncratic details (the woven ribbon roll, echoing gravestone designs across a cemetery). The central conceptual turn—monuments as nodes in a community’s grief network—is non-obvious yet feels inevitable in hindsight. It’s distinctive and memorable, though not so formally or conceptually risky as to feel radically new.</comment>
