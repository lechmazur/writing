<question>2</question><grade>7.8</grade><comment>The structure is clean: a single night’s work that begins in quiet preparation, deepens through decoding, and culminates in a revaluation of what the tile means. Causality is sound—the discoveries unfold logically from her methods—and orientation (who/where/when) is solid from the opening. Closure lands as a conceptual repricing: the covenant is recognized rather than created, and her choice to safeguard the tile and continue observing offers a clear before/after. The only limitation is that the on-page cost of this realization remains conceptual rather than sharply tested by events.</comment>
<question>3</question><grade>8.3</grade><comment>The reimagined Babylonian palace is vivid and specific—ziggurat terraces, salvaged solar panels, lattice windows—and consistently filtered through an astronomer’s priorities and scarcity. Setting details (candle stubs, precious paper, old star chamber) feel lived-in and subtly constrain her choices and methods. The environment powerfully mirrors the thematic liminality between eras and between human understanding and the cosmos, and the final ascent toward the telescope is charged by that space. A slightly greater sense of the environment pushing back on her (e.g., physical discomfort, political presence) would push this higher.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>6.9</grade><comment>The dominant stake—preserving and understanding a fragile, subversive knowledge under unstable regimes—is clear, and the discovery that the square maps a seventeen-light-year perspective is a genuine deepening. However, conflict is largely internal and contemplative, with no active opposition or reversal; tension resides in implication rather than acute on-page pressure. Her key “choice” (to carry the tile forward and persist in her work) is meaningful but only lightly contested. As a result, escalation feels more like steady illumination than tightening conflict.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>7.5</grade><comment>The theme of cross-millennial devotion to knowledge as a secular covenant is strong and organically tied to the magic square, palace, and political void. That said, the story leans into direct statement (“proof that devotion to understanding transcended time,” “the covenant wasn’t something to create but to recognize”), edging toward essayistic moralizing. A bit more reliance on image and implication, less on explicit thematic language, would deepen resonance.</comment>

Rhythm varies enough to keep the prose from feeling mechanical, and psychic distance is well-controlled. <question>8</question><grade>8.2</grade><comment>The synthesis of a future reimagined Babylon, a magic-square exoplanet map, and the idea of encrypted ancient scientific devotion feels genuinely fresh. The piece takes a quiet, conceptual risk rather than a plot-twist-driven one, and it mostly pays off.
