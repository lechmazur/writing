<question>3</question><grade>8.9</grade><comment>The storm-damaged greenhouse is vivid, tactile, and consistently filtered through Mara’s scientific eye (salt deposits, strata of sediment, netting, pipes). The setting’s altered state after the flood is integral to both plot and theme. This is near top-tier environment work, only just shy of extraordinary constraint-driven stakes.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>8.2</grade><comment>The conflict is primarily internal—Mara’s need for purified, compressed “essence” versus the greenhouse’s chaotic, generative decay—and the piece leans into accumulative pressure rather than overt confrontation. Escalation arrives via the evolving dust patterns and the impossible-seeming butterfly, which together force her to reconsider her stance. The central stake is her interpretive framework and inherited control impulse, and the renunciation at the end forecloses a prior path of sterile abstraction. The quietness of the conflict is intentional, though a slightly sharper on-page decision point could have heightened the felt cost.</comment>

Despite that, the emotional and conceptual resonance is strong and coherent.</comment>

The narrative distance tightens appropriately around key realizations without head-hopping. A mild essayistic flavor in the final paragraph is the only thing keeping this from a higher band.</comment>

The slight exposition in the last sentences is more about thematic directness than prose weakness.</comment>

The risk is modest but thoughtful, especially in committing to a quiet, accumulative structure.</comment>
