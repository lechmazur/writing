<question>4</question><grade>7.2</grade><comment>The dominant stake—Vera’s desire to reframe the quarry murders as a site of potential mercy and empathy rather than pure monstrosity—is clear, and there is a meaningful deepening once she reinterprets the ribbon and the killer’s role. <question>5</question><grade>7.8</grade><comment>Themes of seeing versus being seen, empathy for perpetrators, and the unity of opposites (artist/destroyer, sight/blindness) are strongly woven through Vera’s condition, the mask-maker twist, and the final decision to “give the faceless killer a portrait.” The recurring concern with how masks reveal true selves while faces mislead is conceptually rich and specific to this setup. The weakness is a tendency toward explicit thesis-stating (“The murders weren’t about hatred. This directness blunts some of the potential lingering ambiguity that a more oblique ending image could have carried.</comment>

Trimming a few of these rhetorical echoes would sharpen momentum and let the strongest images breathe.</comment>
