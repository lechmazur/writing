<question>4</question><grade>7.9</grade><comment>The dominant stake—whether Maren will find meaning/connection in the captain’s encrypted diary rather than only loss—is clear and tightens as entries move from joy to the encroaching sickness. Maren’s agency is quieter and mostly attentional (choosing to read, choosing how to hold her grief), but it does crystallize in her decision to see the diary as “what remains.” Because the external situation never presses a hard choice or visible sacrifice in the moment, the pressure feels modest rather than acute.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>8.8</grade><comment>Theme is strongly integrated: attention as love, joy preserved against erasure, the idea of being “written into” someone’s private sky, all built from concrete particulars rather than abstraction. The final conceptual repricing—Maren valuing proof of noticed joy over immediate sorrow—reweights the entire ascent and the early cloud lesson. There is a faint risk of neatness in the closing articulation (“encrypt it against forgetting”), but it’s still rooted in the specific situation enough to avoid sermonizing.</comment>

A few lines edge toward authorial register (“affectionately detached from her own grief”) but don’t fully break POV plausibility.</comment>

<question>8</question><grade>8.7</grade><comment>The combination of an ice tower hospice, airship captain, and optical encryption keyed to scattered light feels fresh and particular rather than generic steampunk. The emotional arc—grief reframed through discovery of having been deeply seen—is familiar but here rendered with distinctive imagery (cloud personalities, private sky, light through ice). It’s inventive and cohesive, though not quite boundary-pushing enough in structure or moral calculus to reach the very topmost band.</comment>
