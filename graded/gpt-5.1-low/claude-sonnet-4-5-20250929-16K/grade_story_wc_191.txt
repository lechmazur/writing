World rules (waterfall as data, orchard as compressed wisdom) are learned through observation and action rather than exposition dumps. While the environment could constrain her more directly, it still meaningfully shapes the path and charges the final image of the throne.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>6.8</grade><comment>The dominant stake—reaching the unclaimed throne via the hidden stairway—is clear from the outset, and the reveal of the mural/stairway is a genuine tightening of options. However, opposition is light: the paint-peeling puzzle yields quickly, the staircase offers no real danger, and the sunrise deadline never bites, so escalation feels smooth rather than pressured. The internal conflict at the end (to claim or not) is resolved in a single, untroubled gesture. The piece works, but at an elite level one would expect sharper costs or reversals.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>7.9</grade><comment>Themes of stewardship vs. power, memory as layered palimpsest, and the ethics of custodianship are well woven through the orchard, the painted-over walls, and Vess’s choice to leave the throne empty. One line (“This was why lore keepers existed”) edges toward explicit thesis but doesn’t fully tip into sermonizing. The ending image of her descending with “the weight of possibility and the lightness of hope” offers a resonant, if somewhat tidy, thematic aftertaste.</comment>

The narrator’s perceptions track plausibly with what Vess could notice; there’s no head-hopping or stance confusion. While the voice is strong and polished, it doesn’t yet feel singular enough to be unmistakable.</comment>
<question>7</question><grade>7.8</grade><comment>The prose is clean, rhythmic, and mostly economical: each paragraph advances image, world, or stake with minimal scaffolding. This is strong, professional line work, just shy of the exceptional compression and surprise of top-tier literary pieces.</comment>
<question>8</question><grade>7.4</grade><comment>The synthesis of mythic throne-quest with glitchy, post-digital ruins (pixel waterfall, memory-circuits, data-orchard) is fresh and appealing, and the choice to leave the throne empty is a nice subversion of the usual coronation climax.
