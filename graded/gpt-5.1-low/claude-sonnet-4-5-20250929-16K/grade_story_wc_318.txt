The pull cord and leaf blowers function as tools in a ritualized, tactile way that charges the final image. A slightly wider sense of the estate grounds above might have deepened contrast, but what’s here is focused and effective.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>7.0</grade><comment>The dominant stake—piercing the family’s silence—is clear and emotionally weighty, and the piece does track a meaningful shift when the brother speaks. His agency is strong (he designs and executes the ritual), but he is never truly tested or forced to choose between competing harms. The ending’s cost is felt but gentle, more a soft deepening than hard conflict.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>8.6</grade><comment>Themes of grief, silence, and finding oblique ways to “tend to the unspoken” emerge organically from Marcus’s actions and the environment, not from explicit moralizing. The final image—Marcus gripping the pull cord like a talisman, planning more performances—is a resonant answer to the question of how to live with loss rather than cure it. Complexity is maintained: the house is not healed, only reconfigured, which keeps the piece from sentimentality.</comment>

Rhythm and diction are varied and purposeful, leaning into long, winding sentences for the symphony and tighter ones for key turns. A hair more idiosyncratic bias in Marcus’s perceptions could push this into truly distinctive territory, but it’s already strong.</comment>

Emotional beats (especially the brother hearing “something that sounded like her”) feel specific rather than templated.
