<question>2</question><grade>8.5</grade><comment>The structure is clean: one continuous scene with a clear present-time ethical dilemma, interleaved with retrocognitive glimpses that are causally triggered by the damaged tessera. Causality is tight and easy to follow; no set-up is left hanging. <question>3</question><grade>8.7</grade><comment>The shattered sky and ancient plaza are vividly rendered and recur in a way that charges the mood and the final image. The micro-setting of the mosaic under Vessa’s hand is especially strong, functioning as both tool and stage for the ethical choice. Setting is more atmospheric than constraining tactically, but it’s deeply integrated with meaning.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>8.4</grade><comment>The dominant conflict—expose the complicated truth about Davos or let the myth stand for a traumatized society—is sharply framed and escalates through Vessa’s deepening visions of his culpability and sacrifice. Her agency at the climax (prying out the tessera) is clearly the decisive turn that narrows options, with the cost of carrying the unshared truth. The stakes are moral and symbolic rather than physical, but they feel proportionate to the length and world. The pressure could be heightened by a bit more immediate social or interpersonal risk on the page.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>8.2</grade><comment>Thematic threads of mercy vs. justice, the construction of heroes, and the ethics of forgetting are woven tightly through actions and images (tessera, shattered sky, renaming). However, explicit lines such as “This was aberrant mercy—the kind that…” and “Perhaps that was the burden…” verge on direct thesis-stating and slightly undercut the otherwise implicit power. The piece still invites reflection beyond itself and avoids simplistic moralizing.</comment>

Psychic distance is handled smoothly, moving between external description and close interiority without confusion. There is no head-hopping or register drift that breaks immersion.</comment>

<question>8</question><grade>8.5</grade><comment>The synthesis of elements—retrocognition through damage, post-fire augment culture, a “gentle radical” cyborg commander, and the notion of aberrant mercy—is fresh and feels internally necessary rather than gimmicky.
