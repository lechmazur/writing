<question>4</question><grade>7.6</grade><comment>The dominant stake—whether the drowned can find proper rest and whether Meridian will change her practice—is clear and escalates as the distress signals intensify and differentiate into “distinct voices.” Her decision to reconfigure the lens and devote the library to souls is an earned resolution that meaningfully changes the situation. Still, conflict is largely internal and conceptual with little on-page resistance or risk; nothing fights back, and there’s no moment where a real option is foreclosed with visible immediate cost. The pressure ramps in image-density rather than in sharp dilemmas, which keeps tension at a quiet but solid level.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>8.5</grade><comment>The story’s thematic spine—peace as a constructed, element-appropriate resting place rather than a default endpoint—is clear and emerges organically from the shift in Meridian’s practice and the library’s new function. There is a slight tilt toward explicit thesis in that line, bordering on summarizing the theme. Even so, the accumulating particulars and closing image of souls at rest give it real emotional and conceptual weight.</comment>
<question>6</question><grade>8.6</grade><comment>The third-person voice is confident, lyrical, and consistent, with a distinct sensibility attuned to decay, duty, and strange geometry. There’s clean control of distance—close enough to carry her realizations, far enough for atmospheric description—without head-hopping or register glitches. A few sentences approach overt exposition of theme, but they remain within the established voice rather than reading as external commentary.</comment>
