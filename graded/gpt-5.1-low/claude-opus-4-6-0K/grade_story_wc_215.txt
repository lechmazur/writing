World rules (bees gone, drought, ghosts visible) are consistent and absorbed without clunky exposition. The setting constrains plausible options (her isolation, the materials for the box) and shapes the texture of every major beat.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>8.0</grade><comment>The dominant stake—whether Maren will break or reinterpret her covenant of silence in the face of her sister’s ghost—is clear and emotionally legible from early on. Pressure deepens meaningfully when Elara appears and when Maren opens the box, narrowing her options to either maintain or shatter the silence; the cost of maintaining it is felt. However, the conflict remains largely internal and tonal, with limited on-page escalation or reversal beyond that central decision. The story earns its quiet resolution, but a slightly more acute moment of doubt or near-breach could sharpen the sense of price paid.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>8.7</grade><comment>Themes of silence, unspoken covenant, grief, and the idea of the dead as listeners rather than speakers arise organically from action and image, not merely from assertion. A few lines verge on explicit thesis (“Some promises exist only in the spaces between words”), but they’re supported by sufficient concrete groundwork to avoid feeling like tacked-on moralizing. The final redefinition of silence as presence rather than absence functions as a clean conceptual repricing of the entire narrative.</comment>

<question>7</question><grade>8.9</grade><comment>The prose is dense, image-rich, and mostly avoids redundancy; nearly every sentence carries thematic, atmospheric, and narrative weight simultaneously. The execution stays committed to its conceit without hedging, following through on the risk of a protagonist who never speaks on the page.
