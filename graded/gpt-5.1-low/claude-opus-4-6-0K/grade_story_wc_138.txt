<question>4</question><grade>8.7</grade><comment>The dominant stake—whether Vaelen will annihilate the architecture of reality to satisfy his longing for an ending—is clearly signaled early and tightened effectively as the storm peaks and the shard “screamed.” The escalation is mostly internal but maps cleanly onto the external clock of the solar event, and the moral calculus (his desire vs. the lives he can sense) is sharply drawn. His refusal is unmistakably a high-cost act of agency, not a default. If anything, the universe-scale consequence is handled almost too smoothly for the brevity of the scene, but it doesn’t feel hollow.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>8.3</grade><comment>Themes of mortality, surrender, and the nature of “goodbye” emerge organically from the situation and Vaelen’s history rather than from imposed allegory. However, the last paragraph tilts into explicit thematic statement (“Goodbye, he realized, was not a threshold…”) that edges toward essayistic moralizing rather than letting the image and choice alone carry the weight. The resonance is strong, but a touch more obliqueness would deepen it.</comment>
