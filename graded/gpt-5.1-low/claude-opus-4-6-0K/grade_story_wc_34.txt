<question>2</question><grade>8.2</grade><comment>The piece follows a clean accumulative structure: arrival, intensifying messages during lightning, translation, song, and a reconfigured courtyard. Closure is a resonant reconfiguration—“Silence that felt full” and the graffiti no longer desperate—and the before/after is legible. The only limitation is the absence of any real structural snag or complication; the path from intent to success is smooth.</comment>
<question>3</question><grade>8.7</grade><comment>The setting is richly particular: “misty courtyard floated above the clouds,” slick flagstones, luminous shifting murals, and the lightning/thunder dynamic all feel lived-in and specific. Environmental rules are learned through action (the glow between lightning and thunder) rather than exposition. The courtyard and the bolt plate as tool both charge the final image, where the place “holds their shared language like a lantern holds its flame.” It’s atmospheric and functional, though an additional moment where the physical environment constrains Lira’s options would push it even higher.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>7.2</grade><comment>The dominant stake—whether the ghost’s encoded loneliness can be truly heard and answered—is clear early and deepens via the new symbols and the plea “SING WHAT I CANNOT SAY.” Pressure escalates modestly through the intensified glow and Lira’s act of opening herself as conduit. However, there is little real resistance or risk; nothing meaningfully threatens failure or imposes a sharp trade-off in the moment. The resolution feels earned but low-friction, with the cost of serving as vessel implied rather than dramatized.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>8.4</grade><comment>Themes of voice, translation, and the ache of being unheard emerge organically from details like “binary sequences disguised as the veins of leaves” and the villagers who never quite hear Lira’s songs. Some lines veer toward explicit statement (“the longing to be heard, to matter, to connect...”), slightly reducing subtlety. Still, the emotional aftereffect—quiet, grateful connection—lingers convincingly.</comment>

Rhythm modulates well, with longer, lush sentences for atmosphere and slightly tenser cadence around the lightning beats. The voice is strong enough to be memorable without tipping into self-conscious flourish.</comment>

There’s little scaffolding or paraphrase looping, and the final paragraph lands with a clean, reweighted image rather than explanation. A few phrases edge toward purple, but they don’t obscure sense or closure.</comment>

While the core theme (longing to be heard) is familiar, the chosen particulars—the flower press bolt plate, the lightning interval, the mist-top courtyard—keep it from feeling generic. It’s inventive and coherent, if not radically risk-taking in structure.</comment>
