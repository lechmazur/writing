<question>2</question><grade>8.9</grade><comment>The structure is clean and causal: purposeful wandering → discovery of sundial → decision to rebuild → installation in the orchard → charged final gesture. Closure is a resonant reconfiguration—she re-enters a ritual posture with a new, wary belief, and the world seems to meet her halfway through the warm ground. The stakes and costs are subtle but legible in that moment of vulnerability to a faith she has partly rejected; nothing essential feels missing or arbitrary in the arc.</comment>
<question>3</question><grade>8.8</grade><comment>The catacomb orchard is strikingly original and sensorially vivid, with roots “threaded through ribcages” and fruit trees above that metabolize the dead; this environment is inseparable from both plot and theme. Setting details are consistently filtered through Adaeze’s sensibility as a returnee and archaeologist, and the recurring earth/roots imagery directly charges the final image of her palms on the warm ground. The one constraint keeping this shy of perfection is that the physical layout never has to actively constrain tactics; it’s an atmospheric and thematic driver more than a logistical one.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>8.2</grade><comment>The dominant stake—whether Adaeze can meaningfully reconnect with her ancestral faith and line—is clear and threaded throughout. Escalation comes from her shift from passive volunteer to active restorer of a forbidden ritual instrument, culminating in the risky act of enacting a remembered gesture. The conflict remains internal and fairly low-tempo, with little counterforce beyond her own numbness, but within a quiet, accumulative design it still achieves one solid tightening.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>8.8</grade><comment>Interrupted journeys, colonial suppression, and ancestral continuity through the land cohere into a strong thematic weave: the buried sundial, the banned priestesses, the diaspora child returning as archaeologist. The final realization—that “it had been waiting”—reprices both time and tradition without stating a moral, reweighting the earlier images of roots, bones, and stolen rituals. The nuance is high, though the piece leans slightly toward a single hopeful direction rather than embracing more jagged ambivalence about return.</comment>
<question>6</question><grade>8.7</grade><comment>The close third-person voice is confident, lyrical without blur, and distinctly Adaeze’s in its mixture of material specificity and spiritual hunger. There’s no POV leakage or awkward explainers; if anything, the voice is so consistently elevated that it slightly smooths the roughness one might expect at moments of sharper doubt.</comment>
<question>7</question><grade>8.8</grade><comment>Line-level craft is excellent: sentences are varied, images precise, and most lines do at least two jobs (world-building plus emotion, for example “a sundial wrenched from sunlight and swallowed by earth”). A few “as though” constructions and explicit thematic echoes (e.g., listing the interrupted journeys) edge toward telling what the images already imply, which holds this just under the very top band.</comment>

It’s not formally radical, but within a traditional single-scene frame the imaginative synthesis is distinctive and memorable.</comment>
