<question>2</question><grade>8.5</grade><comment>Structure is clean: an initially solitary working night is interrupted by Kosta’s arrival, which leads to collaboration and culminates in the plankton “fireflies” and Marina’s conceptual shift about belonging. Closure lands as a resonant reconfiguration: her definition of belonging changes and is immediately enacted by staying and sharing her feather. The cost is mostly emotional and understated (risking her privacy and sleep), which suits the piece but keeps it just shy of truly seismic.</comment>
<question>3</question><grade>8.8</grade><comment>The underwater research facility and drowned city are vividly and economically rendered, from pressure-treated domes to silted courtyards and bioluminescent plankton “galaxies.” Setting is always filtered through Marina’s professional and emotional lens, which gives details like the processional avenue and summer horizon orientation double duty as worldbuilding and character interiority. The environment constrains options (no sun, only simulated dawn, observation instead of direct contact) and directly charges the final image of them watching the glow together with the feather on the console. This is exemplary setting work, though the physical environment shapes mood and perception more than concrete tactical choices.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>7.8</grade><comment>The central stake—whether Marina will remain an outsider or find a foothold of belonging—is clear from early on and coherently threaded. Pressure deepens when Kosta appears and asks to see her work, forcing her to decide whether to reveal her idiosyncratic system; the subsequent collaboration and shared viewing of the plankton bloom are meaningful escalations in intimacy. However, the conflict remains entirely gentle—there is little sense of what she truly risks by opening up beyond continued loneliness, and no serious friction between them. The result is a satisfying but low-intensity arc rather than a sharply tested one.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>8.0</grade><comment>Themes of belonging, translation between ways of seeing, and finding “fire” in a drowned world emerge organically from the feather, the star-ruin alignments, and the shared observation of the plankton. That said, some lines tilt toward explicit thesis (“Belonging wasn’t a rhythm you learned by listening”) and momentarily edge into mild didacticism rather than letting the images alone carry the weight. Overall, the emotional aftereffect is tender and contemplative, with complexity appropriate to the length.</comment>

Dialogue is sparse but natural, with Kosta’s brief lines contrasting effectively with Marina’s more elaborate explanations. There is no head-hopping or unjustified omniscience; everything we know flows plausibly from Marina’s perceptions.</comment>

A few phrases (“shadowseam where wonder lived,” “permanent twilight”) flirt with lushness bordering on purple, but they don’t derail clarity or momentum. The closing paragraph in particular braids object, light, and emotional shift with precision.</comment>
