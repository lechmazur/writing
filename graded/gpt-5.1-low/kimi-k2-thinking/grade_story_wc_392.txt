Closure lands via a Decisive Change (refusal and transmission of evidence) with an implied cost of retaliation, even if that cost is not fully dramatized. <question>3</question><grade>8.4</grade><comment>The tunnel, water seepage, rust, and darkness are vividly drawn and consistently filtered through Mara’s professional eye and family history. The lantern functions as a micro-setting object that constrains how she moves and literally/figuratively lights her choices, charging the final image. A slightly broader sensory palette (sound and touch are good; smell and temperature are thinner) is the only thing keeping this from the very top band.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>7.5</grade><comment>The central stake—truth and worker safety versus career/physical risk under Voss’s control—is clear from early on and tightens as she uncovers sabotage and the ignored report. The external conflict remains mostly offstage (Voss is a voice, not a body in the space), which keeps the tension more conceptual than visceral. Still, the narrowing of options is evident and the final refusal feels like a genuine test of commitment rather than a formality.</comment>

Nonetheless, the thematic throughline is coherent, organically emerges from choices, and leaves a resonant aftertaste.</comment>
