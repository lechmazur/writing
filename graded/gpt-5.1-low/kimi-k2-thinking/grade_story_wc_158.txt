<question>4</question><grade>5.5</grade><comment>The dominant stake—ending the merfolk war through a blossom armistice—is articulated early, and there is a nominal escalation from lone singing to merfolk gathering and peace blooming. Once the merfolk first lay down their spears, the pressure softens into reaffirmations rather than new complications or trade-offs. The fragility of peace is told but not materially tested, so the emotional cost of her continued vigilance stays conceptual.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>7.0</grade><comment>Themes of transforming pain into beauty, the endurance of love across vast distances, and the fragility-but-possibility of peace are coherent and woven through recurring motifs (photosynthesis metaphor, blossoms, postcard, reef-shadow joy). The moral/emotional argument feels sincere but somewhat smoothed, with limited counter-pressure or ambiguity.</comment>

There is good control of distance, staying close enough to her sensibility without overt authorial intrusion. <question>7</question><grade>7.0</grade><comment>The prose is generally strong, rhythmic, and image-rich; many lines efficiently carry setting, mood, and theme at once. Despite this, clarity remains high and there are few, if any, outright muddled metaphors. Tightening redundancy and varying image vehicles would sharpen momentum without sacrificing lyricism.</comment>

The narrative risks a fully lyric, low-incident structure and largely pulls it off in terms of mood and imagistic logic. Its main limitation is that conflict and cost stay gentler and more abstract than the originality of the premise would allow at elite-publication level.</comment>
