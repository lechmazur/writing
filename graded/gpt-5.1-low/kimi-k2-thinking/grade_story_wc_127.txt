<question>2</question><grade>7.4</grade><comment>The structure is clean and linear: arrival, preparation, administration, observation, aftermath and walk back to the city. The cost is conceptual and emotional rather than eventful, but the “residue” of emotion in the dealer and the client’s impending morning void are clear. <question>3</question><grade>7.8</grade><comment>The amber grotto is vividly rendered and tonally apt—“honeyed light pooling on stone,” resin scent, water drip—and functions as more than a white room. The brief glimpse of city lights as “a constellation of strangers’ stories” nicely extends setting into theme. A slightly clearer sense of the grotto’s practical limits or dangers could have made it even more structurally integral.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>6.2</grade><comment>The dominant stake—can an artificial dose of empathy bridge an estranged relationship, and what does that mean for both men—is interesting but stays mostly conceptual. Pressure doesn’t so much escalate as gently continue; there’s no real narrowing of options or on-page trade-off, only a deepening of a foreknown outcome. This makes the ending thoughtful but not particularly tense.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>6.8</grade><comment>Themes of authenticity versus artifice, transactional empathy, and the ethics of emotional engineering are strong and coherent. The result is resonant but a bit over-articulated for top-tier literary work.</comment>

<question>7</question><grade>7.2</grade><comment>The line-level craft is generally strong: good rhythm, clear syntax, and images that are concrete and memorable without frequent confusion. The emotional arc (a professional empathizer who cannot fully feel) is familiar but handled with specificity. It stops short of a truly surprising turn, but the synthesis of elements is memorable and coherent.</comment>
