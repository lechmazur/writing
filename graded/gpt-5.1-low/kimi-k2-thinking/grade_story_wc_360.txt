The environment doesn’t strongly constrain his choices, but it does meaningfully charge the final image when the stars vanish and only the workshop’s residual glow remains. World rules (halo fragment, transposition) are consistent and learned through on-page action rather than exposition.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>7.2</grade><comment>The dominant stake—whether Cassian can reduce empathy to manipulable mathematics—escalates as he moves from one subject to two and then faces the unintended reversal of the transposition. The cost surfaces in his hollowed state, the halo fragment’s depletion, and the cosmic-scale echo of vanishing stars, which deepen the sense of risk beyond a mere intellectual exercise. However, the tension stays largely conceptual; there’s little sense of immediate danger or high-stakes failure beyond his own disappointment and disorientation. The piece achieves one clear deepening turn (the empathy reversal), but does not fully exploit the ethical or interpersonal stakes its premise suggests.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>7.4</grade><comment>Thematically, the story engages rich questions about empathy as a bidirectional current and the limits of reducing feeling to formal systems, and it earns a nuanced final stance: an open problem rather than a solved theorem. However, several lines lapse into overt statement—“He realized the experiment was flawed; emotion could not be solved like a linear equation”—which flattens what the imagery and events already imply. The result is resonant but slightly over-explicit, where a touch more reliance on subtext would strengthen it.</comment>
