The setting constrains tactics—her music depends on that particular glass and environment—and it charges the final image of the conservatory as “cathedral.” A bit more tactile interaction with the space under stress could push this even higher, but it’s already strong and integral.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>7.0</grade><comment>The dominant stake—whether Mara can find a non-auditory “music” to counter the world’s suffocating silence—is clear, and the tuning-fork experiment is a meaningful deepening. However, the conflict is mostly internal and contemplative, with no on-page resistance beyond ambient grief; every step works on the first try, and no alternative path is visibly foreclosed. The emotional escalation from private experiment to communal ritual is real but gentle, lacking a sharp test or sacrifice. As a result, the piece has lovely pressure but not gripping tension.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>8.2</grade><comment>Theme—silence as medium rather than void, beauty persisting under constraint, grief converted into shared language—emerges organically from recurring objects (forks, glass, frost) and Mara’s evolving stance. That said, the prose sometimes states its thesis too explicitly (“The silence was no longer a void; it was a canvas”), undercutting subtlety. The emotional aftereffect is gentle and contemplative rather than wrenching but still resonant.</comment>

The story commits to its premise and follows the implications—no cheating with sudden restored sound.
