<question>4</question><grade>7.4</grade><comment>The central stake—whether the artist can responsibly give form to the community’s erased trauma—is clear, and pressure coheres around the tile’s imperative to “complete the circle, but break it at the seventh turn.” The main escalation is her decision to act on that instruction, transforming a passive listening posture into an intervention that literally reconfigures the mandala and grotto. However, the danger remains mostly implied; the “ravenous” grotto and potential harm to villagers are suggested rather than tested in concrete, escalating obstacles. The result is intellectually engaging but somewhat low in visceral tension.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>8.3</grade><comment>Theme emerges organically from action: how communities metabolize fear and trauma, and how art can give silence a generative rather than oppressive form. There is slight over-clarity in lines like “Her motivation had never been mere curiosity,” which edges toward telling the thesis, but the final paradox—that the grotto remembers while the village forgets—lands with resonant complexity. The piece invites reflection on the ethics of making buried pain legible without dictating a moral.</comment>
