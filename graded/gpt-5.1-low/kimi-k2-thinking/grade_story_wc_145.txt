However, internal contradiction is minimal: we get little sense of fear, doubt, or competing desires, so his final self-sacrificial transformation lands more as mythic fulfillment than as a hard-won, conflicted choice. The main weakness is that the “cost” of leaving the outpost life and entering the rivers remains more conceptual than felt; we’re told the maps will be redrawn and crews will find him gone, but the loss doesn’t bite deeply on the page. Still, patterning around the falcon, condensation, and convergence pays off coherently.</comment>

<question>4</question><grade>7.4</grade><comment>The dominant stake (whether Kael will merely map endings or actually find/become the true source) is present and does tighten as the falcon returns and the condensation pattern gives him a new option. As a result, the cost reads more like an inevitable rite than a pressured dilemma.</comment>
