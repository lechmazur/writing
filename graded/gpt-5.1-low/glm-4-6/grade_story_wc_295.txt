<question>2</question><grade>6.7</grade><comment>The causal spine is straightforward: insomnia and research → unexpected witness of Whitmore → revelation of prophecy → decision to collaborate. Structurally it’s sound but feels relatively safe and low-cost.</comment>
<question>3</question><grade>7.3</grade><comment>The observatory, telescope, brass fittings, and star charts create a vivid, coherent atmosphere that suits the material, and the daylilies give a specific, tactile anchor. The environment largely supports mood rather than constraining choices, though the presence of charts and photographs gives plausible tools for their decoding work. Strongly atmospheric and specific, but not yet indispensable to the conflict.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>5.8</grade><comment>The dominant stake—whether Elara will be drawn into a dangerous, prophetic legacy—appears, but the danger is mostly implied. The main tightening is her catching Whitmore at her charts, which forces him to disclose the prophecy and accept her involvement. Conflict resolves quickly and amicably once they speak; there’s little real resistance, ethical friction, or visible cost on the page beyond shared grief. Tension is present but modest and never truly escalates.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>6.5</grade><comment>Themes of shared burden, grief, and transforming isolating knowledge into collaborative responsibility are coherent and organically linked to the temple/variable star conceit. However, the piece leans into explicit statement (“the shared burden wasn’t hers alone,” “transforming a frightening burden into manageable knowledge”) that edges toward moralizing rather than trusting subtext. The resonance is genuine but somewhat tidy and on-the-nose.</comment>
<question>6</question><grade>6.6</grade><comment>The close third-person POV is stable and clear, with a calm, slightly elevated narrative voice that suits the reflective tone. Elara’s perceptual lens (stars, dreams, inscriptions) is consistent, though her voice isn’t strongly individuated beyond role. Overall, the voice is controlled and competent but not yet distinctive enough for elite venues.</comment>
<question>7</question><grade>7.0</grade><comment>The prose is clean, grammatically sound, and generally well-paced, with a few effective images (“brass fittings gleaming in the moonlight,” “pulsing dance beyond the telescope lens”). Still, sentences usually carry multiple functions (setting plus emotion plus plot) and avoid obvious purple excess. Line-level craft is solid but not strikingly economical or surprising.</comment>
