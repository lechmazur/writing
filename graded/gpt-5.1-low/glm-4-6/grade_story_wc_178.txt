The cost—risk of inspectors, leaving the tile behind, subsuming himself into the covenant—is mostly implied rather than sharply dramatized, which limits impact at an elite level.</comment>

Description is lush but mostly purposeful, with the setting’s liminal decay mirroring dynastic and spiritual transition.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>6.3</grade><comment>The dominant stake—completing the cosmic pact before the new regime strips the site—is clear, but opposition stays largely notional (inspectors never arrive; nothing interrupts the ritual). Escalation is mostly a gentle tightening of time and atmosphere toward the alignment rather than a pressured sequence of constrained options. As a result, the climax reads more as a foregone consummation than a choice with sharply felt alternatives.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>7.6</grade><comment>Themes of continuity versus regime change, empirical inquiry as a form of devotion, and the reimagining of the “unknown” as covenant rather than void are woven into the images and Elias’s stance. However, the piece edges into thesis-like phrasing (“empirically ironic truth,” “encrypted devotion”) that states its ideas a bit too directly for the most rarefied literary venues.</comment>

There are mild tendencies toward echo and abstraction, but no damaging POV slips or register shocks.</comment>
