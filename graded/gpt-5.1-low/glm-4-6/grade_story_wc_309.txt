The recurring verticality (below: soulless systems; above: sacred, impossible magic) charges the final image of him descending changed. Internal conflict is relatively subdued; his faith is never seriously tested in-scene. As a result the escalation reads more as a steady procession than a tightening, high-cost trial.</comment>

The emotional aftereffect is genuine but a bit didactic rather than earned purely through image and implication.</comment>

There’s no head-hopping or stance confusion, and psychic distance is steady. While effective, the voice doesn’t quite achieve a uniquely idiosyncratic texture that would make it unmistakable among other literary fantasies.</comment>

<question>8</question><grade>7.2</grade><comment>The premise of human, imperfect craft versus automated precision is familiar, but here it’s given a fresh magical treatment via the keystone, mist walking, and the notion of spells existing “outside measurable reality.” The idea of a stone that “accommodates impossibilities” and a manual, belief-based magic system has conceptual charm. Nonetheless, the thematic arc—art/soul versus automation/perfection—tracks closely with genre norms and doesn’t take a notably surprising ethical or emotional turn.
