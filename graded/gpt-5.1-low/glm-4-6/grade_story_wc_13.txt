<question>4</question><grade>7.2</grade><comment>The dominant stake—preserve or redeem lost lives/echoes before the giant wakes and erases everything again—is clearly established, and pressure escalates well with the interrupted cadence and ticking end of the nap. <question>5</question><grade>7.8</grade><comment>Themes of memory, extinction, cyclical destruction, and the transformation of loss into new life emerge organically from the mechanics of echoes, time-borrowing, and the giant’s nap. The piece avoids explicit moralizing and lets its world-logic carry ideas about resistance to inevitability and the cost of breaking out of loops. The resolution leans slightly toward tidy optimism, muting some potential ambivalence about what’s been destroyed to enable this rebirth.</comment>

Momentum is maintained despite long sentences, in part because transitions are causal and concrete. There are no serious loops, scaffolding, or syntactic artifacts that would disqualify it from a strong market.</comment>

The ending’s conceptual repricing of echoes as seeds of life is both surprising and retrospectively grounded in the premise.
