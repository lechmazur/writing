<question>4</question><grade>6.8</grade><comment>The dominant stake—whether he can complete the statue’s reconstitution and thus resist oblivion—is clear, and the sudden submersion is a meaningful escalation. Internal and external pressures echo thematically but do not force a sharp choice between competing goods or harms. The ending resolves on a conceptual victory more than on a hard-won, costly success, so tension doesn’t fully peak.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>7.0</grade><comment>Theme—brokenness as a site of beauty and survival, kintsugi as metaphysic rather than just craft—is clear and organically tied to the act of matching scars to memories. However, the prose drifts into explicit thesis statements (“beauty lives not in perfection but in survival,” “not about saving the statue… but about showing oblivion itself”) that flatten subtext into moral. More reliance on implication and image, less on paraphrasing the message, would lift this substantially.</comment>
