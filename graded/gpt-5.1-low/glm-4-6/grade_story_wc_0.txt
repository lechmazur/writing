<question>3</question><grade>8.5</grade><comment>The greenhouse and flood-wrecked world are described with sensory precision (“condensation… fractured glass,” “mud-clogged streets”), and the neutron stars overhead deepen the thematic contrast. The greenhouse functions as a sanctum that both constrains and enables her work, and the environment (broken glass, drowned orchids) is literally what her power acts upon. This integration of place, image, and action gives the final panorama of stars vs. greenhouse strong atmospheric charge.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>5.8</grade><comment>The dominant stake—guiding reality and others’ pain toward “humble grace” without abrupt, chaotic repair—is clear but only lightly pressured. Elara’s choice to work on Mr. Gable is framed as routine extension of her philosophy rather than a fraught decision with alternatives or risk. There is no real escalation of conflict; nothing seriously threatens her method, her secrecy, or her relationship with him. As a result, tension stays low and the piece reads more as a meditative vignette than as a conflict-driven story.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>8.0</grade><comment>Thematically, the contrast between violent cosmic events and slow, merciful decay is rich and sustained, with the butterfly dust as an elegant emblem of transformation and gentleness. The idea of “gradual change” versus catastrophic fixes infuses her methods and the post-flood setting. Some thematic material edges toward explicit exposition (e.g., direct analogies to star spin and flares), but it generally arises from her lived experience rather than authorial lecturing. The final image of her quiet prayer with the dust against the backdrop of neutron stars offers a resonant, non-literal answer about how to live with devastation.</comment>

Rhythm and syntax are mostly well-controlled, with a good mix of long, sinuous sentences and tighter phrases. At times the narration edges toward essayistic explanation, but it still reads as Elara’s reflective, trained mind rather than generic exposition.</comment>
<question>7</question><grade>8.2</grade><comment>The prose is polished, with high information density and strong, concrete images (the dust, cracked terracotta pot, jagged glass) doing multiple jobs at once. There is some risk of lyrical overload (multiple metaphor-laden sentences in sequence), but the comparisons are coherent and thematically apt rather than purple for its own sake. Grammar and flow are clean, and the final line lands with clarity rather than obscured by ornament.</comment>
<question>8</question><grade>8.4</grade><comment>The synthesis of a neutron star researcher who uses butterfly-wing dust to “preprocess reality” toward gradual decay is genuinely fresh. Emotional truth—quiet kindness, resistance to drama—grounds the conceptual novelty. The main constraint is that the storyline itself remains fairly static and could take more structural risks to match the originality of the premise.</comment>
