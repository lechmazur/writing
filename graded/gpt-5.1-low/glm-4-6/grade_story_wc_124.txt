<question>2</question><grade>7.9</grade><comment>The causal chain is clean: his longstanding grief → targeted experiment → lunar-timed bloom → UV revelation → captured shadow scent → conceptual shift at the end. Closure lands as a resonant reconfiguration: he ends with a new understanding of his sorrow embodied in the vial, and the cost (facing the father-memory) is legible. The main limitation is lack of complicating turns or tactical adjustments—the experiment works on first pass, keeping structure solid but not maximally dynamic.</comment>
<question>3</question><grade>8.2</grade><comment>The greenhouse for extinct plants is vividly and economically rendered—the glass panes, crystal shadows, central terrarium, and Lunaria resurgentia all feel particular and purposeful. The environment interacts with action (he lays tools on a stone bench; the moonbeam activates the flower) and charges the final image (vial to moonlight), rather than sitting as a backdrop. It could push higher if physical constraints of the space more directly pressured his tactics or choices in the climax.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>7.2</grade><comment>The dominant stake—whether he can face and anatomize the sorrow bound to certain scents—appears early and remains coherent, with a meaningful deepening when the UV reveals not just the flower but his father’s coat/perfume memory. Pressure escalates texturally as the transformation completes and the hidden scent-layer appears, forcing him into an emotional confrontation. However, there’s little resistance or risk of failure: no serious chance the experiment might misfire, or that he might reject the revelation or mishandle the capture. The result is a satisfying but relatively smooth arc of pressure rather than a sharply tested one.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>8.0</grade><comment>Themes of memory, grief, and how sensory triggers carry layered losses are woven through events (the UV method, father’s coat scent, the rare flower) rather than spelled out as thesis. The final image—a vial holding the ultraviolet shadow of a scent, “the reason why a smell could break a man’s heart”—offers a resonant, non-literal answer about understanding and possibly healing. The story avoids overt moralizing and lets the conceptual framing of “visible light of a scent vs. invisible shadow” do thematic work. It could gain further complexity by complicating his father or the promise represented by that perfume, resisting the relatively tidy emotional framing.</comment>
<question>6</question><grade>8.0</grade><comment>The close third-person voice is stable, with a lyrical, slightly elevated diction that suits a “wistful lantern broker” obsessing over scent and light; metaphors (crystal shadows, fragrances as ghosts) feel coherent with his worldview. A few abstract phrases (“complex architecture of his own sorrow”) skim toward essayistic, but they’re brief and don’t break voice plausibility. Overall, the perceptual signature—scientist-of-emotion with artisanal tools—is distinct and maintained through the climax.</comment>
<question>7</question><grade>7.7</grade><comment>The prose is polished, mostly concise, and rhythmically varied, with many sentences doing double-duty for atmosphere and character. Still, there are no major loops or muddled metaphors, and line-level craft supports rather than obscures the closure.</comment>
