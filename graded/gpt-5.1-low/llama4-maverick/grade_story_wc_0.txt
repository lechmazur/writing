<question>5</question><grade>6.0</grade><comment>Themes of gradual change, decay as both destructive and creative, and reality “preprocessing itself” are clear and coherent, with the butterfly dust and flood sediment providing apt images. Subtext is minimal; the piece tells us what its symbols mean rather than letting them accrue implication through dramatized tension. The closing sense of peace does offer some resonance, but without much ambiguity or felt cost.</comment>

Reflection dominates, yet temporal and spatial control remain clear. There’s little modulation of voice under pressure because the story never truly pushes Maria into a stressed or destabilized state.</comment>
<question>7</question><grade>6.3</grade><comment>The prose is grammatical, fluent, and largely clear, with some pleasant images (“delicate scales glinting in the faint light”). Many sentences summarize ideas instead of advancing scene or character, which flattens momentum. There’s no egregious purple prose or syntactic confusion, but line-level craft doesn’t yet reach the precision or density expected for elite literary publication.</comment>
<question>8</question><grade>6.5</grade><comment>The juxtaposition of a neutron star researcher in a flood-damaged greenhouse, meditating on decay and dust as a kind of cosmic preprocessor, is a fresh and promising conceptual frame. It feels like a thoughtful sketch of a more ambitious piece rather than a fully realized, distinctive story.</comment>
