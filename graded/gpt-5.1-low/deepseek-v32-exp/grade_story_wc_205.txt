Still, the sensory palette leans heavily visual and atmospheric, with limited tactile or bodily grounding in the environment, especially during high-pressure moments. A bit more lived-in interaction with Veridia’s physicality would elevate this further.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>6.2</grade><comment>The dominant stake—a contest over how a civilization is understood and whether Elara’s poetic reading will matter—remains mostly intellectual and lightly contested. The dispute “reaches a climax” off-stage, so we’re told of conflict rather than feeling it tighten. As a result, escalation is present but relatively soft, with limited sense of what Elara truly stands to lose beyond reputation.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>7.0</grade><comment>Themes of interpretation, history as poetry, and the necessity of blocking out distractions to see truth are coherent and well-motifed through mirrors, storms, and the shard. However, several lines state the thesis directly (“truth often lay in the spaces between facts,” “The method…became a symbol”), which flattens subtext into commentary. The ending is resonant but edges toward neat moral rather than complex ambiguity.</comment>

There are a few repeated constructions and thematic statements that give the prose a slightly essayistic cast.</comment>
<question>7</question><grade>6.8</grade><comment>The line-level writing is clean and grammatically sound, with a mostly smooth rhythm and a clear progression from sentence to sentence. Many crucial beats are handled in compressed exposition rather than dramatized, which blunts immediacy and momentum. <question>8</question><grade>8.0</grade><comment>The synthesis of visible time, archaeological controversy, and a revolutionary-turned-poetic-interpreter is genuinely fresh, particularly the experiment where time strands project the past via the shard.
