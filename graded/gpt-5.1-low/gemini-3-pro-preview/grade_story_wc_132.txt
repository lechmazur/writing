<question>2</question><grade>8.5</grade><comment>The causal spine is clean: accidental exposure → escalating symptoms and failed control → suffocating greenhouse → storm-yard collapse → realization that resistance kills → surrender. No orientation issues; time and space remain legible. The structure is straightforward rather than daring, but very solid for the length.</comment>
<question>3</question><grade>8.8</grade><comment>The setting is vivid and functional: the Victorian greenhouse, oxygen-heavy air, storm, and mossy trellis all shape his physical experience and choices. Environmental details (hyper-oxygen, rain absorption, trellis and moss cradling his spine) are tightly tied to the metamorphosis and the thematic pivot from threat to nourishment. The recurring object-as-setting—the linden leaf—charges the final image. This is near top-tier work; a marginally more surprising or concretely grounded greenhouse detail could push it into true exceptional territory.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>8.4</grade><comment>The dominant stake—retain human identity vs. survive via transformation—is established early and tightened as his body hardens and the air becomes unbreathable. Conflict escalates as his attempts to “fix” himself actively worsen his suffering, culminating in the revelation that his insistence on reconstruction is killing him. The climax is his renunciation of control and research, which forecloses the human path in exchange for continued existence. The emotional and ethical calculus is strong, though a more explicit glimpse of what he’s forfeiting (beyond abstract “humanity”) could sharpen the price.</comment>

A few lines (“You cannot reverse a season by yelling at the wind”; “the observer must eventually become the participant”) tread close to stated thesis, slightly reducing subtlety. Overall, the resonance is strong and lingers.</comment>

At times, the register flirts with ornate, but it remains controlled and character-justified.</comment>
