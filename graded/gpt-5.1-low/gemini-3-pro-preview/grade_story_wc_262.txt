<question>2</question><grade>8.2</grade><comment>The structure is cleanly accumulative: set-up of space and motive, acquisition and reading of the strip, incubation, transfer, and a reweighted final image. Causality is intact at each step (can’t project → must touch → vision → warming → transfer into lens). Closure is a conceptual repricing: the discovery that unused love persists as stored light, with the strip’s spent state as cost. The drive lens (accumulative/textural) holds consistently through to the ending.</comment>
<question>3</question><grade>8.9</grade><comment>The projection booth is vividly realized in smell, sound, and function, and the 1940s station emerges organically via Elara’s gift rather than exposition. Crucially, the setting constrains tactics (the projector lamp would destroy the strip) and then charges the ending by making the lens the literal repository of love. Weather (“hushed thunder,” pressure drop) interacts with the ritual, reinforcing the liminal tone rather than sitting as backdrop. The specificity is rich yet purposeful, rarely feeling like cataloguing.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>7.4</grade><comment>The dominant stake—locating where unused love goes—is clear and present throughout, and tension builds via the intensifying storm, the swelling emotional weight in her hands, and the risk of losing the strip’s ghost. Escalation is more atmospheric than oppositional; no antagonist pushes back, so pressure comes from timing and the delicacy of the ritual. Her agency in choosing to siphon the energy and accept the strip’s inertness provides a credible, if quiet, price. The piece stops short of sharper internal or external resistance that might lift the stakes further.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>8.5</grade><comment>Themes of emotional residue, archival love, and the afterlife of abandoned gestures emerge naturally from the goodbye letters, the old film medium, and the final amber nebula. Motifs (letters, film, projector lens, thunder) return with shifted weight at the end, especially the projector as both machine and reliquary. The resonance is strong and complex without collapsing into moralizing.</comment>

Rhythm varies well, with denser stretches for visionary moments and cleaner lines at closure. The prose’s texture is distinctive enough to read as a specific consciousness rather than generic lyricism.</comment>

<question>8</question><grade>8.8</grade><comment>The fusion of occult psychometry, analog film technology, and the metaphysics of “unused love” feels genuinely fresh. Risks in diction and conceit (e.g., “waiting for a screen large enough to display it”) largely pay off as specific rather than quirky for its own sake. The piece commits fully to its premise and follows its implications to a satisfying, singular closure.</comment>
