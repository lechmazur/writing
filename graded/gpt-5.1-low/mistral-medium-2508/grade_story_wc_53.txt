Environmental details like the skeletal bridge below concretize Lira’s failed ambitions in space. A touch more tactile interaction with the decayed carousel during the decision to jump could link the opening image even more tightly to the ending.</comment>

The cost is legible: she allows herself to be taken by the very past she’s tried to quarantine. Conflict remains mostly internal/psychic; a slightly sharper articulation of what she risks by *not* jumping would amplify the trade-off.</comment>

The final surrender “just until the carousel started again” invites complex readings about cyclical trauma versus renewal without moralizing. A slightly more individuated beat for one past self could further sharpen the ethical cost of leaving them “behind.”</comment>

Rhythm modulates well between longer, image-heavy sentences and tighter, cutting lines at turns (“The lagoon didn’t ripple. It remembered.”). The story commits fully to its weirdness without over-explaining, and the cyclical hint of the carousel restarting avoids both neat redemption and simple nihilism. It stops just shy of utterly singular by leaning on a few familiar regret tropes, but the synthesis reads publication-ready.</comment>
