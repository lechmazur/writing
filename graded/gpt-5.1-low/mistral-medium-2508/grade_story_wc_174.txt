The story leans heavily on reflection over on-page action, but it remains anchored and doesn’t drift. Stakes feel scaled to an internal, artistic dilemma, which is apt but limits the sense of structural urgency.</comment>
<question>3</question><grade>8.7</grade><comment>The decommissioned radar dome, salt flats, and *Static Hound* are vividly rendered and filtered through Elias’s sensibility, with strong sensory and tonal specificity. The recurring campfire and salt-flat imagery further deepen atmosphere without feeling like generic scenery. The environment doesn’t strongly constrain physical choices, but it decisively charges the thematic and emotional frame.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>7.2</grade><comment>The central conflict—whether his manipulative art harms people and how he should change—is clear and meaningfully sharpened by the miner’s outburst and question. The cost of loosening control is implied rather than dramatized, so the stakes remain somewhat abstract. Overall, tension is steady and thoughtful but rarely feels acute.</comment>

Subtext is present in the setting/object choices, but the piece occasionally explains what its images already convey.</comment>

<question>7</question><grade>8.2</grade><comment>Line-level craft is high: sentences are varied, metaphorical vehicles are mostly coherent, and almost every line does double duty for mood and character. <question>8</question><grade>8.5</grade><comment>The combination of an urbane calligrapher, subversive puppet shows in a seedy bar, and a Cold War radar dome repurposed as a listening shrine feels fresh and non-template.
