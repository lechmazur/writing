<question>2</question><grade>8.7</grade><comment>The structure is clean: one continuous sequence that moves from corridor entry to shrine discovery to reconfigured relationship with memory. Causality is tight—each new echo or object (hum, negative, door, mural) leads logically to the next choice, and the realization that “the walls didn’t just dream. They remembered.” is both earned and decisive. Closure lands as a resonant reconfiguration: his role shifts from mapmaker of echoes to active participant in his family’s remembered life, with a clear before/after. The cost (accepting the legacy and its pain) is legible without being overstated.</comment>
<question>3</question><grade>8.9</grade><comment>The ivy-veiled passage, damp stone, sealed door, and shrine-like room are vividly rendered and tightly integrated with Elias’s perception. Environmental elements constrain and shape choices—the split passage, the sealed door, the ivy-throttled path—and recur meaningfully (the ivy at dawn, the vanished door, the glowing mural). World hints like drowned coasts and acid rain are light but sufficient to place this in a distinct ruined future without bogging the scene. The final threshold image is strongly charged by the physical setting, though the broader climate-collapse backdrop remains more atmospheric than pressuring.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>8.0</grade><comment>The primary stake—whether Elias will confront or continue to sidestep the burden of familial memory—is clear and intensifies from eerie curiosity to intimate confrontation. Pressure escalates effectively when he recognizes his mother’s eyes, hears his own name at the sealed door, and chooses to burn the varnish and enter. The final act of redrawing the mural as faces and stories credibly resolves the conflict by transforming his mapping practice into an act of acknowledgement. The stakes are largely internal and could bear one more concrete threat or loss to feel fully high-band in terms of cost.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>7.6</grade><comment>Themes of memory, legacy, and the ethics of recording versus living are well woven through objects (film negatives, half-burned diaries), images (thresholds, ivy, erased faces), and the echo-mapping conceit. The shift from “the house dreams in corners” to the realization that walls actively remember, and from mapping to living dreams, offers a genuine conceptual repricing. However, the final two sentences (“Some dreams weren’t meant to be mapped. Some were meant to be lived.”) verge on explicit thesis-statement, slightly flattening otherwise rich subtext. The piece still leaves a resonant aftertaste, but with a touch of moralizing on the surface.</comment>
<question>6</question><grade>8.5</grade><comment>The close third-person voice is lyrically charged yet controlled, with a clear perceptual signature rooted in sound, texture, and ruin. Diction and rhythm feel consistent with a quiet, obsessive listener; the narrative does not drift into expository or alien registers. There is no dialogue, but interior access is steady and never omniscient beyond Elias’s plausible inferences. Under rising pressure, the voice tightens rather than frays, maintaining clarity while sustaining a distinct, atmospheric tone.</comment>
<question>7</question><grade>8.8</grade><comment>Line-level craft is strong: sentences are varied, image-rich, and multi-functional, advancing mood, character, and world without evident scaffolding. Metaphors (e.g., the corridor’s heartbeat, the door’s paint like sunburnt skin) are apt and not overstacked, and there’s little to no synonym churn or repetition-for-padding. Rhythm modulates nicely between longer, meditative lines and shorter emphatic beats (“But the walls remembered.” “The door groaned open.”). A few phrases gesture toward familiar lyricism, but they don’t materially dull momentum or clarity.</comment>
<question>8</question><grade>8.6</grade><comment>The notion of a “corridor echo cartographer” in a climate-ravaged world is fresh, and the fusion of haunting, post-apocalyptic ruin with intimate familial memory feels distinctive. The story commits to its sonic/architectural premise instead of falling back on generic ghost tropes, and the wall-dreams/wall-remembers reconfiguration is non-obvious yet inevitable in hindsight. While the emotional core—child confronts legacy of dead parents—is familiar, it’s filtered through a highly specific practice and set of objects that make it feel new. The risk-taking is more conceptual than structural, but it pays off coherently.</comment>
