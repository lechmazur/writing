<question>1</question><grade>7.8</grade><comment>Daniel’s erasure and compulsion to collect others’ fragments create a coherent, particular inner life, and his final act of embedding the label/page feels motivated by that arc. However, his interior conflict stays mostly on one register—haunted, observant, resigned—without much on-page contradiction or struggle between competing impulses. The cost of his choice (leaning into ghosthood vs. reclaiming identity) is more implied than dramatized. The result is strong, but not yet as knotted or pressured as it could be for elite publication.</comment>
<question>2</question><grade>8.2</grade><comment>The accumulative, vignette structure is clear and coherent under a textural drive lens: rumors → deeper roaming → sleeplessness → ledger discovery → tidal offering. Closure lands as a resonant reconfiguration when Daniel inserts the bundle into the container and accepts the labyrinth’s recognition in place of civil existence. Causality is soft but consistent; each beat grows out of the previous one’s obsession rather than coincidence. A sharper hinge or on-page dilemma at the ledger discovery could elevate sense of structural inevitability.</comment>
<question>3</question><grade>8.7</grade><comment>The port and container labyrinth are vividly evoked (“salt and rust,” “labyrinth’s maw”), and the environment tangibly shapes events: shifting alleys, upper tiers, and flooding lower paths constrain Daniel’s movement and frame the climax. The setting is consistently filtered through his sensibility (hearing “layers” of sound, containers “leaning in”), and the final image of dawn on the label inside this space is charged. A slight increase in how physical constraints directly force micro-choices could nudge this into truly exceptional territory.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>7.4</grade><comment>The dominant stake—how Daniel will exist after bureaucratic death—remains legible, and pressure accumulates from passive listening to sleepless haunting to an active ritual offering. The ledger scene and the unusually high tide are credible tightenings, and his decision to relinquish the page/label bundle is a clear stance. Still, there is little explicit opposition or risk beyond mood and metaphysical cost; conflict rarely bites back in concrete terms. Escalation is effective for a quiet piece but not maximally bracing.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>8.1</grade><comment>Themes of erased histories, bureaucratic violence, and memory as both poison and sustenance are well woven through recurring images (the label, the ledger, forgotten people in containers). Daniel’s shift from collecting to becoming part of the labyrinth’s memory provides a satisfying thematic turn. However, the closing lines (“Some poisons were meant to be absorbed. Some histories were meant to be rewritten.”) edge toward explicit thesis-statement territory, slightly flattening the otherwise rich subtext. Letting the final image speak with less overt moralizing would strengthen the lingering resonance.</comment>

Dialogue snippets feel natural and serve as textural nodes for the stories he collects. The voice could push a bit further into idiosyncrasy under peak pressure, but it already reads as polished and publication-ready.</comment>

<question>8</question><grade>8.5</grade><comment>The synthesis of a bureaucratic death error, a living man haunting a container labyrinth, and the practice of collecting others’ discarded stories feels fresh and memorable. While the “ghost in the port” frame is not wholly new, this execution commits to its own logic and symbolism rather than leaning on genre cliché.
