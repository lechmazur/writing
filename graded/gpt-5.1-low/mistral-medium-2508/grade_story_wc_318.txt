Recurring objects—the hourglass, pull cord, leaf—serve as micro-settings that charge the final images rather than as decorative motifs. The environment constrains his tactics (he can only influence via sound) and inflects the theme without overstatement.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>8.2</grade><comment>The dominant stake—whether Elias will remain a hidden caretaker of others’ grief or step into shared laughter—is coherent and signaled early. Internal and external conflicts braid effectively, though the turn to family acceptance is quite smooth, with little resistance or risk on their part. The ending reweights earlier material (especially the false laughter) without collapsing into a neat moral; there’s a lingering sense of how ritual can both sustain and imprison. Subtext operates mainly through image rather than dialogue and feels proportionate to the length.</comment>

Limited dialogue at the end is simple but natural, and the shift into a slightly lighter register there works as a tonal modulation rather than a break in voice. Motifs like the hourglass and pull cord are familiar objects given fresh conceptual work without feeling like puzzle pieces from a prompt.
