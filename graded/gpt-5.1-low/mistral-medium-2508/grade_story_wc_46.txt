<question>2</question><grade>7.8</grade><comment>The structure is clean: a present-tense grotto scene accumulates pressure via interleaved memories, culminating in the girl’s arrival and transfer of role. The drive is largely accumulative/textural rather than causal, but the closure—Liora accepting succession and reframing the villagers’ “miracle”—lands as a resonant reconfiguration with a legible before/after. Causality is simple but coherent (Liora’s history → her practice → her response to the girl), with no orientation issues. The cost is quiet and mostly implicit: Liora yields centrality as keeper and accepts being misunderstood, which suits the mode but keeps the structural impact modest.</comment>
<question>3</question><grade>8.3</grade><comment>The serpentine grotto, mist, tide-breath, and quartz veins create a vivid, coherent micro-world that’s tightly fused to Liora’s interiority. Setting details recur and evolve (mist exhaling/retreating, grotto as serpent and constellation chamber), charging both the opening and closing images. While the environment doesn’t strongly constrain specific physical choices, it meaningfully frames the alternative “light” she practices in opposition to village torches. Sensory detail is rich and selective, though the focus is heavily visual and metaphoric rather than fully multisensory.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>6.8</grade><comment>The dominant stake—how light is kept and shared, and whether Liora’s non-fire ethos will continue—is clear but largely internal and philosophical. Escalation consists mainly of deepening texture (memories of grandmother and Elias) and a single external tightening when the girl appears asking to “keep” fire; that turn is effective but gentle. There’s little sense of risk or real possibility of refusal, so the cost of Liora’s choice stays conceptual rather than tense on the page. The result is thematically satisfying but low-pressure in conflict terms.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>8.0</grade><comment>Themes of stillness, non-destructive light, grief, and generational transmission are woven consistently through objects (sea glass, pearl, amber) and practices (polishing vs. burning). However, some key lines voice the theme a bit baldly (“Light isn’t something you take. It’s something you become.”), edging toward thesis-statement territory. The piece still achieves a lingering resonance, but with slightly more explicitness than top-tier literary work usually allows.</comment>

The narrative stance sometimes edges into a more authorially ornate register than a humble village gem-keeper might plausibly think in, but the mythic/lyric frame mostly justifies it. Point of view remains stable and unconfused throughout.</comment>
<question>7</question><grade>8.0</grade><comment>The prose is lush and image-dense, with many metaphors (“sleeping serpent,” “breath of the tide,” “trapped storm of green and gold”) that mostly cohere and build atmosphere rather than muddling sense. That said, figurative density is high enough that in a few spots it skirts purple, and a bit more spareness around peak beats might sharpen impact. Line-level control is nonetheless strong and well above typical submission quality.</comment>
<question>8</question><grade>7.8</grade><comment>The core conceit—a keeper of non-burning light, a “pearl of stillness,” and the quiet apprenticeship in a mist-breathing grotto—feels fresh and thoughtfully realized. The inversion of fire-as-light into light-as-enduring-stillness gives a non-obvious angle on grief and legacy. Structurally and narratively, the mentor-passes-torch pattern and dead-friend backstory are familiar, executed more elegantly than innovatively. Overall, the story stands out for its specific synthesis of sea, stone, and spiritual light rather than for radical formal or conceptual risk.</comment>
