<question>2</question><grade>7.8</grade><comment>Structure is clean and causal: hedge gap → garden → tooth → changeling → memory-flood → bargain → restored brother, with the backstory of the substitute body neatly interleaved and anchored. The chief weakness is the thinly articulated cost of the bargain—the garden’s rules and the price of reversing a changeling swap remain abstract, making the resolution feel a touch convenient. <question>3</question><grade>8.4</grade><comment>The hedge maze, impossible garden, and well are vividly rendered with selective, purposeful detail—the scent of crushed mint and iron, pulsing flowers, and aching symbols create a distinct, charged atmosphere. Setting is not postcard decoration: the garden is the liminal space where rules bend, and the well literally mediates memory, magic, and closure. The recurring objects (well, hedge, river) evolve meaningfully toward the ending image of Liora stepping back through the hedge to a reconfigured village. A slight gap is that physical constraints of the garden rarely force hard choices; it functions more as a metaphysical stage than an active obstacle.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>7.2</grade><comment>The dominant stake—confronting and repairing the “crack in the world’s skin” that took her brother—is established early through the substitute body and sustained through the garden encounter. Pressure escalates via the tooth’s activation, the changeling’s appearance, and the overwhelming memory-sharing, which are all good tightening beats. However, after that peak, conflict resolves quickly once Liora proposes the “turn them into stars” solution; the changeling assents and reality resets without visible resistance, cost, or real risk of failure. The result is emotionally effective but slightly under-pressured for elite-level stakes.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>8.4</grade><comment>Themes of repair, the uses of lies, and transforming wounds into guidance are richly integrated: bone-setting becomes a metaphor for mending reality, the needle and tooth are mistakes made material, and the constellations image reframes harm as navigation. Some cracks were meant to let the light in.") offer a satisfying conceptual repricing without feeling purely decorative. There is a slight tilt toward explicitness in those final moral-inflected sentences, which nudge the piece a bit closer to stated thesis than necessary. Nonetheless, the aftereffect is resonant and conceptually coherent.</comment>

Dialogue is sparse but natural and charged; lines like “You’re not him, but you remember him” carry subtext efficiently. There are no POV slips or stance mismatches, and modulation from eerie wonder to gentle closure is smooth.</comment>
<question>7</question><grade>8.5</grade><comment>Line-level craft is strong: sentences vary in length and structure, imagery is vivid yet generally economical, and most lines do double duty advancing scene and mood. A few formulations skate close to the poetic-over-concrete edge, but they do not obscure causality or dull the closure image. The prose maintains momentum throughout, with no visible loops, template cadences, or clutter.</comment>

The idea of a changeling’s baby tooth as a lock, a substitute body stitched with reeds, and wounds turned into constellations are memorable, specific inflections. The final conceptual turn—that a village bone-setter learns to mend reality itself—reads as both surprising and inevitable given the established motifs. It doesn’t quite break into radical new territory, but it demonstrates high-level, confident synthesis and risk-taking within the mode.</comment>
