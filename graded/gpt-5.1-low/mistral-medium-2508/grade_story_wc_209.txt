The cathedral window and garden actively shape the key discoveries and her final choice not to paint, charging the ending image. A slightly broader sensory range beyond sight and scent could deepen it further, but what’s here is already strong and purposeful.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>7.3</grade><comment>The dominant stake is Liora’s need to understand the flowers/silence, and there is a clear tightening when the postcards become “clues” and the glass finally “shivers,” pushing her into a new understanding. The key turn—choosing to listen and not paint—is meaningful but low-pressure, with only a lightly implied price in relinquishing control and explanation. For a top-tier venue, one more concrete or emotionally fraught pressure point would help.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>7.0</grade><comment>The theme of silence as the true medium of revelation is coherent and threads naturally through pavilion, glass, and jasmine. However, several explicit thesis-like lines (“Some things only reveal themselves when you stop looking”; “Some questions weren’t meant to be solved”) edge into sermonizing and undercut the subtlety the imagery has already earned. The piece would be stronger if it trusted its images and actions more and stated the takeaway less.</comment>
