Details are filtered through Elara’s professional and emotional lenses, so worldbuilding never reads like a catalog. It stops just short of 9-band because the environment constrains choice less than it frames and charges the imagery.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>7.2</grade><comment>The dominant stake—Elara’s need to understand transition to reframe her loss—is clear early and deepens as the plant develops. Pressure escalates via anticipation and narrowing focus on the plant’s “knowledge” moment, culminating in the bloom and departing light that forces a reinterpretation of Anshar’s disappearance. Agency is present in her decision to plant and to attend to the process rather than retreat into grief, with the cost being the risk that no answer will come and her loss will remain senseless. However, the conflict remains almost entirely internal and contemplative; there is little sense that she might meaningfully fail or pay a sharper price, which keeps tension moderate.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>7.5</grade><comment>Theme coheres around transition, latency versus expression, and the idea of loss as completion rather than annihilation, developed through concrete parallels (spirit/seed/tablets/thought→symbol). However, the explicit sentences “The physics of transition was not about force or trajectory, but about release” and related lines edge into essayistic statement, slightly undercutting the power of the images themselves. The piece still leaves an emotional after-effect, but with more explanation than an elite venue typically tolerates.</comment>

Metaphoric language is patterned and coherent (latency/expression, seeds, fossils of words) rather than scattershot. Without dialogue or more extreme stylistic risk, it stops just short of exceptional but is clearly publication-grade.</comment>

A few abstract phrases (“haunted ambition,” “physics of transition”) could be grounded even more in image, but they do not significantly dull the closure.</comment>
