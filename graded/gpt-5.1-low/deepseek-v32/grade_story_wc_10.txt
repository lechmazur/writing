<question>2</question><grade>8.2</grade><comment>The structure is clean and legible: initial goal, first attempt, partial result, realization, adjusted method, and transformed outcome. Overall, the piece is tightly built and immediately publishable in terms of structure, if not formally daring.</comment>

The final images of prismatic light, frost-fall, and “ice flowers chimed in glittering dissolution” show the setting charging the closure in a concrete way. A slight reliance on familiar abstractions (“roaring void”) keeps this just shy of the very highest band.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>7.4</grade><comment>The dominant stake—rectifying the conservatory’s dissonance by translating silence into music—is clearly present and tightened by her initial failure. However, the process from realization to success is quite smooth, with limited sense of risk or potential loss beyond a generalized emotional imbalance, so the pressure never becomes truly acute. The conflict is coherent and satisfying but slightly muted in terms of felt stakes.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>7.2</grade><comment>Themes of attunement to non-human experience, reimagining perception after catastrophe, and harmony between inner and outer silence are well-integrated through the tuning forks, heat-mapping, and the conservatory’s “patient resilience.” The reweighting at the end—“a new way to hear, not with ears, but with the heart”—is thematically consistent but veers toward explicit, somewhat sentimental statement. The piece still invites reflection, but the closing moral overtone dampens what could be a more haunting resonance.</comment>
