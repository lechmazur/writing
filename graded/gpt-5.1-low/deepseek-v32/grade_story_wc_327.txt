Sensory detail (rasping melody for stone, brittle chime for bone) is selective and powered by the protagonist’s priorities. A minor limitation is that non-aural senses recede somewhat once we enter the tunnel’s conceptual space.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>7.8</grade><comment>The primary stake—whether any essence of the dying language can survive—is clear and grows toward the tunnel experiment and memory-loss cost. Escalation reads as increasing density and risk: from studying birds, to entering a cursed space, to sacrificing his own memories. However, pressure is more conceptual than felt, and we don’t see many moments where a viable alternative path is considered or foreclosed in-scene. The conflict is strong for a lyric piece, but not maximally tightened for high-stakes drama.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>8.5</grade><comment>Themes of translation, loss, and the transformation of language into a new sensory medium are deeply embedded in the images of echoes, birds, and bone towers. The cost of art and preservation—sacrificing personal memory and accepting cultural extinction to create a new “pure sensory” language—emerges naturally without moralizing. The final image of the towers “singing” their own substance and birds wheeling above reweights the entire story in a resonant, non-literal way. A hairline limitation is that the “pure sensory truth” phrasing edges toward explicit statement rather than staying entirely in image.</comment>

Patterns like “quietly articulate,” “across the death of a language,” and “copper-veined” give the voice a distinct texture without becoming cloying. It could edge higher with slightly more modulation under peak pressure to vary rhythm and deepen immediacy.</comment>

The birds’ patterns and bone towers’ “self-singing” give the ending a non-obvious yet inevitable quality.
