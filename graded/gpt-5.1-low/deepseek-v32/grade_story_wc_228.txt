<question>4</question><grade>7.4</grade><comment>The dominant stake—whether Elian can find a truth “worthy of his ultimate canvas” and thus fulfill his hidden promise—is clear and followed through. Pressure escalates gently: the wrong turn into a risky ledge, the eerie cave, the uncanny shell, and the encroaching tide that forces him to finish and leave. The key choice is to paint not the obvious symbolic shell but the fleeting reflections, and then to relinquish the shell to await another soul, trading possession for fidelity to the insight. Conflict is almost entirely internal and stays low-intensity; there’s little real sense of danger or sacrifice beyond the emotional/temporal, which limits the perceived stakes at a “prestige-lit” level.</comment>

However, the text leans into explicit statement—e.g., “This revelation was not about permanence, but about the act of witnessing and honoring the ephemeral”—which tips into mild moralizing and undercuts some subtextual power. The final paragraphs still resonate, but they articulate their ideas a touch too directly for the highest literary tier.</comment>
