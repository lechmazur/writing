<question>2</question><grade>7.6</grade><comment>The causal chain is clean: breakers cool → he initiates the scan → detects the pool → the geode resonates → he pushes the system to see it → power dies, leaving him with a new question. Closure lands as a resonant reconfiguration: his theory is confirmed and the story ends on the cost of that knowledge rather than a physical event. The main limitation is that the final shift is more confirmation than transformation; options don’t narrow so much as the emotional frame deepens.</comment>
<question>3</question><grade>8.4</grade><comment>The floating observatory, high desert, and quantum array are well-specified and integral to the action rather than decorative. The geode functions almost as a micro-setting, a tactile anchor that becomes a literal resonator and charges both the leak scene and the final image when it goes cold. Spatial and physical logic are clear, and the “wound in the air” over the desert elegantly extends the setting into the metaphysical. This is very strong atmospheric work that both grounds and amplifies the premise.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>7.2</grade><comment>The dominant stake—proving his theory about pooled potential—is clear and present early, and tension does escalate as he overrides safety and pours the last power into the array. Internal stakes (what this proof will do to his already-devastated psyche) interplay with the external experiment in a satisfying way. However, the safety override and “last of the array’s power” never bite as concrete cost beyond exhaustion; no new danger or irreversible external consequence manifests. The conflict remains largely conceptual and emotional, which works but keeps the pressure slightly abstract.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>7.4</grade><comment>The theme—that emotional experience leaves indelible, quasi-physical scars on reality—is tightly woven through Amir’s trauma, the geode, and the literal pool of unused joy. The ending’s question (“what one could possibly do with it”) is a strong, open thematic beat that resists neat moralizing and invites reflection. However, some lines state the thematic thesis rather baldly (“Not a philosophical idea, but a physical law laid bare: everything mattered, everything left a trace”), edging into on-the-nose exposition. The piece mostly earns its resonance through image and event but occasionally explains what those images already imply.</comment>
<question>6</question><grade>8.0</grade><comment>The close third-person voice is controlled and consistent, marrying technical diction (“perturbation theory,” “local quantum field”) with emotionally charged description in a way that feels specific to Amir. Rhythms vary, and the prose modulates well between clinical description and lyric intensity, especially in the leak sequence and the bleakly ecstatic realization. There’s no POV leakage or unjustified omniscience; everything stays within what Amir could perceive or infer. A few abstract formulations are slightly more essayistic than embodied, but they don’t undermine the voice’s integrity.</comment>

There are minor tendencies toward abstraction and reiteration (e.g., “intangibly tangible” spelled out and then rephrased), but they don’t bog momentum or obscure clarity. No significant artifacts or mixed metaphors appear in the climax, which remains lucid and striking.</comment>
<question>8</question><grade>8.6</grade><comment>The fusion of clandestine science facility, drone-strike survivor guilt, and a quasi-physical theory of pooled emotional potential feels genuinely fresh. The geode as a resonant bridge between trauma and quantum field, and the “wound in the air” leaking aborted joy, are memorable, high-concept images that grow organically from the setup. The story commits to its premise without retreating to a twist or easy catharsis, ending instead on a hard conceptual and emotional question. This is a thoughtful, high-level riff on SF and grief tropes rather than a template plot.</comment>
