<question>4</question><grade>7.0</grade><comment>The central stake—whether she can transform censorship and inner chaos into an authentic, self-chosen form of expression—is present from the outset and sharpened by the storm/timer countdown. However, conflict stays almost entirely interior and is resolved without any genuine sense of jeopardy; no path feels meaningfully foreclosed besides a general renunciation of “dense, angry verses.” The result is thematically satisfying but structurally mild in terms of tension and cost.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>7.4</grade><comment>Themes of censorship, scars as maps, and silence as a mode of speech are integrated through recurring images (lightning, the envelope, the map of wounds, the timer/darkroom). That said, several lines state the theme quite explicitly (“The observatory was not for looking out, but for looking in”; “She understood the cartography now”), edging toward authorial explanation rather than letting the images fully carry the weight. The piece is thoughtful and coherent but not yet subtle enough for the very top tier.</comment>

There are no POV slips or awkward explanatory intrusions; the narrative trusts interior perception and image to carry meaning. Minor repetition of certain constructions and motifs keeps this from feeling utterly distinctive, but the voice is confident and publication-ready.</comment>
<question>7</question><grade>7.8</grade><comment>The prose is generally strong, with vivid metaphors, varied sentence rhythm, and good information density—most lines serve both mood and character. Tightening a few redundancies and letting some images stand without rephrased reinforcement would elevate this into a higher band.</comment>
