<question>2</question><grade>7.6</grade><comment>The structure is clean: present-tense encounter at the train, interleaved with backstory on the father and the startup, leading to Leo’s choice to refuse and dissuade Ben. The main limitation is that the outcome feels inevitable early; there’s little sense Leo might choose otherwise, so structural tension is modest. Still, the circular framing (inheritance → startup → train) holds together effectively.</comment>
<question>3</question><grade>7.8</grade><comment>The abandoned circus train, overgrown tracks, and improvised territorial markers are vivid and specific, strongly coloring the atmosphere and reflecting Leo’s liminal state. Setting details (faded elephants, flaking paint, broken glass patterns) are well filtered through his memory and need for refuge. The environment functions conceptually more than tactically—it symbolizes cycles and temporary claims rather than forcing hard choices—but it does frame his only real option: retreat and observation. The final image of him sorting money under a lantern in this space nicely recharges the opening description.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>6.8</grade><comment>The dominant stake—whether Leo allows or prevents Ben from repeating the criminal/illicit cycle—is clear and thematically apt. There is a meaningful turn when Ben arrives with a laundering scheme, forcing Leo to articulate his renunciation. However, pressure does not escalate much: Ben’s resistance is mild, his ambition cools quickly, and there’s no palpable risk Leo will be swayed or that Ben will walk away unchanged. The conflict is thoughtful but low-friction, so stakes feel more conceptual than urgent.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>7.2</grade><comment>Themes of cyclical paths, inherited crime, territorial claiming, and the difference between chasing and observing are strongly woven through money, setting, and Leo’s history. Yet the piece leans into explicit statement (“Some paths circle back, not to trap you, but to show you how far you’ve come”), edging toward moralizing rather than letting images and choices fully carry the weight. The insight is coherent and sincere but somewhat over-articulated.</comment>

Dialogue is functional and mostly natural, though it carries a slightly elevated, thesis-serving feel at times (“He thought he was conquering something, but he was just running in circles”). Overall, POV control is solid, if a bit didactic in key lines.</comment>

<question>8</question><grade>7.8</grade><comment>The fusion of a highway robber’s legacy, startup fintech, and an abandoned circus train is fresh and idiosyncratic; the foreign paper money as both criminal trophy and failed-tech relic is especially sharp.
