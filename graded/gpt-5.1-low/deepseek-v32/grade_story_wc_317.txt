The corpse flower and brook do charge the final image conceptually, but not with much sensory specificity.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>4.8</grade><comment>The nominal stake—protecting the haven from corporate development—is compelling in concept but arrives late and mostly in summary. Most moves (research, following the map, designing new puzzles) occur without resistance or complication. As a result, tension stays low and the ending reads as a smooth resolution rather than a tested commitment.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>5.4</grade><comment>Themes of conservation, safeguarding innovation, and the protection of rare ideas are coherent and consistently echoed through the corpse flower and haven. The result veers toward sermonizing and platitude, especially in the final paragraphs. Subtext is thin because the story tells the reader what its symbols mean rather than leaving room for interpretation.</comment>

Recurrent phrasing (“storm-born silence,” “across the hush of a possibility just born,” “orthogonal approaches”) starts to feel templated rather than organic. Overall the voice is competent but not yet memorable at a literary-venue level.</comment>
<question>7</question><grade>5.0</grade><comment>The prose is grammatically clean and easy to follow, but it suffers from repetition of key phrases and concepts, reducing information density. Figurative language is serviceable but often abstract and interchangeable, with limited vivid, concrete phrasing. Momentum is blunted by these loops, making the story feel longer and flatter than its word count.</comment>
<question>8</question><grade>5.7</grade><comment>The combination of an escape room, a corpse flower seed, and environmental activism has genuine potential and is not a standard template. The puzzle/escape-room angle is underutilized as an engine for conflict or structure. The piece hints at a fresher, stranger story than the one it actually dramatizes.</comment>
