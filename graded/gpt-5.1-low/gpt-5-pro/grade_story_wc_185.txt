Closure is a resonant reconfiguration: the caves now “map” them, the hush can guide others, and they step onto “readiness,” marking a before/after in how travel and risk will work. <question>3</question><grade>9.0</grade><comment>The setting is rich, specific, and always doing work: harmonic caves, ice drums, crawling ice sheets, and stalactites that would fall under harsher light all constrain tactics and tone. Sensory detail is multi-modal (taste of metal and snow, fragrance like frostbitten thyme) and filtered through a mapkeeper’s professional lens. World rules about resonance and quiet stay consistent and integral to both action and theme.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>7.4</grade><comment>The dominant stake—finding a survivable quiet so maps and travelers endure the resonance storms—is clearly stated and threads through the piece. Escalation comes via deepening: from reading cracks to confronting the “tuned darkness” room and committing to merge the stone’s power with the beings’ hunger. The protagonist’s agency in setting the stone on the drum and refusing to leave until the hush can guide others resolves the stake credibly. Still, the danger remains largely notional; we’re told of crushing storms and erasure, but the present-tense conflict never quite tightens to a felt brink.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>8.8</grade><comment>Themes of quiet vs silence, mapping vs being mapped, and collaborative rather than extractive travel emerge organically from action (“today the cave mapped us, and we agreed to be found”). The “bloom of hush” motif evolves from technique to ontology, recasting quiet as a lattice that holds motion, which reweights the whole story at the end. The shared motivation with the energy beings—that both have failed alone to find non-erasing passage—adds a nuanced, non-binary stance on contact and coexistence. The thematic insight about quiet is explicit but grows convincingly from prior particulars rather than reading as bolt-on moral.</comment>
<question>6</question><grade>9.0</grade><comment>The first-person voice is distinctive, consistent, and clearly that of a specialist whose metaphors and perceptions arise from stone, sound, and mapping. There is no POV leakage or gratuitous explanation; the voice trusts its own strangeness.</comment>

Rhythm varies and the prose has clear propulsion despite being contemplative. There is some risk of over-saturation in the middle stretch, but it does not materially obscure causality or blur the closure image.</comment>

The final reconfiguration—travel as entering “weather that loved us” under a hush lattice—feels both unexpected and retrospectively inevitable.
