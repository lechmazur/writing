<question>3</question><grade>9.2</grade><comment>The ancient Babylonian palace-observatory is rendered with exceptional specificity—“ceiling mapped with chipped lapis…a river of ash,” boiling glue and cedar—and every detail is filtered through an astronomer’s priorities. Setting, more than backdrop, is the medium of the covenant and is indispensable to the story’s logic and mood.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>8.4</grade><comment>The dominant stake—achieving a verifiable yet devotional covenant—tightens as his rational methods unlock genuinely uncanny responses from the palace. His key choice—touching the eyelid with the tile despite “doubts pull[ing] him back”—is clearly motivated and paid off in the ash’s transformation and reconfiguration of his self-image. <question>5</question><grade>9.0</grade><comment>Themes of faith versus empiricism, obligation beyond regimes, and devotion as attention emerge organically from his methods and the palace’s responses. “Encrypted devotion,” “piety with proofs,” and the vow “to ensure the palace never paid twice for darkness” concretize big ideas through specific metaphors of ledgers, taxes, and repairs. The final image—his figure reduced to curves and sums and his plea to “uncloak only what a careful heart can keep”—reweights the entire piece as an ethic of humble, bounded knowledge. There is no sermonizing; the complexity of devotion, power, and uncertainty is allowed to resonate with genuine ambiguity.</comment>

There is no dialogue to test, but narrative stance under pressure—especially at the eyelid and ash mirror—remains precise and character-true.</comment>

The only minor limitation is that the sustained lyricism could, for some readers, slightly blunt urgency, but it does not obscure causality or closure.</comment>
