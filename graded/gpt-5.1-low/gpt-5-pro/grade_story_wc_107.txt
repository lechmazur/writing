<question>3</question><grade>8.7</grade><comment>The abandoned control room is vividly, specifically inhabited: “walls of gauges,” dripping panels as metronomes, thawing rafters, lakes of water on the floor, and the generator’s cough all feel tactile and functional. <question>4</question><grade>7.0</grade><comment>The dominant stake—can she truly make and inhabit a “safe zone for inquiry” after past harm—is clear, and it tightens modestly as she moves from solitary ritual to creating an invitation for strangers. However, tension is almost entirely internal and gentle; there is no sharp reversal or cost that significantly narrows her options beyond the quiet decision to stay and continue. For elite venues, one more concrete on-page test of that ideal, with some visible risk, would deepen the sense of escalation.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>8.3</grade><comment>Themes of shared heat, non-hoarding of power, and creating hospitable spaces for vulnerable inquiry emerge organically from action: breath-counting, dome-making, and the contrast between past hostile critique and present mercy. <question>6</question><grade>8.8</grade><comment>The first-person voice is distinctive, controlled, and consistent: baroque but purposeful diction, ritualistic “Cue the…” refrains, and a gently theatrical self-awareness that suits someone who narrates to dust and machines. Rhythmic control is strong, with sentence length and parallelism used to build a liturgical cadence without falling into monotony. The tonal blend of earnestness and sly humor (“even machines fear the cold of indifference”) feels character-driven rather than imposed.</comment>
