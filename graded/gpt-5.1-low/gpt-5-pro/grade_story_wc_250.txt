<question>3</question><grade>8.8</grade><comment>The setting is exceptionally vivid and integrated: the cobalt dock, after-rain hush, black arch, moored boats, and shutter-laced alleys all shape not just mood but method (the guardian’s work literally depends on water, alleys, and shutters). The dental mirror and shutters function as environment-linked tools that constrain how aid can be offered (light must be bent, patterns woven, not voices raised). It stops just short of perfection because the physical constraint of the environment could be made to pinch tactics slightly harder in the rescue itself.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>7.8</grade><comment>The dominant stakes—recovering the brother without breaking the vow, and coaxing the dark rather than commanding it—are clear and organically seeded. The key turn is the choice to proceed entirely through ritual and silence instead of direct intervention, culminating in the brother’s emergence and the transference of the mirror to the younger boy. Yet the conflict remains very one-sided; the night “softens” readily and there is little visible price paid beyond sustained effort, which mutes the sense of narrowing options.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>8.5</grade><comment>Themes of non-coercive care, silent empathy, and teaching rather than shaming are deeply woven into concrete action: the vow of silence, the mirror passed to the boy, and the final praise of the dark for “learning.” Recurring motifs (shutters, star, mirror, pages) shift meaning by the end, emphasizing shared responsibility and gentle instruction. The story resists didacticism, though lines like “the city lighter by one lesson” brush close to explicit statement. Overall, the ending offers a resonant, non-literal answer to its central question about how to intervene without violence or ego.</comment>

A few phrases verge on ornamental or slightly over-rich, but they do not materially obscure causality or dull the closure image.</comment>

The emotional truth—help as invitation rather than command, silence as respect—is handled with originality rather than platitude.
