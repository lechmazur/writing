Closure lands as a resonant reconfiguration (“ending it is a practice”), with the city’s relationship to signal and silence permanently altered. <question>3</question><grade>8.8</grade><comment>The harbor, rain, quays, and trumpet are richly realized and tightly integrated with theme; details like “wet marble quays glow like dry bones waking” feel both concrete and charged. World rules (exile, signals, harbor life) are consistent and efficiently sketched. This is high-level atmospheric craft, only held back from higher scores by the relatively static spatial stakes.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>7.5</grade><comment>Conflict is primarily conceptual and communal—how to live after a misread silence—rather than sharply dramatized, but it does escalate: from a mysterious exile to the discovery and reinterpretation of the failed warning. The valve’s fitting and the decision not to sound the trumpet but to listen and document instead function as the key tightening. Stakes remain mostly in the register of meaning and practice, not immediate jeopardy, which keeps pressure gentle. Still, the piece credibly forecloses the old punitive order and commits to a costlier, slower mode of response.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>8.4</grade><comment>Theme—about absence, misread silence, accountable memory, and the ethics of response—emerges organically from objects and actions (valve, ledger, unblown trumpet), with strong subtext around authority and communal trauma. Some lines verge on explicit thesis (“What remains after rain is not proof, but practice”), slightly tipping into overt statement rather than pure inference. Even so, the final image reweights the whole piece without collapsing into a neat moral.</comment>

<question>8</question><grade>8.9</grade><comment>The conceit of an “ocean-spray archivist” maintaining a ledger of absences and mis-signals in a post-exile harbor is striking and memorable. Integrating bureaucratic record-keeping (tax records, tide tables) with lyric mysticism around breath and valves is an unusually deft synthesis. It’s ambitious, formally and conceptually, without reading as gimmicky.</comment>
