<question>4</question><grade>7.0</grade><comment>The dominant stake—preserving the brook as a space for innovation against developers—is clear and increasingly tied to action as the protagonist moves from private puzzlemaking to communal, politically inflected play. Tension stays mostly conceptual and tonal rather than viscerally escalating.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>8.4</grade><comment>Themes of play as resistance, guarding liminal spaces for uncertainty, and using art/experience instead of direct confrontation to protect the future emerge organically from events (the design of “Seed,” the community’s room-sized lung, Mae’s explanation about monitored meetings). There’s no sermonizing; the ethics of a garden and development are embedded in choices and images. The only limiter is that the piece lands on a relatively clear optimism, with little thematic bite from genuine loss or compromise.</comment>
