Environmental constraints (height, sway, tide-timed bloom) shape how she moves and breathes, and charge the final descent image. <question>4</question><grade>8.0</grade><comment>The central stake—whether Jun can obtain the relic seed without harming the village’s safety—appears early and stays coherent, and the main tightening comes when she agrees to witness the bloom and endure its stench without stealing. The vigil scene escalates pressure nicely by making the conflict bodily and immediate (the reek, her knees buckling) and tying it to her value of “serenity within motion.” However, opposition is principled rather than dangerous, so the risk of social or moral failure feels real but not biting; the village seems inclined to trust her fairly quickly. The final condition (planting in the mudflats, ongoing reporting) does give the resolution a real, if gentle, price.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>8.3</grade><comment>Themes of wisdom as an unseen undercurrent, decay as necessary compass, and serenity as riding rather than negating motion are well embodied in particulars: corpse flower ecology, odor-mapping doodles, breathing with the treetop sway. Still, the final image of the inward spiral that “meant both stink and grace” offers a resonant, non-literal answer to the story’s question.</comment>

There’s no head-hopping or register drift, and even expository lines (about the corpse flower’s function) are folded into character-specific discourse. It isn’t radically experimental, but it’s assured and memorable.</comment>
<question>7</question><grade>8.6</grade><comment>The prose is dense but controlled, with most sentences doing multiple jobs (world-building, character, and theme) without obvious scaffolding or paraphrase loops. A slight tendency toward abstraction (“undercurrent of wisdom”) keeps it just short of the very top band.</comment>
