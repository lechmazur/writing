<question>3</question><grade>8.6</grade><comment>The settings—the fog-softened water clock shop and the steamy birthing house—are vivid, selective, and tightly filtered through Iris’s altered senses. The world rules (water clock repair, midwife’s tools) feel coherent without exposition bloat. The only small limitation is that the environment rarely constrains tactics in a hard way; it mostly supports mood and perception rather than forcing different choices.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>7.6</grade><comment>The dominant stake—whether Iris can mediate Aster’s unspoken fear of blame into recognition rather than accusation—is clear and threads from package to birthing room. Resolution is earned by her deliberate framing, but the pressure never quite bites; it feels like things are likely to go well, so the cost of failure is more implied than viscerally felt in the prose. The piece succeeds as a quiet, situational conflict, but that quietness also caps the intensity.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>8.0</grade><comment>Theme—seeing the truths people can’t or won’t articulate, and the possibility of “ordinary miracles” in that act—is grounded in concrete particulars: dents in paper, trembling hands, the repaired screw changing from symbol of failure to proof of care. The final paragraph does edge toward statement (“she could still unveil ordinary miracles by teaching people to read what they already meant”), which slightly flattens the otherwise beautifully implied theme. Even so, the closing conceptual repricing of Iris’s loss is coherent and resonant.</comment>

The central turn—reframing a botched screw as evidence of life saved—feels both surprising and inevitable once Iris’s worldview is established.
