Closure lands both concretely (the stand down order) and conceptually (reframing the cast as a labyrinth that can be unknotted), with visible social cost avoided and emotional cost acknowledged. Environment doesn’t heavily constrain tactics in a physical sense, but it powerfully frames the choice between violence and vigil. The specificity and integration with theme and closure are excellent.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>8.5</grade><comment>The dominant stake—whether fear and mistranslation will reignite violence—is clear early and sharpened by the ambiguous word that can mean funeral or meeting. Escalation comes through increasing interpretive pressure as Lani reads the cast and realizes how close both sides are to seeing the vigil as an ambush. Her agency in choosing what to translate first and how to frame it for each man directly changes the outcome, with rifles “already loaded” underscoring the near-miss. The cost is more conceptual than bloody, but the sense of averted disaster is credible and felt.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>8.4</grade><comment>Themes of language as weapon/healing tool, shared fear, and the possibility of grafting peace onto damage emerge organically from actions and images (cast as safe paper, coral as maze, shared anxieties as dictionary). However, a couple of lines edge toward explicit thesis statements (“she was trying to graft new meaning onto old wounds,” “the day his scribbles unknotted a war”), slightly reducing the subtlety of an otherwise finely implied theme. The piece still leaves meaningful, charged ambiguity rather than a neat moral.</comment>

There is no head-hopping; we never slip into other minds beyond what Lani can infer. The voice balances poetic texture with clarity, rarely drifting into overwrought territory.</comment>
