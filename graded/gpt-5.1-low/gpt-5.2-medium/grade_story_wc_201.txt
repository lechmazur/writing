<question>3</question><grade>8.8</grade><comment>The ultraviolet forest is vividly rendered through metal-sap air, bruise-light sky, moss, glowing trunks, and pollen, all firmly filtered through the narrator’s body and history. The only ceiling issue is that spatial choreography stays mostly impressionistic rather than situationally constraining specific tactical options.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>7.8</grade><comment>The dominant stake—uncover the forest’s mercy calculus to free others and herself—is clear from early on and coherently maintained. The ending stance is earned, but the conflict curve feels more like steady descent than sharply tightening screws.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>8.4</grade><comment>Theme emerges strongly from particulars: torture repackaged as “rehabilitation,” the forest as an archive of guilt, and mercy quantified as a function that discounts pain by intention and punishes hidden power. The explicit articulation “The mathematics of mercy is not a single number but a function…” edges toward direct statement but remains grounded in-character and concept, avoiding pure sermonizing. The final realization—that mercy releases the tortured more than it absolves torturers—reweights everything without collapsing into a pat moral.</comment>

Patterned elision (“remembering through trauma responses”) is taught early and reused as motif rather than cheap suspense, and there’s no head-hopping or stance wobble. A few abstract formulations in the climax are high-level, but they still sound like this specific mind rather than authorial intrusion.</comment>

The only restraint on a higher score is the near-constant metaphorical pressure, which very occasionally risks blurring immediacy in favor of concept.</comment>
