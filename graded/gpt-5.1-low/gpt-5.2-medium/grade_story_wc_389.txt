<question>2</question><grade>7.9</grade><comment>The structure is cleanly causal under a mostly single-scene frame: Eli’s eavesdropping on Mara’s broadcasts leads to her accusation, confession, and their collaborative experiment, which in turn produces the skywide shadow-show that reconfigures the valleys’ loyalties. <question>3</question><grade>8.6</grade><comment>The observatory above the cloud ocean, the cobalt glass portico, and the storm itself are vividly rendered and more than decorative—they enable the cloud projections, color Mara’s entrance, and charge the final dawn image. Tools and environment (antennas, telescope mount, stormlight) are used tactically, not just described. The world’s rules feel consistent within its lightly speculative frame and the setting’s recurrence from first line to last ties atmosphere directly to closure.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>7.0</grade><comment>The central stake—how hope and allegiance in the valleys are shaped, and whether Eli’s rationalist project can coexist with Mara’s prophetic pull—is clear and does tighten when she confronts him about “stealing” her words. Their choice to merge methods and broadcast the puppet-giants is a meaningful turn that alters the social landscape below. However, conflict is relatively low-friction: their opposition never bites hard on the page, there’s little sense of what either truly risks by joining forces, and the experiment’s success comes without a visible setback or trade-off, which blunts escalation.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>8.4</grade><comment>Theme emerges organically from particulars: “variant allegiance,” shadow puppets that “still knew how dawns were made,” and radios built from junk all braid into a nuanced meditation on how past and myth can power a future grounded in bricolage and shared narratives. The final blueprint for junk radios reweights everything that came before, turning prophecy into replicable praxis without flattening Mara’s mystique. The piece avoids sermonizing, though the harmony between reason and prophecy resolves somewhat tidily given the charged premise.</comment>

POV distance is steady and legible, and brief dialogue is character-specific and purposeful. At times the diction borders on ornate saturation, but it remains controlled enough not to obscure meaning.</comment>

A few metaphors push toward purple, and compression occasionally leaves emotional beats under-voiced, but clarity and momentum are largely maintained.</comment>
<question>8</question><grade>8.8</grade><comment>The combination of a stormlit observatory above clouds, a street prophet with a soldering-iron talisman, ancient shadow puppets projected as sky-giants, and “variant allegiance” as a governing idea feels strikingly fresh rather than assembled from familiar genre parts. The main ceiling is that the emotional and conflict beats, while solid, are slightly gentler and more linear than the conceptual inventiveness might allow.</comment>
