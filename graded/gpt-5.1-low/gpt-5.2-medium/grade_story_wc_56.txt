<question>4</question><grade>7.8</grade><comment>The dominant stake—whether to expose engineered myth-making at the cost of communal comfort and entrenched power—is well signaled and coherently pursued. However, the external conflict around the broadcast itself is under-pressured; she infiltrates and airs the message with minimal on-page obstacle or risk, which slightly blunts tension at the climax. <question>5</question><grade>8.6</grade><comment>Theme is rich and organically embedded: “architecture of lies,” myth as governance infrastructure, and the tension between collective comfort and informed agency all emerge from concrete particulars (the tape order, purchased legends, loyalty mapping). A few lines verge on explicit thesis (“lies were architecture and truth was sunlight that kept shifting”), but they remain image-driven enough to avoid sermonizing. The ending offers a resonant, non-literal answer: she now seeks the moment communities can revise their stories, not the myths themselves.</comment>
