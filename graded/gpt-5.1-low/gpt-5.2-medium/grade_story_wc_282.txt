<question>4</question><grade>7.8</grade><comment>The dominant stakes—surviving the flood-sealed cave and fulfilling her mapping/archival duty—are clear and escalate once the tide traps her and the fossil begins to act. Still, the physical peril from the tide recedes quickly once the vent is found, so the conflict leans heavily on conceptual stakes rather than a sharp, narrowing choice under duress. The ending’s key trade-off (personal comfort vs. honoring the other presence) is effective but could be made more acute.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>8.4</grade><comment>Themes of archives, evidence vs. magic, and “transitional objects” as bridges between subjectivities are woven tightly through the fossil and the caves-as-body conceit. The story avoids explicit moralizing, letting the act of placing the fossil and singing the cave’s name do thematic work. It could push ambiguity slightly further around what is owed to a non-human intelligence and to the sleeping humans, but it already resonates strongly.</comment>

There is no head-hopping or gratuitous exposition; reflection is anchored in present stimuli. The turn from personal comfort-object to evidence of another sleeper reshapes the entire setup in a non-obvious but earned way.
