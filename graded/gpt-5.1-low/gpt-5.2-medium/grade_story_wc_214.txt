<question>2</question><grade>8.8</grade><comment>The structure is clean and accumulative: solitary maintenance → meteor-tracing → anomalous convergence → dawn exploration and logbook → communal reopening of the dome. Causality is sound and legible while the piece also functions texturally, and the closure—Lio reconfiguring the station and his grief into a shared practice—lands with clear cost and before/after. There are no orientation issues, and every major setup (glass shard, tracing method, station’s half-abandonment) sees meaningful payoff.</comment>
<question>3</question><grade>8.9</grade><comment>The station on a basalt ridge, the closed domes, generators ticking “like bones,” and the yellow lantern glass all create a lived-in, atmospheric setting that is inseparable from the story’s mood. Environment constrains and enables choice (remote ridge, collapsed service tunnel, old dome, logbook entombed in pumice), and the glass functions as a micro-setting object that directly charges the final image. The sense of place is both concrete and thematically resonant without turning into a static “tour.”</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>8.4</grade><comment>The dominant stake—whether Lio will remain sealed in solitary grief or risk porous connection—is clear and tight. Escalation is quiet but real: the uncanny meteor convergence compels him into physical risk at the tunnel and then emotional risk as he reopens the dome to others. The price is implicit but credible—relinquishing his protective solitude—and the final shared tracing scene reads as an earned, not easy, stance shift.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>8.7</grade><comment>Themes of vulnerability as strength, grief as a form of listening, and stillness as nourishing rather than neglect emerge organically from Lio’s rituals, the director’s logbook, and the repetition of tracing meteors. The final image—laughing “not because the world was fixed, but because they had found a way to stand within its breaking”—offers a resonant, non-literal answer to the story’s question. Occasionally the prose edges toward explicit thematic statement, but it remains mostly grounded in concrete moments and images.</comment>
<question>6</question><grade>8.8</grade><comment>The close third-person voice has a distinct texture: meditative, image-rich, with a consistent lexicon that fits Lio’s contemplative, quasi-mystical temperament. Rhythm and sentence length modulate well, with longer lyric stretches in reflection and tighter lines around discoveries and decisions. There’s no head-hopping or stance confusion, and the narrative maintains a coherent, confident distance throughout.</comment>
<question>7</question><grade>8.8</grade><comment>The prose is lush but controlled; images like “clung to a basalt ridge like a barnacle on the night” and “generators ticking like bones” are memorable and coherent, doing character, setting, and thematic work simultaneously. Line-level momentum remains strong despite lyric density, and there is little to no scaffolding or paraphrase looping. At the level of elite editorial scrutiny, a few metaphors could be trimmed to avoid any hint of overload, but they don’t materially blur sense or closure.</comment>
<question>8</question><grade>8.9</grade><comment>The “cosmic janitor” tending an almost-abandoned meteor station and using meteor-tracing as a grief ritual is a fresh, specific angle on mourning and connection. The synthesis of cosmic observation, maintenance work, and emotional vulnerability feels singular and memorable rather than templated.</comment>
