<question>4</question><grade>7.6</grade><comment>The stakes are primarily internal—can she bear learning that some breaks cannot be mended, including her own past losses—and this is signaled before she enters the Hall. The cost is conceptual and identity-based (surrendering the fantasy of total repair and the apron’s glow), which lands, but there’s little external or temporal squeeze to intensify the decision. A more pointed moment of possible refusal or wrong choice inside the Hall could heighten the sense of risk.</comment>

However, the line “It taught her that repair is not restoration, but reharmonization” states the thesis a bit baldly, nudging into on-the-nose territory. The piece still achieves a genuine aftereffect, but a touch more reliance on image over direct formulation would deepen its literary resonance.</comment>
