Closure lands as a resonant reconfiguration: the arbor’s prophecy reframed as ever-unfolding past and Elira’s vocation enlarged into a public liturgy. <question>3</question><grade>8.4</grade><comment>The ashen skyborne arbor is richly and consistently rendered, with details like “petrified roots…suspended between earth and ether” and dew-cradled bark crevices doing real worldbuilding work. Sensory specificity is high and POV-filtered—Elira touches, sorts, hums, and builds within this space in ways that feel lived-in rather than touristic. The only ceiling is that the environment doesn’t strongly constrain a concrete choice under pressure so much as steadily enable wonder.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>6.4</grade><comment>Conflict here is almost entirely gentle and internal: Elira wants to listen and decode; the world mostly yields to that desire rather than resisting it. Stakes are thematically significant (will the forgotten be honored?), but they are never felt as precarious—the outcome doesn’t seem in doubt. The result is a beautiful but low-pressure arc with limited escalation beyond increased density of wonders.</comment>

The relatively soft conflict keeps the thematic exploration powerful but not searing.</comment>

The lushness would need only minor trimming for certain elite venues, but the craft is already strong.</comment>

Elira’s tiny monuments and the idea of “sorting silence” are striking, memorable inflections on familiar themes of memory and listening.
