Internal contradiction—her simultaneous attraction to and weight from legacy—is present but could be sharpened under more direct pressure. <question>2</question><grade>8.5</grade><comment>The structure is a clean single-scene accumulative build: entering corridor → sensing echoes → discovering diaries → reading and reconfiguring her role → leaving an offering. Closure lands as a resonant reconfiguration: she shifts from extractor-mapper to co-dreamer and participant in legacy. Stakes are modest but legible throughout and the before/after line at the ending (“The walls dreamed. / And now, so would she.”) reads as a clear hinge.</comment>
<question>3</question><grade>8.7</grade><comment>Setting is richly particular: ivy-veiled passage, breathing buildings, collapsed antechamber, moss, shattered brick, bruised twilight sky all create a strong atmospheric frame. The ivy and walls meaningfully charge the final image (the passage exhaling; walls dreaming) even if they don’t tactically constrain choices. World rules (post–climate shift, Great Retreat) are sketched lightly but consistently and never break credibility for this lyric frame.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>7.6</grade><comment>The dominant stake is existential/emotional: how to bear and relate to inherited grief (“legacy’s burden”) and whether to merely map or to witness and share it. Pressure escalates primarily when she discovers that someone else was “trying to map the ache,” which deepens her task from technical to spiritual and forces the choice to leave her own sketch. The conflict remains largely internal and contemplative, with no sharp reversal or constrained options beyond whether to engage or turn away. That keeps the piece from higher tension scores, but within a quiet mode the deepening is coherent and sufficient.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>8.0</grade><comment>Themes of memory, grief, inheritance, and the ethics of witnessing are strongly woven through concrete images (half-burned diaries, negative strip, wildflowers in glass, initials in wet concrete). However, a few explicit thematic lines (“Legacy’s burden was not meant to be carried alone.”) edge into on-the-nose statement and slightly flatten what the imagery already implies. Overall the ending offers a resonant, if somewhat articulated, answer: to transform solitary burden into shared, ongoing dreaming.</comment>

There’s no head-hopping or stance wobble; the mild omniscient-style abstractions feel motivated by her attunement rather than authorial intrusion. Diction remains cohesive—elevated but not incongruous for this fabulist, reflective mode.</comment>

A couple of lines verge on purple or thematic over-assertion, but they don’t obscure clarity or dull the closure image. Information density is high without feeling cramped, and paragraphs cut cleanly around shifts in focus and pressure.</comment>
<question>8</question><grade>8.9</grade><comment>The “corridor echo cartographer” mapping emotional residues in architecture after climate shifts is an original and memorable premise, and the story commits to it instead of drifting into generic apocalypse. The final reconfiguration—walls and humans co-dreaming, sharing burden instead of merely recording loss—is a non-obvious but inevitable-feeling synthesis of earlier elements.
