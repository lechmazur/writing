The infantryman’s button on her desk functions as micro-setting and charges the final image as a compass-object. A slight tendency toward abstracted phrases (“paper and shadow”) keeps this just shy of fully exceptional.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>7.5</grade><comment>The central stake is her relationship to the archive—whether she is condemned to preserve loss or can ethically reorient it toward evidence of love. Pressure escalates gently as she discovers the button’s story and then maps seventeen artifacts, culminating in the decision to formalize this as a new practice (“Magnifications”). The emotional and ethical cost is present but relatively low-intensity; there’s no hard trade-off or serious risk beyond the vulnerability of re-believing in tenderness. The conflict is finely wrought but remains largely contemplative rather than sharply tested.</comment>

The piece still leaves a resonant aftertaste of moral complexity about how we read archives and pain.</comment>

<question>7</question><grade>8.0</grade><comment>Sentence-level craft is generally strong: varied rhythm, clear syntax, and high information density where most lines do at least two jobs. <question>8</question><grade>8.3</grade><comment>The combination of a post-emancipation foster youth archivist, dust-pattern forensics, and a single infantryman’s button as a keystone of quiet love is distinctive. The accumulative, observational drive suits the material and avoids gimmickry. It stops short of a truly startling turn, but the synthesis of concept, character, and object is memorably specific.</comment>
