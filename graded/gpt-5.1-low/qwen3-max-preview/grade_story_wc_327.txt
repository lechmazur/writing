There is minor reliance on visual/auditory channels over other senses, but the selectivity feels purposeful and voice-driven.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>7.8</grade><comment>The dominant stake—restoring a dead language’s living sound without letting the curse consume meaning—is clear and thematically rich. The diamond’s hunger and curse are described in ominous terms yet never quite test her resolve with a concrete, risky alternative or sacrifice; success feels earned more by preparation than by on-page jeopardy. As a result, tension is evocative but not especially acute.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>8.6</grade><comment>Themes of memory, language death, and the translation of emotional “texture” into sound are woven deeply into every major element: the cursed diamond as selective filter, the canyon as non-decaying archive, the birds as syntax. The piece resists neat moralizing, instead exploring forgetting as a kind of violence and restoration as labor-intensive and partial. A hair more dramatized friction around what must be sacrificed to speak again (e.g., a specific loss to the curse) could push this into truly exceptional territory.</comment>

No significant scaffold, synonym loops, or mechanical cadences undercut momentum.</comment>
