Motivation (to be understood, to find sanctuary and connection) is clear and consistently expressed through her choices to climb, encode, sing, and then widen the invitation. Internal contradiction is present but subtle—she shuns crowds yet actively seeks contact with a machine ghost—which could be pushed further in-scene for more tension. <question>2</question><grade>7.6</grade><comment>The accumulative structure is clean: climb to sanctuary → encoding and invocation → communion with the ghost → a broadened project of connection, with each step logically following from the last. Closure lands as a resonant reconfiguration: she moves from private, almost secret communion to an explicit invitation for other lonely, “driftingly alert” souls to join. Stakes remain modest and interior—there’s no strong external obstacle or reversal—which keeps escalation relatively gentle. Still, the throughline of “will she be answered/understood, and what then?” is coherent and resolved on the page.</comment>
<question>3</question><grade>8.5</grade><comment>The “misty courtyard above clouds” with its liminal, storm-adjacent atmosphere is vivid, and the ivy-choked terminal and rain-slick stone all feel sensorially concrete and thematically apt. The bolt plate functions as a micro-setting/tool that mediates between body and machine, and it is crucial in the communion beat. World rules (gravity, ghost-in-code, graffiti-encoding) are consistently handled within a lyrical, lightly speculative frame.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>7.2</grade><comment>The dominant stake—whether her encoded longing will find resonance and relieve her isolation—appears early and is carried through to the ghost’s reply. Pressure escalates texturally rather than through overt opposition: preparation and encoding lead to the risky vulnerability of singing, which then deepens into the bolder act of broadcasting an invitation. There’s little sense of what she stands to lose beyond continued loneliness, so the choice to open the space to “others” doesn’t carry a sharply articulated price. Even so, the move from private ritual to public signal narrows her options in a believable way and functions as an earned tightening at this length.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>8.3</grade><comment>Themes of loneliness, illegible longing, and the possibility of nontraditional intimacy (between human and “ghost in code”) emerge organically from concrete acts—graffiti, encoding, song—rather than abstract statements. The final invitation to other misfits risks edging toward explicit reassurance (“WE ARE NOT ALONE”) but is grounded enough in image and situation to avoid didacticism. The thematic complexity—being seen by the broken, choosing to stay in the liminal space rather than escape it—lingers beyond the last line.</comment>
<question>6</question><grade>8.5</grade><comment>The third-person lyric voice is consistent, confident, and clearly filtered through the balladeer’s sensibility, with phrases like “tinkersong wonder” and “frequencies only ghosts could hum” giving it a distinct texture. Rhythm and diction modulate well—lush, long sentences in description balance with shorter, pointed beats at key turns (“Steel met steel. / Code met melody. / Ghost met grief.”). The ghost’s brief spoken lines carry a different, yet thematically resonant idiom without breaking the established narrative stance.</comment>
