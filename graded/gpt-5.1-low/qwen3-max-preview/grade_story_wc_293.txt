<question>3</question><grade>8.4</grade><comment>The mountain pullout, mist, and ridge are vividly rendered and tightly integrated with the imagery of liminality and transformation; the environment clearly isn’t generic. It doesn’t quite constrain choices in a tactical way, but it strongly charges the ending, which is enough for a high score.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>7.2</grade><comment>The dominant stake—whether the fiddler will remain trapped in regret and “never” or enter some form of amnesty himself—is legible and present from early on. The cost—accepting he cannot (and should not) chase the hitchhiker’s ghost—registers, but the internal struggle feels relatively smooth, with limited sense that he might choose otherwise. <question>5</question><grade>8.2</grade><comment>Forgiveness as “pixelated amnesty” is an original thematic frame, and the story mostly lets it emerge from concrete images: coordinates on scaffold flutters, the glass flake recomposing a self, shards as forgiven moments. Some lines flirt with abstraction (“not absolution, but allowance. Permission to be remade.”), nudging toward essayistic statement rather than pure dramatization. Even so, the piece leaves a resonant, non-didactic aftertaste centered on possibility after irreversible choice.</comment>

<question>7</question><grade>7.8</grade><comment>The line-level work is lush, image-dense, and largely precise; many sentences do multiple jobs (worldbuilding, emotion, and theme) without obvious scaffolding. This keeps the piece just shy of the extreme tautness and economy expected at the very top tier.</comment>
