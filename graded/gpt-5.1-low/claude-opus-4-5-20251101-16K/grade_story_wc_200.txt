<question>2</question><grade>7.2</grade><comment>The accumulative structure—play harp, draw spirits, elicit paired confession and joy, then release—is clear and cohesive, with a legible before/after in how souls depart more whole and Maren stands as the cost-bearing repository. However, the arc is very linear and low-friction; no beat forces a new tactic or truly tests her method, so the final recommitment feels more like confirmation than earned shift.</comment>
<question>3</question><grade>8.2</grade><comment>Echo Gorge, the hidden sunrise, the warped harp, and the crocodile tooth create a vivid, coherent liminal world whose rules we learn through use rather than exposition. A slightly stronger sense of physical risk or constraint from the environment itself would push this toward the very top tier.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>6.3</grade><comment>The dominant stake—helping lost souls pass while accumulating their emotional weight—is clear, but pressure barely shifts: each soul arrives, confesses, laughs, and moves on with no resistance or complication. Maren’s cost is described rather than tested; she never has to choose between her own thinning self and their relief, or confront a soul her method can’t easily soothe. As a result, the conflict feels more like a beautifully rendered ritual than a scene that tightens or forecloses options.</comment>

The one explicit thesis line about “integrat[ing] laughter into healing” edges toward telling; a touch more reliance on image over statement would deepen the afterglow.</comment>

There’s some mild omniscient flavor around the grandmother and the collectors’ world, but it doesn’t break focal coherence or tone.</comment>
<question>7</question><grade>8.2</grade><comment>The line-level work is polished: sentences vary in rhythm, images are mostly clean and specific, and there’s virtually no scaffolding or paraphrase loops. <question>8</question><grade>7.9</grade><comment>The conceit of a psychopomp who heals by pairing confession with remembered absurd joy, using a misaligned harp in an echoing canyon, feels fresh and thoughtfully integrated. The piece stops short of a bolder narrative risk—there’s no unexpected reversal or deeply unsettling cost revelation—which keeps it just below the most memorable, high-risk work at this length.</comment>
