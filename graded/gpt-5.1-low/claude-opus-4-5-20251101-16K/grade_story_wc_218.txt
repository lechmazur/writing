<question>4</question><grade>7.4</grade><comment>The dominant stake—what Maren can achieve with the orchard before she dies—is clearly established and deepened as the network begins to respond to her interventions. However, the conflict is almost entirely internal and unopposed; there’s no real resistance from people or world, so the path toward her goal is rarely challenged. This keeps the piece meditative rather than tense, effective but not maximally pressurized.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>8.3</grade><comment>Themes of reciprocity, forgotten symbiosis, and nonhuman agency emerge organically through concrete particulars—the copper wiring, root-pillar embrace, and multi-species habitat—rather than overt moralizing for most of the piece. The conceptual reweighting at the end—that architecture, ecology, and death are all parts of one network—is resonant and lingers. A bit more friction or ambiguity around human incomprehension (beyond “visitors from the mainland noticed nothing”) could deepen the complexity further.</comment>
