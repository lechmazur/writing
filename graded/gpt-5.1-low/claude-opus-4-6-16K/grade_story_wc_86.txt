Closure lands as a charged poise: the terms have not been accepted, but the envelope, lunar schedule, and nine-day clock create a clear before/after with cost-laden uncertainty. <question>3</question><grade>8.8</grade><comment>The parapet, mist, wind, and unseen valley are vivid and purposeful, embodying “neutral territory” as precarious, weather-scoured space and shaping behavior (no weapons, dim lantern, wind tugging at the chart). The environment and the recurring moon motif both charge the final image of the valley “balanced between what had been broken and what might…still be repaired.” This integration of place, weather, and cosmic clock into theme and tension is exemplary.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>8.0</grade><comment>The dominant stake—whether the exchange can proceed under terms that acknowledge Verdaine—is clear and present from Dashev’s opening objection. The cost is largely conceptual/political rather than viscerally dramatized, and the exchange remains courteous rather than sharply testing someone’s breaking point, so escalation feels measured rather than bracing. Still, the nine-day deadline and the unresolved question of whether “sorry means anything” give the conflict a tangible edge.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>8.7</grade><comment>Themes of guilt versus action, the sanctity and fragility of neutrality, and the ownership of time (clocks vs. moon) emerge organically from particulars—the drawer pull, the parapet’s shifting allegiance, the moon schedule—rather than from abstract sermonizing. A few lines edge close to thesis-statement territory, but they’re brief and well-supported by scene work.</comment>
