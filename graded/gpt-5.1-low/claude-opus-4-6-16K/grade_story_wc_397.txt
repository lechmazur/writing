The Arctic setting’s bleakness and physical instability quietly pressure her choices and mirror the thematic preoccupation with collapse without overbearing symbolism.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>8.3</grade><comment>The dominant stake—how she will relate to her own death—is clear early and tightens through her choice to seek endings in disaster zones rather than in a clinic room. Escalation is mostly internal and accumulative: the stroke’s preview hum, weeks of listening among ruins, and then the on-page moment when she hears that sound again and must decide whether it calls her now. The final choice to turn away from the edge and back to work is a genuine stance crystallization, though the internal push-pull at that peak remains fairly serene, with limited felt risk of relapse into the earlier euthanasia plan.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>8.8</grade><comment>Themes of mortality, agency over one’s death, and the texture of endings emerge from concrete particulars—the clinic arrangements, the stroke’s “eleven seconds,” Arctic collapses—rather than abstract sermonizing. One line edges toward explicit statement (“the ending had its own music, and that knowing the melody did not mean you had to sing it today”) but it feels earned by prior scene work and functions more as crystallization than as moral.</comment>
