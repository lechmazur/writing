<question>4</question><grade>8.4</grade><comment>The dominant stake—whether Maren can reclaim a stolen piece of identity and work—is clear early and deepens as she realizes the ring fed on wonder. The cost is internal and conceptual rather than life-or-death, but it is sharply felt at the moment she chooses to face the mechanism that has already taken so much. The conflict remains primarily interior and could bear one more moment of friction or hesitation, but for this length it coheres well.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>8.8</grade><comment>Themes of memory, self-erasure through scholarship, and the ethics of curiosity are woven through concrete motifs (the empty notebook, the memory-eating ring, the drowned orchard meant to preserve what was lost). Her final choice to stand in the ring and then begin writing reframes folklore as something that can reclaim the self rather than only extract from others. The aftertaste is quietly haunting and reflective, with enough ambiguity around the ring’s nature to sustain thought.</comment>
