Closure is a resonant reconfiguration: the community resumes the ritual in altered form, and the final “thaw” marks a clear before/after in their cultural stasis. The waterfall and its filtered light recur and culminate in the final image of thaw within its unending roar, directly charging the closure. Setting and small objects-as-setting (the lens, the tuber, the loom) feel indispensable rather than ornamental.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>8.2</grade><comment>The central stake—whether the displaced can preserve a living culture rather than dissolving into formless exile—is clearly framed and tightened through the loss of bread and the beekeeper. Maret’s decision to improvise a new flour source and restart the ritual is a credible act of agency that addresses that stake and carries cost (inferior food, risk of failure, acknowledgement that the old world is gone). The escalation is more thematic than confrontational, which suits the piece’s mode but does mean there’s little concrete resistance or complication once she chooses to act. A single on-page snag or objection could sharpen the sense of price paid.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>8.8</grade><comment>Themes of cultural erasure, memory as embodied practice, and the politics of “degenerate” art are woven through events rather than stapled on. The final exchange—“What does K taste like?” / “Like remembering”—reweights everything before it without feeling like a tidy moral.</comment>

There is no POV leakage or gratuitous explanation; perception is clearly filtered through her values and profession. The piece flirts with high lyricism but maintains clarity and control throughout.</comment>
