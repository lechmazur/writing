<question>3</question><grade>9.1</grade><comment>The topaz workshop under an open, star-losing sky is vivid, specific, and constantly active: the cut topaz floor, projections, and roofless exposure all shape both perception and what’s possible. The environment is not postcard; it literally functions as a lens, constraining how Maren gathers data and forcing her to read the changing sky as equation. Key objects (press, type, halo fragment, drying line, pillars) are integrated into action and the final image, with the setting reweighted at the end when she stands under the altered constellations counting absence. The world’s rules (equations from starlight, emotional mathematics) are consistent and learned through on-page observation rather than exposition dumps.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>8.4</grade><comment>The dominant stake—whether Maren can capture/understand the “mathematics of emotion” before the stars (her data source) vanish—is clearly signaled and coherently pressured by the ongoing erasures. Escalation comes through density and revelation: the halo fragment’s properties, the recognition that the star loss follows her sought-after waveform, and the fragment going cold as another star disappears. The final choice is one of stance and practice (accepting the equation as feeling, integrating the fragment into her toolset, committing to “learning the mathematics of what remains”), which works well for the lyric mode but remains mostly internalized and conceptual rather than facing a sharply defined alternative path. The cost—embracing unbearable clarity instead of an abstract solvable problem—is legible but could be made a hair more concrete or perilous to enter the very top band.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>9.0</grade><comment>The thematic weave among grief, empathy, ambiguity, and the desire to mathematize feeling is impressively tight: the letterpress, the equations, the borrowed personalities, the halo fragment, and the vanishing stars all converge on a single question about how, and whether, emotion can be rendered knowable. Lines like “Deliberate ambiguity was not evasion; it was architecture” and “Empathy required not knowing…” articulate ideas, but they arise organically from Maren’s practice and choices rather than reading as bolt-on thesis. The final repricing—equation as “the precise, turbulent, unbearable clarity of feeling what the universe feels when it subtracts something it loved”—reweights everything that came before without collapsing into a tidy moral. The piece invites sustained reflection on loss and connection through its particulars rather than through overt sermonizing.</comment>
<question>6</question><grade>9.1</grade><comment>The third-person close voice has a distinct, consistent texture: analytical yet haunted, with metaphoric precision matched to Maren’s sensibility as a typesetter and quasi-mathematician of feeling. Psychic distance is well-controlled, dipping into near-free-indirect without leakage, and the modulation of rhythm as the stars vanish and the fragment changes state is deft. There is no dialogue to test, but the interior narrative cadence carries subtext and pressure on its own.</comment>

Rhythm varies nicely, with longer sinuous lines balanced by shorter, cutting statements at key beats (“But the stars were vanishing.” / “She understood then.”). The density is high enough that one or two spots edge toward ornateness, but clarity and momentum remain intact, including at the closure beat.</comment>
