The daylily as a recurring physical object becomes a micro-setting anchor that charges the final image. The setting rarely constrains her choices physically, so it’s slightly more atmospheric than tactical, but it’s tightly woven to tone and theme.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>8.3</grade><comment>The dominant stake—whether Elena continues to bear her grief alone or begins to share/witness it—is clear from early on. Pressure deepens from mere insomnia to a forced confrontation with the telegram’s message, culminating in the choice to write a new kind of text (a letter) and to enter sleep differently. The dream acceptance is a small but decisive turn that forecloses the path of pure avoidance. Escalation is quiet and interior, but the narrowing of options—from numbing distance to engaged witnessing—reads.</comment>

However, the line “She was a woman who healed with words, though she had never permitted herself to believe it” slides into explicit statement, slightly undercutting subtext. Overall the ending image (stone held between them, lighter) gives a resonant, non-literal answer without needing a moral spelled out.</comment>

The core emotional arc (reconciling with a dead parent and long-avoided grief) is familiar, but the astronomy and archaeology frame, plus the dream-work, give it distinctiveness.
