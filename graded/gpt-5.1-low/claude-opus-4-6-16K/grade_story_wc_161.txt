<question>2</question><grade>8.8</grade><comment>The causal chain is clean: attic work → discovery of the chip → paper-trail investigation → contact with Liv → invitation and emotional reckoning → decision to send the chip and go herself. The structure balances reflection and present action without disorientation or dead weight; if anything, the external stakes remain modest, which slightly softens the sense of risk.</comment>
<question>3</question><grade>8.8</grade><comment>The attic above Hargrove’s is rendered with precise, lived-in detail—foxed pages, ledgers, skylight, dust motes—and these elements are not decorative: the archive enables the plot and the skylight/dust motif charges both the meteorite moment and the final pigeon image. Setting and objects (attic, books, skylight, chip) participate in the story’s emotional logic of decay and fragile continuity. There’s little to fault; this is immersive and economical.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>8.4</grade><comment>The dominant stake—whether she will remain in self-protective isolation or risk reclaiming her true history—is clear and deepens steadily through the meteorite investigation and Liv’s offer. The relatively low external pressure and the smoothness of her final decision keep the tension from feeling as sharp as it could at elite levels.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>8.5</grade><comment>Motifs of birds, paper trails, dust, and the meteorite braid into a resonant theme about memory, survival, and the ethics of self-reinvention, with the final line about balance elegantly reframing her prior behavior. The one slightly on-the-nose articulation—“Balance…is not the absence of falling”—edges toward aphorism, trimming a bit of the piece’s otherwise organic subtlety.</comment>

The voice borders on ornate at times but remains controlled and character-true.</comment>
