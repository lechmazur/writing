<question>1</question><grade>8.2</grade><comment>Maren’s professional lens and inherited obsession from Odile give her clear, distinctive motivation, and her actions under pressure (sabotaging the bell, borrowing time, choosing to “uncage” rather than just observe) align tightly with that drive. Interiority is concrete and filtered through her illustrator’s eye. What’s faint is any deeper ambivalence or self-division about risking herself and the city for this discovery; the internal conflict could be sharpened. <question>2</question><grade>8.9</grade><comment>The causal chain is clean: belief → long study → engineered bell failure → temporal manipulation → encounter with larval echoes → choice to free them before the giant wakes. The single-scene structure with interleaved backstory is tight, with no orientation debt and a clear time-boxed frame (the giant’s nap). Closure lands as a Conceptual Repricing: Maren discovers and redefines echoes with on-page cost (time debt, giant stirring, canals roughening). The only thing keeping it just short of exceptional is that the consequences of freeing the echoes remain conceptually, not concretely, risky.</comment>
<question>3</question><grade>9.0</grade><comment>The Roofscape—towers, rooftop canals, jade water, bell towers, and the sleeping giant—is vivid and particular, and details are always filtered through Maren’s priorities as a natural history illustrator. Setting is not decorative: the calm canals, bell sequence, and giant’s breathing actively constrain tactics and clock the action. The environment also charges the final image as she paddles home through rising waters to paint what she’s seen. World rules around canals, tides, and bells are consistent and legible without exposition drag.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>8.0</grade><comment>The dominant stake—witnessing and capturing the birth of echoes before the nap ends and the canals become dangerous—is clear and crisply set up. Pressure escalates via the ticking Carillon sequence and the giant’s approaching wakefulness, culminating in Maren’s decision to strike her paddle and free the echoes instead of remaining a passive observer. The cost is present but understated: the time debt, the city’s potential instability, and the loss of a repeatable, pristine experimental condition. More explicit friction between preserving safety/order and pursuing discovery would lift the sense of stakes further.</comment>

The mentor’s legacy and Maren’s choice to intervene deepen a quiet theme about what science owes to its subjects. A hint more moral or emotional complication around uncaging the echoes could add further resonance.</comment>
