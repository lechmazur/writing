<question>3</question><grade>8.7</grade><comment>The subway maintenance tunnel is vividly realized—“wet earth and cold iron,” brick-lined passage, high-water mark, and the rumbling active line above all feel tactile and purposeful. The lantern functions as an object-as-setting, a charged tool whose history and physical presence shape perception and the final image. The only ceiling is that the environment constrains emotion and perception more than it forces hard tactical choices.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>7.8</grade><comment>The dominant stake is Elias’s choice between signing off a convenient “misadventure” and honoring the complex, psychologically driven preventability of Finch’s death. His final decision to write a fuller, more resistant report is an earned stance crystallization under modest but real institutional pressure. The conflict never quite reaches a sharp on-page confrontation with Fletcher or a clearly costly consequence, which keeps the stakes from feeling fully acute.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>8.0</grade><comment>Theme coheres around how people become possessed by narratives—Finch by the lantern’s history, Elias by his duty to prevent flattening of death into simple accidents—and how instruments of freedom can become cages. The final image of the lantern as both promise and cage and Elias reclaiming “the fortified quiet of his own mind” provides a resonant answer without a bald moral. Some thematic statements (“a man possessed by a narrative,” “controlling influence”) edge close to essayistic exposition, which slightly blunts subtlety.</comment>

Psychic distance is well modulated, with the prose tightening around the uncanny jolts and relaxing into more reflective description as he interprets them. A few abstract phrases (“residual consciousness,” “entangled memories were a concept for poets”) flirt with analytic register, but generally remain in character.</comment>
