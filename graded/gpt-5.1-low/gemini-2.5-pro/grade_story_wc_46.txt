Closure lands: before she is an “earthbound star watcher with no stars,” after she literally holds a star-like light she can share. Stakes and costs remain small-scale and largely internal, but legible and coherent.</comment>
<question>3</question><grade>8.5</grade><comment>The serpentine grotto, mist, salt, and stone are vividly and economically rendered; sensory detail is selective and strongly filtered through Elara’s priorities as an observer of light. The sea glass and grotto walls serve as charged micro-settings in the final image, reconfiguring the space from confinement to embrace. World rules are consistent and unobtrusive, and description rarely feels like static postcarding.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>6.8</grade><comment>Conflict is primarily internal and philosophical—fire vs starlight, destruction vs patient radiance—with the main pressure being her need to reclaim light without repeating past harms. There is a meaningful mid-scene tightening when she pauses to weigh the shard’s potential against the memory of the fire, choosing to continue; and an external deepening when the stray sliver of twilight catalyzes the glass’s change. However, stakes remain relatively abstract, and the felt cost is more conceptual than visceral, keeping tension in a contemplative register rather than a sharply escalated one.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>7.5</grade><comment>Themes of reclamation, gentler power, and transforming refuse into quiet solace arise organically from concrete particulars—sea glass, star charts, village fire—without overt moralizing. The final image of a “star she could share” that comforts rather than dominates offers a resonant, non-literal answer to the opening problem. The piece edges toward explicitness (“a different kind of power”) but remains mostly earned through scene and image.</comment>
