<question>2</question><grade>8.2</grade><comment>The structure is cleanly causal: suspicions accumulate → she synthesizes them into a coup narrative → a narrow communication window appears → she acts to transmit the warning. Closure lands as a charged poise: the message is sent with clear stakes and risk, and the final waiting stance feels earned and bounded by the coup clock. The only limit is that the external consequences are all off-page, so the cost remains conceptual rather than viscerally demonstrated.</comment>

<question>3</question><grade>8.4</grade><comment>The cobalt observatory, grand telescope, eternal candle, and turning dome are vivid and integrated into action rather than set-dressing. The environment genuinely constrains choices—the calibration protocol, unmonitored narrow-band channel, and dusty auxiliary terminal create the vector for her intervention. The eternal candle returns in the last image, charging the final moment with a quiet, almost mythic continuity between her small act and the observatory’s larger history. Autumn as a detail is more atmospheric than functional, but the micro-setting work overall is strong and purposeful.</comment>

<question>4</question><grade>7.4</grade><comment>The dominant stake—forestalling a coup via a single covert transmission—is clear and escalates when the calibration window is framed as a one-shot chance and the system lag threatens failure. The tension is mostly procedural and internal; there’s little on-page antagonistic pressure (no guards interrupting, no immediate risk of discovery) to sharpen the conflict. Her agency is strong and the timing challenge provides a real tightening, but the conflict could feel more dangerous if a concrete on-page consequence pressed against her act. As is, the stakes are high conceptually but somewhat muted experientially.</comment>

<question>5</question><grade>8.0</grade><comment>Themes of overlooked wisdom, the power of listening to “unheard stories,” and the translation of pattern-making (embroidery) into political resistance are coherently woven through the piece. The final image—silence now “filled with a new echo”—offers a resonant answer to the premise of who gets to record history. The complexity is solid, though a deeper hint of moral ambiguity (e.g., collateral costs of her choice) could push it higher.</comment>

<question>6</question><grade>7.8</grade><comment>The third-person-limited voice is controlled, consistent, and suits a reflective, slightly elevated SF tone. There is no head-hopping, and psychic distance is modulated smoothly between description and interior inference. The voice is strong but not yet unmistakably singular; an extra trace of idiosyncratic perception under peak pressure would move it into exceptional territory.</comment>

<question>7</question><grade>8.0</grade><comment>The prose is polished, with varied sentence rhythm and high information density; most lines do double duty for setting, character, and stakes. There is minimal scaffolding or repetition, and action order is always clear, including the technical sequence of the transmission. A very slight tendency toward abstract phrasing (“story of treason plotted just before the coup”) rather than concrete embodiment keeps it shy of the very top band.</comment>

<question>8</question><grade>8.2</grade><comment>The fusion of an elderly embroiderer, an eternal candle, and a high-tech observatory coup plot feels fresh and specific, especially the idea of encoding troop movements into altered star charts and hijacking a calibration signal. The closure image—quiet, candlelit endurance after a single decisive, unseen act—avoids standard thriller beats in favor of a more literary poise.
