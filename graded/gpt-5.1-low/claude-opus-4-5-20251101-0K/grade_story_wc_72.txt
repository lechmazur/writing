<question>3</question><grade>8.4</grade><comment>The drowned garden is vivid and specific—coral-claimed marble, phosphorescent algae, statues as habitats—and this atmosphere permeates the entire piece. The environment meaningfully charges the final image of an immortal listener watching bubbles rise in a place designed to wait. However, the setting rarely constrains tactics or choices beyond being a beautiful, eerie backdrop; the underwater constraints on Mira’s body and options are underused. World rules about the consciousness-essence are consistent enough for the length.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>7.6</grade><comment>The dominant stake—Mira’s need to know what really happened to her brother and what it means for death—is clear and present from her first appearance. Pressure deepens as Veris reveals that her brother chose to “rejoin,” forcing Mira to abandon the simpler desire for vindication. That said, the conflict remains largely internal and resolves without much resistance; Mira doesn’t meaningfully push back on or test the revelations, so escalation feels more like steady illumination than a hard choice. The “invitation” to rejoin is powerful but deferred, so the felt cost at this beat is more potential than actual.</comment>

However, the last paragraphs drift into abstract, essayistic phrasing (“some truths existed between the certainties,” “where light divided and rejoined”) that states the theme rather than fully trusting the images. This mild moralizing flattens what would otherwise be an even more resonant ending.</comment>

The telepathic dialogue is handled cleanly and advances both plot and emotional stakes. There’s minor convergence into more generic “literary” abstraction at the end, but not enough to break the voice.</comment>

The main weakness is a slight increase in abstract, conceptual language in the final two paragraphs, which softens immediacy compared to the stronger concrete images earlier. Still, clarity, momentum, and information density remain high throughout.</comment>

The story wisely keeps its scope intimate, focusing on one loss and one conversation rather than inflating toward cosmic stakes.
