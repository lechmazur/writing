<question>3</question><grade>9.1</grade><comment>The midnight glade above the clouds is intensely specific and sensorial—cloud sea, pressing stars, jasmine and ozone, “grief preserved in amber”—and always filtered through Morrigan’s emotional state. Recurring objects within the setting (silver ladder, saucer, cards) charge the final return to earth and the pocketed saucer. Environment and concept meaningfully constrain options, supporting a high-band score.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>8.0</grade><comment>The dominant stake (reclaiming three fugitive years of love and selfhood) is clear and emotionally weighty, and the pressure does tighten when the family ritual unexpectedly activates and the door appears. However, once she commits, the retrieval itself proceeds almost frictionlessly; there’s little on-page risk of loss, failure, or unwanted consequence beyond tears. As a result, the conflict feels deep but relatively smooth, more contemplative than sharply embattled.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>8.7</grade><comment>Thematic throughline—motherhood, time, exhaustion, and the distinction between losing time and being unable to hold it—is strong and grows organically from cards, saucer, cord, and glade. At moments the piece edges toward explicit statement (“divination was not prophecy but archaeology”; “too exhausted…to hold it all”), but the images generally carry the weight. The cracked saucer and tablecloth/hand-tracing custom give the story a resonant, non-didactic final image.</comment>
