<question>1</question><grade>8.2</grade><comment>Maren is sharply drawn: a hyper-competent, dry, slightly obsessive cartographer whose need for geographical truth clearly compensates for relational failures (“Why competence had never been enough to make her lovable”). The final image of her crossing the groaning lake toward unmapped territory is charged by everything we’ve learned about this place. The piece fully meets the “object/setting charges final image” gate without excess exposition.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>7.4</grade><comment>The dominant stake—reaching the true headwaters before the ice closes, versus staying safe and unresolved—is clear from early on. Still, escalation is relatively gentle; nothing new complicates her goal between falcon’s return and departure, so the pressure never quite sharpens into a full test.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>8.6</grade><comment>Themes of knowability versus unknowability, maps versus messy lives, and “where water remembers its purpose” versus people losing theirs emerge organically from concrete details. There is a touch of aphoristic phrasing, but it stops short of sermonizing.</comment>

Diction is elevated but plausible for the character and setting, and psychic distance stays stable in a close third. The only mild limitation is that the overall density borders on lush for a quiet scene, which might risk heaviness in a longer piece but works here.</comment>

While an expedition-into-the-unknown frame is familiar, the execution feels particular to this character and profession rather than templated. It stops short of radical formal ingenuity but sits comfortably at the high end of strong, original work.</comment>
