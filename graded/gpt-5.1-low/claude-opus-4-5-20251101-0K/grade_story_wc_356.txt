<question>3</question><grade>8.2</grade><comment>The treetop village and forest canopy are vividly rendered with selective, sense-driven details, especially smell, and feel deeply filtered through an herbalist with an acute nose. Setting doesn’t strongly constrain her options tactically, but it does charge both the corpse flower experience and the final descent image. The world-building is specific enough to feel singular without bogging the story down.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>7.0</grade><comment>The dominant stake is Mira’s quest for a deeper mode of wisdom/serenity, signaled early and paid off in her choice to leave despite the village’s allure. Escalation is primarily internal: she endures the flower’s stench, reframes her perception through drawings, and then must decide whether to remain in this apparent haven. However, the pressure remains gentle—there’s little sense that staying would cost her or others something concrete beyond stagnation, so the trade-off at the end feels conceptually weighty but emotionally soft. A sharper test of her resolve in the vigil or a more fraught offer from Thessaly could have tightened stakes.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>7.4</grade><comment>Themes of death feeding life, attraction/repulsion, and wisdom found in what we reject are woven through the corpse flower’s nature and Mira’s vigil, culminating in her decision to embody “serenity within motion” rather than seek a static refuge. The final realization—that serenity is in how she moves through the world, not the village itself—organically reweights the earlier material. However, several thematic lines are stated almost thesis-like (“the smell taught her…,” “wisdom hides in what we instinctively reject”), which blunts subtext and subtlety. The piece could trust its images a bit more and explain them less.</comment>

The ending doesn’t hinge on a twist, but on a quiet conceptual reweighting, which suits the material.
