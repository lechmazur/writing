World rules—lost languages, crystalline trees, grafters—are suggested through use rather than exposition. The environment charges the final images and theme, though it rarely constrains concrete choices, which keeps this just shy of the very top band.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>6.8</grade><comment>The dominant stake—finding a form of belonging and being found—is clear, and the introduction of the mysterious tag after the elder’s death provides a decent externalization of that need. There is some escalation as the search becomes ritual and community investment grows, culminating in the reveal of Senna and her need to be noticed. However, there’s little real resistance or risk; no option is truly foreclosed, and the “search” proceeds without credible threat of failure or fracture. The result is emotionally affecting but low-pressure, more contemplative than conflict-driven.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>7.2</grade><comment>The story’s thematic core—belonging, being sought, and integrating one’s strangeness into community—emerges naturally from the pattern of actions and images (tags at transformation sites, reed figures incomplete alone). However, the final paragraphs tip into explicit thesis-stating (“To graft oneself onto community…”; “Webs of kinship, she called it…”) that edges toward moralizing rather than trusting subtext. The complexity is there, but the on-the-nose articulation slightly dulls its afterglow.</comment>

<question>8</question><grade>8.3</grade><comment>The fusion of a speculative orchard of crystalline trees, lost languages remembered by land, and reed-sculpted emotion feels distinctive and memorable. The choice to build stakes around being noticed and necessary, rather than around grand catastrophe, reads as a deliberate, thoughtful risk.
