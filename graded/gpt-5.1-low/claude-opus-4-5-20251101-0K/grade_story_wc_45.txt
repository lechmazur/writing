<question>4</question><grade>7.8</grade><comment>The dominant stake—Eleanor’s need to reconcile skepticism with her mother’s “madness” and her sister’s fate—is established early and deepens as she chooses to desecrate the grave in pursuit of truth. The final decision to keep and study the bones, turning grief into scholarship and a search for Charlotte, crystallizes her stance. However, the final paragraphs edge into explicit thesis statements (“Science could expand to hold mystery without breaking”; “turn sorrow into stars”) that feel a bit like moralizing rather than letting images carry the weight. This slight over-explication blunts what is otherwise strong thematic resonance.</comment>

Rhythm and syntax vary enough to keep the prose alive, and there’s no head-hopping or stance confusion. Occasionally the narration drifts into essayistic abstraction, but not enough to disrupt the POV’s integrity.</comment>

<question>8</question><grade>7.8</grade><comment>The fusion of a changeling myth with a rigorously trained doctor who treats fairy evidence as empirical data is a fresh, appealing angle, especially in the choice to transform grief into a scholarly project. However, the core ingredients (rational skeptic confronted with supernatural proof, stolen-by-fair-folk sister) are familiar in speculative fiction, and the piece doesn’t quite push them into startling territory. Its ingenuity lies more in the elegant synthesis and emotional honesty than in raw novelty.</comment>
