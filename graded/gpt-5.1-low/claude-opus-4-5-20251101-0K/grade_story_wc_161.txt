Closure is clear and on-page: beginning a journal and the recharged meteorite image mark a before/after with emotional cost. The design is quiet and largely internal, but there are no orientation or lens slips, and the final beat properly gathers prior motifs.</comment>
<question>3</question><grade>9.0</grade><comment>The attic and skylight are vividly rendered (“smelled of decay and possibility”) and consistently refracted through Elara’s sensibility, giving the piece a strong, coherent atmosphere. The micro-setting of trunk and skylight—not just seen but used as a balancing platform and meditation site—shapes her rituals and how she lets Mira in. The meteorite as an object-in-setting is exquisitely integrated, charging both the central realization and the closing image. Environmental details never feel touristic; they’re sparse, chosen, and thematically live.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>8.2</grade><comment>The central stake is Elara’s capacity to trust and risk visibility versus remain safely erased, signaled early via her flight from investigators and reinforced by her attic seclusion. Pressure tightens when Mira asks, “Did you always work with books?” and then “Show me,” forcing Elara to choose between her practiced lie and authentic, dangerous disclosure. The decision to speak, hand over the meteorite, and then start a journal forecloses the prior path of total anonymity with an implied but real potential cost. The conflict remains largely internal and could stand one more concrete external pressure, but at this scale the escalation reads coherent and sufficient.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>8.4</grade><comment>Themes of trust, reinvention, and the persistence of origins (“decay and possibility,” paper trails, messages across distances, the meteorite’s memory of where it began) interlock elegantly through image and event. The repetition of messengers (pigeons, meteorite, then Elara’s journal) creates a satisfying conceptual lattice about what we carry for others and for ourselves. The line “That’s what happens to everything that matters, I think. We lose things. We find them transformed.” and the explicit definition of trust at the end edge toward stating the thesis rather than leaving it fully in subtext. Even so, the closing bloom image and meteorite on the sill give a felt, not just told, answer to the story’s question.</comment>

Dialogue is sparse but natural and purposeful, with Mira’s simpler, direct speech contrasting effectively with the more reflective narration. There’s no head-hopping or stance confusion, and the modulation into slightly more declarative reflection near the end remains anchored in character.</comment>
<question>7</question><grade>8.8</grade><comment>The prose is clean, with high information density and minimal redundancy; nearly every sentence advances character, atmosphere, or theme. Rhythm and paragraphing are well-judged, allowing reflection without bogging momentum. A few sentences border on aphoristic, but they don’t significantly blunt the closure image or create notable scaffolding.</comment>

The “decay and possibility” / “withering and bloom” tonal pairing gives the piece a distinctive sensibility that could be recognized as its own.
