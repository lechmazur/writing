Closure lands as a resonant reconfiguration—Maren shifting from silent courier to active guardian—with a clear before/after. <question>3</question><grade>9.1</grade><comment>The fogbound lighthouse, rusted stairs, bird’s nest, dead lens, and haunted lantern room are vivid and tightly filtered through Maren’s seafarer gaze. The notion of a “dead” light replaced by a faint, grateful glow enacts the thematic shift on a spatial, sensory level. Setting and objects are so integral that removing or flattening them would collapse both mood and meaning.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>8.0</grade><comment>The central stake—whether the chain of safety and memory will break here—emerges clearly once Maren realizes there is no keeper and feels the ghost’s longing. The escalation is primarily emotional and atmospheric rather than oppositional; the outcome never feels truly in doubt, which softens tension slightly. Still, her decision to commit, rebuild the cairn, and add her letter closes a genuine trade-off between isolation and responsibility.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>8.5</grade><comment>Theme coheres around guardianship, inheritance, and the transference of safety—some lights outlive their physical form via human continuity. A few lines (“To bottle the feeling of safety”) edge toward explicit thesis, but they’re grounded enough in concrete action to avoid sermonizing. The emotional aftereffect—gentle, bittersweet endurance—is clear and earned.</comment>

Psychic distance is well-controlled, moving smoothly between concrete perception and interior reflection without leakage or authorial intrusion. The quiet, slightly archaic cadence suits the haunted, long-memory setting and holds under emotional pressure.</comment>

The closure image—the faint, grateful glow replacing the great beam—is a non-obvious yet inevitable-feeling synthesis of the story’s elements. It stops short of radical innovation, but it’s distinctly realized and thematically cohesive.</comment>
