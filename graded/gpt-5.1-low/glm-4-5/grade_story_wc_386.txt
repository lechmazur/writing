<question>2</question><grade>6.4</grade><comment>The causal chain is coherent: dive → retrieval of the globe → amplified dream-contact → uncovering sabotage → gentle exposure → captain’s release. Closure is present as a resonant reconfiguration—the truth changes how the wreck is understood and frees the captain—but the cost of that truth is mostly conceptual, not dramatized. Sensory detail leans heavily visual, yet the “voice like the sound of ice forming” hints at a richer, more embodied palette the story only partially exploits.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>5.8</grade><comment>The dominant stake—whether and how the buried truth about the wreck will be brought to light—is clear, and Elara’s concern with the manner of revelation provides some ethical tension. However, there’s little real resistance: the dreams work, the descendants accept them, and the truth emerges without serious pushback or sacrifice on the page. The surviving crew’s crime remains offstage and unopposed, so escalation feels conceptual rather than experiential. As a result, the resolution is thematically neat but emotionally under-pressured.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>6.2</grade><comment>Themes of truth, purity salvaged from ruin, and healing the relationship between living and dead are coherent and rooted in recurring images (wreck, snow globe, “windows of connection”). Complexity is limited: the perpetrators remain abstract, and ethical ambiguity (e.g., potential harm to descendants) is acknowledged but not deeply troubled. The final paragraphs feel more like an essay on careful truth-telling than an earned, quietly ambiguous revelation.</comment>
