<question>3</question><grade>8.7</grade><comment>The arboretum, cliff, and dark lighthouse are vivid and specific, with the sea and oyster shell functioning as charged objects that mirror her inner state. Setting isn’t just backdrop: the cliff and wind physically enable the key act of releasing the photograph, and the arboretum’s cycles of growth thematically frame her resilience. Details stay selective and character-filtered, avoiding tourist-list description. The environment meaningfully charges the final image of her standing at the cliff’s edge between darkness and light.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>7.8</grade><comment>The primary conflict—Maya’s struggle to live with trauma while remaining open to beauty—is clear and coherent, and the stakes (her ability to keep functioning and teaching others) feel real. Pressure escalates internally from contained daily ritual to the uniquely loaded anniversary scene, forcing a choice about whether to hold or relinquish the photo. However, tension remains relatively low-key and meditative, with no sharp reversal or unexpected complication. The final renunciation is earned but emotionally controlled rather than searing.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>7.4</grade><comment>Themes of resilience, coexistence of love and loss, and “optimism anchored in realism” are deeply woven through repeated images (plants, oyster, stillness, photograph). The best moments (the oyster comparison, teaching children to notice their own signals) embody theme without explaining it. Strong resonance, but the didactic edges keep it short of top-tier subtlety.</comment>

POV is stable and free of head-hopping, and the narrative distance is well managed. The perceptual signature—attention to plants, bodies, and small sensory cues—is distinct and coherent.</comment>
