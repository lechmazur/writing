<question>3</question><grade>9.2</grade><comment>The setting is vivid, original, and constantly in play: basalt pillars, orchard, storm, and copper network all shape what is possible. The world’s rules—stone learning to speak, trees negotiating with rock—are taught through action, not exposition. The final image of him dying “between them, listening, as everything negotiated peace” is charged precisely because the environment’s agency and history have been so well established.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>8.5</grade><comment>The dominant stakes—what he does with his remaining time and whether he can forge a genuine cross-species architecture before he dies—are clear and consistently pressured. The cost of his project is principally his spent body and the foreclosed option of returning to the mainland; that price is visible at the end. The only limitation is that opposition remains mostly situational and internal, so tension never quite sharpens into a true crisis beat.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>8.8</grade><comment>Themes of apology, interspecies respect, and architecture as dialogue emerge organically through his work with stone, trees, birds, and lightning. The piece resists a neat moral; his project is tender but not grandiose, and his death is both a success and an irrevocable loss. The one slightly on-the-nose line (“Now his motivation was to foster interspecies respect…”) is the main restraint on an otherwise superbly implicit thematic weave.</comment>
