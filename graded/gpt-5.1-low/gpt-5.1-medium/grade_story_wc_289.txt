The choice to write a letter that is for “whoever steps onto the path” and also for herself and the missing architect-figure constitutes a real, if understated, narrowing of options. <question>5</question><grade>8.6</grade><comment>Theme—connection, what keeps people apart, and the cost of carrying others’ unsent truths—emerges organically from her job, the commissioned monument, and the nest/thread imagery. Lines like “We want the letter to speak to whatever keeps people from each other” come close to thesis-stating but are grounded in the architect’s personal need. The ending’s partial release offers a complex, non-moralizing answer: connection is a shared, ongoing act rather than a solved mystery.</comment>

The architect’s dialogue and described whimsy subtly distinguish his register from hers. Psychic distance stays stable, letting the lyricism heighten pressure instead of blurring it.</comment>

There’s mild risk of density overload in places, but clarity and momentum remain intact.</comment>
<question>8</question><grade>8.7</grade><comment>The premise—a professional ghostwriter of love letters hired by a temple/public art project to inscribe a communal letter—is fresh, and the thrush’s nest as “seed” of a walkway woven from discarded messages is an elegant twist. Emotional stakes are intimate yet scaled to a city’s attempt to make memory walkable, which is a non-obvious, intriguing synthesis. The risks are mostly in concept and language rather than experimental structure, but they pay off.</comment>
