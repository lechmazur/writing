<question>4</question><grade>8.0</grade><comment>The central conflict—whether solitude is a dead end or a resource—is clear and sharpened by the apparition’s warning about isolation-as-purity killing a world. The decisive internal shift—choosing to “feed [the library’s] movements with my own decisions” and tie independence to resisting despair—earns the ending. Stakes remain primarily conceptual and emotional rather than situationally urgent, but the deepening is substantial for the scope.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>7.8</grade><comment>Themes of isolation vs independence, reincarnation as continuity, and preparing for an unknowable future emerge organically from setting and action, especially the layered faces in the sundial and the responsive library at the end. However, key thematic lines (“Independence is different… it means choosing connection, not needing it”) verge on thesis statement and slightly blunt the subtext. A bit more reliance on implication over articulation would lift this into a higher band.</comment>

The specific fusion of sky whales, forest cities, atmospheric tides, and time-walking librarians feels like a singular vision rather than genre pastiche. It doesn’t quite break into utterly surprising territory in its thematic resolution, but the synthesis is elegant and memorable.</comment>
