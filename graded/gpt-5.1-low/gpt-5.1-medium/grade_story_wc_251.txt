Costs are conceptual rather than visceral but legible: a near-permanent reconfiguration of the town’s trajectory.</comment>

Environmental detail evolves with the action—the fog on the night of the displays sharpens lights and attention. The final grotto scene recharges the entire physical world of the story as a medium for “forests of second thoughts.”</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>8.2</grade><comment>The dominant stake—saving the hillside woods—appears early and tightens coherently through the choice of a quiet, indirect tactic (the window forest). The escalation from private perception to town-wide, synchronized action is well-paced and climaxes effectively in the council’s balcony-view and vote. Internal/external conflicts interweave, but Daniel rarely faces a stark trade-off or real personal jeopardy, so the felt price remains relatively gentle. Still, the rejection of louder, confrontational tactics in favor of quiet influence is a clear, earned stance.</comment>
<question>5</question><grade>8.5</grade><comment>Themes of second thoughts, quiet resistance, and how attention can reconfigure civic decisions emerge organically from repeated images (chess, leaves, forests, glass, unspoken words). There is slight thesis-forward language (“forests of second thoughts,” “a humble rebellion, broadcast in glass”) that borders on declared theme, but it still grows from Daniel’s perception. Overall, the piece invites reflection without sermonizing.</comment>
<question>6</question><grade>8.7</grade><comment>The third-person-limited voice is controlled and steady, with a distinct sensibility that fuses Daniel’s careful, chess-like thinking and the story’s magical quiet. Rhythms are well-modulated—lyric without becoming opaque—and the diction stays consistent and apt. Occasional elevated phrases match the slightly enchanted realist frame rather than feeling like register drift. POV access is clean; we never lose track of whose experience filters the world.</comment>
<question>7</question><grade>8.6</grade><comment>Line-level craft is strong: sentences are musical, information-dense, and usually do more than one job (advancing plot while building mood or motif).
