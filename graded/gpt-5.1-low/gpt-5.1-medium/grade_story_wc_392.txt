Spatial clarity is maintained despite the lyric mode.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>8.0</grade><comment>The core conflict—protect the Transit Authority vs. acknowledge preventable deaths—is clear early via the supervisor’s warnings and tightens as apparitions confront the narrator with personalized evidence of past complicity. Escalation is largely internal but effective: the ghosts force a re-reading of every prior signed form and transform “hauntings” into “data,” which raises the ethical cost of denial. The final choice to write the report truthfully forecloses the safer path, though the material consequences remain implied rather than dramatized. A slightly more concrete sense of immediate risk in-scene could have heightened the pressure.</comment>

The piece edges close to overt thesis in lines like “How many deaths had I signed off as unavoidable because someone above me preferred the math that way,” but it mostly earns its insights through prior scene work. The ending leaves a resonant, morally charged ambiguity rather than a tidy moral.</comment>

Alvarez’s sparse dialogue contrasts subtly with the narrator’s more analytic cadence. A few lines restate an idea (“entangled memories tightening like wires” vs. later “entangled memories were not just hauntings”) with similar language, but not enough to drag momentum. Figurative density is high yet controlled, and syntax remains clear even at emotional peaks.</comment>

The final conceptual turn—reframing reports to include these testimonies—is a non-obvious but inevitable extension of the premise. Overall, it reads as a singular, confident synthesis rather than a template plot with new window dressing.</comment>
