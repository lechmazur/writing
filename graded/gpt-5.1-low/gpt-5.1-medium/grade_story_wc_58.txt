The garden is not mere backdrop; its paths and remembered incidents literally map Sera’s pain and frame the walk that constitutes the story’s central action. The recurring carpet/thread metaphor ties interior spaces (palace corridors, unfinished carpet) to this external garden. The final image of carrying her presence “like a lantern” is charged by all the preceding environmental work.</comment>

Stakes are emotional and reputational rather than physical, centered on whether vanishing can be framed as healing. Pressure escalates through the garden walk as spaces are redefined by the pain they hold, tightening the question of whether these rituals are legitimate. The cost is somewhat implicit, resting on understood loneliness and social misreading, but it’s still felt.</comment>

The final reframing—her obituary as “a map of explained absences”—reweights all prior material without moralizing. Subtext around social judgment, self-exile, and the ethics of observing others’ pain is rich and lingering.</comment>

<question>8</question><grade>8.8</grade><comment>The premise—a traveler who writes obituaries for the living, commissioned to explain a noblewoman’s deliberate absences—is fresh and executed with integrity. While grief-as-landscape is not wholly new, the specific synthesis of harbor departures, palace carpets, and an autumn garden is distinctive. The ending’s conceptual turn (nothing disappears; it moves beneath the pattern) feels both surprising and inevitable in light of the setup.</comment>
