<question>4</question><grade>8.0</grade><comment>The dominant stake—whether and how Oren can alter the eclipsed covenant without destroying the fragile peace that cost him exile—is clear and tight. His agency in offering a new “guideline for play” and accepting a limited, subtle shift feels earned and carries conceptual/emotional cost. The tension is more intellectual and ethical than visceral, which slightly softens the sense of pressure despite strong design.</comment>

The boy’s phrase about discovering beauty in the difficult is reweighted at the end through Oren’s new stance, giving the piece a resonant, non-didactic answer to its central question. Ambiguity remains where it should: he is neither absolved nor condemned, only repositioned.</comment>

The ending’s stance—that he cannot unmake the shadow of past promises, only carve out a narrow beauty within their difficulty—is a distinctive moral calculus, not a stock moral.
