Closure lands as a resonant reconfiguration: he accepts ongoing, applause‑less labor as the path to “tenuous absolution.” The cost is conceptual and vocational rather than external, but it’s on‑page and felt.</comment>

Objects function as micro‑setting (bread, bamboo knife, teapots) and charge the final image of moonlit rivulets and seed mounds. Environment constrains and enables action, not just decorates it. The world’s soft rules (who the tea house admits) stay consistent and meaningful.</comment>
<question>4</question><grade>8.3</grade><comment>Stakes—seeking sacredness amid erosion and guilt—are clear early and deepen at the pavilion and during his admission of complicity. The turn is the door’s opening and the ritual that reframes his work; pressure escalates through his voiced guilt and Ko’s answer. The visible price is acceptance of quiet, ongoing, recognition‑free labor and the refusal of sterile cataloging as an endpoint. It’s a quiet escalation, but coherent and sufficient for the form.</comment>

However, the near‑final explicit thesis (“sacredness is merely the ordinary relieved of its disguise”) drifts into moralizing, reducing resonance. Even so, the final vow and image reweight prior motifs without didactic wrap‑up. The piece invites reflection on attention as agency with credible ambiguity around absolution.</comment>
<question>6</question><grade>8.8</grade><comment>The voice is assured, lyrical, and consistently filtered through Elias’s perceptual signature (field‑tuned noticing, soil and seam metaphors). Dialogue is minimal but carries subtext and shifts status without exposition. Diction remains coherent to a contemplative, skilled observer; rhythmic control supports the quiet turns. No head‑hopping or stance slips undermine the effect.</comment>

Opening and final images align cleanly without repetition loops.</comment>
